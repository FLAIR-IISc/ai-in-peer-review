**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard approach, Expectation-Maximization (EM), is often slow for large datasets. The authors' key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single, large HMM with a MK-state block-diagonal transition matrix. They then leverage an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this large model. This standard procedure recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main technical contribution of this paper is a "de-permutation" algorithm that exploits the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By performing a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvectors and clustering the columns of the resulting matrix, the algorithm recovers the block structure and thus identifies the parameters of the individual HMMs. The authors also propose a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" of eigenvalues to estimate the number of components, K, from the data. Experiments show the proposed method is significantly faster than EM and provides comparable accuracy, while also serving as an excellent initialization strategy for EM.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of spectral learning to MHMMs is a novel and important contribution. MHMMs are a powerful modeling tool, but the computational cost of EM is a well-known practical barrier. This paper addresses this bottleneck directly. The core idea of treating the MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is elegant and enables the application of existing powerful MoM techniques.

2.  **Clever Technical Approach:** The proposed de-permutation procedure is the main technical novelty and is well-motivated. The use of the spectral properties of the transition matrix (specifically, the structure of its limiting power `Ā^∞` in the noiseless case) to identify the component HMMs is a clever solution to the parameter-mixing problem inherent in this approach.

3.  **Strong Practical Implications:** The experimental results demonstrate a significant practical advantage. The dramatic speed-up compared to EM (Figure 4) makes this method highly attractive for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, the real-data experiment (Table 1) highlights a very valuable use case: even if the spectral method alone is not superior to a well-initialized EM, it provides a principled, fast, and high-quality initialization that significantly improves the performance of EM. This is a strong practical result.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The progression from the model definition, to the key insight (Lemma 1), to the de-permutation strategy for the noiseless and then noisy cases is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the theoretical analysis of the noisy case, which is central to the algorithm's practical utility.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The claim that the estimated low-rank reconstruction `Ar` is close to the true one is presented as a conjecture. The justification provided is quite hand-wavy, relying on the triangle inequality and citing a bound from a different model [1] without a formal proof of its applicability here. For a top-tier conference like NIPS, a more rigorous analysis of the error bounds for the core estimation procedure is expected.
    *   **Lemma 3 and K Estimation:** The method for estimating the number of clusters K is presented as a lemma but feels more like a heuristic. The concept of "spectral longevity" is novel but its theoretical properties are not well-established. The proof sketch relies on intuitive arguments about convergence rates rather than a formal derivation. The robustness of this method to the choice of `γ` and the range of exponents `e` is not analyzed or discussed.

2.  **Limited Experimental Validation of K Estimation:** While the proposed method for estimating K is a key part of the algorithm, its performance is not empirically evaluated. The experiments are conducted with a known K. It would be crucial to see how well the `Lλ̃K′` criterion works in practice, especially as the noise level (i.e., amount of data) varies and how it compares to standard model selection criteria like BIC or cross-validation.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The feature transformation in the real data experiment (Section 4.3) is mentioned briefly ("cubic polynomial feature transformation such that L = 10"). More details are needed for reproducibility. What was the exact transformation? This is an important detail as it directly impacts the applicability of the underlying MoM algorithm.
    *   The discussion around Figure 1 is a bit confusing. It's not immediately clear how `(ĀP)∞` is computed in the top row vs. `(Ā P )e` in the bottom row. Clarifying that the former is a rank-K reconstruction based on the top K eigenvectors would improve readability.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and practically significant contribution. The proposed method for spectral learning of MHMMs is elegant and addresses a real-world computational problem. The experimental results, particularly the speed-up and its effectiveness as an EM initializer, are compelling.

The main drawback is the weakness of the theoretical justification for the noisy case and the K-estimation procedure. These parts are presented more as plausible conjectures and heuristics than as rigorously proven results.

However, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the empirical findings are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The paper opens up a new, computationally efficient avenue for learning an important class of models. I would recommend **Acceptance**, with the strong suggestion that the authors acknowledge the limitations of their theoretical analysis more explicitly and, if possible, either provide more rigorous proofs or tone down the claims in Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3 to reflect their heuristic nature. Adding an experiment on the performance of the K-estimation procedure would also substantially strengthen the paper.
