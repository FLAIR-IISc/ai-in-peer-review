**Summary**

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs [1, 2] to learn the parameters of this large HMM. This standard approach, however, recovers the parameters only up to a permutation, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main technical contribution of this work is a "de-permutation" method that uses the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix to identify the blocks and recover the parameters of the individual HMM components. The authors also propose a heuristic method based on the eigenvalue spectrum to estimate the number of components, K. Experiments on synthetic and real data compare the proposed method to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in terms of accuracy and speed.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The formulation of the MHMM learning problem as a de-permutation problem on a single large HMM is an interesting and elegant idea. While spectral methods for HMMs are established, their application to the mixture setting and the proposed solution to the resulting parameter-mixing problem is novel.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for MoM-based approaches is their computational advantage over iterative methods like EM. The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed algorithm is significantly faster than EM, particularly as the amount of data grows. This is a valuable contribution, as EM's per-iteration cost can be prohibitive for large datasets.

3.  **Non-Iterative Nature:** The proposed spectral method provides a closed-form solution (post-moment estimation), thus avoiding the issues of local optima that plague EM and its sensitivity to initialization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justification for the algorithm's performance in the presence of noise is weak. The analysis hinges on "Conjecture 1," which provides an error bound for the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix. The authors justify this conjecture by borrowing a result from [1] for a different model (a mixture model, not an MHMM) and explicitly state that a more detailed analysis is "out of the scope of this paper." For a conference like NIPS, a core theoretical claim presented as an unproven conjecture with a justification of "we believe" is a significant weakness. The soundness of the entire approach in the practical, noisy setting is not formally established.

2.  **Heuristic Model Selection:** The method for determining the number of clusters K (Lemma 3 and "spectral longevity") appears ad-hoc and is not compared against established model selection techniques (e.g., BIC, cross-validation). Its robustness is not thoroughly evaluated; for instance, its sensitivity to the `γ` parameter is not discussed. While clever, this heuristic lacks the rigorous validation expected of a primary contribution.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly support the paper's claims of being a competitive alternative to EM.
    *   **Weak Baseline:** The comparison against EM uses only four random restarts. Given the non-convexity of the MHMM likelihood, this is an insufficient effort to establish a strong EM baseline. It is unclear if the spectral method is truly superior to a more carefully tuned EM implementation (e.g., with more restarts or smarter initialization schemes).
    *   **Underwhelming Real-Data Results:** The results on the handwritten character dataset are not compelling. The authors' own conclusion is that "spectral learning doesn’t always surpass randomly initialized EM on its own, it does serve as a very good initialization scheme." This significantly weakens the paper's positioning. The method is presented as a standalone learning algorithm, but the evidence suggests its primary utility may be as a pre-processing step for EM. This pivots the contribution from being a replacement for EM to being an initializer for it, which is a less significant claim.
    *   **Practical Limitations:** The underlying MoM algorithm requires `MK < L` (total states < feature dimension). The authors address this in the real-data experiment by applying a feature transformation, but this is a significant practical constraint that is not adequately discussed. This limitation can make the method inapplicable to many real-world problems with low-dimensional observations.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The key assumption in Lemma 2 is that each component HMM `Ak` has a unique eigenvalue of 1. This implies each `Ak` is irreducible and aperiodic. While common, this assumption should be stated more explicitly as a condition for the method to work.
*   In Section 4.1, the experiment varies sequence length `T` but keeps the number of sequences per cluster fixed at a very low value (2). This seems like an unusual setup. How does the de-permutation algorithm's performance vary with `N`, the number of sequences, which is often the more critical factor in moment-based estimation?
*   The results in Figure 4 show EM achieving higher accuracy in low-data regimes. While the spectral method eventually catches up, this trade-off is critical. The paper would be stronger if it characterized this frontier more thoroughly and provided guidance on when one method should be preferred over the other.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents a novel and conceptually interesting spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs. The core idea of identifying and resolving the permutation ambiguity in a global HMM is clever. However, the work in its current form is undermined by a lack of theoretical rigor regarding its noisy-case performance and by an experimental evaluation that fails to convincingly demonstrate its superiority over a well-established EM baseline. The theoretical analysis rests on an unproven conjecture, and the empirical results suggest the method's main strength may be as an initializer for EM rather than a standalone algorithm. While the speed advantage is clear, the questions surrounding accuracy and theoretical soundness prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of the MHMM learning problem.
*   Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck of the standard EM algorithm.
*   Provides a non-iterative approach that avoids local optima.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Core theoretical claim for the noisy case is presented as an unproven conjecture.
*   Experimental validation is not thorough; the EM baseline is weak, and real-data results are not strong enough to support claims of being a competitive standalone method.
*   The proposed method for model selection (estimating K) is heuristic and not well-validated.

**Recommendation:** Reject
