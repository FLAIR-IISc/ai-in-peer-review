

**Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"**

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recognizing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices. The authors adapt spectral learning for HMMs [1, 2] to recover global parameters, then address permutation ambiguity via spectral properties of the global transition matrix. Key contributions include a depermutation algorithm leveraging eigenvalue structure and a method to estimate the number of clusters *K* using "spectral longevity." The work extends Anandkumar et al.’s spectral HMM learning [1, 2] to MHMMs, addressing computational bottlenecks of EM (e.g., forward-backward for each sequence). Related work includes Smyth’s MHMM clustering [4] and Qi et al.’s HMM mixtures for music [5].

**Strengths**  
1. **Computational Advantage**: The method avoids per-sequence EM steps, yielding scalability for large datasets (Fig. 4). The speed-accuracy trade-off (e.g., spectral learning is 10× faster than EM with comparable accuracy) is well-demonstrated synthetically and on real data.  
2. **Practical Utility**: The method serves as a strong EM initialization (Table 1), addressing a known EM limitation. This is valuable for practitioners.  
3. **Theoretical Insight**: The MHMM-as-block-diagonal-HMM equivalence (Lemma 1) is clear and well-motivated, providing a clean foundation for the spectral approach.  
4. **Experiments**: Synthetic tests (Figs. 3–4) validate robustness to sequence length/number. Real-data results (Table 1) show practical relevance, though the polynomial feature transformation for UCI data requires justification (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Theoretical Gaps**:  
   - Conjecture 1 (error bound for *Ar*) lacks proof and improperly reuses [1]’s sample complexity without justification for MHMMs. A rigorous analysis is essential for NIPS.  
   - Lemma 3’s "mixing rate condition" (*|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|*) is critical but untested in experiments. The paper does not explore failure cases when this fails (e.g., overlapping clusters).  
   - The spectral longevity metric (Def. 3) is ad-hoc; no empirical analysis shows *why* it outperforms simpler methods (e.g., gap-based *K* selection).  

2. **Methodological Flaws**:  
   - The UCI experiment transforms 3D trajectories to 10D via cubic polynomials to satisfy *MK < L* (a MoM requirement). This distorts data geometry without justification (e.g., loss of temporal structure), undermining real-data validity.  
   - No comparison to MoM-based MHMM methods (e.g., [9] cited but not evaluated). The paper claims "spectral learning" but does not clarify how it differs from existing MoM approaches for mixtures.  

3. **Clarity & Rigor**:  
   - Section 3.2.2’s noise analysis is vague. Terms like "sufficiently small noise" lack quantification.  
   - Algorithm 1’s "depermutation" step (clustering columns of *Ar*) is not justified—why does this recover *K* blocks? A small synthetic example would clarify.  

**Significance**  
The computational efficiency for large-scale MHMM learning is significant, especially for sequence clustering. The EM initialization use case is promising. However, the theoretical shortcomings and unvalidated assumptions (e.g., eigenvalue conditions) limit the contribution’s impact. The work does not advance the *state of the art* in spectral learning for latent models, as it primarily repurposes existing techniques rather than introducing novel spectral theory.

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. While the paper identifies a valid problem and offers a useful computational alternative to EM, the theoretical gaps (Conjecture 1, unproven *K*-estimation) and methodological issues (data transformation, missing baselines) prevent it from meeting NIPS’s standards for technical soundness. The significance is limited by the lack of rigorous guarantees and untested assumptions.

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Strengthen Theory**: Prove Conjecture 1 or reframe it as a theorem with explicit conditions. Rigorously analyze Lemma 3’s eigenvalue conditions via synthetic experiments (e.g., varying *|λL2,k|*).  
2. **Address Data Transformation**: In the UCI experiment, either (a) justify the cubic transformation’s preservation of cluster structure, or (b) use a different dataset where *MK < L* holds naturally (e.g., higher-dimensional observations).  
3. **Add Baselines**: Compare to [9] and other MoM-based MHMM methods (e.g., tensor-based [3]) in real-data experiments.  
4. **Clarify Algorithm**: Include a small synthetic example (e.g., *K=2, M=2*) demonstrating depermutation. Explain why spectral longevity is superior to simpler *K*-selection methods.  

The core idea is promising, but the paper must deliver rigorous theory and validated methodology to qualify as a NIPS contribution. With these revisions, it could become a strong submission.
