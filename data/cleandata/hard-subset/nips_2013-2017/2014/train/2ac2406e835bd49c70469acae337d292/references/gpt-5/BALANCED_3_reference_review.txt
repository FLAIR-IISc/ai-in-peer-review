Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach. The key observation is that an MHMM over K components with M states each can be viewed as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal and whose emission matrix is the horizontal concatenation of the component emission matrices. Given this equivalence, the authors propose: (i) apply an existing spectral HMM learner to estimate the global MK-state HMM, which yields parameters up to an unknown permutation over states; (ii) recover the block structure (and hence the component HMMs) by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix; in the noiseless case, powers of the transition matrix converge to a block-constant form, and in the noisy case the authors propose a low-rank reconstruction and a clustering-based “de-permutation” step; (iii) estimate the number of components K by analyzing the sorted eigenvalues of the estimated transition matrix via a “spectral longevity” measure. Experiments on synthetic data and a small real dataset compare to EM, showing speed advantages and comparable accuracies, with the spectral estimates also serving as a useful EM initializer.

This work builds on spectral methods for HMMs and latent variable models (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang 2012; Anandkumar et al. 2012) and on the classical EM for MHMMs (e.g., Smyth 1997) with applications to sequence clustering. The idea of exploiting block structure is natural given the MHMM-to-HMM reduction; the paper’s main novelty is the de-permutation strategy and the heuristic for selecting K using eigenvalue trajectories.

Strengths
- Clear reduction of MHMM to an HMM with block-diagonal transitions, enabling reuse of spectral HMM learners.
- Practical de-permutation idea: low-rank reconstruction and column clustering of the transition matrix to recover blocks.
- A heuristic to estimate K from eigenvalue “longevity” that is simple to compute.
- Empirical evidence that the approach is significantly faster than EM while achieving comparable clustering quality in several regimes, and that spectral estimates help initialize EM.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- The central robustness results are stated as conjectures rather than theorems. Conjecture 1 reuses sample complexity bounds from prior spectral work without a careful adaptation to the block-diagonal MHMM setting, and the inequality chain (and the bound on the Frobenius norm of the residual) is not rigorous. The argument that ||Ar − Ar||F = O(1/√TN) “w.h.p.” needs precise conditions and a proof.
- The de-permutation relies on assumptions: each local Ak has exactly one eigenvalue of magnitude 1 and nondefective structure; global “top-K” eigenvalues stay separated under estimation noise. These conditions and their failure modes (e.g., nearly equal subdominant eigenvalues, repeated eigenvalues across components, slow mixing) are not analyzed.
- The spectral longevity metric L requires selecting γ and exponentiation range; no sensitivity analysis or theoretical justification beyond an intuitive gap argument is provided. It may be fragile when spectral gaps are small or when local second eigenvalues approach the smallest global eigenvalue.
- The spectral HMM learner used for continuous observations is underspecified. Hsu et al. primarily handle discrete observations; continuous-output spectral HMMs typically require feature expectations or regression-based observable operators (e.g., Siddiqi, Boots, Gordon; Song et al.). The paper sets O(:,u)=E[x|r=u] and requires L ≥ MK, but does not detail the moment construction, whitening, or identifiability conditions for the continuous case, nor cite the most relevant continuous-observation spectral HMM literature.
- Experiments are limited. Synthetic tests use small K,M and moderate L, with short sequences per cluster; real-data validation is on a single dataset with pairwise clustering tasks, and no variance bars, ablations, or comprehensive baselines beyond EM. There is no stress test for challenging spectra (near-degenerate eigenvalues), mis-specified K, or overlapping emissions.

Clarity
- Overall presentation is readable and the main idea is conveyed. However:
  - The statement “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation” should be clarified: PAP^T is similar to A, hence has the same eigenvalues, and eigenvectors are permuted by P.
  - Notation occasionally overloads bars and hats; the roles of θ̄, θ̄P, and their estimates could be better delineated.
  - Algorithm 1 references “HMM MethodofMoments” without specifying which variant is used and the conditions under which it is consistent for continuous data.
  - Definitions of noise factors αk, βj,k in the eigenvalues need a model for perturbations (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin-style perturbation bounds) to connect to sample size.

Originality
- The MHMM-to-HMM reduction is straightforward and well-known. The novelty lies in the specific spectral de-permutation/clustering heuristic and longevity-based K selection. This is a modest, practical extension rather than a fundamentally new theoretical contribution.

Significance
- If made robust, a scalable MHMM learner that avoids per-sequence forward–backward passes could be impactful for sequence clustering at scale. As it stands, the method is promising but lacks theoretical guarantees and comprehensive empirical validation, limiting confidence in broad applicability.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical and simple de-permutation idea with encouraging speedups.
- Sensible use of spectral structure to estimate K.
- Clean conceptual link enabling reuse of spectral HMM methods.

Cons:
- Key results are conjectural; insufficient theoretical backing for robustness and sample complexity.
- Limited experiments and missing baselines; no stress tests or sensitivity analyses.
- Incomplete treatment and citation of continuous-observation spectral HMMs and identifiability conditions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace Conjecture 1 with a theorem under explicit assumptions using standard matrix perturbation tools (e.g., Weyl’s, Davis–Kahan), quantifying how sample size, mixing times, and spectral gaps affect recovery and K estimation.
- Analyze failure modes: near-equal local second eigenvalues; components with similar stationary distributions; non-ergodic or nearly reducible Ak; repeated eigenvalues across components; non-diagonalizable cases.
- Formalize and justify the longevity score (choice of γ, exponentiation range), or consider alternative, more stable criteria (e.g., eigengap statistics with bootstrap, information criteria on low-rank approximations).
- Specify the spectral HMM learner for continuous emissions (feature construction, whitening, rank conditions), and cite relevant continuous spectral literature.
- Expand experiments: larger K and M, varying spectral gaps, mis-specified MK and L, robustness to noise, and comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., variational MHMMs, HDP-HMM with mixture components, subspace clustering baselines). Report wall-clock times and memory.
- Provide ablations (with/without K estimation, different clustering algorithms), sensitivity to model mismatch, and confidence intervals across seeds. Release code.

Recommendation
Reject (constructive). The idea is appealing and potentially useful, but the paper currently lacks rigorous guarantees where they matter most and provides limited empirical evidence. With stronger theory, clearer specification for continuous observations, and more thorough experiments, this work could become a solid contribution.
