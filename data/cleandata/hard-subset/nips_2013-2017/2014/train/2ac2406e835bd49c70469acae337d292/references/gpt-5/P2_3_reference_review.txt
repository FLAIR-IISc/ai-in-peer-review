Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be recast as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with each block corresponding to one mixture component. The authors then: (i) apply an existing spectral HMM learner to recover the global parameters up to a permutation of the MK states; (ii) exploit spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix to estimate a de-permutation mapping that reconstructs the K blocks (hence, the per-HMM parameters); and (iii) propose a heuristic to estimate K by examining the “spectral longevity” of the top eigenvalues. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten trajectory dataset suggest the method is computationally cheaper than EM and a useful initializer for EM.

This builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent-variable models (e.g., Hsu-Kakade-Zhang; Anandkumar et al.) and on classical EM for MHMMs (Smyth). The reduction of an MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is straightforward and known in various contexts. The novel contribution is the de-permutation procedure and the eigenvalue longevity heuristic for choosing K.

Quality:
- Positive: The reduction is correct (Lemma 1), and Lemma 2/Corollary 1 describe standard spectral properties of block-diagonal Markov chains. The proposed pipeline is plausible: a global spectral recovery followed by clustering columns of a low-rank reconstruction to group states by component. The synthetic experiments show the de-permutation can work and that runtime is favorable relative to EM when data are large.
- Concerns: The core steps that make the method practical are supported largely by heuristics. Conjecture 1 (recovery of the rank-K part and an O(1/√TN) bound) is central, yet unproven and justified by reusing bounds from different settings; the claimed sample complexity and stability under noise need a concrete statement and proof tailored to block-diagonal HMMs. The condition |λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k| is strong and its prevalence in realistic MHMMs is not assessed; when local chains mix slowly (second eigenvalues close to 1), the method may fail. The K-selection via “spectral longevity” (Definition 3, Lemma 3) is also heuristic and depends on an eigengap ratio; there is no comparison to standard eigengap methods or model-selection criteria (BIC), and algorithmic choices (γ, exponentiation range) are not specified or justified.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is generally readable and well organized. The reduction and the noiseless intuition are clearly explained.
- Missing details hinder reproducibility: which specific spectral HMM routine is used for continuous emissions; exact moment construction; requirements such as MK < L and how the polynomial feature map affects identifiability and estimation; how e, γ, and clustering are chosen; and complexity of each stage. The statement “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation” should be phrased precisely (eigenvalues invariant; eigenvectors permuted).
- Proofs are uneven: Lemma 1 is fine; Lemma 2/Corollary 1 rely on standard Markov chain theory but assume each Ak has a single eigenvalue 1; this needs conditions (irreducible, aperiodic). Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3 are key for practice but lack rigorous conditions and finite-sample guarantees.

Originality:
- The MHMM→block-diagonal HMM equivalence is not new. The main originality is the de-permutation via low-rank reconstruction centered on the near-unit eigenvalues and the spectral longevity criterion. These are interesting ideas, but currently positioned as heuristics without theoretical backing. Related work on block structure recovery and eigengap selection (e.g., spectral clustering, block models, eigengap heuristics) should be discussed and contrasted.

Significance:
- Practical significance could be substantial if the method reliably yields fast, decent MHMM estimates and good EM initializations. However, the experimental evaluation is limited: one synthetic setting and one real dataset, modest K and M, and no stress tests on challenging spectra (slow-mixing components), larger K, or misspecification. There is no comparison for K-selection against standard baselines. The reliance on MK < L and the use of a polynomial feature expansion to satisfy it raise questions about when this is feasible and whether it biases the model.

Pros:
- Clear and simple reduction; intuitive spectral de-permutation idea.
- Potentially large computational advantage over EM, especially for large datasets.
- Promising as an EM initializer; preliminary real-data results support this.
- Addresses an important practical task: clustering sequences via MHMMs.

Cons:
- Central claims are heuristic (Conjecture 1; Lemma 3) without rigorous guarantees; sensitivity to spectral conditions not quantified.
- Limited experiments; lack of comparisons for K selection; no ablations on spectral gaps, noise, or parameter regimes.
- Practical requirements (MK < L) and handling of continuous observations are under-specified; the feature-expansion workaround may be problematic.
- Reproducibility details and hyperparameter choices are missing.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a theorem (with proof) giving sufficient conditions for correct de-permutation under finite samples, explicitly involving gaps between global and local eigenvalues and sampling noise.
- Evaluate robustness by varying the second-largest eigenvalues of Ak to be near 1; study failure modes.
- Compare K estimation to eigengap heuristics, BIC/AIC, and cross-validation; include sensitivity to γ and exponentiation range.
- Detail the moment construction for continuous emissions, the precise spectral HMM routine, and computational complexity. Discuss when MK < L holds and alternatives if it does not (e.g., multi-view moments using time-lagged views rather than feature expansion).
- Expand experiments: larger K, M, more real datasets, and runtime/accuracy trade-offs.

Overall assessment:
The paper tackles a relevant problem with a clever and potentially useful idea, but as written it falls short on theoretical rigor and empirical validation. With stronger analysis and more comprehensive experiments, it could make a solid contribution. In its current form, it partially meets the quality and significance criteria, is reasonably clear but missing key details, and has moderate originality.
