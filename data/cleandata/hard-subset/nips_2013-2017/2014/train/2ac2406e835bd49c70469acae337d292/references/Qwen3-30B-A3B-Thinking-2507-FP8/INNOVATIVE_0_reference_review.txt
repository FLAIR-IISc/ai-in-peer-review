

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper presents a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using method of moments (MoM) techniques, addressing a significant computational bottleneck in existing EM-based approaches. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The authors then propose a clever method to "undo" the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning to recover the parameters of individual HMMs in the mixture.

The paper builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning framework for HMMs [1,2] but extends it to MHMMs through a novel observation about the block-diagonal structure of the global transition matrix. This is a significant conceptual leap rather than a minor extension of existing methods. The authors establish theoretical conditions under which their approach works, including a spectral condition for identifying the number of clusters (Lemma 3), and provide a clear algorithmic procedure (Algorithm 1) for implementing their method.

The paper's main strengths are its conceptual novelty and the clear demonstration of computational advantages over EM. The authors show that their spectral approach avoids the expensive forward-backward message passing required by EM for each sequence in every iteration, making it significantly more scalable for large datasets. The experimental results (Section 4) convincingly demonstrate this advantage, showing that the spectral approach achieves comparable accuracy to EM with dramatically reduced computation time. The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories further validates the approach's practical utility, showing that it can serve as an effective initialization for EM.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly contextualizes its contribution within existing literature. The theoretical development is sound, with appropriate citations to related work on spectral learning for HMMs and MHMMs. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the spectral conditions required for the method to work), demonstrating intellectual honesty.

The main weaknesses are that some theoretical claims are presented as conjectures without full proof (Conjecture 1), and the real data experiment is somewhat limited in scope (only one dataset with a polynomial feature transformation). However, these are minor concerns given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

In terms of novelty, this paper makes a bold and non-trivial leap by recognizing the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs as an HMM and leveraging spectral properties to recover individual HMM parameters. This is not merely an incremental improvement over existing MHMM learning methods but a fundamentally new approach to the problem. The connection to spectral learning for HMMs is clever and demonstrates how existing techniques can be creatively applied to new problems.

The significance of this work is substantial. MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering, but their computational complexity has been a major limitation. By providing a computationally efficient alternative to EM that can also serve as a good initialization for EM, this paper addresses a practical problem with real-world impact. The authors also hint at broader applicability to other hierarchical latent variable models in their future work, suggesting the approach could have wider implications.

While the paper doesn't break entirely new theoretical ground, it makes a significant conceptual contribution that transforms how we approach MHMM learning. The authors successfully bridge the gap between existing spectral learning techniques and MHMMs through a novel observation and a clever algorithmic adaptation.

In my view, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. It offers a genuinely new approach to a well-studied problem (MHMM learning) that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The computational advantages demonstrated in the experiments are substantial, and the method could become a standard alternative to EM for MHMM learning in practice.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and properly contextualized within the literature. While some theoretical details could be strengthened, the core contribution is compelling and novel. Given my preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a strong case for acceptance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel conceptual insight (MHMM as HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix)
- Clear theoretical development with practical algorithm
- Demonstrated computational advantages over EM
- Good experimental validation on synthetic and real data
- Potential as initialization for EM

**Weaknesses:**
- Some theoretical claims presented as conjectures
- Real data experiment limited to one dataset
- Spectral conditions for method to work might be restrictive

This paper advances the state of the art in latent variable model learning by providing a fundamentally new approach to MHMMs that is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient. The authors have successfully transformed a challenging problem into one that can be addressed with existing spectral learning techniques through a clever observation. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS should celebrate.
