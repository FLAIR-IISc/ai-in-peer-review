Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning Mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs) via a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach. The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M latent states, is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. Building on spectral HMM learning (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.), the authors first estimate the “global” HMM parameters up to a permutation. The central contribution is a de-permutation procedure using spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, K eigenvalues equal to 1 yield a rank-K limit Ā∞ whose columns cluster by HMM component; with noise, they propose using the top-K eigenvalues and a “spectral longevity” heuristic to estimate K and recover the permutation. Experiments on synthetic data, a speed/accuracy comparison to EM, and a UCI handwritten character dataset suggest the method can be much faster than EM and provide a good initializer.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is correct and useful. The noiseless de-permutation using the eigen-structure is sound. However, the noisy case, which is the realistic setting, relies on an unproven Conjecture 1 and a heuristic “spectral longevity” metric (Definition 3, Lemma 3). No formal perturbation bounds (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) are provided to justify when the K leading “global” eigenvalues separate from local ones, how close the rank-K reconstruction is to the ideal projector, or when k-means on columns of Ar recovers the permutation with high probability.
- Completeness: Key algorithmic details are abstracted away as a black-box “HMM MethodofMoments.” It is unclear how moments are computed from sequences (choice of features, windowing, handling continuous emissions) and what conditions are required for identifiability in the global MK-state HMM setting (e.g., L ≥ MK is mentioned but not justified). The de-permutation clustering step lacks a precise specification (distance metric, normalization, sensitivity to eigen-gap).
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are small-scale and mainly qualitative; they do not quantify permutation recovery accuracy or robustness to noise/eigen-gap. The EM comparison uses few restarts and does not explore stronger baselines (e.g., H3M/variational MHMMs, better initializations). The real-data experiment requires a polynomial feature lift to satisfy L > MK but does not analyze how this affects the generative assumptions or MoM consistency.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, and the main idea comes across. That said, there are several clarity issues: references are incomplete and sometimes duplicated; some notation is sloppy (e.g., mixing of Ā, Ar, Ar̄, and permuted versions), and the assumptions behind O (e.g., O(:, u) = E[x|r = u] for various emissions) and the need for MK < L should be stated and motivated clearly up front. Algorithm 1 is high-level; it does not specify practical settings (choice of e-range, how to threshold longevity, clustering details). The justification for Conjecture 1 is informal and reuses a sample-complexity result from mixture models without a clear mapping.

Originality
- The reduction MHMM → block-diagonal HMM is known. The contribution lies in exploiting the eigen-structure to undo the permutation and to estimate K via the proposed “spectral longevity.” This is a reasonably novel angle, but the core technical advances are modest and largely heuristic. Related work on scalable MHMMs (e.g., hierarchical EM/H3M, variational Bayes) and spectral methods for mixtures of Markov chains/HMMs should be discussed more thoroughly; the current related work is sparse and references are incomplete.

Significance
- If robust, a fast, initialization-free MHMM learner would be valuable. The idea of using global transition spectral structure is elegant and potentially impactful. However, the current state (heuristic de-permutation; unproven spectral conditions; limited empirical validation) limits confidence in reliability on real-world data and larger models. As presented, the work is more a promising idea with preliminary evidence than a finished contribution ready for adoption.

Strengths
- Clear and useful equivalence of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM and leveraging existing spectral HMM tools.
- Intuitive de-permutation via eigen-analysis; potentially efficient and scalable once moments are computed.
- Empirical speed advantage over EM; promising as an initializer for EM on real data.

Weaknesses
- Core noisy-case theory is conjectural; no finite-sample guarantees for permutation recovery or K selection.
- Insufficient algorithmic detail to reproduce results; several design choices left unspecified.
- Limited, small-scale experiments; missing comparisons to stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., sensitivity to eigen-gaps, K/M/L, noise).
- Reference list is incomplete and formatting is poor.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a precise procedure and hyperparameters for the longevity computation (range of e, γ, stopping), and a principled threshold to pick K?
- Can you derive perturbation bounds (e.g., using Davis–Kahan) that guarantee correct clustering of columns of Ar under a separation condition?
- How sensitive is de-permutation to nearly identical stationary distributions vk across components?
- How are moments computed from sequences for continuous emissions, and what identifiability assumptions are required? Why is MK < L necessary, and can random feature maps alleviate this?
- Can you compare against H3M/variational MHMM baselines and report parameter recovery error and permutation accuracy?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, intuitive idea with practical potential for speed.
- Clear reduction to a known spectral learning setting.
- Could provide a useful EM initializer.

Cons:
- Lacks theoretical guarantees in the noisy, finite-sample regime.
- Algorithmic details and empirical validation are insufficient.
- Related work and references are incomplete; clarity issues remain.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents an appealing idea and preliminary empirical evidence, but needs stronger theory for the noisy case, clearer and more complete algorithmic exposition, and more comprehensive experiments and comparisons to meet the NeurIPS bar. I encourage the authors to develop perturbation guarantees for de-permutation/K-selection, flesh out the method, and broaden the empirical evaluation.
