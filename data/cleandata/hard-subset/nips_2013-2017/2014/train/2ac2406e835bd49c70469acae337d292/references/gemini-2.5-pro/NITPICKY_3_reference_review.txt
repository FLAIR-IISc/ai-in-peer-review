This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a KM x KM block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose using an existing spectral algorithm to learn the parameters of this large HMM. The main technical challenge, which is the focus of the paper, is that the learned parameters are only identifiable up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The authors' proposed solution is to recover the block structure by performing a low-rank reconstruction of the learned global transition matrix and clustering its columns. This "de-permutation" step relies on the spectral properties of the block-diagonal matrix. The paper also presents a heuristic method based on the eigenvalue spectrum to estimate the number of mixture components, K. This work builds upon the line of research on spectral learning for latent variable models [1, 2, 3] and presents a computationally efficient alternative to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for MHMMs [4].

**Strengths**

1.  The observation that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal structure is elegant and provides a clear path for applying existing spectral learning machinery.
2.  The paper addresses a critical and non-trivial problem: recovering the individual component parameters from the permuted global parameters learned by the spectral HMM algorithm.
3.  The proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over EM, as its learning time is independent of the dataset size after the initial moment computation step. This is a valuable practical contribution for large-scale sequential data clustering.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor notational sloppiness to significant theoretical and experimental weaknesses.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the precision and polish expected of a NIPS submission.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** The notation for the emission distribution is confusing and inconsistent. In Eq. (1), `O(xt)` is defined as `p(xt| :, O)`, implying it is a vector of probabilities. However, in the definitions that follow, `O(:, u)` is defined as the expected value `E[xt|rt = u]`. These are not the same; for a Gaussian, one is a density function and the other is its mean vector. This fundamental ambiguity confuses the model definition.
*   **Typos and Errors:** There are numerous small errors. A footnote on page 2 has a stray "1". In Eq. (2), the notation `1>J` appears, where `J` is undefined (presumably it should be `M`). The proof of Lemma 1 uses `Ok(xt)` while the preceding definition was `Ok(xt,n)`. The reference list is improperly formatted, with missing venue and year information for most entries, and critically, references [2] and [3] appear to be identical. Reference [9] is cited in the text but is missing from the reference list.
*   **Vague Descriptions:** Key parts of the algorithm are described too vaguely. Algorithm 1 states to "Exponentiate eigenvalues... in a sufficiently large range" without specifying the range or the procedure. It also says to "Cluster the columns" without specifying the clustering method (k-means is only mentioned in the experiments section).
*   **Missing Figures/Tables:** The text extensively refers to Figures 1, 2, 3, 4 and Table 1, none of which are included in the paper provided for review. This makes it impossible to fully evaluate the experimental claims.

**2. Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis underpinning the de-permutation and model selection steps is weak and relies heavily on conjecture.
*   **Unstated Assumptions:** Lemma 2 and Corollary 1 rely on the assumption that each component HMM's transition matrix `Ak` is irreducible and aperiodic, which guarantees a unique stationary distribution and a single eigenvalue of 1. This crucial assumption is never stated or discussed.
*   **Hand-wavy Justification:** The justification for Conjecture 1 is not a proof. It relies on loose bounding arguments and improperly imports a sample complexity result from [1] for a different model (mixture model) by simply stating the models are "similar". This is not rigorous. The notation also switches confusingly (`A` for `ĀP`, etc.).
*   **Heuristic Model Selection:** The method for determining the number of clusters K via "spectral longevity" (Lemma 3) is a complex heuristic. The proof is merely a qualitative argument about convergence rates, not a formal mathematical proof. The method's sensitivity to the threshold `γ` is not analyzed. This part feels more like an engineering trick than a principled method.
*   **Critical Condition `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|`:** This condition is central to the paper's success, as it requires a clean separation between the K eigenvalues close to 1 and all other eigenvalues. The paper fails to discuss the implications of this assumption. The method would fail if any component HMM mixes very slowly (i.e., its second-largest eigenvalue is close to 1), but this significant limitation is ignored.

**3. Methodological and Experimental Weaknesses:** The experiments are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness and practical utility.
*   **Practical Limitations:** A key limitation of the underlying MoM algorithm, the constraint that the total number of states `MK` must be less than the observation dimension `L`, is not discussed in the main paper. It is only revealed in Section 4.3, where a feature expansion is required to meet this condition. This is a major practical hurdle that should be discussed upfront.
*   **Weak Evaluation:** In Section 4.1, the evaluation metric is the k-means clustering error on the columns of the reconstructed transition matrix. This only shows that the columns become clusterable; it does not measure the accuracy of the recovered HMM parameters (`Ô`, `Â`), which is the ultimate goal. The experiment also uses an extremely small number of sequences (2 per cluster).
*   **Underwhelming Results:** In Section 4.2, the results show that EM achieves higher accuracy on smaller datasets, a weakness that is downplayed. The conclusion from the real-data experiment (Section 4.3) is that the spectral method "serve as a very good initialization scheme" for EM. While this is a reasonable use case, it undermines the paper's primary claim of being a standalone alternative to EM.

**Summary of arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and computationally efficient approach for a relevant problem (learning MHMMs). The core idea of identifying and then reversing the permutation ambiguity via spectral clustering is clever.
*   **Con:** The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor. The presentation is sloppy, with numerous notational inconsistencies and errors. The theoretical foundations are built on unjustified conjectures and heuristics. The experimental validation is weak, uses inappropriate metrics, and ultimately suggests the method is more suited as an initializer for EM rather than a replacement.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea is interesting, the execution is severely lacking. The combination of poor presentation, weak theoretical arguments, and an unconvincing experimental evaluation prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper feels premature and requires a major revision to address the numerous issues in its theoretical analysis and experimental design before it can be considered a solid contribution to the field.
