This paper proposes a method-of-moments based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to recognize that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, can be equivalently represented as a single, larger HMM with MK hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose using an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this larger model. The primary challenge, and the paper's main contribution, is addressing the state permutation ambiguity inherent in the spectral learning output, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. To solve this, they propose a "de-permutation" algorithm that exploits the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix to identify the block structure and thereby recover the parameters of the individual HMMs. This approach builds directly on the foundational spectral learning work for latent variable models by Anandkumar et al. and Hsu et al.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem: the efficient learning of MHMMs, which are computationally expensive to train using traditional EM. The proposed method provides a non-iterative, computationally cheaper alternative that avoids the issue of local optima inherent in EM. The central idea of learning a global HMM and then spectrally "un-mixing" the components is elegant and conceptually appealing.

2.  **Methodological Contribution:** The key contribution—the de-permutation algorithm based on the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix—is a clever technique. The insight that the eigenvectors corresponding to the K largest eigenvalues (which are close to 1) can reveal the cluster structure is powerful.

3.  **Practical Implications:** The algorithm offers a potentially significant speed-up over EM, especially for large datasets. The experiments, particularly the real-data results, suggest that even if it does not always surpass a well-initialized EM in accuracy, it serves as an excellent initialization scheme, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor presentational issues to significant gaps in theoretical rigor.

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's central theoretical claims are not adequately proven.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The error bound for the low-rank matrix reconstruction is presented as a conjecture, not a theorem. The provided "Justification" is not a formal proof; it relies on hand-wavy arguments and directly reuses a bound from a related but different model ([1]) without a proper derivation for the MHMM case. This is a major theoretical weakness for a NIPS submission.
    *   **Lemma 3:** The proof for identifying the number of clusters `K` is descriptive rather than formal. It relies on qualitative arguments about convergence rates ("The ratio of the two spectral gaps determines the spectral longevity") without precise mathematical formulation. The proposed "spectral longevity" metric itself is a heuristic whose statistical properties are not analyzed.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish and contains numerous ambiguities and inconsistencies.
    *   **Notation:** The notation is frequently non-standard or confusing. For instance, `1>M` is used for a vector of ones (presumably `1_M^T`), and the double subscripting in `x_{t,n}` and `r_{1:T_n,n}` is clumsy. The notation `O(xt) := p(xt| :, O)` is also opaque.
    *   **Typos:** There appear to be several typos, most notably in Equation (5), where `ŌP = Ō P >` likely means `Ō P^T`. Such errors erode confidence in the technical correctness of the work.
    *   **Vagueness:** Key parts of the algorithm are not specified with sufficient detail for reproducibility. In Algorithm 1, the phrase "exponentiate eigenvalues for each discrete value e in a sufficiently large range" is too vague. The text suggests a more direct low-rank reconstruction, creating a contradiction.
    *   **Missing Figures:** The text references Figures 1, 2, 3, and 4, which are absolutely critical for understanding the de-permutation method and evaluating the experiments. Their absence in the provided material makes a full assessment impossible and constitutes a major flaw in the submission.

3.  **Methodological Details and Assumptions:**
    *   The crucial assumption that each component HMM's transition matrix `Ak` has a single eigenvalue of 1 (implying ergodicity) is stated but not emphasized. The consequences of violating this assumption are not discussed.
    *   A significant limitation of the underlying method from [1, 2, 3], the requirement that the total number of hidden states `MK` be less than the observation dimension `L`, is only mentioned parenthetically in the experiments section (4.3). This is a critical constraint that should be stated upfront in the methodology section.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   In Section 4.1, the experiment is run with only 2 sequences per cluster. This is an extremely small sample size for the number of sequences and may not be representative of the algorithm's performance in more realistic scenarios.
    *   The real-data experiment (4.3) concludes that the method's primary value may be as an initializer for EM. While useful, this moderates the paper's claim of being a standalone replacement for EM.

5.  **Formatting:** The bibliography is incomplete and improperly formatted. For example, reference [3] has a duplicated title, and none of the references include standard publication details like conference/journal name, year, or pages. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** A novel, computationally efficient spectral algorithm for the important problem of learning MHMMs.
*   **Pro:** The core de-permutation idea is elegant and well-motivated.
*   **Pro:** The method avoids the local optima problem of EM and can serve as a strong initialization strategy.
*   **Con:** Major theoretical weakness: the key error bound is an unproven conjecture.
*   **Con:** The paper is rife with notational inconsistencies, typos, and vague descriptions, hindering clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Con:** The experimental design has limitations, and key figures are missing.
*   **Con:** Significant practical constraints of the method (e.g., `MK < L`) are not adequately highlighted or discussed.
*   **Con:** The reference list is incomplete and poorly formatted.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising and clever research direction, the execution is not at the level required for NIPS. The combination of a major theoretical gap (Conjecture 1), numerous clarity and notational issues, and an overall lack of polish is too significant to overlook. The work is incomplete in its current form. I would encourage the authors to formalize their theoretical claims with rigorous proofs, thoroughly revise the manuscript to improve clarity and correctness, and conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation. With substantial revision, this could become a strong paper, but it is not ready for publication at this time.
