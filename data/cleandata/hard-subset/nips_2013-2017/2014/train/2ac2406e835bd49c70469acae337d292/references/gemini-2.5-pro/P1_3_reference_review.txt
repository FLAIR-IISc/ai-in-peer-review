This paper presents a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard approach for learning MHMMs, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, can be computationally expensive on large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. This work aims to provide a computationally lighter alternative with theoretical grounding. The core idea is to recognize that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is equivalent to a single large HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose to first learn the parameters of this equivalent large HMM using existing spectral learning techniques [1, 3]. This standard approach, however, recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which scrambles the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this paper is a spectral de-permutation method to recover the block structure and thus the parameters of the individual HMMs. The method relies on analyzing the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix. In the ideal noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1, and raising the matrix to a high power reveals the block structure, allowing the columns to be clustered to find the correct permutation. For the more realistic noisy case, the authors propose using a rank-K reconstruction of the transition matrix and provide a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of components K from the data. The method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world data, showing significant speed advantages over EM and demonstrating its utility as an effective initialization strategy for EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The central idea of viewing an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is insightful. The subsequent use of the spectral properties of this structure to solve the resulting permutation problem is a novel and elegant solution to a non-trivial issue. It cleverly extends the existing spectral learning framework for HMMs to the more complex MHMM case.

2.  **Significance and Practicality:** The proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over the standard EM algorithm, as its core learning cost is independent of the number of sequences after the initial moment computation step. This makes it highly suitable for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, the real-data experiment convincingly demonstrates a highly practical application: using the spectral method as a fast and effective initializer for EM, which can help overcome EM's sensitivity to random initialization and lead to better final models. This hybrid approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The problem with EM for MHMMs is well-established, and the proposed spectral solution is built up logically, starting from the model equivalence (Lemma 1), to the ideal noiseless de-permutation case (Corollary 1), and finally to the realistic noisy case. The figures, particularly Figure 1, provide a good visual intuition for the de-permutation algorithm.

4.  **Solid Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. The synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate the algorithm's robustness to noise (4.1) and provide a clear comparison of the speed-accuracy trade-off against EM (4.2). The real data experiment on character trajectories (4.3) grounds the work in a practical application and highlights the strength of the MoM-initialized EM approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Rigor for the Noisy Case:** The theoretical justification for handling the noisy case is the weakest part of the paper.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This is a crucial assumption for the noisy algorithm to work, but it is presented without a formal proof. The justification provided is based on a hand-wavy argument using the triangle inequality and reuses a sample complexity bound from [1] for a different model. While the conjecture is plausible, a more rigorous analysis or at least a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which it is expected to hold would significantly strengthen the paper.
    *   **Estimating K (Lemma 3):** The method for estimating the number of clusters K via "spectral longevity" feels heuristic. The proof for Lemma 3 is a brief sketch and the connection between maximizing the proposed eigenvalue ratio and maximizing longevity is not fully established. The method's sensitivity to the hyperparameter `γ` is not discussed.

2.  **Lack of Algorithmic Detail:** Algorithm 1 is high-level and omits key details needed for reproducibility. For instance, it mentions exponentiating eigenvalues in a "sufficiently large range" without guidance on how to choose this range. It also says to "Cluster the columns" without specifying the algorithm (k-means is used in the experiments but should be specified in the algorithm itself) or how to handle its potential randomness.

3.  **Unstated Assumptions:** The entire spectral de-permutation approach relies on the property from Lemma 2 that the global transition matrix `Ā` has K eigenvalues of 1. This, in turn, requires that each individual transition matrix `Ak` is stochastic and has a unique stationary distribution (i.e., is irreducible and aperiodic). This is a standard assumption for HMMs but it is a critical requirement for this method to work and should be explicitly stated as a condition or limitation. The paper does not discuss how the method would be affected if one of the component HMMs were reducible.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents a novel, elegant, and computationally efficient spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of HMMs. The key contribution is a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in applying spectral methods to the MHMM structure. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating significant speedups over EM and showing its value as a powerful initialization technique.

The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical rigor for the noisy case, where key results are presented as conjectures or justified with heuristic arguments, and some missing details required for easy reproduction.

Despite these theoretical weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, its clear practical significance, and the compelling empirical results make this a strong contribution to the field. The work successfully extends powerful spectral learning techniques to a more complex and widely used model class.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem (efficiently learning MHMMs).
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution by combining spectral HMM learning with a spectral de-permutation technique.
*   Demonstrates significant computational advantages over the standard EM algorithm.
*   Provides a practically valuable method for initializing EM, which is shown to improve results on real data.
*   The paper is well-written and the core ideas are easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis for the noisy case is not rigorous (relies on an unproven conjecture and a heuristic method for model selection).
*   Some algorithmic details are missing, which could hinder reproducibility.
*   Important underlying assumptions about the component HMMs are not explicitly stated.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the method and its demonstrated practical utility, outweigh the theoretical weaknesses. I would encourage the authors to acknowledge the heuristic nature of the noisy-case analysis more explicitly in the final version and to provide the missing algorithmic details for reproducibility.
