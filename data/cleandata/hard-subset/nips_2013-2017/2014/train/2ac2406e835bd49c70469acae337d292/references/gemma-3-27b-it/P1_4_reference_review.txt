## Peer Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using a method of moments (MoM) approach. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Building on existing MoM techniques for HMMs, the authors address the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation by leveraging spectral properties of the global transition matrix to “de-permute” the parameters and recover the individual HMMs’ parameters. Furthermore, they propose a method to estimate the number of HMMs (clusters) based on spectral analysis. The paper includes theoretical justification (Lemmas and Conjecture) and empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the growing body of work utilizing MoM for latent variable model estimation, referencing key papers from Anandkumar et al. [1, 2, 3].  It’s well-situated within the context of alternatives to Expectation-Maximization (EM), particularly highlighting the computational advantages of MoM, especially for large datasets. The connection to spectral methods for HMM learning, specifically Hsu et al. [3], is also explicitly acknowledged.  The work differentiates itself by extending these techniques to the more complex MHMM setting, a challenge previously unaddressed in the MoM literature. The comparison to existing clustering techniques like EM is also relevant and provides valuable context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying MoM to MHMMs is a significant contribution, extending a promising learning paradigm to a widely used and challenging model. The spectral de-permutation technique is a clever and potentially impactful solution to a key problem in MoM estimation.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, using lemmas to demonstrate the equivalence between MHMMs and a special HMM structure and conjecture to support the spectral de-permutation method.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The authors convincingly argue for the computational advantages of MoM over EM, particularly for large datasets and many sequences, and the experiments support this claim.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, both in terms of accuracy (comparable to EM) and computational speed. The inclusion of a real-world dataset (handwriting recognition) adds practical relevance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The model definitions are clear, and the algorithm is described in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conjecture Dependence:** The core of the de-permutation algorithm relies heavily on Conjecture 1, which requires relatively strict conditions on the noise level. The sensitivity of the algorithm to noise isn't fully explored in the experiments, and it's unclear how well it would perform in more realistic, noisy scenarios.
* **Spectral Condition Justification:** While the authors provide a proof for Lemma 3 relating spectral longevity to the number of clusters, the underlying assumptions about the eigenvalue separation and the robustness of this condition to noise aren't fully discussed.
* **Limited Experiment Scope:**  The experimental evaluation, while well-executed, could be broadened. Comparing to other MHMM learning algorithms beyond EM would provide a more comprehensive benchmark. Exploring different observation models (e.g., Poisson) would also enhance the paper's robustness.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The impact of parameters like the polynomial feature transformation degree in the real-world experiment is not thoroughly analyzed. This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** Some of the proofs and derivations feel slightly hand-wavy. A more rigorous mathematical treatment, particularly for the conjecture, would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to learning MHMMs, addressing a significant challenge in the field.
* The computational advantages of the MoM-based method are compelling, especially for large-scale applications.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a conjecture, with limited exploration of its robustness, is a concern.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and explore a wider range of scenarios.
* The mathematical rigor could be improved in certain sections.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of latent variable model learning. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically motivated, and empirically validated. While the reliance on a conjecture and limited experimental scope are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper. I would recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Noise Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct experiments to specifically assess the algorithm’s performance under varying levels of noise.
* **Expanded Experimentation:** Include comparisons to other MHMM learning algorithms (beyond EM) and explore different observation models.
* **Strengthen Conjecture Justification:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying Conjecture 1 and potentially explore ways to relax these assumptions.
* **Refine Mathematical Rigor:** Review the proofs and derivations for potential improvements in clarity and rigor.
* **Address Feature Transformation:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of polynomial degree in the real-world experiment.
