### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard approach for this problem, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. The authors' approach circumvents these issues by leveraging spectral learning techniques.

The key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is equivalent to a single large HMM with KM states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. This allows the authors to apply existing spectral algorithms for HMMs, such as those by Anandkumar et al. and Hsu et al., to learn the parameters of this equivalent large HMM. However, a critical challenge arises: spectral methods recover parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states. In the MHMM context, this permutation mixes states from different component HMMs, making it impossible to disentangle the parameters of the individual models.

The main contribution of this work is a procedure to "de-permute" the estimated parameters. The authors exploit the spectral properties of the ideal block-diagonal transition matrix. They propose that by computing a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix using its top K eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors, one can reveal the underlying cluster structure. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix yields the permutation required to group the states correctly and recover the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also proposes a heuristic method based on eigenvalue decay rates ("spectral longevity") to estimate the number of components K from data. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than EM and provides comparable clustering accuracy, while also serving as an effective initialization strategy for EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of efficient MHMM learning. The core idea of framing the MHMM as a large HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is elegant. The subsequent spectral de-permutation strategy is a novel and clever solution to the fundamental state-ambiguity problem that arises in this context. This work successfully extends the powerful framework of spectral learning to a more complex, hierarchical latent variable model, which is a significant contribution.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and generally easy to follow. The central argument is built step-by-step, starting with the equivalence lemma (Lemma 1), identifying the permutation problem, and then presenting the spectral solution. Figure 1 provides an excellent visual illustration of the core problem and the intuition behind the proposed solution.
3.  **Potential Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers a compelling alternative to EM, providing global convergence guarantees (inherent to MoM) and a significant computational advantage, as confirmed by the experiments. The finding that it can serve as a high-quality initializer for EM is also of great practical value, potentially combining the speed of MoM with the high accuracy of a local search method like EM.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Feedback

While the core idea is strong, the paper could be improved in terms of theoretical rigor and experimental validation.

1.  **Gaps in Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical underpinnings for the noisy case, which is the most relevant practical scenario, rely on conjectures that are not fully substantiated.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This conjecture provides a finite-sample error bound for the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix, which is central to the de-permutation algorithm. However, the justification provided is quite brief and appears to directly adapt a result from Anandkumar et al. [1] without a formal proof of its applicability to this specific estimation procedure (i.e., the low-rank reconstruction of a transition matrix learned via MoM). A more rigorous analysis, perhaps leveraging matrix perturbation theory (e.g., Davis-Kahan theorems), would be needed to turn this conjecture into a proven result and significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical claims.
    *   **Lemma 3 and Estimating K:** The method for estimating the number of clusters K is presented as a key feature, but its justification is heuristic. The "spectral longevity" metric (Eq. 7) is novel but also unnecessarily complex and not well-motivated compared to simply looking for a spectral gap or "elbow" in the sorted eigenvalues. The condition provided in Lemma 3 involving the ratio of eigenvalue gaps is interesting but not sufficiently justified. Why is this specific ratio the determining factor for the longevity criterion? This section would be much stronger with either a simpler, more standard heuristic (e.g., finding the largest ratio `|λ̃K′| / |λ̃K′+1|`) or a more thorough justification for the proposed metric.

2.  **Missing Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the algorithm's speed and accuracy for a *known* K, but a crucial piece of the proposed contribution is not validated: the ability to *estimate* K. The paper proposes a novel method for this in Section 3.2.2, but no experiment shows how well it performs in practice. I strongly recommend adding an experiment that evaluates the performance of the K-estimation procedure under varying conditions (e.g., different noise levels or degrees of separation between clusters).

3.  **Reproducibility and Clarity of Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is presented at a high level, which may hinder reproducibility. For instance, it states to "Cluster the columns of Ar...to find a depermutation mapping," but the specific clustering algorithm (presumably K-means, as used in the experiments) is not mentioned. The step "Exponentiate eigenvalues...Identify K̂ as the eigenvalue with largest longevity" is also vague. Clarifying these details would be very helpful for readers wishing to implement the method.

4.  **Discussion of Limitations:** The MoM approach used has a prerequisite that the number of observation dimensions L must be greater than the total number of hidden states MK. This is a significant practical limitation. While the authors cleverly use a polynomial feature expansion to overcome this on the real dataset, this workaround and its implications deserve a more detailed discussion. How does the choice of feature map affect performance? What are the alternatives when L is inherently small?

### Final Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel, insightful, and potentially impactful contribution. It extends the reach of spectral learning methods to MHMMs in a non-trivial way. The primary weaknesses are the lack of rigor in some theoretical claims and the absence of experimental validation for the proposed method of estimating the number of model components. Despite these points, the core ideas are compelling and the initial results are promising. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by adding an experimental evaluation of their K-estimation procedure and by either strengthening their theoretical arguments or being more explicit about their heuristic nature.
