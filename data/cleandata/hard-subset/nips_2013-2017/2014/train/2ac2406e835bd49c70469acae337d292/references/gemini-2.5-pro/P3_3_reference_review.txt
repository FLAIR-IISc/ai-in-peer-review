**Summary**

This paper presents a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single large HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix of size MK x MK. The authors then leverage existing spectral algorithms for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the parameters of this large HMM. The primary technical challenge, and the paper's main contribution, is addressing the state permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral methods. This permutation mixes the parameters of the individual HMMs, making them unrecoverable. The proposed solution is to identify and "de-permute" the estimated global transition matrix by exploiting its spectral properties. Specifically, they propose clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix to recover the block structure. The paper also provides a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components, K, from the eigenvalue spectrum. Experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate the algorithm's computational advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: efficiently learning MHMMs, which are widely used for sequence clustering. The standard EM approach can be slow and prone to local optima. Providing a fast, non-iterative, and globally consistent alternative is a significant contribution. The core insight of treating the MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM and then developing a principled way to recover the blocks post-estimation is elegant and novel. This work is a non-trivial extension of the well-established spectral learning framework for HMMs to a more complex hierarchical model.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The formulation of an MHMM as a special HMM (Lemma 1) is correct and provides a solid foundation for the approach. The identification of the permutation problem as the key bottleneck is accurate. The proposed de-permutation strategy, based on the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix, is clever. In the noiseless case, the idea of clustering columns of the exponentiated transition matrix is intuitive and well-explained. The extension to the noisy case via a low-rank reconstruction based on the top K eigenvalues is a reasonable and practical approach to handle estimation error.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It effectively builds the argument from the model definition, through the block-diagonal insight, to the noiseless de-permutation strategy, and finally to the more realistic noisy case. The figures, particularly Figure 1, are helpful in illustrating the core idea behind the de-permutation algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Theoretical Gaps:** The theoretical support for the noisy case, which is the most critical part of the contribution, is weak.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The paper's central claim about the accuracy of the low-rank reconstruction `Ar` is presented as a conjecture. The justification relies on hand-waving arguments and reuses a bound from [1] for a different model without a formal proof of applicability. For a top-tier conference like NIPS, leaving a cornerstone of the method's theoretical justification as a conjecture is a significant weakness. A more rigorous analysis, even under simplifying assumptions, would be necessary to make the claims fully convincing.
    *   **Lemma 3 and Estimating K:** The method for estimating K via "spectral longevity" (`Lλ̃K'`) is heuristic. The proof sketch connects it to the ratio of spectral gaps, which is standard intuition, but the definition of `Lλ̃K'` itself seems somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive is this measure to the choice of `γ`? What are its statistical properties? The paper lacks a formal analysis of its robustness or consistency.
    *   **Assumptions:** The method's success hinges on a critical spectral gap condition: `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|`. The paper does not discuss how restrictive this condition is, when it might be violated in practice (e.g., when one HMM component is much less persistent than another), or how the algorithm would fail if the condition is not met.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While promising, the experiments could be more thorough.
    *   **Comparison with EM:** The results in Figure 4 show that EM achieves higher accuracy, especially in the low-data regime. The claim that the spectral method is "comparable in accuracy" is an overstatement; "competitive in some regimes" would be more accurate. The main advantage shown is speed, which is indeed significant.
    *   **Real-world Data:** The experiment on the UCI dataset is a good inclusion. However, the conclusion that the primary benefit is as an *initializer* for EM somewhat tempers the claims of it being a standalone replacement. Furthermore, the need for a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint is an important practical limitation of this class of spectral methods that should be discussed more explicitly. This pre-processing step is not always trivial and can impact results.
    *   **Robustness:** The experiments do not sufficiently test the robustness of the K-estimation procedure or the de-permutation clustering step. How does the column clustering perform as the spectral gap closes? A more detailed ablation study would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Clarity of Details:**
    *   The de-permutation step is described as "Cluster the columns of Ar". This is a crucial step, but the paper does not specify the clustering algorithm used (e.g., K-means, spectral clustering) or discuss potential challenges. Clustering in a high-dimensional (MK) space can be difficult, and the success of the entire method depends on it.
    *   The notation in Section 3.2.2 is quite dense. A small table summarizing the different eigenvalues (`λGk`, `λLj,k`, etc.) could improve readability.

**Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses an important problem with a novel and elegant spectral approach.
*   The connection between MHMMs and block-diagonal HMMs is a powerful insight.
*   The de-permutation strategy is the key creative contribution.
*   The method is computationally much faster than EM, offering a significant practical advantage.

**Cons:**
*   Major theoretical claims (Conjecture 1) are not proven, which undermines the paper's technical depth.
*   The method for estimating the number of components (K) is heuristic and lacks rigorous analysis.
*   The experimental results, while positive on speed, show weaknesses in accuracy compared to EM and rely on data pre-processing in the real-world example.
*   Crucial assumptions (e.g., spectral gap) and implementation details (e.g., clustering algorithm) are not discussed in sufficient depth.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a very interesting and promising direction for learning MHMMs. The core idea is strong, and the potential impact is high. However, the lack of theoretical rigor for the noisy case (Conjecture 1) is a serious flaw for a NIPS paper. The claims of the paper currently outstrip the evidence provided. While the idea is worthy of publication, it feels premature in its current form. I would recommend rejection, but I would strongly encourage the authors to formalize their theoretical claims, particularly Conjecture 1, and conduct a more thorough experimental analysis of the method's robustness before resubmission. The work has the potential to be an excellent contribution once these issues are addressed.
