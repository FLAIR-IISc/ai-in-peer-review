

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

## Summary

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a key computational challenge in MHMM learning. The authors make the insightful observation that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs. The main contribution is a method to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters by exploiting the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, including lemmas on the spectral properties of the global transition matrix and a spectral longevity measure to estimate the number of clusters K. They also present experimental results on synthetic and real data demonstrating the computational advantages of their method over EM.

This work builds on the growing literature of spectral learning for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012; Hsu et al., JCSS 2012), extending it to the more complex MHMM setting. While spectral learning for HMMs has been well-studied, applying it to MHMMs presents unique challenges due to the permutation ambiguity that arises when recovering individual HMM parameters from the global model.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The key insight that an MHMM can be viewed as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix (Lemma 1) is well-established and forms the basis for the entire approach. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the spectral properties of the global transition matrix (Lemma 2, Corollary 1), which is crucial for addressing the permutation ambiguity problem.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including synthetic data experiments showing the algorithm's robustness to varying sequence lengths (Section 4.1), a comparison of accuracy and speed against EM (Section 4.2), and a real-world application on handwritten character trajectories (Section 4.3). The results consistently show that the proposed method achieves comparable accuracy to EM while being significantly faster, and can serve as an effective initialization for EM.

The main technical weakness is the presentation of Conjecture 1, which provides a sample complexity bound for the spectral learning approach. While the justification is reasonable, it would have been stronger with a more rigorous proof or a clearer explanation of why the bound from [1] applies to the MHMM case. The authors acknowledge this limitation by stating "further analysis of the spectral learning algorithm is out of the scope of this paper."

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key concepts are well-explained with helpful figures (Figure 1 and 2 illustrate the spectral properties and depermutation process particularly well). The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented with inputs, outputs, and a step-by-step description.

One minor weakness is that the transition from the noiseless case to the noisy case (Section 3.2.2) could be smoother. The authors introduce the spectral longevity measure (Definition 3) without sufficient intuition about why it effectively estimates K. A brief explanation of the intuition behind the measure would have improved clarity.

The real-world experiment (Section 4.3) uses a cubic polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the condition MK < L required for MoM learning. While this is a reasonable workaround, the paper could have more explicitly discussed this as a limitation of the method rather than simply presenting it as a technical necessity.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the spectral learning literature by extending these techniques to MHMMs. The key novelty lies in recognizing that the MHMM can be viewed as a block-diagonal HMM and then developing a method to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters using spectral properties. This approach to handling the permutation ambiguity is novel for MHMMs.

The spectral longevity measure (Definition 3) is a particularly elegant contribution that provides a principled way to estimate the number of clusters K without requiring prior knowledge. While the general approach of using spectral methods for latent variable models is not new, the specific application to MHMMs with the permutation problem addressed in this paper represents a meaningful advancement.

The paper properly cites the relevant prior work on spectral learning for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., Hsu et al.) and MHMMs (Smyth), but it could have more explicitly contrasted their approach with recent tensor-based methods for learning mixture models (e.g., Anandkumar et al.'s tensor decomposition approaches).

## Significance

The paper addresses an important practical problem: the computational intractability of EM for large-scale MHMM learning. The authors successfully demonstrate that their spectral method provides a computationally efficient alternative that achieves comparable accuracy to EM. This is significant because it enables MHMM learning on larger datasets where EM would be prohibitively expensive.

The finding that the spectral method can serve as an effective initialization for EM (Section 4.3) has practical implications for the broader community, as it provides a way to improve the quality of EM solutions without significantly increasing computational cost. This could be particularly valuable for practitioners dealing with large sequence datasets.

The significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the spectral method still requires matrix operations of size MK × MK, which could become computationally challenging as the number of clusters K or hidden states M increases. However, for moderate values of K and M (which are common in practice), the method offers a compelling advantage.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the spectral learning literature. The authors have identified a meaningful problem (computational challenges in MHMM learning), provided a novel solution based on insightful theoretical observations, and validated their approach with thorough experiments.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-motivated insights
- Novel approach to handling permutation ambiguity in MHMM learning
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing practical advantages
- Good comparison with EM and demonstration of utility as initialization
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Weaknesses:**
- The justification for Conjecture 1 could be stronger
- The spectral longevity measure could be better motivated
- The polynomial feature transformation in the real-world experiment is presented as a technical necessity rather than a limitation
- Could better contrast with recent tensor-based approaches for mixture models

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, addresses an important practical problem, and provides a significant advancement over existing methods for MHMM learning. The computational advantages demonstrated in the experiments are substantial and could have meaningful impact for practitioners. While there are some minor weaknesses in the theoretical justification and presentation, they do not undermine the overall contribution of the paper.

The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models, and its findings are likely to be of interest to a broad audience at NeurIPS. The experimental results are convincing, and the method is well-motivated and well-executed.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings and would be of interest to many researchers working on sequence modeling, latent variable models, and spectral learning methods.
