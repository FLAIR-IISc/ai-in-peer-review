### Summary

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a KM x KM block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an established spectral learning algorithm for HMMs to this larger model. This standard procedure recovers the model parameters up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is a method to undo this permutation. This is achieved by analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. Specifically, they propose clustering the columns of the exponentiated (or low-rank reconstructed) transition matrix to identify the blocks corresponding to individual HMMs. The paper also presents a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components, K, based on the spectrum of this matrix. Experimental results on synthetic and one real dataset are provided to compare the proposed method with the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The conceptual framing of an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is clean and provides a clear path for applying existing MoM techniques. The subsequent identification of the core problem as one of "de-permuting" the learned parameters is an interesting and novel contribution.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for this work is to provide a faster alternative to the iterative EM algorithm. The proposed method is non-iterative (post moment computation) and the experiments effectively demonstrate a significant speed advantage over EM, which is a valuable contribution for large-scale sequence clustering tasks.

3.  **Model Selection Component:** The paper attempts to address the important problem of model selection (i.e., choosing K), which is often a difficult issue for mixture models. The proposed "spectral longevity" criterion, while heuristic, is an interesting idea for automatically determining the number of components from data.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The analysis of the algorithm's behavior in the presence of noise is not rigorous. The core result that guarantees the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix `Ar` is close to the true noiseless matrix `Ar` is presented as **Conjecture 1**. The error bound is borrowed from a prior work on a different model without a formal proof of its applicability here. This is a significant theoretical gap. For a paper introducing a new spectral method, a formal analysis of its statistical properties under noise is expected. Without it, the method's reliability is questionable.

2.  **Strong and Unverified Assumptions:** The method for determining K (Lemma 3) and for de-permutation both rely on a strong spectral gap condition: `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|`. This assumes that the eigenvalues corresponding to the stationary distributions of the K components are all larger in magnitude than any of the other non-unity eigenvalues from any component. It is not clear how often this condition holds in practice, especially for HMMs with slow mixing rates. The paper provides no discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the violation of this assumption.

3.  **Unconvincing Empirical Validation:** The experimental results do not sufficiently support the claim that the proposed method is a strong standalone alternative to EM in terms of accuracy.
    *   On the synthetic data (Figure 4), the spectral method is shown to be less accurate than EM, particularly in low-data regimes. While it eventually catches up, it does not demonstrate superior performance.
    *   The real-data experiment (Table 1) is the most concerning. The proposed spectral method, on its own, is outperformed by the standard baseline (EM with 5 random restarts) in 6 out of the 10 character-pair classification tasks. The paper concludes that it serves as a "very good initialization scheme," which is a much weaker claim than the one implied by the paper's positioning as a viable alternative learning algorithm. While being a good initializer is useful, it significantly reduces the overall significance of the contribution.

4.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The validation is limited. The synthetic data is generated from a simple Gaussian model that perfectly matches the model assumptions. The real-data evaluation is performed on a single dataset (handwritten characters), which is pre-processed with a polynomial feature expansion to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint, a practical limitation of the method that is not heavily discussed. The performance of the method on other types of sequence data (e.g., with discrete observations, or from domains like bioinformatics) is not explored.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and computationally efficient algorithm for learning MHMMs. The core idea of using spectral properties to resolve the permutation ambiguity from a global HMM estimation is clever. However, the work in its current form is undermined by a lack of theoretical rigor and unconvincing empirical results. The reliance on a conjecture instead of a proven theorem for the noise analysis is a major weakness. Furthermore, the experiments, particularly on real data, fail to demonstrate that the method is competitive with, let alone superior to, the standard EM algorithm in terms of accuracy. The results suggest its primary utility may be as a fast initializer for EM, which, while valuable, is a less impactful contribution than a standalone learning algorithm.

Given the preference for established and rigorously validated methods, the weaknesses in the theoretical analysis and the empirical evidence make it difficult to endorse this paper for publication at NIPS. The ideas are promising, but the work is not yet a complete and convincing contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of the MHMM learning problem.
*   Demonstrably large computational speed-up over EM.
*   Includes a method for model selection (estimating K).

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Key theoretical result (Conjecture 1) is an unproven conjecture.
*   The method relies on strong, unverified spectral gap assumptions.
*   Experimental results on real data show the method is generally less accurate than a standard EM baseline.
*   The contribution is effectively reduced to a fast initialization scheme, which lowers its significance.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

For the authors to improve the paper, I would strongly recommend:
1.  Providing a formal proof for the error bounds stated in Conjecture 1.
2.  Conducting an analysis of the method's robustness when the spectral gap assumptions are weakened or violated.
3.  Expanding the experimental section to include more diverse and challenging datasets, and comparing against more sophisticated EM initialization schemes beyond random starts.
