## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel method for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. By applying a spectral learning algorithm for HMMs, the authors address the computational burden of traditional EM approaches, particularly for large datasets. A key contribution lies in developing a de-permutation strategy to recover the individual HMM parameters obscured by the permutation ambiguity inherent in the MoM approach. The paper also proposes conditions under which the number of HMMs can be recovered spectrally.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the growing body of research utilizing MoM for latent variable model learning (Anandkumar et al., COLT 2014, Anandkumar et al., JMLR 2016).  It specifically leverages spectral methods for HMM learning (Hsu et al., JCSS 2009) within the MoM framework. The authors correctly position their contribution as extending these techniques to the more complex MHMM setting, which presents unique challenges due to the mixture component and the resulting permutation ambiguity.  Related work in MHMM learning (Smyth, NIPS 1997) primarily focuses on EM-based approaches, highlighting the computational advantage of the proposed method.  The authors also acknowledge connections to spectral decomposition methods used in other machine learning contexts (Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2012).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying spectral learning and MoM to MHMMs is a novel and promising direction. Addressing the permutation ambiguity is a significant technical contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The proposed algorithm promises substantial computational savings compared to EM, especially for large datasets, as the runtime is independent of the number of sequences. This is a critical advantage for real-world applications.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The authors provide theoretical arguments (Lemmas 1 & 2, Conjecture 1) to support their approach, establishing the connection between the MHMM structure and spectral properties. While some aspects are presented as conjectures, they provide a clear roadmap for future theoretical development.
* **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness, including comparisons with EM. The results on both synthetic and real-world data (handwritten character trajectories) are encouraging.  The use of the UCI dataset is a strong point.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the key concepts. The use of matrix notation is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conjectural Nature:**  A substantial portion of the theoretical justification relies on Conjecture 1.  While the reasoning is plausible, rigorous proof is needed to fully establish the robustness of the method.  The conditions for successful de-permutation (specifically, the eigenvalue gap) appear quite restrictive and may not hold in many practical scenarios.
* **Sensitivity to Noise:** The performance of the spectral de-permutation step is explicitly stated to be sensitive to noise. The paper acknowledges this but lacks a detailed analysis of how different levels of noise impact the accuracy of the algorithm.  A more comprehensive sensitivity analysis is crucial.
* **Limited Real-World Data Experiments:** While the UCI dataset experiment is a positive step, the feature transformation applied (cubic polynomial) seems somewhat ad-hoc and could potentially mask the true benefits of the algorithm. Exploring the performance on more diverse real-world datasets without significant pre-processing would be valuable.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper does not provide detailed guidance on parameter tuning for the spectral learning or de-permutation steps.  The choice of the exponentiation range in the spectral longevity calculation (Definition 3) is critical, and a more systematic exploration of its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparisons to EM are useful, but the paper could benefit from a comparison to more recent advancements in MHMM learning, beyond standard EM.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in MHMM learning – computational scalability.
* The proposed approach offers a novel and theoretically grounded alternative to EM.
* The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on both synthetic and real-world data.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on conjectures and the sensitivity to noise raise concerns about the robustness of the method.
* The lack of a comprehensive sensitivity analysis and more diverse real-world experiments limits the confidence in the generalizability of the results.
*  The limited discussion of parameter tuning makes it difficult to assess the practicality of the algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the reliance on conjectures and the sensitivity to noise are legitimate concerns, the authors present a compelling case for their approach. The computational benefits and the clear theoretical justification, combined with the promising experimental results, make this paper worthy of acceptance.  However, I recommend that the authors address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by providing more rigorous theoretical analysis, a more detailed sensitivity analysis, and exploring the algorithm's performance on a broader range of real-world datasets.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**  The revisions should focus on strengthening the theoretical justification, performing a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis, and expanding the experimental evaluation.
