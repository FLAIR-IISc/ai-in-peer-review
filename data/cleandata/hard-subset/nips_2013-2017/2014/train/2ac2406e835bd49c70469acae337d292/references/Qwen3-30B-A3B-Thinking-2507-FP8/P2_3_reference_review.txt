

# Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a computational bottleneck in the standard EM approach. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Building on spectral learning techniques for standard HMMs, the authors develop an algorithm to learn the global parameters and then recover the individual HMM parameters by addressing a permutation ambiguity using spectral properties of the transition matrix. This work extends the growing body of spectral learning methods for latent variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2009) to the more complex MHMM setting.

## Quality

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach with well-justified lemmas and a clear algorithmic procedure. The mathematical foundation is solid, with Lemma 1 establishing the equivalence between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices, and Lemma 2 characterizing the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The authors demonstrate that the permutation ambiguity can be resolved using spectral properties, and they provide a method to estimate the number of clusters K.

However, the theoretical guarantees are somewhat limited. The authors present Conjecture 1 regarding the error bound for the low-rank reconstruction without formal proof, which is a significant gap in the theoretical development. While the authors justify this by citing related work, a formal proof would strengthen the paper substantially. The experimental section is reasonable but lacks thorough comparison with alternative non-EM approaches for MHMM learning. The real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories is limited (only K=2 clusters tested) and relies on a polynomial feature transformation to meet the MK < L constraint, which might affect the generalizability of the results.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined throughout. The authors build logically from fundamental HMM definitions to the MHMM representation, then to the spectral learning approach. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented concisely and clearly. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate key concepts like the spectral properties and algorithm performance. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with HMMs and spectral learning methods.

## Originality

The key insight that MHMMs can be viewed as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is novel and well-motivated. The authors' approach to resolving the permutation ambiguity using spectral properties is a significant contribution that distinguishes this work from previous MoM approaches for latent variable models. However, the paper could better contextualize this contribution against related work. While it cites some prior MHMM work (e.g., Smyth, 1997), it doesn't sufficiently contrast with other non-EM approaches for MHMM learning, such as those based on tensor decompositions (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2014). The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this approach compares to these alternatives.

## Significance

The problem of learning MHMMs is important for sequence clustering applications, and the computational advantage over EM is significant for large datasets. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves comparable accuracy to EM with substantially reduced computational requirements, as shown in Figure 4. The finding that the spectral method can serve as an effective initialization for EM (Table 1) is a particularly valuable practical contribution. However, the paper doesn't quantify the speedup in absolute terms (e.g., "our method is 10x faster than EM on this dataset"), which would strengthen the significance claim. The real-world experiment, while limited in scope, provides evidence of practical utility.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the spectral learning of latent variable models. The core idea of representing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is novel and well-executed. The method offers a computationally attractive alternative to EM for MHMM learning, with experimental results supporting its effectiveness.

The main weaknesses are the limited theoretical guarantees (relying on conjectures rather than proofs) and the somewhat limited experimental comparison with alternative methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a formal proof of the error bound in Conjecture 1 and a more comprehensive comparison with existing non-EM approaches for MHMM learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-executed approach. The theoretical foundation is solid, the writing is excellent, and the experimental results are promising. The main issue to address is strengthening the theoretical claims and providing a more thorough comparison with related work.

**Pro:** Well-motivated problem, novel representation of MHMM as HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix, clear algorithmic procedure, strong theoretical foundation with sound lemmas, excellent writing and organization.

**Con:** Limited theoretical guarantees (relying on conjectures), insufficient comparison with alternative non-EM approaches for MHMM learning, real-world experiment limited in scope.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the spectral learning literature and would be of interest to the NIPS community. The minor issues identified above can be addressed in the camera-ready version.
