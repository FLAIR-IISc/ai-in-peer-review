### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard algorithm for this task is Expectation-Maximization (EM), which can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. The authors' approach is based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single, large HMM with a total of MK hidden states. This larger HMM possesses a special block-diagonal transition matrix.

The proposed algorithm proceeds in two main stages. First, it applies an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) to the data to estimate the parameters of the equivalent large HMM. However, this estimation is only unique up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the K individual HMM components. The main contribution of the paper is the second stage: a "de-permutation" algorithm to recover the individual component HMMs. This is achieved by exploiting the spectral properties of the estimated block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors argue that in the noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1, and its powers reveal the block structure. By performing a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvectors of the (noisy) estimated matrix and clustering the columns of the resulting matrix, they can recover the permutation and separate the parameters. The paper also proposes a heuristic method based on eigenvalue "longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components, K. Experiments on synthetic and real data suggest the method is much faster than EM and can achieve comparable accuracy, or serve as a strong initialization for EM.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The idea of treating an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is elegant. While reformulating hierarchical models is a known technique, its application here to enable spectral learning for MHMMs is novel. The subsequent de-permutation algorithm, which uses spectral clustering on the estimated transition matrix to resolve the inherent ambiguity of the initial learning step, is a clever and well-motivated solution to a non-trivial problem.

2.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** Learning MHMMs is an important problem with applications in sequence clustering. The EM algorithm's computational cost (scaling with the number of sequences, sequence lengths, and iterations) is a major practical bottleneck. This paper offers a non-iterative, direct estimation procedure whose core learning complexity is independent of the dataset size after the initial moment computation step. This could represent a significant practical advance for applying MHMMs to massive datasets.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are communicated effectively. The equivalence lemma (Lemma 1) is clearly stated and proven, and the problem of permutation ambiguity is well-motivated. The figures, particularly Figure 1, provide a helpful visual intuition for the de-permutation algorithm.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical underpinnings of the de-permutation method in the presence of noise are weak and rely on conjecture rather than formal proof.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This is a central claim that the low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix (`Ar`) is close to the true low-rank matrix (`Ar`). The justification provided is hand-wavy and reuses a bound from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for a different model without a formal proof of its applicability here. For a NIPS paper, a more rigorous analysis using matrix perturbation theory (e.g., the Davis-Kahan theorem) would be expected to bound the error in the estimated eigenspace, which is crucial for the subsequent clustering step to succeed. The statement that further analysis is "out of the scope of this paper" is a significant weakness.
    *   **Estimation of K (Lemma 3):** The proposed "spectral longevity" metric for estimating the number of components K feels ad-hoc. While the intuition of using the spectral gap is sound, the proposed formula is novel but not well-justified against simpler, more standard methods like finding the largest eigengap ("elbow method"). The robustness of this metric to its parameter `gamma` and to noise levels is not analyzed.

2.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments provide some evidence for the method's utility but are not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing.
    *   The synthetic experiments use a small number of sequences (e.g., N=1-100 per cluster in 4.2). While the speedup is clear, the accuracy comparison shows EM is superior for smaller datasets. It would be valuable to see if the spectral method's accuracy catches up or surpasses EM as N grows significantly larger, which is where its computational advantage would be most relevant.
    *   The real-data experiment on the character trajectory dataset is somewhat underwhelming. The proposed method is often outperformed by randomly initialized EM. While its utility as an initializer for EM is a valid contribution, it weakens the claim of it being a strong standalone alternative.
    *   The paper notes that a feature transformation was required to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint of the underlying spectral method. This is a critical practical limitation that deserves more prominent discussion. It implies the method may not be applicable "out-of-the-box" to datasets with low-dimensional observations and a moderate number of states.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Details:**
    *   The de-permutation step relies on "clustering the columns" of the reconstructed matrix `Ar`. The specific algorithm (k-means is used in experiments) and its theoretical guarantees for this task are not discussed. Why should the columns of this matrix be well-clusterable by k-means?
    *   The assumptions required for the underlying spectral HMM algorithm (e.g., on parameter non-degeneracy, singular values of moment matrices) are not explicitly stated or discussed in the context of the MHMM.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Strengthen Theory:** The top priority for improving this work is to formalize its theoretical claims. Replacing Conjecture 1 with a proven theorem that bounds the subspace distance between the true and estimated top-K eigenspaces would make the paper significantly stronger. This would provide a solid foundation for the claim that clustering the columns of `Ar` can correctly identify the block structure.
*   **Improve K Estimation:** For estimating K, either provide a stronger theoretical motivation for the "longevity" criterion or compare it empirically against standard baselines (e.g., eigengap heuristic, Bayesian Information Criterion) to demonstrate its superiority.
*   **Expand Experiments:**
    *   On synthetic data, add a direct evaluation metric for the de-permutation step, such as the permutation recovery error (e.g., normalized Hamming distance).
    *   Scale the experiments to much larger N to better showcase the method's practical advantage and investigate asymptotic accuracy relative to EM.
    *   Test on additional, more complex real-world sequence clustering datasets to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
*   **Discuss Limitations:** Be more upfront about the `MK < L` constraint. Discuss its practical implications and potential workarounds (like the feature expansion used in the paper) in the main body of the text, not just in the experimental section. This would provide a more balanced view of the method's applicability.
*   **Refine Algorithm Description:** Explicitly state the clustering algorithm used in Algorithm 1 and briefly justify its choice.

### **Decision**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to a significant problem. The central idea is clever and elegant. However, the work is let down by weak theoretical guarantees for its core claims and a somewhat limited experimental evaluation. The reliance on an unproven conjecture for the main de-permutation step is a major drawback for a top-tier conference like NIPS. While the empirical results show promise, particularly in terms of speed, they are not strong enough to overcome the theoretical weaknesses.

Therefore, I lean towards **rejecting** the paper in its current form. However, the core idea is strong, and I believe that a revised version with rigorous theoretical analysis and a more comprehensive experimental section would be a strong candidate for acceptance at a future conference.
