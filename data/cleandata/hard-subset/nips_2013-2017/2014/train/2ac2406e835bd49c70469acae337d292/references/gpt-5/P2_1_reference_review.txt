Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral approach to learn Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M latent states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first estimate the global HMM parameters via an existing spectral HMM learner (e.g., Anandkumar/Hsu/Kakade), which returns parameters up to a permutation of the MK states. They then propose a “de-permutation” step that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix Ā: in the noiseless setting, Ā has K eigenvalues equal to 1 (one per block), and powers of Ā converge to a matrix whose columns fall into K clusters (one per mixture component). In the noisy setting, they propose using a rank-K reconstruction from the top-K eigenvalues and clustering columns to recover the block structure, along with a heuristic “spectral longevity” metric to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data show speed advantages and reasonable clustering accuracy relative to EM; a UCI handwritten trajectory task suggests the spectral estimate is a good initializer for EM.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., Hsu et al.) and classical EM for MHMMs. The reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is straightforward. The main novelty is the de-permutation step via spectral properties of the transition matrix and a heuristic for estimating K. Prior spectral work on mixtures and HMMs is cited; however, related lines such as block-structure recovery in Markov chains, spectral clustering of Markov transition matrices, and alternative MHMM learning methods (e.g., Bayesian nonparametrics, variational approaches) are not discussed in depth.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The equivalence of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM and the spectral properties in the noiseless case are sound. The key step in the noisy case rests on Conjecture 1 and a heuristic longevity metric (Lemma 3 relies on strong separation conditions). The finite-sample behavior and identifiability conditions are not rigorously established. The reliance on MK < L (and full-rank Ō) can be restrictive, especially for continuous emissions, and is only addressed via feature lifting in the real-data experiment.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments indicate the de-permutation recovers blocks with moderate data and that the method is faster than EM. However, the evaluation lacks ablations (e.g., K-estimation accuracy across settings, sensitivity to eigenvalue gaps/noise), comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., EM with good initialization, variational/Bayesian MHMMs), and more varied real-world datasets. The real-data experiment requires ad hoc feature expansion to satisfy identifiability, which may distort the distribution.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation details are missing: which specific MoM algorithm is used for HMMs with continuous emissions, how moments are computed/whitened, computational complexity and memory costs for moment estimation, how the e-range and γ are chosen for longevity, and how M or MK are selected in practice.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written at a high level. The reduction and noiseless spectral properties are well explained. The noisy-case analysis mixes conjecture and heuristic criteria without a precise noise model or rigorous perturbation analysis. The algorithm box is helpful but underspecified in several practical choices (e.g., clustering method and stability, selection of MK, handling equal/unknown M across components). Some notational switches and claims (e.g., “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation”) could be more carefully stated.

Originality
Framing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is known; using spectral de-permutation by clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction of Ā is a simple and interesting idea. The “spectral longevity” heuristic for estimating K is novel to my knowledge but ad hoc. The overall contribution is a pragmatic combination of known spectral HMM estimation with a block-structure recovery step; theoretical novelty is limited by the lack of guarantees.

Significance
The problem is relevant: MHMMs are widely used and EM can be expensive. A fast spectral initializer or estimator could be impactful. However, the method’s applicability seems constrained by identifiability requirements (MK < L, full-rank emissions, equal M across components) and by the absence of theoretical guarantees under noise. The empirical section suggests speed benefits and utility as an EM initializer, but the evidence is not yet strong enough to demonstrate state-of-the-art performance or robust applicability.

Pros
- Clear, intuitive reduction to block-diagonal HMM and use of spectral properties.
- Potentially substantial speed gains versus EM; practical value as an EM initializer.
- Simple column-clustering de-permutation that works well in synthetic demos.
- Attempt to infer K from eigenvalue behavior.

Cons
- Key consistency/perturbation claims are conjectural; no rigorous finite-sample guarantees for de-permutation or K estimation.
- Restrictive assumptions: MK < L, equal M across components, each Ak having a single eigenvalue 1; unclear robustness when violated.
- Practical details underspecified; sensitivity to hyperparameters and clustering not analyzed.
- Limited empirical validation: few baselines, narrow datasets, no systematic evaluation of K estimation accuracy, and reliance on feature lifting to meet identifiability.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a precise noise model and a perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) to justify recovery of the K-dimensional 1-eigenspace and column clustering, with sample complexity in terms of eigen-gaps and data size.
- Characterize when K can be identified: conditions on spectral gaps between global and local eigenvalues, and robustness to near-degenerate blocks.
- Clarify how moments are computed for continuous emissions and what identifiability assumptions are needed on Ō; provide complexity and memory costs for moment estimation.
- Discuss selection of M and MK and handling unequal M across components; can the method be extended to unknown/unequal M?
- Expand experiments: report K-estimation accuracy; compare against EM initialized with spectral HMM per-component, variational/Bayesian MHMMs, and other sequence clustering methods; add more real datasets; report statistical variability and runtime breakdown.
- Explore alternatives to the longevity metric (e.g., spectral projectors, eigengap heuristics with stability analysis).

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is appealing and the empirical hints are promising, but the current paper lacks theoretical guarantees for its central step and presents a limited empirical evaluation under restrictive assumptions. Strengthening the analysis and experiments could make this a solid contribution.
