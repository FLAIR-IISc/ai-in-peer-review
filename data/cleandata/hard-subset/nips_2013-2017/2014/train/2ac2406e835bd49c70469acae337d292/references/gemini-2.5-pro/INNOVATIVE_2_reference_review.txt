**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) based on the method of moments, specifically using spectral learning techniques. The standard algorithm for learning MHMMs is Expectation-Maximization (EM), which can be computationally expensive and prone to local optima. The authors' core contribution is a two-step approach. First, they make the elegant observation that an MHMM with K components can be equivalently represented as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. This allows them to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this aggregate model. However, this estimation is only unique up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The second, and more significant, contribution is a novel "de-permutation" algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix to recover the block-diagonal structure, thereby separating the parameters of the K constituent HMMs. The paper also proposes a heuristic method for estimating the number of components, K, from the data. The method is validated on synthetic and real-world data, showing a significant speed advantage over EM.

**Strengths:**
1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Approach:** The central idea of this paper is clever and highly original. Re-framing the MHMM learning problem as learning a single, structured HMM and then solving the resulting "de-permutation" problem is a significant conceptual contribution. This is not an incremental improvement on existing methods but a fundamentally new direction for tackling this problem, moving away from iterative likelihood maximization. This is precisely the kind of creative and insightful work that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Potential for High Impact (Significance):** The proposed algorithm addresses major, well-known weaknesses of the standard EM approach for MHMMs: high computational cost (especially with many sequences) and sensitivity to initialization. By providing a non-iterative, polynomial-time algorithm with provable guarantees in the asymptotic regime, this work could become a standard technique, either as a standalone method or, as the authors demonstrate, as a highly effective initialization strategy for EM. The ability to also estimate the number of clusters K is a major practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The key insight (Lemma 1) is presented clearly, and the subsequent problem of permutation ambiguity is well-motivated. The proposed solution is developed step-by-step, starting from the ideal noiseless case and moving to the more realistic noisy scenario.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Incomplete Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical foundation for the crucial de-permutation step in the presence of noise is not fully solidified. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction, is stated without proof and relies on reusing a bound from a related but different model. While the justification seems plausible, the lack of a formal proof for this specific setting is a weakness. Similarly, the conditions for estimating K in Lemma 3 are quite specific and may not hold universally. However, given the novelty of the core idea, I view this not as a fatal flaw but as an honest acknowledgment of the current theoretical limits, which can be addressed in future work.

2.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental results, while promising, could be more comprehensive. The real-data experiment shows the proposed method is not consistently superior to randomly initialized EM in terms of clustering accuracy, though it excels when used as an initializer. This slightly tempers the claim that it can fully replace EM. A more thorough comparison on a wider range of datasets, perhaps including scenarios where EM is known to fail badly due to poor initializations, would have strengthened the paper's claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core contribution—the re-formulation of the MHMM learning problem and the spectral de-permutation solution—is highly novel and elegant.
*   The work addresses a significant, practical problem and offers a computationally efficient alternative to the standard EM algorithm.
*   The ability to estimate the number of components (K) from data is a very strong feature.
*   The paper opens up a new and promising direction for learning hierarchical latent variable models using spectral techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis for the noisy case rests on a conjecture rather than a formal proof.
*   The empirical evidence that the method is a superior standalone algorithm to EM is not entirely convincing; its strength appears to be more as a fast and robust initialization scheme.

**Recommendation:**
Accept.

This paper presents a bold and highly innovative approach to a classic problem in machine learning. While the theoretical analysis is not yet complete and the experimental validation could be stronger, the novelty and significance of the core idea are exceptional. The authors have identified a beautiful underlying structure (MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM) and developed a clever algorithm to exploit it. This kind of creative, high-risk, high-reward research should be encouraged at NIPS. The paper's weaknesses, primarily the incomplete theoretical justification, are forgivable in light of the strength and originality of its main contribution. This work provides a new lens through which to view a familiar problem and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
