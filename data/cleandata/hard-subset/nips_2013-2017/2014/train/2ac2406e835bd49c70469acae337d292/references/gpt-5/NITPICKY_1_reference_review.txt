Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to one component HMM. The authors propose to (i) run an existing spectral/MoM algorithm for HMMs to estimate a global HMM with MK states (M states per HMM, K mixtures), which is known to be identifiable only up to a permutation of hidden states; (ii) exploit spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition to recover a “de-permutation” that groups states into blocks; and (iii) estimate K using a novel “spectral longevity” heuristic computed from the eigenvalues of the estimated transition. Experiments on synthetic data examine robustness to noise and compare speed/accuracy to EM; a small real-data test on UCI character trajectories is also included.

Strengths
- The reduction of an MHMM to an HMM with a block-diagonal transition is simple but useful, and it clarifies how spectral HMM tools might be leveraged.
- The intuition that the top K eigenvalues (≈1 in the noiseless case) carry cluster information is reasonable; the “low-rank reconstruction + column clustering” idea to undo permutations is interesting.
- The paper targets a relevant problem (fast learning for MHMMs), and the empirical comparison shows expected speed gains over EM.
- The algorithm is conceptually simple and modular once moments are available.

Major weaknesses and technical concerns
- Methodological gaps in the MoM step for continuous emissions:
  - The paper relies on “an existing MoM algorithm for HMMs” but does not specify which exact algorithm and which moments are used. Classical spectral HMM methods (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang 2009; Anandkumar et al. 2012) assume discrete observations or specific multi-view constructions; extensions to continuous emissions require feature maps and careful identifiability assumptions (e.g., Song et al. 2010; Siddiqi, Boots, Gordon 2010). None of these details are provided.
  - There is a notation inconsistency: equation (1) defines O(xt) = p(xt|:, O), yet later O(:,u) is defined as E[xt|rt=u] for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial. If O stores means, it is insufficient to compute p(xt|rt) unless variances are included; if O stores probabilities (discrete case), then the Gaussian/Poisson discussion is inconsistent. This undermines the likelihood expressions and how moments are formed.
  - The real-data experiment increases L via a cubic polynomial map to enforce MK < L. This change modifies the emission model and moments, but the paper does not show that the chosen MoM method remains valid/identifiable under such a transformation.
- The de-permutation via eigendecomposition is fragile and underspecified:
  - The approach assumes each Ak has exactly one eigenvalue equal to 1 and is diagonalizable with real spectrum. Stochastic matrices can be non-diagonalizable and have complex eigenvalues; no discussion of Jordan blocks or complex spectra is provided.
  - In the noisy case, Conjecture 1 is central but unproven. The error bound borrows a sample complexity result for mixtures without justification that the same conditions hold here (the text itself concedes this).
  - The “spectral longevity” metric (Definition 3) and Lemma 3 are ad hoc. The indicator-based sum depends on the choice of γ and on the range of e; sensitivity analyses and theoretical justification are absent.
  - The column-clustering step on Ar may fail when stationary distributions across components are similar, when eigenvectors are ill-conditioned, or when K eigenvalues are close to local eigenvalues. No robustness analysis is given.
  - Practical issues (complex eigenvalues, sign/scale ambiguities of eigenvectors, choice of clustering algorithm/init, how to choose the exponent range) are not addressed.
- Identifiability and inputs:
  - The algorithm requires MK as input to the initial MoM learner, yet later purports to estimate K. If M is unknown, how is MK set? Conversely, if K is unknown, how is MK chosen? This circularity is not resolved.
  - Assumptions needed for identifiability (e.g., distinct emission means, full column rank of O, mixing conditions, separation of spectra |λGK| > max |λL2,k|) are strong but not verified in experiments.
- Empirical evaluation is limited:
  - Synthetic tests are small-scale (K=3–5, M=3–4, L=20) and tuned to favorable settings. No stress tests (unequal mixture weights, near-degenerate transitions, highly overlapping emissions, repeated stationary distributions) are shown.
  - Figures and quantitative results are referenced but not sufficiently described to assess statistical significance (e.g., error bars, multiple runs, sensitivity to hyperparameters).
  - The real-data test uses feature engineering to meet MK < L; no ablation to show its necessity or impact; EM is run with multiple restarts but spectral method as an initialization is not fairly contrasted with strong EM initializations (e.g., spectral for each component or k-means on sequence summaries).
- Presentation and rigor:
  - Multiple claims appear as “Lemma” with proofs that assume diagonalizability and omit conditions. Central performance statement is a conjecture.
  - Some derivations (e.g., the limit e→∞ for ĀP with noise) are not mathematically justified.
  - Algorithm 1 is underspecified (black-box MoM, choice of e-range, clustering details, how blocks are extracted after permutation).
  - The references are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues; duplicated titles; typos).

Minor issues and formatting
- Numerous typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., forwardbackward; inconsistent use of bold/overbars; switching between 1M and 1J; misuse of P versus P̃; missing transposes).
- Equation (6) uses v̄k1>M notation with mixed sizes; define dimensions clearly.
- A footnote marker “1” appears for Ak ∈ RM×M but no footnote text is provided.
- Clarify whether ν̄ includes π multiplicatively (as in (3)) and how normalization is enforced.
- Clarify complexity: moment computation and eigendecomposition cost O((MK)^3); discuss memory for moments.

Related work to consider/cite
- Nonparametric/spectral HMMs with continuous observations: Song et al., 2010 (Hilbert space embeddings of HMMs); Boots et al., 2011; Siddiqi, Boots, Gordon, 2010 (Reduced-Rank HMMs).
- Spectral methods for mixtures and multi-view models beyond discrete settings (Anandkumar et al., 2014 tensors; Chaganty & Liang, 2013 for regression mixtures).

Pros
- Clear, intuitive reduction of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM.
- Potentially fast post-moment estimation independent of dataset size.
- Practical idea to undo permutation by grouping columns via spectral structure.

Cons
- Core MoM step for continuous emissions is unspecified/inconsistent.
- Theoretical guarantees are weak (central claim is a conjecture; strong unverified assumptions).
- Depermutation and K-estimation are heuristic and fragile; no robustness analysis.
- Limited empirical validation; reliance on feature mapping to satisfy assumptions.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains an interesting idea (using spectral structure to unpermute MHMM components), but the methodological and theoretical gaps, inconsistencies in the emission modeling and MoM specification, and the limited, somewhat ad hoc empirical evidence make the contribution incomplete. Strengthening the paper would require: a precise and consistent MoM procedure for the chosen emission family (with identifiability and sample complexity), rigorous conditions for the depermutation and K-estimation steps (including handling complex spectra/defective matrices), and more comprehensive experiments with ablations and robustness tests, as well as substantial improvements in clarity and references.
