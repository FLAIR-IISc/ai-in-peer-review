- Summary: Proposes a method-of-moments/spectral approach to learn Mixtures of HMMs by treating MHMM as a single block-diagonal HMM, performing spectral HMM estimation, then de-permuting recovered states via eigenstructure and estimating K by “spectral longevity.”

- Relation to prior work: Builds on spectral HMM literature (Hsu, Anandkumar) but omits relevant metastability/PCCA-style literature for near-invariant eigenstructures.

- Strength: Simple, actionable reduction of MHMM to HMM plus principled de-permutation exploiting Markov eigenstructure.

- Strength: Computationally efficient — after moments are computed, parameter recovery is dataset-size independent and empirically faster than EM.

- Strength: Provides an intuitive heuristic (spectral longevity) for estimating number of components K that works in synthetic tests.

- Strength: Empirically useful as a strong initializer for EM on real data, improving over random restarts.

- Strength: Clear motivation and noiseless lemmas that explain the algorithm’s behavior in idealized settings.

- Weakness: Main theoretical claims lack finite-sample/perturbation analysis; Conjecture 1 is unproven and sample-complexity reuse is informal.

- Weakness: No formal Davis–Kahan/Weyl-style perturbation bounds for stability of the K-dimensional invariant subspace or column clustering under noise.

- Weakness: Assumes spectral HMM recovery yields only permutation ambiguity without stating necessary identifiability/rank conditions or which MoM variant guarantees this.

- Weakness: Requires strong rank/dimension conditions (rank Ō = MK and L ≥ MK), limiting applicability to high-dimensional observations.

- Weakness: Spectral separability assumption (global eigenvalues near 1 separated from block second eigenvalues) can fail for slow-mixing HMMs and is not analyzed or empirically tested.

- Weakness: Ambiguity about estimating M and block boundaries — unclear whether M must be known and how it’s inferred if not.

- Weakness: Eigenvalue collisions across blocks can mix eigenvectors and break clustering; this failure mode is unaddressed.

- Weakness: Related literature on metastability and spectral clustering for block structures is not cited and could strengthen analysis.

- Weakness: Experimental scope limited — only Gaussian emissions, modest K/M, no discrete/Poisson tests, and no slow-mixing regimes; baselines limited to EM with few restarts.

- Quality: Moderate — noiseless analysis correct and algorithm coherent, but reliance on conjectures and restrictive assumptions weakens claims.

- Clarity: Generally clear and organized, but notation is heavy, some typos remain, and key assumptions (rank, knowledge of M) are not explicit.

- Originality: Moderate — block-diagonal reduction is known, but de-permutation via eigenstructure and K heuristic are novel in this context.

- Significance: Potentially useful for scalable MHMM learning and EM initialization, but constrained by dimensionality/rank requirements and theoretical gaps.

- Suggestion: Provide formal perturbation bounds for de-permutation (e.g., Davis–Kahan) and show column clustering stability under noise.

- Suggestion: State precise identifiability conditions under which the chosen MoM recovers parameters up to permutation (not arbitrary similarity).

- Suggestion: Replace or complement longevity with standard eigengap/model-selection criteria and justify K selection statistically.

- Suggestion: Analyze and empirically test robustness when second eigenvalues are close to 1; include slow-mixing synthetic experiments.

- Suggestion: Expand experiments to discrete/Poisson emissions, larger K/M, sensitivity to L vs MK, and stronger EM/alternative baselines.

- Suggestion: Clarify whether M must be known or propose a method to infer M from data.

- Suggestion: Cite and leverage metastability/PCCA+ and spectral clustering literature to improve de-permutation and guarantees.

- Pros for acceptance: Scalable, clear algorithm with promising EM initialization benefits and demonstrated speedups.

- Cons for acceptance: Unproven conjectures, strong/risky rank assumptions, fragility to slow-mixing, limited experiments and missing theoretical rigor.

- Recommendation: Borderline reject — interesting idea but needs stronger theory, clarified assumptions, and broader empirical validation.
