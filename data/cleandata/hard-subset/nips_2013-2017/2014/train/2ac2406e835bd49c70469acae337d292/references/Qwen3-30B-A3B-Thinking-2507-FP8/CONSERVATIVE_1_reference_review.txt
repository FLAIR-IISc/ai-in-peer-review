

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), leveraging the insight that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors apply existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs, then propose a depermutation method to recover individual HMM parameters using spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The authors claim computational advantages over EM (which requires forward-backward message passing for each sequence) and present limited experimental results showing comparable accuracy with EM but faster computation.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies an interesting connection between MHMMs and HMMs that could potentially simplify learning. The algorithmic description is clear, and the problem is well-motivated. The authors provide a reasonable justification for why their approach might be computationally advantageous over EM, particularly for large datasets. The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories is relevant and demonstrates potential utility as an initialization method for EM.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical Gaps:** The paper relies heavily on unproven conjectures. Conjecture 1, which is central to the depermutation procedure, lacks rigorous justification. The authors cite a different model (mixture model) and claim "further analysis is out of scope," which is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The spectral conditions for depermutation (Definition 3, Lemma 3) are not validated through experiments or additional theoretical analysis.

2. **Experimental Limitations:** The experiments are limited in scale and scope. The synthetic data experiments use extremely small datasets (2 sequences per cluster with length T=20 in Figure 3), which doesn't demonstrate the claimed computational advantage. The real data experiment uses a questionable data transformation (cubic polynomial feature transformation to satisfy MK < L) that isn't well-justified and may affect results. The paper doesn't compare against other spectral learning approaches for MHMMs or establish a clear baseline for computational efficiency at scale.

3. **Lack of Rigorous Validation:** The authors don't properly validate the key claim that their depermutation method works reliably in practice. The spectral longevity measure (Definition 3) appears to be a heuristic without sufficient experimental validation. The paper claims "correct number of clusters K=3 as well as the block-diagonal structure... is correctly recovered even in the case where T=20," but doesn't provide error metrics or statistical significance for these claims.

4. **Insufficient Comparison:** The paper only compares against EM, without benchmarking against other established methods for MHMM learning. The real data experiment compares against "EM initialized at random" and "EM initialized with MoM algorithm" but doesn't provide clear evidence that the spectral method outperforms other initialization approaches.

5. **Practical Concerns:** The requirement that MK < L (where M is the number of hidden states per HMM, K is the number of clusters, and L is the observation dimension) is restrictive and may not hold for many practical applications. The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where this condition fails.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper identifies an interesting connection between MHMMs and HMMs, the proposed method lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and experimental validation to support its claims. The reliance on unproven conjectures and the limited experimental evidence make it difficult to assess the true value of the approach. The computational advantage claimed is not demonstrated on sufficiently large datasets, and the real data experiment is marred by an arbitrary data transformation.

The paper's contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing spectral learning techniques rather than a significant advance. The methodological soundness is questionable due to the unproven theoretical claims, and the experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. For a NIPS paper, the authors would need to provide rigorous theoretical guarantees or extensive experimental validation on larger, more realistic datasets to support their claims.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper presents a novel idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The theoretical gaps are substantial, and the experimental validation is limited. The authors should either provide rigorous theoretical justification for their key claims or conduct more comprehensive experiments on larger datasets before resubmitting. The current submission does not meet the high standards of theoretical rigor and empirical validation expected at NIPS.
