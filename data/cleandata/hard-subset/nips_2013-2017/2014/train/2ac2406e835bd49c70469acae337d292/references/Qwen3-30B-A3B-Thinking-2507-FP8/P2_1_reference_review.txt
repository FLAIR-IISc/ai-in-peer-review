

# Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a computational bottleneck in traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors build on existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs, but introduce a novel method to handle the permutation ambiguity that arises during MoM parameter estimation. Their approach involves spectral analysis of the global transition matrix to recover the depermutation mapping needed to identify individual HMM parameters. The paper also provides theoretical conditions under which the method works and demonstrates its effectiveness through synthetic and real-world experiments.

This work extends the line of spectral learning for latent variable models pioneered by Anandkumar et al. (2012) and Hsu et al. (2012), which has been influential in the NIPS community. The authors make a natural extension to MHMMs, which are useful for clustering sequences but computationally challenging to learn with EM.

## Strengths

### Quality
1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper establishes a rigorous mathematical equivalence between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices (Lemma 1), which forms a strong foundation for the proposed approach.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Insight**: The authors' approach to handling permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the transition matrix is a significant contribution. The spectral longevity concept (Definition 3) provides a principled way to estimate the number of clusters K.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The experiments cover multiple aspects: the effect of noise (Section 4.1), comparison with EM in terms of accuracy and speed (Section 4.2), and a real-world application (Section 4.3). The results consistently show that the spectral approach is computationally much faster than EM while maintaining comparable accuracy.

4. **Practical Value**: The real data experiment demonstrates that the method can serve as a good initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution.

### Clarity
1. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is logically organized, with clear sections on model definitions, theoretical development, and experimental results.

2. **Effective Use of Figures**: Figures 1 and 2 effectively illustrate the spectral clustering concept and the spectral longevity metric, making complex ideas more accessible.

3. **Clear Algorithm Description**: Algorithm 1 provides a step-by-step description of the proposed method, making it straightforward for readers to understand the implementation.

### Significance
1. **Addresses a Real Computational Bottleneck**: The paper directly tackles the computational inefficiency of EM for MHMMs with large datasets, which is a practical problem in sequence clustering applications.

2. **Demonstrates Practical Utility**: The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows tangible benefits of the approach, particularly as an initialization method for EM.

3. **Potential for Broader Impact**: The authors suggest this approach could be extended to other hierarchical latent variable models, indicating potential for wider applicability.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
1. **Incomplete Theoretical Analysis**: The paper relies on Conjecture 1 to establish the error bound for the low-rank reconstruction, without providing a formal proof. While the justification is reasonable, the lack of a rigorous theoretical guarantee is a significant weakness for a NeurIPS submission, where theoretical contributions are highly valued.

2. **Limited Discussion of Practical Constraints**: The real data experiment transforms the input to satisfy the condition MK < L (required for MoM learning), which is a non-trivial limitation that isn't adequately addressed. The paper doesn't discuss how this constraint affects the method's applicability to real-world problems where this condition might not hold.

3. **Lack of Complexity Analysis**: The paper claims the spectral method is computationally cheaper than EM but doesn't provide a formal complexity analysis comparing the two approaches.

### Originality
1. **Insufficient Distinction from Related Work**: While the paper cites foundational work on spectral learning for HMMs, it doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from other existing methods for MHMM learning. A more thorough discussion of related work would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Underdeveloped Novelty**: The core idea of using spectral learning for MHMMs is a natural extension of existing techniques. The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating why this extension is non-trivial and how their specific approach to handling permutation ambiguity differs from potential alternatives.

### Clarity
1. **Complexity of Spectral Conditions**: The spectral conditions in Definition 3 and Lemma 3 are mathematically sophisticated but not sufficiently explained in intuitive terms, which may make the paper difficult for some readers to follow.

2. **Ambiguity in Algorithm Implementation**: The paper doesn't specify how the "sufficiently large range" for exponentiating eigenvalues (in Algorithm 1) is determined, which could affect reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of learning MHMMs. The core idea of viewing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is clever and well-executed. The experimental results demonstrate practical advantages over EM, particularly in terms of computational efficiency.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in its theoretical development and presentation of limitations. The reliance on a conjecture without proof is a significant concern for a NeurIPS submission, where theoretical rigor is highly valued. The real data experiment's data transformation requirement also limits the practical applicability of the method.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper presents a solid contribution with clear practical benefits. To strengthen it for NeurIPS, I recommend:

1. Providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for Conjecture 1 or clearly stating it as a conjecture with a discussion of its limitations.

2. Adding a discussion of the MK < L constraint and its implications for real-world applications.

3. Including a formal complexity analysis comparing the spectral method and EM.

4. Expanding the related work section to better distinguish this contribution from existing MHMM learning approaches.

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Addresses a significant computational problem in MHMM learning
- Provides a theoretically grounded solution with practical benefits
- Demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches through comprehensive experiments
- Offers a novel approach to handling permutation ambiguity in spectral learning
- Has potential for broader application to hierarchical latent variable models

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Relies on an unproven conjecture for the theoretical foundation
- Has a limitation (MK < L) that affects real-world applicability without sufficient discussion
- Lacks formal complexity analysis comparing computational costs
- Does not sufficiently distinguish from related work in MHMM learning

Overall, the paper has strong merit and would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS, especially with the recommended revisions addressing the theoretical and practical limitations.
