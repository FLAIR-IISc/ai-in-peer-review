### Summary

This paper presents a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The work is built upon the recent line of research in spectral learning for latent variable models, particularly for HMMs [1, 2, 3]. The authors' core contribution is a two-step approach. First, they make the key observation that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is equivalent to a single, larger HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This allows them to apply existing spectral HMM learning algorithms to estimate the global parameters. However, spectral methods are only identifiable up to a permutation of the hidden states, which in this setting mixes the parameters of the K distinct HMMs, preventing their recovery. The second, and more significant, contribution is a novel spectral "de-permutation" technique. By analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix, specifically its top K eigenvalues, they propose a method to cluster the permuted states and recover the block-diagonal structure, thereby identifying the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The paper also proposes a heuristic based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components K from the data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its core formulation. The derivation showing the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM (Lemma 1) is elegant and correct. The intuition behind the spectral de-permutation algorithm is also very strong; in the noiseless case, the argument holds perfectly.

However, the theoretical justification for the noisy case is not fully rigorous. Conjecture 1, which provides error bounds for the low-rank matrix reconstruction, is a central piece of the analysis but is left unproven, with its justification borrowed from a related but different model setting. While the intuition is plausible, the lack of a formal proof is a weakness. Furthermore, the success of the entire approach hinges on a critical spectral gap condition (`|λ_GK| > max |λ_L2,k|`), which separates the eigenvalues corresponding to the mixture components from the internal eigenvalues of each HMM. The paper does not provide a deep analysis of how sensitive the method is to this condition or how frequently it might be violated in practice.

The experimental validation is sufficient for a proof-of-concept but leaves some questions open. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the significant speed advantage over EM. The real-world experiment is less convincing; the need to perform a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint highlights a practical limitation of this MoM approach. Moreover, the results show the method is a very effective initializer for EM, but does not consistently outperform randomly-initialized EM on its own, which slightly tempers the claim of it being a superior standalone replacement.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The central idea is communicated effectively, starting from the simple model equivalence and progressively building the argument for the de-permutation strategy. The use of figures to illustrate the column clustering of the powered-up transition matrix is particularly helpful for building intuition. The algorithm is presented clearly.

**Originality:** This is the paper's greatest strength. While it builds upon existing spectral HMM learning techniques, the application to the MHMM setting and, critically, the proposed solution to the parameter-mixing problem are highly novel. The idea of using the spectral signature of the global transition matrix to *undo* the permutation ambiguity inherent in the learning algorithm is a creative and powerful concept. To my knowledge, this is a new and non-trivial extension of spectral methods to a more complex hierarchical model. It moves beyond simply applying an existing tool and instead develops a new technique to overcome a fundamental barrier.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. Learning MHMMs with EM is notoriously slow, especially on large datasets with many long sequences, and is plagued by local optima. This paper proposes a completely different, non-iterative paradigm that is computationally efficient and comes with theoretical guarantees (even if some are not fully proven here). This could make learning MHMMs practical for much larger-scale problems. Beyond the specific application, the core idea of spectral de-permutation could inspire similar approaches for other mixture-of-experts or structured latent variable models where MoM/tensor methods produce permuted parameters. Even if the method's primary practical use turns out to be as a superior initialization strategy for EM, that alone is a valuable contribution to the community.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   **High Novelty:** The core idea of spectral de-permutation to recover parameters for a mixture model is a bold, elegant, and original contribution.
*   **Potential for High Impact:** Provides a much-needed, computationally efficient alternative to EM for a widely-used model. The approach could be generalized to other mixture models.
*   **New Direction:** This paper opens up a new and promising research direction for learning hierarchical models using moment-based methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-motivated.

**Con:**
*   **Incomplete Theory:** Key theoretical results (Conjecture 1) are not proven, and the analysis of the necessary spectral conditions is not fully developed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the concept but do not exhaustively establish the method's robustness or its superiority over EM as a standalone algorithm in real-world settings. Practical constraints of the MoM approach (`MK < L`) are also apparent.

### Recommendation

Despite the weaknesses in the theoretical completeness and experimental validation, the novelty and conceptual elegance of the core idea are outstanding. This paper introduces a significant new direction for learning a challenging class of models. The proposed spectral de-permutation strategy is a powerful concept that is likely to inspire follow-up work. In line with valuing bold new ideas over incremental perfection, I believe the contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's limitations are clearly stated and provide fertile ground for future research. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
