Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) techniques. The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M hidden states, is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose: (i) learn the “global” HMM parameters with an existing spectral HMM algorithm (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, 2012; Anandkumar et al.), which recovers parameters up to a permutation of the MK states; (ii) exploit spectral properties of the global transition matrix to “de-permute” and recover the individual component HMMs; and (iii) estimate K by a heuristic “spectral longevity” criterion based on the decay of eigenvalues of the learned transition matrix. Experiments compare the approach to EM on synthetic data and a UCI pen trajectory dataset, and suggest speed advantages and reasonable clustering accuracy.

The reduction from MHMM to an HMM with block-diagonal transition was known in the modeling literature, and mixtures of HMMs have been studied extensively (e.g., Smyth, NIPS 1997). Spectral learning for HMMs and for mixtures (static) is well-established (Hsu et al., JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al., ICML/COLT/Tensor methods). To my knowledge, a spectral method specifically targeting MHMMs via a de-permutation step that recovers component HMMs from a global spectral estimate is not standard and appears to be the main novel contribution.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The equivalence lemma is correct and clearly stated.
- The de-permutation idea leverages the block structure: in the noiseless case, the global transition matrix has K unit eigenvalues (one per block), and powers of the matrix reveal K clusters of columns—this is sound in the ergodic/aperiodic setting.
- The proposed practical pipeline is simple: learn global HMM spectrally, compute a rank-K reconstruction from the top eigenpairs, cluster columns to infer blocks, and assign states to components.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- The central robustness argument in noise is left as Conjecture 1. The paper relies on it to justify extracting K and recovering blocks when estimates are noisy. No proof or finite-sample perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis-Kahan or Wedin) is provided to relate spectral gaps and moment-estimation error to recovery guarantees.
- The condition “each Ak has only one eigenvalue equal to 1” (and all others strictly inside the unit disk) excludes periodic chains and is not discussed; the behavior with near-periodic or reducible components is unclear.
- The requirement to run spectral HMM with MK states effectively demands a full-column-rank “emission expectation” matrix Ō ∈ R^{L×MK}. This implies L ≥ MK and rank(Ō)=MK—strong and often impractical for low-dimensional observations. The paper enforces L > MK by polynomial feature lifting in the real-data experiment but does not analyze the sample complexity impact or alternatives (e.g., learned features/whitening).
- The “spectral longevity” measure for K selection is heuristic. It depends on a threshold γ and a range of powers e. No guidance is given on choosing these, nor a consistency result.
- Practical details are missing: which spectral HMM variant is used (discrete vs continuous emissions, choice of observable moments, window sizes), how moments are estimated from variable-length sequences, and how the permutation clustering is implemented (distance metric, initialization, K-means stability).
- Experimental evaluation is limited: no quantitative parameter recovery (e.g., permutation error, Frobenius or MSE), limited exploration of K estimation accuracy, and no ablations on eigenvalue gaps or noise levels. Reported results (e.g., “total error from centroid to point”) are not standard; there is no uncertainty quantification.
- Some references are incomplete or duplicated; related work on block-structured HMMs and spectral identifiability conditions could be broadened.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with clear motivation and a concise algorithm summary.
- However, several definitions conflate emission models with E[x|state] notation, which glosses over noise modeling and the exact observable moments used by spectral HMM methods.
- The K-estimation procedure is described abstractly; a more precise algorithmic description (selection of e, γ, stopping rules) is needed for reproducibility.
- The input of Algorithm 1 requires MK, but the method also estimates K—clarify whether MK is an upper bound, how over/under-specification affects learning, and whether model selection over MK is needed.

Originality
- The reduction from MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is known.
- Applying standard spectral HMM learning to the global model is straightforward.
- The novel component is the de-permutation via spectral clustering of a rank-K reconstruction and the heuristic for K estimation. This is an interesting idea but needs stronger theoretical and empirical support to establish originality beyond a practical trick.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could offer substantial computational savings over EM for large collections of sequences, and provide a good initializer for EM.
- However, the L ≥ MK rank requirement and lack of guarantees limit the immediate applicability. Demonstrating performance on higher-K, higher-M settings and discrete data with larger alphabets would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, elegant de-permutation idea grounded in the block structure of MHMMs.
- Potentially significant speed improvements over EM; practical utility as an initializer.
- Addresses an important problem (sequence clustering via MHMMs) with a scalable approach.

Cons for acceptance
- Key robustness claim is a conjecture; no theoretical recovery guarantees under noise.
- Strong and under-discussed identifiability/rank requirements (L ≥ MK).
- Limited experimental validation and missing implementation details; K-estimation not rigorously assessed.
- Incomplete related work and citations; clarity gaps around the spectral HMM module used.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace Conjecture 1 with a formal perturbation analysis: bound the difference between the true and estimated invariant subspaces, and conditions under which column clustering recovers blocks (e.g., requirements on minimal inter-block separation vs noise).
- Provide a consistent K-selection method with theoretical justification, or at least a robust practical criterion with sensitivity analysis.
- Clarify the spectral HMM pipeline: exact moments used, rank conditions, how to handle continuous emissions, and how to proceed when L < MK (e.g., random features, learned embeddings, trade-offs).
- Expand experiments: report parameter recovery metrics, K estimation accuracy, sensitivity to eigenvalue gaps and periodic chains, and larger-scale benchmarks. Include run-time breakdowns and variance across runs.
- Improve references and position relative to prior work on mixtures of HMMs, block-structured HMMs, and spectral identifiability results.

Overall, the paper contains an appealing idea with promising empirical indications, but it currently lacks theoretical grounding and thorough evaluation to meet NeurIPS standards.
