- Proposes a novel supervised and semi-supervised learning framework reformulating learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation and introduces data-dependent "Fredholm kernels."  
- Claims Fredholm kernels can effectively use unlabeled data under a newly defined "noise assumption" via the integral operator KpX.  
- Positions the approach as a generalization of standard kernel methods and connects to density-ratio estimation, kernel mean embeddings, shrinkage estimators, and manifold regularization.  
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound: Fredholm kernels shown PSD under conditions (Prop.1) and noise-suppression properties supported by Theorems 2–4.  
- Theoretical results depend on strong, restrictive assumptions (e.g., Gaussian data, infinite unlabeled data), limiting practical applicability.  
- Experimental results: strong performance on synthetic data designed to test the noise assumption; competitive on some real-world datasets.  
- Real-world empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comprehensive comparison to modern state-of-the-art semi-supervised methods.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with precise math, illustrative figures, and reproducible details including kernel variants.  
- Distinction between normalized and unnormalized Fredholm kernels is clear, but guidance on when to use each variant is insufficient.  
- The framework and explicit "noise assumption" are original and offer a new mathematical foundation for semi-supervised noise suppression.  
- Significance is conditional: valuable if the noise assumption holds in practice, but practical prevalence of that assumption is not established.  
- Missing discussion on scalability and computational complexity relative to existing methods.  
- Strengths: elegant mathematical framework, clear articulation of a new assumption, and compelling synthetic evidence.  
- Weaknesses: limited justification of the noise assumption’s real-world relevance, restrictive theoretical assumptions, and a narrow experimental comparison set.  
- Pro for acceptance: novel theoretical framework with supporting theory and experiments.  
- Con for acceptance: practical relevance uncertain due to strong assumptions and limited empirical benchmarking.
