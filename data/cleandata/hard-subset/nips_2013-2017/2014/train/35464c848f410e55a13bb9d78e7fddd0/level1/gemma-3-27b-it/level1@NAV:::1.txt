## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The core idea is to construct “Fredholm kernels” that inherently incorporate information from both labeled and unlabeled data, particularly relevant under a proposed “noise assumption” where low-variance directions are considered uninformative. The authors connect this approach to existing kernel methods and density ratio estimation techniques, and provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation demonstrating competitive performance.

The paper builds upon a rich history of kernel methods (Schölkopf & Smola, 2002; Cristianini & Shawe-Taylor, 2000) and integral operators in machine learning (Gretton et al., 2009; Muandet et al., 2014). It's particularly related to work using Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation (Que & Belkin, 2019) and kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al., 2009). The formulation as a data-dependent kernel is reminiscent of manifold regularization approaches (Belkin et al., 2006; Chapelle et al., 2006), a connection the authors acknowledge. 

**Strengths:** The paper presents a mathematically sound and well-motivated approach. The connection between the Fredholm formulation and existing kernel methods is clearly articulated, and the introduction of the "noise assumption" provides a useful perspective on the benefits of incorporating unlabeled data. The theoretical results, demonstrating noise suppression under specific conditions (Theorems 3 & 4), are significant. The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets provide evidence of the framework’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is valuable, it relies on specific assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific kernel choices) that may limit its general applicability. The derivation of the theoretical results is complex and could benefit from further simplification or a more intuitive explanation.  The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks detailed ablation studies to specifically isolate the contribution of the Fredholm kernel compared to baseline kernel methods. The presentation of experimental results is sometimes difficult to interpret (e.g., Table 1 & 2) and lacks statistical significance testing. The connection to the manifold assumption, while mentioned, could be elaborated upon.



**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to semi-supervised learning.
*   Clear connection to existing kernel methods and density estimation techniques.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance on various datasets.
*   Introduces a potentially valuable "noise assumption" for understanding SSL.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Theoretical results rely on specific assumptions.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks detailed ablation studies and statistical testing.
*   Complexity of theoretical derivations could hinder broader adoption.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with potential for significant impact. While the theoretical assumptions and experimental presentation could be improved, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the approach warrant acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should consider including more ablation studies, statistical analysis of experimental results, and a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results.
