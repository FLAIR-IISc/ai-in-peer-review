Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes “Fredholm learning,” which reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The key idea is to map predictors f in an RKHS H through an integral operator KpX induced by an “outer” kernel k and the unlabeled marginal pX, and to learn by minimizing a squared loss on Kp̂Xf with RKHS regularization. By algebraic manipulation, the method is equivalent to a standard kernel algorithm using a data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F(x, z) = E[k(x, U)kH(U, V)k(z, V)] estimated from unlabeled data. This connects to integral operator methods and RKHS embeddings [5–7], density-ratio estimation via Fredholm equations [10], and data-dependent kernels/manifold regularization [1,13]. The paper advances semi-supervised learning under a “noise assumption,” orthogonal to the cluster assumption, arguing that low-variance directions locally are label-irrelevant. Theory shows variance-reduction for estimating target linear/Gaussian kernels with Fredholm kernels under Gaussian manifold+noise models, and experiments show gains over RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC on synthetic and several real datasets.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound, with a clear representer-theorem solution and PSD guarantees (when KH is PSD). Theoretical results (Theorems 2–4) are insightful but rely on restrictive assumptions (Gaussian pX, orthogonal noise, infinite unlabeled data, matched inner/outer kernels). A finite-sample analysis of Kp̂X (concentration, bias/variance vs u) and generalization bounds for data-dependent k̂F would strengthen the claims. Computational complexity and stability (e.g., small bandwidths; normalized variant) deserve deeper analysis.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written. It would help to (i) move core proofs (PSD, representer derivation) into the main text in brief form, (ii) detail computational costs (forming KH and KF, O((l+u)^3) solves), and (iii) clarify inductive vs transductive use at test time.

Originality: The Fredholm-kernel construction—effectively a density-weighted convolution of kernels—is a novel, clean unification of integral operators and semi-supervised kernels, distinct from manifold Laplacians and from [10]. The “noise assumption” is a useful, explicit articulation.

Significance: Results suggest a practical, general recipe to leverage unlabeled data for denoising base kernels. If scalability and finite-sample guarantees are addressed, this could be impactful for semi-supervised text/vision.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled framework yielding a PSD, data-dependent kernel.
- Theoretical variance-reduction under a plausible manifold+noise model.
- Consistent empirical gains on synthetic and real datasets.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong distributional assumptions; no finite-sample excess risk bounds.
- Computational scalability and stability not fully addressed.
- Limited baselines (e.g., label propagation, diffusion/graph kernels) and ablations (u-sensitivity, normalized vs unnormalized).

Suggestions: Add finite-sample analysis of Kp̂X; provide runtime/memory benchmarks; include stronger graph-based baselines; study sensitivity to k/kH, bandwidths, and u; discuss relations to diffusion kernels and marginalized/data-dependent kernels beyond [13]. I recommend weak accept.
