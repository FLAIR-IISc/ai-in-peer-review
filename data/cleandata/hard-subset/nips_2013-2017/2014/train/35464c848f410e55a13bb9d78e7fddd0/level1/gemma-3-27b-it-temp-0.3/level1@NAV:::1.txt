## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The core idea is to construct “Fredholm kernels” which naturally incorporate unlabeled data and can be interpreted as data-dependent kernels. The authors connect this approach to existing work in kernel methods, density ratio estimation, and manifold regularization, and provide both theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the established kernel framework and addressing the challenge of effectively utilizing unlabeled data. The formulation using Fredholm integral equations is a clever approach, and the connection to data-dependent kernels provides a practical implementation pathway. The “noise assumption” introduced is a valuable contribution to the discussion around semi-supervised learning, offering a different perspective compared to the more common manifold and cluster assumptions. The theoretical results, particularly those demonstrating noise suppression under specific conditions, are a strong point. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate competitive performance, supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, it is quite specific to certain kernel choices (linear and Gaussian) and distributional assumptions (Gaussian noise). Extending these results to more general scenarios would significantly strengthen the contribution. The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of different components of the Fredholm kernel. The comparison to existing semi-supervised learning methods is somewhat limited; a more thorough comparison, including state-of-the-art techniques, would be beneficial. The notation is dense and could be improved for clarity.  The discussion of related work is extensive but could be more focused on highlighting the *specific* differences and advantages of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated framework for semi-supervised learning.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with results demonstrating noise suppression.
*   Competitive empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Clear connection to existing kernel methods and related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results are limited to specific kernel choices and assumptions.
*   Experimental section lacks a detailed ablation study and comprehensive comparison.
*   Dense notation and potentially improved clarity.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical insights, and the promising experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and comprehensiveness of the experimental evaluation.
