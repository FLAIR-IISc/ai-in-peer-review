- Proposes “Fredholm learning”: reformulate supervised/semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation mapping RKHS predictors via an integral operator induced by an outer kernel and the unlabeled marginal.

- Shows equivalence to a standard kernel method using a data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F(x,z)=E[k(x,U)kH(U,V)k(z,V)] estimated from unlabeled data.

- Situates work relative to integral operator methods, RKHS embeddings, density-ratio estimation via Fredholm equations, and data-dependent/manifold regularization.

- Introduces a “noise assumption” alternative to the cluster assumption: low-variance directions locally are label-irrelevant and can be denoised.

- Theoretical results demonstrate variance reduction for estimating target linear/Gaussian kernels under Gaussian manifold+noise models.

- Empirical results show gains over RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC on synthetic and several real datasets.

- Technical formulation is sound with a clear representer-theorem solution and PSD guarantees (when KH is PSD).

- Theoretical analyses rely on restrictive assumptions: Gaussian pX, orthogonal noise, infinite unlabeled data, and matched inner/outer kernels.

- Lacks finite-sample analysis of Kp̂X (concentration, bias/variance versus u) and generalization bounds for the data-dependent k̂F.

- Computational complexity and stability (e.g., small bandwidths, normalized variant) are not fully analyzed and need deeper treatment.

- Paper is generally well written but would benefit from moving core proofs into the main text, detailing computational costs, and clarifying inductive vs transductive testing.

- The Fredholm-kernel construction is novel—a density-weighted convolution of kernels—distinct from manifold Laplacians and prior work.

- The explicit articulation of the noise assumption is a useful original contribution.

- Potentially significant: offers a practical recipe to leverage unlabeled data to denoise base kernels for semi-supervised tasks if scalability and finite-sample guarantees are improved.

- Pros: principled PSD, data-dependent kernel; theoretical variance-reduction; consistent empirical gains.

- Cons: strong distributional assumptions; no finite-sample excess risk bounds; unresolved scalability/stability; limited baselines and ablations.

- Suggested improvements: add finite-sample analysis of Kp̂X, provide runtime/memory benchmarks, include stronger graph-based baselines, study sensitivity to k/kH, bandwidths, and u, and discuss relations to diffusion/marginalized kernels.

- Reviewer recommendation: weak accept.
