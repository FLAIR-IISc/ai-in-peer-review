This paper proposes a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning, formulating the learning problem as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to "Fredholm kernels." The authors connect this approach to existing kernel methods and explore its theoretical properties under a “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning, providing empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The core idea of reformulating learning as solving a Fredholm equation is interesting and potentially valuable, offering a different perspective on integrating unlabeled data. The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to provide both theoretical justification (particularly regarding noise suppression) and practical demonstration of the proposed Fredholm kernels. The connection to existing kernel methods, specifically through the derivation of a data-dependent kernel, is clearly explained and provides a useful interpretation of the proposed framework.  The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments, with comparisons to several baselines, strengthens the empirical validation. The extensive related work section is well-structured and demonstrates an awareness of the relevant literature.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The "noise assumption," while intuitively appealing, feels somewhat vaguely defined. While the authors connect it to the cluster and manifold assumptions, a more precise mathematical formulation would be beneficial. The theoretical results in Section 4, while providing insights into noise suppression, are presented with considerable mathematical detail that may be inaccessible to a broader audience. The conditions under which these theoretical results hold (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific kernel choices) may limit their generalizability.  Furthermore, the experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a thorough ablation study examining the sensitivity of performance to different kernel parameters and the amount of unlabeled data. The notation is also somewhat dense, occasionally hindering readability. For example, the multiple definitions of kF and the varying normalizations (with and without 'N') require careful attention. While the authors acknowledge the naming conflict with existing uses of "Fredholm kernel," the explanation feels cursory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation provides a fresh perspective on semi-supervised learning and introduces a new class of data-dependent kernels.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper attempts to provide theoretical support for the proposed framework, particularly concerning noise suppression, although the conditions are restrictive.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The extensive experiments demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Connections to existing work:** The paper clearly situates the work within the broader context of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Vague Definition of Noise Assumption:** The "noise assumption" is not rigorously defined, hindering a deeper understanding of its implications.
*   **Limited Generalizability of Theory:** The theoretical results rely on strong assumptions (e.g., Gaussian distributions) that may not hold in practical scenarios.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The experimental section lacks a detailed ablation study to assess the sensitivity of the method to different parameters and data conditions.
*   **Dense Notation:** The notation can be complex, potentially hindering readability and reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to semi-supervised learning. However, the somewhat vague definition of the key “noise assumption” and the restrictive nature of the theoretical results prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.  The empirical validation is solid, but a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial.  I recommend **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the issues related to the noise assumption’s definition and including a more comprehensive experimental analysis. Specifically, they should clarify the noise assumption, perhaps with a more formal mathematical statement, and conduct an ablation study to understand the impact of different parameters on performance.  Simplifying the notation where possible would also improve readability.
