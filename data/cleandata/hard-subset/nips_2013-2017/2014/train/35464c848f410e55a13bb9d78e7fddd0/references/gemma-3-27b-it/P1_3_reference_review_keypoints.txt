- Proposes a novel learning framework casting supervised and unsupervised learning as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind.  
- Introduces "Fredholm kernels" via regularization that naturally incorporate unlabeled data and yield a data-dependent kernel interpretation.  
- Provides theoretical analysis suggesting Fredholm kernels can suppress noisy dimensions and improve estimation under a stated “noise assumption.”  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets support the method and specifically illustrate the noise-assumption scenario.  
- Builds on kernel methods, integral operators, density-ratio estimation, kernel mean embeddings, and semi-supervised techniques like manifold regularization.  
- Explicitly connects the Fredholm framework to standard kernel methods with data-dependent kernels, easing comparison to prior work.  
- Strength: fresh perspective that integrates unlabeled data with theoretical justification and empirical validation.  
- Strength: clear presentation and logical organization.  
- Weakness: the “noise assumption” is vaguely formalized and not clearly related to manifold or cluster assumptions.  
- Weakness: theoretical results rely on strong assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, linear subspace) and some claims (choice of t) lack practical justification.  
- Weakness: experimental details and hyperparameter tuning for baselines (TSVM, LapRLSC) are insufficiently described.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability of evaluating/inverting Fredholm kernel matrices are not adequately addressed.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to prior uses/definitions of “Fredholm Kernel.”  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded framework with promising empirical results and relevance to the community.  
- Against acceptance: vague noise assumption, strong theoretical assumptions limiting generality, and missing computational analysis.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Revision recommendations: rigorously define the noise assumption and relate it to existing concepts; explicitly acknowledge theoretical limitations; detail experimental hyperparameter tuning and kernel choices; discuss computational complexity and scalability; expand comparison with prior Fredholm-kernel usages.
