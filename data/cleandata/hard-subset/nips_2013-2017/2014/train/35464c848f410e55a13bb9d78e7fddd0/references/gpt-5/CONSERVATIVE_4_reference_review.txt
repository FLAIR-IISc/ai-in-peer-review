Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework that casts supervised prediction as a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Practically, this results in learning f in an RKHS H but measuring fit via a smoothed operator K_pX built from an “outer” kernel k and the unlabeled data distribution pX. The discretized formulation yields a closed-form solution equivalent to kernel ridge regression with a data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F(x, z) = ∑_{i,j} k(x, xi) kH(xi, xj) k(z, xj), optionally normalized. The authors argue that unlabeled data enters through k̂F and present two theoretical results: (i) for linear outer k and Gaussian inner kH under a Gaussian pX, the induced inner product reweights principal directions, suppressing low-variance directions (“noise assumption”); (ii) under a subspace-plus-orthogonal-Gaussian-noise model and infinite unlabeled data, kF provides unbiased lower-variance estimators of the “true” linear/Gaussian kernels than applying those kernels directly to noisy observations. Empirically, they report improvements on synthetic setups designed to violate the cluster assumption and on text and digit datasets.

Relation to prior work
The idea of data-dependent kernels and integral-operator-based semi-supervised learning is well-studied (manifold regularization; diffusion kernels; Gaussian fields/harmonic functions; kernel mean embeddings; density ratio via Fredholm equations). The proposed kernel can be seen as composing an “outer” operator induced by unlabeled data with a base kernel KH, i.e., KF = A KH A^T, which is a standard way to build PSD, data-dependent kernels. The paper cites several relevant threads (e.g., manifold regularization and [10] on Fredholm for density ratio), but the connections to diffusion kernels (Kondor & Lafferty, 2002), diffusion maps (Coifman & Lafon, 2006), and information regularization (Corduneanu & Jaakkola, 2004) are not fully developed.

Strengths
- Simple, mathematically clean formulation that unifies an operator view with kernel methods; the reduction to a PSD data-dependent kernel is transparent.
- Intuitive “noise suppression” perspective; the linear/Gaussian analyses under Gaussian pX are easy to interpret (soft-thresholding of PCs; variance reduction).
- Inductive and broadly applicable: reduces to standard KRR/SVM with a computable kernel; normalized variant addresses numerical stability.
- Includes semi-supervised baselines in experiments and covers both linear and Gaussian regimes.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to established data-dependent kernels is limited. The Fredholm kernel is effectively a smoothed/composed kernel using unlabeled-data-induced features; similar constructions exist in manifold regularization (data-dependent kernels), diffusion kernels, and density-sensitive kernels. The paper would benefit from a clearer differentiation and empirical/theoretical comparison to these.
- Theory relies on strong assumptions (Gaussian pX, infinite unlabeled data, linear subspace + orthogonal Gaussian noise) and evaluates variance of kernel estimators rather than excess risk/generalization. The main practical claim—unlabeled data “provably improves inference”—is not supported beyond idealized settings, and finite-sample error bounds for the empirical operator K_p̂X and k̂F are missing.
- Computational aspects and scalability are not addressed. Building KF = Kl+u KH Kl+u^T is O((l+u)^2) in memory and at least O((l+u)^3) if naively forming/inverting matrices; no discussion of Nyström/random features, sparsification, or complexity vs u.
- Experimental evidence, as presented, is insufficiently detailed. Numerical results are referenced but not shown here; comparisons omit influential data-dependent baselines (diffusion kernels, Gaussian fields/harmonic functions, information regularization, Laplacian SVM), and there is no study of sensitivity to hyperparameters (two kernel widths plus regularization), u, or departures from the “noise assumption.” Failure modes (e.g., when low-variance directions are label-informative) are not analyzed.
- The “noise assumption” overlaps the manifold-with-noise model widely used in SSL; its novelty is more in emphasis than in formalization. It is not precisely defined or tested beyond Gaussian toy models.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide finite-sample analysis: bounds for approximating K_pX by K_p̂X, stability of k̂F and k̂NF, and generalization guarantees (e.g., Rademacher complexity) for KRR/SVM with k̂F.
- Clarify computational complexity and propose scalable approximations (Nyström on KH and/or Kl+u, random features for k/kH). Include runtime/memory comparisons.
- Add baselines and discussion connecting to diffusion kernels (Kondor & Lafferty, 2002), diffusion maps (Coifman & Lafon, 2006), and information regularization (Corduneanu & Jaakkola, 2004). How does k̂F relate to applying a diffusion operator to features and then using KH?
- Report complete experimental details (numbers, variances, hyperparameter selection with few labels, whether experiments are inductive vs transductive). Study sensitivity to u and robustness when the noise assumption fails.
- Discuss potential harms: if low-variance directions are informative for labels, does k̂F degrade performance? Provide a counterexample or diagnostic.
- The PSD claim for the normalized variant should be formalized (it seems to be of the form T KH T^T with data-dependent T). Please include proof or a clear derivation.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat operator-to-kernel reduction with intuitive denoising interpretation.
- Some theoretical insight (soft PCA, variance reduction) and empirical gains on selected tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty vs existing data-dependent kernel methods; missing crucial related work and comparisons.
- Theory does not address finite-sample or generalization and rests on strong assumptions.
- Scalability and implementation details absent.
- Empirical section, as presented, lacks breadth and depth to justify the claims.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sensible and may be useful, but given the emphasis on established baselines and rigorous validation, the current submission does not yet provide sufficient theoretical guarantees or comprehensive empirical evidence to justify publication at NeurIPS. Strengthening the finite-sample theory, clarifying computational aspects, and adding thorough comparisons to established data-dependent kernels would significantly improve the paper.
