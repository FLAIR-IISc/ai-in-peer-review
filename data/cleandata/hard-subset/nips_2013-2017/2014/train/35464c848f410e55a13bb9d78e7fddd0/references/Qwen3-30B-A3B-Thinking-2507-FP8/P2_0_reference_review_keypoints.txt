- Proposes a novel supervised and unsupervised learning framework based on solving the Fredholm integral equation of the first kind using data-dependent "Fredholm kernels."  
- Frames learning as an inverse problem that naturally incorporates unlabeled data and introduces a new "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning.  
- Theoretical results (Theorems 2–4, Proposition 1) show Fredholm kernels can suppress noise by reweighting principal components, reduce variance, and are positive semi-definite.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, especially under the constructed noise scenarios.  
- Technical quality is strong: rigorous proofs and precise mathematical exposition support the claims.  
- Originality is strong: introduces the noise assumption and applies Fredholm equations to produce a new data-dependent kernel framework.  
- Significance is good: addresses leveraging unlabeled data when cluster/manifold assumptions fail and offers practical noise-handling benefits.  
- Clarity is good: well-organized paper, accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods, with effective illustrative figures.  
- Limited baseline comparisons: only RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC are used; missing comparisons to recent SSL methods (e.g., GCNs, consistency-based methods) and other data-dependent kernels.  
- Strong theoretical assumptions: analysis assumes known marginal distribution or infinite unlabeled data, with limited discussion of finite-sample implications.  
- Empirical scope is moderate: evaluated on a standard but limited set of datasets and lacks sensitivity analyses for kernel parameters and varying amounts of unlabeled data.  
- Recommendation: accept — contributions and empirical/theoretical support outweigh the limitations.  
- Pro summary: novel theory (noise assumption), strong variance-reduction analysis, clear connection to kernel methods, and solid empirical validation.  
- Con summary: limited modern baselines, reliance on infinite-unlabeled-data assumption, and narrower empirical validation.
