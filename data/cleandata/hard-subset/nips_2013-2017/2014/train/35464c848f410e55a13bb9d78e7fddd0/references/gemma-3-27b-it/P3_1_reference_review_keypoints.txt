- Proposes a novel supervised and semi-supervised learning framework via solving a Fredholm integral equation, introducing "Fredholm kernels" to incorporate unlabeled data.  
- Shows Fredholm kernels can be viewed as data-dependent kernels and connects the framework to existing kernel methods and integral-operator approaches.  
- Provides theoretical analysis for linear and Gaussian kernels, including noise-suppression properties and a link to soft-thresholding PCA.  
- Proves the Fredholm kernel is positive semi-definite.  
- Explicitly formulates a "noise assumption" arguing Fredholm kernels can denoise classifiers and improve semi-supervised performance under this assumption.  
- Empirically validates the approach on synthetic and real-world datasets, testing multiple kernel combinations and baselines.  
- Strength: offers a principled, novel way to integrate unlabeled data beyond traditional manifold regularization.  
- Strength: theoretical justification clarifies why Fredholm kernels help, especially under the noise assumption.  
- Strength: recasting as a standard kernel method with a data-dependent kernel aids practical understanding and adoption.  
- Strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation across datasets.  
- Weakness: computational cost—matrix inversion in the solution may not scale to large datasets, and scalability is not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian width t); more discussion on tuning and comparison of theoretical vs. cross-validated choices is needed.  
- Weakness: theoretical results are limited to linear and Gaussian kernels and should be extended to other common kernels.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation lacks statistical rigor (no confidence intervals or significance tests; standard deviations not reported).  
- Weakness: the "noise assumption" may be restrictive and its relation to other semi-supervised assumptions (manifold/cluster) needs deeper exploration.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, mathematically grounded framework with useful theoretical and empirical insights and accessible connections to existing methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns, parameter sensitivity, limited theoretical scope, and insufficient statistical analysis in experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested minor revisions: expand scalability discussion; detail parameter tuning and compare theory vs. CV; add statistical significance/confidence intervals; elaborate relationship between the noise assumption and other semi-supervised assumptions.
