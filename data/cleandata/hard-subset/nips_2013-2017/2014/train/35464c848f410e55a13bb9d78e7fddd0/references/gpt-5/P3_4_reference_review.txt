Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework that formulates prediction as a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Given an RKHS H with kernel kH and an “outer” kernel k used to define an integral operator KpX via the marginal pX, the method minimizes squared loss on labeled data in the image space KpX H with Tikhonov regularization in H. By applying the representer theorem and an algebraic identity, the solution is shown to be equivalent to standard kernel ridge regression with a data-dependent kernel, the Fredholm kernel: k̂F(x, z) = ∑i,j k(x, xi) kH(xi, xj) k(z, xj) (and a normalized variant). Thus unlabeled data shape the effective kernel through the empirical approximation of KpX. The paper motivates this through a “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning: directions of low marginal variance are label-irrelevant noise. Two theoretical results in simplified Gaussian settings argue that Fredholm kernels suppress low-variance directions (linear outer kernel) and can reduce the variance of kernel estimates of kH on noisy inputs (Gaussian outer kernel), compared to directly applying kH to noisy data. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets report improvements over RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

The work connects to several strands: data-dependent kernels and manifold regularization (Sindhwani et al.; Belkin et al.), kernel mean embeddings and integral operators, diffusion kernels, marginalized/invariant kernels that integrate over nuisance distributions, and prior use of Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation.

Strengths:
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that naturally incorporates unlabeled data; the equivalence to a PSD, data-dependent Gram matrix KF = Kl+u KH Kl+u^T is elegant and useful.
- The “noise assumption” is a sensible and practically relevant perspective distinct from the cluster/manifold assumptions; the linear outer-kernel analysis makes the denoising mechanism concrete.
- The variance-reduction claims (Theorems 3–4) provide a crisp, if stylized, justification for using unlabeled data to improve kernel estimates under corruption.
- The method is flexible: it reduces to standard supervised kernels when k is a delta kernel; normalized and unnormalized variants are discussed.
- Empirical results reportedly show improvements in both synthetic and real settings.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty relative to existing data-dependent kernels is underplayed. The Fredholm kernel KF = K KH K^T is essentially a smoothed or “convolved” kernel with respect to the empirical marginal, closely related to diffusion kernels, graph-based semi-supervised methods, marginalized/invariant kernels, and to supervised learning with covariance-reweighted inner products. Please clarify precise conceptual and technical differences from diffusion kernels, Laplacian-based data-dependent kernels, and marginalized corrupting-noise kernels.
- Theory is limited to idealized Gaussian models with infinite unlabeled data and often assumes knowledge of signal/noise scales (λ, σ) to set t. There are no finite-sample analyses quantifying the deviation between KpX and its empirical approximation and the downstream excess risk. Concentration of integral operators and stability bounds should be feasible and would materially strengthen the claims.
- Computational scalability is not addressed. Building KF requires KH on l+u points and two dense multiplications Kl+u KH Kl+u^T, which is O((l+u)^2 l) time and O((l+u)^2) memory, prohibitive when u is large. Practical approximations (Nyström, random features for kH and/or k, low-rank factorizations, block computations) and complexity vs. accuracy trade-offs should be discussed and validated.
- Baselines are incomplete for the linear case. With linear k and Gaussian kH, the induced ΣF amounts to a covariance-weighted inner product computed from unlabeled data. Stronger baselines include whitening/diagonal rescaling or shrinkage Mahalanobis kernels estimated from unlabeled data, diffusion kernels, and simple local PCA denoising followed by a standard kernel. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains specifically to the Fredholm construction.
- The “noise assumption” is intuitive but broad. The paper would benefit from a discussion of failure modes (e.g., when low-variance directions are predictive, class-conditional covariances differ, multi-modal marginals), and an empirical stress test that demonstrates degradation in these regimes.
- Inductive vs. transductive use is not clearly articulated. The normalized kernel k̂NF at test time requires sums over the unlabeled set. How is prediction performed when the test distribution differs or the unlabeled pool is not available at deployment?
- Clarity/detail issues: proofs are deferred; the PSD claim for the normalized kernel should be justified via factorization W KH W^T. In Theorem 2 the “rescaling factor” appears to incorrectly include a square root; please check. Hyperparameter selection with scarce labels is nontrivial; discuss robust tuning strategies.

Requests for clarification:
- Provide finite-sample bounds on the error between k̂F built from u unlabeled points and the population kF, and its effect on excess risk, at least for kernel ridge regression.
- Specify the computational complexity and memory footprint; include experiments with large u and approximate implementations.
- Add stronger baselines as noted above, especially covariance-reweighted linear kernels and diffusion kernels.
- Report quantitative experimental details: splits, number of unlabeled samples used, parameter grids, standard deviations across runs, and ablations as u varies.
- Discuss how to deploy inductively without access to the unlabeled pool at test time.

Pros:
- Elegant, unified operator view; simple to implement given KH and unlabeled data.
- Theoretical variance-reduction insight under plausible noise models.
- Promising empirical performance and a practical path to leverage unlabeled data beyond manifold/cluster assumptions.

Cons:
- Incremental novelty w.r.t. established data-dependent/diffusion/marginalized kernels is not convincingly delineated.
- Lack of finite-sample theory and scalability discussion.
- Missing strong baselines and detailed empirical methodology reduce the evidential weight.

Overall evaluation:
This is a thoughtful and potentially useful contribution that could resonate with the NeurIPS audience working on kernels and semi-supervised learning. However, in its current form I view it as borderline: the conceptual advance over prior data-dependent kernels needs to be sharpened, and the empirical/theoretical support strengthened as outlined above. I would lean toward a weak reject now, with a clear path to a strong paper after addressing the finite-sample analysis, scalability, and baselines.
