Summary
This paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework based on solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Given a RKHS H with kernel kH (“inner”), and an integral operator KpX defined by a kernel k (“outer”) and the marginal pX, the authors minimize a supervised loss over f∈H but apply K̂pX to f before matching labels. The representer theorem yields a solution equivalent to a standard kernel ridge/SVM using a data-dependent kernel, the Fredholm kernel: k̂F(x,z) = (l+u)−2 ∑i,j k(x,xi) kH(xi,xj) k(z,xj). A normalized variant addresses numerical issues for narrow Gaussian k. The paper argues that unlabeled data enters via K̂pX and induces a denoising effect under a “noise assumption”: low-variance directions in the marginal are uninformative for labels. Theory shows: (i) with linear outer k, k̂F reduces to an inner product with a covariance-like matrix ΣF that soft-thresholds low-variance directions (Theorem 2); (ii) in a signal+orthogonal Gaussian-noise model, Fredholm kernels (with Gaussian outer k and linear/Gaussian inner kH) are unbiased estimators of the “true” kernel on clean data but have lower variance than kernels computed directly on noisy observations (Theorems 3–4). Experiments on synthetic tasks violating the cluster assumption, text/sentiment with linear kernels, and digits with Gaussian kernels indicate benefits, particularly as artificial noise increases.

Relation to prior work
The formulation connects to integral operator methods and kernel mean embeddings, and is close to the Fredholm approach for density ratio estimation. The data-dependent kernel KF = K KHK⊤ is akin to “data-dependent kernels” for manifold regularization (Sindhwani et al.) and to diffusion/smoothing kernels on graphs (e.g., Kondor & Lafferty, 2002), though those are not cited. The “noise assumption” resonates with manifold+noise models and shrinkage in RKHS (Muandet et al.); the variance-reduction perspective is a useful addition.

Quality
- Positive: The algebraic reduction to a PSD, data-dependent kernel is clean (KF = K KHK⊤ ensures PSD if KH is PSD), giving a practical path to implementation with standard solvers. Theorems 2–4 are well-motivated and formalize denoising/shrinkage under Gaussian models, with unbiasedness and variance reduction clearly stated. The synthetic and real-data experiments, especially with increasing injected noise, empirically support the denoising claim.
- Concerns:
  - Finite-sample analysis is missing. All theory assumes known pX or infinite unlabeled data. There is no bound quantifying the effect of approximating KpX with K̂pX (labeled/unlabeled sample sizes, stability to u, concentration, and consequences for prediction risk).
  - The normalized kernel’s PSD property is asserted as “easy to check,” but not obvious: the per-argument normalization depends on x and z and typically breaks simple B A B⊤ factorization. A proof or a condition under which PSD holds is needed.
  - Computational scalability is not discussed. Building KH is O((l+u)2), and computing KF = K KHK⊤ is O(l2(l+u)) time and O((l+u)2) memory. Prediction requires O(l+u) per test point. How does this scale to modern u (10^5–10^6)? Any Nystrom/low-rank/approximation options?
  - The learning objective is only analyzed for square loss. The claim that an analogous SVM/hinge-loss derivation holds is deferred. For completeness, state the primal/dual and whether the same KF reduction applies under hinge loss (it should if the decision function is linear in training examples).
  - Experimental methodology lacks detail: sizes of labeled/unlabeled splits, variance across runs, and hyperparameter selection for baselines (TSVM, LapRLSC) matter greatly. Using a validation set for model selection is standard, but how many labeled examples were allocated to validation (and is this counted within the labeled budget)? Provide sensitivity to u and to kernel widths t,r.
  - Connections to diffusion kernels, anisotropic diffusion/graph-based normalization, and other data-dependent kernel constructions are under-cited; situating differences more explicitly would strengthen the novelty claim.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. The operator view, representer theorem reduction, and equivalence to a data-dependent kernel are explained succinctly. However:
- Precisely state H and kH assumptions (bounded kernels? separability?) for the representer theorem.
- Clarify inductive vs transductive use: the kernel depends on the unlabeled pool; how to handle new test-time unlabeled distributions or changing pX?
- Provide the missing proofs (especially PSD of the normalized version) and a brief derivation for hinge loss.
- The noise assumption section could benefit from a concrete local/noise model and an explicit discussion of when the assumption fails (e.g., when low-variance directions are label-informative).

Originality
The core idea—wrap a supervised RKHS learner with an integral operator built from unlabeled data, yielding a PSD data-dependent kernel—is a neat and compact unification. The exact KF = K KHK⊤ form and the variance-reduction theorems under a Gaussian orthogonal-noise model seem novel. That said, the construction is closely related to existing data-dependent and diffusion kernels; positioning the contribution relative to those (what is new beyond a different factorization and the stochastic denoising view?) would help.

Significance
If scaled and theoretically grounded in the finite-sample setting, the approach could be a practical, plug-in way to exploit unlabeled data beyond manifold/cluster assumptions, and the noise-suppression perspective is compelling. The empirical gains on noisy settings are promising. The potential impact is tempered by computational cost and the current lack of generalization guarantees.

Pros
- Simple, implementable mechanism to inject unlabeled information into standard kernel methods via a PSD, data-dependent kernel.
- Clear variance-reduction theory under an interpretable noise model; soft-thresholding PCA interpretation.
- Empirical improvements on noisy and some real-world tasks.

Cons
- No finite-sample/statistical guarantees for using K̂pX (dependence on u, bias-variance trade-offs).
- Scalability concerns and absence of approximation strategies.
- Normalized kernel PSD claim lacks proof; hinge-loss derivation omitted.
- Related work on diffusion/data-dependent kernels not fully acknowledged; novelty could be better delineated.
- Experimental details and ablations are insufficient to fully assess robustness.

Questions and suggestions
1) Provide finite-sample bounds: e.g., excess risk or kernel approximation error in terms of u, kernel bandwidths, and dimension; stability of KF to sampling noise.  
2) Prove PSD for the normalized version, or state conditions; otherwise, discuss using symmetric normalization that preserves PSD.  
3) Discuss computational strategies: Nystrom on KH and/or K, random features for k and kH, sparsification, or mini-batch approximations to KpX.  
4) Include hinge-loss derivation and results.  
5) Expand related work: diffusion kernels, anisotropic diffusion normalization, data-dependent kernel learning (e.g., Sindhwani et al. ICML’05/’06), and clarify differences.  
6) Add ablations: curves vs u; sensitivity to t and r; effect of mismatch where “low-variance” directions are predictive; robustness when pX is multimodal/non-Gaussian.  
7) Clarify inductive use: how to apply when test-time marginal differs (covariate shift)? Can one update K̂pX incrementally?

Overall: promising, elegant idea with supportive theory in an idealized setting and encouraging experiments, but requires stronger positioning, finite-sample analysis, and computational considerations to reach NeurIPS standard.
