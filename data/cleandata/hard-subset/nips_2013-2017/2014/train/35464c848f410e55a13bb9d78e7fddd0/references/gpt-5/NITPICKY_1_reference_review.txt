Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework derived from a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Given an “outer” kernel k (not required to be PSD) and an RKHS kernel kH (PSD), the predictor is c(x) = (Kp̂X f*)(x) where Kp̂X is an empirical integral operator constructed from unlabeled samples. The optimization with square loss leads (via the representer theorem) to a solution equivalent to a standard kernel method with a data-dependent kernel k̂F, whose l × l Gram matrix on labeled points is KF = Kl+u KH KTl+u (up to normalization), i.e., a “Fredholm kernel.” Proposition 1 states KF is PSD if KH is PSD. A normalized variant is proposed to mitigate numerical instability when using narrow Gaussian outer kernels.

The paper motivates a “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning: locally low-variance directions of pX are uninformative for labels and should be attenuated. The theory shows: (i) with linear outer kernel, the induced inner product rescales principal directions with a soft-thresholding effect akin to PCA (Theorem 2); (ii) under a Gaussian signal-plus-orthogonal-noise model, Fredholm kernels provide lower-variance estimators of the “true” kernel than directly evaluating the target kernel on noisy observations, for both linear and Gaussian targets (Theorems 3 and 4, with appropriate scaling). Experiments on synthetic datasets (constructed to violate the cluster assumption) and several real datasets (text, sentiment, and digits) suggest improvements over RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

Relation to prior work
The construction KF = K KH K^T falls squarely within data-dependent kernels and integral operator methods, closely related to manifold regularization and “data-dependent kernel” formulations (e.g., Sindhwani–Niyogi–Belkin, 2005/2006), kernel mean embeddings/conditional mean operators, and convolution/smoothing of kernels with pX (diffusion kernels, anisotropic/normalized kernels). The connection to density-ratio Fredholm formulations (Que & Belkin) is apt. However, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate from existing data-dependent kernels built from unlabeled data (e.g., K (I + ηL)−1 K^T, diffusion-based normalizations) nor compare empirically to them.

Strengths
- Clean, simple formulation with an attractive interpretation: a two-layer convolution of kernels with pX, yielding a PSD, data-dependent kernel that incorporates unlabeled data.
- The connection between linear outer kernels and PCA-like soft-thresholding is insightful and aligns with the stated noise assumption.
- Variance reduction results under stylized Gaussian models are plausible and help explain when/why unlabeled data can denoise kernel evaluations.
- Implementation is straightforward given standard kernel machinery.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Key matrix identity appears incorrect as stated. The paper claims (KTK KH + λI)−1 KT = KT (K KH KT + λI)−1. In general, (A^T A + λI)−1 A^T = A^T (A A^T + λI)−1 holds for A, but matching requires A = K KH1/2; the printed identity omits KH1/2 factors. The equivalence to α = (KF + λI)−1 y is likely true via a correct derivation, but the presented identity is misleading.
- Scaling inconsistencies:
  - k̂F includes 1/(l+u)^2, but this factor is not consistently propagated in subsequent formulas (e.g., c*(x)). The δ-kernel reduction to standard RKLS also ignores necessary rescaling of λ.
  - In Theorem 2, the rescaling factor alternates between denominators 2σ2 + t and σ2 + t; later a square root appears. These inconsistencies undermine clarity.
- Overreliance on “full version.” Multiple essential components (proofs, hinge-loss extension, representer derivation) are deferred. NeurIPS requires a self-contained submission (supplementary allowed, but core derivations should be present).
- The “noise assumption” is intuitive but never formalized precisely (e.g., as a conditional independence statement or smoothness constraint w.r.t. local covariance). Its relationship to manifold learning and diffusion kernels (which also downweight low-density/noisy directions via normalization) is not rigorously contrasted.
- Theory is limited to infinite-unlabeled Gaussian models and does not quantify finite-sample effects. There are no bounds on the approximation error from replacing integrals with empirical sums, nor generalization bounds for data-dependent kernels (e.g., stability or (transductive) Rademacher complexity).
- Positive-definiteness and symmetry of the normalized Fredholm kernel are asserted as “easy to check” but not proven. Given the per-point normalizations, a concise matrix argument (K̃ = D−1 K, KF = K̃ KH K̃^T) would help.
- Computational aspects are not discussed: memory/time complexities scale with l+u; forming KF = K KH K^T is O((l+u)^3) naively. No discussion of Nyström/low-rank approximations or sparsity.
- Experimental details are insufficient:
  - The paper references figures and tables; in the submission excerpt they are missing. Parameter selection (“validation set”) is under-specified given few labels. It is unclear whether unlabeled test points are used (transductive vs inductive setting), which affects fairness.
  - No error bars, multiple random splits, or statistical significance. No ablations (effect of u, normalization, outer vs inner kernels, sensitivity to t and λ).
  - Baselines are limited (TSVM, LapRLSC); no comparison to other data-dependent kernels or modern semi-supervised baselines.
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “distint,” “senario,” “kernle,” “unormalized,” inconsistent notation (p vs pX), repeated/misspelled author names in references, missing years/venues, and unpolished reference formatting. These detract from clarity.

Significance
The idea of forming KF = K KH K^T as a data-dependent kernel that averages/smooths kernel evaluations with respect to pX is natural and potentially useful, but closely related constructions exist in the literature. The theoretical insights about variance reduction and PCA-like reweighting are nice, yet restricted to narrow models and lack finite-sample analysis. The empirical section, as presented, is too thin to convincingly establish practical impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the linear algebra identity and present a correct, self-contained derivation of the equivalence to kernel ridge regression with KF (and hinge-loss extension if claimed).
- Clean up scaling/normalization factors throughout; explicitly address the δ-kernel reduction and how λ should be rescaled.
- Formalize the noise assumption and clarify its relationship to manifold/diffusion kernels and prior data-dependent kernel methods. Expand related work (e.g., Sindhwani–Niyogi–Belkin data-dependent kernels, diffusion kernel normalization).
- Add finite-sample analyses: concentration for empirical Kp̂X, stability/generalization with data-dependent kernels, and computational strategies (Nyström).
- Strengthen experiments: clear inductive vs transductive protocol; multiple splits with error bars; ablations (u, normalization, kernel choices); runtime/memory; comparisons to additional semi-supervised methods (including other data-dependent kernels).
- Thorough proofreading and complete, correctly formatted references.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the core idea is appealing and the paper contains interesting insights, the submission has too many technical inaccuracies, inconsistencies, and missing details (both theoretical and experimental) for acceptance. With corrected derivations, tighter theory, and stronger, reproducible empirical evaluation, this work could be competitive in a future submission.
