This paper introduces a novel learning framework based on solving Fredholm integral equations, termed "Fredholm kernels." The core idea is to formulate supervised and semi-supervised learning as an inverse problem and leverage regularization techniques to solve it, naturally incorporating unlabeled data. The authors connect this formulation to existing kernel methods, demonstrating it as a kernel method with a data-dependent kernel. They further explore the “noise assumption” in semi-supervised learning and provide theoretical results showing performance improvements under this assumption, alongside empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, framework, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. The connection to existing kernel methods and integral operator approaches (e.g., [10, 12, 11]) is effectively established. The introduction of the "noise assumption" offers a fresh perspective on semi-supervised learning, complementing existing assumptions like the manifold and cluster assumptions. The theoretical results (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) provide valuable insights into the behavior of Fredholm kernels under specific conditions, particularly regarding noise suppression. The experiments are comprehensive, covering both synthetic datasets designed to highlight the noise assumption and real-world datasets from various domains.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while insightful, relies on specific assumptions (e.g., Gaussian distributions, linear kernels) and may not generalize readily to more complex scenarios. The derivation of the theoretical results, while referenced to a "full version," would benefit from more detailed explanations within the main manuscript, especially the connection between the assumptions and the observed results. The notation could be improved for clarity, particularly in equations involving the Fredholm kernel. While the authors acknowledge the similarity to existing work like [13], a more thorough discussion of the specific differences and advantages of the proposed approach compared to these alternatives would strengthen the contribution. The experimental section, while extensive, lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the framework. Specifically, a comparative analysis of the performance gains attributable solely to the Fredholm formulation versus those arising from specific kernel choices (e.g., Gaussian vs. linear) would be insightful. The choice of parameters for the experiments, especially the selection of the kernel width `t` in the Gaussian kernel settings, isn’t fully justified. The "full version" is repeatedly referenced, creating a barrier to fully understanding the results presented in the main manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation and the resulting "Fredholm kernels" offer a fresh perspective on kernel methods and semi-supervised learning.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating the potential benefits of Fredholm kernels under specific conditions, particularly regarding noise suppression.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the core ideas and methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Generalizability of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions, potentially limiting the applicability of the results.
*   **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** The experimental section lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components.
*   **Heavy Reliance on “Full Version”:** Critical details are deferred to the "full version," hindering a complete understanding of the work.
*   **Insufficient Comparative Analysis:** A more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those with similar goals, is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning. While the theoretical results have limitations and the experimental section could be strengthened, the overall approach is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by empirical evidence. I believe the paper is of sufficient quality to be accepted, but with the caveat that the authors should address the weaknesses identified above in a revised version, particularly by providing more details in the main manuscript and conducting more thorough ablation studies. A strong recommendation is to include more details of the theorems in the main paper, to allow for better assessment.
