

# Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper proposes a novel framework for supervised and unsupervised learning based on solving Fredholm integral equations of the first kind, introducing what the authors call "Fredholm kernels." The main idea is to formulate learning as an inverse problem that naturally incorporates unlabeled data, resulting in a data-dependent kernel that can suppress noise. The paper makes several claims about the theoretical properties and empirical performance of this approach.

## Strengths
The paper presents a mathematically interesting framework that connects kernel methods with integral equations. The theoretical analysis of noise suppression via Fredholm kernels (particularly in Sections 4.1 and 4.2) is novel and well-motivated. The experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate promising performance, especially in scenarios where noise is present.

## Weaknesses and Minor Flaws

1. **Technical Errors**: The paper contains several mathematical errors that undermine the credibility of the work. Equation (2) should be "f* = arg min_f∈H (1/l)∑_{i=1}^l ((K̂_pX f)(x_i)-y_i)^2 + λ‖f‖_H^2" but is written as "l l∑ i=1" which is clearly a typo. Similarly, in Equation (4), the summation should be "∑_{i,j=1}^{l+u}" but is written as "l+u∑ i,j=1".

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the integral operator. It defines K_pX in Equation (1) but later uses K̂_pX in Equation (2) without explaining the difference, creating confusion.

3. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper claims that "Fredholm kernel was derived using L2 loss here, it could also be derived when hinge loss is used" but provides no justification or reference for this claim. Theoretical results for hinge loss would significantly strengthen the paper.

4. **Theoretical Gaps**: The claim that "the Fredholm kernel k̂_NF is still symmetric positive semi-definite" is made without proof. This is a critical property for kernel methods, and the absence of a proof is problematic.

5. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper has inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "σ 4 i 2σ2i+t" should be "σ^4_i / (2σ^2_i + t)"). The reference to "Section 4" in the introduction is misleading since the paper only has sections 1-5.

6. **Experimental Details**: The paper claims "clear improvement" of Fredholm kernels over other methods in Tables 1-2 but provides no statistical significance testing. The synthetic examples in Figure 2 are described as violating the cluster assumption, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how the examples violate this assumption.

7. **Reference Issues**: The paper cites [13] as "somewhat resembling the approach taken in [13]" but doesn't provide a full reference for [13] in the references section. The paper also claims the term "Fredholm Kernel" has been used in mathematics ([8], page 103) and [14], but these references are missing from the provided reference list.

8. **Theoretical Claim Overreach**: The paper claims that "Fredholm kernels perform well on synthetic examples designed to illustrate the noise assumption as well as on a number of real-world datasets," but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to identify which components of the Fredholm kernel are most important for performance.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting framework with potential theoretical contributions, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, unsubstantiated claims, and missing proofs significantly undermine its credibility. The minor flaws identified above are not trivial; they represent fundamental issues with the presentation and theoretical foundation of the work. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, even though the core idea has some merit.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to fix the mathematical errors, provide missing proofs, and clarify the theoretical claims. Without these corrections, the paper fails to meet the technical standards expected at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting to a venue with higher standards for technical rigor.

I recommend rejection.
