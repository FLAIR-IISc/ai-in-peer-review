This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and unsupervised learning based on solving Fredholm integral equations. The core idea is to formulate learning as an inverse problem and leverage regularization techniques to handle it, leading to what the authors call “Fredholm kernels”. The framework naturally incorporates both labeled and unlabeled data and can be re-expressed as a kernel method with a data-dependent kernel. The paper provides theoretical analysis under a “noise assumption” and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its formulation and derivation of the Fredholm kernel. The connection to existing kernel methods is clearly established, and the representer theorem is appropriately used to obtain a computationally tractable solution. The theoretical results regarding noise suppression, particularly Theorems 3 and 4, are interesting and provide insights into the potential benefits of this approach. However, the theoretical analysis feels somewhat limited in scope. The assumptions made (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific kernel choices) restrict the generality of the results. The proofs are referenced to a "full version," making it difficult to fully assess their rigor within the scope of the review. The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of different components of the Fredholm kernel. The claim of better performance requires more robust statistical testing. The paper acknowledges limitations but doesn't thoroughly explore the impact of those limitations on the broader applicability of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work and outlines the main contributions. The mathematical notation is reasonably consistent, although some equations could benefit from more detailed explanations. The connection between the Fredholm formulation and standard kernel methods is well-explained. However, the discussion of the "noise assumption" feels somewhat intuitive rather than rigorously defined. The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts, but could be enhanced with clearer labels and explanations. The numerous references, while demonstrating awareness of related work, can be overwhelming. A more focused discussion of the most relevant papers would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The idea of framing learning as a Fredholm integral equation and the resulting Fredholm kernel are novel. While related work exists on integral operators and kernel methods (references [10], [5-7], [9]), this specific formulation and its application to semi-supervised learning with a focus on noise suppression appear to be new. The noise assumption, while intuitive, is presented as a distinct perspective within the semi-supervised learning landscape. The derivation of the data-dependent Fredholm kernel, and the analysis of its properties, contribute to the originality of the work. However, the connection to other data-dependent kernel approaches, such as those discussed in [13], could be more thoroughly explored to better position the contribution.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work lies in its ability to effectively leverage unlabeled data, particularly in noisy scenarios. The theoretical results, if broadly applicable, could provide a principled justification for using unlabeled data in semi-supervised learning. The empirical results demonstrate promising performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, suggesting that the Fredholm kernel can be a valuable addition to the toolbox of machine learning methods. However, the practical impact remains to be seen. The computational complexity of the Fredholm kernel, particularly for large datasets, needs to be considered. Whether the performance gains justify the added computational cost will be a key factor in determining its widespread adoption. The method could be particularly valuable in applications where noise is a significant concern, such as medical imaging or financial modeling.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Development of the Fredholm kernel, a data-dependent kernel with potential advantages for semi-supervised learning.
*   Theoretical analysis demonstrating noise suppression properties.
*   Empirical validation on diverse datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions and limited scope.
*   Empirical evaluation lacks a thorough ablation study and robust statistical testing.
*   The “noise assumption” is not rigorously defined.
*   Computational complexity and scalability are not fully addressed.
*   Connection to existing data-dependent kernel methods could be stronger.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning. The core idea of formulating learning as a Fredholm integral equation is novel, and the resulting Fredholm kernel shows promising results. However, the theoretical analysis is somewhat limited, and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive. While not a definitive breakthrough, the paper offers a valuable new perspective on semi-supervised learning and warrants acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above, particularly by providing more rigorous theoretical justification and a more thorough empirical evaluation. Specifically, extending the theoretical results to more general distributions and kernel choices would be highly beneficial. The full version of the paper should be made available to reviewers.
