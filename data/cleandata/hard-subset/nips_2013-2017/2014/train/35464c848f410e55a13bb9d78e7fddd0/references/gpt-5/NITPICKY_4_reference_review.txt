Summary
This paper proposes “Fredholm kernels,” a data-dependent kernel constructed by solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The approach introduces an operator KpX that averages an “outer” kernel k(x, z) against the marginal pX and composes it with an “inner” RKHS kernel kH. The empirical operator uses unlabeled data. The learning objective is kernel ridge regression on the transformed function Kp̂X f. Using the representer theorem, the solution reduces to kernel ridge regression on a new kernel matrix KF = K KHK⊤, where K is the (l × (l+u)) matrix of outer-kernel evaluations between labeled and all (labeled+unlabeled) points. The resulting Fredholm kernel k̂F(x, z) equals a data-dependent triple sum/integral of k-kH-k terms; a normalized variant is proposed for stability.

The paper motivates semi-supervised use via a “noise assumption”: locally low-variance directions are label-irrelevant noise. Theory for Gaussian models claims (i) with linear outer kernel and Gaussian inner kernel, kF reweights principal directions with a “soft threshold” effect; (ii) under a latent-subspace-with-orthogonal-noise model, kF provides unbiased estimates of target linear/Gaussian kernels with reduced variance versus naïve kernels. Experiments on synthetic data violating the cluster assumption and on text and digit datasets suggest gains over RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

Strengths
- Simple, appealing construction: Fredholm learning reduces to kernel ridge with a PSD, data-dependent kernel KF = K KHK⊤. The PSD claim is correct if KH is PSD.
- Semi-supervised use is natural: unlabeled data only enter via K and the empirical operator.
- The “noise assumption” provides a complementary perspective to cluster/manifold assumptions.
- The theoretical Gaussian-model results (variance reduction; PCA-like reweighting) are insightful and align with the intuition of denoising via local averaging.
- Practical instantiations (FredLin1/2, FredGauss) are easy to implement on top of existing KRR/SVM pipelines.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Quality/technical soundness:
- The key algebraic identity “(K⊤K KH + λI)−1K⊤ = K⊤(KKHK⊤ + λI)−1” is stated as “easily verified” but is not correct as written without additional structure. A correct relation follows by setting α = (KKHK⊤ + λI)−1y and v = K⊤α, which implies v solves (K⊤K KH + λI)v = K⊤y. Please fix the statement and provide a derivation; as written it is misleading.
- Representer theorem: the loss depends on (Kp̂X f)(xi), not on pointwise evaluations f(xi). A proof (or citation) that the representer theorem applies in this composition setting is deferred to the “full version.” This is critical and must be included.
- Scaling inconsistencies: The empirical operator includes a factor 1/(l+u), but the matrix definitions (K, KF) and Eq. (3)-(4) sometimes omit or move these factors. Similarly, the normalized kernel Eq. (5) changes scaling again. Carefully unify scaling throughout; otherwise KF and k̂F are ambiguous.
- Notation collision: λ denotes the KRR regularization in Eq. (2) and the signal standard deviation/scale in the Gaussian-model theory of Section 4. Please disambiguate (e.g., use λreg for regularization, λsig for variance) to avoid confusion.
- Theorem 2: the “rescaling factor” is alternately written with and without a square root; check and correct. Also, a global multiplicative factor of ΣF is introduced via a product over dimensions; its role in reweighting should be clarified (does it cancel via normalization?).
- The normalized kernel (5): denominators can be near-zero with narrow Gaussian k; practical stabilization (e.g., ε-regularization) is not discussed. Positive semidefiniteness for k̂NF is claimed without proof.
- Continuous formulations (6)-(7) assume independence p(u)p(v); formally, these correspond to composing two integral operators. This is fine, but the paper should state assumptions clearly and justify interchange of integrals and kernels.
- No statistical learning bounds: there is no generalization analysis quantifying when unlabeled data help (beyond Gaussian toy models). Existing SSL theory (e.g., assumptions linking pX and p(y|x)) is not engaged beyond informal narrative.
- Computational complexity is not addressed: forming KF = K KHK⊤ costs O(l(l+u)2) time and requires O((l+u)2) storage for KH; test-time evaluation of k̂F(x, xs) is O(l+u) per s, hence O(l(l+u)) per test point unless precomputation is used. This may be prohibitive when u is large.

Clarity/presentation:
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors: “distint,” “on on,” “senario,” “kernle,” “unormalized,” “SteinJames,” etc.
- Figures and tables referenced (Figure 1–2, Tables 1–6) are missing. This prevents evaluation of empirical claims.
- Many proofs are deferred to a “full version”; NeurIPS requires self-contained submissions.
- References are incomplete and sometimes mismatched (e.g., [8] is cited as a math Fredholm reference but the bibliography entry corresponds to a kernel mean shrinkage preprint; many entries lack years/venues). Ensure correct, complete bibliographic data and numbering.
- The “outer kernel need not be PSD or symmetric” statement is fine for KF, but clarify whether k̂F(x, z) is a Mercer kernel over arbitrary sets that include new test points when the unlabeled set is fixed.

Originality:
- The data-dependent-kernel perspective is known (e.g., manifold regularization/data-dependent kernels, convolution/smoothing of kernels, conditional mean embeddings/shrinkage estimators). The specific composition KF = K KHK⊤ is clean, but the connection to prior data-dependent kernels (e.g., Sindhwani et al. 2005) and kernel smoothing (Nadaraya–Watson-style averaging in RKHS) should be discussed in more depth to delineate novelty.

Significance:
- Potentially useful in practice as a simple semi-supervised plug-in kernel with denoising behavior. However, the current submission lacks essential proofs, coherent notation, computational discussion, and complete experiments, which limits confidence in the contribution.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, general framework; PSD guarantee via KH.
- Intuitive and theoretically supported denoising/variance-reduction under Gaussian models.
- Natural semi-supervised use of unlabeled data via Kp̂X.
- Practical instantiations likely easy to adopt.

Cons
- Missing proofs and reliance on a “full version.”
- Algebraic identity misstated; multiple notation/scaling inconsistencies.
- No complexity analysis; potential scalability issues.
- Vague “noise assumption” (global Gaussian in theory vs. local statement in intro).
- Incomplete references; numerous typos; missing figures/tables.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The idea is promising and could be impactful, but the paper needs substantial revision to fix algebra/notation, include full proofs, provide a clear and stable definition of the kernels with consistent scaling, add complexity analysis and practical stabilization, and include complete experimental results and references.
