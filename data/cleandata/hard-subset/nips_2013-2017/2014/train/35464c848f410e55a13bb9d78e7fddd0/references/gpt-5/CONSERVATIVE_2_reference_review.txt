Summary
The paper formulates supervised/semi-supervised learning as a regularized inverse problem for a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Starting from the operator K_pX f(x) = ∫ k(x,z) f(z) pX(z) dz, the authors propose minimizing empirical square loss on K_p̂X f with RKHS regularization. By a representer argument, this induces a data-dependent kernel k̂F(x,z) = (1/(l+u)^2) ∑_{i,j} k(x,xi) kH(xi,xj) k(z,xj), with KH PSD; a normalized variant is also proposed. They argue unlabeled data enters via p̂X and can denoise learning. Theoretically, under Gaussian models and with infinite unlabeled data, they show: (i) with linear outer kernel and Gaussian inner, kF corresponds to an inner product that “soft-thresholds” low-variance principal directions; (ii) in a model with signal in a d-dimensional subspace and orthogonal Gaussian noise, both linear and Gaussian target kernels can be better estimated (unbiased with reduced variance) by kF than by the target kernel on noisy observations. Empirically, they report gains on synthetic datasets violating the cluster assumption and on several text and vision benchmarks compared to RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is closely related to integral operator methods and kernel mean embeddings, where data-dependent operators built from pX are central. The induced kernel kF can be seen as composing integral operators (K* KH K), reminiscent of diffusion kernels/anisotropic diffusion and data-dependent kernels in manifold regularization and semi-supervised kernel learning (e.g., Sindhwani–Niyogi–Belkin; diffusion maps; Cortes/Mohri data-dependent kernels). It also resembles Haussler-style convolution/smoothing of kernels against pX. The connection to density ratio estimation via Fredholm equations (Que & Belkin) is noted by the authors.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: shows that a regularized Fredholm equation leads to a standard kernel method with a data-dependent kernel that leverages unlabeled data.
- Theoretical intuition under clear (if strong) generative assumptions: “soft-thresholding PCA” view and variance-reduction results are informative and align with the proposed “noise assumption.”
- The induced kernel is PSD if KH is PSD; the algebraic reduction to a standard kernel machine is useful for implementation.
- Empirical evaluation includes both synthetic settings tailored to the assumption and several real datasets.

Weaknesses
- Finite-sample guarantees are missing. Key theory assumes infinite unlabeled data or exact knowledge of pX. There are no bounds quantifying the effect of approximating K_pX with K_p̂X, nor generalization bounds comparing excess risk with/without unlabeled data under the noise assumption.
- Normalized kernel PSD claim is asserted but not substantiated. The PSD of k̂NF is “easy to check” but not proved; this is nontrivial given the per-input normalizations.
- Methodological novelty appears incremental relative to established data-dependent kernels and operator compositions. The kernel kF is essentially K^T KH K constructed from unlabeled data; connections to diffusion kernels, anisotropic kernels, Laplacian-based data-dependent kernels, and convolution kernels should be made explicit, with clear distinctions.
- Experimental evidence is thin for a new paradigm: only a few baselines (RLSC, TSVM, LapRLSC) are considered. Several strong semi-supervised kernel baselines (label propagation/harmonic functions, Laplacian SVM, diffusion kernels, graph-based anisotropic kernels, data-dependent kernel learning) are missing. No statistical significance or variability is reported, and details (hyperparameter search spaces, computational cost) are sparse.
- Scalability concerns are not addressed. Computing kF naively is O((l+u)^2) kernel evaluations and storage, and training requires inverting l×l systems involving KF; this limits applicability with large unlabeled sets. There is no discussion of Nyström/low-rank approximations or sparsification.
- Practical guidance is limited. Sensitivity to inner/outer kernels and their bandwidths, choice between normalized vs unnormalized forms, and robustness to mis-specified noise are not systematically explored. Hinge-loss/SVM variant is mentioned but not demonstrated.
- The “noise assumption” overlaps with established manifold+noise and anisotropic diffusion perspectives; its positioning as a distinct assumption would benefit from tighter formalization and empirical diagnostics to check when it holds.

Quality
Technically, the algebraic derivations are sound, and the variance-reduction calculations (as stated) are plausible under the Gaussian models. However, several important claims are deferred to a “full version” (proofs, representer derivation, PSD of normalized kernel), which weakens the submission. The lack of finite-sample analysis and missing baselines limit the evidential support.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured. Some notational choices (k vs kH, normalized/unormalized kernels) could be tightened, and the normalized kernel PSD claim should be proved or carefully referenced. More implementation details (regularization, parameter ranges, computational complexity) are needed for reproducibility.

Originality
Recasting the learning objective as a Fredholm equation and deriving kF is a neat perspective, but the resulting kernel is close to known operator compositions/data-dependent kernels. The “noise assumption” is a rephrasing of manifold+noise/low-variance irrelevance used in local PCA-based and anisotropic kernels; the contribution would benefit from clearer differentiation.

Significance
Potentially useful as a unifying view, but without stronger theoretical guarantees (finite-sample, generalization with unlabeled data) and broader empirical validation against established data-dependent kernels, the impact is uncertain.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide finite-sample bounds quantifying ||k̂F − kF|| and downstream risk improvements as a function of u, l, and kernel parameters under the noise assumption.
- Prove PSD of the normalized kernel or state precise conditions under which it holds.
- Compare against diffusion kernels/anisotropic kernels, label propagation, Laplacian SVM, and data-dependent kernel learning methods; include ablations (inner vs outer kernels, normalization).
- Report computational complexity and memory, and demonstrate scalable approximations (Nyström/Random Features) for kF.
- Study sensitivity to unlabeled sample size and to violations of the noise assumption; when does unlabeled data hurt?
- Include the hinge-loss/SVM variant results.

Pros
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation; simple to implement as a kernel method.
- Insightful variance-reduction analysis under a transparent model.
- Demonstrates how unlabeled data can denoise standard kernels.

Cons
- Key proofs and finite-sample analysis missing; reliance on strong assumptions.
- Limited and dated baselines; no scalability discussion.
- Normalization PSD claim unsubstantiated.
- Overlap with existing data-dependent kernels insufficiently acknowledged.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the submission lacks the theoretical and empirical rigor needed to justify adoption over established data-dependent kernels. Strengthening finite-sample theory, clarifying novelty, and broadening/scaling experiments would substantially improve the work. Confidence: high.
