- Proposes a semi-supervised framework casting learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation and derives a data-dependent "Fredholm kernel" k̂F that yields an equivalent PSD kernel method.  
- Introduces a normalized variant to mitigate numerical instability.  
- States a "noise assumption": low-variance directions of pX are uninformative for labels and should be suppressed.  
- Theoretical results: (i) linear outer kernel gives reweighted principal directions and a soft-thresholding effect under Gaussian unlabeled data (Theorem 2).  
- Theoretical results: (ii) under subspace+orthogonal Gaussian noise, Fredholm kernels produce unbiased (up to constant) estimators with strictly smaller variance than naive noisy kernels (Theorems 3–4).  
- Empirically, synthetic experiments match theory and show clear improvements; real-data results are promising, especially with added noise.  
- Connects to prior work on Fredholm equations, kernel mean embeddings, data-dependent kernels, diffusion-style kernels, and two-step smoothing.  
- PSD property and equivalence to a standard kernel method are correct and useful.  
- Variance-reduction analysis is plausible and insightful but relies on strong, idealized assumptions (single Gaussian, subspace+Gaussian noise, exact pX/infinite unlabeled data).  
- Lacks finite-sample concentration/generalization analysis and proofs are deferred to a full version.  
- Real-data experiments lack detail (l and u, hyperparameter selection, variance across runs, computational costs).  
- Important baselines (PCA de-noising, diffusion kernels, other data-dependent kernels) are missing; TSVM/LapRLSC may be outdated.  
- Computational cost and scalability are unaddressed; forming K_H and KF has O((l+u)^2) memory and O((l+u)^3) time.  
- No discussion of approximation schemes (Nyström, sparsification, inducing points) for large u.  
- Exposition is generally clear and well organized; notation is heavy but standard.  
- Missing essential details: proofs, hinge-loss/SVM variant, numerical stability techniques, parameterization of constants in theorems.  
- Originality: neat framing and tri-factorized kernel form; variance-reduction analysis is the most original technical contribution, but approach is close to existing data-dependent kernels.  
- Significance: provides a simple mechanism to use unlabeled data and theoretical lens for when it helps; moderate impact without scalability and finite-sample support.  
- Pros: simple general construction, PSD data-dependent kernel, clear intuition/theory for suppressing low-variance directions, complementary to manifold methods, aligned empirical results under noise.  
- Cons: relies on infinite-data assumptions, missing finite-sample theory, scalability/approximation omissions, limited baselines, partial formalization of noise assumption, many details deferred.  
- Questions for authors: provide finite-sample concentration bounds, practical complexity scaling and approximation options, sensitivity to pX mismatch or informative low-variance directions, and hinge-loss/SVM performance.  
- Suggestions: add finite-sample/operator-norm analysis, include computational strategies and scaling plots, broaden baselines to diffusion/PCA de-noising, run ablations on kernels/normalization/u/bandwidth, and clarify constants and implementation details.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — promising, but needs stronger empirical baselines, scalability discussion, and finite-sample guarantees.
