Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework based on a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The key idea is to consider predictors of the form KpX f, where KpX is an integral operator with kernel k integrated against the unlabeled marginal pX. Regularized empirical risk minimization with square loss in the RKHS H of an “inner” kernel kH yields, via a representer theorem, a solution equivalent to kernel ridge regression with a new data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F(x, z) = (l+u)−2 ∑i,j k(x, xi) kH(xi, xj) k(z, xj). This formulation naturally incorporates unlabeled data through the empirical p̂X in Kp̂X. The authors show k̂F is PSD if KH is PSD and also introduce a normalized variant for stability.

They motivate semi-supervised gains via a “noise assumption”: low-variance directions in the unlabeled distribution are uninformative for labels and should be suppressed. Theoretical results analyze two views: (i) with linear “outer” k and Gaussian inner kH under Gaussian pX, the induced kernel corresponds to an inner product with a diagonal rescaling, effectively soft-thresholding principal components; (ii) with Gaussian noise orthogonal to a d-dimensional signal subspace, Fredholm kernels (with Gaussian k and kH) yield unbiased estimators of target linear/Gaussian kernels with reduced variance relative to using the noisy data directly, under parameter choices and λ > σ. Empirically, the method improves on synthetic settings designed to violate cluster assumptions and shows competitive performance on text, sentiment, and digit datasets compared to RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

This connects to prior integral operator methods and kernel mean embeddings (e.g., Gretton/Fukumizu/Sriperumbudur et al.), the Fredholm approach to covariate shift [10], shrinkage in RKHS means [9], and data-dependent kernels/manifold regularization [1,13]. The paper’s contribution is a unified Fredholm formulation that yields a PSD data-dependent kernel leveraging unlabeled data and selective noise suppression.

Quality
- Positive: The reduction to a PSD data-dependent kernel is clean, and the operator identity relating solutions is correct. The PSD proof sketch (via KH PSD) is plausible. The theory provides transparent intuition: soft-thresholded PCA for the linear outer kernel, and variance reduction for linear and Gaussian targets under a Gaussian mixture model with additive orthogonal noise. The empirical results span synthetic and several real datasets, and the normalized variant addresses stability concerns.
- Concerns:
  - Assumptions are strong: infinite unlabeled data (population pX), Gaussian pX and additive Gaussian noise in an orthogonal complement, and specific parameter couplings (e.g., t solving a cubic relation). The variance reduction theorems require λ > σ and unbiasedness only up to constants.
  - No finite-sample analysis: there is no bound on the approximation error between k̂F computed from u unlabeled samples and its population limit, nor on the induced excess risk. Concentration for the triple kernel composition or operator norm deviations would strengthen the claims.
  - Computational complexity and scalability are not discussed: constructing KF = Kl+u KH Kl+u^T requires O((l+u)^2) memory and O(l (l+u)^2) flops, which can be prohibitive for large u typical of semi-supervised regimes. Practical approximations (Nyström, inducing points, sparsifying k, block structure) are not explored.
  - Experimental details are insufficient in the current write-up: tables are referenced but not shown; it is unclear how many unlabeled points are used, whether gains persist across random splits, and whether improvements are statistically significant. Baselines that directly implement “denoise then learn” (e.g., PCA using unlabeled data followed by supervised RLSC/SVM) or diffusion/data-dependent kernels beyond LapRLSC would be informative. Ablations varying u and t, and inner/outer kernel choices, would clarify when unlabeled data helps/hurts.
  - The “noise assumption” is reasonable but lacks diagnostics: there is no measure to detect when low-variance directions are label-irrelevant, nor a discussion of failure modes (e.g., when labels vary along low-variance directions).

Clarity
The core derivation and high-level intuition are clear. The representer form (Eq. 3) and the Fredholm kernel definition are easy to follow. However, the paper frequently defers proofs and details to a “full version”; for NeurIPS, more self-contained exposition is desirable. Please add:
- A brief proof of PSD for the normalized kernel variant.
- Discussion of computational complexity and memory, with guidance on approximations.
- Clear experimental protocols (splits, u values, hyperparameter search ranges) and full tables/figures.

Originality
Formulating semi-supervised learning via a Fredholm integral and deriving a PSD data-dependent kernel that composes outer and inner kernels is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel synthesis. While related to kernel mean embeddings, shrinkage, and manifold/data-dependent kernels, the specific composition and the variance-reduction analysis under the noise model are new and insightful.

Significance
If the empirical gains are robust and the method can be scaled, this is a useful addition to the semi-supervised toolkit, offering a simple drop-in kernel that leverages unlabeled data without explicit graph construction. The theoretical intuition about suppressing low-variance directions is broadly relevant. However, significance would be strengthened by finite-sample/generalization analysis and comparisons to strong denoising baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general formulation; yields a PSD, data-dependent kernel usable with standard solvers.
- Clear intuition and theory demonstrating variance reduction under a sensible noise model.
- Normalized variant for stability; empirical results suggest practicality across domains.
- Bridges integral operator viewpoints with semi-supervised learning in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance
- Theory relies on restrictive Gaussian/population assumptions; lacks finite-sample bounds.
- Scalability concerns not addressed; complexity may limit applicability when u is large.
- Experimental section (as presented) lacks detail, strong baselines, and ablations; effect of u and parameter sensitivity not systematically studied.
- Noise assumption not operationalized; failure modes not explored.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample guarantees bounding ||k̂F − kF|| (e.g., in Hilbert–Schmidt/operator norm) and propagate to excess risk via kernel ridge regression analysis.
- Analyze and report computational and memory complexity; propose and evaluate approximations (Nyström on KH and/or k, sparse neighborhoods for the outer k, mini-batch approximations).
- Add baselines: PCA/autoencoder denoising using unlabeled data followed by supervised learning; diffusion/double-smoothing kernels; other data-dependent kernels.
- Perform ablations: vary u systematically; study sensitivity to t and kernel choices; compare normalized vs unnormalized; include regimes where the noise assumption is violated.
- Clarify the relation to kernel mean embedding operators and diffusion kernels to better position the contribution.
- Include guidance for selecting inner/outer kernels based on domain characteristics.

Recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning weak accept if the supplementary convincingly addresses proofs, scalability, and experimental details). The idea is elegant and potentially impactful, but the current version needs stronger finite-sample analysis, scalability considerations, and more thorough empirical validation to meet the NeurIPS standard.
