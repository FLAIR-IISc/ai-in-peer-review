Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework by casting learning as a (regularized) Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This leads to a data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F that incorporates unlabeled data via an integral operator KpX built from an “outer” kernel k and an “inner” supervised kernel kH. The authors show that the resulting learning algorithm reduces to a standard kernel method with kernel matrix KF = Kl+u KH KTl+u (and a normalized variant), enabling unlabeled data to modulate the geometry seen by the supervised learner. They motivate this with a “noise assumption” (low-variance directions are label-uninformative) and provide analyses showing (i) for linear outer k, the induced inner product reweights principal directions with a soft thresholding effect; (ii) under a manifold+orthogonal-noise Gaussian model, the Fredholm kernel offers unbiased (up to scaling) but lower-variance estimates of the “true” linear or Gaussian target kernel than simply using the noisy observations. Empirically, on synthetic datasets violating the cluster assumption and several real tasks (text classification/sentiment with linear kernels; digits with Gaussian kernels), Fredholm kernels outperform supervised RLSC and often TSVM/LapRLSC, with larger gains under added noise.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to a broad literature on kernel integral operators and RKHS embeddings [5–7], and closely to the Fredholm-equation approach to density-ratio estimation [10]. It is also related to data-dependent/semi-supervised kernels, including manifold regularization and graph Laplacian methods [1,13], and shrinkage estimators in RKHS [9]. The presented kernel k̂F resembles a smoothed or convolved kernel that integrates over pX; there are conceptual links to diffusion kernels/heat kernels on data graphs and data-dependent kernels constructed from unlabeled data. The paper positions itself as leveraging the Fredholm viewpoint to derive a new, simple-to-use PSD kernel that naturally incorporates unlabeled data and is geared toward noise suppression rather than cluster/manifold smoothness.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Casting semi-supervised learning as a Fredholm inverse problem and deriving a concrete, PSD, data-dependent kernel that can drop into standard KRR/SVM pipelines is elegant and broadly applicable. The two-kernel “outer/inner” factorization is simple yet expressive.
- Theoretical insight under a clear assumption: The “noise assumption” is explicitly articulated and analyzed. The results show soft PCA-like reweighting (linear outer, Gaussian inner) and variance reduction for estimating target kernels under a Gaussian manifold+noise model. These are intuitive and supportive of the claimed denoising benefits.
- Practical and flexible: The method supports various choices of k and kH (linear/Gaussian), with normalized variants addressing numerical issues. It integrates unlabeled data without bespoke graph construction or additional regularizers.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic tasks that violate the cluster assumption highlight the intended advantage; real datasets show competitive or improved performance, with stronger gains as noise increases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of theory: The analysis largely assumes infinite unlabeled data and Gaussian distributions with orthogonal noise. It is convincing as a mechanism study but leaves open how robust the benefits are beyond these assumptions (e.g., multimodality, non-Gaussian noise, limited u).
- Relation to existing data-dependent kernels needs tightening: The construction KF = K KHKT and its normalized counterpart seem closely related to (i) diffusion/smoothed kernels on graphs; (ii) data-dependent kernels in manifold regularization [13]; (iii) double-kernel smoothing/empirical kernel maps. A more explicit comparison (both conceptual and empirical) would clarify novelty and avoid overlap concerns.
- Scalability: Building KH on l+u points and forming KF entails O((l+u)^2) memory and O((l+u)^3) solves. There is no discussion of low-rank/Nyström approximations, sparsification, or mini-batch estimators, which limits applicability to large unlabeled corpora.
- Experimental details: The excerpt omits exact numbers, variance bars, and details (sizes of unlabeled sets, selection of t and r, normalization constants). Baselines could be broadened (e.g., modern SSL methods beyond TSVM/LapRLSC), though depending on the time frame this may be acceptable. An ablation on the effect of normalization and outer/inner choices would strengthen the case.
- Generalization guarantees: Beyond variance reduction of kernel estimates, the paper does not provide generalization bounds or stability analyses for the learned predictor with a data-dependent kernel.

Questions for the authors
- Can you position k̂F relative to diffusion kernels and other smoothed/data-dependent kernels more explicitly? Under what conditions are they equivalent or complementary?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of t and to the normalization in k̂NF? Any heuristics beyond CV?
- Can the approach be scaled with Nyström/Random Features for both KH and the outer k to handle millions of unlabeled points?
- Does the variance reduction carry over when u is finite? Any finite-sample bounds?

Clarity and presentation
The paper is generally well written and the core idea is clear. Some derivations and all proofs are deferred to the full version; including at least the key steps (PSD proof; representer derivation) would improve self-containment. A brief complexity analysis and guidance on scalable approximations would be valuable. Minor typos remain (“senario”, “unormalized/unnormalized”, “SteinJames”).

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh, simple, and potentially impactful way to inject unlabeled data into kernels via a Fredholm integral perspective.
- Provides clean theoretical intuition for denoising effects under a transparent assumption.
- Shows consistent empirical gains, especially in noisy regimes where the method is designed to help.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to idealized settings; connections to closely related data-dependent kernels need to be sharpened.
- Scalability and practical deployment details are underdeveloped.
- Experimental section (as provided) lacks some rigor in reporting and breadth of baselines.

Overall evaluation
I value the conceptual novelty and the bold reframing. Despite some limitations in scope and scalability, the idea is elegant, likely to inspire follow-up work, and shows practical promise. I recommend acceptance, contingent on improving positioning to related data-dependent kernels and adding guidance on scalable implementations.
