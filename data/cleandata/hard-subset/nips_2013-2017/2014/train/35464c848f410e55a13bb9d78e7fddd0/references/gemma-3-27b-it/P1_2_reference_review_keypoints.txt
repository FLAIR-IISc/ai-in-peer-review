- Casts supervised and unsupervised learning as solving a Fredholm integral equation and defines “Fredholm kernels.”
- Framework naturally incorporates unlabeled data and can improve performance under a “noise assumption” where low-variance directions are uninformative.
- Provides theoretical results showing variance reduction for the linear Fredholm kernel and approximation guarantees under the noise assumption.
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims; synthetic examples are well-designed to illustrate benefits.
- Connects to existing work in kernel methods, density ratio estimation, kernel mean embeddings, and shrinkage estimators.
- Positions the contribution within related literature and distinguishes itself by explicitly framing learning as a Fredholm problem.
- Novelty: the Fredholm formulation offers a new perspective on semi-supervised and kernel methods.
- Theoretical contributions are clear, insightful, and form a solid foundation for the method.
- Formalizing the “noise assumption” is a useful contribution that clarifies future research directions.
- Empirical validation is encouraging but focused; synthetic cases effectively demonstrate the claimed advantages.
- Shows that the Fredholm kernel reduces to standard kernel methods under specific conditions, aiding interpretability.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, making complex math accessible.
- Main weakness: computational cost—matrix inversion with many unlabeled points raises scalability concerns.
- Kernel selection is critical; the impact of inner (kH) and outer (k) kernel choices needs deeper investigation.
- Ambiguity between unnormalized and normalized Fredholm kernels—criteria for choosing normalization are unclear.
- Experimental scope is limited; more diverse datasets and tasks are needed to establish generalizability.
- Theoretical results rely on specific assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, linear subspace); robustness to realistic, complex data is uncertain.
- The “noise assumption” may overlap existing manifold/statistical learning concepts and needs clearer positioning regarding novelty.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded framework with potential practical gains and strong presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability, kernel/normalization sensitivity, and limited empirical validation.
- Overall recommendation: accept, but authors should address computational complexity, kernel selection, and experimental scope.
- Suggestion: add a section on computational complexity and scaling strategies (approximate inversions, subsampling).
- Suggestion: provide concrete guidelines for choosing inner and outer kernels and analyze sensitivity to kernel choices and noise levels.
- Suggestion: expand empirical evaluation across more datasets/tasks and clarify the relation of the noise assumption to existing work.
