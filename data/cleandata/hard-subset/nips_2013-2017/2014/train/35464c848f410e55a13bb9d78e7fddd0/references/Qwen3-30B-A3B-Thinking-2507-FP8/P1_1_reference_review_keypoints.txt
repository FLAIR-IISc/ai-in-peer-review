- Summary: Proposes learning via the Fredholm integral equation using data-dependent "Fredholm kernels" and a "noise assumption" that low-variance unlabeled directions are uninformative; provides theory and experiments showing improvements.

- Theoretical novelty: Introduces the noise assumption and proves (Theorems 2–4) that Fredholm kernels can suppress noise and improve performance under this assumption.

- Technical soundness: Rigorous analysis connecting Fredholm equations to kernel methods; Proposition 1 shows Fredholm kernels are positive semi-definite.

- Empirical validation: Synthetic examples and real-world text/digit experiments demonstrate consistent gains over baselines, especially where cluster assumptions fail.

- Clarity: Well-written, well-organized, good notation and illustrative figures (e.g., Figure 1).

- Limited theoretical scope: Analysis is confined to Gaussian noise models and may not generalize to more complex real-world noise.

- Computational complexity: Does not address the cost of large matrix inversions involving many unlabeled points, raising scalability concerns.

- Comparison to related work: Needs clearer contrast with graph-based semi-supervised methods and recent deep learning approaches.

- Practical implementation: Lacks concrete guidance on kernel parameter selection and stability (e.g., issues with small Gaussian widths), despite normalization discussion.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated noise assumption; strong theoretical foundations; comprehensive empirical support; approach maps to standard kernel methods making it accessible.

- Arguments against acceptance: Scalability concerns, theory limited to Gaussian noise, and insufficient comparison with modern deep semi-supervised methods.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically solid contribution that offers a novel perspective on semi-supervised learning; minor limitations do not outweigh its value.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Specific suggestions: Add complexity analysis and large-scale approximations; extend theory beyond Gaussian noise; compare with deep semi-supervised methods; provide practical kernel-parameter selection advice.
