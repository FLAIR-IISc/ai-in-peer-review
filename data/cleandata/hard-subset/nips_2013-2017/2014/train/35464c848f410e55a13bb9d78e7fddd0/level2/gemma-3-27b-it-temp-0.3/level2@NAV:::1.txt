## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the concept of “Fredholm kernels.” The authors argue that this approach naturally incorporates unlabeled data and can be interpreted as constructing data-dependent kernels that effectively denoise classifiers under a “noise assumption.”

The core idea of formulating learning as a Fredholm problem is interesting and builds upon existing work in kernel methods and integral operators (e.g., [10, 12]). The connection to data-dependent kernels, reminiscent of [13], is clearly articulated. The introduction of the “noise assumption” – that low-variance directions are uninformative – provides a compelling motivation for utilizing unlabeled data to improve classification, and the theoretical analysis in Section 4, particularly Theorems 3 & 4, demonstrates variance reduction under specific conditions. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets are encouraging, showing competitive performance compared to standard and semi-supervised learning methods.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are valuable, they rely on strong assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific kernel choices) that may limit their generalizability. The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of the Fredholm kernel itself versus other hyperparameters or algorithmic choices. The discussion of the “noise assumption” feels somewhat intuitive and could benefit from a more rigorous mathematical formulation or connection to existing concepts in information theory. The notation is dense, and the derivation of some equations could be more clearly presented.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of learning as a Fredholm problem.
*   Theoretical justification for improved performance under the “noise assumption.”
*   Competitive empirical results on a variety of datasets.
*   Clear connection to existing kernel methods and related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
*   Limited ablation study in the experimental section.
*   Intuitive, rather than rigorously defined, “noise assumption.”
*   Dense notation and occasionally unclear derivations.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to semi-supervised learning. While the theoretical results are constrained by assumptions and the experimental evaluation could be more thorough, the novelty of the framework and the potential for improved performance warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed ablation study and a clarification of the “noise assumption.” The authors should also consider expanding the discussion of the limitations of their theoretical results.
