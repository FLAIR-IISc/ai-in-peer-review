- Introduces a novel framework formulating supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, yielding “Fredholm kernels.”  
- Leverages unlabeled data via an integral operator and connects the approach to existing kernel and manifold-regularization methods.  
- Provides theoretical analysis under a “noise assumption” showing potential benefits of unlabeled data.  
- Empirically validates the method on synthetic and real-world datasets.  
- Well-motivated placement within kernel methods and integral-operator literature.  
- Clear and interesting contribution in framing learning as a Fredholm equation.  
- Appropriately notes connections to data-dependent kernels used in manifold regularization.  
- The “noise assumption” discussion offers a fresh perspective and is a key strength.  
- Theoretical results indicate advantages (e.g., linear Fredholm kernels suppress noisy components, improved approximation to “true” kernel) under specific conditions.  
- Experiments provide a reasonable and comprehensive evaluation across datasets.  
- Mathematical notation is dense and can be challenging to follow.  
- The link between the “noise assumption” and the core Fredholm formulation is not made sufficiently explicit.  
- Theoretical analysis is somewhat narrow, focusing mainly on linear kernels and Gaussian noise.  
- Empirical evaluation lacks a detailed ablation study assessing key parameters (e.g., regularization λ, kernel width t).  
- Parameter choices for comparisons to existing methods are insufficiently detailed to assess fairness.  
- Key proofs (e.g., representer theorem) are deferred to a “full version,” hindering complete evaluation of the submission.  
- Needs a more intuitive explanation of why the Fredholm formulation inherently handles noise beyond formal theory.  
- Connection to prior work on Fredholm equations in density-ratio estimation [10] should be elaborated to clarify distinctions and complementarities.  
- Terminology such as “inner” and “outer” kernels appears ad-hoc and lacks strong justification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formulation, clear connections to kernel methods, interesting noise assumption with supporting theory/experiments, and comprehensive empirical evaluation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense notation, limited theoretical scope, missing ablation studies, insufficient parameter-sensitivity analysis, and weakly linked noise-assumption justification.  
- Overall assessment: novel and promising with significant contributions, but requires clarification and elaboration; acceptance recommended contingent on addressing weaknesses.  
- Essential recommendation: provide an intuitive explanation of how the Fredholm formulation addresses the noise assumption and include or sketch the representer theorem proof in the main paper.  
- Major recommendation: broaden theoretical analysis to other kernel/noise settings and conduct thorough ablation studies on key parameters.  
- Minor recommendation: clarify mathematical notation, better justify “inner”/“outer” kernel terminology, and elaborate differences/connections to prior Fredholm-equation work [10].
