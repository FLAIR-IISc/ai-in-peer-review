This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on formulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to “Fredholm kernels.” The core idea is to leverage unlabeled data via an integral operator and demonstrate connections to existing kernel methods. The paper provides theoretical analysis under a “noise assumption” and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the broader context of kernel methods and integral operators. The formulation of learning as a Fredholm equation is a clear and interesting contribution. The connection to data-dependent kernels, particularly those used in manifold regularization, is appropriately noted. The discussion of the “noise assumption” provides a fresh perspective on the benefits of unlabeled data and is a strength of the paper. The theoretical results, although somewhat specialized, demonstrate potential advantages of the proposed approach under specific conditions (linear Fredholm kernels suppressing noisy components, improved approximation to the “true” kernel).  The experiments, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, provide a reasonable evaluation of the method’s performance.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. The mathematical notation, while mostly clear, can be dense and requires careful attention. The discussion of the “noise assumption” feels slightly disconnected from the core Fredholm formulation; it would be helpful to more explicitly link how solving the Fredholm equation inherently addresses this assumption. The theoretical results, while present, are somewhat limited in scope – focusing primarily on linear kernels and Gaussian noise.  The empirical evaluation, while thorough, lacks a detailed ablation study showing the impact of key parameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λ, kernel width t) on performance. Specifically, the choice of parameters for comparison with existing methods is not detailed enough to assess fairness. The paper refers to “full version” details for the representer theorem proof, which hinders complete evaluation within the submitted manuscript. A more intuitive explanation of *why* the Fredholm formulation inherently handles noise, beyond the theoretical analysis, would be beneficial. The connection to prior work on Fredholm equations in density ratio estimation [10] could be elaborated further, highlighting the distinctions and complementary aspects. The terminology around "inner" and "outer" kernels seems somewhat ad-hoc and lacks strong justification beyond the derivation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Clear connection to existing kernel methods and a relevant discussion of data-dependent kernels.
*   Interesting "noise assumption" and supporting theoretical/empirical results.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical notation can be dense and challenging.
*   Theoretical results are somewhat limited in scope.
*   Lack of a detailed ablation study.
*   Limited discussion on parameter sensitivity.
*   The "noise assumption" and Fredholm formulation link could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to semi-supervised learning. The theoretical and empirical results are promising, but the paper could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. The contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above.

**Recommendations:**

*   **Essential:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of how the Fredholm formulation inherently addresses the "noise assumption." Include the proof of the representer theorem in the main manuscript or provide a more detailed sketch.
*   **Major:** Expand the theoretical analysis to consider other kernel combinations and noise distributions. Conduct a thorough ablation study to assess the impact of key parameters.
*   **Minor:** Improve the clarity of the mathematical notation and provide more justification for the terminology used (e.g., "inner" and "outer" kernels). Elaborate on the differences and connections to previous work on Fredholm equations, specifically [10].
