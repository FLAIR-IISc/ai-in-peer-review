- Introduces a novel semi-supervised framework using regularized Fredholm integral equations to create "Fredholm kernels" that incorporate unlabeled data.  
- Proposes a new "noise assumption" alternative to manifold/cluster assumptions and argues Fredholm kernels exploit it.  
- Strong mathematical formulation and clear connection between Fredholm equations and kernel methods.  
- Proposition 1 proving Fredholm kernels are positive semi-definite is a valuable theoretical contribution.  
- Theorems 2–4 theoretically show Fredholm kernels can suppress noise and reduce variance versus standard kernels.  
- Noise assumption is clearly defined and illustrated with compelling synthetic examples (Figure 1).  
- Synthetic experiments show clear improvements over baselines in noise-designed scenarios (Figures 2, Tables 1–2).  
- Real-world experiments are competitive, with Gaussian variants (FredGauss, FredGauss(N)) showing notable gains on noisy MNIST (Tables 3–6).  
- Connection to related work is made, particularly to [10] on Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation.  
- Fails to sufficiently distinguish the approach from prior work ([10]) and underdevelops the relation to kernel mean shrinkage ([9]), weakening novelty claims.  
- Theoretical results are limited to specific settings (Gaussian distributions, linear kernels/combinations) and may not generalize.  
- Assumption of infinite unlabeled data is unrealistic and the paper lacks guidance for finite unlabeled-data scenarios.  
- Empirical evaluation is incomplete—omits comparisons to state-of-the-art graph-based and deep semi-supervised methods and reports only modest real-data gains (1–3% reductions).  
- Does not validate that real-world datasets satisfy the proposed noise assumption or show it is more relevant than manifold/cluster assumptions.  
- Conclusion: mathematically interesting and promising, but novelty overstated and empirical/theoretical validation insufficient; recommends rejection pending stronger comparisons, real-world validation of the noise assumption, and practical guidance.
