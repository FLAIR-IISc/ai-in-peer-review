Summary and relation to prior work: The paper reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, yielding a data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” that integrates unlabeled data through an estimated integral operator. The authors motivate a “noise assumption” (low-variance directions are label-irrelevant) distinct from cluster/manifold assumptions, and provide two theoretical lenses: (i) with a linear outer kernel, Fredholm kernels reweight principal directions, suppressing low-variance components; (ii) with Gaussian assumptions, Fredholm kernels produce lower-variance estimators of target kernels than using noisy observations directly. Empirically, Fredholm kernels are compared to RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC on synthetic tasks designed to violate the cluster assumption, and on text, sentiment, and digit datasets. The work builds on operator-theoretic kernels, density-ratio estimation via Fredholm equations (Que & Belkin, NIPS), manifold regularization (Belkin et al.), and RKHS mean-shrinkage (Muandet et al.), while connecting to data-dependent kernels (e.g., Sindhwani et al.).

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean operator view that naturally incorporates unlabeled data; the induced kernel is PSD and tractable via representer theorem.
- Theory clearly explains denoising: soft-thresholding of low-variance directions and variance reduction for linear/Gaussian targets under explicit models.
- Empirical results suggest competitive performance, especially when cluster assumptions are violated.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on strong assumptions (Gaussianity, orthogonal noise, infinite unlabeled data). No finite-sample generalization bounds under the noise assumption.
- Practical aspects underexplored: computational/memory complexity for forming KF with large u; sensitivity to k/kH choices, kernel widths, and normalization; ablations on inner vs outer kernels.
- Related work could discuss closer connections to diffusion/data-dependent kernels and label propagation; comparison set omits graph-based label propagation and modern consistency-based SSL.
- Clarity: typos and notational overload; the “noise assumption” would benefit from a formal definition and testable implications.

Quality: Technically sound within its modeling scope; proofs appear plausible, though some are deferred. Completeness is good for a conference paper but missing finite-sample analysis and scalability strategies. Clarity: Generally well-organized; could tighten notation and provide more guidance on hyperparameters/normalization. Originality: Moderate-to-good; a novel synthesis that yields a specific, useful data-dependent kernel. Significance: Potentially useful to practitioners facing high-dimensional nuisance variation; empirical benefits are promising.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide finite-sample bounds or excess-risk analysis under the noise assumption; analyze dependence on u and model misspecification.
- Add runtime/memory analysis and scalable approximations (Nyström, random features, sparsification).
- Expand baselines (e.g., label propagation, graph diffusion) and include ablations on k vs kH, normalized vs unnormalized variants, and sensitivity to t/r.
- Clarify and formalize the noise assumption; release code for reproducibility.

Overall: Promising idea with solid analysis and encouraging results; I lean toward acceptance if scalability and clarity improvements are addressed.
