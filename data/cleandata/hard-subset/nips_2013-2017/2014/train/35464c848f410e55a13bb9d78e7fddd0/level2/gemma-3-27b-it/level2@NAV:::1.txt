## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the definition of “Fredholm kernels.” The core idea is compelling – leveraging the Fredholm formulation to naturally incorporate unlabeled data and effectively denoise classifiers under a proposed “noise assumption.” The authors connect this approach to existing kernel methods and density estimation techniques, and provide both theoretical analysis and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper's strengths lie in its mathematical grounding and the potential for a new perspective on semi-supervised learning. The derivation of Fredholm kernels as a data-dependent kernel is clearly presented, and the theoretical results regarding noise suppression are intriguing, particularly the analysis under a Gaussian noise model (Theorem 4). The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, and the inclusion of both synthetic examples tailored to the “noise assumption” and standard benchmark datasets adds credibility. The authors appropriately reference relevant prior work, including connections to kernel mean embeddings and manifold regularization. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The “noise assumption” itself, while intuitively appealing, lacks a rigorous definition beyond being the opposite of the cluster assumption. A clearer formalization would strengthen the theoretical contributions. While the experiments demonstrate good performance, the gains over strong baselines like LapRLSC are often marginal, and a more in-depth ablation study to isolate the impact of specific components (e.g., the choice of inner/outer kernels) would be beneficial. The paper’s length and density make it somewhat challenging to follow; more concise explanations and illustrative examples could improve clarity.  Furthermore, the practical implications of choosing the regularization parameter *t* in the Gaussian kernel setting are not fully explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of semi-supervised learning with potential theoretical advantages.
*   Clear mathematical derivation of Fredholm kernels.
*   Empirically competitive performance on a range of datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   “Noise assumption” lacks a precise definition.
*   Performance gains over baselines are not always substantial.
*   Paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new direction for semi-supervised learning. While the “noise assumption” requires further refinement and the empirical gains could be more pronounced, the theoretical contributions and innovative framework warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with a request for the authors to revise and resubmit, focusing on clarifying the noise assumption, conducting a more comprehensive ablation study, and improving the overall clarity of the presentation.
