This paper presents a semi-supervised learning framework based on Fredholm kernels. The authors extend the classical optimization problem for supervised kernel methods to incorporate information from unlabeled data, resulting in a discretized Fredholm integral equation.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality, with claims well-supported by both theoretical analysis and convincing experimental results. The proposed formulation yields a novel data-dependent kernel that incorporates information from unlabeled data. Although the resulting classifying function differs from the solution given by the classical representer theorem, it remains elegant and computationally efficient.

I have a few comments that could further strengthen the paper:
1.  Regarding Equation 3, which presents the solution to the optimization problem: The authors state that the associated kernel need not be positive semi-definite (PSD). It is unclear whether Equation 3 remains valid in the non-PSD case. Including a derivation or proof for Equation 3 would be beneficial.
2.  In the experimental section, the authors state that an "optimal" parameter was chosen for all benchmarked methods. Could the authors clarify how these parameters were determined and what "optimal" signifies in this context? For instance, were they selected via cross-validation, or tuned to achieve the best possible performance for each method on the test set?

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized for the most part. The main ideas are explained adequately, making the paper easy to follow. However, there are some minor typos that should be corrected:
*   Line 82: "... will a Euclidean ..." should be "... will be a Euclidean ..."
*   Line 83: "or" is missing.
*   Section 4 title: "Fredhom" should be "Fredholm".
*   Line 247: "porportional" should be "proportional".
*   Line 283: "That is, or..." should be "That is, for...".
*   Line 352: This sentence requires rewriting for clarity.

**Originality**
The proposed approach is novel, despite its connection to a recent NIPS publication [9]. The authors clearly articulate the distinctions between their work and prior art.

**Significance**
The results are both important and significant. The authors have made a substantial effort to validate their method by testing it on several datasets across different application domains.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with a strong theoretical foundation and a convincing experimental evaluation.
