The authors present an interesting new method for supervised and semi-supervised learning using Fredholm kernels. Many aspects of the method are discussed in detail. Overall, I find the paper very interesting.

Nevertheless, I still have a few suggestions and questions:

- Section 2 (from eq 1 to eq on line 99): The formulation based on approximating an integral equation is reminiscent of the Nyström method, particularly in the context of kernel PCA. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss this connection, highlighting similarities and differences between their approach and Nyström-based techniques.

- Section 2 eq 2: Approaches formulating learning problems as operator equations are present in the literature, especially within the framework of support vector machines and kernel methods.  The authors should acknowledge this related work and clarify how their equation (2) relates to these existing formulations – whether it represents a special case, or offers a distinct perspective.

- Section 2 line 128 (Representer Theorem): The optimization problem in Eqn 2 is not standard in learning theory. The authors invoke a Representer Theorem, but should either provide a proof of its applicability in this specific context, or furnish a precise reference and explain how Eqn 2 fits within the established framework of the theorem.

- Section 4 eq 8: The interpretation of the linear Fredholm kernel as a soft-thresholding PCA is insightful.  However, it would strengthen the analysis to discuss how this differs from other feature-based methods, such as Fisher kernels and probability product kernels, which also aim to emphasize informative directions in the data.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel approach to supervised and unsupervised learning through the use of Fredholm kernels, which are data-dependent. The method reformulates learning as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation, offering a natural way to incorporate unlabeled data. The authors provide theoretical analysis, particularly focusing on a "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning, and demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation and the resulting Fredholm kernels offer a fresh perspective on kernel methods.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides theoretical insights into the behavior of Fredholm kernels under the proposed “noise assumption,” offering conditions under which unlabeled data can provably improve inference.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that Fredholm kernels achieve competitive performance on a variety of datasets, including both synthetic examples and real-world benchmarks.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of kernel methods, integral operators, and semi-supervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While the authors cite relevant papers, the connections to Nyström methods and operator equation formulations of SVMs could be more explicitly discussed.
*   **Representer Theorem Assumption:** The reliance on a Representer Theorem without proof or detailed justification weakens the rigor of the theoretical analysis.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** A more thorough comparison to other dimensionality reduction or feature learning techniques (e.g., Fisher kernels, probability product kernels) would further clarify the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Minor Typos:** A few minor typos are present throughout the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to kernel-based learning with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The experimental results support the claims of the authors and demonstrate the practical utility of the method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a proof for the Representer Theorem and the limited discussion of related work slightly weaken the technical rigor of the paper.
*   The comparison to alternative methods could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation of the proposed approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
