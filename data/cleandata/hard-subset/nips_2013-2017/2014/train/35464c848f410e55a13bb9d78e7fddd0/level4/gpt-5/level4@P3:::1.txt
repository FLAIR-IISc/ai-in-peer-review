Summary
This paper proposes a unified framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The key idea is to introduce an operator K_pX that averages a function f against a kernel k with respect to the marginal distribution pX, and to learn f in an RKHS H by minimizing a loss on K_pX f with RKHS regularization. Discretizing K_pX with unlabeled samples leads to a practical objective whose solution induces a new, data-dependent kernel on the labeled points, termed the Fredholm kernel. The authors show that (i) this kernel is PSD when the “inner” kernel kH is PSD, even if the “outer” kernel k need not be, and (ii) the approach can be interpreted as a standard kernel method with a nonstandard, unlabeled-data–dependent kernel matrix.

The paper develops the “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning: locally low-variance directions in the marginal are uninformative for the labels and should be suppressed. Theoretical results illustrate how Fredholm kernels implement noise suppression: with a linear outer kernel, the induced inner product down-weights low-variance principal components (a soft-thresholded PCA effect), and with Gaussian outer/inner kernels, the Fredholm kernel acts as a variance-reduced estimator of a target kernel on a latent clean space. The work is positioned relative to kernel mean embeddings, density ratio estimation via Fredholm equations, shrinkage estimators in RKHS, and data-dependent kernels/manifold regularization. Empirically, the method performs well on synthetic data designed to violate the cluster assumption and on real datasets in text, sentiment, and digits, including robustness gains under added noise.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound formulation that cleanly integrates unlabeled data via an operator discretization; the induced kernel perspective is elegant and computationally useful.
- Theoretical results are clear and insightful: linear Fredholm kernels yield PCA-like soft thresholding under Gaussian marginals, and both linear and Gaussian cases demonstrate variance reduction relative to kernels computed directly on noisy observations.
- Experiments span synthetic settings tailored to the proposed “noise assumption” and several real datasets, with competitive or superior performance, especially under injected noise.
- The PSD property of the Fredholm kernel (given PSD kH) and the equivalence to a standard kernel method provide reassuring foundations.

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Equation (3) and the representer theorem: Because the loss depends on K_p̂X f evaluated at labeled points rather than on f(xi) directly, a brief proof sketch would help. In particular, please clarify the functional-analytic assumptions under which the representer theorem applies when the outer kernel k may be non-PSD (while kH is PSD and H is an RKHS). It would also help to state explicitly that the learned Fredholm kernel matrix KF is PSD whenever KH is PSD (Proposition 1), thereby ensuring a well-posed linear system.
- Hyperparameter selection: The experiments state that “optimal” parameters are used. Please specify the validation protocol for each method (size/splits, search grids for t, r, λ, normalization choice, and model-specific parameters), whether the same validation budget was used across methods, and whether results are averaged over multiple random splits. For the scaling constants in Theorems 3–4 and the normalized kernel in Eq. (5), please explain how these are chosen in practice (e.g., subsumed into λ or selected via validation).
- Computational complexity and scalability: Computing k̂F involves O((l+u)2) kernel evaluations and storing an l×l kernel on labeled points, with matrix solves/inversions at O(l3) (or O((l+u)3) depending on the formulation). Please discuss runtime/memory as u grows, and whether Nyström/random feature approximations or sparsification are applicable to k̂F (both unnormalized and normalized).
- Inductive vs. transductive use: Since unlabeled data are fixed when computing k̂F, how is the method applied when test points arrive after training? Please clarify the inductive prediction mechanism and whether additional unlabeled data at test time can be incorporated without retraining.
- Assumptions in theory: The analysis relies on Gaussianity and (for some results) noise in an orthogonal complement. This is reasonable for insight but potentially restrictive. A short discussion of how conclusions might extend beyond Gaussian models (e.g., local Gaussianity/manifold+noise settings) would strengthen the narrative.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The Fredholm-to-kernel reduction and the inner/outer kernel roles are explained effectively. A few minor issues:
- Line 82: “will a Euclidean” → “will be a Euclidean”
- Line 83: missing “or”
- Section 4 title: “Fredhom” → “Fredholm”
- Line 247: “porportional” → “proportional”
- Line 283: “That is, or...” → “That is, for...”
- Line 352: sentence needs rewriting for clarity.
Additional suggestions:
- Provide guidance on choosing kernel widths and when to prefer normalized vs. unnormalized k̂F (noting instability for small Gaussian widths).
- Briefly restate the definition and motivation of the “noise assumption” more formally, and contrast it directly with the cluster/manifold assumptions.

Originality
The work is novel in reframing learning via a Fredholm equation to induce a data-dependent kernel that exploits unlabeled data, with a distinct “noise assumption” perspective. While related to Fredholm-based density ratio estimation, kernel mean embeddings, shrinkage in RKHS, and data-dependent kernels for manifold regularization, the proposed learning formulation and analyses provide new contributions.

Significance
The approach offers a principled and practical route to leverage unlabeled data by shaping the kernel with the marginal distribution. The results suggest meaningful gains under realistic noise conditions, and the induced-kernel viewpoint makes the method accessible to existing kernel machinery. With further clarification on scalability and hyperparameter selection, the method could be broadly useful.

Pros
- Elegant operator-to-kernel reduction; PSD and representer-based solution.
- Theoretical insight into noise suppression and variance reduction.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise.
- Compatible with standard kernels and losses; flexible design via inner/outer kernels.

Cons
- Theory rests on Gaussian assumptions; limited discussion of generality.
- Scalability and inductive use not fully detailed.
- Hyperparameter/validation protocol needs clearer specification.
- Some clarity/typo issues.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and thoughtful contribution that advances semi-supervised learning by constructing data-dependent kernels from unlabeled data. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the representer theorem application, experimental protocols, and scalability considerations.
