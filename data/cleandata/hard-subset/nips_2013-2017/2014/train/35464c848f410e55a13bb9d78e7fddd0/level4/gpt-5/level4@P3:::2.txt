Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a learning framework that reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The key idea is to apply an integral operator K_pX, defined using the marginal pX and an “outer” kernel k, to functions f in an RKHS H with “inner” kernel kH, and to fit (K_p̂X f)(xi) to labeled targets via Tikhonov regularization. The authors show that the resulting solution is equivalent to a standard kernel method with a data-dependent kernel, the Fredholm kernel, constructed from labeled and unlabeled data. They provide a PSD guarantee when KH is PSD, and a normalized variant for stability.

The paper motivates semi-supervised gains via a “noise assumption”: directions of low variance in the local unlabeled distribution are uninformative for labels and should be suppressed. Theoretical results show that, under Gaussian models and with infinite unlabeled data, (i) a linear Fredholm kernel implements soft-thresholding of principal components, and (ii) for linear and Gaussian target kernels, Fredholm kernels provide unbiased estimates (up to scale) of the target clean-kernel values with strictly smaller variance than direct computation on noisy observations when signal variance exceeds noise. Experiments on synthetic data violating the cluster assumption and on several real datasets show performance competitive with or better than RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

The approach relates to several lines: (a) integral operator methods and regularization in RKHS; (b) Nyström approximations to integral operators (e.g., in kernel PCA); (c) data-dependent kernels in manifold regularization; and (d) Fredholm integral equations for density ratio estimation.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation connecting integral equations, unlabeled data, and kernel methods via a data-dependent kernel.
- Theoretical variance-reduction results under explicit generative assumptions make the “noise assumption” concrete and testable.
- Practical algorithms that reduce to standard kernel machinery; positive semidefiniteness is addressed.
- Empirical results support the claims, especially when cluster assumptions are violated or additional noise is injected.
- The framework flexibly accommodates different inner/outer kernel choices and a normalized variant for stability.

Weaknesses and questions
- Relation to Nyström and operator-based learning: The construction K_p̂X is closely related to Nyström approximations of integral operators used in kernel PCA and spectral methods. Please discuss similarities and differences: e.g., Nyström approximates the eigensystem of an integral operator, while here K_p̂X composes with an RKHS mapping and appears inside a Tikhonov objective. How does your kernel KF compare to Nyström-induced kernels operationally and computationally in semi-supervised settings?
- Operator equations and representer theorem: Eq. (2) minimizes a data-fitting term that applies linear functionals of f through K_p̂X. Please prove a representer theorem for this objective or cite a precise version showing that any minimizer admits a finite expansion over {x1,…,xl+u}, and clearly state required assumptions (e.g., boundedness of K_p̂X, properties of k and kH, the loss). Given that k need not be PSD or symmetric, clarify the conditions under which the representer theorem applies and whether uniqueness holds.
- Scope of “unsupervised” claims: The introduction mentions unsupervised learning, but the rest of the paper focuses on supervised and semi-supervised settings. Please clarify what (if any) unsupervised tasks are addressed, or adjust the framing.
- Comparison to related kernels: The soft-thresholding PCA view (Sec. 4.1) is appealing. Please position it relative to Fisher kernels (which derive discriminative kernels from generative models via score features) and probability product kernels (which integrate products of probability models). How does Fredholm learning differ conceptually and practically (assumptions, use of unlabeled data, computational cost, and scope)?
- Assumptions in theory: The variance-reduction theorems rely on Gaussian distributions, orthogonal additive noise, and infinite unlabeled data. Please discuss robustness: finite u, model misspecification, non-Gaussian heavy-tailed noise, and manifold-plus-noise settings with curvature. Are there consistency guarantees as u→∞ and l fixed, or joint asymptotics in l,u?
- Computational complexity: Provide complexity and memory costs of forming KF and solving the linear system as a function of l and u, and discuss scalability. Are there low-rank or Nyström variants for KF itself? How sensitive are results to the choice of t and to normalization?
- Normalized kernel: The PSD claim for k̂NF is important; please include a short proof sketch or clear reference, and discuss numerical stability when Gaussian k has small bandwidth and data are high-dimensional.
- Experiments: 
  - Ensure fair hyperparameter selection across methods (e.g., validation protocols) and report variance over multiple splits.
  - Clarify the number of unlabeled samples used and sensitivity to u. 
  - Provide ablations for inner/outer kernel choices and normalized vs unnormalized forms. 
  - Consider adding computational time and memory comparisons.
  - Fix missing or ambiguous table references (e.g., “Table ??”).

Quality
- Generally sound with clear derivations and insightful theory under stylized assumptions. Some key steps (representer theorem, PSD proof for normalized kernel) should be included or precisely referenced.

Clarity
- Well written overall. Please tighten notation (consistent use of l, u; dimensions of matrices in Eq. (3)), define all matrices at first use, and move “full version” essentials (proof sketches, conditions) to the appendix/supplement. Clarify when k must be symmetric and any consequences for Mercer properties of k̂F.

Originality
- The Fredholm reformulation leading to a concrete data-dependent kernel that exploits unlabeled data under a noise assumption is novel relative to closely related threads (density-ratio Fredholm, manifold regularization kernels). The PCA soft-thresholding perspective is an interesting new lens.

Significance
- Potentially useful and broadly applicable; the data-dependent kernel may be adopted in semi-supervised pipelines where cluster assumptions fail but local noise suppression is beneficial.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat bridge from integral equations to practical kernels.
- Theoretically justified variance reduction and noise suppression.
- Strong empirical performance where unlabeled data help beyond cluster/manifold assumptions.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing representer theorem proof/reference and some technical details.
- Limited theoretical scope (Gaussian/orthogonal noise) without robustness analysis.
- Need clearer positioning relative to Nyström, Fisher, and probability product kernels.
- Some clarity and presentation issues (tables, typos).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the representer theorem and sharpening the discussion of related operator/Nyström methods and alternative data-dependent kernels, plus fixing clarity and presentation issues. The idea is original and practically promising, with theory and experiments that substantiate the claimed benefits.
