This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. This approach naturally incorporates information from unlabeled data and can be interpreted as a kernel method using a new, data-dependent kernel, which the authors term a "Fredholm kernel". The paper proposes a "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning, arguing that unlabeled data can help identify and suppress uninformative, noisy feature dimensions. The authors provide both theoretical analysis and extensive experimental results to demonstrate that Fredholm kernels can effectively leverage unlabeled data under this assumption, achieving strong performance on a variety of tasks.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (e.g., demonstrating variance reduction for specific kernel choices) and convincing experimental results. The formulation is elegant, leading to a new data-dependent kernel that effectively incorporates information from unlabeled data. The final solution, while different from the standard Representer Theorem, is straightforward and computationally accessible.

I have a few minor questions for the authors:
1.  The authors state that the "outer" kernel *k* does not need to be positive semi-definite (PSD). However, the derivation for the solution in Equation (3) is typically based on the Representer Theorem, which assumes an RKHS setting generated by a PSD kernel. Could the authors clarify how this solution is derived in the absence of the PSD property for the outer kernel, perhaps by including a brief derivation in the appendix?
2.  In the experimental section, it is mentioned that "optimal" parameters were chosen for all benchmark methods. To improve reproducibility, could the authors provide more detail on this process? For example, was a fixed validation set used, or was cross-validation performed on the labeled training data? This clarification would further strengthen the empirical claims.

**Clarity**

The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The core ideas are explained well, and the logical flow is easy to follow. I noticed a few minor typos and grammatical errors that could be corrected in the final version:
*   Line 82: "...will a Euclidean..." should be "...will be a Euclidean..."
*   Line 83: "the label set Y is either {-1, 1} for binary classification the real line R for regression" appears to be missing "or".
*   Section 4 title: "Fredhom" should be "Fredholm".
*   Line 247: "porportional" should be "proportional".
*   Line 283: "That is, or..." should be "That is, for...".
*   Line 352: The sentence beginning "The experiment use subsets..." could be rewritten for clarity (e.g., "The experiments use subsets...").

**Originality**

The work is original and makes a novel contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning. The authors do a good job of positioning their work relative to prior art. While Fredholm integral equations have been used in machine learning for tasks like density ratio estimation [10], their application to construct data-dependent kernels for semi-supervised classification and regression is new. The proposed framework is clearly distinguished from related lines of work, such as manifold regularization [1, 13] and kernel mean shrinkage estimators [9].

**Significance**

The paper's contributions are significant. The introduction of the "noise assumption" provides a valuable conceptual framework for understanding how unlabeled data can be useful, complementing the more established cluster and manifold assumptions. The proposed Fredholm kernels offer a principled and practical method for leveraging this assumption. The comprehensive experiments, which show strong performance across synthetic and diverse real-world datasets (including text and image data), demonstrate the method's potential for broad applicability. This work is likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in semi-supervised learning and may inspire further investigation into data-dependent kernels.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical basis and a convincing experimental section that presents a valuable contribution to the field.
