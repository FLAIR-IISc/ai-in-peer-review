This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. This approach leads to the construction of data-dependent kernels, which the authors term "Fredholm kernels." A central contribution is the formalization of a "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning, which posits that low-variance directions in the data distribution are uninformative for the learning task. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence that Fredholm kernels can leverage unlabeled data to suppress such noise. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that under specific noise models, Fredholm kernels (constructed from linear or Gaussian base kernels) can act as variance-reducing estimators for the true underlying kernel, with one case leading to an interesting interpretation as a soft-thresholding PCA. The proposed methods are evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, where they show competitive performance, particularly in settings designed to highlight the noise assumption. The work is well-contextualized within the literature on kernel methods, manifold learning, and density ratio estimation.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral problem is an elegant and novel contribution. The introduction and analysis of the "noise assumption" provides a valuable new perspective for semi-supervised learning, distinct from the more common cluster and manifold assumptions. This could inspire new directions in the field.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis presented in Section 4 is a key strength. The results showing that Fredholm kernels can provide lower-variance estimates of the "true" kernel on a noise-free subspace (Theorems 3 and 4) are compelling. The interpretation of the linear Fredholm kernel as a re-weighting of principal components (Theorem 2) offers a clear intuition for why the method works.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. The synthetic examples effectively isolate and demonstrate the method's ability to handle the noise assumption where other semi-supervised methods might fail. The performance on a range of real-world text and image datasets shows that the approach is not only theoretically sound but also practically competitive.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the Fredholm framework and the noise assumption is clearly explained, and the connection between the integral equation formulation and the resulting data-dependent kernel is derived step-by-step.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against acceptance / for revision):**
1.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** While related work is cited, the connections to some highly relevant concepts could be elaborated upon to better highlight the paper's unique contributions.
    *   The use of an integral operator approximated by unlabeled data is reminiscent of the Nystrom method, which is also based on approximating an integral equation to find a low-rank kernel approximation. A brief discussion comparing and contrasting the Fredholm framework with the Nystrom method would be very helpful for the reader.
    *   Operator equations have been explored previously in the context of SVMs and kernel methods. It would strengthen the paper to situate the optimization problem in Eq. 2 within this broader literature, clarifying how this formulation differs from or specializes previous operator-based regularization schemes.
    *   The insightful interpretation of the method as a "soft-thresholding PCA" invites comparison to other techniques that learn a data-dependent metric, such as Fisher kernels or probability product kernels. A short discussion on the differences would further solidify the paper's contribution.

2.  **Technical Justification of the Representer Theorem:** The derivation of the solution in Eq. 3 relies on the Representer Theorem. However, the objective function in Eq. 2 is non-standard due to the presence of the `Kp̂X` operator within the loss term. For full technical rigor, the authors should either provide a brief proof showing that a representer theorem applies to this specific formulation or cite a generalized version of the theorem and explicitly state how its conditions are met in this case.

**Minor Issues:**
*   A few typos were noted: "Not that" (line 165), "achived" (line 221), "chossing" (line 341). Table numbering in the text should be checked (e.g., "Table ??" on line 672 in the supplementary).

**Summary:**
This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically-grounded, and empirically effective framework for semi-supervised learning. The main strengths are the originality of the Fredholm formulation, the insightful "noise assumption," and the strong theoretical and experimental support. The primary weaknesses are minor and relate to providing a more complete context with respect to prior work and fully justifying a key theoretical step. Addressing these points would make an already strong paper even better. I recommend acceptance.
