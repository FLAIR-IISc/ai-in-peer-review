This paper presents a compelling framework for semi-supervised learning leveraging Fredholm kernels. The authors extend the standard optimization problem in kernel methods to incorporate unlabeled data, resulting in a discretized formulation of the Fredholm integral equation.

**Quality**

The paper demonstrates a high level of technical quality. Claims are rigorously supported by theoretical analysis and validated through convincing experimental results. A key contribution is the introduction of a novel, data-dependent kernel that effectively integrates unlabeled information. While the resulting classification function deviates from the classical representer theorem solution, it remains computationally tractable and possesses an appealing elegance.

I have a few specific questions and suggestions. Regarding Equation 3, which details the solution to the proposed optimization problem, clarification would be beneficial. Given the authors acknowledge that the associated kernel is not necessarily positive semi-definite (PSD), it’s unclear if Equation 3 remains valid in these cases. Including a derivation or justification for Equation 3 would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the experimental section mentions the selection of “optimal” parameters for all benchmark methods.  It would be helpful to understand the precise procedure used to determine these parameters, and a more detailed definition of "optimal" in this context is needed. Was it determined via cross-validation, grid search, or another method?

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the core ideas. However, a few minor typos and grammatical errors were identified:

* Line 82: “… will a Euclidean …” should be “… will be a Euclidean …”
* Line 83: A conjunction (“and” or “or”) is missing.
* Section 4 title: “Fredhom” should be “Fredholm”
* Line 247: “porportional” should be “proportional”
* Line 283: “That is, or…” should be “That is, for…”
* Line 352: The sentence is awkward and requires rewriting for clarity.

Addressing these minor points would further enhance the paper's readability.

**Originality**

The proposed approach builds upon related work, including a recent NIPS publication ([9]), but presents a demonstrably novel contribution. The authors clearly articulate the distinctions between their work and existing methods, highlighting the unique aspects of their framework.

**Significance**

The results presented are significant and impactful. The authors have made a substantial effort to rigorously evaluate the proposed method across a diverse set of datasets and application domains. This thorough empirical evaluation reinforces the potential of the framework. 

Overall, this is a well-written paper with a strong theoretical foundation and a compelling experimental validation. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning.
