1. Introduces a novel supervised and semi-supervised learning framework using regularized Fredholm integral equations, yielding new data-dependent "Fredholm kernels."  
2. Interprets unlabeled data incorporation as a Fredholm problem of the first kind, enabling principled use of unlabeled samples.  
3. Proposes a new "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning, distinct from manifold and cluster assumptions.  
4. Provides theoretical evidence that Fredholm kernels can suppress noise in classification by reweighting feature directions based on unlabeled-data distributions.  
5. Connects and builds on prior kernel-method literature (kernel mean embeddings, density ratio estimation, manifold regularization).  
6. Shows the framework generalizes standard kernel methods (e.g., recovers them with a δ-function kernel).  
7. Uses the Representer Theorem to derive a computationally feasible solution and presents a clear mathematical formulation.  
8. Theoretical results (Theorems 2–4) analyze linear and Gaussian cases, demonstrating better approximation to clean-data kernels and reduced variance.  
9. Experimental section is comprehensive: synthetic tests isolating the noise assumption and multiple real-world datasets (text, sentiment, digits).  
10. Empirical results show competitive performance versus baselines (transductive SVM, LapRLSC), especially when the noise assumption holds.  
11. Paper is well-written, organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility (kernel construction and experimental setup).  
12. Originality is substantial: novel application of Fredholm equations to semi-supervised learning and a fresh semi-supervised assumption.  
13. Significance: offers a theoretically grounded method for using unlabeled data to suppress noise, with practical applicability when labeled data is scarce.  
14. Concern 1: Lacks a systematic approach for selecting kernel parameters (outer kernel width t and inner kernel width kH); cross-validation details should be elaborated.  
15. Concern 2: Would benefit from experiments showing failure cases when the noise assumption is violated to clarify method limitations.  
16. Overall recommendation: Strong contribution with solid theory and experiments; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
