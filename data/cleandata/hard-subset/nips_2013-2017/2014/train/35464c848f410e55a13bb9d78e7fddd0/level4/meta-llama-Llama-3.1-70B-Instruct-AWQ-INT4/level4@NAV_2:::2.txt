The paper "Learning with Fredholm Kernels" proposes a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The authors introduce a new data-dependent kernel, called the Fredholm kernel, which can be interpreted as constructing new kernels from existing ones. The paper discusses the "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning and provides both theoretical and experimental evidence that Fredholm kernels can effectively utilize unlabeled data under this assumption.

The authors' approach is closely related to the standard kernel framework, but with an extra layer introduced by the Fredholm integral equation, allowing for the integration of information from unlabeled data distribution. The paper provides a detailed derivation of the Fredholm kernel and discusses its properties, including its positive semi-definiteness.

The theoretical results in Section 4 are particularly interesting, as they show that Fredholm kernels can be used to replace traditional kernels to inject them with "noise-suppression" power with the help of unlabeled data. The authors provide two views to illustrate how such noise suppression can be achieved, including a soft-thresholding PCA interpretation and a kernel approximation with noise.

The experimental results in Section 5 demonstrate the effectiveness of Fredholm kernels in various settings, including synthetic examples and real-world datasets. The authors compare their approach to other semi-supervised learning methods, such as transductive support vector machines and LapRLSC, and show that Fredholm kernels can achieve better performance in certain scenarios.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of a new data-dependent kernel, which can be used to construct new kernels from existing ones.
* The provision of theoretical results that show the effectiveness of Fredholm kernels in suppressing noise with the help of unlabeled data.
* The demonstration of the effectiveness of Fredholm kernels in various experimental settings.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other related work, such as the Nystrom method and operator equations in support vector machines and kernel methods.
* The authors assume the existence of a representer theorem for their framework, but do not provide a proof or a precise reference.
* There are a few typos in the paper that need to be corrected.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to supervised and semi-supervised learning.
* The theoretical results and experimental evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other related work.
* The authors assume the existence of a representer theorem without providing a proof or a precise reference.
* There are a few typos in the paper that need to be corrected.

Overall, I find the paper very interesting and think that it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, I believe that the paper could be even stronger.
