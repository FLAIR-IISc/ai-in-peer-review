This paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to supervised and semi-supervised learning based on data-dependent Fredholm kernels. The authors provide a detailed discussion of many aspects of their method, and the overall contribution is compelling.

While the paper is well-presented, several points could be clarified to strengthen the work and better position it within the existing literature. My main suggestions and questions are as follows:

**1. Comparison to the Nystrom Method (Section 2, around lines 90-99):**
The core idea of approximating an integral equation is also central to the Nystrom method, particularly in its application to kernel PCA. To better position their contribution, the authors should discuss the relationship between their approach and the Nystrom method. A clarification of the similarities and key differences would significantly strengthen this section and help readers understand the novelty of the proposed framework.

**2. Relation to Operator Equations in Kernel Methods (Section 2, Eq. 2):**
The use of operator equations, as seen in Eq. 2, is not entirely new to the kernel methods community; similar formulations exist in the literature on Support Vector Machines and other kernel-based algorithms. The paper would benefit from situating Eq. 2 within this existing body of work. The authors should clarify whether their formulation is a special case of these prior methods or how it fundamentally differs.

**3. Justification for the Representer Theorem (Section 2, line 128):**
The formulation in Eq. 2 is less standard in machine learning theory, and the paper currently proceeds on the assumption that a representer theorem holds for it. This is a crucial assumption that requires justification. The authors must either provide a formal proof of a representer theorem for their specific formulation or cite a precise theorem from the literature and explicitly demonstrate how Eq. 2 satisfies its conditions.

**4. Contextualization with Other Data-Dependent Kernels (Section 4, Eq. 8):**
The interpretation of the method as a form of "soft-thresholding PCA" is both elegant and insightful. To further contextualize this contribution, it would be valuable to compare and contrast it with other established data-dependent kernel methods, such as Fisher kernels and probability product kernels. A discussion on how the proposed approach relates to or improves upon these alternatives would be highly beneficial.
