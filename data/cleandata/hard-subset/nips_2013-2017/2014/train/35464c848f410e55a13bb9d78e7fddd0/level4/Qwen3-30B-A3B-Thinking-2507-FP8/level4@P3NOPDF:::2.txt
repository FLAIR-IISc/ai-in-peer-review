

The authors propose a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning using Fredholm kernels, which are data-dependent integral operators. The work presents a theoretically grounded approach to learning via integral equations, with detailed discussions of the method's derivation, connections to existing techniques, and empirical validation. The core idea—formulating learning as a solution to a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind—offers a fresh perspective that merits attention. The paper is well-structured and makes a significant contribution to kernel-based learning theory. However, several clarifications and comparisons are needed to strengthen the theoretical foundation and contextualize the work within the broader literature.

**Key strengths:** The connection between the proposed method and soft-thresholding PCA (Section 4, Eq. 8) is insightful and well-motivated. The semi-supervised extension via graph-based regularization (Section 4.2) is also compelling. The empirical results on standard benchmarks demonstrate the method’s practical viability.

**Major concerns requiring clarification:**
1.  **Section 2 (lines 1–99):** The authors frame their approach as a new method for solving integral equations in learning. However, the Nyström method for kernel approximation (e.g., in kernel PCA) also involves approximating integral operators. The authors should explicitly discuss this relationship: *How does their approximation strategy differ from Nyström in terms of assumptions, error bounds, or computational trade-offs?* A brief comparative remark would significantly enhance the paper’s context.
2.  **Section 2, Eq. 2:** The formulation of the learning problem as an operator equation (Eq. 2) appears distinct from standard kernel methods. The literature contains work on operator-theoretic approaches to SVMs and kernel learning (e.g., [cite seminal papers on operator equations in SVMs, e.g., *Bottou & Vapnik, 1992* or *Schölkopf et al., 2001*]). The authors should clarify: *Is Eq. 2 a special case of these operator formulations, or does it represent a fundamentally different paradigm?* If the latter, they must justify why this distinction matters for learning theory.
3.  **Section 2, line 128 (Representer Theorem):** The paper assumes the existence of a representer theorem for the solution (Eq. 2) without proof or reference. This is a critical gap. The standard representer theorem for kernel methods (e.g., for Tikhonov regularization) does not directly apply here due to the integral equation structure. The authors must either: (a) prove a new representer theorem for their specific problem, or (b) provide a precise reference showing Eq. 2 is a special case of an existing theorem (e.g., for linear inverse problems in learning). Without this, the theoretical validity of the proposed solution is incomplete.
4.  **Section 4, Eq. 8 (Soft-Thresholding PCA):** The interpretation as soft-thresholding PCA is valuable, but the paper lacks comparison to related kernel-based methods. Specifically: *How does this approach differ from Fisher kernels (which combine generative models with kernels) or probability product kernels (which use likelihood ratios)?* The authors should clarify the unique advantages of their method in terms of computational efficiency, theoretical guarantees, or performance on specific tasks (e.g., when data is noisy or high-dimensional).

- **Clarity:** The phrase "Fredholm kernels" in the abstract is misleading; Fredholm refers to the *integral equation*, not the kernel itself. The kernel is data-dependent, but the framework uses *Fredholm equations of the first kind*. The abstract and introduction should clarify this to avoid confusion.
- **Context:** The claim that "Fredholm kernels" are a new concept needs support. The authors should briefly distinguish their use of Fredholm equations from prior work on Fredholm operators in machine learning (e.g., in spectral methods or inverse problems).

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a promising theoretical framework with strong empirical results. Addressing the above points—particularly the representer theorem gap and the comparisons to Nyström/SVM operator methods—would significantly elevate the work. The core idea is novel and potentially impactful, but the current theoretical justification is insufficient. If these issues are resolved, this could be a valuable contribution to the kernel learning literature.

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The paper has strong potential but requires deeper theoretical grounding and clearer contextualization. The suggested revisions are essential for ensuring the method’s theoretical soundness and distinguishing it from existing approaches.
