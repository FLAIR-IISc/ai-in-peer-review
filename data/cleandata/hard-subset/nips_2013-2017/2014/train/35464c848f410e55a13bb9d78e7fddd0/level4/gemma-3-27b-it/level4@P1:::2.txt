The authors present an interesting new method for supervised and semi-supervised learning using Fredholm kernels. Many aspects of the method are discussed in detail. Overall, I find the paper very interesting.

Nevertheless, I still have a few suggestions and questions:

- Section 2 (from Eq. 1 to Eq. on line 99):
The approach shares similarities with Nyström methods, which also approximate integral equations (e.g., in kernel PCA). It would strengthen the paper if the authors discussed this connection, explaining in what sense their approach is similar or different.

- Section 2, Eq. 2:
The use of operator equations also appears in the support vector machine and kernel methods literature. It would be beneficial to mention relevant work in this area and clarify how Eq. 2 relates to, or differs from, these existing approaches.

- Section 2, line 128 (Representer Theorem):
Eq. 2 represents a less common formulation in learning theory. The authors assume the existence of a representer theorem. Either a proof of the representer theorem should be included, or a precise reference provided, explaining how Eq. 2 fits as a special case within existing representer theorem frameworks.

- Section 4, Eq. 8:
The interpretation as soft-thresholding PCA is insightful. However, it would be valuable to discuss how this differs from other dimensionality reduction or feature weighting techniques, such as Fisher kernels and probability product kernels.

- Typos:
Line 165: “Not that” should be “Note that.”
Line 221: “achived” should be “achieved.”
Line 341: “chossing” should be “choosing.”
Line 672: “Table ??” should be a valid table number.

The paper proposes a novel approach to supervised and unsupervised learning using data-dependent Fredholm kernels. The authors formulate learning as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation, naturally incorporating unlabeled data and relating the resulting method to standard kernel techniques. They explore the “noise assumption” in semi-supervised learning and demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that Fredholm kernels can effectively leverage unlabeled data under this assumption. The experimental results show competitive performance on several datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation and the resulting Fredholm kernels offer a fresh perspective on kernel methods and semi-supervised learning.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides theoretical analysis supporting the effectiveness of Fredholm kernels under the proposed “noise assumption,” including results on variance reduction.
*   **Clarity of Explanation:** The authors attempt to provide a comprehensive explanation of the method, its connection to existing techniques, and its potential benefits.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes both synthetic and real-world experiments to evaluate the performance of Fredholm kernels.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While some related work is mentioned, the connection to Nyström methods and operator equation approaches within SVM/kernel methods is not sufficiently explored. This limits the contextualization of the contribution.
*   **Representer Theorem Assumption:** The reliance on a representer theorem without proof or a clear reference weakens the theoretical justification.
*   **Comparisons to Existing Methods:**  The comparison to other dimensionality reduction and feature weighting techniques (e.g., Fisher kernels) could be more detailed.
*   **Typographical Errors:** The presence of minor typos detracts from the overall polish of the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to semi-supervised learning.
*   The theoretical analysis provides some justification for the observed performance gains.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the competitiveness of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The connection to existing related work could be strengthened.
*   The assumption of a representer theorem needs to be addressed.
*   Further analysis of the method’s limitations and potential extensions would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that proposes a novel method for semi-supervised learning. While there are some minor weaknesses, the potential benefits of the approach and the solid experimental validation make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding related work and the representer theorem assumption.
