Summary
The paper proposes a learning framework that reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to a new class of data-dependent kernels termed Fredholm kernels. The operator K_pX is estimated from unlabeled data, naturally injecting information about the marginal distribution into the predictor. The authors show that the resulting method is equivalent to a standard kernel algorithm using an induced, unlabeled-data–dependent kernel. They introduce the “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning, arguing that directions of low variance in the unlabeled data are uninformative for the labels and should be attenuated. Theoretical results demonstrate that certain Fredholm kernels suppress low-variance principal components (soft-thresholding behavior) and yield lower-variance estimators of the “true” kernel in noisy settings, compared to applying a standard kernel directly on noisy observations. Empirically, the method performs well on synthetic datasets designed to violate the cluster assumption and on several real-world text, sentiment, and digit recognition tasks.

The approach is related to prior work that uses Fredholm integral equations for density ratio estimation and covariate shift, kernel mean embeddings, and shrinkage estimators in RKHS. It also connects to semi-supervised learning with manifold regularization and data-dependent kernels, while focusing on the distinct “noise assumption.”

Quality
Overall, the technical quality is high. The formulation is sound, and the theoretical claims are supported by clear analyses that connect Fredholm kernels to variance reduction and PCA-like reweighting under Gaussian models. The induced kernel perspective is elegant and yields a practical algorithm.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Equation (3) and PSD assumptions: The paper states that the “outer” kernel k need not be PSD (or symmetric), while Proposition 1 ensures PSD of the Fredholm kernel when KH is PSD. Does the closed-form solution in Eq. (3) and the representer-theorem–style derivation still hold when k is indefinite? Please make the precise assumptions under which Eq. (3) is valid explicit (e.g., requirements on KH, on k, and on the composite operator), and include a self-contained proof or a detailed sketch.
- Derivation details: A brief proof or appendix deriving Eq. (3) from the optimization in Eq. (2), including the matrix identities and the role of unlabeled points in Kl+u and KH, would improve rigor and reproducibility.
- Experimental protocol: The text mentions selecting “optimal” parameters for all methods. Please specify the validation protocol, search spaces, and whether the same labeled validation budget is used across methods. Clarify whether unlabeled data is used in model selection and how this is controlled for fairness.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow. The induced-kernel interpretation and the normalization variant are helpful. Minor issues:
- Line 82: “... will a Euclidean ...” → “... will be a Euclidean ...”
- Line 83: missing “or.”
- Section 4 title: “Fredhom” → “Fredholm.”
- Line 247: “porportional” → “proportional.”
- Line 283: “That is, or ...” → “That is, for ...”
- Line 352: Sentence unclear; please rewrite for readability.
It would also help to explicitly define the “noise assumption” in a concise formal statement and to summarize the computational complexity and memory footprint of constructing k̂F (and its normalized variant), since Kl+u and KH can be large in the semi-supervised setting.

Originality
While related to prior work on Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation and to data-dependent kernels in manifold regularization, the proposed formulation is novel in its focus on semi-supervised learning under the noise assumption and in the specific induced-kernel construction that integrates unlabeled data through a Fredholm operator. The theoretical lens (variance reduction and soft-thresholding behavior) provides fresh insights.

Significance
The contributions are significant. The framework offers a principled way to leverage unlabeled data beyond cluster/manifold assumptions, and the theory clarifies when and why unlabeled data should help. The empirical evaluation spans synthetic stress tests and multiple real-world domains, showing competitive and often superior performance.

Pros
- Clear, principled reformulation that naturally incorporates unlabeled data via an induced kernel.
- Solid theoretical results linking Fredholm kernels to noise suppression and lower-variance kernel estimation.
- Strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets.
- Positive semi-definiteness of the induced kernel under mild conditions (KH PSD).

Cons
- The assumptions required for Eq. (3) with possibly indefinite k should be stated and proved more explicitly.
- Experimental model-selection details need clarification to assess fairness and reproducibility.
- Minor typographical issues and a few sentences needing polish.

Overall evaluation
A well-written paper with strong theoretical underpinnings and convincing experiments. Addressing the points about Eq. (3) and the experimental protocol would further strengthen the submission.
