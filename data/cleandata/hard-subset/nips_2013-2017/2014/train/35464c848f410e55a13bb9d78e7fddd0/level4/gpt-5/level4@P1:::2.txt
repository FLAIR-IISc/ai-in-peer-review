This paper proposes a framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating prediction as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to data-dependent “Fredholm kernels.” The authors show that the learned predictor can be interpreted as a standard kernel method using a new kernel constructed from both labeled and unlabeled data. They motivate semi-supervised learning via a “noise assumption,” distinct from the manifold and cluster assumptions, and provide theoretical results suggesting that Fredholm kernels can suppress low-variance (uninformative) directions and reduce estimator variance. Empirically, they report competitive results on synthetic tasks designed to isolate the noise assumption and on several real datasets.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to integral operators and RKHS methods, to density ratio estimation via Fredholm equations (Que & Belkin), to kernel mean embeddings, and to manifold regularization and data-dependent kernels. The theoretical perspective relating the Fredholm construction to noise suppression and variance reduction is interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in this semi-supervised context.

Strengths:
- Clear and appealing formulation: the Fredholm viewpoint leads to a principled, data-dependent kernel k̂F that is PSD if the inner kernel is PSD.
- Solid intuition and theory: the linear outer kernel yields a reweighted inner product that soft-thresholds small-variance directions; Gaussian choices yield variance reduction relative to using target kernels on noisy observations.
- Practicality: the method reduces to standard kernel machinery with a new kernel matrix KF, and accommodates normalization to address instability with narrow Gaussian outer kernels.
- Empirical evidence: results on both synthetic and real datasets suggest benefits in settings aligned with the noise assumption.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The connection to other operator-based and data-dependent kernel approaches could be sharpened. In particular, the relation to the Nyström method (and kernel PCA), to operator-equation formulations in SVMs/kernel ridge regression, and to Fisher and probability product kernels is not fully clarified.
- The representer theorem for Eq. (2) is assumed rather than established. Given the nonstandard composition via Kp̂X, a precise statement and proof, or an explicit reference showing Eq. (2) fits an existing representer theorem, would strengthen the paper.
- Some exposition details (e.g., notation choices and line references) and typos detract from clarity.

Detailed comments:
- Section 2 (from Eq. 1 to the equation on line 99): Please discuss the Nyström method, which also approximates integral operators (e.g., in kernel PCA), and explain similarities and differences. In what sense is your use of unlabeled data through Kp̂X analogous to, or distinct from, Nyström approximations?
- Section 2, Eq. (2): There is prior work in SVMs and kernel methods that formulates learning via operator equations (e.g., Tikhonov regularization with integral operators on RKHS). Please cite and explain whether Eq. (2) is a special case of such operator formulations or where it differs materially (e.g., the role of the outer kernel and density-weighted operator composing with the RKHS).
- Section 2, line 128 (representer theorem): Because Eq. (2) is less common in learning theory, the paper should either (i) provide a representer theorem tailored to this objective, or (ii) give a precise citation to a generalized representer theorem and explicitly show how Eq. (2) satisfies its conditions. For example, relate your setting to the generalized representer theorems for Tikhonov regularization with linear operators on RKHSs.
- Section 4, Eq. (8): The soft-thresholding PCA interpretation is compelling. Please discuss how this differs from Fisher kernels and probability product kernels. In particular, how does your data-dependent reweighting via unlabeled density (outer kernel + pX) compare to Fisher kernels (which use gradients of generative models) and PPKs (which integrate products of densities)? What assumptions and statistical effects (e.g., variance reduction, robustness to noise) are shared or distinct?
- Minor consistency: the introduction mentions “supervised and unsupervised” learning, while the abstract and rest of the paper focus on supervised and semi-supervised learning. Please unify the terminology.

Evaluation against criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is sound, with informative theory and a clear algorithmic path to implementation. The main gap is the missing representer theorem justification for Eq. (2) and a more detailed comparison to related operator-based approaches.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but certain derivations and references should be made more explicit; several typos remain.
- Originality: The Fredholm-based construction of data-dependent kernels for semi-supervised learning under a noise assumption appears novel and distinct from manifold/cluster-based methods.
- Significance: If broadly validated, the approach offers a principled way to use unlabeled data to suppress nuisance variation and reduce variance, which is valuable in many practical settings.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled framework connecting integral equations and data-dependent kernels.
- Theoretically motivated noise-suppression effects with variance reduction.
- Competitive empirical performance on synthetic and real data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficiently detailed theoretical justification of the representer theorem in the proposed setting.
- Limited discussion of how the approach contrasts with Nyström, Fisher kernels, and probability product kernels.
- Minor clarity and consistency issues.

Overall, I find the paper interesting and promising. Addressing the theoretical justification for Eq. (2) and clarifying the relationships to closely related operator-based and data-dependent kernel methods would significantly strengthen the submission.
