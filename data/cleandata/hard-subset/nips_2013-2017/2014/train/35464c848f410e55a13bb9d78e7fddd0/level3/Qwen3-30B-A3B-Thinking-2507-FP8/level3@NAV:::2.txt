

This paper proposes a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The authors introduce "Fredholm kernels" as data-dependent kernels that naturally incorporate unlabeled data under a "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning. The theoretical analysis connects the approach to kernel methods, with interesting interpretations such as "soft-thresholding PCA," and the empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper's main strengths include its theoretically grounded framework, the novel "noise assumption" perspective, and compelling empirical validation. The connection to kernel methods through the Fredholm kernel is well-motivated, and the "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation provides valuable intuition.

However, several critical issues require resolution. First, the paper fails to discuss the relationship between the proposed Fredholm kernel method and established integral-equation approximations like Nystrom and kernel PCA, which are commonly used for large-scale kernel methods. The authors should clarify similarities and differences with these approaches.

Second, the paper does not adequately cite operator-equation approaches in SVM/kernel literature (e.g., [10] uses Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation). The relationship between Eq. 2 and standard SVM formulations needs explicit clarification.

Third, while the paper references a representer theorem, it does not provide a proof for Eq. 2 or cite a precise reference showing it as a special case. This is a significant theoretical gap that must be addressed.

Fourth, the "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation should be compared to established methods like Fisher kernels and probability product kernels, which also handle noise in different ways.

Finally, the paper contains several typos: line 165 "Not that" (should be "Note that"), line 221 "achived" (should be "achieved"), line 341 "chossing" (should be "choosing"), and line 672 "Table ??" (should be "Table 1").

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with potential practical value, but requires substantial revisions to address these theoretical and presentation issues before it can be considered for publication. The significance would be greatly enhanced by better contextualization with existing literature and more rigorous theoretical grounding.
