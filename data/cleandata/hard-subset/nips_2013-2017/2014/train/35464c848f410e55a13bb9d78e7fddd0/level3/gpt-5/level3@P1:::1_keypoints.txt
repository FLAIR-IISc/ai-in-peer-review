- Reformulates supervised/semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral and introduces a data-dependent "Fredholm kernel" that incorporates unlabeled data.
- Learns f in an RKHS for an inner kernel kH and fits K_p̂X f at labeled points, where K_p̂X is an empirical integral operator using an outer kernel k over labeled+unlabeled data.
- Using the representer theorem, the solution becomes a kernel method with k̂F(x,z) = (1/(l+u)^2) ∑i,j k(x,xi) kH(xi,xj) k(z,xj), which is symmetric PSD if kH is PSD.
- Theoretical analysis under a "noise assumption" shows how Fredholm kernels exploit unlabeled data and proves variance reduction for linear and Gaussian target kernels under Gaussian data/noise models.
- Experiments on synthetic data (violating cluster assumption) and multiple real datasets show competitive or superior performance to RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.
- Connects to prior Fredholm/density-ratio and kernel mean embedding work and to data-dependent kernels for manifold regularization, but differs by producing an explicit PSD composition kernel with a denoising interpretation.
- Distinct from manifold regularization: does not use graph Laplacian geometry encoding but composes inner/outer kernels via an empirical integral operator.
- Technical strength: PSD property proven via factorization Kl+u KH Kl+u^T; Theorems quantify noise suppression and variance reduction.
- For linear Fredholm kernels, principal directions are reweighted with a soft threshold; estimators are (up to scaling) unbiased and have reduced variance versus noisy-input kernels.
- Analysis is clean under Gaussian assumptions and illustrates why unlabeled data can help beyond cluster/manifold assumptions.
- Experimental evaluation is broad and generally supports theoretical claims.
- Need clear derivation and conditions for Eq.(3) and the "push-through" identity, including required symmetry/PSD assumptions on KH and any constraints on k (which may not be PSD/symmetric).
- Clarify whether the matrix inverted in Eq.(3) is guaranteed symmetric positive definite or if the KF reformulation is necessary in practice.
- Explicitly state the representer theorem variant used (loss depending on K_p̂X f) and the role of unlabeled samples in producing the finite expansion.
- Discuss computational complexity and memory costs of computing KH over l+u points and forming KF, and whether Nyström, rank-reduction, or random features are applicable.
- Paper is well organized and the denoising intuition and inner/outer kernel factorization are helpful for understanding.
- Experimental protocol is under-specified: define "optimal parameters", hyperparameter search spaces, validation strategy, number of runs/seeds, and whether unlabeled data is used in tuning; report standard deviations and trial counts.
- Add sensitivity analysis for amount of unlabeled data u, choice of normalized vs unnormalized kernels, and robustness to mis-specified k and kH.
- Minor typos, capitalization, and punctuation errors need correction (specific line/section references provided).
- Originality: novel semi-supervised Fredholm formulation and concrete PSD data-dependent kernel with provable variance reduction under a noise model.
- Significance: broadly applicable and easy to implement on top of kernel solvers; theory provides principled reasons for gains from unlabeled data, potentially stimulating further work.
- Impact would be strengthened by better scalability discussion and analysis for more diverse (non-Gaussian/misaligned) noise models.
- Pros: principled unified framework, new PSD data-dependent kernel, formal denoising/variance-reduction results, and broad empirical support.
- Cons: key algebra and representer theorem variant need explicitness; insufficient experimental protocol and uncertainty reporting; scalability and approximations not discussed; theory relies on Gaussian assumptions.
- Questions for authors: provide full derivation/conditions for Eq.(3); specify hyperparameter search/validation strategy; show performance vs u and normalized vs unnormalized k̂F; outline computational complexity and feasible approximations; quantify sensitivity to mismatched outer/inner kernels.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — compelling, well-grounded framework with solid theory and empirical results; fix derivation and experimental-protocol details to remove remaining concerns. Confidence: high.
