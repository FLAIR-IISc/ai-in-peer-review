Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a semi-supervised learning framework that reformulates supervised risk minimization as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. By introducing the integral operator K_pX tied to the marginal pX and an inner RKHS H with kernel kH, the method learns f in H but predicts via c(x) = (K̂_pX f)(x), where K̂_pX is discretized using both labeled and unlabeled data. A representer-style derivation leads to a closed-form solution that can be reinterpreted as a standard kernel method using a new data-dependent kernel, the Fredholm kernel k̂F(x, z) = Σ_{i,j} k(x, x_i) kH(x_i, x_j) k(z, x_j)/(l+u)^2 (with a normalized variant). This yields an elegant and tractable way to incorporate unlabeled data into the kernel itself.

The paper motivates semi-supervised gains via a “noise assumption”: low-variance directions (under pX) are label-uninformative and should be suppressed. The theory shows (i) with linear outer kernel, the Fredholm kernel induces a data-dependent inner product that soft-thresholds small-variance principal components, and (ii) for linear and Gaussian target kernels under a Gaussian manifold+noise model, Fredholm kernels provide unbiased (up to scaling) but lower-variance estimators than directly applying the target kernel to noisy observations. Empirically, Fredholm kernels perform strongly on synthetic tasks designed to violate the cluster assumption, and on multiple real datasets (text, sentiment, digits), competing with or outperforming RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

The work relates to prior kernel/integral operator approaches (e.g., manifold regularization, mean embeddings, density ratio via Fredholm equations) and to data-dependent kernels for semi-supervised learning, but is distinct in its Fredholm formulation, the data-dependent kernel construction, and the explicit noise-suppression perspective.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation and Fredholm-kernel reinterpretation are well-motivated. Proposition 1 (PSD of the Fredholm kernel if kH is PSD) is important; moving the proof to the main paper would strengthen it.
- One concern: Equation (3) and the representer derivation when the outer kernel k is not PSD or symmetric. Since the representer theorem applies in H (with PSD kH) and K̂_pX acts linearly via evaluations of k(·, x_i), the objective remains quadratic, but convexity/uniqueness hinge on the PSD of the induced Fredholm kernel and regularization. Please include a precise derivation for Eqn. (3) in the general (possibly indefinite, non-symmetric) k case, and conditions ensuring invertibility.
- Theory: The variance-reduction results under Gaussian mixture/subspace noise are clear and insightful, directly supporting the noise assumption. Assumptions are stylized (Gaussian, infinite unlabeled), but the conclusions (soft-thresholding PCA effect; better approximation to the “true” kernel) are convincing and practically relevant.
- Experiments: The suite is broad and shows consistent gains, especially when noise is injected. However, the experimental protocol needs to be specified more precisely (see below).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow. The transition from the Fredholm objective to the data-dependent kernel is clear and helps intuition.
- Please clarify the use of “unnormalized vs normalized” kernels throughout, and the role of scaling constants (e.g., in Theorems 3–4). A consolidated algorithm box would help practitioners.
- Experimental setup needs a clearer description: how “optimal” parameters are chosen, split strategy, and fairness across baselines.

Originality
- The formulation as a Fredholm integral problem and the ensuing data-dependent kernel are novel and distinct from related lines (e.g., manifold regularization, density ratio via Fredholm equations, kernel mean shrinkage). The noise-assumption angle provides a fresh semi-supervised perspective.

Significance
- The approach provides a principled and practical mechanism to exploit unlabeled data by constructing a density-dependent kernel. The theoretical variance-reduction insights and the soft-thresholding PCA effect are valuable and may inspire follow-up work. Results indicate meaningful gains on both synthetic and real datasets.

Pros
- Elegant unification: semi-supervised learning as kernel learning with a data-dependent Fredholm kernel.
- Sound theory supporting the claimed noise-suppression effect; clear variance reduction results under interpretable models.
- Competitive to strong empirical performance across diverse datasets.
- Method remains computationally tractable in the small-to-medium regime; compatible with standard kernel machinery.

Cons
- Missing derivation details for Eqn. (3) when the outer kernel is not PSD/symmetric; conditions for convexity/uniqueness should be explicit.
- Experimental protocol is under-specified: parameter search spaces, validation splits, and transductive vs. inductive usage of unlabeled data are not fully clear.
- Scalability: constructing KH and KF is O((l+u)^2) memory and potentially O((l+u)^3) time; discussion of approximations (Nyström, random features, block methods) would strengthen practical impact.
- Theory focuses on Gaussian assumptions and infinite unlabeled data; some finite-sample discussion or bounds would add depth.

Specific questions and requests
- Please provide the full derivation of Eqn. (3) for general k, and clarify the precise assumptions required for the representer-style solution and invertibility.
- Experimental details: 
  - How are “optimal” parameters chosen? Exact validation protocol, ranges/grids for (t, r, λ), and whether unlabeled data are used in validation.
  - Number of random splits, standard deviations/confidence intervals, and seed control.
  - TSVM and LapRLSC: implementations, graph construction (neighbors, weights), and hyperparameter tuning fairness.
  - Inductive vs. transductive setting at test time; whether u includes test inputs.
- Complexity: Report training/prediction time and memory vs. l and u, and discuss approximate schemes for large u.
- Normalization: Provide guidance on when to prefer the normalized Fredholm kernel and practical stability considerations when k is Gaussian and narrow.
- Can k be non-symmetric in practice without issues? Any empirical differences observed?

Minor comments and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “distint” → “distinct”; “on on” → “on”; “senario” → “scenario”; “kernle” → “kernel”; “unormalized” → “unnormalized”; “SteinJames” → “James–Stein”.
- Please check and fix typos around lines 82–83, Section 4 title, and lines 247, 283, 352 (as currently written). 
- Clarify that the method focuses on supervised and semi-supervised learning (the intro briefly mentions unsupervised).

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper presents a novel and well-argued approach to semi-supervised learning with both theoretical and empirical support. Addressing the derivation/convexity details and clarifying the experimental protocol would solidify the contribution.
