This paper reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to a data-dependent kernel—the Fredholm kernel—that naturally incorporates unlabeled data. The core idea is to learn f in an RKHS H for an “inner” kernel kH while fitting the transformed function Kp̂X f at labeled points, where Kp̂X is an empirical integral operator built from both labeled and unlabeled samples via an “outer” kernel k. Using the representer theorem, the solution reduces to a kernel method with a new kernel k̂F(x, z) = 1/(l+u)2 ∑i,j k(x, xi) kH(xi, xj) k(z, xj). This yields a symmetric PSD kernel as long as kH is PSD. The authors analyze how Fredholm kernels exploit unlabeled data under a “noise assumption” (low-variance directions are uninformative) and prove variance reduction properties for linear and Gaussian target kernels under Gaussian data/noise models. Experiments on synthetic data violating the cluster assumption and on several real datasets demonstrate competitive or superior performance versus RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

Relation to prior work: The approach connects to earlier use of Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation (Que & Belkin, NIPS), kernel mean embeddings, and data-dependent kernels for manifold regularization. Unlike manifold regularization, which encodes geometry via a graph Laplacian, this work induces a new PSD kernel by composing k and kH through the empirical integral operator, with a clear denoising interpretation. The “noise assumption” is distinct from cluster/manifold assumptions and is argued to be compatible with manifold+noise models.

Quality: The paper is technically strong. The PSD property of k̂F follows from the factorization Kl+u KH Kl+u^T. Theorems 2–4 quantify noise suppression: linear Fredholm kernels reweight principal directions with a soft threshold, and for both linear and Gaussian target kernels, the Fredholm estimator is (up to scaling) unbiased with reduced variance compared to using the target kernel directly on noisy inputs. The analysis is clean under Gaussian assumptions and illustrates why unlabeled data can help beyond cluster/manifold assumptions. The experimental section is broad and generally supports the claims.

That said, a few technical points need clarification:
- Equation (3) and the “push-through” identity: Please include a clear derivation and conditions under which (KT Kl+u KH + λI)−1 KT = KT (Kl+u KH KT l+u + λI)−1 holds. In particular, specify required symmetry/PSD assumptions on KH and whether any constraints on k are needed (since k need not be PSD or symmetric). Also clarify whether the matrix inverted in Eq. (3) is guaranteed to be symmetric positive definite or if the right-hand reformulation via KF is necessary in practice.
- Representer theorem instantiation: Because the loss depends on Kp̂X f evaluated at labeled points, the standard representer theorem applies with a bounded linear operator; it would be helpful to explicitly state this version and the role of unlabeled samples in the finite expansion.
- Computational aspects: Training with KF requires computing KH over l+u points and forming KF = Kl+u KH Kl+u^T. Please discuss complexity and memory for large u, and whether Nyström/rank-reduction or random features are applicable.

Clarity: The paper is well organized, and the main ideas are easy to follow. The denoising intuition is compelling, and the factorization into inner/outer kernels helps. However:
- Experimental protocol: The term “optimal parameters” is ambiguous. Please specify hyperparameter search spaces (kernel widths, λ, normalization choices), selection criteria (validation splits, number of runs, seeds), and whether unlabeled data is used during tuning. Report standard deviations and the number of trials. Clarify the transductive vs. inductive protocol (e.g., whether test data is ever used as unlabeled input).
- Please add a brief discussion of sensitivity to u (amount of unlabeled data), the choice between normalized and unnormalized kernels, and robustness to mis-specified k and kH.
- Minor typos/edits (examples): lines 82, 83; Section 4 title capitalization; lines 247, 283, 352; a few punctuation/grammar fixes throughout.

Originality: The formulation is novel in the semi-supervised context and distinct from prior Fredholm/density ratio work and from manifold regularization. Turning the Fredholm view into a concrete PSD, data-dependent kernel that provably reduces variance under a noise model is a substantive contribution. The “noise assumption” is clearly articulated and fills a gap between existing assumptions.

Significance: The method is broadly applicable, easy to implement on top of standard kernel solvers, and empirically competitive across diverse datasets. The theoretical results give a principled reason to expect gains from unlabeled data beyond standard assumptions, which could stimulate further work on operator-induced kernels and task-specific data-dependent kernels. Scalability discussion and more diverse noise models would further strengthen impact.

Pros:
- Clear, principled framework unifying semi-supervised learning and kernel methods via a Fredholm operator.
- New PSD, data-dependent kernel with strong denoising intuition and formal variance reduction results.
- Broad and convincing empirical evaluation.
- Distinct from and complementary to manifold/cluster-based methods; well-positioned within related work.

Cons:
- Key algebra (Eq. 3 and identity) and representer theorem variant should be made explicit.
- Experimental protocol details and uncertainty reporting are insufficiently described.
- Scalability for large unlabeled sets and practical approximations not discussed.
- Theory relies on Gaussian assumptions; some empirical analysis under non-Gaussian/misaligned noise would help.

Questions for the authors:
- Provide a full derivation and conditions for Eq. (3) and the stated identity; is the symmetry/PSD of KH alone sufficient?
- How are hyperparameters chosen exactly? What is the validation strategy and search space?
- How does performance scale with u and with the choice of normalized vs. unnormalized k̂F?
- Can you comment on computational complexity and feasible approximations (Nyström, random features, sparsification)?
- How sensitive is the method to mismatches between the “outer” and “inner” kernels relative to the task?

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling, well-grounded framework with solid theory and strong empirical results. Addressing the derivation and experimental protocol details would remove the remaining concerns. Confidence: high.
