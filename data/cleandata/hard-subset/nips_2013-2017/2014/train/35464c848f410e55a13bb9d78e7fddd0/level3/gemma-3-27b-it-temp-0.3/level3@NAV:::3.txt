## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the definition of “Fredholm kernels.” The core idea is compelling: leveraging the Fredholm formulation allows for a natural incorporation of unlabeled data, effectively addressing the semi-supervised learning problem through a data-dependent kernel construction. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating properties of these kernels, specifically relating to noise suppression under a newly proposed “noise assumption,” and support these findings with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, presenting a complex idea in a digestible manner. The connection to existing kernel methods and related work (e.g., kernel mean embeddings, manifold regularization) is thoughtfully discussed, positioning the contribution within the broader landscape of machine learning. The theoretical results, particularly those concerning the linear Fredholm kernel and its relation to principal component analysis, are interesting and provide valuable insight into the kernel’s behavior. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, bolstering the practical relevance of the proposed approach. 

However, a key practical consideration remains largely unaddressed: the selection of the Fredholm kernel width (parameter *t* in their notation). While the authors mention cross-validation, a more systematic approach, potentially leveraging assumptions about the underlying data distribution, would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the experiments demonstrate strong performance, they lack a comprehensive analysis of *when* the Fredholm kernel excels. The authors suggest a “noise assumption,” but a more detailed exploration of failure cases – perhaps through targeted toy experiments with varying noise levels and data distributions – would clarify the settings where this method is most effective. This would help delineate its advantages over existing techniques. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised learning.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Competitive experimental results.
*   Interesting theoretical properties of the proposed kernel.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of systematic guidance for kernel width selection.
*   Limited exploration of failure cases and conditions for optimal performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding parameter selection and failure case analysis, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and experimental validation justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance to the conference.
