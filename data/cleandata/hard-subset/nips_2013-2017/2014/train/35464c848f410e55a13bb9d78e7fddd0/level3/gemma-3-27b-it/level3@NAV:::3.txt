## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the definition of “Fredholm kernels”. The core idea is to naturally incorporate unlabeled data into the kernel framework through this formulation, effectively performing denoising under a proposed “noise assumption”. The paper provides theoretical analysis of these kernels, demonstrating properties related to noise suppression and variance reduction, and empirically validates their performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, achieving competitive results compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper's strength lies in its elegant combination of mathematical foundations and practical applicability. The derivation and interpretation of the Fredholm kernel as a data-dependent kernel are well-explained, and the connection to existing work in kernel methods, density ratio estimation, and semi-supervised learning is clearly established. The theoretical results, particularly those showing noise suppression, are a valuable contribution. The experimental validation is also robust, demonstrating effectiveness across various datasets and comparison to relevant baselines. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the arguments and understand the proposed method.

However, a key weakness is the lack of a systematic method for selecting the kernel width (parameter *t*) beyond relying on cross-validation. While cross-validation is mentioned, exploring theoretical justifications or heuristics for choosing *t* based on the data characteristics would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the experiments demonstrate strong performance, they lack detailed failure case analysis. Including toy experiments or specific dataset configurations where the Fredholm kernel *doesn’t* perform well would offer a more nuanced understanding of its limitations and the conditions under which it’s most effective. This would clarify the scenarios where the “noise assumption” holds and the method is expected to succeed. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised learning.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of complex concepts.
*   Competitive empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Provides a new perspective on the role of unlabeled data and noise in learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a systematic method for kernel width selection.
*   Absence of detailed failure case analysis.

**Overall Assessment:** This is an interesting and well-executed work that offers a valuable contribution to the field of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning. Despite the minor weaknesses, the originality, clarity, and strong empirical results make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance to the conference.
