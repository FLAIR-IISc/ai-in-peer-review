## Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"

This paper introduces a novel semi-supervised learning framework based on reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the definition of “Fredholm kernels.” The approach elegantly extends kernel methods to incorporate unlabeled data and offers a new perspective on semi-supervised learning through the “noise assumption.” The paper is well-written and clearly presents a technically sophisticated idea, building upon existing kernel methods and relating to recent work in density ratio estimation and kernel mean embeddings. 

The core strength of the paper lies in its theoretical grounding. The authors provide a solid mathematical analysis, deriving a computationally tractable solution and demonstrating how the resulting classifier differs from the standard representer theorem solution while remaining within the kernel framework. The experimental validation is also compelling, with extensive results across diverse datasets demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The connection to the “noise assumption” is a particularly interesting contribution, offering a potentially valuable lens for understanding when and why semi-supervised learning is effective. 

However, several points require attention. First, the validity of Equation 3 is questioned when the associated kernel is not positive semi-definite (PSD). A derivation or proof addressing this scenario is missing and would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution. Second, the experimental protocol lacks clarity. The paper mentions “optimal” parameters but doesn’t detail *how* these parameters were chosen – was it grid search, Bayesian optimization, or another method? A more precise description of the optimization process is crucial for reproducibility. 

Furthermore, while the paper acknowledges related work, a more detailed comparison with recent advances in data-dependent kernels for semi-supervised learning (beyond the citation of [13]) would be beneficial. The paper also contains several minor errors (as noted by the supervisor: Lines 82, 83, Section 4 title, Lines 247, 283, 352) that should be addressed during revision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and novel approach to semi-supervised learning.
*   Convincing experimental results across multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of complex ideas.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for Equation 3 when the kernel is not PSD.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the experimental setup and parameter selection.
*   Minor writing errors throughout the manuscript.



**Overall:** Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical analysis and experimental validation, makes it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding Equation 3, clarifying the experimental protocol, and correcting the identified errors.
