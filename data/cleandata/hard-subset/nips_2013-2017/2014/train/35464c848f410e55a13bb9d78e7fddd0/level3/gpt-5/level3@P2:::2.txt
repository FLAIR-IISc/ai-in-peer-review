Summary and relation to prior work
This paper reformulates supervised and semi-supervised learning as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, KpX f = y, where KpX is an integral operator defined by an “outer” kernel k and the marginal pX. With finite labeled and unlabeled data, this yields an empirical operator Kp̂X and the regularized ERM objective in Eq. (2), which, via a representer argument, reduces to a standard kernel method with a novel data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F built from an “inner” kernel kH and k (Eq. 4), with a normalized variant (Eq. 5). The authors analyze a “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning—low-variance directions (under pX) are label-uninformative—and prove that, under Gaussian models and infinite unlabeled data, Fredholm kernels (a) implement a soft-thresholded PCA-like reweighting when k is linear (Theorem 2), and (b) yield unbiased estimates (up to scaling) of target linear or Gaussian kernels with reduced variance relative to using noisy observations directly (Theorems 3–4). Experiments on synthetic data violating cluster assumptions and on text and digit datasets show competitive or improved performance compared to RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

The work connects to integral operator approaches and RKHS methods (e.g., Schölkopf & Smola; Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini) and to density-ratio estimation via Fredholm equations (Que & Belkin) and kernel mean embeddings (Grünewälder et al.; Muandet et al.). The data-dependent kernel view resembles manifold-regularization kernels and graph/Laplacian-based data-dependent kernels (Belkin, Niyogi & Sindhwani; Chapelle et al.’s cluster kernels). It is also related in spirit to Nyström/kernel PCA views that approximate integral operators from samples and to shrinkage/variance-reduction themes in RKHS estimators.

Strengths
- Conceptually neat reduction of semi-supervised learning to a Fredholm operator equation and an induced data-dependent kernel that is PSD (Prop. 1) when kH is PSD.
- Clear mechanism for leveraging unlabeled data: the operator Kp̂X builds pX into the hypothesis class via k̂F, yielding a principled semi-supervised kernel.
- The “noise assumption” is a useful addition to the taxonomy (orthogonal to cluster; compatible with manifold+noise), with intuitive geometric pictures.
- Theoretical results under Gaussian models are clean and interpretable: soft-thresholding PCA view (Theorem 2) and variance reduction for both linear and Gaussian target kernels (Theorems 3–4).
- Empirical results include synthetic settings constructed to violate cluster assumptions and real datasets where linear and Gaussian kernels are natural.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Representer theorem: Eq. (3) is central. Please prove a representer theorem tailored to Eq. (2) (function-of-operator-of-f setting) or give a precise reference showing Eq. (2) is a special case and derive Eq. (3). At minimum, state assumptions on H, k, and Kp̂X (boundedness/compactness) under which the result holds.
- Prop. 1: Provide the proof (or include in main text) that k̂F is PSD if KH is PSD, and state conditions under which normalization preserves PSD.
- Finite-sample analysis: Most theory assumes infinite unlabeled data (operator defined w.r.t. pX). Please bound the approximation error from replacing KpX with Kp̂X (e.g., in operator norm or in excess risk), and discuss the l,u trade-off.
- Scope of the noise assumption: Theorems 2–4 are for Gaussian pX with additive orthogonal Gaussian noise. Please clarify robustness to non-Gaussian tails, multimodality, or local manifold structure; local versions (with k defining neighborhoods) would strengthen the claim.
- Computational complexity: Building k̂F requires O((l+u)^2) base-kernel computations and O((l+u)^2) for KH, and solving an l×l system. Please report time/memory complexity and discuss scalability. Can Nyström/low-rank approximations be used at the level of kH, k, or KF?

Clarity
- The paper relies on “full version” for key derivations; include essential proofs (Prop. 1, representer theorem sketch) and a self-contained algorithm description.
- Clarify inductive vs transductive use: k̂F is computed with a fixed unlabeled set. How are new test points handled in deployment when pX shifts or when more unlabeled data arrives?
- Notation: be explicit about symmetry/PSD requirements on k vs kH; define all matrices in Eq. (3) and their sizes once; ensure consistent normalization between Eq. (4) and Eq. (5).

Originality
- The specific composition k̂F = k • kH • k with averaging against pX and the noise-suppression analysis are novel to my knowledge. Please more clearly distinguish from:
  - Data-dependent kernels in manifold regularization and cluster kernels (Belkin–Niyogi–Sindhwani; Chapelle–Weston–Schölkopf).
  - Nyström/kernel PCA/integral-operator approximations that also exploit unlabeled data to build operators.
  - Operator-equation perspectives in SVM/KRR (e.g., Tikhonov regularization of integral operators).
  - Mean map and probability product kernels, and Fisher kernels: your “soft-thresholding PCA” and “kernel averaging” views are reminiscent of variance-weighted inner products and model-based kernel constructions. A short comparison would help position the contribution.

Significance
- The idea is broadly useful: a generic way to inject unlabeled density information into a kernel with variance reduction under a plausible noise model. The synthetic and real-data gains are promising, especially when cluster assumptions are violated.
- To increase impact, add stronger baselines (e.g., label propagation/consistency regularization, recent data-dependent kernels) and report variance/std errors and ablations (normalized vs unnormalized; choice of k vs kH; sensitivity to t and λ). Include runtimes.

Specific questions for the authors
- Can you provide learning-rate bounds that account for both labeled and unlabeled sample sizes via operator concentration?
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specification of k and kH? Any guidance for choosing inner vs outer kernels beyond cross-validation?
- Is there an interpretation of k̂F as a convolution/smoothing of kH with respect to pX? Can this be viewed as preconditioning or as composing integral operators K and KH?
- Could you relate Eq. (2) to operator-equation approaches used in SVM/KRR and clarify what is genuinely new in your formulation?

Pros
- Elegant, principled, and general semi-supervised kernel construction.
- Theoretical variance reduction and PCA-like interpretation under clear assumptions.
- Competitive empirical performance; conceptually orthogonal to cluster/manifold assumptions.

Cons
- Missing representer-theorem proof/reference and finite-sample analysis for unlabeled sampling.
- Limited comparisons to closely related data-dependent kernels and operator approximations (Nyström, kernel PCA).
- Scalability and deployment details not discussed.

Minor comments and typos
- Please compare to Fisher kernels and probability product kernels, and to Nyström/kernel PCA operator-based approximations; clarify similarities/differences.
- Cite prior operator-equation approaches in kernel methods and clarify how Eq. (2) differs.
- Typos: line 165 “Not that” → “Note that”; line 221 “achived” → “achieved”; line 341 “chossing” → “choosing”; line 672 “Table ??” → supply correct table number.
- Several references are incomplete; please provide full bibliographic details.

Overall recommendation
Interesting and promising contribution with clear ideas and partial theory; I encourage acceptance conditional on clarifying representer and PSD arguments, adding finite-sample analysis or at least a discussion, and strengthening positioning and experimental comparisons. My confidence is medium.
