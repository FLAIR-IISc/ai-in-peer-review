

This paper introduces a novel semi-supervised learning framework based on Fredholm integral equations, extending kernel-based optimization to incorporate unlabeled data through a discretized Fredholm integral. The authors propose "Fredholm kernels" as data-dependent kernels that effectively leverage unlabeled data under a new "noise assumption" for semi-supervised learning. The framework naturally integrates labeled and unlabeled data, with theoretical analysis demonstrating how Fredholm kernels can suppress noise in high-dimensional feature spaces. The paper connects to related work on kernel methods ([10], [13]) while clearly differentiating its approach from manifold regularization and cluster assumptions.

The paper excels in technical quality and significance. The theoretical results (Proposition 1, Theorems 2-4) provide strong evidence that Fredholm kernels can reduce variance and improve classification under the noise assumption. The extensive experimental evaluation across synthetic and real-world datasets (20 Newsgroups, WebKB, IMDB, MNIST) demonstrates clear performance improvements over baseline semi-supervised methods, particularly when labeled data is scarce. The approach represents a meaningful advancement in semi-supervised learning that could be valuable to practitioners.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making complex ideas accessible. However, two critical issues require attention: (1) The validity of Equation 3 when the kernel is not PSD is not properly addressed, and a derivation or proof is missing for this important case; (2) The experimental protocol lacks clarity regarding how "optimal" parameters were selected and what precisely constitutes "optimality," which undermines reproducibility. Additionally, several minor typos and writing errors need correction (Lines 82, 83, Section 4 title, Lines 247, 283, 352).

The work demonstrates strong originality by introducing a fundamentally different approach to incorporating unlabeled data that is theoretically grounded and empirically validated. While the paper makes a significant contribution to semi-supervised learning, addressing the theoretical and methodological concerns would substantially strengthen it. Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential impact in the field.
