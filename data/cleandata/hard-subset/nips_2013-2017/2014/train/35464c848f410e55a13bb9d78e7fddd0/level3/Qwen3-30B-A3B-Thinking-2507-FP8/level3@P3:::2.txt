

**Review of "Learning with Fredholm Kernels"**

This paper proposes a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning by reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation. The authors introduce "Fredholm kernels" - data-dependent kernels derived from solving the Fredholm problem - which naturally incorporate unlabeled data. The main contribution is a framework that interprets semi-supervised learning through a new "noise assumption" (where low-variance directions in unlabeled data are considered noise), with theoretical and empirical evidence that Fredholm kernels can effectively utilize unlabeled data under this assumption.

The paper connects to several strands of prior work. Most directly related is [10] on Fredholm equations for density ratio estimation. The authors also reference kernel mean embedding [5,6,7], shrinkage estimators in RKHS [9], and manifold regularization [1]. The "noise assumption" provides a fresh perspective that complements rather than replaces the manifold and cluster assumptions in semi-supervised learning.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The Fredholm kernel framework is genuinely novel, and the "noise assumption" provides a compelling theoretical justification for when unlabeled data can be beneficial. The data-dependent kernel approach is a significant contribution to the semi-supervised learning literature.
- **Theoretical Insight:** Section 4's "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation (Theorem 2) is particularly insightful, showing how linear Fredholm kernels suppress low-variance principal components. The theoretical results on noise suppression (Theorems 3-4) are well-motivated and clearly presented.
- **Experimental Validation:** The synthetic experiments (Figure 2) designed to violate the cluster assumption demonstrate clear improvements over baseline methods (TSVM, LapRLSC). The real-world results on text and image datasets show competitive performance, supporting the practical relevance of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Missing Representer Theorem:** The paper claims Eq. 2 can be solved using the Representer Theorem but provides no proof or precise reference. This is a critical gap, as the Representer Theorem is fundamental to kernel methods. The authors should either provide a proof or cite a specific reference showing Eq. 2 as a special case (e.g., Theorem 3.2 in [11]).
- **Insufficient Comparison to Nystrom/Kernel PCA:** The "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation deserves comparison to established methods like Nystrom approximation [16] and kernel PCA [17]. Both methods approximate integral operators but differ significantly in their approach and computational complexity. The paper should clarify how Fredholm kernels differ from these methods and whether they offer advantages.
- **Operator-Equation Context:** The paper should better situate Eq. 2 within the broader literature on operator equations in kernel methods. For example, standard SVM formulations can be viewed as solving a regularized inverse problem (e.g., [12, Section 2.3]), and it would be helpful to clarify how the Fredholm approach differs from this perspective.
- **Comparison to Fisher Kernels:** The "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation is interesting but lacks comparison to other kernel methods that address noise, such as Fisher kernels [18] or probability product kernels [19]. These methods also incorporate additional information for better kernel estimation, and the paper should discuss how Fredholm kernels relate to or differ from these approaches.
- **Typos:** Line 165 ("Not that" → "Note that"), line 221 ("achived" → "achieved"), line 341 ("chossing" → "choosing"), line 672 ("Table ??" → "Table 1").

**Quality:** The theoretical claims are generally sound, but the missing representer theorem weakens the technical foundation. The experimental results are well-conducted and support the main claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though the connection to related work could be strengthened. The "noise assumption" is well-explained, but the paper would benefit from more precise comparisons to existing methods.

**Originality:** The Fredholm kernel framework is genuinely novel, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from manifold and cluster assumptions.

**Significance:** The approach addresses a meaningful problem in semi-supervised learning, with the potential for noise suppression being particularly valuable. The competitive performance on real-world datasets suggests practical utility.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Novel framework with insightful theoretical contributions, strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data, and a fresh perspective on when unlabeled data can be beneficial.
*Con:* Missing representer theorem, insufficient comparison to established methods like Nystrom approximation and Fisher kernels, and incomplete contextualization within operator-equation approaches in kernel literature.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to semi-supervised learning, but the missing representer theorem and insufficient comparison to related work are significant weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions** to address these issues. The authors should provide a proof of the representer theorem for Eq. 2, strengthen the comparison to Nystrom approximation and Fisher kernels, and better contextualize their approach within the literature on operator equations in kernel methods.
