## Review of “Learning with Fredholm Kernels”

This paper presents a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the concept of “Fredholm kernels.” The approach naturally integrates labeled and unlabeled data and offers a potentially interesting perspective on semi-supervised learning through the lens of noise reduction. The paper is well-motivated and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The core idea of framing learning as solving a Fredholm equation is intriguing and connects well with existing kernel methods and integral operator approaches. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its relationship to established literature. Specifically, the authors mention Nystrom/kernel PCA integral-equation approximations (e.g., [12, 11]) but don’t clearly delineate the similarities and differences with their proposed method. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the novelty claim. Furthermore, the connection to operator-equation approaches in SVM/kernel literature (e.g., [10, 11]) should be explicitly addressed, clarifying how Eq. 2 relates to or differs from existing formulations. 

A critical omission is a representer theorem. While the authors mention it, they either need to provide a proof for Eq. 2 or a precise reference and demonstrate how Eq. 2 fits as a special case. This is crucial for establishing the theoretical foundations of the proposed method. The “soft-thresholding PCA” interpretation is interesting, and a comparison to related techniques like Fisher kernels and probability product kernels would be valuable to contextualize the approach and highlight its unique characteristics.

The experimental section is comprehensive, but the presentation could be improved. The choice of parameters for each method should be clearly justified. The discussion of results could also be more nuanced, focusing on scenarios where Fredholm kernels demonstrably outperform other methods and analyzing the reasons behind these improvements. 

Finally, there are a few minor typos that need to be addressed: line 165 “Not that”, line 221 “achived”, line 341 “chossing”, line 672 “Table ??”.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of learning as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Potential for effective utilization of unlabeled data under the “noise assumption.”
*   Competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a representer theorem.
*   Insufficient comparison to related work (Nystrom methods, operator-equation SVMs).
*   Limited discussion of the theoretical underpinnings and connection to existing kernel methods.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel idea. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions addressing the theoretical gaps and providing a more thorough comparison to related work.
