Summary and relation to prior work
This paper reframes supervised and semi‑supervised learning as solving a regularized Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The resulting estimator can be written as a standard kernel method with a novel, data‑dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F that incorporates the unlabeled marginal via an empirical integral operator. The framework handles both square loss and, in principle, margin losses. The authors motivate a “noise assumption” for semi‑supervised learning (low‑variance directions locally are uninformative) and provide two theoretical lenses: (i) with linear outer kernel, the induced inner product performs a soft thresholding of principal directions; (ii) under Gaussian signal+noise models, Fredholm kernels yield unbiased, lower‑variance approximations to a target kernel than directly using noisy observations. Empirically, the method is competitive on synthetic tasks designed to match the assumption and on several real datasets.

The approach connects to a rich line of operator‑theoretic learning: Tikhonov regularization in RKHS via integral operators (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito 2007; Smale & Zhou 2007; Rosasco, De Vito & Verri 2004), density‑ratio estimation via Fredholm equations (Que & Belkin 2009), manifold regularization (Belkin, Niyogi & Sindhwani 2006), diffusion/heat kernels on graphs (Kondor & Lafferty 2002; Coifman & Lafon 2006), and to data‑dependent kernels. It also resonates with classical inverse problems (Engl, Hanke & Neubauer 1996).

Requested clarifications and comparisons
- Nyström and kernel PCA/integral‑equation links. Please discuss how your construction relates to:
  • Kernel PCA as an eigenproblem of the Mercer operator T_k f = λ f (integral equation of the second kind) and its Nyström approximations (Williams & Seeger 2001; Drineas & Mahoney 2005). Your Eq. 2 addresses a first‑kind equation K_pX f = y with Tikhonov regularization, not an eigenproblem, and k̂F = K K_H K^T/(l+u)^2 resembles a two‑sided Nyström/“sandwich” that smooths with the empirical operator. Clarify similarities (use of empirical integral operators; data‑dependent smoothing) and differences (goal: prediction vs eigenfunction approximation; need not have PSD outer kernel; not approximating the spectrum).
  • Diffusion kernels/graph heat kernels, which also convolve with data‑dependent operators. Your normalized Fredholm kernel is especially close—please position relative to diffusion maps and random‑walk kernels, and cite accordingly.
- Operator‑equation approaches in SVM/kernel literature. Please cite and situate Eq. 2 within the Tikhonov framework in RKHS with linear operators: Caponnetto & De Vito (2007) on optimal rates for kernel ridge regression via integral operators; Smale & Zhou (2007) learning theory estimates via integral operators; Rosasco, De Vito & Verri (2004) regularization operators; Engl et al. (1996) for inverse problems. Explain that Eq. 2 is Tikhonov regularization for the linear operator L = (δx1∘K_p̂X, …, δxl∘K_p̂X), i.e., minimizing ∑i V((L f)i, yi) + λ||f||_H^2.
- Representer theorem. The paper states a “standard derivation” but does not provide a proof/reference. Because the loss depends on bounded linear functionals of f, a generalized representer theorem applies (Kimeldorf & Wahba 1971; Schölkopf, Herbrich & Smola 2001; De Vito, Rosasco & Verri 2005; Argyriou, Micchelli & Pontil 2009). Please either:
  • Prove that L_i(f) = (K_p̂X f)(x_i) is a bounded linear functional on H, and that the optimizer lies in span{ k_H(·, x_j) }_{j=1}^{l+u}, yielding Eq. 3; or
  • Give a precise theorem/citation and show Eq. 2 is a special case by specifying L and H’s kernel.
  Also state conditions under which Proposition 1 holds when the outer kernel is not PSD and clarify boundedness of K_p̂X from H to L2.
- “Soft‑thresholding PCA” and connections to Fisher and probability product kernels. The linear‑outer case leading to ΣF‑reweighted inner products is elegant. Please compare empirically and conceptually to:
  • Fisher kernels (Jaakkola & Haussler 1999): mapping x to score features ∇θ log pθ(x) and using Fisher information—data‑dependent via a generative model, vs your data‑driven marginal smoothing without a parametric model.
  • Probability product kernels (Jebara, Kondor & Howard 2004): inner products between distributions using a kernel on the sample space; relate this to your convolution k → k̂F that averages k_H(u,v) against local neighborhoods weighted by pX and k.
  Clarify when Fredholm kernels may outperform/underperform these distributional kernels.

Quality
- Theory is sound for the Gaussian models considered and offers intuition (variance reduction; soft PCA). Please expand proofs (or move from “full version” to appendix) and state assumptions (bounded kernels; operator norms; consistency with u→∞). Discuss inductive vs transductive use: k̂F depends on the unlabeled pool; how do you handle new test points if the unlabeled set changes?
- Experiments are diverse and generally supportive. Report computational cost: forming K̂F costs O((l+u)^2) memory and O((l+u)^3) time for solving—please discuss scalability and whether Nyström/random features could approximate K̂F.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized. Please add a positioning subsection covering Nyström, diffusion kernels, and operator‑theoretic regularization. Provide a precise representer‑theorem statement. Define clearly when to use normalized vs unnormalized k̂F and how to choose t vs r (beyond cross‑validation).

Originality
- The Fredholm‑kernel construction is a novel and interesting data‑dependent kernelization route for semi‑supervised learning, distinct from graph Laplacian regularization and from prior Fredholm use in density‑ratio estimation.

Significance
- The idea is simple to implement (K̂F = K K_H K^T) yet broad. If scalability issues are addressed and positioning clarified, this could be a useful addition to the semi‑supervised toolkit, especially in noise‑dominated regimes.

Pros
- Conceptually novel reformulation; clear kernelization.
- Theoretical insight linking to soft‑thresholding PCA and variance reduction.
- Competitive empirical results on synthetic and real datasets.
- Flexibility to combine different inner/outer kernels.

Cons
- Missing formal representer theorem and precise operator assumptions.
- Limited discussion of connections to Nyström, diffusion kernels, and operator‑regularization literature.
- Scalability and inductive use not fully addressed.
- The “noise assumption” could be formalized further and contrasted with alternatives.

Minor issues and typos

- Line 221: “achived” → “achieved”
- Line 341: “chossing” → “choosing”
- Line 672: “Table ??” → fix reference
- Please ensure all external proofs are included or linked for the camera‑ready.

Overall: interesting and promising data‑dependent kernel framework; with the above clarifications (especially representer theorem and related‑work positioning), the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
