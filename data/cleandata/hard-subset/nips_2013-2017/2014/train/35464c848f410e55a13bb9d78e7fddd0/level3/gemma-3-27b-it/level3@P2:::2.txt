This paper introduces a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, leading to the definition of “Fredholm kernels.” The core idea is compelling: leveraging unlabeled data to denoise classifiers by interpreting the learning problem through the lens of integral operators. The paper provides theoretical justification under a “noise assumption” and demonstrates competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution.

The paper effectively positions its work within the broader context of kernel methods and integral operators in machine learning, referencing key works such as [12, 11] and related approaches in density ratio estimation [10] and kernel mean embeddings [5, 6, 7]. The authors clearly articulate the connection to manifold regularization [13] and data-dependent kernels within that framework. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work leveraging integral equation formulations. Specifically, the authors should discuss similarities and differences with Nyström methods and kernel PCA, which also rely on integral equation approximations of kernel matrices. Furthermore, the existing literature on operator-equation approaches within the SVM and kernel literature (e.g., works building on the representer theorem) should be acknowledged, and a clear explanation of how Eq. 2 relates to or differs from these established formulations would strengthen the contribution.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel theoretical framing and the demonstrated empirical performance. The “noise assumption” is a useful and intuitive way to characterize scenarios where unlabeled data can be beneficial, and the theoretical results in Section 4 provide valuable insight into the behavior of Fredholm kernels under this assumption. The experiments, encompassing both synthetic and real-world datasets, offer strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The authors appropriately demonstrate the performance against several baselines and provide thorough ablation studies.

However, several weaknesses require addressing. A crucial omission is a formal justification of the solution obtained via the representer theorem. The authors mention it, but either a proof of the representer theorem for the proposed formulation (Eq. 2) or a precise reference to a suitable theorem and a clear demonstration of how Eq. 2 arises as a special case is essential. Without this, the computational tractability of the method is not fully justified. The discussion of the “soft-thresholding PCA” interpretation in Section 4.1 is intriguing. The authors should expand on this, explicitly comparing the Fredholm kernel's behavior to that of Fisher kernels and probability product kernels, which also perform feature weighting based on class discrimination. Furthermore, the normalization of the Fredholm kernel (Eq. 5) seems crucial for stability, and more discussion about why this normalization is necessary and under what conditions would be helpful.

Clarity is generally good, but there are several minor typos that should be addressed: line 165 “Not that” should be “Note that,” line 221 “achived” should be “achieved,” line 341 “chossing” should be “choosing,” and line 672 “Table ?? ” should be replaced with the correct table number.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of semi-supervised learning based on Fredholm integral equations.
*   Clear theoretical justification under a plausible “noise assumption.”
*   Competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Potential to provide insights into the role of unlabeled data in learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Missing proof or justification via the representer theorem.
*   Insufficient discussion of related work on integral equation approaches (Nyström, kernel PCA).
*   Lack of detailed comparison to related feature weighting methods (Fisher kernels, probability product kernels).
*   Minor typos throughout the manuscript.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the justification of the representer theorem and the expanded discussion of related work, will substantially strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. The clarity and presentation are generally strong, and the empirical results are convincing.
