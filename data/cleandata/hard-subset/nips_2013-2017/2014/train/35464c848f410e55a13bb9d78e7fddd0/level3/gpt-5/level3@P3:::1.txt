Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a semi-supervised learning (SSL) framework that reformulates supervised optimization as a discretized, regularized Fredholm integral equation. The key idea is to compose an “outer” kernel k with an “inner” RKHS kernel kH via an integral operator KpX built from the marginal pX and approximated by unlabeled data. The resulting decision function can be seen as a standard kernel method using a new, data-dependent “Fredholm kernel” k̂F(x, z) = (l+u)−2 Σi,j k(x, xi) kH(xi, xj) k(z, xj). This kernel is PSD whenever KH is PSD. The paper motivates SSL via a “noise assumption,” complementary to cluster and manifold assumptions, and proves that Fredholm kernels can suppress low-variance directions (a soft-PCA effect) and yield variance-reduced estimators of target kernels under simple latent Gaussian models. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets (text, sentiment, digits) show competitive to superior performance versus RLSC, TSVM, and LapRLSC.

The approach is related to manifold regularization and data-dependent kernels [e.g., Sindhwani–Niyogi–Belkin, ICML’05], to Fredholm approaches in density-ratio estimation (Que–Belkin, NIPS), and to kernel mean embedding/shrinkage work. The novelty here is a principled Fredholm-based construction that uses unlabeled data to induce a PSD, data-dependent kernel for general losses, with theory targeting noise suppression rather than manifold smoothness or cluster separation.

Quality
- Theory: The technical contributions are solid. Proposition 1 (PSD of k̂F) is clean; Theorem 2 (soft-thresholding of principal components for linear outer kernel) and Theorems 3–4 (variance reduction and unbiasedness up to scaling for linear/Gaussian target kernels) substantiate the “noise assumption” story. The derivations are plausible and useful.
- Validity of Eq. (3) and representer theorem conditions: Please provide a clear derivation. The objective minimizes squared loss on {K̂pX f(xi)} with Tikhonov regularization in H (with kernel kH), where K̂pX depends linearly on f via evaluations at xl+1,…,xl+u through the (possibly indefinite/asymmetric) kernel k. A standard representer theorem for linear functionals implies f*(·) lies in span{ kH(·, xj) }j=1..l+u provided kH is PSD and the linear evaluation functionals are bounded. Then the normal equations yield v = (KTl+u Kl+u KH + λI)−1 KTl+u y; the identity (KTl+u Kl+u KH + λI)−1 KTl+u = KTl+u (Kl+u KH KTl+u + λI)−1 is a special case of the push-through/Woodbury identity and holds for any A := Kl+u and symmetric C := KH. Thus Eq. (3) does not require k to be PSD; it does require KH to be PSD (to define H and ensure convexity/strong convexity when λ > 0) and KH symmetric. Please include this derivation and explicitly state the assumptions (boundedness of evaluation functionals, PSD/symmetry of KH, no PSD requirement on k).
- Experiments: The breadth is convincing, with gains where the noise assumption is plausible and parity where classical SSL should help less. However, the experimental protocol needs clarification: “optimal” parameters are mentioned, but the selection process is not fully specified. Please detail:
  - Which hyperparameters were tuned for each method (λ, t and/or r for k and kH, normalization constants when applicable, TSVM/LapRLSC parameters)?
  - How was “optimal” chosen—cross-validation on labeled data only, or was a labeled validation set carved out of the labeled pool? Were unlabeled labels ever used in tuning? Provide grid ranges and selection criteria.
  - Number of random splits, seeds, and whether results are reported as mean ± std.
  - For fairness, ensure all baselines use the same tuning budget and access to unlabeled data only via their standard formulations.
  - Complexity/scalability: computing KF = Kl+u KH KTl+u is O((l+u)^2) storage and O((l+u)^3) time for naive solves; please discuss practical complexity, use of low-rank approximations (Nyström, random features), and sensitivity to u.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The Fredholm-to-kernel reformulation is elegant and makes the method easy to implement. Suggestions:
- Move a concise derivation of Eq. (3) and the matrix identity into the main paper (or at least summarize assumptions).
- Provide a short algorithm box (inputs, construction of KF, training procedure) and a complexity discussion.

Originality
The work is novel in how it uses a Fredholm integral formulation to induce a PSD data-dependent kernel for SSL, distinct from Laplacian/manifold regularization and from prior Fredholm usage in density-ratio estimation. The “noise assumption” perspective and variance-reduction analyses are new and complementary to cluster/manifold assumptions.

Significance
The idea is broadly applicable wherever unlabeled data is plentiful and noise corrupts observed features. The method slots into standard kernel pipelines, is computationally tractable, and shows consistent gains on diverse datasets. The theoretical insights should stimulate further work on task-appropriate data-dependent kernels and SSL under realistic noise models.

Questions for the authors
- Eq. (3) and representer theorem: Please add a formal derivation and precise conditions under which Eq. (3) holds when k is not PSD or symmetric. Are there pathological k for which boundedness fails?
- Stability of the normalized variant (Eq. (5)): any guarantees or empirical guidance on when normalization is preferable? Sensitivity to small kernel widths?
- Experimental protocol: Please specify hyperparameter grids, validation strategy, splits, repetitions, and ensure fairness across baselines. Clarify “optimal” precisely.
- Sensitivity analyses: How do performance and compute scale with u? What happens if unlabeled data are mildly distribution-shifted? An ablation where unlabeled are replaced by noise would help.

Pros
- Elegant Fredholm-to-kernel reduction; PSD kernel guaranteed if KH PSD.
- Strong and relevant theory (PCA-like shrinkage; variance reduction).
- Effective across multiple domains; unlabeled data clearly helps under noise.
- Clear distinction from related data-dependent kernel and manifold methods.

Cons
- Missing derivation/assumptions for Eq. (3) when k is indefinite/asymmetric.
- Incomplete hyperparameter/validation protocol details; fairness needs clearer documentation.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability with large u.
- Several typos and minor presentation issues.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is technically sound, novel, and practically impactful. With added clarity on Eq. (3), a more transparent experimental protocol, and minor edits, it will make a strong contribution to SSL and kernel methods.
