## Review of “Learning with Fredholm Kernels”

This paper presents a novel framework for supervised and semi-supervised learning based on reformulating the learning problem as a regularized Fredholm integral equation, resulting in “Fredholm kernels.” The core idea of leveraging the Fredholm equation for kernel construction and its connection to data-dependent kernels is interesting and potentially valuable, particularly for semi-supervised learning. The authors explore the “noise assumption” and provide both theoretical and empirical support for the effectiveness of their approach. 

The paper successfully positions its work within the broader context of kernel methods and semi-supervised learning, referencing relevant literature (e.g., manifold regularization, kernel mean embedding). The exploration of the "noise assumption" as a justification for semi-supervised learning is a valuable contribution, offering an alternative perspective to the standard cluster and manifold assumptions. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the competitive performance of the proposed Fredholm kernels.

However, several areas require clarification and further development. The connection to existing operator equation approaches in SVM/kernel literature (e.g., work leveraging representer theorems directly on integral equations) is not fully explored. Specifically, Eq. 2 should be explicitly discussed in relation to these prior works – how does it generalize, or differ? Crucially, a representer theorem needs to be either proven for Eq. 2 or a precise reference provided, demonstrating Eq. 2 as a specific case. 

The authors mention a "soft-thresholding PCA" interpretation, which is intriguing. A detailed comparison to established techniques like Fisher kernels and probability product kernels – which also aim for feature selection/dimensionality reduction – would strengthen the paper. How do the theoretical guarantees (or lack thereof) differ? How does the computational complexity compare? 

Finally, there are a few minor issues: line 165 contains “Not that”, line 221 contains “achived”, line 341 contains “chossing”, and Table ?? is referenced on line 672. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation leveraging Fredholm equations for kernel learning.
*   Interesting “noise assumption” for semi-supervised learning.
*   Competitive empirical performance.
*   Clear presentation and connection to relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion regarding the connection to existing operator-equation approaches.
*   Missing representer theorem proof or precise reference.
*   Insufficient comparison to related kernel techniques like Fisher kernels.



Overall, the paper presents a promising approach with strong potential. Addressing the concerns regarding theoretical justification and comparative analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its likelihood of acceptance.
