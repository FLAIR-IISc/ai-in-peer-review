This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the challenge of extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, which is known to be non-trivial. PDMM randomly updates a subset of blocks in parallel, aiming for better scalability and potentially faster convergence compared to existing methods like Gauss-Seidel ADMM or Jacobian variants. The paper establishes convergence guarantees with iteration complexity and demonstrates performance improvements in robust PCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of multi-block ADMM methods. It accurately describes the two main categories: Gauss-Seidel variants and Jacobian variants, referencing key papers in each area ([15, 14, 10, 17]). The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of existing approaches, such as strict convergence conditions or limited parallelism (e.g., RBSUMM [14] only updating one block at a time). The connection to sADMM and PJADMM is a key contribution in understanding the broader context of these methods.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The convergence analysis, while presented concisely in the main text with detailed proofs deferred to the supplement, seems rigorous. The assumptions made (Assumption 1) are standard for ADMM-type methods. The introduction of the backward step on the dual update and the consideration of sparsity in the step size calculation are well-motivated. The connection between PDMM and sADMM/PJADMM provides valuable insights. However, a critical weakness lies in the lack of discussion regarding the computational cost of each iteration. While parallelism is highlighted, the overhead associated with synchronization and the impact of choosing different K values (number of blocks updated) on overall runtime are not thoroughly analyzed. The provided experimental results help somewhat, but a more detailed cost analysis would strengthen the claims. The formulation and derivation of PDMM appear correct, and the justification for the chosen parameters (τi, νi) is convincing.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is reasonably clear, although the use of multiple indices (i, j, k) can be slightly confusing at times. The introduction provides adequate motivation for the problem and the proposed solution. The explanations of the key ideas (dual backward step, sparsity consideration) are understandable. However, the section on "Connections to Related Work" is a bit dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The use of mathematical notation is appropriate for the target audience, but more explanatory text surrounding key equations would improve readability. The summary of the theoretical results in Section 3 is concise, and referring the reader to the supplementary material for detailed proofs is appropriate.

**Originality:** The proposed PDMM algorithm represents a novel contribution to the field of multi-block ADMM methods. The combination of randomized block updates, the dual backward step, and the consideration of sparsity in the step size selection are not found in existing methods. The extension of PDMM to handle overlapping blocks is also a noteworthy contribution. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM, establishing PDMM as a unifying framework, further enhances the originality of the work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed – scaling ADMM to multiple blocks – is significant in many machine learning applications. The potential for faster convergence and improved scalability offered by PDMM is valuable. The demonstrated performance improvements in robust PCA and overlapping group lasso suggest that PDMM could be a useful tool for practitioners. The theoretical results provide a solid foundation for further research in this area. The fact that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, a common scenario in many applications, increases its practical relevance. The work advances the state of the art by providing a more flexible and potentially efficient alternative to existing multi-block ADMM methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
*   Addresses a significant problem in optimization and machine learning.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements in practical applications.
*   Provides a unifying framework for existing ADMM variants (sADMM, PJADMM).
*   Handles overlapping blocks, increasing applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed computational cost analysis.
*   Some sections (e.g., "Connections to Related Work") could be more clearly explained.
*   Experimental evaluation, while positive, could be more comprehensive. Limited comparison with more recent methods.




**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept. While the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the computational cost and some minor improvements in clarity, the novel algorithm, theoretical results, and promising experimental performance make this a valuable contribution to the field. The originality and significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses.
