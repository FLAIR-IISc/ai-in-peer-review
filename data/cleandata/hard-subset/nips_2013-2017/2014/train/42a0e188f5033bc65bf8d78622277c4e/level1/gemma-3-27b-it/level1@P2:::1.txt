This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method, PDMM, for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly update multiple blocks in parallel, building on and generalizing the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The paper provides convergence analysis and demonstrates empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of extending ADMM to multiple blocks are clearly stated, and the proposed PDMM addresses these limitations by introducing randomization and a backward step on the dual update. The connection to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM is a valuable contribution, providing a unifying perspective and clarifying the role of different components within these algorithms. The theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, is a significant strength. The experimental results, while limited to two applications, support the claims of improved performance compared to existing methods.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the paper extensively cites related work, it occasionally lacks sufficient depth in discussing the nuances of those works. For example, the discussion of RBSUMM could be more detailed, highlighting the specific challenges it faces and how PDMM overcomes them. The notation is dense, especially with the introduction of multiple indices (i, j, K̃i). While the authors attempt to define everything, the reader may struggle to keep track of all variables. The explanation of the intuition behind the step size selection (τi, νi) could be clearer, particularly regarding the influence of block sparsity and the number of randomized blocks. The experimental section, while demonstrating performance improvements, lacks a detailed ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of PDMM (e.g., the backward step, the randomized block selection). Furthermore, the experimental setup appears quite basic; a comparison with more sophisticated solvers or a wider range of datasets would strengthen the results. The claim that “PDMM can solve overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion” requires more elaboration. While the description in Section 2.3 is helpful, the advantages of using PDMM over existing methods designed for overlapping group lasso are not fully explored. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed PDMM algorithm offers a novel approach to multi-block ADMM, addressing limitations of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, with convergence analysis and iteration complexity guarantees.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Establishing the relationship between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is insightful and provides a more complete understanding of the landscape of ADMM-based methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM on relevant applications (RPCA, overlapping group lasso).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation:** Dense and potentially confusing notation.
*   **Intuition:** Step size selection justification could be clearer.
*   **Experimental Depth:** Limited ablation study and basic experimental setup.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** Some related work discussions lack nuance.
*   **Overlapping Groups:** Benefit of PDMM for overlapping groups not fully explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   The algorithm addresses a significant challenge in the field of optimization and has the potential to impact applications in machine learning and data science.
*   The connections to existing work provide valuable insights and contribute to a better understanding of ADMM-based methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The notation and presentation could be improved for better clarity.
*   The experimental evaluation is relatively limited and could benefit from a more thorough analysis.
*   The intuition behind some design choices (e.g., step size selection) is not fully explained.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The technical contributions are significant, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims made by the authors. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental depth, these do not detract from the overall value of the work. The paper offers a valuable addition to the literature on ADMM and has the potential to be influential in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding notation and experimental evaluation.
