- Problem: Introduces PDMM, a parallel randomized block coordinate method for block-separable convex optimization with multi-block linear constraints.  
- Contribution: PDMM randomly updates multiple blocks in parallel, acting like randomized block coordinate descent.  
- Theoretical guarantees: Proves global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size.  
- Unification: Shows sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified framework.  
- Overlapping blocks: Claims PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, addressing an open problem in RBCD literature.  
- Related work: Properly cites and distinguishes PDMM from prior ADMM variants and randomized block coordinate methods.  
- Strength — Theoretical soundness: Rigorous convergence proofs and novel proof elements (dual backward step, adaptive step sizes).  
- Strength — Practical relevance: Empirical improvements on RPCA and overlapping group lasso with careful experiments.  
- Strength — Novel insights: Clarifies connections between existing ADMM variants via PDMM.  
- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, consistent notation, logical flow from formulation to experiments.  
- Weakness — Step size sensitivity: Experimental section lacks analysis of PDMM sensitivity to theoretical step-size choices and potential tuning benefits.  
- Weakness — Limited comparisons: Comparison with RBSUMM and other recent parallel methods is insufficient and lacks verification of required assumptions.  
- Weakness — Overlapping block explanation: Insufficient detail on how PDMM concretely handles overlapping blocks versus other methods.  
- Weakness — Runtime vs iterations: Superficial explanation for runtime vs iteration trade-offs (e.g., GSADMM vs PDMM) without detailed complexity analysis.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, significant contribution with sound theory, practical improvements, and valuable insights; minor weaknesses do not undermine the paper.  
- Recommendation: Accept — meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
