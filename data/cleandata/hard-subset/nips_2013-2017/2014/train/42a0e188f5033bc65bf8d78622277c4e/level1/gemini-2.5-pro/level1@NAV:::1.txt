This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with multi-block linear constraints. Addressing the well-known convergence challenges of extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) beyond two blocks, PDMM introduces a parallel randomized block coordinate update scheme. This approach builds upon prior work in multi-block ADMM, such as the Gauss-Seidel methods of Hong et al. and Jacobian methods like sADMM and PJADMM. The key technical innovations are a "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm and an adaptive step-size rule that incorporates the constraint matrix's sparsity and the number of blocks updated in parallel. The authors provide a unifying view by showing that existing parallel methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and difficult problem in large-scale optimization. The proposed dual update mechanism is a novel and well-motivated solution to the instability of naive parallel ADMM. The theoretical unification of sADMM and PJADMM is a valuable conceptual contribution that deepens the understanding of this family of algorithms. Furthermore, the application to overlapping group lasso demonstrates a significant extension over standard randomized block coordinate descent methods.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, which is a strong result for a randomized parallel method. Claims are well-supported by convincing experiments on standard machine learning tasks (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) that show PDMM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical details are explained effectively. The connection to related work is particularly insightful and well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Guidance:** The choice of K, the number of blocks to update in parallel, is a critical hyperparameter. While the experiments effectively demonstrate its impact, the paper would be strengthened by providing more practical guidance or heuristics on how to select an optimal K based on problem structure or computational architecture.
*   **Reproducibility:** The experimental section could be improved by providing more explicit details on the parameter tuning process for the baseline algorithms to ensure the comparisons are fully transparent.

In summary, this is a high-quality submission that makes a significant contribution to the literature on distributed and parallel optimization. The proposed method is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically effective.
