- Summary: Addresses minimizing sum of block-separable convex functions under linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate ADMM that updates K blocks in parallel with a dual backward step and adaptive step sizes.  
- Summary: Provides global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, unifies sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, and shows empirical gains on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

- Strength: Tackles an important multi-block ADMM challenge with a novel combination of randomized updates, dual backward step, and adaptive step sizes.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical analysis—complete convergence proof and O(1/T) ergodic rate using a Lyapunov-based argument.  
- Strength: Clear exposition and insight, including motivation for the dual backward step and unification of existing parallel ADMM variants.  
- Strength: Convincing experiments on representative problems with thorough baselines and useful analysis of performance vs. number of parallel blocks K.

- Weakness: No experiments on true parallel hardware—all reported runs were sequential, so actual parallel speedups are not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: Limited high-level intuition for the specific step-size formulas; more explanation of how they arise from proof requirements would help.  
- Weakness: Strong claim that RBSUMM failed on overlapping group lasso needs more tuning details to substantiate.

- For acceptance: Proposes a novel, provably convergent parallel algorithm for an important optimization class.  
- For acceptance: Offers strong theoretical guarantees with constant step size and a unifying view of prior methods.  
- For acceptance: Demonstrates empirical state-of-the-art performance on challenging ML tasks and practical insights on K.  
- For acceptance: Addresses problems (e.g., overlapping-group) that are hard for standard BCD methods.  
- For acceptance: Likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

- Against acceptance: Core parallelism claim is not empirically validated on parallel hardware.  
- Against acceptance: Derivation and motivation of the key step-size rule could be better explained.

- Conclusion: High-quality, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective paper with minor but addressable weaknesses; strong recommend for acceptance.
