

# Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM for two blocks is well-understood, generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel, using a "dual backward step" to address non-convergence issues in direct Jacobi updates. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, connects PDMM to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM as special cases), and demonstrates superior performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by establishing global convergence and iteration complexity for a parallel multi-block method where such guarantees were previously lacking. The dual backward step insight (equations 6-7) elegantly addresses a fundamental limitation of direct Jacobi updates for multiple blocks, as noted in [7, 15, 10].

2. **Novel Connection to Existing Methods:** The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM (Section 2.2) provides valuable theoretical insight into the role of dual backward steps, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of ADMM variants.

3. **Practical Impact:** The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly compelling, as the paper identifies and solves a problem (randomized block coordinate descent with overlapping blocks) that has remained open [16, 18]. The experimental results (Section 4) consistently show PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both convergence speed and runtime.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear notation and logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experiments. The connection between theory and practice is well-maintained throughout.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Step Sizes:** While the paper proposes specific values for τi and νi (equation 8), it lacks a clear theoretical justification for these particular forms. The authors state they "consider the sparsity of A and the number of randomized blocks," but don't provide a rigorous derivation of these choices. This weakens the theoretical contribution.

2. **Limited Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper shows PDMM's performance with different K (number of blocks updated), but doesn't analyze how sensitive the algorithm is to the choice of K or the impact of different Bregman divergences (ηj). The experimental section shows PDMM3 (K=3) outperforms PDMM1 (K=1) in RPCA, but the reverse is true in overlapping group lasso, suggesting the optimal K depends on problem structure - an important consideration missing from the analysis.

3. **Incomplete Comparison with Overlapping Group Methods:** While the paper claims PDMM solves overlapping groups, it doesn't compare with specialized methods for overlapping groups (e.g., [1, 28, 27]) beyond sADMM and RBSUMM. For the overlapping group lasso experiments (Figure 2), PDMM outperforms ADMM, but it would be valuable to see how it compares with methods specifically designed for overlapping groups.

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper demonstrates PDMM's runtime advantages, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity per iteration across methods. For example, it's unclear whether the per-iteration cost of PDMM (particularly for the dual backward step) is comparable to or higher than other methods.

**Significance Assessment:**
The problem of multi-block optimization is of significant importance in large-scale machine learning. PDMM addresses a critical gap in the literature with a method that offers both theoretical guarantees (unlike most existing multi-block methods) and practical advantages (as shown in experiments). The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides a unified framework that advances our understanding of ADMM variants. The application to overlapping groups is particularly significant as it solves a problem where traditional block coordinate descent fails.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to a well-known problem in optimization. The dual backward step insight is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate practical advantages over state-of-the-art approaches. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**
Accept. The paper presents a novel algorithm with solid theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical results. While there are some minor weaknesses in the theoretical justification for step sizes and limited sensitivity analysis, these do not undermine the overall contribution. The paper significantly advances the understanding of multi-block ADMM and offers a practical method that outperforms existing approaches.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Clear theoretical contribution with convergence and complexity guarantees
- Novel insight on dual backward step addressing non-convergence
- Unified understanding of existing ADMM variants
- Strong experimental results on two important applications
- Practical impact for large-scale optimization problems

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical justification for specific step size choices
- Incomplete comparison with specialized methods for overlapping groups
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to key parameters (K, ηj)

The paper is a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with both theoretical and practical importance. It is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear advance beyond previous work.
