- Introduces PDMM: a randomized, parallel multi-block ADMM with a dual backward step for block-separable convex problems with linear constraints.  
- Aims to fix known convergence issues of naive multi-block ADMM by updating random subsets of blocks each iteration.  
- Provides theoretical global convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on RPCA and overlapping group lasso versus several baselines.  
- Situates work within extensive ADMM literature and draws connections to sADMM and PJADMM, offering a unified perspective.  
- Clearly motivated problem setting and well-presented algorithm and notation.  
- Includes overlapping block scenarios and relates PDMM to randomized block coordinate descent (RBCD).  
- Strength: novel method addressing multi-block ADMM convergence via randomization and dual backward step.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor with convergence proofs and complexity results.  
- Strength: clear presentation and explicit connections to existing methods.  
- Strength: empirical validation shows practical effectiveness on targeted applications.  
- Strength: detailed relationship analysis between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to hyperparameters (τi, νi, K) not thoroughly investigated; tuning details sparse.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed computational cost per iteration and communication overhead analysis for parallel updates.  
- Weakness: reliance on Assumption 1 (existence of a KKT point) needs more discussion regarding real-world applicability.  
- Weakness: comparisons limited — missing newer/accelerated solvers and potential fairness issues with public sADMM code.  
- Weakness: inexact PDMM with Bregman divergences is underdeveloped; needs concrete examples and links to convergence.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound algorithm with compelling empirical results and strong literature integration.  
- Argument against acceptance: incomplete sensitivity analysis, insufficient per-iteration cost evaluation, limited comparisons, and weak Bregman discussion.  
- Overall: significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address parameter sensitivity, computational cost, and Bregman divergence details (e.g., ablation study and runtime/communication analysis).
