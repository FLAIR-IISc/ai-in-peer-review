## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, extending the well-studied Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting. The authors address the challenges of generalizing ADMM, where naive parallelization can lead to divergence, by introducing a randomized block coordinate update scheme with a backward step on the dual update, informed by the sparsity structure of the problem. They establish global convergence and iteration complexity results and demonstrate performance gains over state-of-the-art methods in robust PCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on ADMM and its variants (e.g., [2, 15, 17, 26]). It correctly identifies the difficulties in extending ADMM to multiple blocks and positions PDMM as a flexible framework encompassing existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, is a significant contribution. The connection drawn between PDMM and existing methods provides valuable insight into the behavior of different dual update strategies. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to multi-block ADMM, addressing the convergence issues of naive parallelization through a randomized update scheme and a carefully designed dual update.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds.
* **Generality:** PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, offering a unified framework for understanding their behavior.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly situates itself within the existing literature and highlights the contributions of PDMM.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides formulas for τi and νi, the rationale behind these choices could be further clarified. The impact of K (number of randomly updated blocks) on performance isn’t fully explored beyond the empirical results.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of problems would strengthen the claims of general applicability.
* **Comparison to Recent Methods:** While comparisons to established methods are provided, the paper could benefit from a discussion of more recent advancements in multi-block ADMM variants.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of optimization: extending ADMM to the multi-block setting.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the convergence behavior of PDMM.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details of PDMM.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The parameter tuning discussion could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving multi-block convex optimization problems. While the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the existing results are promising. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity of parameter tuning and potentially include a discussion of more recent related work.
