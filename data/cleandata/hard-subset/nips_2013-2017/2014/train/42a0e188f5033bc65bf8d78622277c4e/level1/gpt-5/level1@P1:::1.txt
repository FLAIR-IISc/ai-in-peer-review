Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. Each iteration selects K primal blocks and updates them in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian with an additional Bregman proximal term (optional), while the dual is updated with a “backward step” (y-hat) that dampens an implicit dual ascent effect arising from the quadratic penalty. The step sizes τi (dual ascent) and νi (backward step) are chosen as explicit functions of the number of randomly updated blocks K and the per-row block degree di (sparsity of A), enabling larger steps when A is sparse.

Theoretical contributions include global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic convergence for constant step sizes, under standard convexity and KKT existence assumptions. The authors show that two known parallel multi-block ADMM variants are extreme cases of PDMM: sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) corresponds to exact primal updates and a small dual step, while PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) corresponds to heavily proximalized primal updates with full dual steps. They also demonstrate that PDMM enables randomized block-coordinate updates for overlapping group-structured problems by introducing equality constraints, and analyze the effect of sparsity on admissible step sizes (e.g., τi = 0.5 when row degree di = 2).

Empirically, PDMM is evaluated on robust PCA (3-block formulation with noise) and overlapping group lasso. In sequential Matlab runs, PDMM variants outperform RBSUMM and sADMM, and are competitive with or faster than GSADMM in runtime (despite GSADMM’s fewer iterations), with sensitivity to K highlighted.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithm is well-motivated. The “implicit dual ascent” observation is insightful and justifies the dual backward step. The convergence analysis (potential function h, residual R, monotonicity in expectation, and ergodic bounds) matches the state-of-the-art for multi-block ADMM: O(1/T) under constant step sizes without strong convexity. The use of Bregman terms allows a unified treatment of exact and inexact updates.
- Assumptions are standard (closed, proper, convex fj; existence of a KKT point). Step-size prescriptions τi, νi reflect both K and di; the resulting constants are conservative but interpretable. Most proofs are deferred to the supplement/extended version, which is common at NIPS, but certain key intuitions are presented.
- Experimental methodology is reasonable for a first demonstration but limited: runs are sequential despite the parallel focus; datasets/sizes are modest; and some baselines (e.g., PJADMM) are absent.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy but largely coherent. The roles of τi and νi are explained (duality with implicit ascent, effect of sparsity and K). The connections to sADMM and PJADMM are a strength, though currently proved in an extended version; a brief derivation in the main text would improve accessibility.
- It would help to include explicit pseudo-code with the admissible τi, νi choices; a short interpretive discussion relating νi to ADMM under-/over-relaxation would further clarify the backward step. The overlapping-group reduction is clear and shows nicely how di affects step sizes.

Originality:
- Randomized parallel multi-block ADMM with convergence guarantees and constant step sizes, where step sizes adapt to sparsity and K, is a meaningful extension beyond prior Gauss–Seidel ADMM, sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM (which updates only one block and requires diminishing steps/local error bounds). The observation about implicit dual ascent and the backward step formalization are insightful. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM’s parameters is novel and pedagogically valuable.

Significance:
- The ability to safely parallelize multiple block updates with improved admissible step sizes on sparse A is practically relevant (e.g., in distributed/parallel settings). The O(1/T) rate is standard but sufficient. The application to overlapping groups via equality constraints is useful; highlighting di=2 gives a clear advantage over sADMM’s τi = 1/J scaling. The work is likely to influence algorithm design for large-scale structured problems.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual innovation (dual backward step) and principled step-size design exploiting sparsity and K.
- Unifying view showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM.
- Solid convergence theory with constant step sizes; handles inexact updates via Bregman terms.
- Demonstrated utility on two relevant applications; good empirical behavior vs. RBSUMM and sADMM.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Empirical evaluation:
  - No parallel or distributed implementation; all experiments are sequential Matlab. To substantiate “parallel” advantages, include multi-core or distributed results with varying K and di.
  - Missing comparison with PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) in experiments, despite being a key related method; please add it with tuned proximal parameters and report iteration/time.
  - The sADMM code used reportedly omits the dual update; this undermines fairness. Please implement the correct dual update and include results.
  - Report sensitivity to ρ and to τi, νi; provide principled defaults and robustness analysis.
  - Scale up problem sizes (e.g., larger RPCA; larger group lasso) and include wall-clock on multi-threaded implementations.
- Clarity and completeness:
  - Provide algorithm pseudocode and a self-contained proof sketch for the sADMM/PJADMM equivalences in the main paper.
  - Discuss relation to relaxed ADMM (over-/under-relaxation parameter) and extragradient ideas; cite Bertsekas–Eckstein and recent relaxed/multi-block ADMM works to situate the backward step.
  - Clarify computational costs per iteration vs K; include complexity summaries.
  - Explain how di and K are computed/known in practice and how to choose K adaptively.
- Theory:
  - The analysis is ergodic; comment on prospects for non-ergodic rates (as known for some ADMM variants) or linear rates under additional assumptions (e.g., strong convexity/error bounds).
  - Discuss whether τi choices are tight or can be improved adaptively; any line-search variants?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and challenging setting (multi-block ADMM) with a principled, implementable algorithm.
- Provides new insights (implicit dual ascent; backward step) and unifies existing approaches.
- Solid convergence guarantees with constant step sizes and explicit dependence on sparsity/parallelism.
- Demonstrates benefits on common applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks true parallel experiments; some baselines are missing or unfairly implemented.
- Many proofs and key equivalences are deferred; clarity could be improved.
- Asymptotic rate is standard; incremental advance from a theory standpoint.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a useful algorithmic contribution with sound analysis and insightful connections. Strengthening the experiments (parallel runs, PJADMM baseline, correct sADMM) and adding minor clarifications would make it a solid accept.
