### NIPS 2014 Review

**Paper:** Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

**Reviewer Score:** 7 (Accept)

---

### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the important problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to a general linear constraint, a formulation that captures many problems in machine learning. The authors correctly point out that while the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for two-block problems, its generalization to multiple blocks remains an active area of research with significant challenges. Direct extensions, such as cyclic (Gauss-Seidel) or fully parallel (Jacobian) updates, often suffer from convergence issues or require restrictive assumptions.

The main contribution of this work is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm that performs parallel, randomized block-coordinate updates on the primal variables. The key innovations of PDMM are threefold:
1.  A "dual backward step" is introduced to moderate the dual variable update, which the authors argue is the key to stabilizing the parallel primal updates.
2.  The step sizes for the dual update (τ) and the backward step (ν) are intelligently designed to depend on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (`K`) updated in parallel at each iteration.
3.  The framework is flexible, allowing an arbitrary number of blocks to be updated in parallel, from a single block (`K=1`) to all blocks (`K=J`).

The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. Furthermore, the paper provides an insightful unification of prior work, showing that the splitting-variable ADMM (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) can be viewed as two special cases of PDMM. Finally, the authors demonstrate how PDMM can be used to solve composite minimization problems with overlapping regularizers (e.g., overlapping group lasso) in a randomized block-wise fashion, an interesting application that was previously considered an open problem for block coordinate descent methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, and the analysis appears to be sound. The core idea of using a dual backward step to control the "aggressiveness" of the dual update in a parallel setting is clever and intuitive. The theoretical results, particularly the O(1/T) convergence rate with a constant step size, are strong and place the algorithm on par with standard ADMM. The assumptions for convergence are standard and not overly restrictive.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly established, and the proposed PDMM algorithm is presented in a logical manner. The discussion connecting PDMM to sADMM and PJADMM in Section 2.2 is particularly illuminating and helps position the work within the existing literature. The application to overlapping group lasso is also explained clearly.

**Originality:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel. While randomized and parallel updates for ADMM have been explored (e.g., RBSUMM), the combination of a randomized parallel update with the specific dual backward step and the structure-aware step sizes is a new and significant contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM provides a new perspective on these existing methods. The application to overlapping group lasso is also a novel contribution, showing how a primal-dual method can effectively tackle a problem that is difficult for pure primal coordinate descent methods.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. Multi-block constrained optimization is a fundamental problem in modern machine learning, statistics, and signal processing. An efficient, provably convergent, and parallelizable algorithm like PDMM is of great interest to both researchers and practitioners. The flexibility in choosing the number of parallel blocks (`K`) allows for adaptation to different computational architectures and problem structures. The theoretical insights provided by the framework could inspire further research into parallel optimization methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Validation of Parallelism:** A major motivation for the algorithm is its parallel nature. However, the experiments are run sequentially in Matlab. While this is sufficient to compare iteration counts and demonstrate the algorithm's convergence properties, it does not validate the practical speedups achievable through parallelization. A simple experiment on a multi-core machine demonstrating wall-clock time improvements as `K` increases would significantly strengthen the paper's practical claims.

2.  **Comparison with sADMM:** In the RPCA experiment, the authors state that "the public available code of sADMM does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0" and that sADMM "should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3". This comparison feels potentially unfair. If the publicly available implementation is flawed or not representative of the method, the authors should either implement a correct version of sADMM for a fair comparison or provide a more detailed justification for using the existing code. As it stands, the poor performance of sADMM might be due to a suboptimal implementation rather than an inherent flaw in the method itself.

3.  **Guidance on Choosing K:** The number of blocks to update in parallel, `K`, is a critical hyperparameter. The experiments show its impact on performance, revealing a trade-off between per-iteration computational cost and the total number of iterations required for convergence. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on how to choose `K` in practice. Is there a "sweet spot"? Does it depend on the problem structure (e.g., sparsity) or the underlying hardware? Some practical guidance would be very valuable for users of the method.

4.  **Intuition for Step Sizes:** The specific forms for the step sizes `τi` and `νi` in equation (8) are central to the algorithm and its convergence proof. While the proofs are deferred to the supplement, the main text would benefit from a sentence or two providing some high-level intuition for why these particular forms are chosen and how they relate to ensuring convergence.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in large-scale optimization (multi-block ADMM).
*   Proposes a novel, flexible, and technically sound algorithm (PDMM).
*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation with a rigorous O(1/T) convergence proof.
*   Offers a valuable unifying perspective on related state-of-the-art methods.
*   Demonstrates a novel application to overlapping regularizers, a difficult problem for other methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical validation lacks experiments in a true parallel environment, which is a key motivation for the work.
*   The experimental comparison to at least one key baseline (sADMM) is questionable and requires clarification.
*   The paper lacks practical guidance on setting the crucial parameter `K`.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The methodological novelty, theoretical rigor, and insightful connections to prior work are impressive. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental section and can be addressed in a final version. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance. The proposed PDMM algorithm is likely to be influential and widely used by the NIPS community.
