Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints, a setting encompassing RPCA and structured sparsity problems. It proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM. At each iteration, PDMM selects K primal blocks to update in parallel by minimizing an augmented Lagrangian plus, optionally, a Bregman prox term. A key novelty is a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty, implemented via an intermediate dual y-hat and parameters τi (dual step size) and νi (backward step strength). The authors derive τi and νi as functions of the per-row block-sparsity di of A and the number K of blocks updated: τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)), νi = 1 − 1/K̃i with K̃i = min{di, K}. They show global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity with constant step sizes under standard convexity and KKT existence assumptions, for both exact and inexact (Bregman-linearized) primal updates. They also illustrate how PDMM specializes to sADMM (two-block with splitting variables) and to PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) as extreme cases, and how to handle overlapping group structures by introducing equality constraints, exploiting di = 2. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggest faster convergence than sADMM, RBSUMM, and (sequential) GSADMM.

The work builds on broad ADMM literature (Boyd et al.), multi-block ADMM difficulties and fixes (He et al.; Deng et al.; Hong et al.), randomized coordinate descent (Nesterov; Richtárik–Takáč), and proximal/Bregman variants. The backward-step interpretation connects to extragradient/primal-dual damping ideas familiar in PDHG (Chambolle–Pock) and Mirror-Prox, though these are not explicitly cited.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper provides a clear derivation of step-size rules tied to sparsity and K, global convergence, and O(1/T) ergodic bounds in expectation. The role of the dual backward step is well-motivated by analyzing implicit dual ascent in Jacobi updates. The inexact updates are covered via a Bregman framework with standard smoothness/strong convexity assumptions. However, some pieces are underspecified: the positive semidefinite matrices P^t and Q in the residual and Lyapunov function are not explicitly constructed in the main text; the sharpness of the τi, νi choices (e.g., maximal admissible values) is not discussed; and the dependence of constants on problem data (e.g., norms/spectral properties of A) is not made explicit. The assumptions are mild (closed proper convex fj, existence of a KKT point).
- Empirics: Experiments are run sequentially and demonstrate iteration/runtime gains on two representative problems. The RPCA comparisons include GSADMM (no guarantees), sADMM (with an implementation caveat: no dual update), and RBSUMM (sensitive stepsizes). The overlapping group lasso results highlight the benefit from exploiting di = 2. While promising, the empirical section would benefit from stronger baselines (e.g., PDHG or primal-dual extragradient methods), ablation on the backward step (vary τi, νi with fixed K), and a fairer comparison to sADMM with proper dual updates. Parallel wall-clock results would better support claims of scalability.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but generally clear. The intuition about implicit dual ascent and the need for the backward step is valuable. Notation is heavy; giving a succinct algorithm box with the core update, explicit default settings for φj, ηj per application, and a short paragraph interpreting τi and νi limits (K=1 vs K=J; di sparse vs dense) would help. Several typos and grammar issues are present.

Originality:
The main contribution is an integrated, parallel randomized block-coordinate ADMM variant with a principled dual backward step and step-size rules that account for both block sparsity and the number of active blocks, along with convergence and rate guarantees under constant stepsizes. The unification clarifies relationships among sADMM and PJADMM. While randomized ADMM variants and proximal Jacobian schemes exist, the explicit dual backward step framed as tempering implicit ascent, and the dependence of τi, νi on di and K, are novel and well-argued.

Significance:
The method targets an important class of problems, and the ability to handle overlapping groups with randomized updates is practically relevant. Theoretical guarantees at O(1/T) with constant stepsizes and without restrictive error bounds improve over RBSUMM’s conditions. If supported by stronger empirical validation and parallel implementations, PDMM could become a useful tool in large-scale convex optimization with structure.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual advance: identifies and corrects the failure mode of Jacobi ADMM via a dual backward step.
- Sound convergence analysis with explicit step-size formulas leveraging sparsity and K.
- Unifies and generalizes sADMM and PJADMM; handles inexact updates within a Bregman framework.
- Demonstrates applicability to overlapping groups and RPCA; competitive to stronger than existing ADMM variants.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited, sequential, and includes baseline inconsistencies (sADMM without dual update; RBSUMM tuning).
- Some theoretical elements (matrices P^t, Q; constants in bounds; admissible ranges of τi, νi) are opaque in the main paper.
- Missing discussion/citations of related primal-dual extragradient methods (e.g., PDHG) that conceptually relate to the backward step.
- No ablation to isolate the effect of K, τi, νi beyond rules of thumb; no adaptive strategies.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an algorithm box and a brief guide for choosing φj and ηj in common cases (e.g., logistic loss, nuclear norm, group lasso), including when exact vs inexact updates are preferred.
- Clarify the construction of P^t and Q and make constants in Theorem 2 explicit where possible; discuss whether τi, νi are tight/maximal for stability, and whether adaptive/backtracking strategies are feasible.
- Add comparisons to primal-dual hybrid gradient (Chambolle–Pock 2011) and Mirror-Prox, and discuss conceptual similarities/differences with the dual backward step.
- Ensure fair baselines: include sADMM with correct dual updates; report sensitivity to ρ, τi, νi; include PDHG as a non-ADMM baseline.
- Report parallel/multithreaded runtimes or scalability experiments, since parallelism is a key selling point.
- Minor: fix typos (“seperable,” “defiend,” “an noisy,” “convegence”), and streamline notation.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution with promising empirical results; with the above clarifications and stronger experimental validation, it would be a strong NIPS paper.
