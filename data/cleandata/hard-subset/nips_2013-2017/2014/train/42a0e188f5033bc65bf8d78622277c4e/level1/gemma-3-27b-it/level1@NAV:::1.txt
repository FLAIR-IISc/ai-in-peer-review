## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The authors address a key challenge in extending the well-studied Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, where convergence isn't guaranteed with naive parallelization. PDMM tackles this by randomly updating blocks in parallel, coupled with a novel backward step on the dual update and a step size selection strategy informed by the problem's sparsity structure.  The paper establishes global convergence and iteration complexity, and demonstrates improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso.

The work builds upon a substantial body of literature on ADMM and its variants (references [2, 12, 14, 15, 17, 26]). The authors accurately position PDMM within this landscape, clearly differentiating it from existing methods like Gauss-Seidel ADMM and Jacobian variants. A notable strength is the demonstration that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM emerge as special cases of PDMM, offering a unifying perspective.  The connection to randomized block coordinate descent [16, 18] is also well established, and extending PDMM to handle overlapping blocks is a valuable contribution.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and practical demonstration. The convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds provide a solid foundation for understanding the method's behavior. The experimental results, while limited to two applications, show consistent improvements over competitive baselines. The clarity of the presentation is also commendable.  The authors explain the key ideas effectively, and the notation is generally consistent.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The experimental section feels somewhat limited.  While PDMM outperforms the compared methods, the parameter tuning details are not fully elaborated, hindering reproducibility. Furthermore, a comparison with more recent and advanced methods for sparse optimization would strengthen the evaluation. The theoretical analysis, while thorough, relies heavily on assumptions which might not hold in all practical scenarios. It would be valuable to discuss the robustness of PDMM to deviations from these assumptions. Finally, the writing style occasionally suffers from redundancy and could benefit from more concise phrasing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (convergence and iteration complexity).
*   Demonstrated empirical performance improvements on relevant applications.
*   Unifying framework that connects existing ADMM variants.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Parameter tuning details are insufficiently described.
*   Assumptions in the theoretical analysis might be restrictive.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field of optimization, addressing a significant challenge in scaling ADMM to large-scale, multi-block problems.  Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, primarily focusing on elaborating the experimental setup and discussing the limitations of the theoretical assumptions.
