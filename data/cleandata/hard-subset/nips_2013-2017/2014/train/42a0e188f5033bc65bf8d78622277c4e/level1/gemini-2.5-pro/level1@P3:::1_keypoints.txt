- Addresses minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions under general linear constraints, a common ML optimization problem.  
- Identifies limitations of existing multi-block ADMM extensions (cyclic/Jacobian) that suffer convergence issues or require strong assumptions.  
- Proposes PDMM: randomized parallel block-coordinate primal updates stabilized by a novel "dual backward step."  
- Designs dual step sizes (τ) and backward step (ν) that depend on constraint-matrix sparsity and number of parallel blocks K.  
- Framework allows arbitrary parallelism from K=1 up to K=J, offering flexibility for different architectures.  
- Provides global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with constant step size under standard assumptions.  
- Unifies prior methods by showing sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.  
- Demonstrates PDMM on composite problems with overlapping regularizers (e.g., overlapping group lasso), addressing a previously open issue.  
- High technical quality: well-motivated algorithm with sound analysis and intuitive dual backward-step idea.  
- Clear presentation and helpful discussion linking PDMM to existing literature and applications.  
- Original contribution: novel combination of randomized parallel updates, dual backward step, and structure-aware step sizes.  
- Significant impact: solves a fundamental multi-block constrained optimization problem with practical parallelizability.  
- Major experimental weakness: experiments run sequentially in Matlab, failing to validate real parallel wall-clock speedups.  
- Questionable comparison with sADMM: public sADMM code may be flawed, so reported performance comparisons could be unfair.  
- Lacks practical guidance on choosing K; more discussion or heuristics for selecting K would be valuable.  
- Main-text lacks high-level intuition for chosen step-size forms (τi and νi); user-friendly explanation would help.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses fundamental problem, novel and flexible algorithm, strong theory, unifying perspective, and useful application.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing true-parallel experiments, potentially unfair baseline comparisons, and insufficient guidance on K selection.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with minor experimental and exposition issues; reviewer recommends acceptance.
