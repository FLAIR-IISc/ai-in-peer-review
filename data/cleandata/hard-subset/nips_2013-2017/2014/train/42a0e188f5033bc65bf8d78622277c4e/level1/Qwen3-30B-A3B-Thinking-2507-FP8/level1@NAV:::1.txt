

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been well-studied for two-block problems, generalizations to multiple blocks remain poorly understood. PDMM extends ADMM by allowing parallel updates of randomly selected blocks, establishing global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity under constant step size. The paper demonstrates that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing new theoretical insights into these methods. The authors also show PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, a challenging problem for existing methods, and validate their approach through experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

Strengths include strong theoretical contributions with rigorous convergence proofs, clear connections to prior work (particularly ADMM variants), and compelling empirical results showing PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The paper's organization is excellent, with well-motivated technical details and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates.

The main weakness is that the experimental section could better quantify the parallel speedup achieved by PDMM compared to sequential methods. While the paper claims parallelism, the experiments are run sequentially, making it difficult to assess the actual parallel efficiency gains. Additionally, the step size selection (τi, νi) might be challenging to implement in practice without careful tuning.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical advance in multi-block optimization, strong experimental validation, clear connections to prior work, and practical applicability to important machine learning problems.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited demonstration of parallel speedup in experiments, and the paper doesn't fully address potential implementation challenges in distributed settings.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization algorithms, advancing our understanding of multi-block ADMM variants with both theoretical and practical significance. It meets all quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria expected at NIPS.
