### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a block-separable convex function subject to linear equality constraints, a common formulation in machine learning. The authors note that while the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is effective for two-block problems, its generalization to multiple blocks remains a challenge. Existing multi-block ADMM variants are often sequential (Gauss-Seidel), with restrictive convergence guarantees, or parallel (Jacobian), with potential convergence issues.

The main contribution of this work is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method for multi-block problems. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks to update in parallel. The key innovations are: (1) the introduction of a "dual backward step" to control the dual variable's progress and ensure convergence; and (2) step sizes for the dual update and backward step that adapt to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of parallel updates `K`. The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size under standard assumptions. It also demonstrates that two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, thus providing a unified perspective. A notable application is solving composite minimization problems with overlapping groups, a difficult task for standard block coordinate descent. The method's efficacy is demonstrated empirically on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated contribution. The core idea of combining parallel randomized block updates with a dual backward step, whose magnitude is explicitly linked to problem structure (sparsity) and algorithmic choice (`K`), is a new approach to stabilize Jacobian-style ADMM methods. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is insightful and enhances the understanding of these related methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides rigorous proofs for the global convergence (Theorem 1) and the O(1/T) iteration complexity rate (Theorem 2) of the proposed algorithm. These guarantees are strong for a randomized, parallel method and are achieved with a constant step size, which is practical. The assumptions required for the theory are standard for ADMM-type methods. The experimental evaluation is solid, comparing PDMM against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines (GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on well-known machine learning tasks.

3.  **Significance:** This work has the potential for high impact. It provides a flexible and theoretically-grounded parallel algorithm for a wide class of large-scale optimization problems. The ability to trade off the degree of parallelism (`K`) with per-iteration cost is a valuable feature for practitioners. The application of PDMM to solve problems with overlapping groups in a randomized block-coordinate fashion addresses a known challenge in the literature and is a significant contribution in its own right. Researchers and practitioners working on distributed optimization are likely to build upon these ideas.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for the algorithm is clearly articulated by first discussing the limitations of direct Jacobi updates in ADMM. The roles of the key components—the dual backward step, sparsity-aware step sizes, and randomized updates—are well explained. The relationship to prior work is thoroughly discussed, effectively positioning the contribution within the existing literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Parameter Selection:** The paper provides a theoretical prescription for setting the step-size parameters `τ_i` and `ν_i` (Equation 8). However, in the RPCA experiments, the results in Table 1 are based on "tuned" parameters that differ from the theoretical values. This could be confusing for the reader. It would be beneficial to clarify whether the theoretical guarantees still hold for these empirically tuned values or to provide more intuition on why these settings outperform the theoretical ones. A more in-depth discussion on the practical selection of `K`, the number of parallel blocks, would also strengthen the paper, as its impact on the convergence-runtime trade-off is critical but only briefly touched upon.

2.  **Experimental Comparison Details:** The comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment is based on publicly available code where the dual update step size `τ_i` is set to zero. While this is a valid comparison against that specific implementation, it might not fully represent the performance of the sADMM method as originally proposed. Clarifying this and perhaps comparing against a version of sADMM implemented with its theoretically suggested parameters would make the comparison more direct and compelling.

3.  **Brevity on Inexact Version:** Section 2.1 on the inexact PDMM is quite concise. While it correctly outlines how Bregman divergence can be used to handle difficult subproblems, a concrete, worked-out example (e.g., showing the full update for the logistic loss case mentioned) would significantly improve the accessibility of this section for readers less familiar with these techniques.

### Arguments For Acceptance

*   Presents a novel and practical parallel algorithm for a broad and important class of optimization problems.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees (global convergence and O(1/T) rate) under standard assumptions.
*   Unifies and provides new insights into existing parallel ADMM variants.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against state-of-the-art methods.
*   Addresses the challenging problem of overlapping groups with a randomized coordinate approach.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   Minor lack of clarity regarding the choice of some parameters in the experiments versus the theory.
*   The section on the inexact variant could be more detailed for broader accessibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, technical soundness, and potential significance, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid and important contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.
