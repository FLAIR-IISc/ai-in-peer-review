This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. PDMM performs randomized parallel block-coordinate primal updates and introduces a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian, with step sizes that depend on the sparsity pattern of A and the number K of blocks updated per iteration. The authors prove global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes, show that PJADMM and sADMM emerge as extreme cases of PDMM, and demonstrate empirical gains on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The work is well-situated relative to two-block ADMM (Boyd et al.), multi-block Gauss–Seidel and Jacobian variants including BJADMM/PJADMM (Deng et al.), BSUMM (Hong et al.), and randomized BCD (Richtárik & Takáč), as well as negative results on multi-block ADMM divergence (Chen et al.).

Quality: The analysis is careful: the dual-backward step is motivated, step sizes τi, νi are derived using di and K, and convergence/complexity proofs align with ADMM-class results. The unification with sADMM and PJADMM clarifies when primal progress warrants smaller/larger dual steps. Empirically, PDMM is competitive on two representative applications. However, the rate is standard O(1/T) in expectation; nonergodic rates or accelerated variants are not explored. Sensitivity to τi, νi and K is only partially characterized, and fairness of baselines (e.g., RBSUMM tuning) could be strengthened.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but notation is heavy and some terms (e.g., Q, Pt) are introduced with limited intuition. There are minor typos (“seperable”, “defiend”). A clearer guidance section on choosing K and step sizes in practice would help.

Originality: The dual-backward step with sparsity-aware step sizing and the unification of sADMM/PJADMM within a single randomized parallel framework are novel and insightful. The ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized fashion is practically useful.

Significance: PDMM addresses a recognized gap—robust multi-block parallel ADMM with guarantees—and is likely to be adopted for large sparse problems. Demonstrating true parallel speedups (multi-threaded or distributed) would further strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clear unification of multi-block ADMM variants via a principled dual-backward step.
- Convergence guarantees with constant step sizes; handles overlapping groups.
- Solid empirical performance on two relevant tasks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation of actual parallel/runtime scaling; experiments are sequential Matlab.
- O(1/T) rate without nonergodic or accelerated results; step-size guidance could be more practical.
- Baseline tuning and broader comparisons (e.g., recent stochastic/variance-reduced ADMM) could be expanded.
