- Proposal: PDMM — randomized parallel block-coordinate ADMM updating K primal blocks with a dual “backward step” and sparsity/K-dependent stepsizes (τi, νi); supports inexact Bregman linearization.  
- Stepsize formulas: τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i with K̃i = min{di, K}.  
- Special cases: sADMM and PJADMM arise as PDMM parameter extremes (small vs. unit dual steps).  
- Convergence: global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity and KKT existence; applicable to certain inexact updates.  
- Overlapping groups: PDMM handles randomized updates with overlapping groups where standard RBCD is nontrivial.  
- Empirical results: PDMM outperforms RBSUMM and sADMM and is competitive/faster than GSADMM in sequential MATLAB experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.  
- Theoretical quality: careful Lyapunov-based analysis with explicit dependence on K, J, and di; rates match known ADMM results without strong convexity.  
- Inexact extension: Bregman linearization backed by standard assumptions (Lipschitz ψ gradient, strongly convex ϕ).  
- Algorithmic motivation: dual backward step and sparsity-aware stepsizes prevent dual oversteps from Jacobi primal updates and balance K vs. sparsity.  
- Conceptual contribution: unifies existing Jacobian ADMM variants and elucidates trade-offs between proximal terms and dual steps.  
- Experimental design: uses standard, relevant problems and reports objective/residuals vs iterations and time; studies impact of K and τi, νi.  
- Experimental limitation: all experiments are sequential — no true parallel scalability or multicore/communication studies.  
- Baseline concern: some baselines (e.g., sADMM implementation) may be suboptimally configured, possibly biasing comparisons.  
- Clarity issues: heavy notation; needs concise pseudocode specifying update order (which blocks updated when, then dual/backward step).  
- Missing explanations: Pt and Q used in residual/Lyapunov are referenced but not fully defined or intuitively explained in main text.  
- Practical guidance gap: unclear how to choose ηj and Bregman functions in practice; more concrete examples per problem class needed.  
- Minor presentation issues: typos (e.g., “seperable”) and inconsistent notation (Acj vs Ajt).  
- Originality: novel combination of randomized parallel multi-block updates, dual backward step, and sparsity/K-aware stepsizes enabling K>1 convergent updates.  
- Significance: valuable for large-scale ML/signal processing and overlapping-structure problems; practical impact would be stronger with parallel speedup evidence.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theory, novel design (backward step, unification), applicability to overlapping groups and nonsmooth via Bregman.  
- Cons for acceptance: lack of parallel/scale experiments, potential baseline unfairness, under-specified technical/practical details, only expectation/ergodic convergence results.  
- Suggested additions: include clear pseudocode, parameter-setting guide for τi/νi/ηj/φj, define Pt/Q with intuition, implement parallel scaling studies and communication costs.  
- Suggested experiments: ensure fair baseline tuning, sensitivity analyses for all methods, ablation on backward step (vary νi) and sparsity (vary di), and report memory/computational costs for large I.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and novel with promising empirical performance; clarity improvements and true parallel/scaling demonstrations would strengthen the paper.
