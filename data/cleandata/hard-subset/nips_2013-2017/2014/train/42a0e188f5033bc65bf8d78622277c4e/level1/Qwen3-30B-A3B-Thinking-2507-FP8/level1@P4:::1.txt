

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address the gap in effective generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks, where existing methods either suffer from convergence issues or lack theoretical guarantees. PDMM updates blocks in parallel, introduces a dual backward step to address convergence problems of direct Jacobi updates, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity using constant step size. The paper demonstrates that existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) are special cases of PDMM and shows effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

The paper builds on established NIPS work: ADMM (Boyd et al., 2011), randomized block coordinate descent (Nesterov, 2012), and related methods like BSUMM (Hong et al., 2013). The authors correctly distinguish their work from these by addressing the multi-block extension problem with parallel updates and constant step size convergence guarantees.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical soundness:** The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-structured. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, a significant improvement over methods requiring decreasing step sizes. The paper provides clear assumptions, rigorous proofs, and insightful connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM).

2. **Novelty:** The key innovation is the parallel randomized block coordinate approach with dual backward step, which addresses the convergence issues of direct Jacobi updates. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by providing a unified framework that encompasses existing methods as special cases. The ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized block coordinate fashion is particularly valuable for applications like overlapping group lasso.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear notation and thorough explanation of the algorithm components. The relationship between the dual backward step and convergence issues is well-explained. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is particularly insightful.

4. **Significance:** The experimental results demonstrate PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The paper shows that PDMM can achieve faster convergence than ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, with the added benefit of parallelism. The ability to handle overlapping blocks where RBCD fails is a significant practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper states that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" but doesn't fully explain why RBCD cannot handle overlapping blocks while PDMM can. A brief clarification would strengthen the novelty argument.

2. The experimental comparison could be more comprehensive. The paper compares with ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, but doesn't include recent distributed optimization methods that have appeared in NIPS 2013-2014, such as distributed ADMM variants.

3. The parameter selection guidance is limited. While the paper provides the formula (8) for τi and νi, it doesn't offer practical guidance for selecting these parameters in new applications beyond the two experimental cases.

4. The paper mentions that "PDMM can run in parallel but GSADMM only runs sequentially," but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the parallelism scaling or speedup factors with increasing block counts or processors.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper makes a solid technical contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The PDMM algorithm addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing a theoretically sound method for parallel multi-block optimization with constant step size convergence guarantees. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is compelling, and the practical significance is clear for important machine learning applications.

The main weaknesses are minor and don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental results are convincing, and the paper provides a clear connection to existing work while making a novel contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in optimization for machine learning.

**Pro-con arguments for acceptance:**

Pros:
- Theoretical contribution is significant (O(1/T) complexity with constant step size)
- Connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is insightful
- Demonstrated effectiveness on two important applications with better performance than state-of-the-art
- Clear explanation of the dual backward step and its role in convergence

Cons:
- Limited explanation of why RBCD cannot handle overlapping blocks
- Experimental comparison could include more recent parallel optimization methods
- Parameter selection guidance is limited to the two experimental cases
- Parallelism analysis could be more detailed

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a clear, technically sound contribution to the field with significant theoretical and practical value. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
