This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints, a common framework in machine learning applications like RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors aim to generalize ADMM to multiple blocks in a parallel and randomized fashion, addressing known convergence issues with naive multi-block ADMM implementations. The core idea is to randomly update a subset of blocks at each iteration, combined with a novel backward step on the dual update to promote stability. The paper includes theoretical convergence analysis with iteration complexity bounds and demonstrates empirical performance on two applications, showcasing improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in ADMM and its variants (references [2, 12, 14, 15, 17, 20, 26]). The authors correctly identify the difficulties in extending ADMM to multiple blocks, referencing key contributions like GSADMM and Jacobian variants.  The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM and PJADMM is a valuable contribution, providing a unified perspective on these methods. The problem setting is well-motivated and clearly defined, and the introduction effectively establishes the context and contributions. The inclusion of overlapping block scenarios (Section 2.3) and relating it to existing work on RBCD is a strength.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to multi-block ADMM that addresses the convergence challenges of existing methods through randomized block selection and a dual backward step.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, which is essential for demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed algorithm.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly clear, and the algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner. The connection to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are clearly articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM compared to other methods, including ADMM, sADMM and RBSUMM.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches and emphasizing the unique contributions of PDMM. The detailed analysis and relationship between PDMM, sADMM and PJADMM is excellent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides guidance on choosing τi and νi, the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to these parameters and potentially other parameters (like the number of blocks K) is not thoroughly investigated. The experimental section briefly mentions tuning but lacks detail.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper focuses on iteration complexity, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost per iteration would be beneficial. Given the parallel nature of PDMM, understanding the communication overhead associated with updating the dual variables (Section 2) is important.
* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1 is standard, but further discussion on the practical implications of requiring a KKT point to exist would be valuable. Many real-world problems only have approximate solutions.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons with existing methods are made, the choice of comparison methods appears somewhat limited. Including comparisons with more recent and advanced solvers for these problems (e.g., those leveraging accelerated gradient methods) would strengthen the results. The note regarding the public sADMM code lacking a dual update is concerning and suggests potential issues with fair comparison.
* **Bregman Divergence Details:** The discussion of inexact PDMM with Bregman divergences (Section 2.1) feels a bit hand-wavy. While the authors mention the benefits, a more concrete example of how a specific Bregman divergence is chosen for a particular problem would be helpful. The link between the choice of Bregman divergence and the resulting convergence properties is not fully clear.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a challenging optimization problem.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithm.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM.
* The connection to existing methods is well-articulated and provides a unified perspective.
* The problem setting is relevant to a wide range of machine learning applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks a comprehensive sensitivity analysis of the algorithm’s parameters.
* The computational cost per iteration is not fully analyzed.
* The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited.
* The discussion of Bregman divergences is not fully detailed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization and has the potential to be highly influential. The proposed PDMM algorithm addresses a key limitation of existing multi-block ADMM methods, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding parameter sensitivity, computational cost, and a more detailed discussion of Bregman divergences. Specifically, the authors should include a more comprehensive ablation study of the impact of different parameter settings on performance and include discussion on the computational cost.
