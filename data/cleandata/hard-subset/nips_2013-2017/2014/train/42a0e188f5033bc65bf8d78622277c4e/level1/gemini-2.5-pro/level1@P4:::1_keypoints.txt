- Addresses minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear equality constraints and the challenge of generalizing ADMM to multi-block settings.  
- Proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate method that updates K blocks per iteration with a novel dual backward step and sparsity-/K-aware step sizes.  
- Provides global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size under standard assumptions.  
- Unifies existing parallel ADMM variants (sADMM, PJADMM) as special cases of PDMM.  
- Applies PDMM to composite minimization with overlapping groups, demonstrating empirical effectiveness on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.  
- Originality: introduces a new stabilization technique for Jacobian-style ADMM via a dual backward step tied to problem sparsity and parallelism.  
- Technical quality: rigorous proofs for convergence and rate, with practical constant step sizes and solid experimental baselines.  
- Significance: offers a flexible, theoretically grounded parallel algorithm that trades off parallelism K and per-iteration cost, relevant for distributed optimization.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, motivates design choices, and situates contributions within prior work.  
- Weakness: practical parameter selection is unclear—experimental "tuned" τ_i and ν_i differ from theoretical prescriptions and need more explanation.  
- Weakness: sADMM comparison may be limited by using public code with τ_i=0, which might not reflect sADMM’s theoretically suggested performance.  
- Weakness: the inexact PDMM section is too brief and would benefit from a concrete worked example (e.g., logistic loss).  
- For acceptance: novel, practical parallel algorithm with strong theory and empirical results addressing overlapping-group difficulties.  
- For acceptance: unifies prior methods and provides valuable insights for multi-block ADMM research.  
- Against acceptance: minor clarity issues on experimental parameter choices versus theory.  
- Against acceptance: insufficient detail in the inexact-variant discussion for broader accessibility.
