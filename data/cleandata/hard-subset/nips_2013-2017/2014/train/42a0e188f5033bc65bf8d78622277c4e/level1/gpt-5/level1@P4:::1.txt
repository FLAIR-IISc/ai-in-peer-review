Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that updates K primal blocks in parallel per iteration and performs a “backward step” on the dual variable. The method adapts the dual stepsizes (τi) and the dual shrinkage factors (νi) to the sparsity pattern of A (through row-block degrees di) and to the number K of blocks updated, with τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, where K̃i = min{di, K}. PDMM also admits inexact updates via Bregman linearization, which can lead to closed-form updates and unifies exact/inexact variants.

The authors show that two known Jacobian ADMM approaches are special cases: two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) corresponds to small dual steps (τi = 1/J, νi = 1−1/J), while proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) corresponds to large proximal terms that allow unit dual steps (τi = 1, νi = 0). They establish global convergence in expectation and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions and existence of a KKT point, for both exact and certain inexact settings. They also show how PDMM can perform randomized updates with overlapping groups, a setting where standard RBCD is nontrivial.

Empirically, on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM outperforms RBSUMM and sADMM and is competitive with/ faster than Gauss–Seidel ADMM (GSADMM) in wall-clock, despite all implementations being sequential in MATLAB.

Quality:
- Theory: The analysis is careful and consistent with contemporary ADMM literature. A Lyapunov function h(v*, vt) is constructed to prove monotone decrease in expectation and convergence to KKT conditions, with explicit dependence on K, J, and di. The O(1/T) ergodic rate matches known rates for (variants of) ADMM without requiring strong convexity. The inexact extension via Bregman linearization is supported by standard assumptions (ψ with Lipschitz gradient, ϕ strongly convex).
- Algorithmic design: The dual backward step and sparsity-aware stepsizes are well motivated by the observation that Jacobi primal updates implicitly induce a dual ascent, risking oversteps when combined with explicit dual ascent. The choice of τi, νi balances the number of updated primal blocks and sparsity. The connection unifying sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is informative.
- Experimental evaluation: The two applications are standard and relevant. Results are reported for objective/residuals vs iterations and time, and the impact of K and of τi, νi is explored. However, all experiments are sequential; parallel scalability is not demonstrated. Some baseline configurations (e.g., sADMM implementation without a dual update) may not fully reflect best practices.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable but heavy in notation. The algorithmic description would benefit from a concise pseudocode summary specifying precisely what is computed when (x updates on selected blocks, then dual updates, and the backward step).
- Definitions of Pt and Q in the residual and Lyapunov functions are referenced but not fully described in the main text; brief intuition would help.
- The guidance on choosing ηj and the associated Bregman functions in practice could be clearer, with concrete examples per problem class.
- Minor typos (e.g., “seperable”) and notation switches (Acj vs Ajt) could be standardized.

Originality:
- The combination of randomized parallel multi-block ADMM with a dual backward step and stepsizes explicitly tied to sparsity (di) and K appears novel. The ability to update K > 1 blocks in parallel with convergence guarantees distinguishes PDMM from RBSUMM (which updates one block) and from prior Jacobian ADMM variants that require large proximal terms or more restrictive conditions.
- The interpretation that sADMM and PJADMM are extremes within PDMM’s parameterization is a useful conceptual contribution.

Significance:
- Multi-block ADMM is important in large-scale ML and signal processing. Providing a convergent, randomized, parallelizable variant with O(1/T) convergence and practical stepsize rules is valuable.
- The overlapping group lasso application illustrates PDMM’s utility for overlapping structures where standard RBCD is problematic.
- Demonstrating actual parallel speedups and scaling with processors/cores would strengthen practical significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contribution: global convergence and O(1/T) rate under mild assumptions, with explicit stepsizes depending on sparsity and K.
- Novel design elements: dual backward step; unification of sADMM and PJADMM.
- Applicability to overlapping groups and to nonsmooth objectives via Bregman linearization.
- Empirical improvements over relevant baselines on two representative problems.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation lacks true parallel implementations and scaling studies; all results are sequential MATLAB.
- Some baselines may not be optimally configured (e.g., sADMM without dual update), potentially biasing comparisons.
- Certain technical elements (Pt, Q, selection of ηj, practical heuristics for K) are under-specified in the main text.
- Convergence results are in expectation and ergodic; non-ergodic rates or high-probability bounds are not provided.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Add algorithm pseudocode and a concise “how to set” guide for τi, νi, ηj, and φj for common problem classes.
- Clarify the definitions and roles of Pt and Q in the main text with intuition.
- Report parallel implementations (e.g., multicore) and scaling with K and number of workers; include communication costs.
- Ensure baseline fairness: implement sADMM with standard dual updates and tuned parameters; report sensitivity analyses for all methods.
- Provide an ablation on the backward step (vary νi) and on sparsity (vary di) to empirically validate the stepsize theory.
- Discuss memory and computational cost of maintaining z and of dual-block updates for large I.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution on randomized, parallel multi-block ADMM with clear theoretical guarantees and promising empirical performance. Improvements to the empirical evaluation and clarity would further strengthen the work.
