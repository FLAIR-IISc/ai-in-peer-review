

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving convex optimization problems with multiple blocks of variables subject to linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM for two blocks is well-studied, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates blocks in parallel, behaving like randomized block coordinate descent, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity under constant step size. The paper connects PDMM to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM as special cases), shows applicability to overlapping group problems, and demonstrates superior performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compared to state-of-the-art methods. This work relates to NIPS literature through its connection to ADMM variants (e.g., [2, 12, 20]), randomized coordinate descent [16, 18], and the ongoing research on multi-block optimization [7, 10, 14, 15].

## Strengths

**Quality (High):** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions. The proof techniques are sound and build upon established ADMM theory. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The experimental results on two important applications demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, algorithm description, and theoretical results are presented with precision. The discussion of the dual backward step and its relationship to the sparsity of A is particularly clear. The paper avoids unnecessary jargon and provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the specific optimization techniques.

**Originality (High):** While extensions of ADMM to multiple blocks have been explored, PDMM's parallel randomized block coordinate approach with theoretically justified step sizes represents a significant conceptual advance. The authors explicitly show how their method generalizes previous approaches and provides new insights into the role of the dual backward step. The application to overlapping group problems is particularly novel, as the authors note that randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks remains an open problem.

**Significance (High):** The problem addressed is important for large-scale machine learning, where parallelization and handling of overlapping structures are crucial. The paper demonstrates practical advantages in two key applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso), showing that PDMM can be significantly faster than existing methods. The convergence rate matches state-of-the-art ADMM variants, making it both theoretically sound and practically valuable.

## Weaknesses

**Technical Detail (Moderate):** While the paper establishes convergence, the conditions for the step size selection (τi, νi) could be more thoroughly explained. The connection between the sparsity of A and the step sizes (equation 8) is presented as a technical detail without sufficient intuition for why this particular scaling is optimal.

**Experimental Comparison (Moderate):** The comparison with RBSUMM is limited to a single setting (ρ=1), while the authors note that RBSUMM requires careful tuning. A more comprehensive comparison with various parameter settings for RBSUMM would strengthen the empirical claims. Additionally, the paper does not compare with more recent ADMM variants that have emerged since the cited literature (e.g., [9, 27]).

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence guarantees
- Novel approach to multi-block optimization that addresses a well-known gap in the literature
- Demonstrated practical advantages on two important machine learning problems
- Excellent writing and organization that makes the technical content accessible
- Strong connections to and generalization of existing methods

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The problem of multi-block ADMM has been actively researched, and while PDMM is novel, the incremental nature of the contribution might be questioned by some reviewers
- The experimental section could be strengthened with more thorough comparisons across parameter settings
- The paper doesn't address the limitations of PDMM in cases where the sparsity structure of A is not favorable

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of convex optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are sound, the presentation is excellent, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method. While the experimental comparison could be slightly expanded, the paper's theoretical advances and clear presentation outweigh this limitation.

PDMM represents an important step forward in understanding and implementing efficient parallel optimization for multi-block problems. Its ability to handle overlapping structures in a randomized block coordinate fashion addresses a specific gap in the literature that has been noted by several authors [16, 18, 28]. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides valuable theoretical insight, and the empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are compelling.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original in its approach to multi-block optimization, and significant for both theoretical and practical machine learning applications. I strongly recommend acceptance.

## Recommendation
Accept (Strong Accept)
