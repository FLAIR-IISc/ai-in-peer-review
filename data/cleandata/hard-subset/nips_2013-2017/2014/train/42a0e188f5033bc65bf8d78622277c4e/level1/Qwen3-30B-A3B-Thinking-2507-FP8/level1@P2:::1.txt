

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address a gap in the literature where generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks remain unclear despite preliminary work. The problem considered is minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, which encompasses important applications like robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it introduces PDMM, which randomly updates multiple blocks in parallel, behaving like randomized block coordinate descent. Second, it establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size. Third, it demonstrates that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified understanding of these approaches. Fourth, it shows how PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, addressing an open problem in the literature.

The paper relates well to previous work. It properly cites and discusses relevant NIPS papers, including ADMM variants (e.g., [2, 12, 20]), randomized block coordinate methods (e.g., [16, 18]), and related work on multi-block optimization (e.g., [10, 14, 15, 17]). The authors clearly distinguish their approach from existing methods, particularly highlighting how PDMM's dual backward step and adaptive step size selection address limitations of previous approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis with global convergence and iteration complexity guarantees. The proof techniques build on established methods while introducing novel elements like the dual backward step and adaptive step size selection. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and carefully developed.

2. **Practical Relevance:** The authors demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness on two important applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso), showing clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods in terms of runtime. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful parameter selection and multiple comparisons.

3. **Novel Insights:** The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides valuable theoretical insight into these methods. The paper shows how the dual backward step and step size selection strategy can unify different approaches, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of ADMM variants.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written with clear exposition of the problem, algorithm, and theoretical results. The notation is consistent, and the flow from problem formulation to algorithm to analysis to experiments is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Step Size Selection:** While the paper provides theoretical step sizes in equation (8), the experimental section doesn't thoroughly address how sensitive PDMM is to these choices. The authors claim they "choose τi, νi according to (8)" but don't discuss whether tuning these parameters would lead to further improvements.

2. **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with RBSUMM is somewhat limited. The authors note that RBSUMM "requires certain local error bounds to be hold" for convergence, but don't explicitly verify whether these conditions are satisfied in their applications. A more thorough comparison with other recent parallel methods would strengthen the experimental section.

3. **Overlapping Block Handling:** While the paper claims PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, it doesn't provide a detailed explanation of how exactly it deals with the overlapping structure compared to other methods. This is particularly important given that the paper identifies this as an open problem for RBCD.

4. **Runtime vs. Iteration Trade-off:** In the RPCA experiments, the paper shows that GSADMM converges faster in terms of iterations but PDMM is faster in terms of runtime. However, the explanation for this trade-off is somewhat superficial (noted as "computation per iteration in GSADMM is slightly higher"). A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity would strengthen this claim.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is appropriate, and the insights provided about the relationship between different ADMM variants are valuable. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature and provides both theoretical guarantees and practical improvements for multi-block optimization problems.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria effectively:
- **Quality:** Theoretical results are well-supported, and the paper is a complete piece of work.
- **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
- **Originality:** The randomized parallel block coordinate approach and its connection to existing methods represent a novel contribution.
- **Significance:** The problem addressed is important for large-scale machine learning, and the method provides practical improvements over existing approaches.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications for large-scale machine learning problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides valuable insights into multi-block optimization methods. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing optimization problems that are central to many machine learning applications. It provides both theoretical advances and empirical validation, making it a strong candidate for presentation at NIPS.
