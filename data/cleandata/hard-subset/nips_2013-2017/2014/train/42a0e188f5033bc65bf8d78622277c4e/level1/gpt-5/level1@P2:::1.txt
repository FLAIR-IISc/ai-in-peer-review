Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex, block-separable objectives with linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks to update in parallel and performs a primal update against an augmented Lagrangian evaluated at a “backward-stepped” dual variable ŷ, followed by a dual update with step sizes τi that depend on the row-block sparsity di of A and on K, and a backward step parameter νi. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity for constant step sizes, covering exact and inexact (proximal/Bregman) primal updates in a unified analysis. They also show that two existing multi-block ADMM variants—two-block ADMM with variable splitting (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM)—arise as limiting cases of PDMM (small τ, large ν for exact updates; large τ, ν≈0 with sufficient proximal terms). They highlight the role of matrix sparsity in enlarging allowable dual steps, and demonstrate applications to RPCA (three-block linear constraint) and overlapping group lasso by rewriting overlapping regularization with equality constraints.

This work sits squarely within the active literature on multi-block ADMM, which includes Gauss-Seidel variants (e.g., GSADMM/BSUMM), Jacobian variants (PJADMM), randomized variants (RBSUMM), and analyses showing divergence in the naive multi-block setting. The paper’s main conceptual addition relative to those is the explicit backward dual step coupled with step sizes tied to A’s sparsity and number of updated blocks, together with a convergence analysis for randomized parallel updates of multiple blocks.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithm is clearly specified and the analysis framework is standard for primal-dual splitting with augmented Lagrangians. The introduction of ŷ (dual backward step) to temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty is well-motivated. Theorems establish monotone decrease of a potential and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions with a KKT point, and cover both exact and Bregman-linearized updates. The dependence of τi, νi on di and K is plausible and appears correctly propagated through the proofs (as summarized).
- Completeness: Core proofs are relegated to the supplement; the main paper states assumptions, lemmas, and theorems cleanly. The analysis scope (convex, possibly nonsmooth; constant steps) is appropriate.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are informative but raise concerns about fairness and scope. Comparisons are in sequential Matlab, so the “parallel” advantage is not demonstrated; yet the paper argues PDMM’s advantage over GSADMM partly on potential parallelism. The sADMM baseline is compromised: the public code used reportedly sets τ=0 (no dual update), which is not faithful to the method; concluding that sADMM is slow because τ=0 is not a fair comparison. For RBSUMM, the tuning protocol is limited and the observation that it fails to converge within 5000 iterations may reflect suboptimal tuning. On the positive side, the experiments do study the effect of K and τi, νi and include a structured sparsity application where the di=2 setting plays to PDMM’s design.

Clarity:
- The writing conveys the main ideas, but the exposition is dense and notation-heavy. The motivations for the backward dual step and the interplay between implicit/explicit dual ascent are well explained. The connections to sADMM and PJADMM are important; however, since the equivalences are deferred to the supplement, a concise derivation in the main text would strengthen clarity.
- Minor issues: a number of typographical errors (“seperable”, “defiend”, “convegence”, etc.); algorithm pseudocode would help; some constants (Pt, Q, βi) appear abruptly and could use more intuitive explanation in the main text.

Originality:
- The combination of (i) randomized parallel multi-block updates, (ii) dual backward step with steps tied to sparsity and K, and (iii) a unified exact/inexact analysis is novel relative to existing multi-block ADMM literature. The explicit articulation that sADMM and PJADMM are extremes of PDMM (with interpretable τ, ν) is insightful.
- Randomized block coordinate descent on overlapping groups via an equality-constraint reformulation is not new per se, but the observation that PDMM yields favorable τ, ν (di=2) and thus efficient updates is useful.

Significance:
- The theoretical contribution advances understanding of stable, parallelizable multi-block ADMM with constant steps and clear dependence on problem structure (di and K). An O(1/T) guarantee matches the best known for comparable methods; the structural step-size guidance is practical.
- Practically, PDMM is relevant to distributed/parallel scenarios; however, the lack of actual parallel implementation weakens the empirical evidence for speedups. The two applications are representative and the results are promising, but broader benchmarking (different A sparsities, larger scales, and truly parallel runtimes) would improve the case.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for backward dual step; interpretable step-size rules leveraging sparsity and K.
- General framework covering exact and Bregman-linearized updates.
- Convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant steps for randomized parallel multi-block updates.
- Insightful unification of sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.
- Demonstrated applicability to overlapping group lasso with favorable parameters.

Weaknesses:
- Fairness of empirical comparisons: sADMM baseline with τ=0 is not representative; RBSUMM tuning may be insufficient.
- No actual parallel implementation or multi-threaded runtimes; claims of parallel advantage are not empirically validated.
- Heavy reliance on supplement for key derivations; some notation and constants insufficiently motivated in the main text.
- Limited ablations on how sparsity di affects performance beyond the overlapping lasso case.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and novel algorithmic idea (dual backward step + structure-aware steps) with rigorous analysis.
- Bridges and clarifies relationships among existing multi-block ADMM variants.
- Practical guidance for choosing τi, νi as functions of di and K.
- Promising results on two relevant applications; analysis applicable to a wide class of problems.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical section lacks fair baselines and true parallel experiments; performance claims vs. sADMM and RBSUMM are not fully convincing.
- Clarity could be improved; key equivalences (to sADMM/PJADMM) and constants are only in the supplement.
- Incremental rate (O(1/T)) relative to prior work; significance is more in stability/parallelization details than in asymptotic improvements.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide fair sADMM and RBSUMM baselines (implement proper dual updates for sADMM; more extensive tuning for RBSUMM), and report per-iteration costs.
- Include a parallel implementation with wall-clock scaling vs. number of threads/machines.
- Move a succinct derivation of the sADMM/PJADMM special cases and the intuition for τi, νi into the main paper.
- Clean up typos and add algorithm pseudocode.

Overall assessment:
- Quality: good theoretical quality; empirical support needs strengthening.
- Clarity: moderate; acceptable but could be improved.
- Originality: good; the dual backward step and structure-aware steps in a randomized parallel multi-block ADMM framework are novel.
- Significance: moderate-to-high; practical potential is strong, but current experiments underutilize the parallel angle.
