### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints, a problem central to many machine learning applications. PDMM is a parallel, randomized block coordinate method that extends the popular Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key challenge in generalizing ADMM to more than two blocks is that direct parallel (Jacobi-style) updates often diverge. The authors address this by introducing a "dual backward step," which tempers the dual variable update and ensures convergence. A major contribution is the derivation of step sizes that adapt to both the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (`K`) updated in parallel at each iteration.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a flexible algorithmic framework that allows an arbitrary number of blocks to be updated in parallel, from a single block (like randomized coordinate descent) to all blocks (a full Jacobi update). Second, it establishes a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. Third, the work provides a unifying perspective on prior art, showing that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM (splitting ADMM) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM), are special cases of PDMM. This connection offers new insights into the role of primal update inexactness versus dual update aggressiveness. Finally, the authors demonstrate how PDMM can be applied to solve composite minimization problems with overlapping groups (e.g., overlapping group lasso), a setting where traditional block coordinate descent methods are not directly applicable.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated by the shortcomings of existing multi-block ADMM variants. The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing a global convergence guarantee and an O(1/T) complexity rate, which is standard for this class of first-order methods. This is a complete piece of work, presenting the algorithm, its theoretical analysis, and empirical validation. The authors are careful in their analysis, connecting their method to prior work and clearly stating their assumptions, which are standard for ADMM. The experimental results, particularly on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, effectively support the claim that PDMM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods.

A potential weakness is that the experiments, while well-chosen, are run sequentially. The main selling point of PDMM is its parallelism, and while the runtime comparisons suggest benefits (by showing that fewer, more expensive parallel iterations can be faster than many cheap sequential ones), a demonstration on a true parallel architecture would have provided more conclusive evidence of its practical significance.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, a concise literature review, and a clear summary of the paper's contributions. The core algorithm (Eqs. 5-7) is stated clearly, and the intuition behind the key ideas—the dual backward step and the adaptive step sizes—is explained well. The connection drawn to sADMM and PJADMM is a highlight in terms of clarity, as it helps situate PDMM in the literature and clarifies the relationships between seemingly different algorithms.

One minor point for improvement would be to clarify the iterative loop involving the intermediate dual variable `ŷ`. Equation (5) uses `ŷ^t` for the primal update, while equation (7) defines `ŷ^{t+1}`. The paper should explicitly state how `ŷ^t` is derived at the beginning of an iteration (presumably `ŷ^t = y^t - νρ(Ax^t - a)` based on the motivation in the "Dual Backward Step" paragraph) to make the algorithm fully self-contained for implementation.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the extensive literature on ADMM, the proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel contribution. The combination of parallel randomized block updates with an adaptive, stabilizing dual backward step is new. The specific formulation of the step sizes (`τ_i`, `ν_i`) that depend on matrix sparsity (`d_i`) and the degree of parallelism (`K`) is a significant and novel theoretical contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is a particularly insightful and original conceptual result. Furthermore, successfully applying a randomized coordinate-style method to the overlapping group lasso problem, which the authors note is an open problem for standard RBCD, is a strong demonstration of the method's novelty and power.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. The problem of large-scale, linearly-constrained convex optimization is ubiquitous in machine learning and related fields. Standard ADMM's limitation to two blocks (or its sequential nature for >2 blocks) is a major practical bottleneck. PDMM provides a theoretically sound, flexible, and parallelizable algorithm that directly addresses this limitation. Researchers and practitioners working on problems like robust PCA, graphical model selection, and structured sparse learning are likely to find this method very useful. The ability to trade off the number of blocks updated in parallel (`K`) with the computational cost per iteration offers valuable flexibility for different hardware architectures and problem structures. The theoretical insights connecting different parallel ADMM methods also advance the field's understanding. This work is very likely to be built upon by future research in distributed and parallel optimization.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a key problem:** Provides a theoretically sound and practical solution for the multi-block ADMM problem, a long-standing challenge with high relevance to the NIPS community.
2.  **Novel and powerful algorithm:** The proposed PDMM algorithm with its dual backward step and adaptive step sizes is a novel and elegant contribution.
3.  **Strong theoretical results:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate that matches the standard for related methods.
4.  **Important conceptual unification:** The demonstration that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is a significant contribution that clarifies the landscape of parallel ADMM algorithms.
5.  **Strong empirical results:** Experiments show that PDMM is faster than relevant and competitive baselines on important machine learning tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of true parallel experiments:** The experiments are simulated on a sequential machine, which slightly weakens the claims about practical parallel performance. However, the runtime results are still indicative and compelling.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
