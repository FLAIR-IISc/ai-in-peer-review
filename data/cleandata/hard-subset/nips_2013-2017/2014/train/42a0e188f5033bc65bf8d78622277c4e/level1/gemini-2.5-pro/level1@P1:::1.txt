**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to a general linear constraint, a formulation that captures many problems in machine learning. The authors note that while the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for two-block problems, its generalization to the multi-block setting (J > 2) is non-trivial, with direct extensions often failing to converge. Existing multi-block methods are either sequential (Gauss-Seidel variants) or require strict conditions for convergence.

The main contribution of this paper is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block-coordinate ADMM variant. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of K blocks and updates them in parallel. The key technical innovations are: (1) a "backward step" on the dual variable update to prevent overly aggressive steps that can lead to divergence in parallel settings, and (2) a novel step-size rule that adapts to the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (K) being updated in parallel. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, proving global convergence and establishing an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size under standard assumptions. They also demonstrate that two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated experimentally on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where it outperforms several state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the important and well-known challenge of developing a provably convergent and efficient ADMM for multi-block problems. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a clever and novel synthesis of several ideas: randomized coordinate updates, a corrective dual backward step, and adaptive step sizes. The step-size dependency on the constraint matrix sparsity (`d_i`) and the number of parallel blocks (`K`) is particularly novel and practically important.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong. The authors provide a complete convergence analysis for a randomized, parallel algorithm with a constant step size, which is a non-trivial result. The proofs, based on a carefully constructed Lyapunov function, establish global convergence and the standard O(1/T) ergodic convergence rate for this class of methods. This places the algorithm on a firm theoretical footing.

3.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is well-written and provides excellent intuition. The explanation of the "implicit dual ascent" in the primal update and the resulting motivation for the dual backward step is very clear and insightful. Furthermore, showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM provides a valuable unification and deeper understanding of the relationships between these parallel ADMM variants.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The choice of robust PCA and overlapping group lasso showcases the algorithm's applicability to problems with a few dense blocks and many sparse blocks, respectively. The comparisons against relevant baselines (GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) are thorough, and PDMM demonstrates superior or competitive performance, especially in terms of wall-clock time. The analysis of how performance varies with the number of parallel blocks K is a valuable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of True Parallel Experiments:** The primary motivation for the algorithm is its parallel nature. However, the paper states that all experiments were run sequentially. While the runtime results are still favorable due to lower computational cost per iteration (e.g., vs. GSADMM), the paper would be significantly strengthened by including at least one experiment on a multi-core system to demonstrate actual parallel speedups. This would more directly validate the "Parallel" in PDMM.

2.  **Intuition on Step-Size Derivation:** While the step-size choices in Equation (8) are justified by the convergence proof, the paper provides little high-level intuition for their specific functional form. A brief discussion on how these particular values for `τ_i` and `ν_i` arise from the proof requirements (e.g., to ensure certain matrices in the Lyapunov analysis remain positive semi-definite) would improve the paper's clarity and help readers understand the design choices.

3.  **Strong Claim Against Baseline:** The paper reports that RBSUMM failed to converge for the overlapping group lasso problem. While this is a strong result for PDMM, the claim should be carefully substantiated. A little more detail on the parameter ranges tried for RBSUMM would add credibility and address potential concerns that it was simply poorly tuned.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, provably convergent parallel algorithm for an important class of optimization problems.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees (global convergence, O(1/T) rate) with a constant step size.
*   Offers new insights by unifying and explaining the relationship between several existing methods.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance on challenging, relevant machine learning tasks.
*   The application to overlapping-group problems, which are difficult for standard BCD methods, is a key strength.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core claim of parallelism is not directly validated with experiments on parallel hardware.
*   The derivation of the key step-size rule could be better motivated.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization and ADMM. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for making the paper even stronger. The work advances the state of the art for multi-block ADMM-type methods and is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
