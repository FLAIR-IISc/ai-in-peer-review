## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key challenge with extending the well-studied Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks – ensuring convergence in a parallel setting. They introduce a backward step on the dual update and leverage the sparsity of the constraint matrix to derive step sizes. Theoretical convergence guarantees and empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are provided.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The problem setting – minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints – is fundamental and arises in many machine learning applications, as correctly highlighted by the authors. The paper builds upon the foundation of ADMM, acknowledging its success in the two-block case ([2]) and the difficulties in generalizing it to multiple blocks ([15, 14, 10]).  Prior work broadly falls into two categories: Gauss-Seidel ADMM ([15, 14]) which is sequential, and Jacobian variants ([26, 10, 17]) aiming for parallelization. PDMM positions itself within the latter category, offering a more flexible randomized approach. The connection drawn between PDMM and existing methods like sADMM ([17, 26]) and PJADMM ([10]) by showing them as extreme cases is a valuable contribution, providing a unified understanding of these approaches. The authors also correctly situate their work within the broader context of randomized block coordinate descent ([16, 18]). The authors properly cite related work including references to optimization theory and applications in sparse recovery.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of PDMM, with its randomized block updates and dual backward step, offers a novel approach to multi-block ADMM. The ability to handle overlapping blocks (Section 2.3) is also a strength, as this is a challenging scenario for standard block coordinate descent methods.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees (Theorem 1) and iteration complexity bounds (Theorem 2) for PDMM, which is crucial for establishing the method's reliability. The assumptions are reasonable (Assumption 1).
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The authors clearly demonstrate how PDMM relates to and generalizes existing ADMM variants, providing a unifying framework.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study (effect of K) is helpful.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is mostly clear, although some symbols could benefit from further explanation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Parameter Tuning:** While the theoretical analysis provides guidelines for setting step sizes (τi, νi), the paper lacks a detailed discussion on practical parameter tuning strategies. It's not immediately clear how sensitive the performance is to these parameters, and more guidance for users would be beneficial.  The discussion in the experimental section on “tuning” is vague.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. While PDMM outperforms the included baselines, it would be helpful to compare against more recent and highly optimized solvers for RPCA and overlapping group lasso, such as those available in specialized packages.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper doesn’t explicitly analyze the computational complexity of each iteration of PDMM, which could give more insight into the advantages of random block selection versus a full sweep. Comparing the theoretical complexity with the observed experimental speedups would strengthen the argument.
* **Repetitive Discussion:**  Some explanations, especially surrounding the dual backward step, are repeated in several sections.  Consolidation could improve readability.
* **Supplement Reliance:** Crucial details, like the detailed proofs and some implementation details, are relegated to the supplement. While this is acceptable, it makes it harder for reviewers to fully assess the work without carefully examining the supplement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in optimization with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical results are solid and provide a strong foundation for the method.
* The experimental results demonstrate PDMM's practical effectiveness.
* The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of ADMM and its variants.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed guidance on practical parameter tuning could hinder the method's adoption.
* The limited comparison to more recent and highly optimized baselines leaves some questions about PDMM's competitive advantage.
* The computational complexity analysis is missing, potentially hindering a complete understanding of the method’s efficiency.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and theoretically sound approach to multi-block ADMM, and the experimental results are encouraging. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide more detailed guidance on practical parameter tuning (e.g., sensitivity analysis, heuristics).
2.  Include a comparison to more recent and highly optimized solvers for the considered applications.
3.  Add a discussion of the computational complexity of PDMM.
4.  Reduce redundancy in the explanations of the dual backward step.
