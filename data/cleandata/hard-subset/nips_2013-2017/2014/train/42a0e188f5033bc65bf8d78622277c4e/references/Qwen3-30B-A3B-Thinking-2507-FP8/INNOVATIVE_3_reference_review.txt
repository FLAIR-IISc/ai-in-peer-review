

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: existing parallel ADMM variants either update all blocks simultaneously (leading to convergence issues) or update only one block at a time (limiting parallelism). PDMM uniquely allows for parallel updates of any number of blocks while maintaining convergence guarantees, providing a unifying framework that encompasses existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it introduces a theoretically sound algorithm with a clear conceptual advance: the dual backward step (Equation 7) that prevents the aggressive dual updates that plague direct Jacobi approaches. Second, the paper elegantly connects sparsity in the constraint matrix A to step size selection (Equation 8), showing how the number of blocks updated (K) and block sparsity (di) influence convergence rates. Third, the authors demonstrate how PDMM can solve the previously open problem of randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping group lasso problems, where traditional approaches fail.

The theoretical contributions are significant: the paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size under relatively mild assumptions. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides valuable theoretical insight, showing that sADMM (which updates all blocks in parallel) and PJADMM (which uses proximal terms) are extreme cases of the more general PDMM framework. This unifying perspective is particularly valuable for understanding the role of the dual backward step.

The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing PDMM outperforms existing methods on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems. Notably, PDMM1 (updating one block) converges faster than RBSUMM, and PDMM3 (updating three blocks) achieves the best runtime performance on RPCA. The analysis of how the number of blocks updated (K) affects performance provides practical guidance for implementation.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty and theoretical contribution. While there's a rich literature on ADMM variants, the authors identify a clear gap in parallel randomized block coordinate methods and fill it with a clean, general framework that offers both theoretical guarantees and practical benefits. The paper's bold approach to unifying existing methods while introducing a new algorithm with provable convergence is exactly what NIPS values.

Minor weaknesses include somewhat limited experimental comparisons (particularly missing recent parallel ADMM methods beyond the ones mentioned) and a slightly underdeveloped discussion of practical implementation considerations for very large-scale problems. However, these are minor compared to the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the technical content is sound. The authors carefully address related work, including both the Gauss-Seidel and Jacobian variants of ADMM, and provide a clear explanation of how their method differs from and unifies these approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a new direction for parallel optimization that addresses a fundamental limitation in existing ADMM variants. The ability to parallelize updates of multiple blocks while maintaining convergence guarantees opens up new possibilities for large-scale optimization problems. The connection to overlapping group lasso problems, which have been difficult to handle with randomized block coordinate methods, demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical advances.

The paper excels in originality, presenting a genuinely novel approach that advances the state of the art in parallel optimization. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the experimental validation is appropriate for the claims made. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the literature and provided a compelling solution that both extends and unifies existing work.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization with its novel PDMM framework. The conceptual advances, theoretical guarantees, and practical results make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The paper's bold approach to unifying existing methods while introducing a genuinely new algorithm with provable convergence is exactly the kind of contribution NIPS values most highly.

I recommend acceptance. The novelty and theoretical contribution outweigh minor experimental limitations, and the paper makes a clear, significant advance in the field of parallel optimization.
