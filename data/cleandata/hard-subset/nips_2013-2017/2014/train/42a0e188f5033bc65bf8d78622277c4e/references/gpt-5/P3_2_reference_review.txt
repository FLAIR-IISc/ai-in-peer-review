Summary
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. In each iteration, K primal blocks are updated in parallel using a Jacobi-style step applied to the augmented Lagrangian evaluated at a “backward-stepped” dual variable ŷ; then all dual blocks are updated with step sizes τi that depend on the row-block sparsity di of A and the number K of updated primal blocks. The “dual backward step” (parameter νi) is motivated by the observation that Jacobi updates induce an implicit dual ascent which, when combined with explicit dual ascent, can be too aggressive. PDMM also permits inexact primal updates via Bregman linearization. The authors recover sADMM and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) as special cases and prove global convergence with O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions, with constants depending on K and di. Experiments on synthetic RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate faster convergence than sADMM, RBSUMM, and (sequential) GSADMM, with favorable dependence on K.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates PDMM among multi-block ADMM variants (Gauss-Seidel, Jacobian/proximal, randomized). Key prior works include sADMM (two-block with variable splitting), PJADMM (parallel with proximal terms), and RBSUMM (randomized single-block). The dual backward step is reminiscent of extragradient/relaxation ideas, but this connection is not discussed. The theoretical rate matches typical ADMM O(1/T) ergodic rates (e.g., Deng et al.), but the dependence on K and sparsity is new.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: clean identification of implicit dual ascent in Jacobi primal updates and the introduction of a tunable dual backward step to stabilize and speed convergence.
- Unifying framework: shows that sADMM and PJADMM are extreme cases of PDMM, clarifying when dual steps should be conservative vs. aggressive; supports inexact updates via Bregman linearization with clear guidance on ψ/ϕ choices.
- Theory: global convergence with explicit τi, νi tuned to K and di; O(1/T) ergodic complexity with clear constants; optimality residual constructed to monitor feasibility and block consistency.
- Practical insights: the dependency of step sizes on sparsity (di) and K is intuitive and practically useful; overlapping-group example (di=2) illustrates large gains over sADMM’s 1/J step size.
- Empirical results: on two representative constrained problems, PDMM converges faster than baselines, and the K-sweep study is informative.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Parallelism not validated: all experiments are sequential MATLAB; no multicore/distributed implementation, strong scaling, or communication cost analysis. Claims about “parallel” benefits thus remain speculative.
- Comparisons and tuning fairness: baselines appear underpowered in places (e.g., sADMM code reportedly uses τ=0, which is not representative; RBSUMM uses a particular diminishing schedule); unclear whether ρ and other hyperparameters were consistently tuned across methods. Including strong baselines for RPCA (e.g., IALM/SVT) would strengthen the case.
- Missing connections: the dual backward step is closely related to extragradient/primal-dual hybrid gradient (Chambolle–Pock, Condat–Vu) and over-relaxation in ADMM; these are not cited or discussed, which weakens the positioning.
- Clarity of analysis: key quantities (Pt, Q) in the Lyapunov/residual are not defined in the main text; intuition for the τi formula (K/(K̃i(2J−K))) and its optimality is limited; conditions for inexact PDMM convergence (ηj>0) are stated informally without a main-text theorem.
- Scope of experiments: only synthetic data; no real-world instances; no ablation to isolate the effect of the backward step (e.g., ν=0 vs prescribed νi) or of sparsity-aware τi; no sensitivity to ρ.
- Rate and constants: only ergodic O(1/T) is shown; no non-ergodic rates or linear convergence under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity, full column rank). Constants scale with J/K and may be conservative.

Quality
- The algorithm is sound and the proofs (as summarized) are plausible; however, key technical definitions are deferred to the supplement. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the method’s promise but lacks breadth and parallel validation. Some comparisons may be unfair due to tuning discrepancies.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with helpful intuition for the backward step and sparsity-aware steps. However, notation is heavy and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., Acj vs. A_cj), and some important definitions (Pt, Q) are missing in the main body. Several typos persist (“seperable,” etc.). The exposition would benefit from a clearer derivation/intuition for τi, νi and a diagram of the updates.

Originality
- Combining randomized multi-block parallel updates with an explicit dual backward step and sparsity/K-dependent steps is novel among ADMM variants. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within PDMM is insightful. However, the relation to primal-dual/extra-gradient methods should be made explicit.

Significance
- The problem class is broad and important. If validated in parallel implementations and on real workloads, PDMM could be a useful addition to the ADMM toolkit, especially for overlapping structures. The theoretical rate is standard, but the practical step-size guidance is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a proper parallel implementation and report speedups, communication/computation tradeoffs, and scaling in K and di.
- Ensure fair baselines: correct sADMM implementation with appropriate τ, tune ρ across methods, include IALM/SVT for RPCA and strong proximal gradient baselines.
- Add ablations: effect of ν (including ν=0), effect of τ beyond the prescribed formula, sensitivity to ρ, and explicit failures of direct Jacobi ADMM.
- Clarify definitions of Pt and Q in the main text; formalize inexact PDMM guarantees in the main body.
- Discuss connections to extragradient/PDHG and ADMM over-relaxation; add missing citations.
- Clean up notation/typos and unify Acj notation.

Pros
- Novel, principled stabilization of Jacobi ADMM via dual backward step.
- Sparsity- and K-aware step sizes with solid intuition.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM; supports inexact updates.
- Convergence with explicit rates and promising empirical results.

Cons
- No parallel implementation; limited, synthetic experiments.
- Some baselines and tuning choices questionable.
- Missing related-work connections; clarity gaps in analysis details.
- Only ergodic O(1/T); constants may be conservative.

Overall: A solid and promising contribution, but empirical validation and clarity could be strengthened. I lean borderline accept if empirical and clarity issues are addressed; otherwise, a strong reject-to-revise.
