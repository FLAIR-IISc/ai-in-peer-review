

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key limitation of standard ADMM for multiple blocks: convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates. PDMM introduces a dual backward step, step size parameters that account for the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks updated, and the ability to update arbitrary numbers of blocks in parallel. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates PDMM's superiority over existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM) on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical advance by unifying existing Jacobian ADMM variants (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases of PDMM. The dual backward step concept is elegantly motivated and rigorously analyzed, providing deeper insight into the role of dual updates in parallel ADMM variants.

2. **Practical Innovation**: The step size parameters that incorporate sparsity information (K̃i = min{di,K}) and the ability to randomly update any number of blocks (K) represent a substantial practical improvement over prior work. This allows PDMM to adapt to problem structure and achieve better parallelism.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The convergence analysis is thorough and well-justified, with clear connections to the KKT conditions. The O(1/T) complexity result with constant step size is particularly valuable for practitioners.

4. **Strong Experiments**: The experimental evaluation is well-designed, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines across two important applications. The analysis of how different K values affect performance provides valuable practical insights.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the two experiments are well-chosen, the paper's applicability to a broader range of problems (e.g., distributed optimization, large-scale machine learning) could be better demonstrated. The authors should consider including at least one additional application domain to strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Nonsmoothness Handling**: The paper claims PDMM "can solve a fairly large class of problems, including nonsmooth functions," but the convergence analysis doesn't explicitly address nonsmoothness in the objective (beyond the standard convexity assumptions). The authors should clarify how nonsmooth terms are handled in practice.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that PDMM "can run in parallel" but doesn't provide concrete details about parallel implementation challenges or scalability. For a paper targeting NIPS, more discussion of parallel efficiency and communication costs would be valuable.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites [10] (PJADMM) but doesn't compare with more recent works like [9] (which addresses similar problems with better convergence rates). The connection to [9] could strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the literature on distributed optimization. The dual backward step concept is particularly novel and well-motivated, addressing a fundamental issue with Jacobi-type updates. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate PDMM's practical advantages.

The most compelling aspect is the unification of existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) into a single framework, which provides deeper theoretical understanding of the dual update mechanisms. This is a significant theoretical contribution that advances the field beyond incremental improvements.

The paper's limitations are modest given the quality of the work. The experimental scope could be expanded, and the nonsmoothness handling could be clarified, but these don't undermine the paper's core contributions.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The unification of existing methods through PDMM provides valuable theoretical insights that advance the field. The experimental results are compelling and well-supported by the theory. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the two strong applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) are sufficient to demonstrate the method's value.

The paper addresses an important problem in distributed optimization with a novel solution that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with clear connections to prior work and a well-justified theoretical framework.

The paper's significance is enhanced by its practical applicability to important machine learning problems, particularly those involving overlapping structures where existing methods struggle. The O(1/T) convergence guarantee with constant step size is particularly valuable for practitioners.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of distributed optimization, with strong theoretical foundations, practical insights, and compelling experimental validation. It represents a meaningful advance over existing methods and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
