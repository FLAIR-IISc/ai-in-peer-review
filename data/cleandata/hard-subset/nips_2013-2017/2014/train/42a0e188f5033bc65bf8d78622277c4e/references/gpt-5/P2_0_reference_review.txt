Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints. It proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of ADMM that can update any number K of primal blocks per iteration. The key ideas are: (i) a “dual backward step” to counteract the implicit dual ascent embedded in the augmented Lagrangian’s quadratic term, (ii) step sizes for dual ascent and the backward step that depend on both the number of updated blocks K and the sparsity pattern of A via di (the number of nonzeros in row block i), and (iii) an inexact primal update framework using Bregman divergences to unify exact and proximal variants. The authors show that two popular multi-block Jacobian ADMM schemes lie at the extremes of PDMM: sADMM (exact primal, small dual step) and PJADMM (proximal primal, full dual step), clarifying the role of the backward step. They prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant stepsizes, and present experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, including ablations on K and step sizes.

This work is situated among efforts to extend ADMM to multi-block and parallel settings (GS-ADMM, sADMM with variable splitting, PJADMM with proximal terms, RBSUMM randomized single-block). The paper’s randomized multi-block Jacobian updates with sparsity-aware steps and a formal backward-step interpretation add to this line of work.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper states convergence under standard convexity/KKT assumptions, defines a Lyapunov-like function h(v*,vt) combining primal-dual gap terms and Bregman terms, proves monotone decrease in expectation with τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, and derives O(1/T) ergodic rates for objective suboptimality and feasibility. The intuition around “implicit dual ascent” motivating the backward step is sound, and the dependence of τi, νi on A’s sparsity and K is plausible and precisely stated. The unified exact/inexact treatment via Bregman divergences is technically clean. As the full proofs are in the supplement, I cannot fully verify them here, but the statements appear consistent with recent ADMM analyses, and the conditions (Lipschitz gradient for ψ, strong convexity of ϕ) are standard for controlling inexactness.
- Experiments: The empirical section demonstrates RPCA and overlapping group lasso with synthetic/standard setups, compares to GS-ADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, and studies the impact of K and (τ,ν). Results show PDMM can outperform RBSUMM and sADMM, and be competitive with GS-ADMM in runtime, with clear benefits from exploiting sparsity (larger τi when di is small). However, the evaluation is limited: all runs are sequential in Matlab (despite PDMM’s parallel motivation), datasets are modest scale, and some baselines are not used in their strongest forms (e.g., the sADMM implementation lacked a dual update; GS-ADMM with back substitution is not compared). The claims about RBSUMM’s nonconvergence could benefit from more systematic tuning and reporting.
- Reproducibility: The algorithmic description is mostly clear, with concrete formulas for τi, νi, and examples of Bregman choices. Some quantities (e.g., matrices Pt and Q) are defined as PSD without explicit construction in the main text; pseudocode would help. Proof details are deferred.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized: motivation, algorithm, intuition, connections, theory, and experiments flow logically. The discussion of implicit dual ascent/backward step is insightful. There are many typos and minor grammatical issues (“seperable,” “defiend,” “convegence,” etc.), and some notation is heavy. Providing algorithm pseudocode, a self-contained definition of Pt and Q (or a pointer to where they are concretely constructed), and a clearer description of the randomized block selection scheme would improve readability.

Originality:
- Randomized Jacobian updates of multiple blocks with sparsity-aware step sizes and a principled dual backward step appear novel relative to sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM (which updates a single block and requires local error bounds/decreasing stepsizes).
- The unifying view that connects sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM, tied to how much progress the primal subproblem makes and how aggressive the dual update should be, is conceptually valuable.
- The inexact PDMM framework via Bregman divergences is in line with prior Bregman-ADMM ideas but applied here to the randomized multi-block Jacobian setting.

Significance:
Multi-block ADMM variants with provable convergence and practical efficiency are of continued interest to the NeurIPS community, and the ability to exploit sparsity to increase dual steps is practically relevant. The O(1/T) ergodic rate matches the best-known for first-order operator splitting in general convex settings; the contribution is thus more in algorithm design/unification and practical step-size guidance than in improving asymptotic rates. The empirical evidence suggests PDMM can be competitive and sometimes superior, but the impact would be strengthened by parallel/distributed experiments, larger scales, and stronger baselines.

Pros:
- Clear intuition for why naive Jacobian ADMM diverges and how a backward step fixes dual over-shooting.
- Novel, simple step-size rules that leverage sparsity (di) and number of updated blocks (K).
- Unifying framework bridging sADMM and PJADMM; accommodates exact and inexact updates.
- Global convergence and O(1/T) rates with constant stepsizes; mild assumptions.
- Useful application to overlapping group lasso via equality-constraint reformulation.

Cons:
- Limited empirical evaluation: no parallel implementations or large-scale/distributed results; Matlab sequential runs only.
- Some baseline comparisons may be suboptimal (e.g., sADMM without dual update; GS-ADMM variant choices; RBSUMM tuning).
- Clarity issues: typos, heavy notation; some auxiliary matrices not fully specified in the main text; no algorithm pseudocode.
- Theoretical novelty is incremental in terms of rates; main value is algorithmic design and unification.

Questions/suggestions:
- Provide pseudocode and a concrete definition/construction of Pt and Q used in the Lyapunov function.
- Clarify how to set ηjt and choose ϕjt, ψjt in practice; provide guidelines for common losses/regularizers.
- Report results with a parallel implementation (multi-core or distributed) to substantiate the “parallel” advantage.
- Compare against GS-ADMM with Gaussian back substitution (He et al.) and proximal linearized ADMM variants; ensure sADMM includes a standard dual update.
- Discuss robustness to mis-specifying di or to dense A; can adaptive τi, νi be learned online?
- Include real datasets for RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound algorithm with a unifying perspective and practical step-size guidance, and establishes standard convergence guarantees. The experimental section is the main weakness; strengthening it would make the contribution more compelling.
