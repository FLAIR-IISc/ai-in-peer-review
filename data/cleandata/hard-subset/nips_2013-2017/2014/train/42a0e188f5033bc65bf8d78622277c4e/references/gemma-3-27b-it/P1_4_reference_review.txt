## Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The authors address the challenges of applying ADMM to multi-block problems, particularly the potential for divergence observed with direct Jacobi updates. PDMM tackles this by introducing a backward step on the dual update, strategically utilizing the sparsity of the constraint matrix, and enabling parallel updates of a randomly chosen number of blocks. The paper establishes convergence guarantees and demonstrates PDMM’s effectiveness through experiments on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso, showing improvements over existing methods like ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM. A key contribution is demonstrating connections between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM, offering a unified understanding of these algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundation of ADMM and its extensions for multi-block problems. It correctly positions itself within the existing literature, referencing key works such as Boyd et al. (2011) on ADMM, Chen et al. (2015) on non-convergence of multi-block ADMM, and Wang et al. (2016, 2017) on related Jacobian variants. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of existing methods, like the strict convergence conditions of BSUMM and the limited parallelism of RBSUMM, and effectively motivate the need for PDMM.  The connection established between PDMM, sADMM and PJADMM is a novel contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM offers a genuinely new approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems. The combination of the backward step on the dual variable, consideration of sparsity, and flexible block selection is innovative.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis, establishing global convergence and an iteration complexity bound. This is a significant strength, particularly for an algorithm targeting practical applications.
* **Practical Relevance:** The empirical results demonstrate that PDMM outperforms existing methods on benchmark problems (RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso). This suggests that PDMM is not merely a theoretical contribution but a useful tool for practitioners.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:**  The paper clearly shows how PDMM generalizes sADMM and PJADMM, deepening our understanding of these algorithms.
* **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The authors provide clear reasoning behind their design choices (backward step, sparsity-aware step size, randomized block selection), increasing the credibility of their approach.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper explores the impact of the number of randomly selected blocks (K) on performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** While the authors suggest formulas for choosing step sizes (τi and νi), the experiments show sensitivity to these parameters. A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies and their impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Computational Overhead:** The paper focuses on the algorithmic improvements, but doesn’t extensively discuss the computational overhead associated with the randomized block selection process.  A more detailed analysis of the practical cost of the random block selection, especially for very large-scale problems, would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison:** Although the experiments demonstrate PDMM’s superiority over several algorithms, the comparison is somewhat limited.  Including comparisons with state-of-the-art solvers specifically tailored to RPCA and overlapping group lasso (e.g., solvers leveraging specialized structures) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, particularly involving the indexing of blocks and matrices (e.g., Aij, Ari, Acj), could be more consistently presented throughout the paper. Some readers may find it challenging to follow the derivations without careful attention.
* **Supplemental Material Dependency:**  Important parts of the proof are pushed to the supplement. While acceptable, it makes a thorough review more challenging and requires trusting the correctness of the supplementary details.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to parallelizing ADMM.
* Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
* Strong empirical results on relevant benchmark problems.
* Clear connections to existing work and insights into the ADMM family.
* Addresses a challenging problem in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter tuning sensitivity and lack of detailed guidance.
* Limited discussion of computational overhead associated with randomization.
* Potentially incomplete comparison with highly specialized solvers.
* Some notational inconsistencies that slightly hinder readability.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for applications involving large-scale, block-separable problems. The proposed PDMM algorithm is innovative, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. While there are some areas for improvement (primarily related to parameter tuning and computational overhead analysis), the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity of notation and expanding the discussion on parameter selection and computational cost.  I would also encourage the authors to consider including a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art solvers specific to the application domains tested.



**Confidence:** 8/10
