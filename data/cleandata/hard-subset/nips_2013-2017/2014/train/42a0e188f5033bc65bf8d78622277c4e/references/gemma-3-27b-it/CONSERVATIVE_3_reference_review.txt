This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM, for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors aim to address limitations of existing parallel ADMM methods, particularly convergence issues and computational expense. They introduce a backward step on the dual update, a step size adaptation based on problem sparsity, and the ability to randomly update an arbitrary number of primal blocks in parallel. Connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are also highlighted.

While the problem addressed is relevant – large-scale optimization with block structure is common in machine learning – the paper feels incremental and lacks convincing novelty to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The core idea of randomizing block updates within ADMM isn’t groundbreaking; variations like RBSUMM already exist, and the benefits demonstrated aren’t substantial enough to justify the added complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides convergence guarantees and iteration complexity bounds, which is a positive aspect. Establishing these results, even under standard assumptions, adds some rigor.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:**  Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is a nice theoretical contribution, offering a unifying perspective.
*   **Potential for Parallelism:** The method is explicitly designed for parallel computation, which is valuable for large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Nature:**  The proposed method feels like a collection of refinements to existing ADMM techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach. The backward step and adaptive step size, while potentially helpful, aren’t dramatically different in spirit from existing strategies.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are not particularly compelling. The performance gains over existing methods (ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) are modest, and the sensitivity analysis of K (number of randomized blocks) is only mildly informative. The choice of datasets (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) are standard, but the results don't showcase a clear advantage of PDMM. The experiments lack a thorough comparison with highly optimized, state-of-the-art solvers specifically tailored to these problems.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The performance of PDMM appears heavily reliant on careful parameter tuning (τi, νi), and the justification for the proposed values, while theoretically motivated, could benefit from a more detailed explanation of its practical implications.
*   **Clarity Issues:** The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, especially when multiple versions of variables are introduced (e.g., y, ŷ, z). The motivation for certain design choices (e.g., the specific form of the step size adaptation) could be more clearly articulated.
*   **Lack of Scalability Demonstration:** While the method *aims* for scalability, the experiments don’t convincingly demonstrate superior performance on truly massive datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method.
*   The connection between PDMM and existing methods is a valuable contribution to understanding the ADMM landscape.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method is incremental and doesn’t represent a significant advance over existing techniques.
*   The empirical results are not convincing enough to demonstrate a clear advantage.
*   The paper lacks a compelling demonstration of scalability.
*   The notation and presentation could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and presents some interesting theoretical results, it lacks the originality and significance typically expected of a NIPS publication. The incremental nature of the contribution, coupled with the modest empirical gains, does not justify acceptance. The authors should consider submitting this work to a more specialized venue where the nuances of ADMM variations are more highly valued. They should also focus on conducting more comprehensive experiments with larger datasets and stronger baselines to demonstrate the practical benefits of PDMM. I also recommend a revision focusing on clarity and simplifying the notation.
