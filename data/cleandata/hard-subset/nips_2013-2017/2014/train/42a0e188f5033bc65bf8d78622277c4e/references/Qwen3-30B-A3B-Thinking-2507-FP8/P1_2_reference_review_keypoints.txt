- Summary: Proposes PDMM, a parallelizable multi-block ADMM variant using a dual backward step and sparsity-based step sizes, with global convergence and O(1/T) guarantees and applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.

- Strength 1 (Theoretical Soundness): Provides rigorous convergence analysis and establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size.

- Strength 2 (Novel Contributions): Introduces a dual backward step and step-size selection based on matrix sparsity, and connects PDMM to existing methods as special cases.

- Strength 3 (Practical Relevance): Empirically outperforms existing methods in runtime on RPCA and overlapping group lasso while enabling parallel updates.

- Strength 4 (Clarity and Organization): Paper is well-written with clear mathematical presentation, consistent notation, and thorough experimental description.

- Strength 5 (Addresses Gap): Directly tackles parallelizing multi-block ADMM with theoretical guarantees, notably useful for overlapping-group problems.

- Weakness 1 (Limited Method Comparisons): Insufficient comparison with other parallel multi-block optimization methods (e.g., refs [10,26]) to fully contextualize significance.

- Weakness 2 (K Selection Guidance): Lacks practical guidance for choosing the number of blocks K to update in parallel; experiments give only problem-specific insight (K=20).

- Weakness 3 (Limited Limitations Discussion): Needs deeper discussion of scenarios where PDMM is not advantageous (e.g., dense A reduces to sADMM behavior).

- Weakness 4 (Missing Recent Comparisons): Does not explicitly contrast PDMM in depth with recent parallel multi-block ADMM work beyond noting special-case relationships.

- Weakness 5 (Experimental Depth): Experiments could better analyze parallel speedup and scalability to very large problem sizes.

- Recommendation: Strong accept—paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution and is recommended for NIPS.

- Pro (Acceptance): Strong theory with convergence guarantees, unifies existing methods, demonstrates practical runtime advantages, well-written, and fills an important gap.

- Con (Against): Limited comparison with other parallel methods, insufficient K-selection guidance, and incomplete exploration of limitations.
