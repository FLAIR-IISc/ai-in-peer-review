- Proposes PDMM: a randomized block-coordinate ADMM variant that updates any number of blocks in parallel for block-separable convex problems with linear constraints.
- Introduces a dual backward step to fix convergence failures of direct Jacobi updates.
- Uses sparsity-aware step size selection that depends on the number of blocks updated.
- Establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions.
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM are special cases within the PDMM framework.
- Empirical validation on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates competitive runtime and accuracy.
- Observes PDMM1 (single-block updates) is fastest on RPCA despite being randomized.
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous analysis and clear intuition, especially for the dual backward step.
- Experimental design and baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) are appropriate and results are consistent.
- Needs more detailed analysis of trade-offs between block size K and convergence speed.
- The claim that “PDMM converges at the same rate as ADMM” requires further justification because constants may differ.
- Clarity is strong overall; notation and structure are consistent and readable.
- Main paper could explain the Bregman divergence used in inexact updates (Section 2.1) more clearly.
- Original contributions: randomized selection of any number of blocks, sparsity-aware stepsizes, and convergence with constant stepsizes.
- Should better contrast PDMM with Deng et al. (2014) and Hong et al. (2016) to clarify novelty.
- Significance: addresses overlapping-block optimization gaps and is practically relevant for large-scale ML problems.
- Practical gains are meaningful but not dramatically better than baselines in experiments.
- Does not fully address scalability to extremely large numbers of blocks.
- Recommendation: Accept — meaningful theoretical and practical contribution that generalizes existing methods.
- Pros: novel unifying algorithm, rigorous convergence proof, clear exposition, good experiments, handles overlapping blocks.
- Cons: insufficient contrast with closely related recent work, modest practical improvements, some unclear technical details, limited scalability discussion.
