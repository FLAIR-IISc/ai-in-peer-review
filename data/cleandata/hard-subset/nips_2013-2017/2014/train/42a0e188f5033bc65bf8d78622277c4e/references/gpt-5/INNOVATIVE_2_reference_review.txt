Summary
The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for linearly constrained, block-separable convex problems. The key ideas are: (i) a “dual backward step” to temper the otherwise overly aggressive implicit dual ascent induced by Jacobi-style primal updates, (ii) dual step sizes that explicitly depend on the sparsity structure of A (via per-row degrees di) and the number K of blocks updated in parallel, and (iii) a unified exact/inexact framework via Bregman proximalization that recovers prior Jacobian ADMM variants as extreme cases. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes, and empirically evaluate on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing favorable performance versus GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM in sequential Matlab experiments. The paper also shows PDMM connects sADMM (small dual step when primal updates are “strong”) and PJADMM (full dual step when proximal terms make primal progress conservative), thereby clarifying the role of the backward step and step sizes.

Relation to prior work
Multi-block ADMM (Gauss-Seidel and Jacobian) and randomized block methods have been extensively studied. Prior randomized ADMM (e.g., RBSUMM) updates a single block with restrictive conditions and decreasing steps; PJADMM adds proximal terms to stabilize convergence; sADMM uses splitting to fit two-block ADMM. PDMM differs by allowing arbitrary K blocks to update in parallel with calibrated dual/backward steps that incorporate sparsity, and by unifying exact/inexact variants through Bregman divergences. This appears novel and useful.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The dual backward step is a crisp idea that directly addresses the instability of Jacobi updates by compensating for the implicit dual ascent in the augmented term. Calibrating τi and νi using di and K is elegant and practically interpretable.
- Unifying perspective: Showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM helps demystify when to use small vs. full dual steps and how proximal terms interact with dual dynamics.
- Theoretical guarantees: Global convergence and O(1/T) rates with constant steps for arbitrary K and under standard convex assumptions (without local error bounds) are solid and competitive with the literature.
- Applicability: The formulation covers nonsmooth functions and overlapping groups; leveraging PDMM for overlapping block problems while maintaining randomized parallel updates is valuable.
- Empirical signs: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive or superior in runtime to strong baselines, and the K-tradeoff is studied.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Step-size formula typo: Equation (8) lists νi = 1 − K̃i, which is inconsistent with later text and bounds (νi would be negative). It should be νi = 1 − 1/K̃i (consistent with νi ∈ [0, 1 − 1/di] and later special cases). This needs correction.
- Experimental limitations: Results are from sequential Matlab; the algorithm’s key selling point—parallelism and dependence on sparsity—is not validated in parallel or distributed settings. Communication/synchronization costs (e.g., computing Axt+1) and scalability are not measured.
- Baseline fairness: The sADMM implementation used lacked a proper dual update (τi = 0), acknowledged by the authors; this makes the comparison weaker. A corrected sADMM implementation should be included. For RBSUMM, the best-tuned diminishing steps are used, but broader hyperparameter sweeps or stronger implementations would strengthen claims.
- Presentation density and typos: The paper has numerous typos (“seperable,” “convegence,” etc.) and notational heaviness. Definitions of Pt and Q (appearing in residual and Lyapunov) are deferred and not concretely instantiated in the main text, hampering readability. Some reference years are missing.
- Practical guidance: While the dependence of τi, νi on di and K is nice, more guidance on choosing K and ρ, and robustness to mis-specification, would help practitioners. An adaptive strategy for τi/νi and K would be compelling.
- Scope of theory: Convergence is in expectation with random block selection; statements about cyclic selection appear in experiments—clarify whether the theory extends to systematic/cyclic updates.

Questions for the authors
- Can you clarify and correct Eq. (8) and explicitly state the valid ranges for τi and νi? Please also restate Pt and Q in the main paper for completeness.
- How sensitive is PDMM to ρ and to modest deviations from the prescribed τi/νi? Any adaptive variants?
- Is the backward step related to extragradient or over-relaxation schemes? Can you draw a formal connection?
- What parallel/distributed wall-clock speedups do you observe on multi-core or cluster settings, especially as di varies? How costly is synchronization for Ax?
- For the sADMM baseline, can you provide results with a correct dual update?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a clear, novel mechanism (dual backward step) with principled step-size calibration exploiting sparsity and degree.
- Unifies and clarifies relationships among sADMM, PJADMM, and randomized variants.
- Provides global convergence and O(1/T) rates under standard assumptions with constant steps.
- Practical relevance to important ML problems (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) and to parallel optimization.

Cons for acceptance
- Some clarity/typo issues, including a critical one in Eq. (8).
- Experiments do not demonstrate actual parallel gains; one baseline is likely under-implemented.
- Limited ablation on parameter sensitivity and no large-scale real-world experiments.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some presentation and experimental shortcomings, the paper proposes a genuinely interesting and potentially influential direction—stabilizing parallel multi-block ADMM via a dual backward step with sparsity-aware step sizes—and provides solid theoretical backing and promising empirical results. The novelty and unifying perspective outweigh the minor flaws. I strongly encourage fixing Eq. (8), clarifying notation, and adding a short parallel runtime study if possible.
