- Introduces PDMM, a novel algorithm for linearly-constrained, block-separable convex problems that extends ADMM to multi-block settings.  
- Achieves parallelism via randomized block selection, a backward dual update step for stability, and adaptive step sizes based on matrix sparsity and number of updated blocks.  
- Shows PDMM generalizes existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) and relates to RBCD, placing prior work as special cases.  
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees with O(1/T) iteration complexity.  
- Empirically evaluated on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso, demonstrating competitive performance.  
- Novelty: randomized block updates and backward dual step offer a fresh approach to multi-block ADMM parallelization.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: rigorous analysis and formal convergence proofs.  
- Flexibility: step-size adaptation for problem structure and support for overlapping blocks.  
- Clear presentation and well-organized exposition, aiding reader understanding.  
- Limited experimental scope: only two applications tested, reducing evidence of generality.  
- Parameter tuning complexity: optimal choice of K (number of blocks) is problem-dependent and affects performance.  
- Missing implementation details: insufficient information on Bregman divergence and choice of ηjt hinders reproducibility.  
- Incomplete comparisons: lacks benchmarks against other parallel methods (e.g., distributed gradient descent, other randomized coordinate methods).  
- Strong assumptions: Assumption 1 (existence of a KKT point) may be restrictive and needs discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, novel method addressing ADMM limitations, clear connections to prior work, and promising empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, reproducibility concerns, challenging parameter tuning, and restrictive assumptions.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: broaden experiments, provide implementation details (Bregman, ηjt), add guidance on K selection, and discuss KKT assumption limitations.
