## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address limitations of existing parallel ADMM methods, particularly their potential for non-convergence and sensitivity to step size selection. PDMM introduces a backward step on the dual update, considers sparsity of the constraint matrix, and allows for random selection of any number of primal blocks for parallel updates.  They demonstrate connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, provide convergence guarantees, and validate PDMM's performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Parallelizing ADMM for large-scale problems is a critical challenge, and the paper directly tackles this. The limitations of existing methods (particularly convergence issues and step size sensitivity) are well-articulated.
* **Novelty:** The combination of the backward dual step, consideration of sparsity, and flexible block selection strategy distinguish PDMM from existing approaches.  Demonstrating sADMM and PJADMM as special cases clarifies its relationship to the existing literature.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides global convergence and iteration complexity results, which are essential for establishing the reliability of the proposed method.
* **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on two relevant applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM and compare it favorably to other ADMM variants. The exploration of the effect of the number of randomly chosen blocks (K) is insightful.
* **Clarity (Mostly):** The core ideas are generally well-explained. The notation, while dense, is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense, and some sections (particularly the theoretical analysis) could benefit from simplification. The number of equations and variables can be overwhelming for a quick read.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are encouraging, the comparison to state-of-the-art methods isn’t always comprehensive.  For example, the comparison to GSADMM is somewhat limited by the fact that the parameters were not fully optimized.  More thorough comparisons, perhaps on a wider range of datasets and problem sizes, would strengthen the claims.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on the theoretical aspects. More discussion regarding practical considerations for selecting *K* in real-world scenarios, or recommendations for parameter tuning, would increase its usability. The dependence on the sparsity structure of *A* could be elaborated on in terms of practical applicability.
* **Minor Notation Issues:**  The notation could be slightly improved. For example, the consistent use of different notations for sets (J, N(i)) can be confusing.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review). The empirical results support the claims made in the paper, but a wider range of experiments would strengthen this. **Score: 8/10**
* **Clarity:** While the core ideas are presented relatively clearly, the density of the writing and the complexity of the notation make it difficult to follow at times.  Some sections could be streamlined for better readability. **Score: 6/10**
* **Originality:** The proposed method represents a novel combination of existing techniques, and the theoretical connections to sADMM and PJADMM are insightful. The focus on random block selection and the consideration of sparsity are original contributions. **Score: 8/10**
* **Significance:** The potential for parallelizing ADMM for large-scale problems is significant.  If PDMM can deliver on its promises of improved convergence and robustness, it could have a real impact on applications in machine learning and optimization. **Score: 7/10**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem with a well-motivated approach.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound method with convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results on relevant applications.
* Advances our understanding of ADMM and its variants.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is quite dense and can be difficult to follow.
* The experimental validation could be more comprehensive.
* The discussion of practical considerations is limited.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and is worthy of acceptance at NIPS.  However, I recommend that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by improving the clarity of the presentation and expanding the experimental validation.  A more detailed discussion of practical considerations would also enhance the paper’s usefulness.
