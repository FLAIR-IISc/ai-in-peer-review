### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The method is a variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for parallel execution. The core challenge in parallelizing ADMM (i.e., a "Jacobian" update) is its frequent lack of convergence. The authors identify the cause as overly aggressive updates to the dual variable, resulting from both an "implicit" update within the primal step and an "explicit" update in the dual step.

The paper's central and novel contribution is to introduce a "dual backward step" to temper this aggressiveness, thereby stabilizing the parallel updates. Furthermore, PDMM introduces a principled step-size selection rule that adapts to both the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel. The authors provide a unified theoretical framework, establishing O(1/T) convergence rates and demonstrating that two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM. This connection provides a novel perspective on the trade-off between primal update accuracy and dual update step size. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on robust principal component analysis and the challenging overlapping group lasso problem.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and the boldness of its core ideas. The instability of multi-block Jacobian ADMM is a well-known problem, and previous solutions have often relied on either conservative, globally small step sizes (like sADMM) or adding strong proximal terms (like PJADMM). The introduction of the dual backward step is a genuinely new and elegant mechanism to address this stability issue directly. It is a conceptually clean and powerful idea.

Another significant contribution is the adaptive step-size rule. Many ADMM variants rely on a single, small step size (often proportional to `1/J`, where `J` is the number of blocks) that can lead to slow convergence. By incorporating the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix (`di`) and the number of parallel updates (`K`), PDMM provides a much more sophisticated and practical approach. This allows for larger, more aggressive steps where the problem structure permits, which is a crucial feature for real-world performance. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a fundamental redesign of the step-size selection process.

The paper's theoretical contribution is also commendable. Beyond providing the standard (but necessary) global convergence and rate analysis, the authors use their framework to unify sADMM and PJADMM. This unification is particularly insightful, explaining these two seemingly different methods as extreme points on a spectrum governed by PDMM’s parameters. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and elevates the paper from a mere algorithm proposal to a work that advances our conceptual understanding of parallel optimization methods.

Finally, the application of PDMM to the overlapping group lasso problem is a strong point. The authors correctly note that applying randomized block coordinate methods to this problem structure is an open challenge. By reformulating the problem and applying PDMM, they showcase the versatility and power of their approach, demonstrating a concrete case where their method can solve a problem that is difficult for other related techniques.

The paper's weaknesses are relatively minor and primarily relate to the experimental validation.
1.  While the experiments show PDMM outperforming several baselines, the evaluation is limited to two problems. A more comprehensive study on a wider range of applications would strengthen the empirical claims.
2.  The experiments were run sequentially, simulating parallelism. While this is common in methods papers, a demonstration in a true parallel computing environment, showing wall-clock speedups and analyzing communication costs, would have been more compelling.
3.  The dismissal of RBSUMM's convergence on the overlapping lasso problem is presented without much diagnostic detail.

However, in line with valuing novel concepts, these limitations do not detract significantly from the paper's core contributions. The proposed ideas are strong enough on their own, and the provided experiments serve as a sufficient proof of concept.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality:** The dual backward step is a novel and clever idea for stabilizing parallel ADMM.
*   **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The unification of sADMM and PJADMM provides a deeper understanding of the field and the trade-offs in parallel ADMM design.
*   **Practical Importance:** The adaptive step-size rule that considers problem sparsity and the degree of parallelism is a significant practical advance over existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The algorithm is supported by a solid theoretical analysis, including global convergence and an O(1/T) rate.
*   **New Capability:** It successfully addresses the challenging overlapping group lasso problem with randomized coordinate updates, which is an open problem for standard RBCD.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while positive, are not exhaustive and were not conducted in a true parallel environment.
*   **Clarity:** The intuition behind the specific formulas for the step sizes could be explained more clearly in the main text.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound method for parallel optimization. Its core ideas—the dual backward step and the adaptive step-size rule—represent a significant advance over prior work in parallel ADMM. The conceptual unification of existing methods is a mark of deep insight. While the experimental section could be more comprehensive, it is sufficient to demonstrate the promise of the proposed approach. The paper's novelty and potential impact are high, making it a strong candidate for acceptance. It presents a bold new direction that other researchers are likely to build upon.
