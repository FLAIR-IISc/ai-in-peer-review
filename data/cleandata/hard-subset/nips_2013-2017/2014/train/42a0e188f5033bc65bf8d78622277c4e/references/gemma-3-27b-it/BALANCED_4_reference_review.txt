This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems, termed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The core idea is to randomly select and update blocks in parallel, combined with a backward step on the dual update to improve convergence. The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in ADMM and its variants, correctly situating itself within the existing literature. The problem setting is well-motivated, as linearly constrained block-separable optimization arises in various machine learning applications, including RPCA and sparse recovery. The authors comprehensively review related work, including Gauss-Seidel ADMM, Jacobian variants, and RBSUMM, clearly highlighting the limitations of these approaches and positioning PDMM as an improvement. The connection established between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is insightful and provides a better understanding of the interplay between different dual update strategies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of randomized block selection with the backward step on the dual update appears novel and addresses potential convergence issues of standard parallel ADMM implementations.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. This is a significant strength, providing theoretical backing for the proposed method.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the PDMM algorithm and its rationale. The motivations behind the backward step and the choice of step sizes are well-articulated.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors successfully demonstrate how existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) are special cases of PDMM, offering a unifying perspective.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to other ADMM variants.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper provides a clear formula for calculating τi and νi, the sensitivity of performance to the choice of K (the number of randomly selected blocks) is only briefly discussed. The experiments show improvements with larger K up to a certain point, but a more thorough investigation into the optimal K for different problem structures would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:**  The paper mentions that PDMM can run in parallel, but lacks a detailed discussion of the computational cost compared to other methods, particularly considering the communication overhead associated with parallel updates. A theoretical or empirical analysis of the parallel speedup would strengthen the results.
*   **Bregman Divergence:** The introduction of the Bregman divergence feels somewhat disconnected from the core PDMM algorithm. While theoretically sound, its practical impact and the choice of appropriate Bregman divergences for different applications aren't sufficiently explored.  The discussion could be streamlined or expanded for greater clarity.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications. While RPCA and overlapping group lasso are relevant examples, expanding the evaluation to other problem domains (e.g., distributed optimization, large-scale logistic regression) would enhance the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Notation:** The notation is sometimes dense and could be made more consistent. For instance, the distinction between `x` and `x t` isn't always clear in the equations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in optimization with practical relevance.
*   The proposed method (PDMM) is novel and theoretically sound.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to existing methods.
*   The connection to existing algorithms (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The discussion of parameter sensitivity and computational cost could be more in-depth.
*   The scope of the experimental evaluation is relatively limited.
*   The explanation of the Bregman divergence could be clearer and more focused.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. 

**Recommendations for Revision:**

1.  **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the parameter K, exploring its impact on performance for different problem instances.
2.  **Computational Cost Analysis:**  Discuss the computational cost of PDMM, including communication overhead, and compare it to other methods.
3.  **Bregman Divergence:** Clarify the role and selection of the Bregman divergence, or consider removing it if it doesn't significantly impact the core algorithm.
4.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Evaluate PDMM on additional problem domains to demonstrate its generalizability.
5.  **Notation:** Review and improve the consistency of the notation throughout the paper.
