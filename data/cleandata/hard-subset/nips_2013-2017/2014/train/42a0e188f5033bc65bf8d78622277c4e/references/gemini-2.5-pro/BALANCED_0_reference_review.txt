### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a canonical problem formulation that captures many important machine learning tasks, such as Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors are motivated by the limitations of existing methods based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). While standard ADMM is effective for two-block problems, its extensions to multiple blocks are either sequential (Gauss-Seidel variants), which limits parallelism, or may fail to converge (direct Jacobi variants).

The proposed PDMM is a randomized block coordinate method that, at each iteration, selects a random subset of `K` blocks to update in parallel. The core technical contributions are twofold. First, the method introduces a "dual backward step" to temper the dual variable update, which the authors identify as a key reason for the instability of direct parallel (Jacobi) ADMM. Second, it proposes a principled rule for setting the step sizes of both the forward dual update (`τi`) and the backward step (`νi`). These step sizes adapt to the problem structure, specifically depending on the number of randomly updated blocks (`K`) and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`.

The paper establishes the global convergence and an `O(1/T)` iteration complexity for PDMM with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. A key conceptual contribution is showing that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM when all blocks are updated (`K=J`), thereby providing a unified view and better understanding of these methods. The authors demonstrate the empirical effectiveness of PDMM on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing it can outperform several related ADMM variants.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of large-scale, linearly constrained, block-separable optimization is of high importance to the machine learning community. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel and addresses a clear gap in the literature between sequential and potentially non-convergent parallel ADMM variants. The introduction of the adaptive dual backward step is a clever and well-motivated idea.

2.  **Unifying Framework:** A significant strength of this work is its ability to unify and explain existing methods. By showing that sADMM and PJADMM are two extreme cases of PDMM (corresponding to exact and inexact primal updates when `K=J`), the paper provides valuable insight into the role of the dual backward step and its connection to the accuracy of the primal update. This enhances our understanding of the broader family of parallel ADMM algorithms.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an `O(1/T)` ergodic convergence rate. The analysis, based on a carefully constructed Lyapunov function, is technically solid, and the assumptions are standard for this class of first-order methods. The derived step-size rules (Eq. 8) are a direct outcome of this analysis and are key to guaranteeing convergence.

4.  **Flexibility and Practicality:** PDMM offers a flexible trade-off between the amount of parallel work per iteration (controlled by `K`) and the convergence rate. This allows practitioners to tune the algorithm to the specific parallel hardware available. The step-size rule's dependence on the constraint matrix sparsity (`di`) is another practical advantage, allowing for larger, more aggressive steps on sparse problems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Experimental Validation of Parallelism:** A central claim of the paper is that PDMM is a *parallel* method. However, all experiments were run sequentially, and performance is measured in terms of total runtime on a single core. While PDMM often requires fewer iterations for larger `K`, this does not directly translate to wall-clock speedups in a parallel environment, where communication and synchronization overheads are critical. A simulation or an actual implementation on a multi-core system would be necessary to convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed parallelism.

2.  **Clarity on Step-Size Intuition:** While the analysis proves that the step sizes in Eq. (8) work, the paper lacks an intuitive explanation for their specific functional form. For instance, the role of the `2J-K` term in the denominator of `τi` is not immediately clear from the text. Adding a paragraph to Section 2 to build intuition for *why* these specific step sizes are chosen would significantly improve the paper's clarity and impact.

3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The comparison against sADMM in the RPCA experiment seems potentially unfair. The authors state they used a publicly available code where the dual update step size was effectively zero (`τi = 0`), leading to poor performance. Since sADMM is presented as a special case of PDMM, a fairer comparison would involve implementing sADMM with the correct, theoretically-justified step sizes derived from their framework. This would provide a much stronger baseline for the `K=J` case.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Strengthen Experimental Claims:** To better substantiate the "parallel" nature of the algorithm, I suggest adding a plot showing projected wall-clock time versus `K`. This could be based on a simple performance model (e.g., time per iteration ≈ `max_k(cost(k)) + overhead_constant`). This would more clearly illustrate the trade-off between per-iteration cost and total number of iterations.
*   **Improve Fairness of Comparisons:** I strongly recommend re-running the RPCA experiment with an implementation of sADMM that uses the appropriate step sizes (`τi = 1/J, νi = 1 - 1/J`). This would provide a more meaningful comparison and strengthen the paper's empirical claims.
*   **Enhance Intuition:** As mentioned above, please add a brief, intuitive explanation for the step-size formulas in Section 2. This would help readers grasp the core mechanism of the algorithm without needing to dive into the supplemental proofs.
*   **Minor Clarification:** In Section 2.3, Remark 1(b), the statement that BSUMM "should converge" in a special case not covered by its theory is intriguing but could be confusing. It might be better to either briefly explain the reasoning or remove this speculative comment.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the unification of prior work is insightful. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental section and are addressable. They do not detract from the core novelty and importance of the work. The paper is well above the acceptance threshold for NeurIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, convergent, and flexible parallel randomized block-coordinate ADMM variant.
*   Provides a valuable unifying framework for several existing parallel ADMM methods.
*   Theoretically sound with `O(1/T)` convergence guarantees.
*   Addresses a problem of high relevance to the NeurIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation of parallelism is indirect (sequential runtime).
*   The experimental comparison with sADMM is potentially unfair.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
