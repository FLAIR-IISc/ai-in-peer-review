This paper proposes a new variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), termed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea is to randomly select a subset of blocks to update in parallel, aiming to mitigate convergence issues observed with full Jacobi updates and improve upon existing parallel ADMM approaches. The authors establish convergence guarantees and demonstrate the method’s performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

While the problem addressed – efficient parallel optimization of large-scale block-separable functions – is undoubtedly important, and the proposed PDMM builds upon a well-established and widely used technique (ADMM), I find the contribution to be incremental and lacking the substantial novelty expected at NIPS. The paper feels more like an engineering refinement of existing ideas than a fundamental advance.

The motivation for the method is reasonable. The authors correctly identify the potential instability of direct Jacobi updates in ADMM and seek to address this through a randomized block selection strategy combined with a “backward step” on the dual update. However, the connection drawn between the backward step and existing Jacobian variants of ADMM (sADMM and PJADMM) feels tenuous, presented more as a framing device than a truly insightful unification. The claim that PDMM provides a “better understanding of the dual backward step” is overstated. 

The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and an iteration complexity of O(1/T), is a strength. However, the assumptions required for convergence (Assumption 1) are standard for ADMM and do not represent a significant theoretical breakthrough. The introduction of Bregman divergences adds a layer of technicality but doesn’t seem crucial to the core contribution. 

The experimental evaluation is adequate but not compelling. The results on RPCA show that PDMM can perform well, but the comparisons with existing methods (ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM, GSADMM) are not entirely convincing.  The performance gains over sADMM are moderate, and the authors acknowledge that GSADMM can converge faster (though sequentially). The overlapping group lasso results demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness, but again, the advantage over established methods is not dramatic.  Critically, a rigorous comparison to a highly optimized implementation of a standard ADMM variant, using appropriate parallelization techniques, is missing. The claim of improved performance lacks the robust benchmarking needed to be truly convincing. The extensive parameter tuning mentioned for RBSUMM further highlights the ease with which comparisons can be skewed. The fact that the optimal K value (number of randomly selected blocks) seems problem-dependent suggests a lack of generalizable advantage.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear problem statement and motivation.
*   Theoretical convergence analysis.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance on standard benchmark problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty – the core idea is a relatively minor modification of ADMM.
*   Weak comparison to highly optimized existing methods. The absence of a highly-tuned parallel ADMM baseline is a significant drawback.
*   Overstated claims regarding the “understanding” gained through the connection to sADMM/PJADMM.
*   Experimental results are not overwhelmingly compelling; benefits are moderate and seem problem-dependent.
*   The extensive use of inequalities and conditions in theorems makes it difficult to grasp the main idea.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results are correct and provide a formal analysis of the proposed method.
*   The experimental results demonstrate that PDMM can perform competitively with existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is limited, and the contribution is incremental.
*   The experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough to definitively demonstrate a significant advantage over well-established methods.
*   The paper lacks a clear and compelling narrative regarding its contribution to the field.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject**. The paper presents a technically sound method with reasonable experimental validation, but it lacks the originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. The contribution feels more appropriate for a more applied conference or journal. The authors would significantly strengthen their paper by providing a more thorough comparison to highly optimized ADMM implementations, a more convincing justification for the claims regarding the dual backward step, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach.
