## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the landscape of ADMM-based optimization methods, specifically addressing the challenges of scalability and convergence in multi-block problems. The proposed Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) offers a flexible framework that generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, and provides a theoretical guarantee of convergence with a practical iteration complexity. While the paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity in some sections, the technical depth and promising experimental results justify a **Strong Accept** recommendation.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints – a common formulation in machine learning tasks like RPCA and group lasso. Existing approaches to solving this problem often fall into two categories: Gauss-Seidel ADMM, which can suffer from convergence issues, and Jacobian ADMM variants, which enable parallel updates but lack a comprehensive theoretical understanding. PDMM aims to bridge this gap by proposing a randomized block coordinate method that allows for parallel updates while incorporating a "backward step" on the dual update to ensure convergence. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader ADMM literature, highlighting limitations of existing methods and clearly articulating the novelty of PDMM. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is particularly insightful, demonstrating a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms at play.  The paper acknowledges relevant work in randomized coordinate descent and highlights the challenges in applying similar techniques to overlapping block problems.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. The analysis appears well-motivated and builds upon existing literature on ADMM.
* **Generalization & Insight:** PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, offering a unified framework and providing insights into the impact of different parameter settings and update strategies.  The discussion surrounding the dual backward step and its connection to convergence is particularly strong.
* **Practicality:** The method allows for parallelization, a crucial aspect for tackling large-scale problems. The consideration of sparsity in the constraint matrix (A) to optimize step size selection is a practical and valuable contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to several baseline methods. The ablation studies (effect of K) are informative.
* **Addressing an Open Problem:** The paper explicitly addresses the open problem of applying randomized block coordinate descent to overlapping block problems, offering a promising solution.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples. Some sections (e.g., the discussion of Bregman divergences) feel rushed and assume a high level of prior knowledge. The notation is sometimes heavy and could be simplified.
* **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper proposes a rule for setting τi and νi, the sensitivity of the method to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated. More discussion on practical parameter tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-executed, they are limited to two applications. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a wider range of problems would further strengthen the claims.
* **Connection to Existing Randomized Methods:** The relationship between PDMM and other randomized coordinate descent methods (e.g., those utilizing stochastic gradients) could be explored more explicitly.  How does PDMM compare to these approaches in terms of convergence rates and practical performance?



**4. Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees of convergence and iteration complexity.
* Generalization of existing ADMM methods, providing a unified framework.
* Promising experimental results on relevant applications.
* Addresses an important open problem in optimization.
* Potential for significant impact in large-scale machine learning.

**Con:**

* Density and occasional lack of clarity in the presentation.
* Limited experimental scope.
* Parameter sensitivity is not fully explored.




**5. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Section 2.1 (Inexact PDMM):**  The motivation for introducing Bregman divergences could be more clearly explained.  A more intuitive example of how this can lead to a closed-form solution would be helpful.
* **Section 2.3 (Randomized Overlapping Block Coordinate Descent):** The connection to overlapping group lasso is well-made, but could be further strengthened by discussing the implications of the choice of K for this specific problem.
* **Theorem 1 & 2:** The assumptions underlying these theorems should be stated more explicitly.
* **Experimental Results:**  Consider including runtime comparisons alongside iteration counts.  Report confidence intervals or standard deviations for the experimental results.  Discuss the computational cost of each method (per iteration).



**6.  Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**.  Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity and presentation, the technical depth, theoretical rigor, and promising experimental results make this a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM method has the potential to become a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a well-motivated and thoroughly analyzed solution.
