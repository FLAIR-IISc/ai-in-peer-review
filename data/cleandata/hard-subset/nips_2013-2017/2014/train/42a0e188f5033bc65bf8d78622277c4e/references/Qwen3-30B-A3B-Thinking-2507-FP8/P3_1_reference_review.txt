

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving convex optimization problems with linear constraints and block-separable objective functions. The authors position PDMM as a unifying framework that generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, while addressing the challenge of handling overlapping blocks in a randomized coordinate fashion.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation with clear global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity results under reasonable assumptions. The authors establish a clear connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), showing they are special cases of PDMM. This unification significantly enhances understanding of the role of the dual backward step.

2. **Novelty and Practical Motivation:** The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature—handling overlapping blocks in randomized block coordinate descent is an open problem. PDMM's ability to address this with a randomized parallel approach is a significant practical contribution, especially for applications like overlapping group lasso.

3. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly organized, with logical progression from problem formulation to algorithm design, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation. The connections to related work are well-explained, particularly in Section 2.2.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear advantages of PDMM over competing methods, especially when considering parallel execution. The analysis of how different block selection parameters (K) affect performance is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Convergence Rate Comparison:** While the paper establishes O(1/T) complexity, it doesn't clearly compare this to the rates of competing methods. Since standard ADMM also achieves O(1/T), the authors should clarify how PDMM's rate compares to other ADMM variants.

2. **Experimental Fairness:** The comparison with GSADMM is problematic because GSADMM is sequential while PDMM is parallel. The paper should either run GSADMM in parallel or compare with a parallel version of GSADMM to make a fair comparison of parallel efficiency.

3. **Parameter Selection Guidance:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient practical guidance on choosing the parameters ηjt and the step sizes τi, νi for real-world applications. While the theoretical framework is sound, the practical implementation details need more elaboration.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions about Bregman divergence terms (ηjt ≥ 0, and convexity conditions). The paper could better explain how these assumptions manifest in practice and what happens when they're violated.

**Originality and Significance:**

PDMM makes a meaningful contribution by providing a unified framework for parallel block coordinate ADMM that accounts for constraint matrix sparsity. The ability to handle overlapping blocks with randomized block coordinate updates is particularly significant, as noted by the authors. While the paper builds on existing ADMM variants, the unification and extension to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized parallel fashion represents a substantial advancement.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical foundation is sound, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature regarding overlapping blocks and provides a theoretically grounded approach to solving such problems.

The minor weaknesses noted (particularly regarding experimental fairness and parameter selection) are not sufficient to undermine the paper's main contributions. These could be addressed in a revision, but they don't prevent the paper from making a significant contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Key Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Clear theoretical contribution with global convergence and O(1/T) complexity
- Practical significance for large-scale machine learning problems with overlapping structures
- Well-motivated algorithm that unifies existing methods
- Convincing experimental results on relevant applications
- Addresses a meaningful open problem (randomized block coordinate descent with overlapping blocks)

**Key Considerations for Revision:**
1. Clarify the convergence rate comparison with other ADMM variants
2. Provide a fairer experimental comparison (e.g., parallel vs. parallel)
3. Add more practical guidance on parameter selection
4. Elaborate on how the theoretical assumptions manifest in practice

This paper represents a solid advancement in optimization methods for machine learning, with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. It's a strong fit for NIPS as it combines rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful applications in machine learning.
