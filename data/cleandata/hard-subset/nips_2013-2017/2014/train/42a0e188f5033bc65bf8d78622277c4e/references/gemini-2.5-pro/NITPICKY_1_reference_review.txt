**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM designed for block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The method's key features include the ability to update an arbitrary number of blocks (K) in parallel at each iteration, a "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm, and a novel step size rule that adapts to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. They claim that two existing Jacobian ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM. The paper also demonstrates an application to composite minimization with overlapping groups, a problem they suggest is challenging for standard randomized block coordinate descent. The method's performance is evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Algorithmic Contribution:** The core idea of dynamically setting step sizes based on the constraint matrix sparsity (via `di`) and the number of parallel updates (`K`) is a sensible and novel contribution to the ADMM literature. This provides a principled way to tune the algorithm for different problem structures and hardware capabilities.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate for a parallel, randomized ADMM variant. This is a solid theoretical result that matches the rates of standard ADMM while offering greater flexibility.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The proposed framework is general. The demonstration on overlapping group lasso is particularly interesting, as it highlights a case where the method's ability to handle coupled constraints via variable splitting is effective.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core ideas, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of errors, inconsistencies, and points of unclarity that undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Critical Errors and Inconsistencies:**
    *   There is a critical contradiction in the definition of the dual backward step parameter `νi`. In Section 2, equation (8) states `νi = 1 - K̃i`, which is incorrect (e.g., if `K̃i > 1`, `νi` would be negative, contradicting the stated constraint `0 ≤ νi < 1`). However, Theorem 1 correctly states `νi = 1 - 1/K̃i`, which aligns with the motivating examples given in the text. This is a major error in a key equation of the paper.
    *   The presentation of the algorithm (equations 5-7) is confusing. The primal update (5) depends on `ŷt`, which is described as the result of a "backward step". However, the algorithm block fails to define how `ŷt` is computed from `yt`. The text implies `ŷt = yt − νρ(Axt − a)`, but this should be explicitly stated as part of the iterative procedure. Furthermore, equation (7) defines `ŷt+1` but this variable is never used in subsequent steps, making its inclusion perplexing.
    *   In the definition of the Lyapunov function, the expression for `γi` in (21) is typeset ambiguously: `2(J−K)K̃i(2J−K)`. This could be interpreted in multiple ways and needs clarification with proper parentheses.

2.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Overstated Claims:**
    *   The paper claims that sADMM is a special case of PDMM. However, the provided parameters (`τi = 1/J`, `νi = 1 - 1/J`) only match those derived from PDMM's formulas when `di = J`, i.e., for dense row-blocks in the constraint matrix. This significant limitation is not mentioned, making the claim overly broad.
    *   In Section 2.3, Remark 1(b) makes a speculative claim: "BSUMM should converge... although the theory of BSUMM does not include this special case." Such unsubstantiated assertions about other methods are not appropriate for a scientific paper.
    *   Remark 2 is misleading. It claims the fastest convergence is achieved with `J=K=1`, which reduces PDMM to the standard method of multipliers. While true in terms of iteration count, this ignores the per-iteration cost, which is the entire motivation for decomposition methods. The remark fails to acknowledge this crucial trade-off.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Comparisons and Reporting:**
    *   The comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment is questionable. The authors state that "the public available code of sADMM1 does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0" and then show it performs poorly. This is not a valid comparison against the sADMM *algorithm*, but rather against a flawed implementation. A fair comparison would require implementing the algorithm as described in the original paper.
    *   The experimental results for RPCA are internally inconsistent. Figure 1's caption suggests PDMM3 is the fastest, while the subsequent paragraph "The effect of τi, νi" claims that after tuning, "PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm". This contradiction is not explained.
    *   In the overlapping group lasso experiment, the characterization of sADMM is again a strawman. The authors correctly point out their method's advantage is considering sparsity, but phrase it as "sADMM does not consider the sparsity," which is dismissive. The comparison should be framed as an improvement upon the non-adaptive step sizes used in prior work.

4.  **Minor but Numerous Presentation Issues:**
    *   The paper suffers from numerous minor typos and formatting errors, such as "block-seperable," inconsistent summation notation (`J∑ j=1` instead of `∑_{j=1}^J`), non-standard subscripts (`Acj`), and unordered citations (`[8, 4, 24, ...]`). While individually minor, their high frequency detracts from the paper's professionalism and makes it difficult to read.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated parallel ADMM variant with adaptive step sizes.
*   It provides a complete theoretical analysis with an O(1/T) convergence rate.
*   The core ideas could be significant and useful for practitioners solving large-scale problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript contains critical technical errors, most notably the conflicting formulas for the key parameter `νi`.
*   The presentation of the core algorithm is ambiguous and difficult to follow.
*   Claims regarding connections to prior work (sADMM) are imprecise and overstated.
*   Experimental comparisons are flawed and based on questionable implementations or strawman arguments.
*   The high density of typos and formatting errors indicates a lack of careful preparation.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper's central ideas are interesting and potentially significant, the execution is severely flawed. The combination of critical technical errors, ambiguous algorithm presentation, imprecise claims, and flawed experimental comparisons prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper is not yet at the standard expected for NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should undertake a thorough revision to correct the numerous errors, clarify the algorithmic details, and conduct more rigorous and fair experimental comparisons before resubmitting their work to a future venue.
