

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the convergence issues of parallel ADMM for multiple blocks by introducing a dual backward step and carefully determining step sizes based on the sparsity of A and the number of blocks being updated. The paper establishes that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying framework. Theoretical analysis proves global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and the authors evaluate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems. This work extends the applicability of ADMM to overlapping block problems, which was an open problem for randomized block coordinate descent.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear assumptions, well-structured lemmas, and theorems establishing global convergence and iteration complexity. The authors carefully address the connection between PDMM and existing methods, showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with appropriate baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) and well-chosen test problems (RPCA, overlapping group lasso). The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the specific step size choices (8) and implementation details. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations, noting that while PDMM can be faster than GSADMM in terms of runtime, GSADMM may require fewer iterations.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively frames the problem and positions the work within the literature. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The explanation of the dual backward step and its necessity is particularly clear, with a helpful discussion of the "implicit dual ascent" problem in standard Jacobi updates. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (Section 2.2) is well-explained, providing valuable context for readers. The experimental section clearly describes the setup and results, with appropriate visualizations. Some technical details (like the Bregman divergence terms) may require familiarity with optimization, but the paper provides sufficient explanation for the target audience.

## Originality
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the ADMM literature. While ADMM variants for multiple blocks have been studied (e.g., [15, 17, 26]), PDMM is novel in its specific approach to parallel block updates with a dual backward step and step size determination based on sparsity and block selection. The key novelty is the unified framework that connects existing methods through PDMM, providing better understanding of the role of the dual backward step. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by extending randomized block coordinate descent to overlapping block problems, which was previously an open problem. Compared to recent NIPS papers on ADMM variants (e.g., [10, 14, 17]), this work offers a more general framework with constant step size convergence guarantees.

## Significance
The problem addressed (large-scale block-separable convex optimization) is highly relevant to machine learning and optimization. PDMM provides a theoretically grounded algorithm that can be applied to a wide range of problems, including RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results demonstrate practical significance, showing PDMM can outperform existing methods in terms of runtime while maintaining convergence guarantees. The paper's approach to handling overlapping blocks is particularly significant, as this was an open problem for randomized block coordinate methods. While the convergence rate (O(1/T)) matches existing ADMM variants, the flexibility of PDMM and its constant step size make it more practical for large-scale problems.

## Strengths
- Well-founded theoretical contribution with global convergence and O(1/T) complexity
- Provides a unifying framework connecting existing ADMM variants
- Addresses an important open problem (overlapping blocks in randomized block coordinate descent)
- Comprehensive experimental validation on relevant machine learning problems
- Careful discussion of both theoretical and practical considerations

## Weaknesses
- The convergence rate (O(1/T)) is not improved over existing ADMM variants, which is standard for this class of methods
- The connection to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is somewhat expected given the paper's direct development from them
- The paper could provide more intuition on why the specific step size choices (8) are optimal

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis
- Provides a unifying framework that improves understanding of existing ADMM variants
- Addresses an important open problem (overlapping blocks)
- Demonstrates practical effectiveness in experiments
- Well-written and well-organized

**Con:**
- Convergence rate does not improve upon existing methods
- The novelty is somewhat incremental, building directly on prior work
- The paper does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art in convergence rates

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a technically sound, well-motivated contribution to the ADMM literature. While the convergence rate is not improved over existing methods, the paper's unified framework and practical considerations (constant step size, handling of sparsity) make it a valuable addition to the field. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results support the claims. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the problem of solving large-scale block-separable convex optimization problems. Given the significance of the problem and the quality of the contribution, this paper should be accepted.
