## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper presents the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate descent algorithm for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address a key challenge in extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks: the potential for non-convergence observed in standard implementations. PDMM tackles this by introducing a "backward step" on the dual update, incorporating sparsity information into step size selection, and enabling the parallel update of an arbitrary number of primal blocks. The authors demonstrate theoretical convergence guarantees and present empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showcasing competitive performance against existing ADMM variants.  A significant contribution is establishing connections between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM, offering a unified framework and deeper understanding of these algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Critical Issue:** The paper directly tackles a known limitation of multi-block ADMM – potential non-convergence – offering a practical and theoretically sound solution.
* **Novel Algorithm Design:** The incorporation of a dual backward step, adaptive step size based on problem structure (sparsity), and parallel randomized block updates represent a genuinely novel approach to ADMM.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide convergence analysis with an O(1/T) iteration complexity, a strong result for a method of this type. The assumptions are reasonable and standard for ADMM-like algorithms.
* **Unified Framework:**  Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is a valuable theoretical contribution, providing a unified perspective on these algorithms and enhancing our understanding of their interplay.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on two distinct applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) are encouraging and demonstrate the competitive performance of PDMM. The comparisons against established methods like ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM are well-executed.
* **Clear Motivation & Connection to Prior Work:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind PDMM, situating it within the landscape of existing ADMM variants and highlighting its advantages.
* **Well-Structured & Written:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it relatively easy to follow the algorithmic development and theoretical arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity (Implied):** While the authors provide a formula for calculating τi and νi, the impact of K (number of randomly selected blocks) isn't explored thoroughly in the experiments. The results suggest a sensitivity to K, but a more detailed ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.  How robust are these parameters to variations in the problem structure?
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with existing methods is good, the scope could be expanded.  For example, comparing with more recent accelerated ADMM variants or scalable optimization libraries would be valuable. The choice of algorithms to compare is somewhat limited, and the discussion of why others weren’t chosen would be helpful.
* **Experimental Setup Details:** Some experimental details are missing. For example, the specific parameter settings for the competing algorithms (e.g., penalty parameters in ADMM) are not always clearly stated. Reproducibility would be enhanced with more comprehensive reporting of these details.  The claim that the code of sADMM does not have a dual update seems questionable and should be verified or clarified.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** The paper discusses the benefits of parallelization, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost per iteration (vs. sequential methods) would be beneficial.  Specifically, the overhead of gathering messages due to the parallel updates should be discussed.
* **Bregman Divergence Role:** The role of the Bregman divergence, while mentioned, isn't fully fleshed out. A more intuitive explanation of its benefits and the implications of choosing different Bregman divergences would enhance understanding.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novelty and theoretical soundness of the proposed algorithm.
* Addresses a significant open problem in multi-block ADMM.
* Strong convergence guarantees.
* Empirical results demonstrate competitive performance.
* Unified framework connecting different ADMM variants.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity to the parameter K isn’t thoroughly explored.
* Limited comparison with the broader landscape of ADMM algorithms.
* Some experimental details are missing, hindering reproducibility.
* Computational cost discussion could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems. The proposed PDMM algorithm offers a practical and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its effectiveness through both analysis and experimentation. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding a more detailed parameter sensitivity analysis and broader comparison), the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest including a more thorough ablation study on the impact of K, providing more detailed experimental setup information, and expanding the comparison with other relevant algorithms.  Clarification regarding the sADMM code is also needed.
