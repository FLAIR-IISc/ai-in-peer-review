

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors establish that PDMM generalizes two existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases, while providing a unified framework for understanding the dual backward step. The paper demonstrates global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity and evaluates PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation with well-structured convergence proofs and complexity analysis. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions, which is significant since many existing ADMM variants for multiple blocks lack such guarantees. The parameter choice (8) that accounts for matrix sparsity and number of blocks is a notable technical contribution.

2. **Unified Framework:** The key insight that PDMM generalizes sADMM and PJADMM as special cases is well-justified and provides valuable theoretical insights into the relationship between these methods. This unification helps clarify the role of the dual backward step, which has been a somewhat mysterious component in existing approaches.

3. **Practical Significance:** The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate that PDMM achieves faster convergence than competing methods in terms of runtime, particularly when using multiple blocks. The paper effectively shows how the sparsity of A and block selection strategy impact performance.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm description is clear. The connection between PDMM and existing methods is thoroughly explained, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with ADMM variants.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the paper compares PDMM with sADMM, GSADMM, and RBSUMM, it lacks comparison with more recent ADMM variants (e.g., [9, 10, 20] in the references) that might have better convergence properties. The paper claims PDMM is "faster than existing methods," but the comparison could be strengthened with more comprehensive benchmarks.

2. **Implementation Details:** The experimental section lacks specific details about how blocks are chosen (e.g., random selection vs. cyclic), the exact parameter values used for different methods, and computational resources. For instance, it's unclear why PDMM1 (updating one block) is the fastest algorithm in RPCA, which seems counterintuitive given the parallel nature of the method.

3. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The convergence proof relies on Assumption 1, which requires the existence of a KKT point for the Lagrangian (ρ=0). This assumption might not hold for all practical problems, and the paper doesn't discuss how common this assumption is in the applications they consider.

4. **Scalability Analysis:** The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how PDMM scales with the number of blocks (J) or the size of the problem. While Figure 2 shows that PDMM with K>21 converges faster than with K=1, it doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with extremely large J (e.g., J=1000+), which is a key consideration for parallel methods.

5. **Connection to RBCD:** The paper claims that PDMM solves the open problem of RBCD for overlapping blocks, but this connection isn't fully developed. The relationship between PDMM and RBCD for overlapping blocks should be more explicitly stated and justified.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on ADMM variants. The theoretical framework is well-developed, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper's main strength lies in its unified perspective on existing methods and the practical parameter choices that account for problem structure.

While the paper is strong, it would benefit from more comprehensive experimental comparisons with recent methods, additional implementation details, and a more thorough discussion of scalability. The theoretical analysis could also be strengthened by addressing the KKT existence assumption more thoroughly.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of ADMM variants and provides a practical algorithm with theoretical guarantees. While there are some weaknesses in the experimental comparison and implementation details, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's technical contributions and the clear potential for practical impact. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the experimental comparison to include more recent ADMM variants from the literature.
2. Provide more specific details about block selection strategies and parameter choices in the experiments.
3. Discuss how the algorithm scales with extremely large numbers of blocks (J) and large problem sizes.
4. Clarify the connection between PDMM and RBCD for overlapping blocks, explicitly addressing why PDMM solves this "open problem."
5. Discuss the practical implications of Assumption 1 (existence of KKT point) for the applications considered.

The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of distributed optimization, with clear theoretical insights and practical benefits. The proposed method provides a valuable addition to the toolkit for solving large-scale convex optimization problems.
