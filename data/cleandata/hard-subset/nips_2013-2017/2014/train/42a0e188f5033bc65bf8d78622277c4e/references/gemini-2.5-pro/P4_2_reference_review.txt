### Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a canonical problem form that encompasses many machine learning tasks, such as Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The proposed method is a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` primal variable blocks and updates them in parallel.

The key technical contributions that distinguish PDMM from prior work are threefold. First, it introduces a "backward step" on the dual variable update to prevent the overly aggressive updates that cause nave parallel (Jacobi-style) ADMM variants to diverge. Second, the step sizes for the dual update and the backward step are adaptively chosen based on the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated in parallel. Third, the framework is general, allowing any number of blocks (from one to all) to be updated in each iteration.

The paper situates PDMM in the context of existing multi-block ADMM methods. It contrasts its parallel nature with sequential Gauss-Seidel ADMM variants (like BSUMM by Hong et al., 2014), arguing that PDMM has a more robust convergence theory with a constant step size. More significantly, it shows that two existing parallel ADMM methods are special cases of PDMM. Specifically, the splitting ADMM (sADMM) corresponds to the case where all blocks are updated exactly (`K=J`, `η=0`), while the Proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM by Deng et al., 2013) corresponds to a highly inexact primal update with a large proximal term, which allows for a full dual update step without the backward correction. This unification provides a valuable perspective on the role of the dual backward step. The paper also provides a novel application of this parallel framework to solve overlapping group lasso problems, an area where standard randomized block coordinate descent methods are not directly applicable. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing the algorithm's global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity in expectation, which is standard for this class of methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The core idea of combining a randomized parallel block update with an adaptive dual backward step is novel and well-motivated. The specific formulation of the step sizes `τi` and `νi` (Eq. 8), which incorporate both the degree of parallelism (`K`) and the constraint matrix sparsity (`di`), is a significant technical contribution. The theoretical unification of sADMM and PJADMM as two extremes of the PDMM framework is an insightful result that clarifies the relationship between different parallel ADMM schemes.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions for ADMM-type methods. The use of a Lyapunov-like function `h(v*, vt)` provides a clear path for the convergence proof. The claims appear well-supported by the theoretical framework presented.

3.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers a flexible and parallelizable framework for a wide class of optimization problems in machine learning. Its ability to adapt to the problem structure (sparsity) and available computational resources (number of parallel updates `K`) makes it practically appealing for large-scale applications. The successful application to the overlapping group lasso problem demonstrates its utility in solving complex structured sparsity problems that are challenging for other block coordinate methods.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for the key components of the algorithm, particularly the dual backward step, is explained intuitively by highlighting the failure of direct Jacobi updates. The relationship to prior work is explicitly and effectively detailed.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments show promising results, some aspects could be strengthened.
    *   The primary advantage of PDMM is its parallel nature, yet all experiments were conducted sequentially. The reported runtime improvements are due to reducing the computational cost per iteration (by updating fewer blocks), not due to actual parallel execution. A simulation on a multi-core system or a discussion of the expected parallel speedup, considering communication overhead, would make the practical claims more convincing.
    *   The comparison against sADMM in the RPCA experiment may not be entirely fair. The authors note that the public code for sADMM uses a zero step size for the dual update (`τi=0`), which deviates from the theory and leads to slow convergence. A more direct comparison would involve implementing sADMM with its theoretically prescribed step size or comparing against a PDMM variant that also omits the dual update.
    *   For the overlapping group lasso experiment, the authors state that RBSUMM did not converge. While this highlights the robustness of PDMM, a brief analysis or hypothesis as to *why* RBSUMM failed would strengthen the paper's claims about the necessity of its specific design choices.

2.  **Clarity on Parameter Derivation:** The paper presents the specific formulas for the step sizes `τi` and `νi` in Equation (8) without providing much intuition in the main text for their derivation. While the proofs in the supplement likely justify these choices, a brief, intuitive explanation of why these specific forms are critical for ensuring convergence would improve the paper's self-containedness and readability.

3.  **Practicality of Randomization:** The paper suggests different schemes for selecting blocks, such as partitioning or cyclic permutation. The theoretical analysis, however, is based on expectation over a uniform random selection of `K` blocks. It is not immediately clear if the convergence guarantees hold for these more deterministic sampling strategies. Clarifying this point would be helpful for practitioners.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper presents a solid and valuable contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. It introduces a novel, flexible, and theoretically-grounded parallel algorithm (PDMM) for a broad class of convex optimization problems. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the unification of previous parallel ADMM methods is a notable insight. While the experimental validation of parallel performance could be more direct, the presented results are promising and demonstrate the method's effectiveness on relevant machine learning problems. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed to improve the manuscript further. The work is technically sound, original, and of high potential significance to the NIPS community.
