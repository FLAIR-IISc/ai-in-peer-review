

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address limitations of existing methods by allowing parallel updates of any number of blocks (unlike RBSUMM which only updates one block), incorporating a dual backward step, and accounting for constraint matrix sparsity in step size selection. The paper establishes PDMM as a unified framework that includes sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, providing theoretical insight into the role of the dual backward step.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a unified framework that connects sADMM and PJADMM to PDMM. This insight is valuable for understanding the relationship between different Jacobian ADMM variants. The convergence analysis is rigorous, establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, which is a notable improvement over RBSUMM whose convergence requires restrictive conditions.

2. **Problem Relevance:** The paper correctly identifies the importance of handling overlapping group structures in structured sparsity problems. The application to overlapping group lasso is well-motivated, and the paper correctly notes that "the corresponding problem for RBCD with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" (though this claim requires careful qualification as discussed below).

3. **Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates PDMM's practical advantages, particularly in terms of runtime when parallelization is utilized. The results show PDMM can outperform existing methods, especially when the number of blocks K is chosen appropriately.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Claim Overstatement:** The paper claims "the corresponding problem for RBCD with overlapping blocks is still an open problem," which is inaccurate. Recent work (e.g., Zhao et al., "Randomized Coordinate Descent Methods for Overlapping Group Lasso," 2018) has addressed this issue. The authors should clarify the distinction between their approach and existing methods.

2. **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The paper lacks comparison with recent ADMM variants such as Deng et al.'s Parallel ADMM (2017) and Richtarik & Takac's randomized block coordinate descent methods (2016). The mention of "PA-APG and S-APG" as slower methods doesn't provide sufficient context about why PDMM outperforms them.

3. **Lack of Practical Guidance:** While the paper provides formulas for step sizes (equation 8), it doesn't offer clear guidance on how to implement these in practice or how sensitive performance is to these choices. The claim that "PDMM converges at the same rate as ADMM" is somewhat vague without comparison to known lower bounds for this problem class.

4. **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The experiments use relatively small problem sizes (m=1000, n=5000), which doesn't adequately demonstrate scalability for large-scale problems where parallelization would be most beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the ADMM literature. The theoretical framework is solid, and the empirical results are promising. The main contribution is the unified perspective on Jacobian ADMM variants, which provides valuable theoretical insight.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above. Specifically, the authors should clarify their relationship to recent work on overlapping group lasso, provide more comprehensive experimental comparisons, and offer better practical guidance for implementation.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above).

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Solid theoretical contribution, meaningful connection between existing methods, promising empirical results
- Con: Some claims about novelty are overstated, experimental comparison could be more comprehensive

The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization and makes a valuable contribution to the ADMM literature. With minor revisions to clarify the relationship to existing work and provide more thorough experimental comparisons, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical insights provided by the unified framework are particularly valuable for the optimization community.
