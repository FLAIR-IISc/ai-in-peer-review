- Proposal: PDMM — randomized parallel block-coordinate ADMM with a backward dual step and sparsity-tuned dual steps; supports inexact Bregman linearization; proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate.

- Novelty: allows arbitrary number K of parallel block updates, explicitly uses A's sparsity in τi, and unifies sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases.

- Theoretical strength: presents a Lyapunov function and residual showing monotone expected decrease with constant steps; derivation of τi and νi is plausible and well-motivated.

- Theoretical weaknesses: crucial matrices (Pt, Q) are not specified in the main text; equivalence proofs deferred to an external arXiv paper rather than the supplement; only ergodic/in-expectation rates provided (no last-iterate/high-probability results).

- Algorithmic insight: backward dual step mitigates Jacobi instability; sparsity-aware τi intuitively permits larger dual steps for sparser coupling; Bregman linearization for inexact updates is standard and justified.

- Experiments: RPCA and overlapping group lasso are appropriate and PDMM is competitive or faster in reported runs.

- Experimental limitations: all runs are sequential Matlab so no demonstrated parallel speedup; some baselines may be unfairly implemented (e.g., sADMM without proper dual updates).

- Clarity issues: numerous typos, notational inconsistencies, and missing precise definitions (Pt, Q, βi) hinder readability.

- Empirical suggestions: add ablations isolating the backward step and sparsity-aware τi, show plain Jacobi failure, include real parallel/distributed wall-clock experiments, and ensure fair baseline implementations.

- Practical guidance needed: clarify how to choose K in practice, how di is computed at scale, and report sensitivity plots for τi/νi.

- Originality assessment: meaningful and nontrivial combination of randomized-Jacobi updates, backward dual step, and sparsity-aware steps; novel O(1/T) guarantee for this setting without extra error-bound assumptions.

- Significance: fills a gap between variable-splitting, large-proximal, and sequential-update fixes for multi-block ADMM; potentially valuable for large-scale/distributed problems but not yet empirically demonstrated.

- Pros for acceptance: solid algorithmic design, clear theoretical guarantees, conceptual unification of related ADMM variants, and applicability to overlapping group problems.

- Cons for acceptance: organization/clarity problems, reliance on external appendix for key proofs, limited experimental validation of parallelism, only ergodic guarantees, and only partial exploration of parameter sensitivity.

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improved clarity, self-contained proofs, and stronger empirical validation (particularly real parallel runs and fair baselines).
