- Proposal: PDMM — a randomized parallel block-coordinate ADMM with a dual backward step and per-dual-block step sizes depending on row sparsity di and number of updated blocks K; supports inexact Bregman updates and recovers sADMM/PJADMM as special cases, with global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates; experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

- Relation to prior work: Positions PDMM among GS-ADMM, PJADMM, and randomized single-block methods; notes Jacobi ADMM divergence and connects the backward step to relaxation/damping while highlighting novel sparsity-aware step sizes.

- Conceptual strength: Clear diagnosis of naive Jacobi ADMM failure (implicit dual ascent) and a principled backward dual step remedy with problem-dependent step sizes.

- Generality: Unified treatment of exact and inexact updates via Bregman proximalization; supports arbitrary K parallel updates and standard splitting for overlapping groups.

- Theoretical contribution: Global convergence and explicit O(1/T) ergodic complexity bounds with constants showing trade-offs in K and sparsity; residuals tied to optimality conditions.

- Connections/insight: Unifies sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM, clarifying when to prefer dual damping versus proximalization.

- Empirical indication: PDMM variants are competitive and sometimes faster than baselines on the tested problems.

- Major experimental weakness: No multicore/distributed or wall-clock parallel speedup experiments, no communication or scaling analysis despite parallelizability claims.

- Baseline and tuning concerns: sADMM implementation lacked a dual update; PJADMM was not evaluated; limited RBSUMM schedules and unclear tuning for accelerated proximal baselines.

- Parameter robustness issue: Experiments report “best” τi, νi that deviate from theoretical values without analysis of convergence guarantees or sensitivity.

- Clarity and presentation problems: Typos, inconsistent notation (ρ factors, many variable names), heavy reliance on supplement, and lack of pseudocode or intuitive definitions for Pt/Q.

- Theoretical exposition gap: Key definitions and proof intuition (e.g., how di and K appear in constants) are deferred to the supplement; a brief proof sketch in the main text would help.

- Limited application validation: Only two mostly synthetic problems tested; no real RPCA datasets or experiments showing benefit when di varies widely.

- Questions for authors: Mapping of τi/νi to standard ADMM/Chambolle–Pock relaxation, sensitivity and adaptive rules for τi/νi, concrete definitions and computability of Pt/Q, behavior under rank-deficient/ill-conditioned A and preconditioning, and practical guidance for choosing Bregman terms.

- Suggested improvements: Add multicore/distributed experiments with communication costs, fix and fairly tune baselines (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM), include di/K ablations, provide pseudocode and consistent notation, and add a main-text proof sketch and discussion of asynchronous variants.

- Overall evaluation: Solid theoretical novelty (backward step and sparsity-aware steps) and moderate clarity, but limited practical impact due to insufficient and unfair empirical validation.

- Pros: Clear motivation and fix, theory with explicit K and sparsity dependence, method unifies existing approaches, and supports practical inexact updates.

- Cons: No actual parallel results, incomplete/unfair baseline comparisons, and clarity/notation issues with heavy supplement dependence.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — interesting theory but empirical section and comparisons need significant strengthening.
