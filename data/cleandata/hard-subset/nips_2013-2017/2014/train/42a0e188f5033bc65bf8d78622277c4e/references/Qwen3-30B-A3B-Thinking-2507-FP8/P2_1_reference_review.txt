

# Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

## Summary
This paper proposes a new algorithm called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. PDMM is a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM that updates any number of blocks in parallel. The key innovation lies in its dual backward step, which addresses convergence issues in direct Jacobi updates, and its sparsity-aware step size selection that considers the number of blocks being updated. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, and demonstrates that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The authors validate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing competitive performance.

## Quality

The paper makes technically sound contributions with rigorous theoretical analysis. The global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity results are established under reasonable assumptions, and the connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) provides valuable theoretical insight. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of direct Jacobi updates by introducing the dual backward step, which is well-motivated by analyzing the implicit dual ascent in the primal update.

The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) and two important applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). The results consistently show PDMM's competitiveness, particularly with the observation that PDMM1 (updating one block) is the fastest algorithm in RPCA despite being a randomized method.

However, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between block size (K) and convergence speed. The claim that "PDMM converges at the same rate as ADMM" (Remark 2) needs further justification, as the convergence rate constants likely differ due to the step size parameters.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively introduce the problem, motivate their approach, and present technical details in a logical flow. The explanation of the dual backward step (Section 2.2) is particularly strong, with clear intuition about why direct Jacobi updates fail and how the backward step addresses this.

The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The paper's structure (motivation, method, theory, experiments) makes it easy to follow. The supplemental material appears to provide necessary technical details, though the main paper could include slightly more explanation about the Bregman divergence in the inexact updates (Section 2.1).

## Originality

PDMM represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on ADMM variants. While randomized ADMM variants like RBSUMM (Wang et al., 2014) exist, PDMM is novel in three key aspects: (1) it allows random selection of any number of blocks (not just one), (2) it incorporates sparsity of the constraint matrix into step size selection, and (3) it establishes convergence with constant step size without requiring decreasing step sizes or local error bounds.

The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is particularly valuable, as it provides a unified framework for understanding these methods. This theoretical insight is a significant contribution that goes beyond simply proposing a new algorithm.

However, the paper could better highlight how PDMM differs from Deng et al.'s (2014) "Parallel multi-block ADMM with O(1/k) convergence" and Hong et al.'s (2016) BSUMM. The paper cites these works but doesn't explicitly contrast PDMM with their approaches.

## Significance

The problem of large-scale block-separable convex optimization is highly relevant to machine learning, particularly for problems like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso where block structure is natural but existing methods struggle. PDMM's ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized block coordinate fashion (Section 2.3) addresses a specific gap in the literature, as the authors note that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem."

The practical significance is demonstrated through the experiments, which show PDMM can achieve better runtime performance than existing methods. In the RPCA experiment, PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm, and in the overlapping group lasso experiment, PDMM with K=20 achieves similar performance to using all blocks but with reduced computation per iteration.

## Overall Assessment

PDMM is a well-motivated, technically sound contribution that advances the understanding of ADMM variants for block-separable convex optimization. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with global convergence guarantees and provides practical evidence of its effectiveness.

The paper's strengths are its rigorous theoretical analysis, clear exposition, and the valuable connection between existing methods through the proposed framework. The main weaknesses are that the paper could better articulate the specific gap PDMM fills compared to recent work, and the experimental results don't show dramatic improvements over baselines.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of convex optimization algorithms. It presents a theoretically sound and practically useful algorithm that generalizes existing methods while addressing specific limitations in the literature. The paper is well-written and the experimental results provide good validation of the theoretical claims.

**Pro-con list:**

*Pros:*
- Novel algorithm that generalizes existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM)
- Rigorous convergence analysis with O(1/T) complexity
- Clear explanation of the dual backward step
- Good experimental validation on two important applications
- Addresses a specific gap in the literature (overlapping blocks)

*Cons:*
- Could better articulate the specific gap compared to recent work (Deng et al., Hong et al.)
- The practical advantages over baselines are good but not revolutionary
- Some technical details in Section 2.1 could be clarified
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address scalability to extremely large numbers of blocks

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It advances the state of the art in convex optimization algorithms with practical relevance to machine learning applications.
