

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives subject to linear constraints. The authors address a key limitation of existing methods: while Jacobian ADMM variants can update blocks in parallel, they often fail to converge without careful parameter tuning. PDMM introduces a dual backward step that enables convergence with arbitrary block selection, and the step sizes are determined by the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks being updated. The paper establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity, demonstrates that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, and applies the method to challenging problems like overlapping group lasso. This work builds on the foundational ADMM framework [2] and extends recent advances in randomized block coordinate methods [16, 18].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence for a parallel randomized block coordinate method with constant step sizes. The connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM provides valuable insight into the role of dual backward steps in ADMM variants.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Insight**: The dual backward step (equations 6-7) is a particularly elegant solution to the convergence problem of direct Jacobi updates. The authors correctly identify that implicit dual ascent in the primal update can lead to overly aggressive dual updates, and the backward step provides a principled way to control this.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The method handles overlapping group lasso, a problem where traditional block coordinate descent methods fail due to overlapping blocks. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design to theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The connections to prior work are clearly articulated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares PDMM to sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM, but fails to compare with the most recent ADMM variants. For instance, the parallel multi-block ADMM with O(1/k) convergence [10] and the BSUMM variants [14] are mentioned but not thoroughly compared to PDMM in the experimental section.

2. **Experimental Detail**: The experimental section would benefit from more detailed runtime comparisons. The paper states PDMM is faster than GSADMM "in terms of runtime" but doesn't provide actual timing data. For the overlapping group lasso experiment, it would be valuable to know how much faster PDMM is compared to ADMM (which is the same as GSADMM in this problem).

3. **Theoretical Complexity**: The theoretical analysis is quite dense. While the proofs are likely correct, the presentation could be improved for accessibility. The authors might consider providing a high-level intuition for the key lemmas before diving into the technical details.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions "without tuning the parameters of PDMM" but doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to the choice of τ_i and ν_i. The experiments show good results with the theoretically derived parameters, but it's unclear how robust the method is to suboptimal parameter choices.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous convergence proofs. The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence. The authors carefully address limitations (e.g., the trade-off between number of blocks and subproblem complexity).

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written with consistent notation and logical flow. The key innovations are well-explained, though some parts of the theoretical analysis could benefit from additional intuition.

**Originality**: The randomized parallel block coordinate approach with dual backward step represents a genuine advance over existing methods. The connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM provides a unifying perspective that wasn't previously available. While randomized block coordinate methods have been explored before [16, 18], the specific approach for ADMM variants is novel.

**Significance**: The method addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization (overlapping groups) and provides a theoretically grounded approach to parallelizing ADMM. The empirical results show clear improvements over existing methods, suggesting potential for widespread adoption in machine learning applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages. While the paper has some weaknesses in experimental detail and theoretical exposition, these do not undermine the overall quality of the work.

PDMM fills an important gap in the literature on parallel ADMM variants, particularly for problems with overlapping blocks where traditional methods fail. The connection to existing methods as special cases provides a deeper understanding of ADMM variants that will be valuable to the community. The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.

The authors should address the following minor points in revision:
1. Include more detailed runtime comparisons in the experimental section
2. Provide a brief discussion of parameter sensitivity
3. Consider adding a high-level intuition for the key theoretical results before presenting the proofs

This paper represents a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical and practical significance. It advances our understanding of ADMM variants and provides a practical method for solving challenging optimization problems in machine learning.
