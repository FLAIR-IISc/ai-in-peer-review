Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles block-separable convex minimization with linear constraints and proposes PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized, parallel, Jacobi-style variant of ADMM that can update an arbitrary number K of primal blocks per iteration. The key idea is to correct the well-known instability of naive Jacobi multi-block ADMM by introducing a dual “backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian. The method adapts dual stepsizes τi and backward factors νi to both the number of blocks updated and the sparsity pattern of A (via per-row degree di), and allows inexact primal updates through Bregman divergences, yielding closed-form updates for common losses/regularizers. The authors show that sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) are extreme cases of PDMM, offering a unifying view: when the primal makes large progress (exact update), use conservative dual steps; when the primal is damped by proximal terms, use full dual steps. They prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convexity assumptions with constant stepsizes, without local error bound assumptions, and demonstrate results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

This work builds on and addresses limitations of multi-block ADMM (e.g., known divergence), randomized block-coordinate schemes (RBCD, RBSUMM), and proximal Jacobian ADMM. Compared to RBSUMM, PDMM updates multiple blocks per iteration with convergence guarantees and explicit O(1/T) rates; compared to PJADMM/sADMM, it provides a principled dual-backward mechanism and sparsity-aware steps that enlarge the safe stepsize region.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: The dual backward step is a clear and elegant mechanism to counteract the implicit dual ascent in Jacobi updates, with a principled, sparsity-aware choice of τi and νi. This is a useful contribution to the multi-block ADMM literature where Jacobi-style updates are notoriously delicate.
- Unifying perspective: Showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases clarifies when aggressive vs conservative dual updates are appropriate, and demystifies the role of proximal terms vs step-size shrinkage.
- Generality and practicality: PDMM supports updating any number K of blocks, exploits structural sparsity via di, and accommodates inexact updates via Bregman divergences that often yield closed forms. The overlapping-group application (with τi = νi = 0.5) illustrates how sparsity-aware steps can dramatically improve practical stepsizes compared to splitting-based sADMM.
- Theory: Global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes under standard assumptions, and without local error-bound conditions required by RBSUMM/BSUMM.
- Empirical indications: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants converge faster than sADMM and RBSUMM, and are competitive with or faster than GSADMM in runtime (despite GSADMM’s lack of guarantees). The K-sweep in overlapping lasso provides practical guidance on the block selection trade-off.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Empirical scope: All experiments are sequential MATLAB runs. The main selling point is parallelization; showing multi-core or distributed speedups and communication costs would greatly strengthen the case. A scaling study (weak/strong scaling) and per-iteration wall-clock breakdown would be valuable.
- Baselines and fairness: Comparisons with GSADMM (known to possibly diverge) are informative but limited. PJADMM with tuned proximal terms and modern linearized/proximal multi-block ADMM variants should be included. The sADMM baseline reportedly uses code without dual updates; please ensure a correct and tuned implementation, as this undermines the comparison. Clarify tuning protocols for all methods to avoid “best-case” selection bias.
- Clarity of presentation: The exposition is dense, with notational overhead and some typos. Key objects (e.g., matrices Pt and Q in the residual and Lyapunov function) are only described as PSD; a concrete definition or constructive bound is needed in the main text to aid readability. Provide more intuition for the specific formulas τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, perhaps through a simple quadratic example illustrating Jacobi divergence and PDMM stabilization.
- Bounds and guidance: While the rate matches standard O(1/T), can the sparsity-aware steps yield improved constants in the complexity bound? Offer practical guidance on choosing K given di distributions and compute/communication costs. Sensitivity analyses for ρ and ηjt (and choices of ψjt, ϕjt) would help practitioners.
- Overlapping groups: The claimed advantage (τi = 0.5) is compelling; an ablation confirming that the gain is from sparsity-aware steps (vs other factors) would be useful. Include comparisons to state-of-the-art proximal-gradient variants beyond S-APG/PA-APG, if possible.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a simple, novel, and broadly applicable mechanism (dual backward step) that stabilizes Jacobi ADMM and unifies several methods.
- Exploits sparsity to set larger, principled stepsizes, a practically important idea with theoretical backing.
- Solid theory under mild assumptions with constant stepsizes, addressing gaps in multi-block randomized ADMM literature.
- Promising empirical results on canonical problems; clear path to impact in distributed and large-scale ML optimization.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation of the “parallel” claim; no actual parallel implementation or scalability results.
- Some comparisons may be weakened by baseline implementations/tuning; missing stronger baselines.
- Exposition could be clearer; some technical details needed in the main paper for self-containment.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept leaning to accept, primarily for the conceptual novelty (dual backward step + sparsity-aware stepsizes), unifying perspective, and potential impact on multi-block ADMM practice. Strengthening the empirical section with real parallel runs and clarified baselines would make this a clear accept.

Confidence: Moderate.
