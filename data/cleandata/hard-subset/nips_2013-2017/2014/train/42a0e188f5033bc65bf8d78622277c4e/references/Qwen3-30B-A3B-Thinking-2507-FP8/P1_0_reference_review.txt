

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the convergence issues of direct Jacobi updates by introducing a dual backward step and carefully designing step sizes based on matrix sparsity and the number of blocks updated. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel, unlike standard Gauss-Seidel ADMM, and the paper establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity using constant step sizes. The authors demonstrate that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing valuable theoretical insights into the role of dual backward steps. The algorithm is evaluated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees with constant step size, which is a major improvement over existing methods requiring decreasing step sizes or strict conditions. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides deep insights into the dual update mechanism.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation:** The dual backward step is elegantly motivated by analyzing the implicit dual ascent in the primal update, addressing a fundamental convergence issue with direct Jacobi updates. The principled choice of step sizes based on matrix sparsity and number of blocks is both theoretically sound and practically useful.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are well-designed, comparing against multiple relevant baselines. The results consistently demonstrate PDMM's superiority in terms of convergence speed and runtime, with PDMM1 (updating one block) outperforming RBSUMM and PDMM3 (updating three blocks) being faster than GSADMM in terms of runtime.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation, logical flow, and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce results. The connections to existing work are well-articulated, and the technical presentation is accessible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Comparison:** While the paper establishes O(1/T) convergence, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of this rate with existing methods like sADMM or PJADMM. A more thorough theoretical comparison would strengthen the paper.

2. **Parallel Performance Analysis:** The paper mentions PDMM's parallel capabilities but lacks a detailed analysis of parallel speedup or scalability with increasing processors. For a parallel algorithm, this is a crucial consideration that would strengthen the practical significance.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments don't explore how sensitive PDMM is to deviations from the theoretically optimal step sizes (τi, νi). It would be valuable to see how performance degrades if these parameters aren't set according to the proposed formula.

4. **Related Work Context:** While the paper references relevant work, it could better position itself within the broader landscape of randomized block coordinate methods. A more thorough comparison with recent advances in randomized coordinate descent would be beneficial.

## Overall Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are carefully derived, and the experimental validation confirms the theoretical predictions. The authors honestly address limitations, particularly the trade-off between the number of blocks and subproblem complexity.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is precise, and the mathematical presentation is accessible to experts in optimization.

**Originality:** PDMM represents a meaningful extension of ADMM variants. While building on existing work, it introduces a novel perspective on the dual update mechanism through the dual backward step and the connection to existing methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work.

**Significance:** The proposed algorithm has substantial practical significance for large-scale optimization problems where parallelism is essential. The applications to robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the algorithm's potential to solve important machine learning problems more efficiently than existing methods. The O(1/T) convergence rate with constant step size is particularly valuable for practitioners.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are solid, the algorithm is well-motivated and carefully designed, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributed optimization, advancing both theory and practice.

The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical insights connecting PDMM to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provide valuable understanding of the dual update mechanism, which is a significant contribution to the field. The practical improvements demonstrated in the experiments further underscore the paper's significance.

While the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of parallel speedup and parameter sensitivity, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. The work is well-suited for NIPS, advancing the state-of-the-art in optimization algorithms for machine learning.
