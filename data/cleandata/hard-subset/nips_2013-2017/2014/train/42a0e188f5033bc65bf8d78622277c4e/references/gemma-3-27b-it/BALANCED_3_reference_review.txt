This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems, a common formulation in machine learning applications like RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The core idea is to extend ADMM to a randomized block coordinate update scheme, offering a balance between parallelization and convergence properties. The authors identify issues with naive parallel Jacobi updates and introduce a 'backward step' on the dual update, along with a careful choice of step sizes informed by the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomly chosen blocks. They establish convergence guarantees and demonstrate empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the optimization problem it addresses and motivates its relevance with examples from various machine learning domains.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors effectively explain the limitations of existing methods (particularly direct Jacobi updates in ADMM) and provide a strong rationale for their proposed randomized approach and the inclusion of the backward step.
* **Theoretical Contributions:**  The convergence analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, is a significant contribution. Demonstrating O(1/T) complexity is a positive result.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper thoughtfully connects PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, highlighting the generality of the proposed framework and providing insights into the roles of different parameters.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to other ADMM variants and show the impact of parameter choices. The observation regarding the optimal number of blocks (K) is valuable.
* **Detailed Technical Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough technical analysis, with detailed derivations and proofs (mostly relegated to the supplement). The inclusion of Bregman divergence for inexact updates is a nice touch, adding flexibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Overhead:** The notation, especially regarding block indices (i, j, K̃i) and the introduction of multiple variables for dual updates (y, ŷ), can be dense and somewhat difficult to follow, hindering clarity. A more streamlined notation could improve readability.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to several ADMM variants, the performance against state-of-the-art solvers specifically tailored for RPCA (e.g., those based on accelerated gradient methods or singular value thresholding) or overlapping group lasso is lacking.  A stronger baseline would better establish the practical advantage of PDMM.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (ρ, K, η) appears crucial for performance.  The paper lacks a systematic discussion of parameter tuning strategies or guidance on how to choose appropriate values for different problem instances.
* **Convergence Rate in Practice:** The theoretical iteration complexity of O(1/T) is encouraging, but the empirical results do not always strongly demonstrate a significantly faster convergence rate in practice compared to other methods, particularly GSADMM. Further investigation into the practical convergence behavior is warranted.
* **Duplication in Text:** There are some instances of repetitive phrasing, particularly when discussing the motivation for different components of PDMM. Consolidating these explanations would improve conciseness.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (PDMM) for a challenging optimization problem with broad applicability.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the method.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM in solving practical problems, albeit with some caveats.
* The connections to existing work are thoughtfully discussed, positioning PDMM within the broader landscape of ADMM-based methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The notational complexity and some redundancy in the writing hinder clarity and make the paper somewhat difficult to follow.
* The empirical comparison could be strengthened by including more competitive baselines and a more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity.
* The practical convergence rate doesn’t demonstrably outperform existing approaches as strongly as the theory suggests.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with solid theoretical foundations and promising empirical results. The proposed PDMM algorithm offers a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization, particularly for large-scale problems with block-separable structure. However, the paper would benefit from improvements in clarity, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, and a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, focusing on simplifying the notation, strengthening the empirical comparison, and providing more guidance on parameter selection. The theoretical contributions and potential impact of PDMM justify its inclusion in NIPS.
