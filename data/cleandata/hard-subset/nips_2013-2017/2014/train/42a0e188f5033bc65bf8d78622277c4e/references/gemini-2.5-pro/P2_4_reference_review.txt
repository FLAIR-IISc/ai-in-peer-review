This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. This is a general and important problem formulation in machine learning. The proposed method is a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Instead of updating blocks sequentially (Gauss-Seidel style) or all at once (Jacobian style), PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks at each iteration and updates them in parallel.

The key technical contributions are threefold. First, to ensure convergence in the parallel setting, the authors introduce a "dual backward step", which makes the dual variable updates more conservative and prevents the over-aggressive progress that can lead to divergence in naive parallel schemes. Second, the step sizes for the dual update and the backward step are adaptively chosen based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated in parallel. This is an elegant feature that tailors the algorithm's aggressiveness to the problem structure. Third, the paper provides a unifying view of existing Jacobian ADMM methods, demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are two extreme cases of PDMM. This connection provides valuable insight into the trade-offs between primal and dual update steps in this class of algorithms. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with a constant step size, under standard assumptions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality & Originality: Strong Theoretical Contribution.** The paper presents a solid and novel theoretical result. It successfully extends the ADMM framework to a flexible parallel, randomized block-coordinate setting. The convergence and rate analysis under standard assumptions for such a general scheme is a significant contribution. The method for setting the step sizes (`τi`, `νi`) based on problem structure (`di`) and algorithmic choice (`K`) is particularly insightful and well-justified by the theory.

2.  **Originality & Significance: Unification of Existing Methods.** A major strength is the connection established between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM. By showing that these seemingly different methods are specific instances within the PDMM framework, the paper provides a deeper understanding of parallel ADMM. This helps clarify the role of the dual backward step and its relationship to the inexactness of the primal update (i.e., exact updates in sADMM require a conservative dual step, while inexact proximal updates in PJADMM allow for a more aggressive one). This is a valuable conceptual contribution to the optimization literature.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained by analyzing the failure of direct Jacobi updates. The roles of sparsity and the number of randomized blocks are also articulated well. The algorithm is presented cleanly, and its application to overlapping group lasso is a compelling example of its utility.

4.  **Significance:** The work addresses a problem of high importance to the NIPS community. Developing efficient, scalable, and provably convergent parallel optimization algorithms is critical. PDMM offers a flexible tool that allows practitioners to trade off the degree of parallelism (`K`) with the work per iteration. The results on overlapping group lasso, a notoriously difficult problem for block-coordinate methods, demonstrate the practical potential of this approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Quality: Lack of Parallel Experiments.** The most significant weakness of this paper is the mismatch between its title/motivation and its experimental evaluation. The method is called the "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers," and its main appeal is its parallel nature. However, all experiments were run sequentially on Matlab. The reported runtime improvements are due to a lower computational cost per iteration (e.g., when `K < J`), not due to actual parallel execution. To validate the central claim of the paper, it is crucial to demonstrate wall-clock time speedups in a multi-core or distributed environment. Without this, the practical benefit of the proposed parallelism remains an unverified claim. The authors even note "PDMM can run in parallel but GSADMM only runs sequentially," which makes the absence of such a comparison all the more glaring.

2.  **Clarity: Parameter Choices in Experiments.** The paper presents a principled way to set the step sizes `τi` and `νi` in Equation (8). However, in the experimental section (Table 1), the authors report results for "best" parameters which differ from the theoretical ones (e.g., for PDMM3, `(τi, νi)` is `(1/2, 1/2)` instead of the theoretical `(1/3, 2/3)`). This raises questions about the practical utility of the theoretical step sizes. Is the theory too conservative? How sensitive is the algorithm to these parameters? A discussion on the gap between the theoretical and empirically optimal parameters would strengthen the paper.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** Technically sound in its theoretical analysis, but the experimental validation is incomplete as it lacks parallel experiments to support its primary claim.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well-structured.
*   **Originality:** High. The combination of ideas (randomized parallel updates, backward dual step, adaptive step sizes) is novel, and the unification of prior work is a significant conceptual advance.
*   **Significance:** Potentially high. A provably convergent and flexible parallel ADMM variant would be of great interest. However, the significance is currently limited by the lack of empirical evidence for its performance in a parallel setting.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution and a clear, insightful presentation. The algorithmic framework and the unification of prior work are valuable additions to the literature. The primary drawback is the lack of experiments that validate the "parallel" aspect of the method. Despite this flaw, the theoretical and conceptual contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. I would strongly urge the authors to include at least one experiment demonstrating wall-clock speedup on a multi-core machine in the final version of the paper, as this would make the contribution complete and much more impactful.
