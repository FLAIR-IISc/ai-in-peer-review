### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to a linear equality constraint, a formulation that captures numerous machine learning problems like Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors note that classical parallel methods like dual ascent have convergence issues, while the method of multipliers, and its popular variant ADMM, break the separability required for efficient parallel updates when more than two blocks are present. Direct parallel (Jacobian) extensions of ADMM often diverge.

To overcome these challenges, the authors propose the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate method. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` primal blocks and updates them in parallel. The key contributions of the proposed method are threefold:
1.  A "dual backward step" is introduced, which uses an intermediate dual variable for the primal updates to make them more conservative and prevent the aggressive dual updates that can lead to divergence.
2.  The step sizes for the dual ascent (`τi`) and the dual backward step (`νi`) are adapted based on both the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A` and the number of randomly chosen blocks `K`.
3.  The framework naturally allows for inexact primal updates via Bregman divergence, which can lead to simpler subproblems.

The paper establishes a global convergence guarantee and an O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with a constant step size, under standard convexity assumptions. It provides a valuable conceptual contribution by showing that two prior parallel ADMM variants, the splitting-variable based sADMM and the proximal Jacobian PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM corresponding to different parameter settings. The efficacy of PDMM is demonstrated on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where it is shown to compare favorably to related methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**

The paper is technically sound. It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence (Theorem 1) and an O(1/T) ergodic convergence rate (Theorem 2). The analysis relies on a carefully constructed Lyapunov function `h(v*, vt)`, and the derived step-size rules for `τi` and `νi` are crucial for ensuring this function's monotonic decrease. The assumptions required for the theory are standard for this class of algorithms.

However, the experimental evaluation has a significant limitation. Despite being named the "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers," all experiments were conducted sequentially on MATLAB. The primary motivation for a Jacobian-style, randomized block coordinate method is its amenability to parallel implementation. Without experiments demonstrating wall-clock time speedups on a parallel architecture (e.g., a multi-core CPU), the practical benefits of the proposed parallel structure over efficient sequential methods (like GSADMM) are not convincingly demonstrated. The results only show the trade-off between iteration count and the computational cost of each sequential iteration.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction provides clear motivation by contextualizing the problem within the landscape of existing optimization methods. The description of the algorithm and its key components—the dual backward step and adaptive step sizes—is understandable. The connection to prior work (sADMM and PJADMM) is particularly insightful and enhances the reader's understanding of the field.

A point of weakness in clarity is the derivation of the step-size rules in equation (8). While the formulas are provided, the main text offers little intuition as to why these specific forms are chosen or how they arise from the convergence proof. A brief, high-level sketch of how these parameters ensure the decrease of the Lyapunov function in Theorem 1 would greatly improve the paper's transparency and pedagogical value.

**Originality:**

The proposed algorithm is novel. The combination of a randomized parallel block selection with a dual backward step, and step sizes that adapt to both problem structure (sparsity `di`) and algorithmic choice (`K`), is a new contribution to the ADMM literature. While dual backward steps have appeared in other contexts, their application here to stabilize a general, parallel, multi-block ADMM is original. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is a strong conceptual contribution that clarifies the relationship between different parallel ADMM schemes. Furthermore, the application of this randomized parallel framework to solve the overlapping group lasso problem is a novel and useful extension.

**Significance:**

The problem of large-scale, multi-block constrained optimization is of high importance to the NIPS community. A provably convergent, genuinely parallel ADMM variant would be a significant advance with broad applicability. PDMM represents a meaningful step in this direction. The flexibility to choose the number of blocks `K` to update allows for a trade-off between parallelism and per-iteration progress, which is a valuable feature for practitioners. The successful application to overlapping regularizers, a challenging problem for standard block coordinate methods, further underscores the method's potential impact. If the claimed parallel performance can be empirically verified, the algorithm is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers.

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Algorithm Design:** The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated algorithm (PDMM) that addresses the known stability issues of parallel multi-block ADMM through a dual backward step and adaptive step sizes.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The work is supported by a rigorous convergence analysis, including an O(1/T) rate, under standard assumptions.
3.  **Conceptual Unification:** It provides an elegant unification of two existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), improving the conceptual understanding of the field.
4.  **High Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of major importance in machine learning, and the proposed method has the potential for significant practical impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The central claim of a "parallel" method is not substantiated with experiments on parallel hardware. The evaluation is purely sequential, which fails to demonstrate the key practical advantage of the proposed approach.
2.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The experimental comparison to sADMM in the RPCA experiment seems to use a non-standard implementation of the baseline (`τi=0`), which may not represent its true performance. The authors state that with the correct `τ`, sADMM is a special case of PDMM, which makes the presented comparison less informative.
3.  **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper shows that the choice of `K` (the number of blocks to update) is critical to performance but offers no guidance on how to set this hyperparameter.

In conclusion, the paper presents a theoretically strong and novel contribution to an important area. The main weakness lies in the experimental validation, which does not support the central "parallelism" claim. While this is a serious flaw, the strength of the theoretical and conceptual contributions may warrant acceptance, with a strong recommendation for the authors to add parallel experiments to the final version.
