Summary
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of ADMM for convex, block-separable objectives under linear constraints. The key idea is to (i) update a random subset K of primal blocks in parallel, (ii) damp dual progress via a “backward step” that counters the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty, and (iii) set per-row dual stepsizes τi and backward coefficients νi as functions of the row sparsity di and K. An inexact variant leverages Bregman divergences to linearize difficult terms, covering proximal/linearized updates. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant stepsizes, and show that sADMM (two-block with splitting) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are special cases. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggest PDMM is competitive or faster than several ADMM variants.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to multi-block ADMM literature, where naive Jacobi updates can diverge and convergence typically requires proximal regularization, relaxation, or back-substitution. PDMM’s backward dual step is closely related to under-relaxation/over-relaxation and proximal Jacobian variants (e.g., Deng et al., 2014). RBSUMM (Hong et al.) is another randomized approach but updates one block and needs additional conditions. The unification claim (sADMM and PJADMM as extremes) is conceptually appealing.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-known instability of Jacobi multi-block ADMM with a clear intuition (implicit dual ascent) and proposes a principled correction (backward dual step).
- General framework: supports exact and inexact updates via Bregman divergences; allows updating any number K of blocks; per-row stepsizes adapt to sparsity.
- Convergence theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions and constant stepsizes; unifies several ADMM variants.
- Practical relevance: overlapping group lasso and RPCA are representative applications; the di- and K-dependent stepsizes provide a plausible way to exploit structure.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited and not aligned with the claimed parallelism: all experiments are sequential Matlab on synthetic data, with modest sizes. No wall-clock results from actual parallel/distributed implementations, strong scaling, or real data.
- Baseline comparison fairness is problematic:
  • sADMM implementation used reportedly lacks a dual update; comparing against it is not meaningful. A proper sADMM or two-block ADMM on a split form with tuned relaxation should be included.
  • PJADMM with sufficiently large proximal terms (as in Deng et al.) is only discussed theoretically; empirical comparisons are incomplete and parameter tuning parity is unclear.
  • RBSUMM is run with a particular step schedule; broader tuning or variants with known convergence conditions are not exhaustively evaluated.
  • For overlapping group lasso, strong first-order baselines (e.g., APG variants) are included but appear slower; further tuning and reporting details are sparse.
- Practical guidance is underdeveloped:
  • Stepsize rules τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i depend on di. In practice, di may be heterogeneous and problem-dependent; sensitivity to misspecification is unexplored.
  • Choice of ηj and Bregman generators φj is crucial for efficiency; guidance and sensitivity analyses are limited.
  • The trade-offs between K, per-iteration cost, and convergence speed are shown in one problem; broader evidence is needed.
- Clarity and presentation:
  • The paper is notation-heavy with several typos and formatting issues; many proofs and key derivations (e.g., the exact conditions under which sADMM/PJADMM are recovered) are deferred to the supplement, making it hard to assess rigor.
  • The “implicit dual ascent” rationale is compelling but not formalized; the exact role of τi, νi in stability and their tightness are not discussed.
- Originality: While the combination is interesting, the backward dual step resembles established relaxation/proximal corrections; randomized multi-block updates have been considered before. The main novelty is the specific pairing of randomized K-block updates with sparsity-aware per-row stepsizes and a unified analysis. This feels incremental relative to PJADMM/sADMM frameworks.

Quality
Theoretical claims (global convergence and O(1/T)) are standard for convex ADMM-type methods; the analysis seems plausible but relies on technical lemmas relegated to the supplement. Empirical evidence is limited and, in places, unfairly configured, hurting the practical credibility.

Clarity
Readable but dense; several sections would benefit from more intuition, clearer algorithm pseudocode, and concrete guidance for parameters. Typos and reference formatting need attention.

Originality
Moderate. The unification and sparsity-aware stepsizes are interesting; the backward step is a variant of known relaxation/proximal ideas.

Significance
Potentially useful if the method indeed improves stability/speed in practice, but the current empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate advantages over well-tuned established methods, nor does it validate parallel performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and generally correct convergence analysis; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.
- Conceptually clear fix (dual backward step) with structure-aware parameterization.
- Addresses relevant applications; shows promising trends.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient, and at times unfair, empirical validation; no actual parallel implementation results.
- Incomplete baseline coverage/tuning; questionable comparison to sADMM.
- Limited practical guidance and sensitivity analysis for key parameters.
- Incremental novelty relative to established proximal/relaxed Jacobian ADMM variants.

Suggestions
- Provide fair, tuned baselines (proper sADMM, PJADMM, GSADMM with back-substitution, and recent proximal Jacobian variants), and include real datasets.
- Report parallel implementations with scalability results.
- Add ablations on τi/νi/ηj choices and robustness to di estimates; include per-iteration cost and total time.
- Move key derivations and the sADMM/PJADMM equivalences into the main text; improve clarity and proofreading.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The theoretical contribution is sound and the framework is appealing, but the empirical evidence and practical validation are not yet sufficient to justify publication at NeurIPS. Confidence: 0.7
