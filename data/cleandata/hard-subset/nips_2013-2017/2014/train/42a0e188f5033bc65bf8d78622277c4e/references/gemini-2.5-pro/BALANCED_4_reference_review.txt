### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a general and important problem formulation in machine learning. The key challenge in this area is that the augmented Lagrangian term used in ADMM-style methods couples the variables, preventing straightforward parallelization of the primal update step.

Existing parallel (Jacobian) ADMM variants often suffer from convergence issues or require adding proximal terms (PJADMM) or using variable splitting (sADMM). This paper introduces a novel approach to stabilize the parallel updates. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks and updates them in parallel. The core technical innovation is the introduction of a "dual backward step", where an intermediate dual variable `ŷ` is used in the primal update to make the dual progress more conservative. Crucially, the step sizes for the dual update (`τi`) and the backward step (`νi`) are systematically chosen based on the number of blocks selected (`K`) and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic convergence rate. They also demonstrate that two prominent existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, which provides a valuable unification and deeper understanding of these algorithms. The practical performance of PDMM is evaluated on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso problems, showing favorable results compared to several related methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Insight:** The main contribution, the dual backward step, is a simple, elegant, and effective idea for stabilizing parallel ADMM. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as two extreme cases of PDMM is a significant conceptual contribution that clarifies the relationship between exact primal updates (requiring a conservative dual step) and inexact updates (allowing a more aggressive dual step). This provides a much clearer picture of the landscape of parallel ADMM algorithms.

2.  **Generality and Flexibility:** The proposed algorithm is highly flexible. It allows for any number of blocks `K` to be updated in parallel, from `K=1` (randomized single block coordinate update) to `K=J` (fully parallel update). This provides a tunable parameter that can balance the trade-off between the computational cost per iteration and the number of iterations required for convergence.

3.  **Principled Step-Size Selection:** The method for choosing the step sizes `τi` and `νi` based on the problem structure (block sparsity `di`) and the algorithmic choice (`K`) is a strong practical contribution. This allows the algorithm to adaptively take larger steps for sparser problems, which, as shown in the overlapping group lasso experiment, can lead to significant performance gains over methods with fixed, conservative step sizes.

4.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. While this rate is standard for first-order methods in this class, the analysis for this novel randomized scheme with the backward step is non-trivial and appears to be technically sound, lending strong support to the algorithm's design.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Experimental Evaluation of Parallelism:** The experiments are conducted sequentially in Matlab. While the reported "runtime" reflects the total number of floating-point operations, it fails to demonstrate the primary motivation for a parallel algorithm: wall-clock speedup on parallel hardware. A more convincing evaluation would involve an actual parallel implementation (e.g., using `parfor` in Matlab or a multi-processing library in Python/C++) and reporting wall-clock time on a multi-core machine as `K` is varied. This would directly validate the practical utility of the proposed parallel scheme.

2.  **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment appears to be unfair. The authors note that the public code they used sets the dual step size `τi=0`, which is not representative of the sADMM algorithm and guarantees slow convergence. For a fair comparison, sADMM should be implemented with its proper step size (which the authors correctly identify as `τi = 1/J`). Furthermore, the claim that RBSUMM fails to converge for the overlapping group lasso problem is very strong; it would be helpful to provide more details about the parameter settings tried and a brief discussion of why it might have failed, to ensure it wasn't an implementation or parameter-tuning issue.

3.  **Clarity on Overlapping Group Lasso Contribution:** The paper presents its application to overlapping group lasso (Section 2.3) as a solution to a problem that is "still an open problem" for RBCD. This could be misinterpreted. The proposed approach relies on a standard variable-splitting reformulation, which is then solved by PDMM. The open problem in the literature typically refers to applying RBCD *directly* to the non-separable composite objective without such reformulation. The authors should clarify this distinction to more accurately position their contribution. It is still a valuable application of PDMM, but not a direct solution to the classical RBCD open problem.

4.  **Presentation of the Algorithm:** The flow of the algorithm could be stated more clearly. The primal update (5) at iteration `t` uses `ŷ_t`, which is computed from `y_t` and `x_t`. However, the updates are presented as computing `x_{t+1}`, then `y_{t+1}`, and then `ŷ_{t+1}`. A clearer presentation would be to explicitly state that `ŷ_t` is computed first, based on the state from `t-1`, before being used in the primal update. For instance:
    *   1. Given `x^t`, `y^t`.
    *   2. Compute `ŷ^t = y^t - νρ(Ax^t - a)`.
    *   3. Sample subset `J_t` and compute `x^{t+1}` via (5) using `ŷ^t`.
    *   4. Compute `y^{t+1}` via (6) using `x^{t+1}`.

### Decision

This is a strong paper with significant novel contributions on both a conceptual and algorithmic level. The idea of a dual backward step to stabilize parallel ADMM is insightful, and the unification of existing methods provides real value to the community. The theoretical analysis is solid. The primary weaknesses lie in the experimental section, which does not fully demonstrate the parallel performance and contains potentially unfair baseline comparisons. However, these weaknesses are addressable. The strengths of the paper in terms of originality, theoretical soundness, and potential impact are substantial. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant algorithmic idea (dual backward step).
*   Important conceptual contribution unifying sADMM and PJADMM.
*   Flexible algorithm with a principled, adaptive step-size rule.
*   Rigorous theoretical convergence guarantees.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Lack of experiments demonstrating wall-clock speedup from parallelism.
*   Some baseline comparisons in experiments may be unfair or incomplete.
