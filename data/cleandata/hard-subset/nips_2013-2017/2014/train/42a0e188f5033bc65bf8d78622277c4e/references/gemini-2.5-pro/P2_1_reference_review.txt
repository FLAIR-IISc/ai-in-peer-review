This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a canonical problem formulation that covers a wide range of machine learning tasks, such as Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The key motivation is to overcome the limitations of existing Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) variants. While the standard two-block ADMM is highly effective, its extensions to multiple blocks face challenges: sequential (Gauss-Seidel) versions sacrifice parallelism, while direct parallel (Jacobian) versions often fail to converge.

PDMM addresses these issues by introducing a randomized block coordinate approach where an arbitrary number of `K` blocks can be updated in parallel at each iteration. The main technical innovations ensuring convergence are:
1.  A "dual backward step" that makes the dual variable update more conservative, counteracting the aggressive implicit dual update that occurs within the primal step.
2.  A novel step-size selection rule for the dual update (`τ_i`) and the backward step (`ν_i`) that cleverly incorporates the number of blocks being updated (`K`), the total number of blocks (`J`), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`.

The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. It also makes an insightful contribution by showing that two prior parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, thus providing a unified view of these approaches. The utility of the method is demonstrated on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

### Strengths

**1. Quality and Originality:** The paper is technically strong and presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm. The core idea of stabilizing a parallel, randomized ADMM with a dual backward step, coupled with an adaptive step size that leverages problem structure, is a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis is a key strength, providing convergence guarantees (O(1/T) rate) for a flexible parallel method, which is often missing for multi-block ADMM variants. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is particularly elegant and provides new insights into the design of such algorithms, linking the inexactness of the primal update to the aggressiveness of the dual update.

**2. Significance and Impact:** The work addresses a fundamental and highly relevant problem in large-scale machine learning. Developing provably convergent and practical parallel optimization methods is of great importance to the NIPS community. PDMM is significant because:
*   It offers a flexible framework where the degree of parallelism (`K`) can be tuned, allowing a trade-off between per-iteration computational cost and overall convergence rate.
*   It naturally handles problems like overlapping group lasso in a parallel, randomized fashion, which is a known challenge for standard block coordinate descent methods.
*   The adaptive step size based on matrix sparsity is a practically valuable feature for many real-world problems.
Researchers and practitioners working on distributed optimization are likely to find this method and its underlying principles very useful.

**3. Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem and positioning the proposed method within the extensive literature on ADMM. The core ideas behind PDMM are explained with good intuition, especially the motivation for the dual backward step. The connections drawn to related work in Section 2.2 are very helpful for understanding the landscape of parallel ADMM algorithms.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

**1. Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The primary weakness of the paper is in its experimental validation. The algorithm is named the "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers," and its main advantage over methods like GSADMM is its amenability to parallelization. However, all experiments were conducted sequentially. While the sequential runtime results are promising, they do not directly demonstrate the practical speedups achievable in a parallel environment. For instance, in the RPCA experiment, GSADMM required fewer iterations than PDMM. The paper argues PDMM can be faster in terms of wall-clock time due to parallelism, but this claim is not substantiated with empirical evidence. Adding experiments on a multi-core machine or a distributed setting that measure actual wall-clock time would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method.

**2. Intuition for Step-Size Formula:** While the step-size formulas in Equation (8) are central to the algorithm's convergence, the paper provides limited intuition in the main text for their specific functional form. The derivation is likely in the appendix, but a brief, high-level explanation of why `τ_i` and `ν_i` depend on `K`, `J`, and `K̃_i` in this particular way would improve the paper's readability and self-containedness for the reader.

**3. Comparison with RBSUMM:** The authors report that they were unable to achieve convergence for RBSUMM on the overlapping group lasso problem. To make this comparison fairer and more compelling, it would be beneficial to provide more details about the range of hyperparameters and step-size schedules that were attempted for RBSUMM.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by a solid theoretical foundation. Its ability to unify existing methods and handle challenging problem structures like overlapping groups is impressive. While the experimental section could be strengthened by including results from a parallel implementation, this does not detract from the core theoretical and algorithmic contributions. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and I recommend acceptance.
