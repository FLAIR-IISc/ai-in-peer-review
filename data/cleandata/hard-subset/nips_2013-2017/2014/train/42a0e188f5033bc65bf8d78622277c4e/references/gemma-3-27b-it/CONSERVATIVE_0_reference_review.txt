This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems, dubbed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The authors motivate this work by highlighting limitations of existing methods like dual ascent and ADMM, particularly when dealing with multiple blocks or overlapping groups. They present a method that randomly selects blocks for updates, incorporating a backward step on the dual update and considering sparsity in the constraint matrix. Theoretical convergence results are established, and experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are provided.

While the problem addressed is relevant – efficient optimization of large-scale block-separable problems is a core challenge in machine learning – the paper presents incremental, rather than groundbreaking, advances. The core idea of randomized block updates is not entirely novel (RBCD and RBSUMM are acknowledged), and PDMM feels like a carefully constructed generalization and refinement of existing techniques rather than a truly new paradigm. 

The paper's strengths lie in its thoroughness. The authors establish connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, providing a more unified understanding. The theoretical analysis appears solid, establishing convergence and iteration complexity. The experimental results, while not dramatically superior to existing methods, demonstrate PDMM’s competitive performance. The consideration of sparsity and the dual backward step appear to be principled and potentially useful modifications. The explicit treatment of overlapping groups is a positive contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The motivation for the backward step, while explained, doesn't feel entirely convincing. The authors state the Jacobi updates fail due to "too aggressive progress on the dual variable," but a more detailed explanation of *why* this happens, beyond intuition, would be helpful. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. While RPCA and overlapping group lasso are reasonable choices, the comparisons are not exhaustive, and the performance gains over strong baselines are often modest. The ablation studies to dissect the impact of the key components (sparsity consideration, backward step, random block selection) are lacking. Specifically, how much performance gain does each component bring? Without these studies it's difficult to justify the complexity of the method. The code is not available, hindering reproducibility. The paper relies heavily on theoretical results, and while commendable, a stronger emphasis on practical implications and real-world scalability would have been beneficial. The notation is occasionally dense and could be improved for clarity. The assumption that a KKT point exists is a strong one, and the authors should acknowledge potential implications if this assumption is violated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound with proven convergence properties.
*   Offers a generalization of existing ADMM variants.
*   Handles overlapping groups, a relevant problem.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on standard benchmarks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental advance; lacks a truly novel breakthrough.
*   Experimental results are not compellingly superior.
*   Limited ablation studies.
*   Code not provided.
*   Motivation for key components isn’t fully convincing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While technically sound and well-written, it lacks the originality and impact typically expected of a NIPS publication. The improvements over existing methods are incremental, and the empirical evidence doesn’t strongly justify the increased complexity. I would encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental evaluation, conduct more thorough ablation studies, and release the code to facilitate reproducibility.  If the authors can address these points, the paper’s value would be significantly enhanced.
