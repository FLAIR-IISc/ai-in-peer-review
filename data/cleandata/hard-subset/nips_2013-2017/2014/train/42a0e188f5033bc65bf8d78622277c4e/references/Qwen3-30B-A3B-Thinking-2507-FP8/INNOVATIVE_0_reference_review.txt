

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a novel parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called PDMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a fundamental limitation of existing parallel ADMM variants: the direct Jacobi updates often fail to converge. Their key insight is to introduce a dual backward step that enables convergence while maintaining parallelism, and to determine step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated.

The paper's main novelty lies in its unified framework that connects existing methods as special cases. PDMM generalizes sADMM and PJADMM, revealing how the dual backward step and step size selection parameters relate to these methods. This theoretical insight is particularly valuable, as it provides a deeper understanding of the mechanisms behind parallel ADMM variants. The paper also successfully applies PDMM to overlapping group lasso problems, where previous randomized block coordinate methods faced challenges.

The technical contributions are well-motivated and well-executed. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size under reasonable assumptions, which is a significant theoretical achievement. The step size selection (τi, νi) based on block sparsity and number of blocks updated is both elegant and practical, addressing a critical issue in parallel ADMM implementations. The dual backward step explanation is particularly insightful, connecting the failure of direct Jacobi updates to the implicit dual ascent in the primal update.

The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's practical advantages. PDMM consistently outperforms existing methods in runtime while maintaining convergence rates comparable to ADMM variants. The ablation studies on the effect of K (number of blocks updated) provide valuable practical guidance for implementing the method. The observation that PDMM1 (updating one block) can be the fastest method in some cases challenges conventional wisdom about parallelism and is an interesting practical insight.

Strengths:
- Novel framework that unifies existing methods and provides new theoretical insights
- Significant practical contribution with clear implementation guidance
- Solid theoretical guarantees (global convergence, O(1/T) complexity)
- Convincing experimental results demonstrating runtime advantages
- Addressing a real limitation in parallel ADMM implementations

Weaknesses:
- Some theoretical explanations (particularly regarding the dual backward step) could be more intuitive
- The connection to RBSUMM could be more thoroughly developed
- The paper mentions "a simple way" to choose blocks without justification for why it's optimal
- The comparison with some state-of-the-art methods (e.g., PA-APG, S-APG) could be more comprehensive

This paper represents a significant step forward in parallel optimization for large-scale machine learning problems. The core idea of the dual backward step combined with randomized block selection is both novel and practical. While the paper could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of some theoretical aspects, the overall contribution is substantial and addresses a real limitation in the field.

The authors' claim that PDMM can solve the previously open problem of randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping groups is particularly compelling. This application alone makes the paper valuable to the community. The fact that PDMM can be tuned to achieve optimal performance for different problem structures (by varying K) adds to its practical utility.

In terms of significance, PDMM provides a new direction for parallel optimization that could influence future work on distributed and large-scale machine learning. The theoretical insights about the dual backward step are likely to be influential beyond just the specific algorithm presented. The paper's contributions are clearly distinct from previous work at NIPS, particularly from the recent ADMM variants presented in 2014-2016.

The paper's main limitation is that when K=J (updating all blocks), PDMM reduces to the method of multipliers, which may require solving difficult subproblems. However, the authors appropriately acknowledge this trade-off and provide guidance for selecting K based on problem structure.

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated approach to parallel optimization. The theoretical insights are valuable, the practical implications are clear, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods. While the theoretical exposition could be slightly more accessible, the paper's novelty and significance outweigh this minor weakness.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with both theoretical and practical value. It aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel, impactful approaches to machine learning problems. The authors have successfully bridged theory and practice, providing a new direction for parallel optimization that addresses a real limitation in current methods.
