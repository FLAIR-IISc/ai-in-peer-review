Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM/augmented Lagrangian methods. The key idea is to enable updating any K out of J primal blocks in parallel while maintaining convergence guarantees for multi-block settings, which plain Jacobi ADMM typically lacks. The method introduces:
- A “dual backward step”: use ŷt = yt − νiρ(Aixt − ai) within the primal update to counteract the implicit dual ascent contributed by the quadratic penalty and prevent overly aggressive dual motion.
- Step sizes τi for the dual update that depend on both the row-block sparsity di (number of nonzeros in row block i) and the number K of updated primal blocks: τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)), νi = 1 − 1/K̃i where K̃i = min{di, K}. This leverages sparsity to allow larger dual steps.
- An inexact variant using a Bregman term to linearize “problematic” components and recover closed-form primal updates; for sufficiently large proximal weights, the dual step can be full (τi = 1) without the backward step, aligning with proximal Jacobian ADMM.

The authors establish that sADMM with splitting variables (when all blocks are updated and τi = 1/J, νi = 1 − 1/J) and PJADMM (when using strong proximal terms, τi = 1, νi = 0) are special cases—two extremes in the PDMM design space. They prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity and KKT existence assumptions. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate PDMM is competitive or faster than GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, with benefits increasing with K and when leveraging sparsity.

This work is positioned within the active literature on multi-block ADMM, where Gauss-Seidel-type methods may lack guarantees or require stringent conditions, and existing Jacobian methods typically rely on proximal regularization. PDMM’s “backward step” can be seen as a damping/relaxation mechanism akin to over-relaxed ADMM and He et al.’s Gaussian back substitution, but here tailored to randomized block updates with sparsity-aware step sizes.

Quality:
- Theory: The convergence analysis appears careful and yields global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant stepsizes for general convex problems—on par with best-known rates for ADMM-type methods. The dependence on sparsity via di is a nice refinement. However:
  - The potential function h(v*, vt) involves matrices Pt and Q declared positive semidefinite but not concretely specified in the main text; it would help to state their definitions or minimal sufficient conditions so readers can verify the bounds and constants.
  - The step-size formula (τi, νi) is given, but intuition for its derivation beyond the qualitative discussion, and explicit allowable ranges for ρ and ηjt, would improve reproducibility.
  - The inexact PDMM assumptions (Lipschitz gradient of ψjt and strong convexity of ϕjt with matching constants) are reasonable, but tighter guidance for practical choices of ηjt and φjt would be valuable.
- Experiments: The tasks (RPCA; overlapping group lasso) are relevant and illustrate both dense and structured-sparse constraints. PDMM is run sequentially in MATLAB, yet the selling point is parallelization; a parallel implementation or at least a simulated wall-clock under multi-core would better support claims of scalability. Some comparisons are problematic: the sADMM code used reportedly omits the dual update (τi = 0), which makes the comparison unfair; PDMM uses tuned τi, νi while RBSUMM uses decreasing steps—ensure similar tuning effort across baselines. Reporting per-iteration costs and wall-clock for all methods, and including convergence plots to optimality gap and feasibility, would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized (algorithm, intuition, theory, experiments), but readability suffers from heavy notation, typos (e.g., “seperable”, “convegence”), and reliance on appendices for key equivalence claims (sADMM/PJADMM special cases). A concise algorithm box (parameters, update order, required communications), explicit definitions of Pt and Q, and a clearer summary of assumptions would help. Minor editorial polish is needed.

Originality:
Moderate to good. Randomized Jacobi-style ADMM variants exist (e.g., RBSUMM, proximal Jacobian ADMM), and over-relaxation/back-substitution ideas are known. The novelty here is the explicit backward dual step coupled with sparsity- and K-aware step sizes that allow updating any number of blocks, plus a unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM as extremes within PDMM. This seems a meaningful conceptual and technical contribution.

Significance:
Addressing multi-block ADMM with parallel randomized updates and retaining convergence guarantees is important in ML applications with large-scale structured problems (RPCA, structured sparsity, distributed optimization). If broadly adopted, the sparsity-aware step sizing and the unified perspective could influence practice. The current empirical evidence is promising but would benefit from more robust and parallel experiments.

Pros:
- Clear problem setting and motivation; addresses a known weakness of multi-block ADMM.
- Intuitive and principled “dual backward step” with sparsity-aware step sizes.
- Supports both exact and inexact (Bregman) updates; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.
- Solid convergence guarantees with O(1/T) ergodic rate and mild assumptions.
- Empirical gains on two representative applications; analysis of K’s effect.

Cons:
- Experimental evaluation is sequential, with some questionable baseline implementations/tuning; lacks distributed/parallel results.
- Some theoretical components (Pt, Q, parameter guidance) are underspecified in the main text.
- Relation to over-relaxed ADMM and other primal-dual coordinate methods could be discussed more explicitly.
- Typos and presentation issues; heavy notation may hinder accessibility.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide explicit definitions or constructions for Pt and Q in the main text and discuss the tightness of the resulting constants?
2. How sensitive is PDMM to ρ, τi, νi in practice? Any adaptive strategies?
3. How does the backward step relate to standard ADMM over-relaxation (α) quantitatively?
4. Could you include a parallel/multi-core or distributed implementation to validate wall-clock speedups?
5. For RBSUMM and sADMM, can you re-run with proper dual updates and stronger tuning to ensure fairness?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a clear algorithmic summary with parameter defaults and selection rules.
- Provide implementation details for ηjt and φjt choices in experiments.
- Include parallel results and expand baselines (e.g., over-relaxed ADMM, primal-dual hybrid gradient with coordinate sampling).
- Improve writing/typos and bring key equivalence proofs (to sADMM/PJADMM) into the main text in summarized form.

Overall: A solid and relevant contribution with novel insights and theory; with stronger, fairer experiments and improved exposition, it would be a strong NIPS paper.
