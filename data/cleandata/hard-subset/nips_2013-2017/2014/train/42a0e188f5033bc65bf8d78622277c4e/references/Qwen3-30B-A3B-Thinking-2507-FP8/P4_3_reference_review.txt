

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates by introducing a dual backward step and develop a method to choose step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and number of blocks being updated. PDMM subsumes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, provides global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity, and demonstrates empirical improvements on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions, with well-structured proofs that clearly connect to the algorithm's design. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations, particularly regarding the trade-off between parallelism (number of blocks K) and subproblem complexity. The experimental results are well-designed, with appropriate baselines (ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) and clear stopping criteria. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific parameter choices and implementation details. The theoretical contributions are well-supported by empirical validation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is precise, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The relationship between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM) is clearly articulated, with specific conditions under which they are special cases. The dual backward step mechanism is explained with sufficient mathematical intuition, and the connection to sparsity considerations is well-developed. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations that clearly demonstrate PDMM's advantages.

## Originality
This work makes several novel contributions. The randomized block coordinate approach with dual backward step represents a significant departure from existing ADMM variants, particularly addressing the convergence issues that plague direct Jacobi updates. The connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM provides new theoretical insight into these methods. The paper also demonstrates how PDMM can solve overlapping group problems in a randomized block coordinate fashion, addressing an open problem for RBCD methods. The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on ADMM variants, particularly in its randomized block coordinate approach combined with the dual backward step mechanism.

## Significance
The paper addresses a practical problem in large-scale machine learning: parallelizing optimization algorithms. The proposed algorithm achieves significant speedups in empirical results while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The O(1/T) convergence rate matches the best-known rates for ADMM variants, while providing parallelism that existing methods lack. The connection to real-world applications like RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates practical relevance. The theoretical insights into the role of dual backward steps also advance the understanding of ADMM variants more broadly.

## Strengths
1. Strong theoretical analysis with global convergence and O(1/T) complexity guarantees
2. Novel dual backward step mechanism that resolves convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates
3. Clear connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM)
4. Effective empirical validation on two important machine learning problems
5. Well-structured and clearly written presentation

## Weaknesses
1. The paper could more thoroughly explore the trade-offs between different choices of K (number of blocks updated), particularly regarding computational complexity per iteration
2. The comparison with RBSUMM is limited by the fact that RBSUMM failed to converge within reasonable iterations in the experiments
3. The RPCA experiments use a single problem instance; additional diverse instances would strengthen the empirical claims
4. The paper doesn't explicitly compare the computational complexity per iteration between PDMM and other methods, which would help understand practical trade-offs

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by empirical results. The proposed algorithm addresses a practical problem with a novel solution that advances both theory and practice. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a compelling case for its novelty and significance.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. The quality of the technical contribution is high, the clarity of presentation is excellent, the novelty is well-established through comparison with previous work, and the significance is demonstrated through both theoretical guarantees and empirical results.

The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on ADMM variants, particularly in its randomized block coordinate approach combined with the dual backward step mechanism. The theoretical insights provided are valuable for understanding the convergence behavior of ADMM variants more broadly.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
