Summary
The paper studies convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM that updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel while performing a “backward” step on the dual variable. The backward step is motivated by the observation that the quadratic penalty induces an implicit dual ascent; without correction, a Jacobi-style primal update combined with explicit dual ascent can diverge. PDMM introduces per-row (dual-block) step sizes τi and backward coefficients νi that depend on the sparsity pattern of A (via di, the number of nonzeros in row-block i) and on the number of randomly updated blocks K. The method admits exact and inexact primal updates through a Bregman-divergence regularization and unifies several existing methods: sADMM (τi=1/J, νi=1−1/J) and PJADMM (large proximal terms, νi=0, τi=1) emerge as special/limiting cases. Under standard convexity assumptions, the paper proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes. Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM variants are competitive with or faster than GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM in sequential Matlab implementations.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated in the multi-block ADMM literature. It acknowledges that direct multi-block ADMM can diverge, and relates PDMM to GSADMM with back substitution, BSUMM (which needs local error bounds and diminishing steps), randomized BSUMM (single-block random updates), and Jacobian/proximal ADMM variants (PJADMM). The backward dual step is reminiscent of under-relaxed/over-relaxed ADMM and extragradient corrections; the novelty here is an explicit rule tying νi and τi to K and to the per-row sparsity di, enabling parallel randomized updates with guarantees.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical gap: convergence-guaranteed, parallel, randomized multi-block updates with constant step sizes for general convex (including nonsmooth) problems.
- Clear conceptual insight: separating implicit from explicit dual ascent and compensating with a backward step; principled dependence of step sizes on K and sparsity (di) is attractive and actionable.
- Unifying perspective: establishes that sADMM and PJADMM are extreme cases of PDMM, clarifying when small dual steps or large proximal terms are appropriate.
- Theory: provides global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard assumptions, with an explicit Lyapunov function. The inexact framework via Bregman divergence is flexible.
- Applications: demonstrates applicability to overlapping group lasso via a standard variable-splitting formulation where PDMM can leverage sparsity (di=2) to use relatively large dual steps.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: there are numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “seperable”, “defiend”, “convegence”; missing years in references). The Lyapunov terms (P_t, Q, γi) are introduced without sufficient intuition or concrete definitions in the main text. x̄T is defined as ∑T t=1 xt (missing 1/T), yet used in bounds typical for averages.
- Reliance on an external appendix (arXiv [25]) for important derivations (e.g., the equivalence to sADMM/PJADMM) reduces self-containment. The main text should include at least a succinct proof sketch justifying τi and νi choices and the role of sparsity di.
- Empirical validation is limited: experiments are sequential Matlab and do not measure actual parallel speedups, communication, or scalability. RPCA and group lasso instances are moderate-scale; gains over strong baselines (e.g., GSADMM/ADMM) are sometimes modest. Baseline tuning is uneven (e.g., sADMM code without dual update; RBSUMM requires careful step-size schedules).
- Practical guidance: choosing ηjt and the Bregman divergence in inexact PDMM is discussed only briefly; more concrete recipes and sensitivity analyses would aid practitioners.
- Novelty vis-à-vis related primal-dual randomized coordinate methods and extragradient/relaxed ADMM could be discussed more deeply to delineate what is fundamentally new versus a principled synthesis.

Quality
The technical claims appear sound at a high level, with reasonable assumptions and an ergodic O(1/T) rate matching ADMM variants. However, the main text omits key details (e.g., explicit forms of P_t and Q, exact conditions for inexact updates—Lipschitz/strong convexity constants) and relies heavily on the supplement. Empirically, the experiments support the claims qualitatively but lack breadth and true parallel implementations.

Clarity
Overall organization is reasonable, but readability suffers due to typos, heavy notation, and elided definitions. Including pseudocode, a schematic of the update flow (showing implicit vs explicit dual ascent and the backward step), and a small worked example would help.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea of a backward/relaxation step combined with randomized block updates is related to existing ADMM and primal-dual literature; the contribution lies in the specific parameterization tied to sparsity and K, the unifying view, and the convergence analysis for multi-block randomized parallel updates.

Significance
Potentially useful for large-scale structured problems with sparse coupling. If accompanied by robust parallel implementations, the approach could be impactful. As-is, significance is tempered by limited empirical evidence of practical parallel gains.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a principled, convergence-guaranteed randomized parallel multi-block ADMM with constant steps.
- Insightful unification of sADMM and PJADMM; step-size rules exploit sparsity.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with flexibility for inexact updates.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and purely sequential experiments; no demonstration of real parallel/distributed benefits or large-scale scalability.
- Presentation issues and reliance on external appendix for crucial details.
- Some novelty overlaps with known relaxation/extragradient ideas; prior art positioning could be stronger.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen empirical section: implement true parallel/distributed PDMM, report wall-clock speedups vs K, communication/computation breakdown, and scalability on larger datasets; include more baselines (e.g., over-relaxed ADMM, primal-dual hybrid gradient with block sampling) with careful tuning.
- Clarify algorithm: add pseudocode; provide intuition for τi, νi choices and bounds; explicitly define P_t, Q, γi, βi; correct x̄T definition; tighten notation and fix typos.
- Provide practical guidance for inexact PDMM: default choices for ψjt and ϕjt, setting ηjt, and diagnostics for when to increase proximal strength.
- Include proof sketches in the main text for the sADMM/PJADMM connections and the sparsity-dependent step-size derivation.
- Discuss relation to extragradient/relaxation in ADMM and randomized primal-dual coordinate methods to clearly delineate novelty.

Decision
Weak reject. The paper has attractive ideas and theory, but the current presentation and empirical evidence fall short of NeurIPS standards. With improved clarity and stronger parallel experiments, this work could be competitive.
