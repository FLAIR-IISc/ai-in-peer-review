Summary
The paper addresses linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization and proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM. The key idea is to make Jacobi-style multi-block updates stable by introducing a “dual backward step” that counters the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. The method samples any number K of primal blocks per iteration and updates them in parallel with step sizes that explicitly depend on (i) the number of updated blocks K and (ii) the sparsity pattern (degrees di) of the constraint matrix A. An inexact variant using Bregman divergences unifies exact and proximal updates. The authors show that two prominent multi-block ADMM variants are special cases: sADMM (small dual step when exact primal steps are made) and PJADMM (full dual step with sufficiently strong proximal terms). They establish global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions with constant step size, and demonstrate empirical gains on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

Relation to prior work
Multi-block ADMM is known to be problematic without additional structure or safeguards. Prior approaches include: GSADMM and BSUMM (often requiring error bounds or small/decreasing steps), PJADMM (proximal terms for stability, o(1/T) rate), and randomized schemes that update a single block (e.g., RBSUMM). The proposed PDMM contributes a principled Jacobi-style scheme that can update multiple blocks K≥1 with constant step sizes tuned to the graph degrees di. The dual backward step is reminiscent of over-relaxation/extragradient ideas, but here it is explicitly linked to neutralizing the implicit dual ascent in Jacobi updates and to making sADMM and PJADMM two extremes of the same framework. The ability to treat overlapping groups naturally via a sparse A and exploit di=2 for large steps is appealing.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The “dual backward step” provides a clean explanation of why naïve Jacobi ADMM diverges and offers a simple, effective fix. The explicit dependence of τi, νi on K and di is insightful and practically relevant.
- Unification: Showing sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases of PDMM offers a coherent view of existing methods and clarifies when small vs large dual steps are appropriate.
- Theory: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes for K≥1 under standard convexity assumptions, with randomized selection of multiple blocks. The inexact PDMM analysis via Bregman divergences is useful in practice.
- Applicability: The framework naturally handles overlapping groups, where di=2 enables larger steps and parallel updates.
- Empirics: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive to faster than baselines, especially in settings exploiting sparsity or multi-block updates.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is sequential in MATLAB; the central promise is parallelism. Absence of multi-core/distributed scaling experiments diminishes the practical impact.
- Baseline fairness: sADMM is reported using publicly available code that omits a dual update; comparisons would be stronger with a corrected implementation. RBSUMM parameter tuning seems limited; modern primal–dual baselines (e.g., PDHG/Chambolle–Pock with over-relaxation, randomized primal-dual coordinate methods) are not included.
- Clarity and notation: Several typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “seperable,” switching between Acj and Aj). The definitions of Pt and Q (used in the residual and Lyapunov bounds) are abstracted away; more explicit forms would improve reproducibility.
- Scope of theory: Rates are O(1/T) (ergodic), standard for this literature; no linear rates under stronger assumptions. No analysis of asynchrony or stale updates, which are natural for parallel implementations.
- Practical guidance: The step-size rules are elegant but may require knowledge of di and K; discussion on adaptivity or nonuniform sampling is brief.

Quality
The technical components are plausible and well-motivated; the convergence statements appear standard and correctly conditioned on convexity and the specified τi, νi. The unification with sADMM and PJADMM is a strong conceptual contribution. The experimental section supports the claims but could be more comprehensive and fair in baseline comparisons, and should demonstrate actual parallel speedups.

Clarity
Overall readable, but the paper would benefit from proofreading, a tighter notation pass, and clearer presentation of auxiliary matrices (Pt, Q) and constants (βi, γi). A brief intuitive explanation of the Lyapunov function and how the backward step affects it would help non-experts.

Originality
Good. The dual backward step to counteract implicit dual ascent in Jacobi ADMM, the step-size rules exploiting sparsity degrees, and the unification of sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM together constitute a meaningful conceptual advance beyond incremental tweaks.

Significance
Moderate to high. If adopted, the method could become a standard tool for multi-block constrained problems, especially with overlapping structures. Demonstrating strong parallel scaling would raise the significance further.

Questions for the authors
- Can τi, νi be selected adaptively without prior knowledge of di, or with nonuniform sampling proportional to degrees?
- How does PDMM relate to over-relaxed ADMM and extragradient/mirror-prox variants formally? Is the backward step equivalent to a specific extragradient correction?
- Are there linear-rate guarantees under strong convexity and full column-rank assumptions?
- Can the theory extend to asynchronous or distributed settings with delays?
- Please clarify/define Pt and Q explicitly and give guidance on implementation.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea (dual backward step) that stabilizes Jacobi multi-block updates.
- Principled step-size rules leveraging sparsity and number of updated blocks.
- Unifying perspective connecting sADMM and PJADMM.
- Solid convergence theory with constant steps and support for inexact updates.
- Practical applicability to overlapping group problems and promising empirical results.

Cons for acceptance
- No actual parallel/distributed experiments; evaluation is sequential.
- Some baseline comparisons are potentially unfair or incomplete.
- Presentation could be tightened; some notation and definitions are opaque.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh and useful perspective on multi-block ADMM, with a simple mechanism that enables parallel randomized updates and ties together existing variants. Despite some experimental and presentation shortcomings, the conceptual novelty and potential impact justify acceptance. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section with parallel speedups and to improve clarity and baseline fairness in the camera-ready.
