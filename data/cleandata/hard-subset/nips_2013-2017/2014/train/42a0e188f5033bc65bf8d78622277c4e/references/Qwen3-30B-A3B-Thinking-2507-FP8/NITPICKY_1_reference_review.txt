

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors claim that PDMM is a generalization of existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, and establish convergence results and iteration complexity. The paper demonstrates the approach on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Quality
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The most glaring issue is the poor LaTeX formatting throughout the manuscript, which indicates a lack of attention to detail. For example:
- "block-seperable" (should be "block-separable") appears in the abstract
- Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "J∑ j=1" instead of "∑_{j=1}^J")
- Incorrect notation like "xt+1j" instead of "x^{t+1}_j"
- In equation (2), "ρ ‖Ax− a‖22" should be "ρ\|Ax - a\|^2_2"
- Equation (8) has improper formatting: "τi = K K̃i(2J−K)" should be "τ_i = K / (K̃_i(2J-K))"

The convergence analysis contains serious technical flaws. In Theorem 1, the inequality "≤ Eξth(v∗,vt+1) ≤ Eξt−1h(v∗,vt)" is mathematically incorrect as written—the inequality direction appears reversed. The paper claims to establish O(1/T) convergence with constant step size, but the theoretical analysis lacks necessary details about the assumptions required for this result.

The connection to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is claimed to be established in "Appendix B and C in [25]" but the paper does not provide these appendices, making verification impossible. The paper also fails to clarify how their method differs from the randomized block coordinate descent method in [16, 18] when applied to overlapping groups.

## Clarity
The paper suffers from inconsistent notation throughout. The dual variable is sometimes written as "y" and sometimes as "y_i" without clear explanation. The primal variable notation is similarly inconsistent (e.g., "xjt" vs "x_j^t"). The paper also uses "K̃i" without properly defining it in the main text (it's defined later as "K̃i = min{di,K}"), which disrupts the flow of reading.

The experimental section contains misleading claims. The paper states "PDMM can run in parallel but GSADMM only runs sequentially," which is factually incorrect—GSADMM can be parallelized. The paper also fails to provide sufficient details about the implementation of the algorithms to allow for reproducibility.

## Originality
The paper claims PDMM is a "parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM," but the connection to existing methods like RBSUMM [14] is unclear. The paper states RBSUMM "can only randomly update one block," but fails to explain why their approach (which can update multiple blocks) is fundamentally different from the existing literature.

The paper's claim that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" is questionable, as the authors themselves propose a solution to this problem in Section 2.3 without properly citing the relevant literature.

## Significance
While the problem addressed is significant, the paper's contribution is overshadowed by the technical flaws and lack of clarity. The paper claims to be "faster than existing methods" but fails to provide sufficient runtime comparisons that would support this claim. The experimental results in Figure 1 and Figure 2 are not sufficiently detailed to assess the claimed speedup.

The paper also misrepresents the relationship between PDMM and existing methods. For example, the paper states "sADMM should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3," but then claims that "sADMM is the slowest algorithm because τi = 0" (which contradicts the claim that τi = 1/3).

## Specific Concerns
1. In the abstract: "block-seperable" (typo)
2. In equation (1): "Ax = J∑ j=1 Acjxj = a" should be "Ax = ∑_{j=1}^J A_{cj}x_j = a"
3. In equation (3): "x t k 6=j" should be "x_k^t for k ≠ j"
4. In equation (8): "τi = K K̃i(2J−K)" should be "τ_i = K / (K̃_i(2J-K))"
5. Theorem 1 contains a mathematically incorrect inequality direction
6. The paper claims "PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence
7. The reference list uses "NIPS" instead of "NeurIPS" (outdated)
8. The paper states "PDMM can also be used to solve overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion" but fails to explain how this differs from existing methods

## Recommendation
The paper addresses an interesting problem, but the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and flawed theoretical claims significantly undermine its quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these fundamental issues with the paper's presentation and technical content. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.

The minor flaws identified here are not just superficial; they indicate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS paper. The paper's claims about convergence rates and relationships to existing methods cannot be verified due to the technical inconsistencies. While the idea of a parallel randomized block coordinate method for ADMM is potentially valuable, the execution in this paper is not up to the standard expected for NIPS.
