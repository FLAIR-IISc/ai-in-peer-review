

# Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

## Summary

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the challenge of extending ADMM to multiple blocks by proposing a parallel randomized block coordinate variant that overcomes convergence issues in direct Jacobi updates. PDMM introduces three key innovations: (1) a dual backward step that makes conservative progress on the dual variable, (2) step size parameters that account for the sparsity of the constraint matrix A, and (3) the ability to randomly update any number of primal blocks in parallel. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, showing that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The authors demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, showing significant speedups over existing methods.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation of the paper is strong. The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs under reasonable assumptions (Assumption 1), establishing global convergence (Theorem 1) and O(1/T) iteration complexity (Theorem 2). The analysis is comprehensive, with Lemma 1 providing a clear understanding of the distance function that decreases monotonically. The experimental results are well-designed and support the theoretical claims, showing PDMM's superiority over sADMM, RBSUMM, and GSADMM on both robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems. The authors carefully address the relationship between PDMM and existing methods, showing how sADMM and PJADMM are special cases.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by generalizing existing ADMM variants to a unified framework that addresses a key limitation in multiple-block ADMM. The dual backward step is particularly novel and well-justified through detailed analysis of the implicit dual ascent problem in direct Jacobi updates. The paper also solves an open problem regarding randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks, which was previously unsolved according to the authors.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear from the start, with concrete examples (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) that demonstrate the practical relevance of the problem. The technical explanations of PDMM's key innovations (dual backward step, sparsity consideration, randomized blocks) are particularly clear. The experimental section is well-structured with appropriate comparisons and figures that effectively communicate the results.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (efficient distributed optimization for large-scale convex problems) is highly relevant to machine learning and optimization. PDMM's ability to handle overlapping structures with randomized block updates opens new possibilities for large-scale machine learning applications. The runtime improvements demonstrated in the experiments (e.g., PDMM1 being the fastest algorithm in RPCA) have clear practical significance for practitioners. The paper advances the state of the art in distributed optimization methods, particularly for problems with overlapping structures.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Scope:** While the paper establishes convergence for the general case, the analysis assumes the existence of a KKT point (Assumption 1), which might not hold for all convex problems. The authors might consider discussing how their method would behave when a KKT point doesn't exist.

**Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). While these are strong examples, additional experiments on other problem types (e.g., distributed optimization with different constraint structures) would further demonstrate the generality of PDMM.

**Practical Implementation Details:** While the paper mentions PDMM can run in parallel, the experimental results are reported as sequential runtimes. A brief discussion of how PDMM would scale to distributed systems with multiple processors would strengthen the practical significance.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and clearly addresses the relationship to prior work, including showing how sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria:
- **Quality:** High (sound theory, thorough experiments)
- **Clarity:** High (well-organized, clear explanations)
- **Originality:** High (novel method with key insights)
- **Significance:** High (addresses important practical problem with demonstrable advantages)

The paper advances the state of the art in distributed optimization, particularly for problems with overlapping structures where existing methods have limitations. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides deeper theoretical understanding of the dual backward step, which is a valuable contribution beyond just the algorithm itself.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is a solid contribution that would be of interest to the NIPS community working on optimization methods for machine learning.

## Final Assessment

The paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically effective algorithm for block-separable convex optimization with linear constraints. The key innovation of the dual backward step to address convergence issues in parallel ADMM variants is particularly insightful. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, and the theoretical analysis establishes strong convergence guarantees. This is a high-quality contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.
