Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives under linear constraints. It proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that updates K arbitrary primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks, with two key modifications: (i) a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term by using an intermediate dual ŷt; and (ii) per-row dual step sizes τi and backward coefficients νi that depend on the row sparsity di of A and the number of updated blocks K. The method also allows inexact primal updates via Bregman divergences, unifying exact and proximal variants. The authors show that two-block splitting ADMM (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) emerge as extreme cases of PDMM (small τ/large ν vs. τ=1, ν=0 with large proximal terms), and establish global convergence with O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggest better wall-clock than sADMM/ADMM and faster convergence than RBSUMM; PDMM with larger K is faster up to a point.

The work sits within a well-studied line: two-block ADMM [Boyd et al.], multi-block ADMM (GS/Jacobi variants), BSUMM and RBSUMM [Hong et al.], and proximal Jacobian ADMM [Deng et al.]. It is also related to randomized coordinate descent [Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč] and primal–dual methods with relaxation/extrapolation (e.g., PDHG/Chambolle–Pock), where step sizes and over-relaxation are tuned using operator norms or problem structure. The paper’s contribution is a particular randomized Jacobi ADMM with a principled choice of dual step/backward parameters that exploits the block sparsity of A, together with convergence analysis and connections to known variants.

Strengths:
- Identifies and formalizes the “implicit dual ascent” issue in naive Jacobi ADMM and offers a simple, intuitive backward step to control it.
- Clear structural insight: step sizes τi, νi adapt to A’s sparsity (di) and K, which is practically meaningful and absent in many Jacobi multi-block ADMM papers.
- Unifying view: shows sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM, clarifying when conservative vs. aggressive dual steps are appropriate.
- Convergence guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes for randomized multi-block Jacobi updates; assumptions match standard ADMM settings.
- Practical relevance: covers nonsmooth terms via Bregman linearization; handles overlapping group structures elegantly and provides concrete τ, ν in that setting (di=2 gives τ=ν=0.5 for K=J).
- Empirical results on two representative problems support the claims, including informative ablations over K and τ/ν.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity/presentation: The exposition has numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., Acj vs. Ajc; “addded,” “convegence”), and some key objects (Pt, Q, βi) are introduced with minimal intuition or definition in the main text (definitions are fragmented or deferred to the supplement). This reduces accessibility of the analysis.
- Novelty: While the backward step and sparsity-aware τ/ν are nice, the methodological novelty over existing proximal/Jacobi ADMM (PJADMM) and randomized BSUMM seems incremental. The idea of tempering dual steps relates closely to relaxation/extrapolation in ADMM/PDHG; the paper could better distinguish PDMM’s theory and parameter schedules from those lines.
- Theory scope: Only ergodic O(1/T) is shown; no non-ergodic rates, no linear rates under stronger assumptions (strong convexity and full-row rank), and no adaptive schemes for τ/ν/ρ. The dependence of constants on K, J, di is not discussed in terms of practical guidance for optimizing K beyond empirical plots.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - The implementation is sequential for a parallel method; no multi-threaded or distributed experiments or scaling vs. cores, nor communication/synchronization cost analysis for Ax aggregation.
  - Some baselines are weak or not fairly tuned: RBSUMM reportedly fails to converge under tried schedules; sADMM code lacks a dual update and is acknowledged to be suboptimal; GSADMM is “no theory” but shown faster per-iteration in some cases. A stronger, more consistent tuning and reporting of per-iteration costs would help.
  - Datasets are synthetic, with limited exploration of conditioning, noise, and different sparsity patterns in A, which are central to PDMM’s design.
- Assumptions about randomized sampling and grouping are mentioned informally; it is unclear whether the convergence proofs cover grouped (non-i.i.d.) sampling or only uniform K-of-J selection.

Quality:
- Claims are mostly supported by analysis and experiments, but some definitions and proof dependencies are opaque in the main text. The empirical section, while suggestive, does not fully validate parallel performance or robustness across problem regimes.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable but needs substantial editing for grammar, notation consistency, and clearer definitions (particularly for matrices Pt, Q, constants βi, γi, ζi, and the role of h and L̃ρ). A short “how to choose K, τ, ν” guideline would aid practitioners.

Originality:
- Moderate. The integration of a backward dual step with sparsity-aware parameters in a randomized Jacobi ADMM is a useful twist, and the unification of sADMM/PJADMM is insightful, but the components build on known ideas.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for practitioners solving large, sparse, block-structured problems, especially with overlapping groups. However, the lack of real parallel experiments and stronger baselines limits the demonstrated impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical foundation with clear structural insights (sparsity-aware step sizes).
- Unifying perspective connecting sADMM and PJADMM; addresses a known instability of Jacobi multi-block ADMM.
- Practical algorithm that appears competitive on representative problems.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to existing Jacobi/proximal ADMM and primal–dual methods.
- Presentation issues and incomplete clarity of key definitions.
- Empirical validation lacks true parallel experiments and stronger baselines/tuning.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and centralize definitions of Pt, Q, βi, γi, ζi, and provide intuition for these matrices/parameters; include a compact theorem-box with assumptions and explicit constants.
- Provide guidance on choosing K, τ, ν, ρ in practice; consider adaptive schemes or backtracking for ρ; discuss trade-offs analytically.
- Extend theory to non-ergodic rates and linear convergence under stronger conditions; discuss relations to over-relaxation and PDHG with precise contrasts.
- Add genuine parallel/distributed experiments (multi-core/GPU/cluster) with scaling plots; quantify communication vs compute; report per-iteration costs.
- Strengthen baselines (well-tuned RBSUMM, PDHG/Chambolle–Pock-style methods where applicable), and include real datasets.
- Substantially edit for grammar, typos, and notation consistency.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is interesting and practically motivated, and the analysis is promising, but the novelty is moderate and the experimental validation and clarity need strengthening. With improved presentation, stronger empirical evidence (including parallel scaling), and broader comparisons, this work could be suitable for a future venue. Confidence: medium.
