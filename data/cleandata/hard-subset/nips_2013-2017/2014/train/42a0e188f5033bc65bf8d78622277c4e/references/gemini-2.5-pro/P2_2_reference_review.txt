**Summary**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a general and important problem class with wide applications in machine learning. The proposed method is a parallel, randomized block-coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key contributions are threefold: 1) It introduces a "dual backward step" to temper the dual variable updates, addressing a known instability issue in naive parallel (Jacobian) ADMM. 2) The step sizes for the primal and dual updates are intelligently determined by the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel. 3) The paper provides a unifying perspective, showing that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with a constant step size. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, showing favorable performance against several state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is sound, following a standard Lyapunov-based argument to prove convergence and establish an O(1/T) rate, which is standard for this class of first-order methods. The assumptions are minimal and standard for ADMM-like algorithms. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against a strong suite of relevant baselines (GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on well-chosen, representative problems. The results clearly support the claims made in the paper.
*   **Weaknesses:** While the potential for parallelism is a key selling point, the experiments were conducted sequentially. The reported runtime gains stem from lower per-iteration costs (e.g., updating one block is cheaper than updating all blocks sequentially), not from actual parallel execution. Demonstrating wall-clock speedup on a multi-core system would have made the experimental case even more compelling.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation is presented very clearly in the introduction. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature, explaining the limitations of Gauss-Seidel and existing Jacobian ADMM variants. The intuition behind the "dual backward step" as a way to counteract an "implicit dual ascent" is insightful and well-explained. The formulation for handling overlapping group lasso is elegant and serves as a strong motivating example.
*   **Weaknesses:** The presentation of the algorithm updates (Equations 5-7) could be slightly clearer. The use of `ŷ^t` in the `x^{t+1}` update (Eq 5) can be a bit confusing. An explicit algorithm box summarizing the sequence of operations within a single iteration `t` (e.g., 1. Sample `J_t`, 2. Compute `ŷ^t` from `y^t`, 3. Update `x_j` for `j` in `J_t`, 4. Update `y^{t+1}`) would enhance readability and reproducibility. The section on Inexact PDMM is also quite brief; a more concrete example would be beneficial for practitioners.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** While building on the rich literature of ADMM, the core ideas in PDMM are novel. The combination of a randomized block selection strategy, a dual backward step, and step sizes that adapt to both problem structure (sparsity) and algorithmic choice (number of parallel blocks `K`) is a significant new contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as two extremes of the PDMM framework is a particularly strong conceptual contribution that deepens the understanding of this family of algorithms. The application to overlapping group lasso in a randomized block-coordinate fashion addresses what the authors claim is an open problem for standard RBCD methods.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential for impact.
*   **Strengths:** The problem of block-separable optimization is fundamental to modern large-scale machine learning. By providing a flexible, parallelizable, and theoretically-grounded algorithm, this work is likely to be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners. The ability to adapt to sparsity is crucial for many real-world problems. The fact that PDMM offers a spectrum of trade-offs—from updating a single block (like RBSUMM) to all blocks (like sADMM/PJADMM)—makes it a very practical and versatile tool. The insights provided by unifying previous methods are also significant for future algorithm design in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** PDMM introduces a novel and intuitive "dual backward step" to solve a known convergence problem in parallel ADMM, with step sizes that cleverly incorporate problem structure.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis with standard assumptions, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) rate.
3.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The algorithm is shown to be highly effective in practice, outperforming or being competitive with several state-of-the-art methods on relevant large-scale problems.
4.  **Important Conceptual Contribution:** The unification of sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable insight into the design of parallel optimization algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The experiments are run sequentially, so the claimed benefits of parallelism are not directly demonstrated in terms of wall-clock time reduction.
2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The presentation of the core algorithm loop could be improved for easier implementation and understanding.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. It addresses a key challenge in parallelizing the method of multipliers and provides a flexible framework that unifies and extends previous work. The weaknesses are minor and mostly related to presentation and experimental setup, and they do not detract from the core value of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
