Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel, Jacobian-style variant of ADMM. The key idea is to mitigate the well-known divergence of naive multi-block Jacobi ADMM by introducing a dual “backward step”: instead of using y in the primal subproblems, PDMM uses a shrunk ŷ = y − νρ(Ax − a) to counteract the implicit dual ascent already induced by the quadratic penalty in the augmented Lagrangian. The method updates any number K of randomly chosen primal blocks in parallel, and sets blockwise dual stepsizes τi and backward factors νi as functions of (i) the sparsity pattern of A (via the degree di of each dual block) and (ii) the number of updated blocks K. An inexact variant with Bregman divergences unifies exact and proximal/primal-linearized updates. The authors show that two known algorithms are extreme cases of PDMM: sADMM (two-block with splitting, small dual step) and PJADMM (Jacobian ADMM with large proximal terms, full dual step). Under standard convexity assumptions, PDMM enjoys global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant stepsizes. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM against GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, and include ablations over K and the τ, ν parameters.

Quality:
- Theory: The convergence statements (global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate) are standard and plausible for this class; the derivation of the degree- and K-dependent stepsizes is an interesting contribution. The intuition about “implicit dual ascent” is insightful and ties directly to the design of the backward step. Some technical ingredients (matrices Pt and Q, the exact derivation leading to (8)) are only sketched and deferred to the supplement; within the main text, these appear abruptly and could be better motivated. Assumptions are minimal (closed, proper, convex; KKT exists).
- Correctness: I did not verify the full proofs, but the structure matches the literature on randomized block-coordinate and ADMM-type methods. A few notational inconsistencies (e.g., βi definition) and typos reduce confidence in meticulousness, but likely do not affect the main results.
- Experiments: The empirical section demonstrates the method on two representative problems, includes ablations (effect of K, of τ/ν), and contrasts with related baselines. However, all runs are sequential Matlab; the lack of a genuinely parallel implementation undercuts the practical message of “parallel PDMM.” The sADMM baseline appears handicapped (code “does not have dual update,” τi=0) although the text claims PDMM and sADMM should match under certain τ, ν; a fair re-implementation would be preferable. Tuning details for baselines (especially RBSUMM, PJADMM) are limited, and statistical variability is not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and provides useful intuition (implicit vs explicit dual ascent, role of sparsity). Nonetheless, it is notation-heavy, with several typos (seperable/defined/convergence), incomplete references (years/pages missing), and some abrupt definitions (Pt, Q, βi) that make parts of Section 3 hard to follow without the supplement. The connections to sADMM and PJADMM are valuable, but the main text pushes the derivations to the appendix—adding a brief proof sketch would aid comprehension. The overlapping-group reformulation is clearly presented and illustrative.

Originality:
- Positive: The degree- and K-aware design of τi and νi and the explicit “dual backward step” provide a principled fix for multi-block Jacobi ADMM instability, beyond “add proximal terms.” Allowing randomized updates of multiple blocks (not just one) with constant steps and proving O(1/T) is a nontrivial extension over RBSUMM. Unifying sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM clarifies the landscape.
- Limits: The general convergence rate matches existing o(1/T)/O(1/T) results; the novelty is primarily in the stepsize/backward-step design and the unification. Still, this addresses a known pain point for multi-block ADMM.

Significance:
The work advances our understanding and practice of parallel ADMM for multi-block problems: it offers a simple mechanism (backward step) with theoretically justified, structure-aware stepsizes, and supports inexact updates common in applications. Given the breadth of problems in (1), this is likely to be adopted and built upon. The lack of real parallel experiments and a few fairness issues in comparisons temper the practical impact in the current form, but the theoretical contribution is meaningful.

Pros:
- Clear motivation for instability of Jacobi ADMM; principled remedy via dual backward step.
- Stepsizes adapt to sparsity (di) and number of updated blocks (K).
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM; accommodates inexact/Bregman updates.
- Convergence and O(1/T) rate under standard assumptions with constant steps.
- Empirical ablations (K, τ/ν) and applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

Cons:
- Clarity/notation issues; some key definitions and derivations deferred or opaque.
- Experimental evaluation lacks actual parallel implementation; limited scale and scope.
- Potentially unfair or suboptimal baselines (sADMM without proper dual update; limited tuning for others).
- No comparison to other modern primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock) or asynchronous variants.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide an intuitive derivation of (8): how do τi and νi emerge from the analysis, and what are the tightness/limits as functions of di and K?
- Define Pt, Q, βi clearly in the main text and explain their roles in the Lyapunov function and residual.
- Clarify how to choose ηjt and the Bregman generators in practice; offer default rules.
- Re-implement sADMM with the correct dual update and include stronger PJADMM baselines with tuned proximal weights; add standard deviations.
- Include at least one genuinely parallel/distributed experiment to substantiate scalability claims.
- Discuss robustness to asynchrony and communication costs implied by the degree-aware design.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper offers a well-motivated and theoretically sound contribution to multi-block ADMM with a novel, structure-aware stepsize/backward-step design and a unifying view of related methods. With improved clarity and stronger empirical validation, it would be a solid NeurIPS paper.
