

# Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

This paper proposes a new algorithm called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors position PDMM as a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM that can update arbitrary numbers of blocks in parallel, with a dual backward step to ensure convergence. The paper establishes connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), proves global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity, and evaluates the method on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a theoretically sound analysis of the proposed method, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity with constant step size. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases is well-explained and theoretically interesting. The paper's treatment of the dual backward step and its relation to the sparsity of the constraint matrix is a valuable theoretical contribution. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate reasonable performance improvements over some baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the theoretical claims, the paper falls short in several critical areas. The empirical validation is insufficiently comprehensive - the authors only evaluate on two specific applications, which is inadequate for a method claiming broad applicability. The comparison with state-of-the-art baselines is limited, particularly with respect to established parallel ADMM variants. For instance, the paper states that "PDMMs perform better than RBSUMM and sADMM," but provides no statistical significance testing for these claims, and the comparison with the most relevant method (standard parallel ADMM) is not sufficiently detailed.

The paper makes a strong theoretical claim about the novelty of its approach to overlapping group lasso, stating "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem." However, this claim is unsubstantiated by a thorough literature review, and the paper does not clearly explain why the proposed method fundamentally solves this "open problem" better than existing approaches.

Most critically, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that PDMM represents a substantial improvement over established methods. The experimental results show marginal improvements in some cases (e.g., PDMM1 being faster than RBSUMM), but the paper does not demonstrate consistent superiority across multiple datasets, problem sizes, or applications. The paper claims "PDMM is faster than existing methods," but the experimental results show that PDMM3 is faster than GSADMM in terms of runtime, while GSADMM converges with fewer iterations - a trade-off that the paper does not adequately justify.

The paper also lacks a proper discussion of the method's limitations. For instance, it doesn't address when PDMM might fail to converge, how sensitive it is to the choice of step size parameters, or how it compares to other established parallel optimization methods in terms of scalability to very large problems.

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper's novelty appears incremental rather than transformative. The core idea of parallel block coordinate updates in ADMM is not entirely new, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the specific combination of features (dual backward step, sparsity consideration, arbitrary block selection) provides a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. The paper's claim that "PDMM can also be used to solve overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion" doesn't sufficiently distinguish it from established methods for overlapping groups.

The significance of the work is limited by the narrow scope of the experimental validation. For a method claiming to be a general-purpose optimization technique, the paper should demonstrate performance on a wider range of problems and datasets to establish broader relevance.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting variant of ADMM with some novel insights about the dual backward step, it falls short of providing sufficient evidence to justify its novelty and practical significance. The theoretical contribution is marginal compared to the substantial body of work on ADMM variants, and the empirical validation is insufficiently comprehensive to support the claims of superiority over established methods.

The paper would benefit from more extensive experimental comparisons with a wider range of baselines and problem instances, a more thorough discussion of limitations, and a clearer justification for why PDMM represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods. Without this, the paper does not provide compelling evidence that PDMM is a substantial contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the incremental nature of the contribution does not meet the high bar for acceptance at NIPS.
