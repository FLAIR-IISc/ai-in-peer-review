**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to enable parallel updates of a randomly selected subset of primal variable blocks, a task known to be challenging for standard ADMM-type methods. The key contributions are: (1) A "dual backward step" that stabilizes the algorithm by making the dual update more conservative. (2) A step-size selection rule for the dual updates (`τi`, `νi`) that intelligently incorporates the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel (`K`). (3) A unified framework that shows two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. The method's performance is demonstrated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of using a dual backward step to facilitate stable, parallel, randomized updates is a novel and significant contribution to the ADMM literature. The problem of efficiently parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems is important, and this work presents a promising direction.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including proofs for global convergence and an O(1/T) rate. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is particularly insightful, providing a clearer understanding of the role of exact/inexact primal updates versus conservative/aggressive dual updates.

3.  **Algorithmic Flexibility:** The proposed method is flexible, allowing for any number of blocks (`K`) to be updated in parallel, from a single block (`K=1`) to all blocks (`K=J`). The consideration of matrix sparsity in the step-size design is a sophisticated and practical feature.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, experimental validation, and technical presentation that, in my view, prevent it from meeting the high standards of NIPS.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's clarity is severely hampered by several issues:
    *   **Algorithm Definition:** The description of the core algorithm (Equations 5-7) is critically ambiguous. Equation (5) uses an intermediate dual variable `ŷt`, but the algorithm only defines how to compute `ŷt+1` from `yt+1`. The text mentions `ŷt = yt − νρ(Axt − a)`, but this is not formally part of the algorithm block. This temporal inconsistency makes it extremely difficult to understand, let alone reproduce, the exact update sequence. This is a major flaw that undermines the entire methodological contribution.
    *   **Reliance on External Sources:** The proofs connecting PDMM to sADMM and PJADMM, which are touted as key contributions, are relegated to Appendices B and C of an external ArXiv preprint `[25]`. For a submission to be self-contained, all essential details for verification must be included in the paper or its direct supplement, not in an external, unreviewed manuscript.
    *   **Notational and Formatting Issues:** The paper is littered with minor but distracting errors. For instance: "block-seperable", "Lagragian", "convegence", "precesion", "to be hold". The notation section is placed awkwardly at the end of the introduction. Citations are inconsistent, with a mix of journal names and "ArXiv" or "Preprint" placeholders. The footnote on page 4 is just a raw URL.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results, while appearing positive, are founded on a critically flawed methodology.
    *   **Lack of Parallelism:** The primary motivation for PDMM is its ability to run in parallel. However, the authors explicitly state, "All experiments are implemented in Matlab and run sequentially." This is a fatal flaw. Comparing sequential runtimes of a parallelizable algorithm against inherently sequential ones (like GSADMM) is misleading. The paper fails to provide any evidence of the actual performance gains that parallelism—its main selling point—would confer.
    *   **Flawed Baselines:** The comparison with sADMM is invalid. The authors acknowledge that their implementation of sADMM is incorrect ("does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0") and that a correct implementation would be identical to their PDMM3. Presenting results from a knowingly flawed baseline to make the proposed method look better is poor scientific practice.
    *   **Inconsistent Tuning:** The paper first compares "untuned" PDMM against the "best" results for baselines, which is not a fair comparison. It then presents a separate section with tuned PDMM results, making it difficult to draw clear conclusions from the initial figures.

3.  **Technical Soundness:**
    *   **Misleading Remark:** Remark 2 claims the fastest convergence is achieved when `J=K=1`. This reduces PDMM to the standard method of multipliers. While the iteration complexity *rate* might be better, the per-iteration *cost* becomes intractable, which is precisely the reason for using decomposition methods like ADMM. This statement is technically correct in a narrow sense but highly misleading in the context of the paper's goals.
    *   **Vague Sampling Scheme:** The description of the block randomization ("permutate the J blocks and choose K blocks cyclically") is imprecise. The theoretical analysis likely relies on a specific probability distribution (e.g., uniform sampling of K-subsets), which must be stated explicitly to ensure the theory correctly applies to the algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of a sparsity-aware, randomized parallel ADMM with a dual backward step is novel and technically interesting.
*   The paper provides a theoretical unification of related methods (sADMM, PJADMM), which is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The algorithm's description is critically ambiguous, making it difficult to understand and reproduce.
*   The experimental evaluation is fundamentally flawed: it does not test the algorithm in a parallel setting and uses an admittedly incorrect implementation for a key baseline.
*   Key theoretical claims rely on an external ArXiv paper, violating the principle of a self-contained submission.
*   The paper contains numerous minor errors in formatting, grammar, and notation that collectively indicate a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision:**
While the paper presents a novel and potentially significant idea, the execution is deeply flawed. The ambiguity in the algorithm's definition, the invalid experimental methodology, and the reliance on external materials for key proofs constitute major weaknesses. The sheer number of minor issues further erodes confidence in the work. An interesting idea alone is not sufficient; it must be presented clearly, validated rigorously, and documented thoroughly. This paper fails on all three of these fronts. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to thoroughly revise the paper to address these critical issues for a future submission.
