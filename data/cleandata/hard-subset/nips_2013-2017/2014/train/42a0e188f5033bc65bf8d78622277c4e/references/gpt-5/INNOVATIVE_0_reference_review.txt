Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of the method of multipliers for convex problems of the form min sum_j f_j(x_j) subject to Ax=a. The key idea is to keep the augmented Lagrangian (ρ>0) for better conditioning, yet update primal blocks in parallel in a Jacobi fashion—something known to be unstable for multi-block ADMM—by introducing a “dual backward step.” Concretely: (i) the primal update uses ŷt rather than yt, where ŷt is a conservative dual iterate obtained by stepping “backward” along the residual; (ii) the dual update step sizes τ_i (and backward coefficients ν_i) depend explicitly on the row sparsity degrees d_i of A and the number K of randomly selected blocks updated at each iteration; and (iii) the method allows inexact primal updates via Bregman divergences, unifying exact and proximal/linearized variants.

The paper relates PDMM to two strands of multi-block ADMM literature: Gauss–Seidel multi-block ADMM (GSADMM/BSUMM) with limited theory, and Jacobian ADMM variants (sADMM with splitting variables and PJADMM with proximal terms). It shows that sADMM and PJADMM arise as extreme cases of PDMM through particular (τ,ν,η) choices, thereby clarifying when dual steps should be conservative (exact, larger primal progress) or aggressive (proximal, smaller primal progress).

The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes under standard convexity assumptions, without local error bounds or decreasing step sizes (required by RBSUMM). They also show how PDMM naturally exploits sparsity (d_i) to use larger dual steps, and how it yields a clean formulation for overlapping group regularization by introducing copies and equality constraints. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show favorable iteration/time performance versus ADMM/GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM (the latter often sensitive to tuning).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: The dual backward step to counteract implicit dual ascent from the quadratic penalty is insightful and addresses a core reason Jacobi multi-block ADMM can diverge. This, together with per-row sparsity-aware step sizes and arbitrary-K randomization, is a fresh and principled design.
- Unifying perspective: Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM provides a clear conceptual bridge and helps practitioners reason about step sizes and proximalization.
- Theory: Global convergence with O(1/T) rate under simple assumptions and constant steps; no local error bound conditions or diminishing steps. The role of K and d_i is explicit in the analysis.
- Practical relevance: The ability to update any number of blocks in parallel and to use Bregman linearization for inexact updates targets large-scale, nonsmooth problems common in ML. The overlapping groups example is compelling and highlights where sparsity-aware steps can be much larger than in sADMM.
- Empirical indications: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive or faster than strong baselines in runtime with fewer tuning knobs.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental scope: Experiments are sequential Matlab runs on two synthetic/standard tasks. Given the “parallel” claim, evaluations on multicore/distributed platforms would better substantiate scalability and communication/synchronization costs. The gains from larger K versus wall-clock parallel speedup are not demonstrated empirically.
- Baselines and fairness: sADMM implementation used reportedly lacks a dual update; this muddies fair comparison. Strong modern baselines such as linearized ADMM (L-ADMM/LADMPS), PDHG/Chambolle–Pock-type methods, and accelerated primal–dual schemes are not included. RBSUMM tuning appears challenging; nevertheless, more systematic tuning/reporting would strengthen the case.
- Clarity/presentation: While the main ideas are clear, the paper would benefit from an algorithm box, a concise summary of parameter defaults (ρ, τ_i, ν_i, η_j) and how to compute d_i in practice, and a more intuitive explanation of the z-variable machinery used in the proofs. There are several typos and minor grammatical issues (e.g., “seperable,” “convegence”).
- Theory tightness: The rate is O(1/T) in expectation and ergodic, matching standard ADMM theory; no linear rates under stronger assumptions are explored. The τ_i, ν_i choices are sufficient for convergence but may be conservative; empirical or adaptive rules could improve performance.
- Asynchrony: The method still requires synchronization to form Ax for dual updates; discussion of asynchronous variants or partial dual updates (mentioned as “extended work”) is deferred.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide clear pseudocode for PDMM, including exact and inexact variants, and a table summarizing τ_i, ν_i for typical settings (K=1, K=J, di small/large).
- Can τ_i, ν_i be adapted online based on observed residuals, perhaps leading to faster practical convergence while preserving guarantees?
- Report distributed/multicore experiments to demonstrate real parallel speedups and communication costs. An ablation showing how performance scales with K and with the sparsity pattern (varying d_i) on real datasets would be valuable.
- Include comparisons with PDHG/Chambolle–Pock, linearized ADMM, and accelerated primal–dual methods.
- Clarify the fairness of sADMM comparisons; if the public code omits dual updates, either modify it or report sADMM results with correct updates.
- Minor: fix typos; define all symbols on first use; improve Section 2.3’s notation and statement of τ_i, ν_i for the overlapping-groups construction.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a novel, principled mechanism (dual backward step with sparsity-aware step sizes) that enables stable parallel multi-block updates.
- Unifies and clarifies relationships between sADMM and PJADMM through a single framework.
- Solid theory with constant step sizes and explicit dependence on sparsity/parallelism.
- Applicable to important ML problems (nonsmooth, overlapping structures).

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited and sequential experiments; lack of parallel/distributed validation.
- Missing comparisons to some strong modern primal–dual baselines; one baseline implementation issue (sADMM) raises fairness concerns.
- Presentation can be tightened; algorithm box and parameter guidance would help reproducibility.
- No exploration of stronger rates or adaptivity.

Recommendation:
Leaning accept. The paper advances our understanding of multi-block ADMM by a simple but powerful idea (dual backward step) and provides a unifying and practically relevant framework with clean guarantees. While the empirical section is not as comprehensive as desired and the presentation needs polish, the conceptual novelty and potential impact on large-scale structured optimization are strong.
