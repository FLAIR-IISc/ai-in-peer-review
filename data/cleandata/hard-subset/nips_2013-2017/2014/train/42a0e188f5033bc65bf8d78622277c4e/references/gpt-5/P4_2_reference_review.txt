Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that updates any number K of primal blocks per iteration while updating all dual blocks. The key ideas are:
- A “dual backward step” on the dual variable (parameter νi) to counteract the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term and avoid divergence in fully parallel Jacobi-style updates.
- Dual step sizes (τi) that depend explicitly on the sparsity pattern of A through di (the number of nonzero blocks in row i) and on the number K of blocks updated, enabling larger steps for sparser problems and larger K.
- An inexact variant via Bregman divergence terms to linearize difficult subproblems, unifying exact and inexact updates.

The paper shows that two prominent multi-block ADMM variants are extreme cases of PDMM: the two-block ADMM with variable splitting (sADMM) when τi = 1/J and νi = 1 − 1/J, and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) when sufficiently large proximal terms allow τi = 1, νi = 0. PDMM achieves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convex assumptions with constant step sizes, with rates established in expectation over the block sampling. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso are presented.

The work relates to extensive literature on ADMM and multi-block extensions [2,12,13,14,15,17], randomized coordinate descent [16,18], and proximal/Jacobian variants [10]. It is consistent with reports that naive Jacobi ADMM may diverge for multiple blocks [7] and with prior randomized multi-block ADMM (RBSUMM) [14], but improves by allowing multiple blocks per iteration, using constant step sizes, exploiting sparsity in step-size selection, and providing an O(1/T) complexity bound.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: PDMM introduces a principled dual backward step and step-size selection (τi, νi) derived from K and di, providing a concrete fix to divergence issues in parallel multi-block ADMM.
- Theoretical guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard assumptions, with explicit dependence on sparsity and block sampling. The analysis framework cleanly separates exact and inexact updates via Bregman divergences and recovers known methods as special cases.
- Connection and unification: Identifying sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM clarifies when small vs large dual steps are appropriate, depending on the aggressiveness of primal updates.
- Applicability to overlapping groups: Casting overlapping group lasso into the PDMM framework with di = 2 demonstrates that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks where standard randomized BCD theory is less developed.
- Empirical evidence: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants compare favorably to sADMM and RBSUMM; the dependence on K is explored, and the benefits of larger τi enabled by sparsity are illustrated.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental evaluation lacks true parallel implementations. All methods are run sequentially in Matlab, so the main empirical claim—parallel scalability—is not demonstrated. Including parallel wall-clock results on multicore or distributed settings would strengthen the evidence.
- Fairness and clarity of baselines: The sADMM code used apparently omits the dual update (τi = 0). Since the paper argues sADMM corresponds to specific τi, νi, the comparison should use a corrected implementation (or clearly justify why not) to avoid bias. RBSUMM tuning details are limited; broader hyperparameter sweeps and reporting best-case performance would be preferable.
- Presentation clarity: There are several typos and minor grammatical issues; adding a concise pseudocode box for PDMM with the definitions of τi, νi, K̃i, di, and options for Bregman choices would improve readability. The roles and definitions of matrices Pt and Q in the residual (19) and distance function (20) are only sketched; more explicit definitions in the main text would help readers assess the proof structure without consulting the supplement.
- Scope of experiments: Only two problem classes are considered, both convex and well-studied. Additional tasks (e.g., constrained lasso variants or distributed matrix factorization) and sensitivity to K, ρ, and η across datasets would improve robustness of conclusions.
- Theoretical rate is O(1/T), matching standard ADMM. While acceptable, highlighting conditions where PDMM achieves larger effective constants (e.g., via di) or proving faster rates under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity) would add value.

Quality
- The algorithm is well-defined, and the step-size rules are justified by the analysis. Convergence and ergodic rate results are standard in strength but adapted to the randomized multi-block parallel setting with sparsity-aware parameters. Proof details reside in the supplement; the main paper presents the key lemmas and theorems, though with some notation that could be clarified.

Clarity
- Overall organization is logical (algorithm, connections, theory, experiments). However, presentation could be improved by tightening notation, fixing typos, and adding an algorithm box. The explanation of the dual backward step and its relationship to implicit dual ascent is insightful and motivates the design well.

Originality
- The dual backward step and the specific sparsity- and K-aware step-size selection for randomized parallel multi-block ADMM appear novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within PDMM and the ability to update multiple random blocks per iteration extend prior randomized ADMM approaches.

Significance
- PDMM addresses a practical need for stable, parallelizable multi-block ADMM with theoretical guarantees, applicable to important ML problems, including overlapping-structured regularization. The results are likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners implementing parallel ADMM-like solvers.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple and practically useful modification enabling stable parallel multi-block updates.
- Solid theory with convergence and O(1/T) rate, constant step sizes, and sparsity-aware parameters.
- Unifies existing methods and clarifies their regimes.
- Demonstrated applicability to overlapping groups.

Cons for acceptance
- No actual parallel runtime results; sequential Matlab experiments limit practical evidence.
- Some baseline comparisons may be unfavorable due to implementation choices; broader, fairer benchmarking would help.
- Presentation/notation issues and reliance on supplement for key definitions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide parallel implementations and scalability experiments.
- Ensure fair baselines (sADMM with proper dual updates; thorough RBSUMM tuning).
- Add algorithm pseudocode and clarify Pt, Q, βi, K̃i in the main text.
- Expand experiments and sensitivity analyses; include strong convexity scenarios if applicable.
- Proofread to correct typos and improve exposition.

Overall, the paper presents a meaningful extension of ADMM with clear theoretical and practical contributions, pending stronger empirical validation of parallel performance and improved clarity.
