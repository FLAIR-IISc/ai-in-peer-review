

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors establish connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), showing they are special cases of PDMM. PDMM introduces a dual backward step to address convergence issues in direct Jacobi updates, incorporates sparsity of the constraint matrix A into step size selection, and allows random selection of any number of blocks to update in parallel. Theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and experiments demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by establishing convergence and complexity results for a novel randomized block coordinate ADMM variant. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides valuable insights into the role of dual backward steps, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The analysis is thorough and builds on established frameworks for ADMM variants.

**Practical Impact:** The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning optimization. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly significant, as the authors note that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem." PDMM provides a solution to this previously open problem.

**Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is well-designed, with clear comparisons to relevant baselines on two important machine learning problems. The results consistently demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness, with PDMM1 achieving the lowest residual in RPCA and PDMM outperforming other methods in overlapping group lasso. The analysis of how the number of blocks (K) affects performance provides useful practical guidance.

## Weaknesses

**Intuition for Dual Backward Step:** While the paper identifies that implicit dual ascent in the primal update causes convergence issues, the intuition behind the dual backward step (7) is not sufficiently explained. A concrete example illustrating why the backward step resolves the convergence problem would strengthen the presentation.

**Practical Guidance on Block Selection:** The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to choose K (the number of blocks to update) in practice. While Figure 2 shows the effect of K on runtime, it would be helpful to have a more systematic discussion of how to select K based on problem structure.

**Comparison of Parallelism Benefits:** The paper mentions PDMM's parallelism advantage but runs experiments sequentially. A comparison of sequential vs. parallel runtime would better demonstrate the practical benefits of the parallel updates.

**Context for Overlapping Groups:** The paper claims RBCD struggles with overlapping blocks, but doesn't provide sufficient context on why this is difficult for RBCD but not for PDMM. A brief explanation would help readers understand this key contribution.

## Constructive Suggestions

1. Add a concrete example or simple illustration showing why the dual backward step resolves convergence issues in the direct Jacobi updates.

2. Expand Section 2.3 to provide more intuition about why PDMM can handle overlapping blocks while RBCD cannot. This would strengthen the significance claim.

3. Include a brief discussion of how to choose K in practice, perhaps based on the sparsity of A and the computational cost of solving subproblems.

4. Add a parallel runtime comparison to better demonstrate the practical advantages of the parallel updates.

5. Clarify the relationship between the choice of K and the theoretical convergence rate. The paper mentions that "the number of blocks in PDMM depends on the trade-off between the number of subproblems and how efficiently each subproblem can be solved," but doesn't elaborate on this trade-off.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is strong, and the connection between existing methods through PDMM provides valuable theoretical insights. The paper addresses an important practical problem (overlapping group lasso) where previous methods were lacking.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in clarity and practical guidance, these can be addressed with relatively minor revisions. The quality of the theoretical analysis and the significance of the application to overlapping groups make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed with relatively straightforward revisions. The work is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in distributed optimization for machine learning.
