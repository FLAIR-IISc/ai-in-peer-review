This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM, for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors aim to address the limitations of existing parallel ADMM methods, particularly concerning convergence and efficiency for large-scale problems. The paper introduces a backward step on the dual update and leverages sparsity to optimize step size selection. Connections to existing sADMM and PJADMM are also established.

While the problem setting is relevant and the motivation for developing a more efficient parallel ADMM method is sound, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of minor, yet pervasive, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality. The presentation, while generally understandable, is frequently marred by inconsistencies, unnecessary complexity, and a lack of meticulous attention to detail.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The problem of solving large-scale, linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization is important in machine learning and related fields.
*   **Potential for Parallelism:** The proposed method explicitly targets parallel computation, a crucial aspect for tackling large datasets.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The attempt to establish a relationship between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a valuable insight, potentially providing a unified understanding of these algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides convergence guarantees, which are essential for establishing the reliability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notations:** The notation is frequently inconsistent and needlessly complex. For instance, the definition of *N(i)* and *di* is presented late in the introduction and their subsequent use feels awkward. The multiple definitions for *x* (block coordinate vs. entire variable) are confusing. The use of *ηjt* as both a parameter and an indicator for inexactness is problematic.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** While convergence is claimed, the presentation of the theoretical results feels rushed. Lemma 1 and Theorem 1 state conditions without sufficient justification or clear explanation of how they are derived from the algorithm’s mechanics. The role of *Pt* and *Q* in the convergence analysis is not adequately explained, and their implications are unclear.
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper is overly verbose in several sections. The discussion of the dual backward step, while conceptually important, is repeated excessively. The explanation of the sparsity argument feels needlessly convoluted.
*   **Experimental Setup:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The choice of parameters for the baseline methods (ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) is not clearly justified. The data generation process for the overlapping group lasso experiment is described, but the specific parameter values used are missing. The statement that the public sADMM code lacks dual updates is a concerning claim that requires proper citation or verification. The performance differences between PDMM variants (with different K values) aren't sufficiently analyzed, particularly the trade-off between per-iteration cost and number of iterations.
*   **Formatting and Style:** Numerous minor formatting issues exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of equation numbering, awkward phrasing, typos – notably the repeated 'RBCD' instead of 'RBCD'). The frequent use of "e.g." introduces a casual tone inappropriate for a rigorous technical paper.
* **Lack of Clarity on Bregman Divergence:** The discussion of Bregman divergence is presented without sufficient context. The choice of phi and psi functions in (12) lacks explanation and feels tacked on. The relationship between ηjt and the specific Bregman divergence chosen is not well-defined.
* **Missing Proofs:** The statement that “The technical analysis and detailed proofs are provided in the supplement” is not a sufficient justification for lacking detailed insights into the core theoretical results within the main body of the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method offers a potentially valuable contribution to the field of parallel optimization.
*   The theoretical analysis, despite its shortcomings in presentation, suggests convergence guarantees.
*   The connection to existing methods provides a broader understanding of the ADMM landscape.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous technical and presentational flaws significantly undermine the paper’s clarity and credibility.
*   The lack of a thorough experimental evaluation and detailed justification of parameter choices weakens the empirical support for the proposed method.
*   The rushed and often unclear theoretical exposition hinders a full understanding of the method’s properties and limitations.
*   The pervasive minor errors detract from the overall professionalism of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty of the proposed method, the paper’s numerous flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of meticulousness in presentation, the incomplete theoretical analysis, and the insufficiently detailed experimental evaluation raise serious concerns about the robustness and reliability of the proposed approach. While the ideas presented may be valuable, they are currently obscured by a multitude of minor, yet significant, issues. A major revision addressing these concerns is required before this paper could be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**
