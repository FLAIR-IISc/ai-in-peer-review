## Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors aim to extend the benefits of ADMM to multi-block settings, addressing challenges related to convergence and scalability. PDMM achieves parallelism by randomly selecting and updating blocks, incorporating a backward step on the dual update to promote stability, and adapting the step size based on problem structure (sparsity of A) and the number of updated blocks. The authors demonstrate connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, presenting them as extreme cases of PDMM, and provide theoretical convergence guarantees with an O(1/T) iteration complexity. The method is evaluated on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso, demonstrating competitive performance.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the existing literature on ADMM and its variants. It acknowledges the difficulties encountered when extending ADMM to multiple blocks, referencing works like GSADMM, BSUMM, sADMM, and PJADMM. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches, such as strict convergence conditions for BSUMM and the lack of a convergence guarantee for multi-block GSADMM. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is valuable, providing a unified framework for understanding these methods.  The comparison with RBCD is also relevant, especially given the open problem regarding overlapping blocks in RBCD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The PDMM algorithm offers a fresh perspective on parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems. The randomized block selection and backward dual step are innovative features.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence guarantees and iteration complexity. This is a significant strength, as many practical algorithms lack such theoretical support.
* **Flexibility:** The method is adaptable to various problem structures through the step size adjustment based on sparsity and block selection. The ability to handle overlapping blocks is also a plus.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  Demonstrating sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM offers valuable insights and clarifies the relationships between these methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso provide evidence of PDMM’s effectiveness and competitiveness.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides formulas for τi and νi, the optimal choice of *K* (number of randomly selected blocks) might require careful tuning and could be problem-dependent. The experimental results show performance variations with different *K* values.  More guidance on choosing *K* would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is limited to two applications.  While these are relevant examples, broadening the evaluation to include other problem domains (e.g., distributed optimization, machine learning with more complex constraints) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks sufficient details regarding the implementation, making it harder to reproduce the results. Specifically, details about the Bregman divergence used and the choice of the parameter ηjt would be helpful.
* **Comparison with Baselines:** While the paper compares to several ADMM variants, it does not benchmark against other parallel optimization algorithms like distributed gradient descent or other randomized coordinate descent methods (beyond RBCD).
* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1, requiring the existence of a KKT point, is fairly strong. It might be useful to discuss scenarios where this assumption doesn't hold and the implications for PDMM's convergence.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
* Novel approach to parallelizing ADMM that addresses limitations of existing methods.
* Clear presentation and insightful connections to related work.
* Promising empirical results on relevant applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of experiments and lack of detailed implementation information.
* Parameter tuning (specifically *K*) could be challenging.
*  The existence of a KKT point might be a limiting assumption.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The PDMM algorithm is a well-motivated and theoretically sound approach to solving a class of important problems. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation and implementation details could be improved, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance with the following minor revisions:

1.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Include additional experiments on a broader range of problems to demonstrate the generality of PDMM.
2.  **Provide More Implementation Details:** Clarify the choice of Bregman divergence and the parameter ηjt, and provide sufficient details to enable reproducibility.
3. **Discussion of K selection:** Include a section discussing the impact of *K* on performance and offer practical guidelines for its selection.
4. **Acknowledge limitations:** Briefly discuss the KKT point assumption and its potential implications.
