Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, Jacobi-style variant of ADMM for minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The main idea is to update an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian subproblem, and to stabilize the dual updates by introducing a “dual backward step” (ŷ) that effectively under-relaxes the dual gradient ascent. The step sizes for dual ascent and the backward step, τi and νi, are chosen based on the number of selected blocks and the sparsity pattern of A via the row block degrees di. The paper also includes an inexact variant using Bregman linearization and shows that sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are special cases. Convergence to a KKT point and an O(1/T) ergodic rate are established. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM to GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM, and two accelerated proximal methods.

This work sits among a large body of multi-block ADMM variants: Gauss–Seidel ADMM (with back-substitution) for multi-block problems (He et al.), proximal Jacobian ADMM (Deng et al.; Peng et al.), two-block ADMM with variable splitting for multi-block constraints, randomized block variants (e.g., RBSUMM), and relaxed/under-relaxed ADMM (Boyd et al.). The proposed “dual backward step” is closely related to under-relaxation/damping in ADMM, while the Bregman-regularized updates align with existing inexact/proximal ADMM analyses.

Strengths
- A unifying viewpoint: PDMM is presented as an umbrella that recovers both sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, which helps situate these methods within a single parameterized scheme.
- Attention to sparsity: Tying τi and νi to the block row degrees di is a reasonable, practically relevant design choice.
- Theoretical guarantees: The paper provides global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions, with constant stepsizes, and covers exact and inexact updates in one framework.
- Practical modeling of overlaps: The formulation for overlapping group problems via equality constraints is standard but useful, and PDMM can handle such cases in a randomized parallel fashion.

Weaknesses
Quality (soundness and validation)
- Theory delivers the same O(1/T) ergodic rate as classic ADMM/variants, with no improvement in rate or nonergodic bounds. Constants depend on di and K but their practical impact is unclear without numerical sensitivity analysis.
- Several technical definitions (e.g., positive semidefinite matrices P and Q in the residual and Lyapunov bounds) are referenced but not specified in the main text. This hinders assessment and reproducibility without the supplement.
- The “inexact PDMM” claims to unify analyses via Bregman terms, but it is not fully clear from the main text whether the same convergence rate with ηjt>0 is rigorously established under stated conditions; formal theorems for the inexact case should be explicit.
- The dual backward step appears conceptually equivalent to under-relaxation/relaxed ADMM. The novelty relative to well-studied relaxation schemes (and related randomized parallel updates) needs a more precise comparison.

Clarity
- The paper has numerous typos and grammatical issues (“seperable”, “convegence”, “defiend”, “contast”, “negligable”), and several references lack years and details. The algorithm is not presented in clear pseudocode, and parameter guidance beyond Eq. (8) is limited.
- The relationship to relaxed ADMM (Boyd et al., Sec. on over-/under-relaxation) should be discussed explicitly; at present the “backward step” framing obscures a known mechanism.

Originality
- While the combination of randomized block-Jacobi updates with under-relaxed dual ascent and sparsity-adaptive steps is neat, the components are well-known, and closely related approaches exist (proximal Jacobian ADMM; randomized ADMM; relaxed ADMM). Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases underscores that PDMM is more of a unifying parameterization than a fundamentally new algorithmic principle.

Significance (empirical impact)
- Experiments are small-scale, sequential Matlab implementations. Given the method’s emphasis on parallelism, the absence of parallel/multicore/distributed experiments and wall-clock speedups is a major gap.
- Some comparisons appear unfair or inconclusive. For sADMM, the public code used lacks the dual update (τi=0), which can materially hurt performance; a faithful implementation with tuned parameters is needed. RBSUMM is reported not to converge under tested settings; stronger tuning or references to known settings would be appropriate.
- Limited datasets (mostly synthetic) and narrow metrics (residuals/objective) reduce the practical impact. Real datasets for RPCA/overlapping group lasso and sensitivity to K, ρ, and sparsity would strengthen the case.

Questions for the authors
1) Please provide explicit forms of P and Q and all constants (γi, ζi) in the main text, and discuss how they affect the practical constants in the rate.
2) Do you have a formal theorem for inexact PDMM (ηjt>0) with the same O(1/T) rate? Under what Lipschitz/strong convexity assumptions? Please state it clearly.
3) How does PDMM’s ŷ update differ from standard relaxed ADMM (over-/under-relaxation)? Can you map τi, νi to the relaxation parameter α and discuss when PDMM yields different guarantees?
4) Can you implement and report a fair sADMM baseline with a proper dual update and tuned parameters?
5) Can you provide parallel (multithreaded or distributed) results showing actual speedups vs ADMM/GSADMM and sensitivity to K and di?
6) For RPCA, please compare to standard RPCA solvers (e.g., IALM/SVT) and report reconstruction accuracy metrics. For group lasso, add real datasets and compare to efficient proximal methods using duplication or consensus ADMM with tuned over-relaxation.

Minor issues
- Numerous typos throughout; please proofread carefully.
- Please add years and complete bibliographic details to references; some are listed as “Preprint” without identifiers.
- Clarify in Section 2.3 that while randomized overlapping coordinate descent may be nontrivial, there exist proximal methods and duplication-based approaches widely used in practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Unifying parameterization that connects several ADMM variants.
- Convergence analysis with sparsity-aware stepsizes; clear identification of instability in naive Jacobi ADMM.
- Potentially useful in practice for block-sparse constraints and overlapping regularizers.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond known relaxed/proximal randomized ADMM ideas.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale, sequential, and includes questionable baselines; no parallel speedups.
- Clarity and presentation issues; key technical details deferred or unclear in main text.

Recommendation
Leaning toward reject. The paper’s core ideas are sensible and potentially useful, but the novelty over established ADMM variants is modest and the experimental validation is not yet convincing for NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section with real parallel implementations and fair baselines, clarifying the relation to relaxed ADMM, and tightening the theory presentation would substantially improve the paper.
