

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The problem is formulated as minimizing a sum of convex functions subject to linear equality constraints, a common structure in machine learning problems including robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors establish connections between PDMM and existing methods: they show that two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are special cases of PDMM. The paper also demonstrates how PDMM can handle overlapping group structures, addressing a problem for which randomized block coordinate descent methods remain an open challenge.

The work builds on ADMM and its variants, particularly addressing the limitation that standard ADMM with multiple blocks may not converge. The paper relates to recent NIPS work on distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 10, 12, 14, 15, 17, 26]) and extends these by providing a unified framework that handles both exact and inexact updates through Bregman divergence.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size. The key innovation lies in the dual backward step and the step size selection strategy that accounts for block sparsity and the number of blocks updated. This provides a unified framework that encompasses existing methods as special cases.

2. **Clear Theoretical Contribution**: The paper clearly explains how PDMM differs from existing approaches. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides valuable theoretical insight into the role of the dual backward step, which is not well understood in prior work.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's practical advantages. The paper shows that PDMM with K=1 (updating one block) is the fastest algorithm for RPCA, while PDMM with larger K converges faster for overlapping group lasso. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (ADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) is thorough and well-presented.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses an important practical challenge in large-scale machine learning: parallelizing optimization algorithms. The theoretical results are directly applicable to real-world problems, as demonstrated by the experimental results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: While the paper shows runtime improvements, it doesn't provide concrete details about how the algorithm scales with the number of processors or the overhead of parallel implementation. The experimental section states that "PDMM can run in parallel but GSADMM only runs sequentially," but doesn't quantify the speedup.

2. **Some Theoretical Details Could Be Clarified**: The derivation of the step size parameters (8) and the connection to block sparsity could be made more intuitive. The paper mentions that "di could be far smaller than J" for sparse A, but doesn't provide examples of real-world problems where this sparsity is significant.

3. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods**: While the paper compares with ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, it doesn't include comparisons with other recent parallel optimization methods that might be relevant, such as [10] which also proposes a parallel ADMM variant.

## Assessment Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proofs are comprehensive, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach (e.g., noting that updating all blocks may lead to difficult subproblems).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main contributions are well-motivated, and the connections to related work are clearly explained. The notation is consistent, and the paper provides enough detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, particularly in its approach to handling the dual update with a backward step and its step size selection strategy. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing how sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, which provides new theoretical insights.

**Significance**: The problem addressed (parallel optimization of block-separable convex problems) is important for large-scale machine learning. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a method that achieves O(1/T) convergence with constant step size, while also being parallelizable. The experimental results demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods, making the contribution significant for both theory and practice.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed and parallel optimization. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the proposed method has clear practical advantages. The connections to existing methods provide valuable theoretical insights that advance our understanding of ADMM variants.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important problem in machine learning with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. The technical contribution is significant and advances the state of the art in parallel optimization methods.

**Pro**: Strong theoretical contribution with global convergence and O(1/T) complexity; clear connections to existing methods; practical experimental validation showing runtime improvements; addresses an important open problem (overlapping groups).

**Con**: Limited discussion of parallel scalability; some theoretical details could be clarified; could include more comprehensive comparisons with recent parallel optimization methods.

Overall, this paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
