Summary
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a randomized Jacobi-style variant of ADMM that updates K randomly chosen primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks. The key device is a “dual backward step” introducing parameters τi and νi that shrink the explicit dual update to counteract implicit dual ascent from the augmented term. These parameters are chosen as functions of the row-block sparsity di and the number of updated blocks K. An inexact variant adds Bregman proximal terms to each primal block update. The authors prove global convergence with an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions and show that two known methods are special cases: sADMM (with splitting variables, exact primal updates, small dual steps) and PJADMM (with large proximal terms and full dual steps). Experiments on synthetic RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM to ADMM/GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work
Two-block ADMM convergence is classical; multi-block extensions are known to need care (e.g., back substitution, proximal terms, or variable splitting). Jacobian multi-block ADMM variants with proximal regularization and randomized coordinate updates have appeared (e.g., proximal Jacobian ADMM, randomized BSUMM). The backward (under-relaxation) step is conceptually related to known over/under-relaxation in ADMM. The paper’s main conceptual contribution is unifying sADMM and PJADMM through a single parameterization and tying step sizes to di and K.

Quality
- Theory: The authors state global convergence and O(1/T) rate for arbitrary K, with τi and νi depending on di and K. Assumptions are standard (closed proper convex fj, existence of KKT). However, key pieces of the analysis (definitions and roles of Pt and Q, proofs of positivity and monotonicity) are relegated to the supplement, and the presentation here is too sketchy to fully verify. The rate matches many existing ADMM-type results and is not stronger (e.g., no linear rate under standard additional assumptions).
- Methodological soundness: The backward step is a reasonable fix for Jacobi instability and aligns with intuition about implicit vs explicit dual ascent. The dependence of step sizes on sparsity is plausible, but it is not clear whether these choices are tight/optimal or how sensitive performance is to misspecification.
- Experiments: Limited and not fully convincing. Both tasks are synthetic; implementations are sequential Matlab, so the claimed advantage of parallel Jacobi updates is not demonstrated. Baseline tuning fairness is unclear (e.g., sADMM code seems to omit dual updates; GSADMM has no guarantees but is faster; RBSUMM settings may be suboptimal). There is little sensitivity analysis over ρ and other hyperparameters, and no large real datasets. Runtime comparisons do not reflect communication/aggregation costs that would matter in the intended parallel setting.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but has many typos (“seperable,” “defiend,” “convegence,” etc.), inconsistent notation in places, and missing details in the main text. Important definitions (Pt, Q, βi) and algorithmic steps would benefit from clearer exposition and a pseudocode block. References are incomplete (years sometimes missing). The experimental setup needs more transparency on tuning and stopping criteria per method.

Originality
The unifying view (sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM) and the explicit dependence of τi, νi on di and K are nice observations. However, the core ideas—Jacobi-style multi-block ADMM with stabilization (backward/relaxation) and proximal regularization, randomized block selection, Bregman linearization—are all known. The contribution feels incremental rather than fundamentally new.

Significance
If the analysis is correct, PDMM offers another practical recipe for multi-block problems with guidance on step sizes that accounts for sparsity. This could be useful in some applications (e.g., overlapping group models). However, the convergence rate is standard, and the empirical evidence is too limited to conclude a clear practical advantage over established alternatives. Without a real parallel/distributed implementation or stronger benchmarks, the impact is uncertain.

Pros
- Clear identification of Jacobi instability and principled under-relaxation remedy.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM within one framework; handles inexact updates via Bregman terms.
- Convergence and O(1/T) rate under constant stepsizes for arbitrary K.
- Consideration of sparsity in step-size selection is intuitive and potentially useful.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to proximal Jacobian ADMM and randomized ADMM variants.
- Theoretical presentation in the paper is too terse; key components deferred to supplement.
- Experiments are weak: synthetic, sequential, limited baselines/tuning fairness, no parallel speedups demonstrated.
- Claims of being “faster than existing methods” are not broadly substantiated.
- Writing/notation issues and incomplete references.

Questions for authors
- Provide explicit algorithm pseudocode and full definitions of Pt and Q in the main text.
- Discuss sensitivity to τi, νi and ρ; how robust is PDMM to misspecification? Are the proposed τi, νi near-optimal?
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., modern proximal gradient/primal-dual methods, other randomized ADMM variants) with careful tuning.
- Demonstrate actual parallel/distributed speedups and quantify communication.
- Clarify the conditions under which PJADMM is recovered (how large must η be?).

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the paper contains a sensible stabilization of Jacobi multi-block ADMM and a unifying perspective, the novelty is modest and the empirical validation is insufficient, especially given the paper’s emphasis on parallelism that is not demonstrated. With stronger experiments (including real parallel implementations and fair, thorough baselines) and clearer theory presentation, this work could be competitive.

Confidence: High
