## Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

**Summary:**

This paper presents the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The authors address a key limitation of extending ADMM to multiple blocks – the potential for divergence observed in Gauss-Seidel approaches – by introducing a backward step on the dual update and enabling parallel, randomized block updates.  They connect PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, demonstrating them as special cases, and provide convergence analysis with O(1/T) iteration complexity.  The method is evaluated on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso problems, showing competitive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the well-established ADMM framework [Boyd et al., 2011]. It addresses a gap in the literature concerning the convergence and scalability of multi-block ADMM, contrasting with previous work like GSADMM [He et al., 2015] and BSUMM [Hong et al., 2016], which often suffer from convergence issues or restrictive conditions.  The authors acknowledge and differentiate their work from Jacobian variants like sADMM [Wang et al., 2017] and PJADMM [Deng et al., 2018], framing PDMM as a more general approach.  The application to overlapping group lasso connects to work on composite minimization and structural sparsity [Zhao et al., 2006].

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in applying ADMM to large-scale, multi-block problems – maintaining convergence with parallel updates.
* **Novelty & Generality:**  The introduction of the backward step on the dual update and the flexibility of randomized block selection are novel contributions. Framing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM provides valuable insight into the relationship between these methods.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence analysis, establishing O(1/T) iteration complexity, provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM and its competitiveness with existing approaches.  The analysis of the effect of the number of blocks (K) is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The authors thoughtfully position their work within the broader context of ADMM variants and explain how PDMM relates to and improves upon existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:**  The introduction of parameters τi and νi, dependent on the sparsity structure of A and the number of blocks K, might increase the complexity of implementation and parameter tuning.  While the paper provides formulas for these parameters, more guidance on practical selection would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Setup Limitations:**  The experimental comparison with GSADMM is somewhat limited.  The authors mention that GSADMM results were chosen as “best,” but don’t fully explain the parameter tuning process for comparison.  The statement that sADMM lacks a dual update appears incorrect based on the original publication.
* **Lack of Scalability Analysis:**  While the paper highlights the potential for parallelism, a more detailed analysis of the scalability of PDMM with respect to the number of blocks and the problem size would be valuable. The discussion of the runtime effects of K is a good start, but more in-depth investigation is warranted.
* **Clarity on Bregman Divergence:** While the explanation of how the Bregman divergence facilitates inexact updates is clear, the initial motivation for its inclusion might benefit from further elaboration. The connection to existing work on Bregman ADMM [Wang & Banerjee, 2015] is not explicitly made.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, specifically addressing a key challenge in applying ADMM to large-scale problems. The proposed PDMM method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some aspects of parameter tuning and scalability could benefit from further investigation, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a practically important problem (convergent parallel ADMM).
* Presents a novel and theoretically justified method.
* Demonstrates competitive performance through experiments.
* Provides valuable insights into the relationship between different ADMM variants.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter tuning might be complex.
* Experimental comparison could be more comprehensive.
* Scalability analysis could be more detailed.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically:

1.  Provide more guidance on practical parameter selection for τi and νi.
2.  Clarify the experimental setup for comparison with GSADMM and verify the statements about sADMM.
3.  Expand the scalability analysis to discuss the computational cost and memory requirements of PDMM as a function of the problem size and number of blocks.
4. Explicitly cite and connect the work to related literature on Bregman ADMM [Wang & Banerjee, 2015].
