

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a new parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a gap in existing ADMM variants by developing a method that can update arbitrary numbers of blocks in parallel while maintaining convergence guarantees. The paper establishes connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), showing they are special cases of PDMM. Theoretical analysis proves global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, with experiments demonstrating improved performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous convergence guarantees with O(1/T) iteration complexity, a significant contribution over methods like RBSUMM that require decreasing step sizes. The analysis carefully considers the interplay between block sparsity, number of randomized blocks, and step size parameters.

2. **Novel Insights**: The paper identifies a key reason why direct Jacobi updates fail (implicit dual ascent combined with explicit dual ascent leading to aggressive dual progress) and proposes the dual backward step to address this. This insight provides a deeper understanding of ADMM variants.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The algorithm is well-suited for modern parallel computing architectures, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods in both RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

4. **Connections to Prior Work**: The paper effectively situates PDMM within the broader ADMM literature, showing how it unifies and extends existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) while addressing their limitations.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Theoretical Comparison**: While the paper establishes O(1/T) complexity, it doesn't clearly articulate why this is better than the O(1/√T) complexity of some other randomized methods or how PDMM compares to recent accelerated variants. The theoretical analysis could better highlight the specific advantages over existing methods.

2. **Experimental Depth**: The experimental section, while showing improved performance, lacks a thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs. For example, the paper shows PDMM with K=20 achieves similar runtime to using all blocks, but doesn't analyze the parallelization efficiency gains or communication costs in distributed settings.

3. **Clarity of Overlapping Group Lasso Application**: The connection to overlapping group lasso is promising but not fully developed. The paper states that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem," but doesn't clearly explain how PDMM resolves this specifically.

4. **Step Size Selection**: While the paper provides a theoretically justified step size selection rule, it doesn't sufficiently address how practitioners should select these parameters in practice, particularly for problems with unknown sparsity patterns.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature. The theoretical analysis is sound, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper advances the state of the art in parallel optimization methods by providing a unified framework that encompasses existing methods while offering practical advantages.

The main weaknesses relate to the need for more detailed theoretical comparisons with recent methods and deeper experimental analysis of the computational trade-offs. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the practical advantages of PDMM over simply using ADMM with parallel blocks (when K is large).

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Include a more detailed comparison of the theoretical complexity with recent accelerated methods like those in [9, 27], particularly regarding the practical implications of the O(1/T) rate.

2. Add a section analyzing the computational complexity of PDMM in distributed settings, including communication costs and speedup ratios.

3. Provide a more detailed explanation of how PDMM specifically addresses the overlapping group lasso problem, contrasting it with existing approaches.

4. Include a brief practical guide for selecting τi and νi based on problem characteristics.

## Conclusion

The paper makes a solid contribution to the optimization literature with a theoretically sound and practically useful algorithm. While there are opportunities to strengthen the theoretical comparison and experimental analysis, the core contribution is valuable and well-executed. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS and represents a significant advancement in parallel optimization methods.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the points above)
