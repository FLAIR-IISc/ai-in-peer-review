The paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems, a common formulation in machine learning. The core idea is to extend ADMM to a randomized block coordinate update scheme, offering potential advantages in parallelism and convergence compared to existing methods like GSADMM and sADMM. The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate the method's performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

While the problem addressed is relevant and the proposed method has the potential to be valuable, the paper suffers from a surprising number of shortcomings, ranging from notational inconsistencies to a lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating significant advantages over established techniques. My overall assessment is negative; I recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The linearly-constrained block-separable optimization problem is a fundamental one in machine learning, and efficient solvers are highly desirable.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The authors provide convergence analysis for PDMM, which is a positive aspect. Establishing a connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is also insightful.
*   **Potential for Parallelism:** The randomized block coordinate update scheme is naturally suited for parallel implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity:** The notation is riddled with inconsistencies. For example, the definition of *K̃i* is given but never explicitly used in the equations following its definition; the paper then defaults to *K* in the equations. Similarly, the definition of *N(i)* is introduced in the notation section but isn't used anywhere else in the paper. The variable "τ" is used inconsistently to represent both a shrinkage factor and a step size.  The use of subscripts (e.g., *t*, *i*, *j*) is often unclear and requires careful tracking to understand the flow of equations. The jump from general theory to application without a concrete explanation of how parameters are *chosen* for each application is troubling.
*   **Lack of Empirical Robustness:** The experimental results, while demonstrating that PDMM performs comparably to or slightly better than other methods in *specific* configurations, lack robustness. The experiments are conducted with relatively small problem sizes (m=1000, n=5000 for RPCA). The reported results are highly sensitive to parameter tuning and don’t clearly demonstrate a consistent advantage over existing, well-established methods like ADMM or even GSADMM. The claim of "faster" performance is often qualified with caveats about runtime versus number of iterations. Figure 1’s caption mentioning that “best” results for GSADMM and RBSUMM are chosen is concerning, as it suggests a biased comparison.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The theoretical contribution, while valid, feels incremental. Establishing PDMM as a generalization of sADMM and PJADMM is interesting but doesn’t fundamentally alter our understanding of these algorithms. The proposed "backward step" is a relatively minor modification to ADMM and its benefits are not convincingly demonstrated.
*   **Overly Optimistic Claims:** The paper repeatedly makes claims about the advantages of PDMM without sufficient supporting evidence. For example, the assertion that PDMM can use a larger step size in the overlapping group lasso problem (Section 2.3) needs more justification.  Similarly, the discussion about the interplay between sparsity and block randomization feels more speculative than conclusive.
*   **Missing Details:** The implementation details are scarce. For example, the selection of the Bregman divergence function φjt in Section 2.1 is not discussed in detail, and the choice of this function can significantly impact performance. The extension to randomized dual block coordinate ascent (mentioned in Section 1 but referenced as [25] - a preprint) is not further explained.
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** Several sections contain repetitive explanations and lengthy discussions that do not add significant value to the paper. The introduction, in particular, feels overly long and could be significantly condensed.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound convergence analysis.
*   Potential for parallel implementation.
*   Exploration of the relationship between different ADMM variants.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and clarity issues.
*   Lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   Incremental theoretical contribution.
*   Overly optimistic claims and a lack of robustness in experimental results.
*   Insufficient implementation details.
*   Verbosity and redundancy.



**Overall:**

While the authors present a technically sound algorithm, the paper is hampered by a lack of clarity, insufficient empirical validation, and an incremental contribution. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work and make it difficult to assess the true potential of PDMM. I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the notational inconsistencies, conduct more comprehensive experiments with larger problem sizes, and provide a more convincing justification for the advantages of PDMM.
