### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a common and important problem structure in machine learning. The primary motivation is to overcome the limitations of existing methods based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). While standard multi-block ADMM is often run sequentially (Gauss-Seidel style), which limits parallelism, direct parallelization (Jacobi style) often fails to converge.

PDMM addresses this by introducing a parallel, randomized block-coordinate update scheme. The core technical innovations are:
1.  A "backward step" on the dual variable, which introduces an intermediate dual variable `ŷ` to make the dual updates more conservative and stable.
2.  The introduction of step-size parameters (`τ_i`, `ν_i`) for the dual updates that are intelligently chosen based on the problem structure, specifically the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A` and the number of blocks (`K`) being updated in parallel at each iteration.
3.  The ability to randomly select and update an arbitrary number of blocks (`K`) in parallel, offering flexibility between fully parallel and single-block updates.

The authors provide a unified framework that shows two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM corresponding to different parameter choices. They establish the algorithm's global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, which is standard for this class of first-order methods. The paper also demonstrates how PDMM can be effectively applied to problems with overlapping regularizers, a challenging case for traditional block coordinate descent methods. The method's efficacy is demonstrated empirically on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis appears sound, following a standard Lyapunov-based argument to prove convergence and derive the O(1/T) rate. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical analysis and the experimental results. The work is complete, presenting the algorithm, its theoretical guarantees, and empirical validation. The authors are careful to connect their work to a broad range of existing literature.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem and clearly situates PDMM within the landscape of ADMM variants. The core ideas behind the algorithm, particularly the roles of the dual backward step, sparsity, and randomization, are explained well in Section 2. The experimental results are presented clearly.

One minor point of confusion is the precise flow of updates involving `ŷ`. The text motivates `ŷ_t` as `y_t - νρ(Ax_t − a)`, which is used to compute `x_{t+1}`. However, the algorithm presented in equations (5)-(7) defines the update for `ŷ_{t+1}` after `y_{t+1}` is computed. Clarifying the exact sequence of computations in an "Algorithm" box would improve reproducibility and remove any ambiguity for the reader.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes several novel contributions. While parallel and randomized ADMM variants exist, the specific construction of PDMM is new. The introduction of the dual backward step, combined with the adaptive step-sizes (`τ_i`, `ν_i`) that depend on both sparsity and the number of parallel blocks, is a key novelty. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a deeper understanding of the relationship between these methods. Furthermore, applying this randomized parallel framework to overlapping group lasso problems is a valuable and non-trivial extension.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. Developing convergent, efficient, and truly parallel algorithms for large-scale constrained optimization is a critical research area. PDMM provides a practical and theoretically-grounded tool for this purpose. Its flexibility in choosing the number of parallel blocks (`K`) allows practitioners to trade off per-iteration progress with computational cost and communication overhead. The insights from unifying previous methods and the ability to solve challenging problems like overlapping group lasso suggest that this work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the optimization and machine learning communities.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance (Pros):**

1.  **Addresses a Key Problem:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of creating a convergent and practical parallel multi-block ADMM algorithm.
2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** PDMM introduces a novel combination of a dual backward step and adaptive step-sizes, providing a principled way to stabilize parallel updates.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous proof of global convergence and an O(1/T) complexity rate, placing the algorithm on a solid theoretical footing.
4.  **Unifying Framework:** The work provides significant insight by showing that prominent existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) are special cases of PDMM, clarifying the landscape of parallel ADMM algorithms.
5.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that PDMM is competitive with or superior to several state-of-the-art methods on relevant machine learning tasks, including the challenging overlapping group lasso problem.
6.  **Flexibility and Practicality:** The algorithm is flexible (choice of `K`) and its step-sizes adapt to problem structure (sparsity), which are desirable practical features.

**Arguments against acceptance (Cons):**

1.  **Sequential Simulation of Parallelism:** A notable weakness is that all experiments were run sequentially. The core motivation of the algorithm is parallelism, so its performance benefits are only inferred from operation counts and iteration numbers. A demonstration on a multi-core or distributed system would have provided much stronger evidence of its practical utility in a parallel setting.
2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice in Parameter Tuning:** The experiments in Table 1 show that manually "tuned" parameters for PDMM outperform the theoretically derived ones from Eq. (8). This suggests the theoretical analysis may yield conservative step-sizes, and practitioners may still need to engage in parameter tuning to achieve optimal performance. This could be discussed more explicitly.
3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** As mentioned above, the precise update loop involving `ŷ` could be made more explicit to enhance clarity and ease of implementation.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes significant contributions to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. Its main strengths are the elegant way it stabilizes parallel ADMM updates and its ability to unify existing methods, providing valuable new insights. While the lack of true parallel experiments is a limitation, the quality and significance of the algorithmic and theoretical contributions are high. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
