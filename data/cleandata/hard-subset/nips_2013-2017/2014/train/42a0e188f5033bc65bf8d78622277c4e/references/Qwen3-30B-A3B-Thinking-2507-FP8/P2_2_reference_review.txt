

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel, incorporates a dual backward step for conservative dual progress, and adapts step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and show that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The method is evaluated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and complete, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions. The derivation of step sizes (τi, νi) based on matrix sparsity and block selection is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable theoretical unification. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing PDMM against multiple relevant baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on two meaningful machine learning problems. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the trade-off between subproblem complexity and parallelism.

The paper avoids common pitfalls in optimization papers: it doesn't overstate its contributions, carefully distinguishes its approach from prior work, and provides clear experimental validation. The theoretical results are well-supported by the experiments, with convergence rates matching the theoretical predictions.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the literature. The technical details are presented logically, and the key innovations (dual backward step, adaptive step sizes) are clearly explained. The notation is consistent throughout, though some of the more technical derivations (particularly in Section 3) could benefit from additional explanation for readers less familiar with ADMM variants.

The experimental section is clear and well-presented, with appropriate figures showing convergence behavior. However, the paper could better highlight the practical implications of the theoretical results - for instance, how the choice of K (number of blocks) affects real-world runtime performance in distributed settings.

## Originality
PDMM makes a significant contribution by providing a unified framework for multiple ADMM variants and introducing a novel randomized block coordinate approach with adaptive step sizes. The key insight of the dual backward step to address convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates is particularly valuable. While some elements (block coordinate descent, randomized updates) build on prior work, the specific combination with the dual backward step and adaptive step size selection represents a meaningful advance.

The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is a notable theoretical contribution that enhances understanding of these methods. The application to overlapping group lasso, where randomized block coordinate descent was previously an open problem, is particularly compelling.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in distributed optimization with clear practical implications. The ability to update multiple blocks in parallel while maintaining convergence guarantees is valuable for large-scale machine learning applications. The demonstrated speedups over existing methods (particularly in the overlapping group lasso experiments) suggest real practical benefits. The theoretical insights about the relationship between dual step sizes and problem structure advance the understanding of ADMM variants.

The method could be broadly applicable to many machine learning problems involving linear constraints, making it a potentially valuable tool for practitioners. The O(1/T) convergence rate matches state-of-the-art methods, establishing PDMM as a competitive alternative to existing approaches.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified
- Provides unifying perspective on existing ADMM variants
- Clear experimental validation demonstrating practical advantages
- Addresses a meaningful problem with clear connections to real-world applications
- Well-motivated step size selection based on problem structure

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental results don't fully demonstrate the parallelism benefits in a distributed setting (all experiments appear sequential)
- The connection to related work (particularly RBSUMM) could be more thoroughly discussed
- The paper doesn't explore the theoretical limits of the approach (e.g., when the adaptive step size selection becomes problematic)

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are sound, the method is well-motivated, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of its practical advantages. The paper advances both the theoretical understanding and practical application of ADMM variants.

The paper is appropriate for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with clear relevance to machine learning. The quality of the work meets NIPS standards, and the significance of the contribution is substantial.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable new perspective on ADMM variants with clear theoretical and practical benefits, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. With minor improvements to the discussion of the parallelism benefits, this would be an excellent contribution to the field.
