Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing a sum of convex block-separable functions under linear constraints. Each iteration picks K primal blocks at random, performs parallel primal updates against an “attenuated” dual (a backward step ŷ), then updates all dual blocks with a step size τi tuned by the local sparsity di of A and by K. The authors motivate the backward step by noting that the quadratic term in the augmented Lagrangian induces an implicit dual ascent; adding a backward step avoids overly aggressive dual progress in the Jacobi setting. They generalize to inexact updates via Bregman linearization. They prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates in expectation under standard convex assumptions with constant step sizes. They show that two known multi-block ADMM variants—two-block ADMM with variable splitting (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM)—arise as limiting cases of PDMM. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM (for various K) against ADMM/GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

This work builds on a large literature on multi-block ADMM, randomized coordinate methods, and proximal/Bregman variants. It is closest to: (i) sADMM (two-block splitting to handle multi-block constraints), (ii) proximal Jacobian ADMM with large proximal terms, and (iii) RBSUMM which randomizes one block. Compared to these, PDMM allows updating an arbitrary number of blocks K in parallel, explicitly incorporates sparsity of A in the dual step, uses a backward dual step, and provides O(1/T) complexity with constant steps without additional error-bound assumptions.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The paper presents a clear Lyapunov function h(v*,vt) and a residual R capturing feasibility and successive differences, and proves monotone decrease in expectation with constant steps τi and νi indexed by di and K. The assumptions (closed, proper convex fj; existence of a KKT point) match standard ADMM analyses. The O(1/T) rate is consistent with known results for ADMM-type methods. The derivation of τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i is plausible and well-motivated by implicit/explicit dual steps and sparsity. However:
  - Some crucial objects (Pt and Q in the residual and Lyapunov definitions) are stated as positive semidefinite but not concretely specified in the main text; the reader must rely on the supplement.
  - The equivalence claims to sADMM and PJADMM are deferred to appendices referenced as [25] (an arXiv manuscript), rather than the current supplement. For NeurIPS, all proofs should be self-contained in the submission/supplement.
  - The rate is ergodic and in expectation; no non-ergodic or last-iterate results are given.
- Algorithms: The backward step is a sensible fix for Jacobi-style instability and the dependence of τi on di is intuitively meaningful: sparser coupling allows larger dual steps. The inexact Bregman linearization is standard and well-justified.
- Experiments: The tasks (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) are appropriate. Results suggest PDMM is competitive or faster than baselines. However, all runs are sequential Matlab, so parallel advantages are not demonstrated; some comparisons hinge on specific codebases (e.g., sADMM implementation without a dual update), which weakens fairness.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable, but there are numerous typos/grammatical issues and notational inconsistencies (e.g., Acj vs Ajt, definition of Pt/Q). The explanation of the implicit dual ascent is helpful, but the exposition would benefit from:
  - Self-contained statements of all lemmas/theorems with precise definitions of all matrices used (Pt, Q, βi).
  - A clearer, unified presentation of the relations to sADMM/PJADMM without relying on external appendices.
  - A dedicated subsection explaining the practical selection of K and the computational cost per iteration versus Gauss-Seidel ADMM.

Originality
- Allowing arbitrary K randomized parallel block updates with sparsity-aware dual step sizes and a backward dual step is a nontrivial and useful combination. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of a single framework is insightful. While O(1/T) rates for ADMM variants are known, obtaining them for this randomized-Jacobi setting with constant steps and without additional error-bound assumptions is a novel contribution.

Significance
- The method addresses a real gap: Jacobi multi-block ADMM often diverges; existing fixes either require variable splitting (increasing problem size), large proximal terms (slowing primal progress), or update one block at a time. PDMM provides a principled middle ground with tunable K and step sizes leveraging sparsity. This can be valuable in large-scale and distributed settings. That said, the empirical section does not showcase actual parallel scalability, and comparisons to strong recent baselines could be expanded.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and well-motivated algorithmic design (backward dual step; sparsity-aware step sizes).
- Clear theoretical guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant steps under standard assumptions.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM, offering conceptual clarity about when to use small/large dual steps.
- Demonstrates applicability to overlapping group problems via a clean reformulation.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity/organization issues; reliance on external appendix for key proofs/derivations; unspecified matrices in the main text.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: no actual parallel/distributed runs; some comparisons may be unfair (e.g., sADMM implementation without dual update).
- Only ergodic/in-expectation rates; no last-iterate or high-probability bounds.
- Sensitivity to parameter choices (τi, νi) is only partially explored; sometimes tuned beyond the theoretically suggested values.

Questions and suggestions
- Please include full proofs and the precise definitions of Pt and Q in the supplement; avoid referencing an external arXiv for essential details.
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of the backward step (νi) and the sparsity-aware τi; show failure of plain Jacobi with small τ.
- Add parallel/distributed experiments to demonstrate wall-clock speedups and communication costs; clarify whether dual updates can be block-randomized (you mention this in [25]).
- Ensure fair baselines: implement sADMM with proper dual updates and report its best settings; add comparisons to other recent randomized/proximal multi-block ADMM variants.
- Discuss guidelines for choosing K in practice and how di is computed at scale; include sensitivity plots.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea is interesting and backed by reasonable theory; with improved clarity and stronger empirical validation (especially real parallel runs and fair baselines), the paper would be a clear accept.
