### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear equality constraints, a formulation that captures a wide range of machine learning problems, including Robust PCA and composite regularization with overlapping groups. The authors propose a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which is a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).

The core ideas of PDMM are threefold. First, at each iteration, it randomly selects a subset of `K` primal variable blocks to update in parallel. Second, to counteract the potential divergence of parallel updates in the method of multipliers framework, it introduces a "backward step" on the dual variable. This results in using a corrected dual variable `ŷ_t` for the primal updates, making them more conservative. Third, the step sizes for the dual ascent (`τ_i`) and the dual backward step (`ν_i`) are not constant but are carefully chosen based on the number of blocks selected for update (`K`), the total number of blocks (`J`), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`.

The paper establishes a unifying framework that shows two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM when all blocks are updated (`K=J`), corresponding to exact and inexact primal updates, respectively. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate under standard convexity assumptions, which matches the rates of standard ADMM. The practical performance of PDMM is demonstrated on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where it is shown to be competitive with or superior to related methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated by the difficulties of extending ADMM to the multi-block, parallel setting. The theoretical analysis is a key strength, providing a global convergence proof and an O(1/T) complexity rate with a constant step size. This is a significant result, especially when compared to related work like RBSUMM, for which the iteration complexity is unknown and convergence requires stricter conditions. The formulation is complete, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical derivations.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for the algorithm, particularly the introduction of the dual backward step to temper the aggressive updates of a naive Jacobi-style ADMM, is explained clearly. The connections drawn to sADMM and PJADMM in Section 2.2 are insightful and help place the contribution in the context of existing literature, providing a valuable unification. The experimental setup is described adequately.

However, the clarity could be improved in a few areas. The derivation of the specific step sizes for `τ_i` and `ν_i` in Equation (8) is presented without much intuition. While the proof of their validity is expected in the supplement, a brief, intuitive explanation in the main body for why these forms are chosen (e.g., how they balance the progress from the `K` updated blocks against the stasis of the `J-K` other blocks) would significantly enhance the reader's understanding. Furthermore, Remark 2 is slightly confusing. It states that the fastest convergence is achieved when `J=K=1`, which corresponds to the standard (non-parallel) method of multipliers. While this is true in terms of the constant in the convergence rate, it overlooks the practical trade-off between the convergence rate and the per-iteration computational cost, which is the primary motivation for block-coordinate methods. A more nuanced discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel. While parallel and randomized block-coordinate ADMM-type methods exist, the specific combination of a dual backward step with adaptive step sizes that incorporate both the degree of parallelism (`K`) and the problem structure (sparsity of `A`) is a new contribution. The framework that unifies sADMM and PJADMM as two extreme cases is also an original and valuable conceptual insight. The application of this randomized framework to solve overlapping group lasso problems, which are challenging for standard randomized block coordinate descent methods, further highlights the novelty and utility of the approach.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of broad interest in machine learning and optimization. The development of a provably convergent parallel algorithm for this problem class is a significant contribution. Practitioners are likely to find PDMM useful due to its flexibility (allowing for a trade-off between parallelism and iteration cost via the parameter `K`) and its strong theoretical guarantees. The ability to exploit sparsity in the constraint matrix for larger step sizes is a key practical advantage. The results suggest PDMM could advance the state of the art in solving large-scale problems where data or model variables can be naturally partitioned into blocks.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the algorithm's promise. However, its significance could be further strengthened. The primary advantage of PDMM is its parallel nature, but all experiments were run sequentially. While this ensures a fair comparison of iteration counts and sequential runtime, demonstrating actual wall-clock speedups on a parallel computing architecture would make the practical significance of the work more compelling. Additionally, the comparison against sADMM in the RPCA experiment seems to use a non-optimal implementation of the baseline (as noted by the authors, `τ_i = 0`), which may overstate PDMM's relative performance.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel Algorithm:** Proposes a novel and well-motivated parallel randomized block-coordinate algorithm (PDMM) with a key new mechanism (dual backward step).
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** Provides rigorous proofs for global convergence and an O(1/T) rate, which is on par with state-of-the-art ADMM methods.
3.  **Unifying Framework:** Elegantly shows that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases, improving the understanding of the field.
4.  **Broad Applicability and Significance:** Addresses a fundamental optimization problem with wide-ranging applications and provides a practical method that can exploit problem structure (sparsity) and parallelism.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Clarity of Key Components:** The intuition behind the specific choice of the crucial step-size parameters is lacking in the main text.
2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while positive, do not demonstrate the algorithm's performance in a true parallel environment, which is its main selling point. The comparison with at least one baseline (sADMM) may not be entirely fair.

In summary, this is a strong paper with solid theoretical and algorithmic contributions. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation and the scope of the experiments, and they do not undermine the core technical quality and originality of the work. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on large-scale optimization.
