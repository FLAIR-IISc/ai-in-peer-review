Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies linearly constrained convex optimization with block-separable objectives. It proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel, Jacobi-style variant of ADMM that updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks each iteration. The key design elements are: (i) a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term, (ii) per-row dual step sizes τi and backward factors νi that depend on the sparsity pattern of A (di, the number of non-zero blocks in row i) and on K, and (iii) an optional Bregman proximal term to enable inexact updates. The authors show that two known methods are recovered as limiting cases: sADMM with splitting variables (small dual step, exact primal updates) and PJADMM (large proximal terms, full dual step, ν=0). They establish almost-sure convergence in expectation and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate improved iteration/runtime over sADMM and RBSUMM; GSADMM is competitive in iterations but slower in (sequential) runtime; PDMM scales favorably with larger K.

This work builds on and connects: multi-block ADMM (Boyd et al.; He, Tao, Yuan; Hong et al.), randomized block coordinate descent (Richtárik & Takáč; Nesterov), proximal/Jacobian ADMM variants (Deng et al.; Parikh & Boyd), and BSUMM/RBSUMM. The paper’s main novelty is a principled dual backward step with per-row adaptive τi, νi that yields a convergent, randomized, parallel Jacobi scheme with explicit dependence on sparsity.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity in diagnosing why naive Jacobi ADMM can diverge (implicit dual ascent) and fixing it via a dual backward step.
- A unifying view: PDMM recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extremes, clarifying when to use small vs. large dual steps depending on primal progress.
- Convergence guarantees with constant step sizes for randomized multi-block Jacobi updates; O(1/T) ergodic rate aligns with best-known rates for related ADMM variants.
- Insightful step-size design depending on di and K; for sparse A this permits significantly larger steps than sADMM.
- Practical applicability to overlapping group lasso via a clean equality-constraint reformulation; the di=2 case highlights the advantage.
- Empirical results show PDMM is competitive or faster than sADMM/RBSUMM and matches or outperforms GSADMM in runtime (despite GS being faster in iterations).

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation is limited: synthetic datasets, sequential Matlab implementation, no actual parallel execution to substantiate claimed parallel speedups or scalability.
- Comparisons: misses several strong multi-block baselines (e.g., linearized/proximal ADMM variants with proven convergence and modern tuned implementations). RBSUMM comparisons use diminishing step sizes; other tuned settings might change the picture.
- Theory presentation leaves some key objects opaque in the main text (e.g., exact definitions/construction of P^t and Q, and how ζi, βi, γi interplay), making it hard to verify conditions without the supplement.
- The inexact variant relies on ψ with Lipschitz gradients and ϕ being strongly convex; guidance on identifying σ and choosing ηjt in practice is limited. For composite/nonsmooth components, clarity on when linearization is legitimate would help.
- The analysis assumes synchronous updates and exact knowledge of di; no treatment of asynchrony, communication, or robustness to stale updates, which are central to parallel settings.
- Some experimental tuning appears to deviate from the theoretically recommended τi, νi (authors also “tune” τ, ν in a section), raising fairness/robustness questions.

Quality:
- The algorithmic idea is sound and well-motivated. The theoretical results (global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic bounds) are in line with the literature and non-trivial for randomized Jacobi multi-block settings. However, the proof sketch in the main text is minimal; more intuition on P^t, Q, and the potential function’s construction would improve readability.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear, but notation is heavy and some typos persist (“seperable,” “defiend,” “convegence”). The roles of τi and νi are well explained. Section 2.3 is a nice application, though the overhead of variable splitting (memory/time) is not discussed.

Originality:
- The dual backward step with per-row adaptive τi, νi dependent on sparsity and randomized parallel block updates appears novel, and the unifying relation to sADMM and PJADMM is insightful.

Significance:
- Many applications fit the considered template. A convergent, scalable, randomized Jacobi ADMM with step sizes that exploit sparsity is valuable. The lack of real parallel experiments and limited baselines, however, reduces the immediate practical impact.

Detailed suggestions:
- Experiments: (i) Include multi-threaded or distributed implementations to demonstrate wall-clock parallel speedups; report scalability with cores and communication. (ii) Add larger real datasets for RPCA/overlapping group lasso. (iii) Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., linearized ADMM, recent proximal multi-block ADMMs with convergence guarantees), and include non-ADMM solvers (e.g., specialized proximal gradient where applicable) with careful tuning and stopping criteria. (iv) Report sensitivity to K and show how to pick K under a fixed time budget.
- Parameters: Provide a practical recipe for τi, νi, ηjt selection (including when di is heterogeneous), and discuss adaptivity (e.g., increase τi when residuals decrease, or backtrack when divergence is detected). Clarify when νi=0 is safe in inexact mode.
- Theory: Move key definitions of P^t, Q, βi, γi, ζi to the main text; add intuition for why the potential decreases. If possible, provide non-ergodic convergence (common in recent ADMM analyses) and high-probability bounds for randomized selection.
- Asynchrony: Discuss (or analyze) asynchronous PDMM under bounded staleness; even a brief remark on challenges would be useful.
- Overlapping groups: Discuss the memory/computation overhead of variable splitting and how PDMM compares to direct proximal mapping schemes when available; quantify for your settings.
- Clarity/typos: Fix grammatical errors; unify notation (Acj vs. Aj), and ensure all symbols are defined upon first use.

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated, novel algorithmic mechanism (dual backward step with sparsity-aware step sizes).
- Solid convergence theory for randomized parallel multi-block ADMM.
- Unifying perspective connecting sADMM and PJADMM.
- Promising empirical results and a useful application to overlapping group lasso.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited and sequential experiments; no evidence of true parallel scalability.
- Missing comparisons to some competitive baselines; some tuning choices may bias results.
- Some theoretical details deferred, hurting readability.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea is interesting and backed by theory; with improved experimental validation and clearer theoretical exposition, the impact would be higher. The paper would benefit the community by clarifying when and how to safely run Jacobi-style multi-block ADMM in parallel.
