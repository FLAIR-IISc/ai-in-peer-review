**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The key ideas are: (1) randomly selecting a subset of `K` primal variable blocks to update in parallel at each iteration; (2) introducing a "backward step" on the dual variable to stabilize the algorithm, which is a known issue for naive parallel (Jacobi) ADMM; and (3) proposing adaptive step sizes for the dual update that depend on the number of selected blocks `K` and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate in expectation. They argue that PDMM provides a unified framework that includes existing methods like splitting-based ADMM (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) as special cases. The method is evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to related methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of effectively parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems, which is a well-known bottleneck where direct extensions often fail to converge.
2.  **Novel Framework:** The proposed PDMM framework is flexible and interesting. The idea of incorporating the degree of parallelism (`K`) and problem structure (sparsity `di`) into the step-size selection is a novel contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within this single framework offers valuable theoretical insight.
3.  **Application to Overlapping Groups:** The application of PDMM to the overlapping group lasso problem is a strong point. The authors clearly demonstrate how the block-sparsity of the reformulated problem leads to more aggressive step sizes and superior empirical performance compared to sparsity-agnostic methods like sADMM.
4.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a formal convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate for a randomized parallel method with constant step sizes, which is on par with standard ADMM guarantees.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments**

Despite the promising ideas, the paper suffers from a significant number of technical inconsistencies, lack of clarity in crucial areas, and formatting errors that severely undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Major Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper is plagued by contradictions in its core technical details, particularly regarding the step-size parameters `Î½i` and `Ï„i`.
    *   In the description of PDMM (Eq. 8), the dual backward step parameter is given as `Î½i = 1 - KÌƒi`. This is mathematically nonsensical as `KÌƒi` can be greater than 1, leading to a negative `Î½i`, which contradicts the stated constraint `0 â‰¤ Î½i < 1`.
    *   The theorems (Theorem 1 and 2) state that the analysis holds for `Î½i = 1 - 1/KÌƒi`. This is a different formula from Eq. (8).
    *   The connection to sADMM (Section 2.2) claims it is a special case with `Î½i = 1 - 1/J`. This is yet another distinct formula.
    *   In the RPCA experiment (Figure 1 caption), the reported `Î½i` for PDMM3 is `1/3`, which matches none of the formulas provided in the text for the given parameters (`J=3, K=3`). The correct value according to the theorem would be `Î½i = 1 - 1/3 = 2/3`.
    These inconsistencies regarding a central parameter of the proposed algorithm are a critical flaw and make it impossible to trust the technical presentation and analysis.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Missing Details:**
    *   The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is opaque. The definitions of the residual `R(x^{t+1})` and the Lyapunov function `h(v*, v^t)` rely on matrices `Pt` and `Q` which are only described as "some positive semi-definite matrix" without explicit definition. This is unacceptable and makes the theoretical claims unverifiable from the main text.
    *   The motivation for the dual backward step is purely intuitive ("implicit dual ascent... may lead to too aggressive progress"). A more formal justification would strengthen the paper. The presentation of the `yÌ‚t` update is also confusing, as it is not made explicit how `yÌ‚t` is defined at iteration `t` based on the update rule for `yÌ‚t+1`.
    *   The paper repeatedly refers to an external ArXiv preprint ([25]) for crucial details, such as the proofs showing sADMM and PJADMM are special cases. A conference paper must be self-contained, and outsourcing core derivations to a non-peer-reviewed manuscript is poor practice.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation Concerns:**
    *   The comparison against sADMM is questionable. The authors state they used a publicly available code where "Ï„i = 0", which does not correspond to the sADMM algorithm. A fair comparison would require a correct implementation.
    *   In Table 1, the paper reports results using tuned parameters (e.g., `(Ï„i, Î½i) = (1/2, 1/2)` for PDMM3) that are markedly different from the theoretically derived ones. While tuning is common, the complete absence of discussion about why these tuned values perform better and what this implies for the theory is a significant omission. It weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical analysis.

4.  **Minor Errors and Formatting:** The paper contains numerous typos and formatting issues that detract from its professionalism. Examples include: "block-seperable" (Abstract & Sec 1), "defiend" (Sec 2.1), inconsistent use of "RBSUMM" and "RBUSMM" (Sec 2.2), and imprecise citation styles. **Recommendation**

While the paper presents a promising research direction for parallelizing ADMM, the execution is critically flawed. The sheer number of technical inconsistencies, particularly in the core formulas defining the algorithm, combined with the lack of clarity in the theoretical section and the reliance on an external manuscript for key results, renders the work unsuitable for publication in its current form. The experimental analysis also has weaknesses that call the comparisons into question.

Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong underlying idea, but the paper requires a complete and meticulous overhaul to correct the numerous errors, clarify the theoretical underpinnings, and provide a fair and transparent empirical evaluation before it can be considered a sound and valuable contribution to the field.
