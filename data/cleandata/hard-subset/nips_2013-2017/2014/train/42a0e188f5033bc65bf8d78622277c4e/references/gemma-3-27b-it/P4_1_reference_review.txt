This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems, termed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The method extends ADMM to handle multiple blocks in a parallel fashion, aiming to improve both numerical efficiency and convergence behavior. The authors introduce a backward step on the dual update and consider the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomized blocks when determining the step size. They establish convergence guarantees and demonstrate performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in solving large-scale optimization problems arising in machine learning. The introduction clearly outlines the problem setting and connects it to relevant applications. The authors appropriately position PDMM within the existing landscape of ADMM variants, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the contributions of their approach. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with convergence results established under reasonable assumptions. The connections drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM are insightful and enhance understanding of these methods. The experimental evaluation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provides empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of PDMM.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is clearly defined, and its relevance to various machine learning applications is well-motivated.
*   **Novelty:** PDMM introduces a novel combination of features, including the backward step on the dual update and the consideration of sparsity in step size determination. While building on ADMM, the proposed method offers distinct advantages in terms of parallelization and convergence.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous and provides valuable theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm. The connection to existing ADMM variants through PDMM offers deeper theoretical insight.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM, comparing favorably to existing methods.
*   **Well-written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The core idea of randomized block coordinate descent is not entirely new (see RBCD [16, 18] mentioned in the introduction). While PDMM extends this idea to the augmented Lagrangian setting with specific enhancements, the fundamental principle is not groundbreaking.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't extensively address the sensitivity of PDMM's performance to the choice of parameters, particularly K (the number of blocks to update). More detailed analysis or guidelines for parameter selection would be beneficial. While the theoretical analysis provides insights, practical advice for implementation is lacking.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to existing methods is not exhaustive. Including comparisons to more recent or highly optimized solvers for RPCA and overlapping group lasso would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses on theoretical and algorithmic aspects. Discussing practical implementation details, such as the efficient handling of sparse matrices or parallelization strategies, would increase the paper’s relevance to practitioners.
*   **Overly dense notation:** Some of the notation, particularly in the theoretical sections, is quite dense and could be simplified for better readability.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of ADMM with clear theoretical guarantees.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM on relevant benchmark problems.
*   The paper provides valuable insights into the behavior of ADMM variants and their connections.
*   The work addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization, with potential impact on various machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea of randomized block coordinate descent is not entirely new, limiting the overall novelty.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art solvers is not exhaustive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization. While the core idea isn't revolutionary, the proposed method offers a valuable extension of ADMM with practical benefits in terms of parallelization and convergence. The rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation strengthen the paper's credibility. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh the drawbacks, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors expand the discussion on parameter tuning, provide more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art solvers, and consider simplifying some of the dense notation for improved readability.
