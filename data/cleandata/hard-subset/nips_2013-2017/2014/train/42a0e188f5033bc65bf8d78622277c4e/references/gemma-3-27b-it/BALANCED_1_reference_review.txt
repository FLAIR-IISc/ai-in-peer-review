This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to extend ADMM by allowing the parallel update of a randomized subset of blocks, aiming to improve efficiency and convergence compared to existing methods like Gauss-Seidel ADMM and Jacobian variants. The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate the performance of PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant and challenging problem in optimization, particularly in the context of large-scale machine learning applications. The increasing interest in structural sparsity and the limitations of existing block coordinate descent methods provide a strong rationale for the proposed work. The connection to existing ADMM variants (sADMM and PJADMM) is insightful and provides a better understanding of the dual backward step. The theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees and iteration complexity, is a significant strength. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provide empirical support for the effectiveness of PDMM.  The inclusion of detailed proofs in the supplement is also appreciated.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, especially for readers unfamiliar with the ADMM literature. For example, the definitions of various sets and indices (like *N(i)*, *K̃i*) are introduced without clear explanations of their purpose or intuitive meaning within the algorithm. A more gradual introduction of notation, with accompanying explanations, would be helpful.
* **Experimental Setup Details:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail regarding the implementation and parameter tuning. While the authors mention choosing parameters based on certain criteria, the specific values used for all experiments (learning rates, regularization parameters, etc.) should be clearly stated for reproducibility.  A comparison of runtime *with* parallelization explicitly stated would also strengthen the claims.  Simply stating "PDMM can run in parallel" doesn't illustrate the actual benefits.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to other methods, while present, isn't comprehensive enough. The paper mentions "the best" results for some methods, but doesn't provide the parameter tuning details used to achieve those results. A fair comparison requires a consistent effort to optimize the performance of all methods. There's limited discussion of the computational cost of each method; beyond runtime, memory usage might be important.
* **Overlapping Block Lasso Discussion:** The section on overlapping group lasso is somewhat disconnected from the main narrative.  It states that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks well, but the explanation feels more like a consequence of the general framework than a novel contribution specifically tailored to this problem. More insights into how PDMM specifically benefits overlapping group lasso compared to other methods would be beneficial.
* **Impact of K:** The discussion on the effect of K (number of randomly chosen blocks) could be more nuanced. While the results show that performance isn't monotonic with K, a more detailed explanation of the trade-offs between iteration complexity and parallelization overhead would be valuable.  A visualization of the speedup achieved with different K values would be particularly helpful.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** Assumption 1, while standard, could be briefly discussed in the context of its limitations. Are there classes of problems where this assumption might not hold?



**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, including convergence analysis and iteration complexity results.
* **Novelty:** The introduction of the randomized block update strategy and the connection to existing ADMM variants are novel contributions.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application of PDMM to RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates its practical potential in relevant machine learning tasks.
* **Clear Structure:** The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a class of optimization problems that are important in machine learning.
* The convergence analysis and iteration complexity results are significant contributions.
* The experimental results provide evidence of the effectiveness of PDMM.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from some clarity issues in notation and experimental setup.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.
* The discussion of the overlapping block lasso application is somewhat limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization. While there are some areas for improvement, the theoretical rigor, novelty, and practical relevance of the work make it worthy of consideration. I would lean towards **acceptance, with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should address the clarity issues, provide more detailed experimental setup information, and expand the comparison to state-of-the-art methods.  Strengthening the discussion of the overlapping group lasso application would also be beneficial.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
