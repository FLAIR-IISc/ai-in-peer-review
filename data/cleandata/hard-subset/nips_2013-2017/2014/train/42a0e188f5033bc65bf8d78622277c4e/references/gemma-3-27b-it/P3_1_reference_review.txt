## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of extending ADMM to multi-block scenarios, specifically the potential for convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates. PDMM tackles this by randomly selecting a subset of blocks to update in parallel, incorporating a “backward step” on the dual update for conservative progress, and carefully tuning step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix *A* and the number of randomized blocks. The paper establishes theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrates the method’s performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, showing its competitiveness with existing ADMM-based techniques. Notably, the authors highlight connections between PDMM and other methods like sADMM and PJADMM, framing them as special cases within the PDMM framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant and important problem:** Scaling ADMM to multi-block problems is a key challenge. This work offers a practical and theoretically sound approach to tackle this.
* **Novelty:**  The combination of randomized block updates, the backward step on the dual variable, and the adaptive step size selection based on problem structure (sparsity) are novel contributions.  The unification of sADMM and PJADMM under the PDMM framework provides a valuable insight.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence analysis with an O(1/T) iteration complexity, which is a strong theoretical result. The assumptions are standard and reasonable.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM compared to existing methods.  The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate advantages in specific settings.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical notation is consistent.  The explanation of the connection to sADMM and PJADMM is especially helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper provides a recipe for choosing τ and ν, the optimal performance of PDMM likely depends on careful tuning of these parameters, and potentially the number of blocks *K*.  The sensitivity to these parameters isn’t thoroughly explored in the experiments.
* **Computational Overhead of Random Block Selection:** The paper doesn’t delve into the overhead of randomly selecting blocks. While the intent is parallelism, the random selection itself introduces a computational cost, especially for very large problems. A discussion on the trade-offs is missing.
* **Limited Comparison Scope:** The comparison to other methods (GSADMM, RBSUMM, sADMM) feels somewhat incomplete.  The choice of parameters for the compared methods isn't fully justified or explained, and some results (e.g., RBSUMM failing to converge) are mentioned without a deeper investigation.
* **The role of Bregman divergence feels slightly tacked on:** While its inclusion allows for a more unified theoretical treatment, the practical impact of using a Bregman divergence isn’t strongly emphasized in the experiments.  It’s unclear how the choice of Bregman divergence affects performance.
* **Lack of scalability demonstration:** While the problems tested are of reasonable size, the paper doesn't explicitly demonstrate the scalability of PDMM to truly large-scale problems, which is a key motivation for using randomized methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, specifically concerning ADMM and its extension to multi-block problems. The theoretical results are solid, the method is well-motivated, and the experimental validation is encouraging. The novelty lies in the thoughtful combination of techniques to address the challenges of multi-block ADMM.  While some areas could be strengthened (parameter sensitivity, computational overhead, more extensive comparisons), the overall quality of the work is high.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound method.
* Demonstrates competitive performance in practical applications.
* Provides a valuable unification of existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM).
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*  Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity.
*  Lack of detailed analysis of the computational cost of random block selection.
*  Comparisons to other methods could be more comprehensive.
*  Scalability to truly large-scale problems isn’t demonstrated.




**Rating:**  **Accept (with minor revisions)**.  I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and computational overhead, and a more thorough comparison to competing methods.  Specifically, adding a scalability experiment with a larger dataset would significantly strengthen the paper.
