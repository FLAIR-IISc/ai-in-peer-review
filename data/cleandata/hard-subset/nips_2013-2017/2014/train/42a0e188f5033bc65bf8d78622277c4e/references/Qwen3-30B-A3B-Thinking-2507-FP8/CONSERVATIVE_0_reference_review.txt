

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors claim PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, handles overlapping group lasso problems (which they claim are difficult for existing methods), and provides global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity. The paper establishes theoretical results and evaluates PDMM on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper has several strengths. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear convergence proofs and complexity analysis. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is well-explained and provides useful insights into the dual backward step. The experimental results are generally consistent with the theoretical claims, showing PDMM outperforms ADMM variants on the tested problems. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty, experimental validation, and claims. The central claim that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" is questionable. Overlapping group lasso has been extensively studied in the literature (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Jenatton et al., 2011), and there exist multiple methods for solving it, including those using block coordinate descent. The authors' claim that existing methods cannot handle overlapping blocks in a randomized block coordinate fashion is not supported by sufficient evidence.

The experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous. The paper compares PDMM only to ADMM variants (sADMM, GSADMM) and RBSUMM, but does not compare with state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for overlapping group lasso (e.g., the methods by Jenatton et al. or Zhao et al.). The performance gains shown in Figure 1 and Figure 2 appear marginal (e.g., PDMM1 achieves the lowest residual but requires more iterations than PDMM3), and the paper fails to explain why PDMM would be preferable in practice over simpler alternatives.

The paper's theoretical contribution is incremental rather than transformative. While the connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is interesting, it's not clear that this provides substantial new insights beyond what's already known about ADMM variants. The step size selection rule (τi and νi) is derived from the paper's assumptions but lacks justification for why these specific values are optimal or necessary.

The paper makes a strong claim that PDMM "can solve a fairly large class of problems, including nonsmooth functions," but fails to provide evidence of this on challenging problems beyond the two benchmark applications. The paper also doesn't adequately address the limitations of PDMM, such as when the sparsity structure of A is not favorable for the step size selection.

The main weakness is that the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that PDMM offers significant advantages over existing methods for practical applications. The theoretical contribution is modest, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the paper's claims about solving an "open problem" are questionable given the existing literature.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical analysis with convergence proofs
- Clear connection to existing ADMM variants
- Well-written and organized paper
- Validated on two standard optimization problems

Cons for acceptance:
- Overstated novelty claims about solving "open problems" with overlapping groups
- Insufficient experimental validation against state-of-the-art methods
- Marginal performance improvements in experiments
- Lacks thorough discussion of limitations and comparison with relevant prior work

While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the ADMM literature, the incremental nature of the method and insufficient validation against established baselines make it difficult to justify as a significant contribution for NIPS. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive experiments against relevant methods for overlapping group lasso, a clearer justification for why PDMM provides advantages over existing approaches, and a more measured assessment of the novelty of the proposed method.

I recommend rejection. The paper presents a minor extension to an already well-studied area without providing sufficient evidence of significant improvement over existing methods. The claims about solving an "open problem" are unsubstantiated, and the experimental results don't demonstrate clear practical advantages. For NIPS, we should prioritize papers that make substantial advances in methodology or demonstrate clear, significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches.
