Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized, parallel, block-coordinate variant of ADMM tailored for multi-block settings. The key idea is to address the known instability of parallel (Jacobi) multi-block ADMM by combining (i) a “dual backward step,” which tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term, with (ii) step sizes that depend on both the sparsity pattern of A and the number K of blocks updated per iteration, and (iii) optional Bregman-proximal terms enabling inexact updates with closed forms.

The method generalizes and unifies prior approaches. It recovers sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) as special cases via specific choices of τi and νi. It is distinct from GS-ADMM/BSUMM-type methods that are essentially Gauss-Seidel and may need restrictive assumptions or small/decreasing steps, and from RBSUMM which randomly updates one block but requires local error bounds and has no iteration complexity guarantees. PDMM proves global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions, with step sizes τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i where K̃i = min{di, K} and di is the number of nonzero blocks in row i of A. The paper demonstrates applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso, including the overlapping case reformulated as a constrained problem with di = 2, which permits larger dual steps.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: direct Jacobi multi-block ADMM can diverge; PDMM introduces a principled dual backward step to stabilize parallel randomized updates.
- Theoretical guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity/KKT existence, without local error bound assumptions. Step sizes explicitly leverage A’s sparsity and K.
- Unifying perspective: shows sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of the same framework, clarifying when small vs. large dual steps are appropriate relative to primal progress.
- Practical flexibility: supports exact and inexact (Bregman-prox) subproblem updates; can handle overlapping groups via a standard splitting.
- Empirical evidence: on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants compare favorably to RBSUMM and sADMM; the dependence on K is explored.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/notation: there are numerous typos and inconsistencies (e.g., Acj vs Aj, indices jt, ambiguity around βi, definition of x̄T as a sum instead of an average, “1/J 0.5,” missing division signs). A concise algorithm box and unified notation would help.
- Omitted details: matrices Pt and Q are invoked but not defined in the main text; core proof ingredients are deferred to the supplement, limiting readability.
- Experimental scope: all experiments are sequential Matlab; claims about parallel speedups are not empirically validated on multi-core/cluster hardware. Only two applications are shown, with relatively limited ablations beyond K.
- Baseline configuration fairness: several baselines involve manual tuning and in one case sADMM’s implementation lacks a dual update; a careful, matched implementation and parameter selection would strengthen conclusions.
- Assumptions for inexact updates: the main text briefly mentions conditions (strong convexity of ϕjt and Lipschitz gradient of ψjt) but does not make explicit the exact requirements for all choices used in experiments.

Quality
The algorithmic design is coherent and the analysis is plausible and in line with contemporary results on randomized block methods and ADMM. The proposed step sizes are well-motivated by the “implicit dual ascent” interpretation and by sparsity considerations. The convergence statements (global convergence and O(1/T)) are standard targets for convex ADMM variants. However, more detail on the Lyapunov function h(·,·), definitions of Pt and Q, and the derivation of βi would improve verifiability from the main text. Experimental comparisons are informative but not comprehensive; parallel performance is not demonstrated.

Clarity
The presentation conveys the main ideas but suffers from typographical errors, inconsistent symbols, and occasional ambiguities that hinder comprehension. Suggestions:
- Provide a self-contained algorithm box specifying inputs, sampling of Jt, exact updates, and parameter defaults.
- Unify notation for A’s blocks and consistently use either Aj or Acj; define all indices (i, j, jt) once.
- Clearly define Pt, Q, βi, K̃i in the main text; ensure x̄T = (1/T)∑t xt.
- Tighten the prose in Sections 2–3; move motivation into a concise subsection and reduce repetition.
- Ensure all baselines are correctly implemented (including dual updates) and described.

Originality
The dual backward step for stabilizing parallel randomized multi-block updates, with explicit τi, νi depending on sparsity and K, appears novel relative to sADMM, PJADMM, BSUMM/RBSUMM. Establishing both the unifying perspective and O(1/T) rate under constant steps without local error bounds is a meaningful advance. The overlapping-group application via PDMM is a sensible extension rather than a conceptual novelty.

Significance
Multi-block ADMM remains important in large-scale ML and distributed optimization. A parallelizable, randomized method with provable convergence and rates, that recovers known methods as special cases and exploits sparsity for larger steps, is practically useful. The direct empirical speedups over strong baselines in realistic parallel environments would increase impact. As is, the work advances understanding and provides a tool likely to be adopted by researchers/practitioners dealing with constrained, block-structured problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution (global convergence, O(1/T)) with insightful parameterization tied to sparsity and block count.
- Unification of sADMM and PJADMM within a single framework; clarifies roles of dual step sizes.
- Practical algorithm supporting exact/inexact updates; handles overlapping group structures.
- Empirical improvements over RBSUMM/sADMM; systematic study of K.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation issues (notation/typos) and reliance on supplement for core definitions hinder readability.
- Lack of parallel/distributed experiments; comparisons partly confounded by implementation differences/tuning.
- Limited empirical breadth; no nonconvex or larger-scale datasets; no communication-cost analysis.
- Assumptions and parameter choices for inexact PDMM could be spelled out more precisely in main text.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Define Pt, Q, and βi in the main paper and clarify any ambiguity in their constants.
- Provide a non-asymptotic bound with explicit constants to make the O(1/T) guarantee actionable.
- Include a true parallel implementation (multi-core or distributed) and report wall-clock speedups and communication overheads.
- Ensure baseline implementations include proper dual updates and matched stopping criteria; add more recent multi-block ADMM baselines if available.
- Add an algorithm box, unify notation, and fix typographical errors; clarify that x̄T is an average.

Overall, the contribution is technically sound and novel enough for NIPS, provided the clarity issues are addressed and, if possible, parallel experiments are added to substantiate the practical benefits.
