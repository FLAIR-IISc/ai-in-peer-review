This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method of multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The method extends ADMM to multiple blocks and aims to improve convergence and efficiency, particularly for large-scale problems. The authors establish convergence guarantees and demonstrate performance through experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

The paper clearly introduces the problem setting and motivates the development of PDMM by discussing limitations of existing approaches like dual ascent and standard ADMM variants. The connection to existing methods, specifically sADMM and PJADMM, is well-articulated, providing valuable insight into the landscape of ADMM-based algorithms. The theoretical analysis, including convergence results and iteration complexity, is a significant strength. The derivation of step sizes based on sparsity and number of blocks is a novel contribution. The experiments showcase the effectiveness of PDMM in practical applications and demonstrate its advantages over other methods.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the notation is dense and occasionally difficult to follow, especially in the mathematical derivations. The explanation of the “backward step” on the dual update could benefit from more intuitive justification.  The experimental setup, while demonstrating good performance, lacks a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art solvers beyond ADMM variants. A comparison to more recent and highly optimized solvers would strengthen the conclusions. The benefits of different K values are shown, but the practical guidance on how to choose K for a given problem remains limited. The impact of the Bregman divergence term is mentioned, but its practical implications and parameter tuning are not thoroughly explored. The discussion of the convergence rate (O(1/T)) doesn't offer substantial insight compared to standard ADMM results without considering practical implications. The paper relies heavily on references to a supplementary document, which hinders accessibility and immediate understanding of the technical details. Critical details are relegated to the supplement, making independent verification of the results challenging.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The PDMM algorithm introduces a novel approach to parallelizing ADMM, incorporating sparsity awareness and a backward step on the dual update. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides a useful unifying framework.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of PDMM, establishing convergence guarantees and iteration complexity bounds.
*   **Clarity (moderate):** The paper's introduction and motivation are clear. However, the mathematical derivations can be dense and challenging.
*   **Significance:**  The potential for improved scalability and efficiency in solving large-scale optimization problems, particularly in machine learning applications, is significant.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity (moderate):** The notation and mathematical derivations can be difficult to follow.
*   **Completeness:** Crucial details are relegated to the supplement, hindering independent verification.
*   **Experimental Thoroughness:** The experimental evaluation lacks a comprehensive comparison to current state-of-the-art solvers.
*   **Practical Guidance:** Limited practical guidance on parameter tuning (e.g., choosing K) is provided.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems. The theoretical contributions are strong, and the proposed algorithm demonstrates promising performance. However, the clarity and completeness could be improved.  Given the potential impact, I would lean towards **Accept**, but with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the clarity issues, expand the experimental comparison, and provide more practical guidance for applying the algorithm.  The reliance on the supplement should be minimized by incorporating essential details into the main manuscript.
