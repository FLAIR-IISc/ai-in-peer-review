

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors establish that PDMM generalizes existing methods: sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, and the algorithm handles overlapping group structures for which randomized block coordinate descent (RBCD) remains an open problem. The paper provides theoretical convergence results (global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size) and evaluates PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

The paper builds on the growing literature of ADMM variants for multi-block problems, particularly addressing the limitations of both Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM) and Jacobian ADMM (JADMM) approaches. It distinguishes itself by introducing a dual backward step and a systematic way to determine step sizes based on block sparsity and the number of randomly selected blocks.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a unified framework that connects several existing ADMM variants (sADMM, PJADMM) through PDMM, offering better theoretical understanding of the dual backward step and step size parameters. The global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity results with constant step size are significant contributions to the field.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Insight**: The paper's key insight that the dual backward step can be systematically determined based on block sparsity and the number of blocks being updated is valuable. The connection between τi, νi and the sparsity pattern of A is particularly insightful.

3. **Relevant Application**: The application to overlapping group lasso is well-motivated and addresses a concrete open problem (randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks), which is a meaningful contribution to the machine learning community.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear organization and good notation. The connections to related work are thoroughly explained, and the theoretical results are presented in a logical manner.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Experimental Evaluation**: The experimental section is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The comparisons with existing methods (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) are limited to two applications with only a few parameter settings. More comprehensive experiments comparing with state-of-the-art methods in both applications would strengthen the paper significantly.

2. **Lack of Scalability Analysis**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of computational complexity or scalability with problem size. The runtime comparisons are limited to a single setting without discussing how the algorithm scales with increasing problem dimensions.

3. **Comparison with RBSUMM**: The paper claims PDMM outperforms RBSUMM but doesn't clearly explain why the convergence behavior differs. Given that RBSUMM also updates blocks randomly (though only one block at a time), a more thorough comparison of the theoretical guarantees would be valuable.

4. **Missing Practical Considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss practical considerations for implementing PDMM, such as how to handle non-convex constraints or how to tune parameters for different problem structures beyond the specific examples.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound with correct theoretical results, but the empirical validation is limited. The convergence proofs appear correct based on the presented theorems, but the experimental section lacks sufficient comparison with the state-of-the-art. The paper would benefit from more thorough empirical evaluation.

## Clarity Assessment
The paper is well-written with clear organization and good notation. The key concepts are explained well, particularly the connection between PDMM and existing methods. The algorithm description is precise and easy to follow.

## Originality Assessment
The paper makes a meaningful contribution by unifying existing methods under a single framework and providing a systematic approach to determining step sizes. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that RBSUMM (which updates blocks randomly) is mentioned but not thoroughly contrasted with the proposed method. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM is novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how PDMM differs from other randomized block coordinate methods.

## Significance Assessment
The problem addressed (parallel optimization for large-scale machine learning) is highly significant. The paper's contribution to understanding the dual backward step and providing a unified framework is valuable for the field. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly relevant as it addresses a known open problem. However, the significance would be stronger with more comprehensive experimental validation.

## Recommendation
The paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm that makes a meaningful contribution to the ADMM literature. While the theoretical results are strong, the experimental evaluation is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The paper would be significantly strengthened with more comprehensive experiments comparing PDMM with state-of-the-art methods across multiple problem sizes and structures.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Expand the experimental section to include more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in both robust PCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

2. Add a scalability analysis showing how PDMM's runtime and convergence behavior change with problem size.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with RBSUMM, explaining why PDMM converges faster despite both being randomized block coordinate methods.

4. Discuss practical considerations for implementing PDMM, including how to choose parameters for different problem structures.

5. Include a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and how it compares to existing methods.

Overall, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution but needs more substantial empirical validation to meet NIPS standards. With the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
