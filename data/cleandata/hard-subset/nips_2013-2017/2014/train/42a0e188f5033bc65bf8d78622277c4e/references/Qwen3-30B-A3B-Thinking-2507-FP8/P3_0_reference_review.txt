

# Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

This paper proposes a novel parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors make a clear contribution by unifying existing Jacobian ADMM methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases of PDMM and establishing the theoretical foundations for a more general algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size. The analysis of the dual backward step is particularly insightful, explaining why direct Jacobi updates fail and how PDMM's backward step provides conservative dual progress.

2. **Novel Connections:** The paper's key insight is showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified framework that clarifies the relationship between these methods. This theoretical connection is valuable for understanding the design space of ADMM variants.

3. **Handling Overlapping Blocks:** The paper demonstrates how PDMM can solve overlapping group problems in a randomized block coordinate fashion, addressing an open problem in the literature (RBCD with overlapping blocks). This application is well-motivated and shows practical relevance.

4. **Practical Implementation:** The paper provides clear guidance on setting step sizes based on problem sparsity and number of randomized blocks, which is valuable for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are limited to two problems (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) and only compare against a few methods. The comparison with sADMM is unfair since the authors note that the public code for sADMM has a bug (τi = 0 instead of τi = 1/3). The paper claims PDMM is faster but doesn't convincingly demonstrate this across multiple problem instances or larger-scale experiments.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on choosing the number of blocks K in practice. While Figure 2 shows that K = 20 gives good performance, it's unclear how to select K for new problems. The paper states that "the number of blocks in PDMM depends on the trade-off between the number of subproblems and how efficiently each subproblem can be solved," but doesn't provide concrete criteria for this trade-off.

3. **Communication Complexity:** As a parallel method, the paper should address communication overhead in distributed settings, which is crucial for understanding the practical benefits of parallelism. The paper doesn't discuss how the parallel updates affect communication costs.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper references recent work on randomized ADMM (like RBSUMM), but doesn't thoroughly distinguish its contributions from these methods. The claim that "PDMM can also be used to solve overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion" is interesting but needs stronger justification for why this is novel.

**Critical Assessment:**

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the ADMM literature by establishing a unified framework for Jacobian ADMM variants. The convergence analysis is rigorous and the connection between existing methods is insightful. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claim that PDMM is "faster than existing methods."

The paper's most significant contribution is the theoretical unification of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM. This provides better understanding of the role of the dual backward step, which is a substantive contribution to the optimization literature. The extension to overlapping blocks is also a meaningful application of the proposed framework.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More comprehensive experiments on multiple problem types
2. Clearer guidance on selecting K for practical applications
3. A discussion of communication complexity in distributed settings
4. A more thorough comparison with the most recent randomized ADMM methods

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical contributions, but the experimental evaluation doesn't fully support the practical claims. The paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the ADMM literature by unifying existing methods and establishing convergence guarantees, which would be valuable for the NIPS community. However, the experimental results don't convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of PDMM over existing methods, particularly when considering the communication overhead of parallel updates.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of ADMM variants. The main weakness is the limited experimental evaluation that doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. With more comprehensive experiments and clearer guidance on parameter selection, this paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions (addressing experimental limitations and parameter selection guidance). The theoretical contribution is significant and the paper would be valuable to the optimization community, but the experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened to fully support the practical claims.
