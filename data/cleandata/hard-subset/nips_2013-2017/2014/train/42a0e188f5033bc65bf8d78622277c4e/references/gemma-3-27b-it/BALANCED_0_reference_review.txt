This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, a problem frequently encountered in machine learning applications like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors address limitations of existing ADMM variants, particularly their convergence issues with multiple blocks and lack of efficient parallelization. PDMM aims to improve upon these by randomly selecting and updating blocks in parallel, incorporating a backward step on the dual update, and considering sparsity patterns.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges with existing approaches and positioning PDMM as a promising alternative. The connection drawn between PDMM and existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM is valuable, offering a broader understanding of the algorithm's behavior and parameters. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, is a strong point, though the numerous assumptions require careful consideration (see weaknesses). The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's competitive performance against other ADMM variants and relevant algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Problem Formulation:** The paper effectively frames the problem and highlights the shortcomings of existing solutions.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed PDMM introduces a randomized block coordinate approach with a dual backward step and sparsity consideration, representing a potentially valuable contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides convergence guarantees and iteration complexity bounds, demonstrating a strong theoretical foundation.  Establishing the connection to sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM adds to the theoretical depth.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show promising results, indicating PDMM's practical effectiveness.
*   **Well-written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and easy to follow, although some sections (e.g., the detailed mathematical derivations) are dense.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption Heavy:** The convergence analysis relies on several assumptions (Assumption 1 and others implicit within the theorem proofs), which might be restrictive in practice. It’s unclear how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to violations of these assumptions.  The authors should discuss this limitation.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides formulas for τi and νi, the optimal choice of K (number of randomly selected blocks) seems highly problem-dependent. The experiments show a non-trivial relationship between K and performance. More guidance on how to choose K for different problem structures would be helpful.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two applications. Expanding the evaluation to a more diverse set of problems would strengthen the evidence for PDMM's generality. Comparisons with first-order methods without ADMM structure (e.g., FISTA) would also be informative.
*   **Implementation Details:**  The paper mentions implementation in MATLAB but lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Information on specific libraries used, parameter settings beyond τi and νi, and computational resources would be valuable.
*   **Overlapping Block Experiments:** While Section 2.3 describes the application to overlapping block problems, the experimental results are less detailed than those for RPCA. A more in-depth analysis of the performance gains specifically due to PDMM's handling of overlapping blocks would be useful.
*   **Mathematical Density:** While the mathematical notation is correct, the extensive use of symbols and derivations can be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with ADMM and related techniques.  More intuitive explanations could be incorporated alongside the equations.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm (PDMM) that addresses important challenges in solving block-separable convex optimization problems.
*   The connection to existing ADMM variants provides valuable insights and context.
*   The experimental results demonstrate PDMM's competitive performance in relevant applications.
*   The potential for parallelization makes PDMM attractive for large-scale problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the algorithm's practical applicability.
*   The limited experimental scope and lack of detailed implementation information hinder a thorough assessment of PDMM's robustness and generalizability.
*   The sensitivity to the choice of K and lack of guidance on its selection are concerning.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a worthwhile contribution with the potential to advance the field of large-scale optimization. However, the current manuscript requires some revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Discuss the limitations of the assumptions** made in the theoretical analysis and provide some guidance on how to assess their validity in practice.
2.  **Provide more guidance on choosing the parameter K** and explore its sensitivity in the experiments.
3.  **Expand the experimental evaluation** to include a more diverse set of problems and algorithms.
4.  **Provide more detailed implementation information** to enhance reproducibility.
5.  **Consider elaborating on the theoretical motivations** behind the choice of the step sizes τi and νi.

Given these concerns, my recommendation is **weak reject**.  I would be willing to reconsider a revised version that adequately addresses the identified weaknesses. The core idea is strong, and with some refinement, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
