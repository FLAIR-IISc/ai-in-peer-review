Summary
The paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex, block-separable objectives with linear constraints. The key ideas are: (i) a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term; (ii) step sizes for dual updates that depend on the sparsity pattern of A via di and on the number of updated blocks K; and (iii) randomized selection of an arbitrary number K of primal blocks to update in parallel, with optional Bregman terms enabling inexact updates. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity for specific choices of τi and νi, and show that sADMM and PJADMM arise as extreme cases. Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show promising performance.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing and practically relevant: a Jacobi-style, randomized, multi-block ADMM with principled step sizes that account for sparsity and degree di.
- The “dual backward step” insight usefully explains why naive Jacobi ADMM diverges and connects PDMM to sADMM/PJADMM.
- Convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate are established under assumptions comparable to ADMM, with constants reflecting K and sparsity.
- The overlapping group lasso formulation demonstrates how PDMM applies to overlapping structures; the di=2 case yields significantly larger admissible τi than sADMM.
- Experiments suggest PDMM can be competitive or faster than sADMM/GSADMM/RBSUMM in iteration/time, at least in the presented settings.

Weaknesses (technical and presentation)
- Heavy reliance on a long version: Several core claims (e.g., precise equivalence to sADMM/PJADMM, detailed derivations) are deferred to [25]/appendices that are not part of the submission. This undermines completeness.
- Inconsistent and incomplete definitions:
  - The Lyapunov and residual functions refer to Pt and Q as “some positive semidefinite matrices” without explicit definitions or construction; βi is introduced twice with ambiguous formatting (“βi = KJK̃i”). The formulas for γi, ζi in (21)-(22) appear garbled with missing fractions/spaces, making the bounds uninterpretable.
  - Bregman setup intermixes φ, ϕ, ψ with inconsistent notation; the exact assumptions (strong convexity/Lipschitz constants and how they scale with ηjt) are not stated as a formal assumption.
- Step-size rules are not consistently presented. The convergence theorems require τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i. However, Section 2.1 claims that with sufficiently large ηjt one can set τi = 1 and νi = 0 (PJADMM). It is unclear whether the stated convergence theorems still hold under these different τi, νi; the paper should clearly separate the regimes and provide the corresponding guarantees in the main text.
- Relationship to known ADMM variants is underdeveloped in the main paper: the dual backward step resembles over-relaxation/extragradient-type corrections known in multi-block ADMM; more direct comparisons to over-relaxed ADMM and primal-dual hybrid gradient (PDHG) methods (and randomized variants) would clarify novelty.
- Experiments:
  - All methods are run sequentially in Matlab although the main advantage claimed is parallelism; no wall-clock gains from actual parallel execution are demonstrated.
  - Parameter tuning is uneven: PDMM uses (8) in some experiments, but then τi, νi are further “tuned” to get “best” results; sADMM is run with τi=0 due to “public code” limitations, which is not a fair comparison if the theory suggests τi=1/J should be used.
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not included here; reproducibility is limited. Details such as ρ, stopping criteria, and hyperparameter grids are not systematically reported for all methods. Claims like “best results” without describing the search are problematic.
  - The RPCA objective is written with an unusual squared Frobenius term on X1; motivating this choice and sensitivity to γ2, γ3 are not discussed.
  - RBSUMM is reported as non-convergent within 5000 iterations for many settings, but the stepsize schedules and tested ranges are not fully enumerated; this weakens the empirical claim.
- Clarity/organization issues and many typos/formatting inconsistencies reduce readability and precision:
  - Frequent typos and grammar: “seperable”→“separable”, “defiend”, “addded”, “contast”, “convegence”, “an noisy”, “we call the method of multipliers” (missing article), “is defiend as”, etc.
  - Inconsistent notation: Acj vs A c j; Ajt vs Acjt; bold vs plain; z vs zr vs zri without clear, consistent dimensions; bL used to mean b×L; use of hats and superscripts not always explained; “sADMM1” undefined; “ρ 11√ t+10” likely a typesetting error.
  - Equations missing terms or with spacing that obscures fractions/signs; e.g., (21) γi expression; (23) has a leading “≤” without a left-hand term; some inner products/norms omit subscripts/spaces inconsistently.
  - References: many entries lack years/venues; author lists are corrupted (“E, S. Boyd, Chu N. Parikh”); multiple “Preprint” placeholders; missing or inconsistent bibliographic details. This is below NeurIPS standards.
  - No algorithm pseudocode; a concise algorithmic box with inputs (K, τi, νi, ηjt), sampling scheme, and updates would greatly aid reproducibility.
- Scope of theory: The O(1/T) rate is ergodic; non-ergodic rates and rates under stronger conditions (strong convexity, error bounds) are not discussed. Constants in bounds depend on unspecified Q, Pt, and on di, K; practical guidance on setting K to optimize runtime is mostly empirical.

Quality: The core idea is plausible and the analysis direction (Lyapunov decrease in expectation under random block sampling) is standard. However, the main text omits crucial definitions and relies on an external supplement for key derivations and special-case equivalences. Experimental methodology and fairness need strengthening.

Clarity: The paper is difficult to parse due to notation, formatting, and numerous typos. Important quantities are undefined in the main text.

Originality: Combining randomized Jacobi updates with a dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes is moderately novel within the ADMM literature, though related ideas exist (proximal Jacobian ADMM, over-relaxation/extragradient corrections, randomized primal-dual methods). Positioning relative to these is incomplete.

Significance: If polished and fairly compared on parallel hardware, the method could be impactful for distributed/parallel convex optimization with structured sparsity. As written, the contribution is promising but not yet convincing.

Questions/requests for authors
- Precisely define Pt, Q, βi, γi, ζi in the main paper and correct any formatting errors in (19)–(23).
- Clarify the admissible ranges of τi, νi beyond (8) and state theorems for the PJADMM regime (τi=1, νi=0 with ηjt>0) in the main text.
- Provide a self-contained proof sketch of Theorem 1’s key inequality to justify the dual backward step and sparsity-dependent constants.
- Add algorithm pseudocode and a clear description of the sampling scheme (uniform without replacement? grouped?) and how di is computed.
- Ensure fair baselines: run sADMM with appropriate τi; report parameter grids for all methods; include parallel experiments.
- Fix typos/notation and provide complete references.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant limitation of Jacobi multi-block ADMM.
- Clear conceptual link to sADMM and PJADMM.
- Convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with sparsity-aware constants.
- Demonstrates applicability to overlapping group problems.

Cons
- Incomplete/ambiguous theory presentation in main text; missing definitions.
- Inconsistent step-size story vs. theorems; reliance on external appendices.
- Experimental fairness and lack of parallel results.
- Numerous typos/formatting/reference issues; no pseudocode.

Recommendation: Reject (weak)
The ideas are interesting, but the paper, as submitted, contains too many clarity, rigor, and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. A substantially revised, self-contained version with corrected analysis, fair/parallel experiments, and polished writing could be competitive. Confidence: high.
