

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a new parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called PDMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors claim their method can update any number of blocks in parallel, incorporates a dual backward step for convergence, and adapts step sizes based on matrix sparsity and the number of blocks being updated. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, and shows connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM can be faster than some baselines.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a thorough theoretical foundation for PDMM. The connection to existing methods as special cases is a notable strength, as it situates the work within the broader ADMM literature. The theoretical analysis appears sound, with careful attention to the conditions required for convergence. The experimental setup is clear, and the results demonstrate that PDMM can be faster than some baselines in specific settings.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a compelling contribution to NIPS. The main concerns are:

1. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The paper only evaluates PDMM on two specific applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). This is insufficient to demonstrate the broad utility of the method. The paper claims PDMM is useful for "a fairly large class of problems," but provides no evidence beyond these two examples. Without testing on a wider range of problem types and scales, it's impossible to assess whether the method offers general advantages over existing approaches.

2. **Questionable practical significance**: The paper's main claim is that PDMM can be faster than existing methods, but the experimental results don't convincingly support this. In the RPCA experiment, PDMM3 (updating 3 blocks) performs better than other methods, but PDMM1 (updating 1 block) is actually the fastest algorithm. This undermines the paper's claim about the benefits of parallel updates. The paper doesn't demonstrate a clear advantage in terms of convergence rate that would justify the additional complexity of the method.

3. **Lack of differentiation from existing work**: The paper acknowledges that RBSUMM [14] also uses randomized block coordinate updates, but only updates one block at a time. The paper claims PDMM is different because it can update multiple blocks, but the theoretical connection to RBSUMM is not sufficiently developed. The paper also doesn't convincingly argue why PDMM is superior to simply applying established parallel methods (like sADMM or PJADMM) with standard parallelization techniques.

4. **Unjustified claims about theoretical novelty**: The paper claims to establish convergence with constant step size, but the authors themselves note in Remark 2 that PDMM reduces to the method of multipliers when J=K=1, which is a well-known method. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the theoretical results represent a significant advancement over existing ADMM convergence analysis.

5. **Limited practical guidance**: The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to choose the number of blocks K for real-world applications. The experiments show that K=1 can be fastest in some cases, but the paper doesn't explain why this might be the case or provide general principles for selecting K.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically sound method that connects to existing ADMM variants, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its novelty or practical significance. The limited experimental validation on two specific applications doesn't demonstrate the broad utility the authors claim. The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing ADMM variants rather than a substantial advancement in the field.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims about PDMM's advantages to be overstated. The paper doesn't provide enough evidence to convince me that PDMM offers significant benefits over well-established parallel ADMM methods. The experiments don't convincingly demonstrate that PDMM is faster than existing approaches in a meaningful way, and the theoretical results don't provide clear practical advantages.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More extensive experimental validation across a wider range of problem types and scales
2. Clearer demonstration of when and why PDMM outperforms existing methods
3. A more compelling argument for the practical significance of the theoretical results
4. Comparison with state-of-the-art parallel optimization methods beyond the limited set of baselines used

Given these concerns, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the evidence provided doesn't justify the claims made. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized optimization conference rather than NIPS, where the bar for novelty and significance is higher.
