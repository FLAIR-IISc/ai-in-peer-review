

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a new algorithm called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The problem is formulated as minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The paper addresses a key limitation of existing ADMM variants: direct Jacobi updates (updating all blocks in parallel) often fail to converge. The authors propose PDMM, which updates a random subset of blocks in parallel while introducing a dual backward step to address convergence issues. The paper establishes that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing new theoretical insight into these methods. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size, and demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM. The proof techniques build on established optimization theory while introducing novel elements like the dual backward step. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in the literature.

2. **Novelty**: PDMM introduces a randomized block coordinate approach that can update any number of blocks in parallel, unlike existing methods like RBSUMM (which updates only one block) or sADMM/PJADMM (which update all blocks). The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides new theoretical insight into these methods. The paper also demonstrates how PDMM can solve overlapping group problems, which is an open issue for randomized block coordinate methods.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The paper includes comprehensive experiments on two important machine learning problems (RPCA and overlapping group lasso), showing PDMM outperforms competing methods in terms of runtime while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation, motivation, and algorithm description are accessible to optimization researchers. The connections to previous work are well-explained, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Step Size Selection**: While the paper provides formulas for τi and νi (equation 8), the paper could better explain why these specific values are chosen. The connection to block sparsity (di) and number of blocks (K) is mentioned, but the intuition behind the specific formulas could be strengthened.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper demonstrates runtime improvements but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of PDMM relative to competing methods. A more thorough discussion of how PDMM scales with problem size would strengthen the practical relevance.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are limited to two applications. While these are important problems, a broader range of applications would better demonstrate the generality of the approach. The paper also doesn't compare with more recent ADMM variants beyond those mentioned in the introduction.

4. **Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the algorithm is to the choice of K (number of blocks to update) in practical scenarios. The experiments show that K=20 gives similar performance to K=101, but don't discuss how to choose K for other problem instances.

## Assessment

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis that is well-supported by experimental results. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The proofs are detailed and build on established optimization theory.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and the algorithm is described with sufficient detail. The connections to previous work are well-explained, making the paper accessible to optimization researchers.

Originality: The paper presents a genuinely novel algorithm that addresses a clear limitation in existing ADMM variants. The randomized block coordinate approach with the dual backward step is distinct from previous methods, and the theoretical connection between PDMM and existing methods provides new insights.

Significance: The proposed algorithm solves an important problem in optimization with practical implications for machine learning applications. The O(1/T) convergence rate is significant for optimization algorithms, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. The approach could be applied to a wide range of machine learning problems with linear constraints.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, the algorithm is novel and well-explained, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and don't detract from the overall quality of the work.

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It advances the state of the art in optimization algorithms with clear theoretical and practical contributions. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides deeper understanding of ADMM variants, and the algorithm's performance on important machine learning problems demonstrates its practical value.

Pro: Novel algorithm with rigorous theoretical guarantees, clear connections to existing methods, strong experimental validation on relevant machine learning problems.

Con: Limited discussion of step size intuition, need for more comprehensive computational complexity analysis, and broader experimental validation would strengthen the paper.
