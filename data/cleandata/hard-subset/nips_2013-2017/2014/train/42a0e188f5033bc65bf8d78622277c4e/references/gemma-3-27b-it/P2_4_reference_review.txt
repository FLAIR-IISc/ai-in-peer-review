## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of parallelization in ADMM for multi-block problems, arguing that direct Jacobi updates often fail to converge. PDMM tackles this by randomly selecting blocks for updates, incorporating a backward step on the dual update for conservative progress, and adapting step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomly chosen blocks. The paper establishes convergence guarantees and demonstrates the method's performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems. It also highlights connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, positioning PDMM as a unifying framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant and important problem:** Efficiently solving large-scale linearly constrained optimization problems is crucial in many machine learning applications.  Parallelizing ADMM is a key challenge, and this paper directly tackles it.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of randomized block coordinate descent with a backward step on the dual update and sparsity-aware step size selection is a novel contribution.  The theoretical justification for these design choices is convincing.
* **Theoretical guarantees:** Establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity is a significant achievement.  This provides a strong foundation for practical application.
* **Connections to existing work:**  Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM offers valuable insight into the relationships between these methods and provides a unifying perspective.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are promising, showing that PDMM performs competitively with and sometimes outperforms existing methods.  The exploration of the effect of the number of randomly selected blocks (K) is insightful.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty in the core idea:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel, randomized block coordinate descent itself is not new (references to [16, 18] are made). The core contribution lies in tailoring this approach to ADMM with the dual backward step and sparsity awareness. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty in light of existing randomized coordinate descent literature.
* **Experimental scope:** The experiments, while informative, are limited to two applications. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a broader range of problems would strengthen the claims of general applicability.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The performance gains of PDMM seem heavily reliant on careful parameter tuning (especially τi and νi). The paper does not sufficiently detail the sensitivity of performance to these parameters, or provide clear guidance on how to select appropriate values in practice for different problem instances.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The formulas for τi and νi, while theoretically sound, could lead to increased implementation complexity compared to simpler ADMM variants. A discussion of the practical overhead associated with calculating and applying these parameters would be helpful.
* **Connection to other parallelization techniques:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how PDMM compares to other parallelization strategies for ADMM beyond the ones explicitly mentioned (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM method offers a promising approach for parallelizing ADMM, addressing a critical challenge in solving large-scale machine learning problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are encouraging.  While the core idea builds upon existing techniques, the specific combination and adaptation to ADMM are novel and well-motivated. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in optimization.
* Proposes a novel and well-motivated method with strong theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates competitive performance on benchmark problems.
* Provides insightful connections to existing work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited novelty in the core randomized coordinate descent idea.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
* Potential for implementation complexity and sensitivity to parameter tuning.




**Rating:** **Strong Accept (7/10)**. 

I believe this paper is of sufficient quality and originality to be accepted to NIPS.  Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more guidance on parameter tuning) would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
