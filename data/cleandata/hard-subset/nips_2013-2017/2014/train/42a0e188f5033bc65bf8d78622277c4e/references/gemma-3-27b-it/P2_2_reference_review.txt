## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of applying ADMM to multi-block problems, particularly the potential for divergence with direct Jacobi updates. PDMM tackles this by introducing a "backward step" on the dual update and allowing for parallel updates of a randomly selected number of blocks.  The paper establishes theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/T) iteration complexity) and demonstrates the effectiveness of PDMM through experiments on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso, showing performance competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing ADMM variants.  A key contribution is framing sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases of PDMM, offering a unifying perspective.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:**  Efficiently solving large-scale, linearly constrained block-separable problems is crucial in many machine learning applications. The challenges associated with extending ADMM to multiple blocks are well-recognized, making this work highly relevant.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of randomized block coordinate descent *with* a backward step on the dual update is a novel contribution.  The ability to tune the step size based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix A is also a valuable addition.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  Establishing global convergence and iteration complexity with a constant step size is a significant achievement, especially given the known difficulties with convergence in multi-block ADMM.
* **Unifying Framework:** Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM provides deeper insights into the mechanics of these algorithms and clarifies their relationships.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM, particularly in scenarios with sparse constraints. The ablation study on the number of blocks (K) is helpful.
* **Clear Writing (mostly):** The core ideas are presented in a relatively clear manner, and the paper follows a logical structure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Burden:** The notation, particularly related to block indices and sparsity patterns of A, is quite heavy and can be difficult to follow. While necessary for the technical analysis, it hinders readability. A visual aid (e.g., a diagram) illustrating the block structure and indexing would be beneficial.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper mentions implementation in Matlab but lacks sufficient detail to easily reproduce the results. Information on parameter tuning strategies beyond what is presented for the experiments would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:** While PDMM is compared to several ADMM variants, the comparison isn't always comprehensive. For example, the PA-APG and S-APG methods are mentioned but not deeply compared, and the claim that RBSUMM "could not see the convergence" requires more explanation (were parameters exhaustively tuned?).
* **Discussion of Practical Parallelism:** The paper emphasizes parallel updates, but doesn't discuss practical considerations for *actually* implementing this parallelism (e.g., communication overhead, synchronization issues).
* **Overlapping Block Lasso Experiment:** The section on overlapping group lasso feels somewhat rushed.  The benefits of PDMM over other methods aren’t clearly articulated beyond runtime. A more detailed analysis of the performance gains would strengthen this part of the paper.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous analysis and compelling theoretical results. The convergence proof is a significant contribution. However, the complex notation slightly detracts from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (7/10):**  The writing is generally clear, but the density of notation makes it challenging to grasp the full details of the algorithm and its analysis. More visual aids and a more detailed explanation of implementation choices would improve clarity.
* **Originality (8/10):** The proposed PDMM method is a novel and well-motivated extension of ADMM. The unifying framework connecting sADMM and PJADMM is a particularly original contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):** The work addresses a significant problem in optimization, and the proposed method has the potential to be widely applicable in various machine learning tasks. The empirical results demonstrate its practical effectiveness, and the theoretical guarantees provide confidence in its reliability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
* Novel approach that addresses a challenging problem in multi-block ADMM.
* Competitive performance on relevant benchmark problems (RPCA, overlapping group lasso).
* Unifying perspective on existing ADMM variants.
* Potential for significant impact in areas relying on large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Heavy notation that hinders readability.
* Limited details regarding implementation and practical parallelization.
* Incomplete comparison with all relevant existing methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and effective, and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the weaknesses related to notation, implementation details, and comparative analysis would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I believe the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses.
