This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block-coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core idea is to update a random subset of `K` primal variable blocks in parallel at each iteration. To ensure convergence, which is a known issue for direct Jacobi-style ADMM updates, the authors introduce a "dual backward step" and a step-size rule for the dual update that adapts to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated. The paper provides a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. It also argues that PDMM unifies and generalizes existing parallel ADMM variants, namely sADMM and PJADMM, as special cases. The method is evaluated empirically on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a well-motivated framework that attempts to unify several Jacobian-style ADMM variants. The connection drawn between sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases of PDMM is insightful and contributes to a better understanding of the role of primal update inexactness versus dual update conservatism in these methods.
*   **Methodological Idea:** The introduction of the dual backward step, controlled by the parameter `ν`, is a clear and direct attempt to address the known instability of parallel ADMM. The proposed step-size rules for `τ` and `ν`, which incorporate matrix sparsity (`di`) and the degree of parallelism (`K`), are novel and theoretically grounded.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the new components of the algorithm is clearly articulated, and the theoretical results are presented in a standard format.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this paper lies in the insufficient and questionable empirical validation, which fails to provide convincing evidence that the proposed method offers a significant advantage over established baselines. For a method whose primary contribution is practical utility (as the theoretical rate is standard), this is a critical flaw.

1.  **Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The central claim and name of the method relate to its parallel nature. However, all experiments were conducted sequentially. This is a major shortcoming. The performance of a parallel algorithm cannot be judged by its sequential runtime or even its iteration count alone. A true evaluation would require implementation in a parallel environment to measure wall-clock time, accounting for practical factors like communication overhead and synchronization costs. Without such an evaluation, the claimed benefits of parallelism are purely speculative. For instance, a method with fewer iterations (like GSADMM in the experiments) might still be slower in a parallel setting than a method with more iterations that parallelizes well.

2.  **Questionable Baseline Comparisons:** The experimental comparison with existing methods is not rigorous enough.
    *   In the RPCA experiment, the authors note that "the public available code of sADMM does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0." They then state that "sADMM should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3." Comparing PDMM against a seemingly incorrect or simplified implementation of sADMM is not a fair or valid comparison. To make a credible claim of superiority, the authors should have implemented the baseline method according to its published theory.
    *   In the overlapping group lasso experiment, the authors state they were unable to see convergence for RBSUMM and thus excluded it. While this might be a genuine issue with the baseline, it could also be a result of insufficient parameter tuning or an implementation error. Dismissing a relevant baseline in this manner weakens the experimental section.

3.  **Parameter Tuning and Sensitivity:** The paper's results suggest that PDMM's performance is sensitive to its parameters. The authors first show results "without tuning" where GSADMM is superior, and then show "tuned" results where PDMM can be faster. This raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison. A rigorous study would require a clear and consistent protocol for tuning all hyperparameters (`ρ`, `τ`, `ν`, `K`, etc.) for all methods being compared, potentially using a held-out validation set. The current description of choosing the "best" results is too vague and does not instill confidence that the baselines were given the same level of tuning effort as the proposed method.

4.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the method is elegantly presented, its core components are evolutionary rather than revolutionary. Randomized coordinate methods and momentum/correction steps to stabilize updates are familiar concepts in optimization. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination and the adaptive step-size rule. While this is a valid contribution, its significance hinges on demonstrating a clear practical advantage, which, as argued above, has not been convincingly shown.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The paper provides a sound theoretical framework that unifies existing parallel ADMM methods.
    *   The proposed adaptive step-size mechanism based on sparsity and the number of parallel blocks is a novel and interesting idea.
    *   The paper is clearly written and includes the required convergence analysis.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The empirical evaluation is critically flawed. The lack of any experiments in a parallel environment makes it impossible to validate the central claim of the paper.
    *   The comparisons to key baselines (sADMM) appear to be based on improper implementations, undermining the reported performance gains.
    *   The tuning protocol is not transparent, raising concerns about the fairness of the experimental comparison.
    *   The practical significance of the method is not established due to the weaknesses in the experimental design.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound extension of ADMM, it fails to meet the high standards of empirical rigor required for publication at NIPS. The claims of practical superiority are not supported by the evidence provided. The work is promising, but it requires a much more thorough and convincing experimental evaluation—including actual parallel implementation and fair, rigorous comparisons against correctly implemented baselines—before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
