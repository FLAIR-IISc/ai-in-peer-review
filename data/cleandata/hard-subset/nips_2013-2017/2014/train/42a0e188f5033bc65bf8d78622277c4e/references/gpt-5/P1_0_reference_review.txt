Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for linearly constrained convex optimization with block-separable objectives. The authors target the challenging multi-block setting where Jacobi-style ADMM variants may diverge. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel at each iteration, and introduces a “dual backward step,” i.e., a correction to the dual variable used in the primal updates to temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. Importantly, both the dual step size and the amount of backward correction are chosen per dual row block as functions of (i) the number K of blocks updated and (ii) the sparsity pattern of A via di, the number of nonzero block neighbors per dual block. An inexact variant leverages Bregman divergences to linearize problematic terms and recover proximal/Jacobian-type updates.

The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convex assumptions (existence of a KKT point, closed/proper/convex fj), with explicit parameter choices τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i (K̃i = min{di, K}). These choices allow larger dual steps as A becomes sparser (smaller di) or as fewer blocks are updated. The paper also shows that two known Jacobian multi-block ADMM variants are special cases of PDMM: sADMM (exact primal step, small dual step) and PJADMM (proximal/inexact primal step, full dual step), thus providing a unifying perspective. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso are presented, with empirical comparisons to ADMM/GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work is adequate: the paper positions itself among multi-block ADMM (Gauss–Seidel vs Jacobian), randomized block coordinate methods, and proximal/Jacobian ADMM variants. The “dual backward step” is reminiscent of damping/relaxation or extragradient corrections known to stabilize multi-block or primal–dual methods; the paper’s unifying view and sparsity-dependent step sizes appear to be new.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean mechanism (dual backward step) explains and fixes the instability of naive Jacobi ADMM. The per-row step sizes that depend on sparsity (di) and K are insightful and practically useful.
- Theoretical guarantees: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity with explicit parameter choices; accommodates inexact updates via Bregman divergences under standard smoothness/strong convexity assumptions.
- Unification: Demonstrates that sADMM and PJADMM are extremes of a broader design space governed by the balance between primal progress and dual step sizes; clarifies when large dual steps are safe.
- Practical scope: Can handle overlapping groups via a standard splitting; the analysis explains why larger dual steps (e.g., τi = 1/2) are possible in such structured problems.
- Empirical indications: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive or faster than baselines in iteration count or runtime (even when run sequentially), and the effect of K is studied.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation:
  - There are numerous typos and inconsistencies that impede readability (e.g., “seperable,” “defiend,” “convegence,” RBSUMM vs RBUSMM). Equation (2) inconsistently uses ρ vs ρ/2; please standardize. In (8), νi appears as 1 − K̃i, which must be a typo (later text uses νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, consistent with the discussion for K = 1 and K = J).
  - Some crucial objects are introduced vaguely in the main text (e.g., Pt and Q are only said to be PSD without definition; γi is given but the intuition could be clearer). The main paper would benefit from at least a brief derivation sketch of τi, νi.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - All experiments run sequentially; the core claim is parallelism and scalability, yet no parallel/distributed wall-clock results are shown. This weakens the practical impact claim.
  - Baseline fairness and tuning: sADMM is reported with a publicly available implementation lacking a proper dual update (τi = 0), which disadvantages it; a faithful reimplementation should be used. RBSUMM tuning seems limited, and the claim of non-convergence in 5000 iterations would be more convincing with a broader parameter sweep and a diagnostic (e.g., residual plots).
  - Limited breadth and scale: Only two applications with moderate sizes; no ablation on the backward step vs no-backward step, and no systematic study of sparsity (di) vs performance to validate the theory-driven step sizes.
- Scope of guarantees: Convergence is in expectation and ergodic; no non-ergodic rates or high-probability/almost-sure convergence statements. The constants hidden in O(1/T) are not discussed; practical guidance on ρ, ηjt, and choices of Bregman kernels for common losses would help reproducibility.
- Related work connections: The backward step has ties to over-relaxation/damping and extragradient techniques (e.g., primal–dual hybrid gradient/Chambolle–Pock). While the paper focuses on ADMM lineage, briefly positioning PDMM relative to these would strengthen the narrative.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable assumptions and clear theorems (details in supplement). The intuition for the backward step is compelling. However, the main paper omits some definitions and proof sketches; experimental methodology can be improved (baselines and parallel evaluation).

Clarity
Organization is reasonable, but the manuscript requires significant copyediting and minor corrections (equations, typos, references). More intuition on the derivation of τi, νi and explicit definitions of Pt, Q would help. The bibliography contains many incomplete entries; please fix author lists, years, and venues.

Originality
The randomized multi-block Jacobi approach with a principled dual backward step and sparsity-dependent step sizes appears novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM’s parameters is a useful conceptual contribution.

Significance
Potentially significant for large-scale structured optimization and ML, especially where overlapping structures and sparse A arise. The theoretical step-size guidance is practical. Demonstrating real parallel speedups would materially increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct (8) to νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, standardize the augmented term with ρ/2, and define Pt, Q in the main text. Improve language and references.
- Provide a short derivation/intuition for τi, νi in the main paper; relate the backward step to damping/relaxation/extragradient literature.
- Re-implement sADMM with a correct dual update; tune baselines more systematically; include additional baselines (e.g., PDHG/Chambolle–Pock where applicable).
- Add parallel/distributed experiments (multi-core/GPU/cluster), and scaling studies vs J, K, di; include ablations on νi (backward step on/off).
- Clarify choices of ϕjt/ψjt and ηjt; provide guidance and code.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled algorithmic idea with clear theory.
- Unifies existing methods and explains their behavior.
- Exploits sparsity for larger stable dual steps.
- Empirical results are promising.

Cons:
- Paper needs polishing; some technical details missing in main text.
- No parallel experiments; baseline comparisons could be stronger.
- Limited breadth of applications and ablations.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core idea and theory are solid and of interest to the NeurIPS community; with improved clarity, corrected typos, and stronger empirical evaluation (especially parallel results and fair baselines), this could be a strong paper.
