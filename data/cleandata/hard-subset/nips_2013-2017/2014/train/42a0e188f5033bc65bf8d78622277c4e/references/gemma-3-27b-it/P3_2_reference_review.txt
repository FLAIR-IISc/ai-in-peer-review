## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel extension to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) – the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) – designed for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The work is technically sound, offers a valuable contribution to the understanding of ADMM variants, and presents a well-justified theoretical analysis. While the empirical evaluation is limited, it demonstrates promising results. I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of scaling ADMM to problems with a large number of blocks, where standard implementations can struggle with convergence or require excessive computational cost.  The authors observe the convergence issues in direct Jacobi ADMM and propose PDMM, which randomly selects and updates *K* blocks in parallel, while introducing a 'backward step' on the dual update to improve stability. This backward step addresses the potential for overly aggressive dual variable updates inherent in parallel schemes.  The authors expertly situate their work within the existing ADMM landscape, clearly distinguishing PDMM from Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM), Jacobian variants (sADMM, PJADMM), and RBSUMM. A key contribution is establishing connections between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM, demonstrating that the latter two can be viewed as extreme cases of the more general PDMM framework. The paper also tackles the problem of overlapping group lasso, a relevant application where block-separability is often compromised.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing convergence results and iteration complexity for PDMM. The assumptions are reasonable, and the proofs appear sound (though a closer look at the supplementary material is recommended).
* **Novelty and Insight:** PDMM represents a genuinely new approach to parallelizing ADMM, offering a flexible framework that combines the benefits of parallel computation with the stability of the method of multipliers.  The connection between PDMM and existing variants (sADMM, PJADMM) is a significant insight, providing a deeper understanding of the interplay between primal and dual updates.
* **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The backward step on the dual update and the consideration of the sparsity structure of the problem matrix A are well-motivated and contribute to the algorithm's robustness. The adaptive step size selection, dependent on both the sparsity of A and the number of blocks updated, is a key strength.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent.
* **Handles Overlapping Blocks:** The ability to efficiently handle overlapping block structures is significant, opening up application areas where traditional block coordinate descent methods struggle.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the potential of PDMM, the evaluation is somewhat limited. The comparison with other methods is not exhaustive, and the parameter tuning process is not fully described. A more comprehensive evaluation, including a wider range of problem instances and datasets, would strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the number of randomly selected blocks, K, on performance is clearly demonstrated, but the guidance on choosing an appropriate K for a given problem is limited. More insights into how K should be selected based on problem characteristics would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Explanation:** While the connection to sADMM and PJADMM is a strength, the explanation of these relationships, and the rationale behind the backward step, can be somewhat dense and require careful reading.
* **Overly Strong Claims**: While the paper is strong overall, some statements, such as claiming PDMM is "faster" than GSADMM without fully clarifying the difference in parallelization capabilities, could be softened.  GSADMM is sequential, while PDMM is inherently parallel.



**4. Detailed Evaluation Based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, the theoretical results are well-established, and the analysis is rigorous. The choice to provide a global convergence rate and iteration complexity result is commendable.
* **Clarity:** Good. The writing is generally clear, but some sections, particularly those detailing the connections between PDMM and other methods, could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** High. The PDMM algorithm and its theoretical analysis are novel contributions to the field. The insight into the relationships between different ADMM variants is also original and valuable.
* **Significance:** High.  Addressing the scalability of ADMM is a significant challenge. PDMM offers a promising solution, and its potential impact on a wide range of applications, particularly those involving large-scale, block-separable optimization, is substantial. The ability to handle overlapping blocks further enhances its practical relevance.



**5. Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solid theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees.
* Novel algorithm with potential for significant speedups.
*  Insightful connections between PDMM and existing ADMM variants.
*  Addresses a key challenge in scaling ADMM to large problems.
*  Applicable to important problem classes like RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

**Con:**

* Limited empirical evaluation.
*  Parameter selection guidance could be improved.
*  Some sections could benefit from clearer explanations.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

* **Expand the empirical evaluation:** Include more problem instances, datasets, and a more thorough comparison with other relevant methods.  Investigate the performance of PDMM on a wider range of block sizes and sparsity patterns.
* **Provide Guidance on K Selection:** Offer more practical advice on how to choose an appropriate value for *K* based on problem characteristics.
* **Clarify Explanations:**  Simplify the explanation of the connections between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM to enhance clarity.
* **Moderate Claims:** Rephrase any overly strong claims (e.g., regarding speedups compared to GSADMM) to accurately reflect the benefits of parallelization.




I believe that addressing these minor points will further strengthen this already valuable contribution to the field of optimization.
