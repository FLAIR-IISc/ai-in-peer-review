

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The main contribution is a unified framework that generalizes existing Jacobian ADMM methods (sADMM and PJADMM) by allowing parallel updates of any number of blocks, with a dual backward step that makes conservative progress on the dual variable.

The paper makes several notable contributions: (1) it establishes a connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM, providing better theoretical understanding of these methods; (2) it incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomized blocks into the step size determination; and (3) it demonstrates how PDMM can be applied to overlapping group lasso problems, where randomized block coordinate descent was previously an open problem.

PDMM is theoretically grounded with global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity established under reasonable assumptions. The paper provides clear theoretical connections to existing methods and demonstrates practical effectiveness on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where PDMM outperforms existing approaches in terms of runtime (though not always in iterations).

**Strengths:**
- The unification of existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM) into a single framework is a significant conceptual contribution.
- The dual backward step insight is elegant and provides better theoretical understanding of ADMM variants.
- The connection to problem sparsity and the number of blocks being updated is a practical and novel theoretical contribution.
- The experimental results are well-motivated and demonstrate clear computational advantages in several settings.
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, with good contextualization of related work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical novelty is somewhat incremental, as the convergence results follow standard patterns for ADMM variants.
- The experimental section could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons, particularly regarding why RBSUMM failed to converge within 5000 iterations for the overlapping group lasso problem.
- The specific derivation of the step size parameters in equation (8) could be more thoroughly explained.

**Originality Assessment:**
The paper's main novelty lies in the unified framework and the generalization to parallel updates of arbitrary numbers of blocks. While the core idea of randomized block coordinate descent for ADMM variants has been explored (e.g., RBSUMM), PDMM's ability to update any number of blocks in parallel and its theoretical connections to existing methods represent a meaningful advancement. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work, particularly in Section 2.2, where it establishes sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM.

**Significance Assessment:**
PDMM provides a practical and theoretically sound approach to solving a wide class of optimization problems that arise in machine learning. The ability to handle overlapping group structures with randomized block updates addresses a gap in the literature. While the improvements over existing methods aren't revolutionary, they are substantial enough to be useful in practice, particularly for large-scale problems where parallelism is valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach that makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature. The unified framework provides better theoretical understanding of existing methods, and the practical improvements demonstrated in experiments are significant for real-world applications. While the theoretical results aren't groundbreaking, the conceptual novelty and practical utility make this a strong contribution.

I believe this paper should be accepted at NIPS. The novelty of the unified framework, the elegant theoretical insights regarding the dual backward step, and the practical significance of the method for large-scale optimization problems outweigh the minor limitations in the theoretical novelty. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a useful tool for practitioners working on block-separable optimization problems.
