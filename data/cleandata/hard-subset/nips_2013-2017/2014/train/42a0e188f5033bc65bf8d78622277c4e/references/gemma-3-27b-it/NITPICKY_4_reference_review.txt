The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM, for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors aim to improve upon existing ADMM variants, particularly in scenarios with a large number of blocks where standard approaches struggle with convergence or computational cost. The core idea is to randomly select and update K blocks in parallel, introducing a “backward step” on the dual update to promote stability, and carefully selecting step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix A. Connections to sADMM and PJADMM are established, framing them as special cases of PDMM. Convergence guarantees with O(1/T) iteration complexity are provided, and the method is evaluated on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

While the proposed method presents some interesting ideas and attempts to address limitations of existing ADMM variants, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to potentially significant concerns regarding the practical benefit and clarity of the proposed approach. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Addressing a relevant problem:** Scaling ADMM to many blocks is indeed a challenge, and exploring randomized approaches is a reasonable direction.
*   **Theoretical analysis:** The paper provides convergence analysis, establishing a rate of O(1/T).
*   **Connections to existing work:** Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM offers some insight into the parameter choices and behavior of different methods.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The inclusion of empirical results on standard problems is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Rigor:** The notation is inconsistent and occasionally imprecise. For example, the definition of *N(i)* changes between the introduction and the equations, making it unclear what it represents. The use of *K̃i* is also unnecessarily complicated and poorly motivated. The claim of "unified framework" with Bregman divergence feels forced and adds complexity without clear benefit. Equations (17) and (18) are stated as optimality conditions but lack sufficient explanation of how they relate specifically to PDMM’s updates.
*   **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** The idea of randomized block coordinate descent is not new (references [16, 18] are cited), and the contribution of applying it specifically to ADMM, while reasonable, does not appear particularly groundbreaking. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate how PDMM fundamentally differs in its algorithmic approach beyond the chosen parameters and the backward step, which appears intuitively motivated but lacks a strong theoretical justification.
*   **Overly Complex Parameter Selection:** The formulas for τi and νi, while theoretically derived, seem cumbersome and potentially difficult to tune in practice. The dependence on *di* (the number of nonzero blocks in a row of A) introduces sensitivity to the structure of the constraint matrix.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while present, are not particularly compelling. The comparison to existing methods feels incomplete. Specifically, the claim that “GSADMM converges faster than PDMM” is qualified by acknowledging that GSADMM doesn't run in parallel, diminishing the significance of the comparison. The runtime comparison to sADMM is undermined by stating sADMM lacks a dual update—this is a significant implementation choice, not an inherent property of the algorithm. The discussion regarding K in the overlapping group lasso experiment is confusing; it states that performance increases with K up to 61, and then plateaus. This is a key finding, but the explanation lacks depth.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is dense and often lacks sufficient explanation. The motivation for the backward step is intuitive but not rigorously explained. The organization feels somewhat disjointed, with technical details interspersed with high-level explanations.
*   **Minor Issues:** The statement "Interestingly, the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" feels like a tangential claim and not central to the paper's contribution. The sentence “PDMM does not necessarily choose any K combination of J blocks” is unclear and could be rephrased. Several instances of repetitive phrasing diminish readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical analysis of a randomized ADMM variant.
*   Establishes connections between PDMM and existing algorithms.
*   Offers experimental results, though limited.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution lacks substantial novelty beyond applying a known randomized technique to ADMM.
*   The paper is riddled with notational inconsistencies and lacks clarity.
*   The proposed parameter selection rules are complex and potentially impractical.
*   The empirical results are not convincing enough to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The overall presentation feels rushed and lacks polish.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem, the paper is ultimately flawed by its lack of clarity, excessive complexity, and limited empirical evidence of practical benefit. The theoretical contributions, while present, are overshadowed by the numerous minor and some substantial weaknesses. I recommend **rejection**. Significant revisions addressing the clarity, notation, and empirical validation would be necessary for reconsideration. Specifically, the authors need to clarify the novelty of their approach, simplify the parameter selection, provide a more rigorous justification for the backward step, and present a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods with fair implementations.
