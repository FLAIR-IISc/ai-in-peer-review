Summary and contributions
The paper addresses linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization with possibly nonsmooth terms, a class that includes RPCA and overlapping group lasso. It proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that:

- Updates any K of the J primal blocks in parallel using the augmented Lagrangian with an optional Bregman term (for inexact updates).
- Uses a “dual backward step” to temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty, together with explicit dual ascent with step sizes τi that depend on the row-block degree di (sparsity of A) and the number K of updated blocks. The backward parameter νi similarly depends on di and K.
- Unifies exact and inexact variants via Bregman linearization and shows that two known methods are special cases: sADMM (small dual step; ν close to 1) and PJADMM (large proximal terms; full dual step; ν = 0).
- Provides global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convex assumptions with constant stepsizes, for arbitrary K.
- Demonstrates empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates PDMM among multi-block ADMM variants: Gauss–Seidel ADMM (with sometimes problematic convergence), Jacobian variants with splitting (sADMM), proximal Jacobi ADMM (PJADMM), and randomized BSUMM (RBSUMM). The key novelty relative to sADMM/PJADMM is the explicit coupling of τi, νi to K and to the sparsity di, and allowing arbitrary K>1 randomized updates (RBSUMM updates a single block and requires decreasing steps). The theoretical rate matches O(1/T) results for two-block ADMM and some proximal multi-block ADMM variants.

Technical assessment (quality)
- Algorithmic design: The analysis identifies the instability of naive Jacobi ADMM as arising from implicit dual ascent in the primal step, and counteracts it via a backward dual step. This is a sound and insightful mechanism; the derivation of τi and νi that ensure Lyapunov decrease is the key technical ingredient.
- Theory: The paper states global convergence (in expectation over block sampling) and O(1/T) ergodic rate, under Assumption 1 (closed proper convex fj; existence of KKT point). The unifying framework using a Lyapunov function h(v*,v^t) and the residual R with PSD matrices P^t and Q is standard and reasonable for randomized block methods, but the main text defers most specifics to the supplement. Important details that impact reproducibility/interpretability are currently under-specified in the main paper:
  - The exact forms and roles of P^t and Q;
  - How τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i are derived, and whether these bounds are tight or conservative;
  - Conditions ensuring Q and the coefficients ζi, γi are nonnegative;
  - Assumptions on the Bregman terms for inexact updates (strong convexity/Lipschitz constants) and how to set ηj in practice.
- Connections: The claimed reductions to sADMM and PJADMM are valuable, but the mapping (parameter settings, proximal strengths) should be made explicit in the paper, not only in the supplement.
- Experiments: The experiments support the claims qualitatively but have important limitations:
  - All runs are sequential in Matlab; no actual parallel speedups or communication/runtime profiles are reported, despite parallelism being a primary motivation.
  - Fairness of baselines: the sADMM baseline is said to use a public implementation “without dual update (τ=0)”, which is not the sADMM algorithm analyzed in the cited work; this undermines that comparison. Likewise, RBSUMM tuning appears limited; failure to converge within 5000 iterations could be step-size related.
  - Only two problem classes are tested, both convex, with relatively moderate sizes. There is no ablation of the impact of sparsity di nor detailed sensitivity to K, ρ, τ, ν beyond selected cases.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable for an expert but has several presentation issues:
- Many typos and grammatical errors (“seperable”, “defiend”, “nonnegativeness”), missing years in references, and inconsistent notation.
- Important definitions (P^t, Q, βi, γi) and intuition behind the step sizes are scattered and often deferred to the supplement.
- The algorithmic description (especially of inexact PDMM) would benefit from a concise algorithm box with all parameters and their admissible ranges, and a short proof sketch of the main descent inequality.

Originality
The dual backward step tailored to multi-block Jacobi updates, and the explicit dependence of step sizes on both sparsity di and the number of updated blocks K, are novel and compelling. The unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of the same scheme is conceptually useful. Randomizing K>1 blocks in parallel with provable O(1/T) under constant steps extends beyond RBSUMM’s single-block updates.

Significance
Given the wide applicability of linearly constrained block-separable problems, a practical, parallelizable method with convergence guarantees is significant. The di- and K-aware step sizes are likely to be adopted in practice. However, the lack of actual parallel experiments and somewhat limited empirical validation reduce immediate impact.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of Jacobi ADMM failure and a principled fix via the dual backward step.
- Step-size rules that exploit sparsity and number of updated blocks.
- Unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM.
- Convergence and O(1/T) rate under constant steps for arbitrary K.
- Applicability to overlapping group problems via a standard split.

Weaknesses
- Insufficiently detailed presentation of key theoretical components in the main paper; core derivations and parameter guidance are deferred to the supplement.
- Experimental evaluation lacks parallel implementations and may include unfair baselines (sADMM without proper dual update).
- Limited problem diversity and size; no communication/compute complexity analysis per iteration.
- No discussion of adaptive penalty/stepsize strategies, robustness to mis-specified τ, ν, ρ.
- Notation heavy and some clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include an algorithm box summarizing PDMM (exact/inexact) with explicit admissible ranges for τi, νi, ηj and practical default choices. Provide a brief derivation/intuition for τi, νi.
- Specify P^t and Q in the main text, and give a short proof sketch of the main descent inequality.
- Implement a proper sADMM baseline with the correct dual update; add comparisons to PJADMM with tuned proximal parameters.
- Report parallel wall-clock results (e.g., multi-core or distributed) and communication costs; include per-iteration complexity and memory footprint, especially for overlapping groups.
- Add sensitivity analyses for ρ, K, τi, νi and ablations showing the effect of di.
- Clean up writing, typos, and references (include years and complete bibliographic details).

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a solid and interesting contribution to randomized multi-block ADMM with practical insights (dual backward step, di- and K-dependent steps). The theory appears sound, and the unifying perspective is valuable. However, the current presentation omits key derivations from the main paper, and the experiments fall short of demonstrating practical parallel benefits and fair baseline comparisons. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong contribution.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled algorithmic idea (dual backward step) with clear intuition.
- Theoretically grounded step-size rules exploiting sparsity and parallelism level.
- Unifying connections to existing ADMM variants.
- Convergence with constant steps and O(1/T) rate.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete/opaque theoretical details in the main text.
- Experimental limitations (no parallel results; questionable baselines).
- Clarity and presentation issues.
