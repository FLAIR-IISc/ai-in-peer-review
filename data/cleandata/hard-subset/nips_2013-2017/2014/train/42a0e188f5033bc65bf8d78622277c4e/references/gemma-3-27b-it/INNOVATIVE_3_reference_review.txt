This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea—randomly selecting and updating blocks in parallel—is a compelling and potentially significant contribution. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing parallel ADMM methods (GSADMM, sADMM, PJADMM) and attempt to address them through a backward step on the dual update and a careful consideration of the sparsity structure of the problem. Establishing a connection between sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM is a nice theoretical contribution. The convergence analysis and iteration complexity results are a strong point. The applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso are relevant and provide some empirical support.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The randomized block selection in PDMM, combined with the dual backward step and the connection to existing methods, presents a genuinely novel approach. It goes beyond simply applying randomization to existing techniques.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, which is a significant accomplishment for a new algorithm.
*   **Generality:** PDMM appears applicable to a broad class of optimization problems encountered in machine learning, as demonstrated by the applications.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The discussion of why direct Jacobi updates fail and the motivation behind the dual backward step are insightful.
*   **Clear Connections:** The demonstration that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM provides a unifying framework and deeper understanding of these methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are well-conducted, the performance gains over existing methods, particularly GSADMM, are not always substantial. The claim that PDMM is “faster” needs qualification. GSADMM is sequential while PDMM is designed for parallelism, yet the runtime comparisons don’t fully highlight that advantage. A more extensive empirical evaluation, perhaps on larger datasets and with genuine parallel implementations, would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The theoretical guidance on setting τi and νi is good, but the paper doesn’t fully explore the sensitivity of PDMM’s performance to these parameters.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The choice of applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) is somewhat limited. Exploring other problem domains where block sparsity is prevalent could further demonstrate the versatility of PDMM.
*   **Bregman Divergence:** The discussion of the Bregman divergence feels a bit detached from the core algorithm. While theoretically useful, its practical impact isn’t thoroughly demonstrated.
*  **Implementation Details:** The code is not publicly available.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging optimization problem.
*   The theoretical contributions (convergence analysis, iteration complexity, connection to existing methods) are significant.
*   The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and provide a compelling motivation for their work.
*   Even if the empirical gains are modest, the potential for performance improvements with optimized parallel implementations is high.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical results don’t unequivocally demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods.
*   The parameter sensitivity analysis is limited.
*   The scope of experimental validation is somewhat narrow.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the empirical results aren’t overwhelming, the novelty and theoretical soundness of PDMM are compelling. I am willing to overlook the relatively modest empirical gains, given the potential for future improvements with more extensive experimentation and optimized parallel implementations. This is a bold idea with a strong theoretical underpinning and I recommend **acceptance**, with a request that the authors consider addressing the limitations outlined above in future work and releasing their code. The potential benefits of a truly parallelizable and theoretically grounded ADMM variant are significant.
