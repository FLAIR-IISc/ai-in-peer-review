This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block-coordinate algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core problem is a canonical form in machine learning and statistics, encompassing applications like Robust PCA and composite regularization problems (e.g., overlapping group lasso). The key challenge addressed is the failure of direct parallel (Jacobi) updates in the standard method of multipliers.

The main contributions of PDMM are threefold:
1.  **A Dual Backward Step:** To counteract the overly aggressive updates that cause instability in Jacobi ADMM, the authors introduce an intermediate dual variable `ŷ` that is a conservatively stepped-back version of the main dual variable `y`. This `ŷ` is then used in the primal update step, effectively damping the dual progress.
2.  **Adaptive Step Sizes:** Crucially, the step sizes for the dual update (`τi`) and the backward step (`νi`) are not fixed constants but adapt to the problem structure. They depend on the total number of blocks (J), the number of randomly selected blocks for parallel update (K), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix A (via `di`, the number of non-zero blocks in a row block).
3.  **A Unifying Framework:** The paper demonstrates that two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM (for exact primal updates) and PJADMM (for inexact/proximal updates), are special cases of PDMM corresponding to different choices of K and the inexactness parameter `η`. This provides valuable insight into the relationship between these algorithms.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes under standard assumptions. Empirical results on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate that PDMM is competitive with or superior to related methods like GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** The core idea of the dual backward step is an elegant solution to the well-known instability of Jacobi ADMM. The algorithm's flexibility in allowing an arbitrary number of blocks `K` to be updated in parallel is a significant practical advantage over methods that can only update one block or all blocks.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid convergence proof, including an O(1/T) rate, for a flexible, randomized, parallel ADMM variant. Proving convergence for multi-block ADMM is notoriously difficult, and achieving this with constant step sizes is a notable theoretical contribution. The use of a Lyapunov function that decreases in expectation is a standard but well-executed technique.

3.  **Insightful Unification of Prior Work:** Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are two extremes of the PDMM framework is a high-quality contribution. It clarifies the role of the dual backward step: it should be aggressive (large `ν`) when primal updates are exact and can be omitted (`ν=0`) when primal updates are conservative (highly proximal). This improves the community's understanding of parallel ADMM.

4.  **Effective Use of Problem Structure:** The adaptation of step sizes based on the constraint matrix sparsity (`di`) is a key strength. The overlapping group lasso experiment provides a compelling demonstration of this, where PDMM significantly outperforms sADMM precisely because it can leverage the sparse structure of the reformulated problem to take much larger, more effective steps.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Core Equations:** There appears to be a significant typo or lack of clarity in the definition of the step size `νi` in Equation (8): `νi = 1 - K̃i`. Given that `K̃i = min{di, K}`, `K̃i` can easily be greater than 1 (e.g., if a dual block is connected to `di=5` primal blocks and `K=5` blocks are updated, `K̃i=5`). This would make `νi` negative, which contradicts the stated constraint `0 ≤ νi < 1`. This is a critical point that confuses the reader and undermines confidence in the implementation details. The authors should carefully review and correct this formula and ensure the theoretical analysis is consistent with the corrected version.

2.  **Lack of True Parallel Experiments:** The algorithm is motivated by and named for its parallelism ("Parallel Direction Method..."). However, all experiments are run sequentially. While sequential runtime can give an indication of computational load per iteration, it does not demonstrate the method's primary claimed advantage. A small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine showing actual wall-clock time speedup as `K` increases would make the paper's practical claims substantially more convincing.

3.  **Evaluation on Overlapping Group Lasso:** While the results are promising, the comparison could be strengthened. The authors note that RBSUMM failed to converge on this problem, which is surprising and may warrant further investigation (e.g., was it a tuning issue or a fundamental problem?). A comparison against more state-of-the-art specialized solvers for overlapping group lasso beyond the two cited would also provide a better sense of where PDMM stands in that specific domain.

### Detailed Comments

-   The application of PDMM to solve overlapping group lasso in a randomized block-coordinate fashion is a nice contribution. The claim that this is an open problem for RBCD is plausible since the variable splitting is necessary to enable block-separability for the primal update.
-   The analysis of the effect of `K` in the experiments is very well done and informative. It clearly shows the trade-off between per-iteration cost and total number of iterations, highlighting that there is often a "sweet spot" for `K` that is neither 1 nor J.
-   In the RPCA experiment, the claim that "sADMM should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3" seems to imply sADMM is mis-implemented or mis-tuned. It would be fairer to tune sADMM with its own optimal parameters before comparing. The statement that the public code has `τi=0` is strange and might not be representative of the actual sADMM algorithm.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the literature on ADMM and large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, flexible, theoretically well-supported, and empirically effective. The unification of prior work and the intelligent step-size scheme are particular highlights. The weaknesses, particularly the likely typo in a core equation and the lack of parallel experiments, are addressable. I believe this paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with the strong recommendation that the authors clarify/correct Equation (8) in the final version).
