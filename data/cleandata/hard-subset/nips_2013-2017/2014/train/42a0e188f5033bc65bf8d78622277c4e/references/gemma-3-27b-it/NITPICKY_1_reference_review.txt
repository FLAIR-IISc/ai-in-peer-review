This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method, termed PDMM, for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea is to extend ADMM by allowing random selection of blocks for parallel updates, incorporating a “backward step” on the dual update to improve convergence, and adapting step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix. The authors establish convergence guarantees and demonstrate performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

While the paper tackles a relevant and important problem—scaling ADMM to multiple blocks and leveraging parallelism—and presents some interesting ideas, numerous deficiencies, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant conceptual gaps and incomplete experimental validation, lead me to recommend rejection. My concerns are detailed below.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevance:** The problem of efficiently solving large-scale linearly constrained optimization problems with block structure is highly relevant to many machine learning applications.
*   **Conceptual Idea:** The idea of randomizing block updates to potentially overcome the convergence issues of GSADMM and explore the space more efficiently is a reasonable one. The backward step on the dual update also has intuitive appeal.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors attempt a theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, which is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation and Clarity:** The notation is often convoluted and inconsistent. For example, the definition of *N(i)* and *di* is introduced in the notation section at the end of the introduction, yet is immediately used in the subsequent discussion of τi and νi.  The repeated use of indices (i, j, k) without clear explanation adds to the confusion.  The variable *K̃i* is defined, but its role isn’t adequately motivated – why is it the *minimum* of *di* and *K*?  The flow of the mathematical derivations often feels abrupt and lacks sufficient motivation.
*   **Lack of Novelty & Connection to Existing Work:** The paper claims novelty, but the connection to existing randomized block coordinate descent methods (RBCD, e.g., [16, 18]) isn't sufficiently explored. How does PDMM differ fundamentally from existing parallel approaches? The claim that RBCD cannot solve overlapping block problems is unsubstantiated—RBCD can be applied with appropriate formulation, even if not explicitly discussed in the referenced paper [16, 18].  The paper presents sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, but the discussion is superficial; simply stating they are special cases without a more detailed analysis of the parameter regimes where they emerge doesn't add much value.
*   **Theoretical Gaps & Assumptions:** The convergence analysis relies on several assumptions (Assumption 1) that are standard for ADMM but aren’t thoroughly discussed in the context of PDMM’s randomized updates. What impact does the random block selection have on satisfying these assumptions? The definition of the residual *R(xt+1)* in (19) lacks a clear interpretation and its connection to the convergence proof is not well-explained. The conditions under which the convergence rate is achieved (Theorem 2) seem overly optimistic and might not hold in practice.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is weak. The figures lack proper labeling and axis titles, making it difficult to interpret the results. The comparison to other methods is not thorough. The chosen parameter settings are not adequately justified. The statement that “PDMMs perform better than RBSUMM and sADMM” is an overstatement given the limited scope of the experiments and the lack of statistical significance testing. The impact of varying *K* on performance is explored, but the explanation for the observed behavior (plateauing performance) is speculative. The claim regarding GSADMM’s sequential nature is misleading, as parallel implementations of GSADMM exist. The comments on parameter tuning are insufficient. “Without tuning the parameters of PDMM” doesn’t convey what parameters were tested, and why.
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** There are several minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper. For example, in Section 2.3, τi = 0.5 is stated as being much larger than 1/J in sADMM, but this is only true when J is small. Additionally, the reference to [25] as an “extended work” is vague and doesn't provide sufficient context. The provided equations are also not numbered sequentially.
*   **Overly Assertive Language:** The authors frequently use overly assertive language, making strong claims without sufficient justification. Phrases like "PDMM converges faster" and "PDMM is the fastest algorithm" need to be toned down and supported by rigorous statistical analysis.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization.
*   Presents a potentially useful extension to ADMM with randomized block updates.
*   Includes a theoretical analysis (although incomplete).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and inconsistent notation.
*   Limited novelty and insufficient comparison to existing methods.
*   Weak experimental evaluation with insufficient justification of parameter settings.
*   Theoretical gaps and overly optimistic assumptions.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   Overly assertive language and unsubstantiated claims.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the proposed approach, the numerous shortcomings in clarity, novelty, and experimental validation outweigh the strengths. The paper needs substantial revision to address these issues before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing a more thorough comparison to related work, conducting a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and addressing the theoretical gaps in their analysis.
