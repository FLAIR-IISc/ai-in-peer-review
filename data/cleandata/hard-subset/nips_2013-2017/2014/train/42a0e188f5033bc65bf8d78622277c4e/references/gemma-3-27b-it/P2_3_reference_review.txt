## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of applying ADMM to multi-block problems, particularly convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates. PDMM overcomes this by introducing a backward step on the dual update, leveraging sparsity in the constraint matrix, and allowing for parallel updates of a randomized subset of blocks.  The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/T) iteration complexity) and demonstrate empirical performance on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso problems, showing improvements over existing ADMM variants and related methods. They also highlight connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, positioning PDMM as a unifying framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Practical Problem:**  The paper tackles a key challenge in applying ADMM to large-scale, multi-block problems where standard implementations can struggle with convergence.
* **Novelty:** The combination of the backward dual step, randomized block selection, and consideration of sparsity provides a novel approach within the ADMM framework.  The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is insightful.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The authors provide convergence analysis with established complexity bounds, demonstrating a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. This is a significant strength.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso demonstrate that PDMM can outperform existing methods, including ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM. The investigation of the effect of 'K' (number of randomized blocks) is particularly valuable.
* **Clarity (Generally):** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly presents the PDMM algorithm.  The notation is mostly consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the authors provide guidelines for setting the step sizes (τi and νi), the process still appears somewhat involved and potentially problem-dependent. The impact of different choices of K is explored, but a more systematic analysis of sensitivity to parameters would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the chosen applications are relevant, the experiments feel a bit limited. Comparing PDMM to more modern optimization approaches (e.g., accelerated proximal gradient methods, other parallel solvers) beyond the ADMM family would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Clarity (Specific Points):**  Section 2.3 regarding overlapping block problems feels a bit abrupt and doesn't fully explain the connection to the broader discussion of PDMM. Some of the notation (e.g., related to block indexing) could be made more intuitive.
* **Overly Detailed Mathematical Exposition:** The mathematical exposition, while rigorous, is dense and at times obscures the core ideas.  A slightly more high-level explanation alongside the detailed derivations might improve accessibility.  Much of the detail is relegated to the supplement which while appropriate, makes it harder to quickly grasp the key insights.




**Evaluation against NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The convergence analysis is a strong point.  The discussion of limitations is adequate but could be more prominent. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., 2.3) and mathematical derivations could be improved for better readability. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The approach is novel, particularly the combination of features. While building on existing ADMM concepts, PDMM presents a significant contribution. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is relevant to many machine learning applications. The improvements over existing ADMM variants and the potential for scalability make this work significant. The connection to existing methods provides valuable insights. **(Good to Strong)**



**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrable empirical improvements over existing methods.
* Addresses a practical and important problem in large-scale optimization.
* Novel combination of techniques within the ADMM framework.
* Insightful connections to related work.

**Con:**

* Parameter tuning may be challenging.
* Experimental scope could be broader.
* Some sections lack clarity and require careful reading.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale problems amenable to block-separable formulations. The theoretical rigor, empirical validation, and novelty of the approach outweigh the minor weaknesses in clarity and experimental scope.  The paper is well-suited for the NIPS audience and has the potential to influence future research in this area. I would suggest the authors address the clarity concerns and potentially expand the experimental evaluation in a final version.
