### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear equality constraints. This problem structure is common in many machine learning applications, such as Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The proposed method addresses the well-known convergence issues of direct parallel (Jacobian) variants of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).

The core technical contributions of PDMM are threefold:
1.  **A Dual Backward Step:** To prevent overly aggressive updates of the dual variable which can lead to divergence in parallel settings, the algorithm uses a corrected dual variable `y_hat` in the primal update step. This `y_hat` is a conservatively "backed-off" version of the main dual variable `y`.
2.  **Adaptive Step Sizes:** The step sizes for the primal and dual updates (`tau_i` and `nu_i`) are not fixed but are intelligently chosen based on the problem structure, specifically the total number of blocks (`J`), the number of blocks updated in parallel (`K`), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix (`d_i`). This allows for larger, more aggressive steps when the problem structure permits.
3.  **Flexible Parallelism:** PDMM can randomly select and update any number of `K` blocks in parallel, ranging from `K=1` (randomized coordinate descent) to `K=J` (fully parallel).

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing the global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with a constant step size. Furthermore, it demonstrates that two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM (for exact updates) and PJADMM (for proximal updates), are special cases of PDMM, thus providing a unifying framework and deeper insight into their relationship. The effectiveness of PDMM is demonstrated experimentally on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, where it outperforms several state-of-the-art methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and well-known problem: the lack of guaranteed convergence for direct parallel extensions of ADMM. The proposed solution, combining a dual backward step with structure-aware adaptive step sizes, is both novel and elegant. The resulting algorithm is theoretically sound and practically effective.
2.  **Unifying Framework:** A major strength of this work is its ability to unify and generalize existing methods. Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are two extremes within the PDMM framework is a valuable conceptual contribution. It clarifies the trade-off between the accuracy of the primal update (exact vs. proximal) and the aggressiveness of the dual update (conservative vs. full step), which is an important insight for both researchers and practitioners.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) complexity rate under standard assumptions. This level of rigor is essential and places the work on par with other state-of-the-art first-order methods.
4.  **Practical Applicability:** The method is highly practical. The ability to exploit the sparsity of the constraint matrix to achieve larger step sizes is a key advantage for large-scale problems. Moreover, the successful application of PDMM to the overlapping group lasso problem, which is challenging for standard randomized block coordinate descent methods, is a compelling demonstration of its power and versatility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Algorithmic Flow:** The description of the dual backward step could be improved for clarity. The algorithm is presented via equations (5)-(7), where `y_hat^t` is used in the primal update, and `y_hat^{t+1}` is computed from `y^{t+1}` after the primal and main dual updates. It would be helpful to explicitly write out the complete loop structure, perhaps showing that `y_hat^t` can be expressed as `y^t - nu * rho * (A*x^t - a)`, to make the flow of information between iterations unambiguous.
2.  **Intuition behind Step Size Rules:** The paper presents the specific formulas for the step sizes `tau_i` and `nu_i` in equation (8), and these are critical for the convergence proof. However, there is little intuition provided as to why these formulas take this particular form (e.g., the `2J-K` term). While the derivation is likely complex and relegated to the appendix, a brief, high-level motivation in the main text would greatly enhance the reader's understanding.
3.  **Evaluation of Parallel Performance:** The central claim of the paper is a new *parallel* algorithm. However, all experiments were run sequentially. While the sequential runtime comparisons are interesting and show PDMM's efficiency per-iteration, they do not directly validate the algorithm's scalability and performance in a parallel computing environment, which is its primary motivation. Even a small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine demonstrating wall-clock time speedup as the number of cores increases would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims.
4.  **Baseline Comparison Fairness:** The experimental comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment seems potentially unfair. The authors note that the public code for sADMM uses a zero step size for the dual update (`tau_i = 0`), which cripples the algorithm. A fairer comparison would involve implementing sADMM with its proper, non-zero dual update step, as described in the literature it is based on.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper provides a novel and well-motivated solution to a long-standing problem in parallel optimization.
*   The unifying framework connecting sADMM and PJADMM is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and complete.
*   The method demonstrates strong empirical performance and is applicable to important and challenging machine learning problems.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   The experimental evaluation does not include any actual parallel execution, which somewhat weakens the claims about a parallel method's performance.
*   Minor clarity issues in the algorithm description and a potentially unfair baseline comparison could be improved.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the theory and practice of large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, general, and backed by solid theoretical and empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to presentation and the experimental setup, and do not fundamentally undermine the core technical contribution. The work is of high quality and is likely to be of great interest to the NIPS community. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors are encouraged to address the identified weaknesses in the final version to further improve the paper's impact.
