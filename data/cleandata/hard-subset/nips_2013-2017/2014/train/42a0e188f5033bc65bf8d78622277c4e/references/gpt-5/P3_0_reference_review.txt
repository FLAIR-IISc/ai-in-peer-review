Summary
The paper considers linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization and proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM. Each iteration samples K primal blocks and updates them in parallel by minimizing an augmented Lagrangian augmented with an optional Bregman proximal term (enabling exact or inexact updates). The dual is then updated with a step-size τi and a “dual backward” correction νi that explicitly counteracts the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term in the primal update. The step-sizes depend on the sparsity of A via per-row degrees di and on K through K̃i = min{di, K}. The authors prove global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant stepsizes, and show that two existing Jacobi-style ADMM variants, sADMM (with splitting variables) and PJADMM, arise as extreme cases of PDMM. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggest PDMM is competitive or faster than sADMM, GSADMM/ADMM, and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned among multi-block ADMM schemes: Gauss–Seidel ADMM (possibly with Gaussian back-substitution), Jacobi variants (including proximal Jacobian ADMM), and randomized BCD-type ADMM (RBSUMM). It also relates to linearized/inexact ADMM using Bregman/proximal terms. The paper’s principal novelty is: (i) the explicit dual “backward step” and (ii) the adaptive, sparsity- and K-aware step-sizing for the dual update in a randomized Jacobi setting. The connection that PJADMM and sADMM are special cases is helpful.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The “implicit dual ascent” observation neatly explains why naive Jacobi ADMM may diverge; the backward step offers a principled stabilization.
- Technical contributions: Global convergence and O(1/T) rate with constant stepsizes in a truly parallel randomized multi-block setting, including an inexact variant handled under standard strong convexity/Lipschitz assumptions on the linearized terms.
- Practicality: The dependence of τi, νi on di and K is intuitive and could materially improve performance on sparse problems; the method supports arbitrary K and arbitrary groupings (including overlapping groups via a standard augmentation).
- Unification: Showing sADMM and PJADMM are extreme points of the same family improves understanding of when aggressive dual steps are safe (depending on how much progress is made by the primal updates).

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity and consistency: There are inconsistencies in the augmented Lagrangian scaling (eq. (2) uses ρ‖·‖2; elsewhere ρ/2‖·‖2 appears). Notation alternates between Acj, Ajt, etc. The matrices Pt and Q in the Lyapunov and residual definitions (eqs. (19)–(21)) are only described as positive semidefinite; their precise form and how they enter the bounds should be stated in the main text (not only in the supplement).
- Justification of stepsizes: The derivation of τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1−1/K̃i is central yet is only motivated heuristically (implicit/external dual ascent) and then used in theorems. A brief proof sketch in the main paper explaining how these constants arise from descent in the potential would strengthen the contribution.
- Comparison to related randomized primal–dual methods: Beyond RBSUMM, there is a broader literature on randomized coordinate primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG with coordinate updates, randomized PD splitting, linearized ADMM with corrections). A discussion contrasting PDMM with PDHG-type methods (including diagonal preconditioning and correction steps akin to Gaussian back substitution) would position novelty more crisply.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are sequential Matlab on synthetic or small/moderate problems. Claims about parallel scalability are not validated on multicore/GPU or distributed systems, where synchronization and communication for Aixt+1 matter. Baseline tuning is uneven (e.g., “best” GSADMM vs fixed PDMM τi, νi in some plots, then tuned in others). Standard ADMM diagnostics (primal/dual residuals) are not reported; fairness to RBSUMM is unclear (its theory requires diminishing step-sizes/error bounds). Ablations on the effect of di-aware steps vs uniform steps are missing.
- Inexact updates: The linearization via Bregman divergence is standard, but the conditions on (ψjt, ϕjt, ηjt) that guarantee global convergence and the precise dependence of constants on Lipschitz/strong convexity are only briefly stated. A compact theorem summarizing these conditions would aid reproducibility.

Quality
The main claims (global convergence and O(1/T) rate for randomized parallel updates with adaptive stepsizes and dual backward correction) are plausible and appear technically sound given the provided Lyapunov argument, though key constants and matrices deserve clearer exposition. The empirical support is indicative but not conclusive regarding practical advantages in real parallel settings.

Clarity
Overall readable but notation and scaling inconsistencies hinder understanding. The core ideas (implicit dual ascent, backward step, sparsity-aware steps) are well motivated. Proof details and exact constants should be summarized more concretely in the main text.

Originality
Moderately novel. The dual backward step in a Jacobi randomized multi-block ADMM with explicit sparsity- and K-aware step selection is a useful addition. The unification with sADMM and PJADMM is informative, though related correction/proximalized ADMM ideas exist.

Significance
Potentially useful for large sparse constrained problems, especially where many blocks can be updated in parallel. The theoretical guarantees with constant stepsizes are valuable. Demonstrated practical impact would be stronger with real parallel implementations and broader benchmarks.

Pros
- Clear motivation and insight (implicit dual ascent).
- Convergence and O(1/T) rate for randomized parallel multi-block updates with constant stepsizes.
- Sparsity- and K-aware dual steps; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.
- Handles overlapping groups cleanly.

Cons
- Notational/scaling inconsistencies; key matrices/parameters under-specified in main text.
- Limited empirical validation (no actual parallel runs; small-scale; partial tuning).
- Incomplete positioning vs modern randomized primal–dual splitting beyond ADMM variants.
- Limited ablations (role of di-based steps, ηjt, νi).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix ρ scaling inconsistencies; unify notation (Acj vs Ajt).
- State explicit forms of Pt, Q, βi and provide a concise proof sketch for τi, νi.
- Add ablations: uniform vs di-aware steps; effect of K; sensitivity to ηjt, νi, τi.
- Report standard ADMM residuals and ensure consistent, fair tuning across baselines.
- Include parallel/multicore experiments and at least one real dataset.
- Expand related work to cover PDHG/CP variants and randomized primal–dual coordinate methods, clarifying differences.
