### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a general and important problem formulation in machine learning. PDMM is a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Its key features are: (1) At each iteration, it randomly selects a subset of K (out of J) primal variable blocks and updates them in parallel. (2) It introduces a "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm, which is crucial for convergence in the parallel update setting, unlike a naive Jacobi-style ADMM which often diverges. (3) The step sizes for the dual update and the backward step are intelligently determined by the number of blocks being updated (K) and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix (A). The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size. It also shows that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified view and deeper understanding of these methods. The practical effectiveness of PDMM is demonstrated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems, where it is shown to be competitive with or superior to existing methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a fundamental optimization problem with broad applicability. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and significant contribution to the literature on ADMM and large-scale optimization. The combination of parallel updates, randomization, a stabilizing dual backward step, and sparsity-aware step sizes is a powerful and original concept. The ability to update an arbitrary number of blocks (K) in parallel provides a flexible trade-off between per-iteration cost and convergence speed.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong. The authors provide a rigorous global convergence proof and an O(1/T) iteration complexity result under standard assumptions, which matches the rates of standard ADMM. A key theoretical strength is the unification of sADMM and PJADMM as two extreme cases of PDMM. This connection is insightful, clarifying the relationship between the accuracy of the primal update and the aggressiveness of the dual update, and provides a deeper understanding of the role of the dual backward step.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation for the algorithm is excellent; the authors start by highlighting the failure of a direct Jacobi-style ADMM and use this to justify the introduction of the dual backward step. The roles of the different parameters (`τi`, `νi`) and their dependence on sparsity and randomization are explained well.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors compare PDMM against a strong and relevant set of baselines (ADMM/GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on two important applications. The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of PDMM. The analysis of how performance varies with the number of chosen blocks (K) in the overlapping group lasso experiment is particularly valuable, as it gives practical insight into how to use the algorithm. The application to overlapping group lasso is a strong point, as this problem structure is challenging for traditional block coordinate methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practicality of Step Sizes:** The paper provides theoretically derived step sizes in (8). However, the experimental results in Table 1 show that "tuned" parameters lead to significantly better performance. This suggests that the theoretical step sizes might be quite conservative. A discussion on this gap between theory and practice would be valuable. Is there a way to choose better parameters in practice, or are the theoretical values the only ones with guarantees? Clarifying this would enhance the practical utility of the paper.

2.  **Discussion on Parallel Implementation:** The algorithm is designed for parallel execution, which is a major selling point. However, the experiments were run sequentially. While this is common for proving a concept, the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of the practical aspects of a parallel implementation. For example, what would be the communication overheads (e.g., in the aggregation step `Aix^{t+1}` for the dual update), and how would they scale with K and the problem structure?

3.  **Clarity of Theoretical Constructs:** The Lyapunov function `h(v*, vt)` used for the convergence proof is quite complex, particularly the `L̃ρ` term. While the full derivation belongs in the supplement, the main paper could benefit from a sentence or two of intuition explaining *why* this is the correct quantity to analyze and what the different terms represent. This would make the theoretical claims more accessible.

4.  **Comparison with RBSUMM:** The paper rightly points out the advantages of PDMM over RBSUMM (guaranteed convergence rate, K>1 updates). However, the experimental comparison for the overlapping group lasso problem is incomplete, as the authors state that they could not get RBSUMM to converge. While this may indeed reflect a weakness of RBSUMM on this problem, it is an inconclusive result. It would be better to either report the non-converging behavior with a plot or investigate a simpler problem where RBSUMM is known to work well to provide a more direct comparison.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, well-motivated, and flexible parallel optimization algorithm (PDMM) for a very important class of problems.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis is sound, providing convergence guarantees and a valuable unification of existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM).
*   **Pro:** The algorithm is shown to be practically effective and often superior to state-of-the-art methods in well-conducted experiments.
*   **Pro:** The work significantly advances the understanding and capability of ADMM-style methods in parallel and randomized settings.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The gap between the theoretical step sizes and the empirically tuned ones could be discussed more thoroughly to guide practitioners.
*   **Con:** The lack of discussion on the practicalities of parallel implementation (e.g., communication costs) is a minor omission for a paper focused on a parallel method.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
