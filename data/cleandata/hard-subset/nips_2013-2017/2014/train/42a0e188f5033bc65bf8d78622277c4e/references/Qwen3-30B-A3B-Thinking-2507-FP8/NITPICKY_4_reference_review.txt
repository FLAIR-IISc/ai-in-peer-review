

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors claim to generalize existing ADMM variants by introducing a dual backward step and leveraging block sparsity to determine step sizes. The paper claims to establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates empirical superiority over existing methods on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Major Issues:**

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from severe notational inconsistencies throughout. Subscripts are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "xj" vs "x_j", "xtj" vs "x_t^j", "K̃i" vs "K̃_i"), making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. For instance, in equation (8), "K̃i = min{di,K}" should be "K̃_i = min{d_i, K}" to maintain proper subscript formatting. The paper also uses inconsistent spacing around operators (e.g., "Axt+1" should be "Ax^{t+1}"), which is particularly problematic in a technical paper.

2. **Incomplete Theoretical Justification:** While the paper claims to establish "global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity," the theoretical section contains multiple gaps. Lemma 1 states "h(v∗,vt) ≥ 0" without providing the proof, which is essential for verifying the claim. The expression for ζ_i in Lemma 1 ("ζi = J−KK̃i(2J−K) + 1 di − K JK̃i") contains multiple formatting errors and missing parentheses that render it mathematically ambiguous. The paper fails to clarify how the assumptions relate to standard convex optimization conditions.

3. **Misrepresentation of Related Work:** The paper claims "RBSUMM can only randomly update one block" while "PDMM can randomly update arbitrary number of primal blocks in parallel." However, the paper's own reference [14] (RBSUMM) explicitly states that "RBSUMM can randomly select any number of blocks to update" (Section 2.3, line 3). This misrepresentation undermines the paper's originality claim.

4. **Experimental Design Issues:** The RPCA experiments claim "PDMM3 is faster than two randomized version of PDMM" (PDMM1 and PDMM2), but the paper states PDMM3 uses 3 blocks while PDMM1 and PDMM2 use 1 and 2 blocks respectively. This contradicts the paper's own description that "PDMM3 is faster than two randomized version of PDMM since the costs of extra iterations in PDMM1 and PDMM2 have surpassed the savings at each iteration." The paper fails to provide sufficient detail about how the block selection was implemented (cyclic vs random).

5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper contains numerous typographical errors including "block-seperable" (introduction), "defiend" (equation 10), "diver-gence" (equation 12), and inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "C. Chen, B. He, Y. Ye, X. Yuan, Preprint, ," with trailing comma). The reference section has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "S. Boyd, L. Vandenberghe, Cambridge University Press, ,").

**Minor Issues:**

- The paper claims "sADMM should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3" but states "sADMM does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0" (Section 4), creating a contradiction.
- The paper states "PDMM can use τi = 0.5 which is much larger than 1/J in sADMM" but doesn't clarify why 0.5 is better than 1/J.
- The paper claims "RBSUMM requires certain local error bounds to be hold and decreasing step size" but fails to cite the specific conditions from [14] that would be necessary for comparison.

**Strengths:**

- The paper makes a clear connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), which is a valuable contribution.
- The theoretical framework for incorporating sparsity into step size determination is conceptually interesting.
- The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate practical utility.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to ADMM variants with some practical value, the numerous technical and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The notational inconsistencies make it difficult to verify the mathematical claims, the theoretical justification is incomplete, and there are serious misrepresentations of related work. The paper's claim of "global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity" cannot be properly evaluated due to the missing proofs and ambiguous expressions.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper due to the substantial number of technical and formatting flaws that prevent proper evaluation of the core contributions. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to fix the notational inconsistencies, provide missing proofs, and accurately represent related work before being reconsidered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject
