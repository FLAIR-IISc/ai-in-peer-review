## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method, dubbed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of applying the method of multipliers (MoM) to multi-block problems, where naive parallelization often leads to instability. PDMM introduces a backward step on the dual update, leverages sparsity in the constraint matrix, and allows for random selection of blocks for parallel updates.  The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/T) iteration complexity) and demonstrate empirical performance on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso problems. A key contribution is establishing connections between PDMM and existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, framing them as special cases of the proposed framework.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of ADMM and its variants for solving linearly constrained optimization. It effectively highlights the limitations of existing methods like GSADMM and Jacobian-based approaches, and accurately reflects the state-of-the-art in this field. The reference list is comprehensive and includes key works on ADMM, parallel optimization, and applications like RPCA and group lasso.  The connection made to sADMM and PJADMM is a particularly valuable contribution to understanding the space of possible ADMM implementations. The discussion of RBCD and its challenges with overlapping blocks is a relevant and insightful addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM introduces a genuinely novel approach to parallelizing ADMM, moving beyond simple Jacobi updates. The backward step on the dual variable is a clever idea to address potential instability.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees and iteration complexity results, which is a significant strength. The assumptions are standard and reasonable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The consideration of sparsity in the constraint matrix A and its influence on step size selection demonstrates an awareness of practical implementation details.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper successfully links PDMM to sADMM and PJADMM, providing a unified framework and deeper insight into these methods. This is a significant contribution to understanding the landscape of ADMM variants.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of PDMM. The comparison with other methods is well-conducted.
* **Clear Presentation:** While dense, the paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is mostly standard and consistently used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides formulas for τi and νi, the interplay between these parameters, the number of randomly selected blocks (K), and the sparsity structure of A could be challenging for practitioners to understand and tune effectively. A more detailed discussion of practical parameter selection guidelines would be valuable.
* **Limited Exploration of Inexactness:** The section on inexact PDMM (Section 2.1) feels somewhat incomplete. While the idea of using Bregman divergences is sound, the practical benefits and choices of Bregman divergences are not fully explored. The discussion of closed-form solutions feels somewhat hand-wavy.
* **Experimental Depth:** The experimental results, while promising, could be more extensive. For example, exploring the sensitivity of PDMM’s performance to different sparsity levels of A would be helpful. A comparison to more recent and state-of-the-art solvers for RPCA and overlapping group lasso could provide further context.
* **Overlapping Block Lasso Specifics:** The discussion in Section 2.3 regarding overlapping group lasso feels somewhat disconnected from the main PDMM framework. The observation about potentially using τi = 0.5 is interesting, but it’s not fully integrated into the broader analysis.
* **Supplement Reliance:** Several key details (proofs, detailed parameter settings) are relegated to the supplement. While this is common, it makes it harder to fully evaluate the paper without carefully reviewing the supplementary material.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  The paper addresses a significant challenge in parallel optimization – the instability of naive ADMM implementations.
*  PDMM offers a theoretically sound and practically effective solution, with strong empirical results.
*  The connection to existing ADMM variants provides valuable insights and a unified framework.
* The theoretical convergence analysis is rigorous and important for understanding the method’s behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of parameter tuning could hinder practical adoption.
* The discussion of inexactness feels incomplete and lacks sufficient practical guidance.
*  The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. It presents a novel and theoretically sound method for parallelizing ADMM, and the empirical results are promising. The connections to existing work are a valuable contribution.  While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with the request that the authors address the following:

1.  Provide more detailed guidance on practical parameter selection for PDMM, including a discussion of the sensitivity to different sparsity levels of A.
2.  Expand the discussion of inexact PDMM, providing more concrete examples and guidance on choosing appropriate Bregman divergences.
3. Consider adding a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art solvers for RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso.
4. Clarify the connection between PDMM and overlapping group lasso problem and integrate it more seamlessly into the main framework.
