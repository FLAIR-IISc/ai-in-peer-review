

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the limitations of existing methods like Gauss-Seidel ADMM (which updates blocks sequentially and may not converge for multiple blocks) and randomized block coordinate methods (which typically update only one block at a time). PDMM updates any number of blocks in parallel using randomized block coordinate descent, while maintaining convergence guarantees. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The authors evaluate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for a parallel randomized block coordinate method that generalizes existing approaches. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides valuable insight into the role of the dual backward step.

2. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper thoroughly analyzes the algorithm's design, including the dual backward step, sparsity considerations, and random block selection. The theoretical framework unifies several existing methods under a single framework.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a practically important problem (large-scale convex optimization) and provides a method that can be implemented in parallel computing environments. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the algorithm's practical advantages.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connections to existing work are well-explained.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty Claims**: While PDMM generalizes existing methods, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how much of the approach is truly novel versus a natural extension of existing work. The connection to RBSUMM [14] is mentioned, but it's not clear how PDMM advances beyond the randomized block coordinate approach. The claim that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" needs stronger justification.

2. **Experimental Comparison**: The experimental comparison with GSADMM is potentially unfair since GSADMM is sequential while PDMM is parallel. The paper doesn't provide a fair comparison of parallel speedup. Additionally, the RPCA experiment mentions "PDMMs" without clearly defining what "s" refers to (likely the number of blocks, but this is ambiguous).

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on how to choose the step size parameters τi and νi in practice. While the paper provides formulas for these parameters, it doesn't discuss how robust the algorithm is to different parameter choices or how these parameters should be selected for specific problem instances.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper establishes convergence for constant step size, but it's unclear how sensitive the algorithm is to the specific step size choices (8). The paper mentions that "the number of blocks in PDMM depends on the trade-off between the number of subproblems and how efficiently each subproblem can be solved," but doesn't provide guidance on how to make this trade-off in practice.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Novelty Claims**: Clearly articulate how PDMM differs from existing randomized block coordinate methods, particularly RBSUMM [14]. Provide a more detailed comparison of the theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence conditions, iteration complexity) between PDMM and RBSUMM.

2. **Improve Experimental Section**: Provide a fair comparison with sequential methods by reporting both sequential and parallel runtimes. Clarify the meaning of "PDMMs" in the RPCA experiments. Include more detailed information about the implementation (e.g., how the random block selection is implemented, how many blocks are updated in each iteration).

3. **Add Implementation Guidance**: Include a discussion on practical implementation considerations, particularly how to choose the step size parameters in practice. Consider adding a small section on parameter sensitivity analysis.

4. **Expand Theoretical Discussion**: Discuss the limitations of the theoretical results more thoroughly. For example, how do the convergence guarantees change when the problem is non-strongly convex or when the problem is ill-conditioned?

## Recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a significant problem in convex optimization. The theoretical contributions are valuable, and the experimental results demonstrate practical advantages. However, the novelty claims could be strengthened, and the experimental section would benefit from more careful comparison with existing methods.

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS, but would be improved by addressing the weaknesses noted above. The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach, provide more detailed experimental comparisons, and add implementation guidance.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**: The paper establishes a unified framework for multiple ADMM variants, provides theoretical guarantees with constant step size, and demonstrates practical advantages in experiments. The problem is important, and the solution is well-motivated.

**Con Acceptance Arguments**: The novelty claims need strengthening, the experimental comparison with sequential methods is potentially unfair, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient implementation guidance for practitioners.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of convex optimization. With minor revisions to address the concerns above, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
