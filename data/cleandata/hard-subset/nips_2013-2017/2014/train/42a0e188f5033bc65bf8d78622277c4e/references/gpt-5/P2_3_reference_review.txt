Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives subject to linear constraints. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of randomly selected primal blocks in parallel and uses a novel “dual backward step,” introducing an intermediate dual y-hat so that the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term is tempered. The step sizes for explicit dual ascent (τi) and the magnitude of the backward step (νi) depend on the sparsity of A via di (the number of nonzeros in row block i) and on K. An inexact variant is handled via Bregman linearizations, unifying exact and proximalized updates. The authors prove global convergence with O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions and show that two known methods are special cases: sADMM (small τ, large backward step) and PJADMM (large proximal/Bregman terms enabling full dual step, ν=0). They demonstrate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, including a discussion of the impact of K.

This fits into the active literature on multi-block ADMM and randomized block-coordinate methods. It addresses known convergence issues of Jacobi multi-block ADMM (e.g., He et al., Hong et al., Chen et al.) and complements prior randomized/parallel variants (RBCD, RBSUMM, PJADMM, two-block splitting approaches). The intuition about implicit dual ascent in the primal subproblems is well-motivated and connects to relaxation in ADMM; the sparsity-aware step-size rules are a distinctive design choice.

Quality (technical soundness and support)
- Theory: The main claims (global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic rate) are standard-strength for ADMM-type methods and appear technically sound given the stated assumptions. The derivation of τi and νi as functions of di and K is a substantive contribution; the potential function h and residual R are clearly defined in the main text, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The unification of exact and inexact updates via Bregman divergences is standard but useful.
- Scope and assumptions: Assumptions are mild (closed, proper convex fj; existence of KKT). For inexact updates, the strong convexity/Lipschitz conditions enabling linearization are reasonable and consistent with prior work.
- Empirics: The experiments are informative but limited. They show PDMM is competitive/faster than RBSUMM and sADMM on RPCA and faster than ADMM/GSADMM on overlapping group lasso. However, all code runs sequentially in MATLAB despite the algorithm being designed for parallel execution, and several baselines or implementation choices raise fairness questions (see weaknesses). Overall, empirical support is promising but not definitive.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable with helpful intuition for the dual backward step and the impact of K and sparsity. Notation is heavy but mostly consistent; the choice to define di and use it in step sizes is well explained. That said:
- Several typos, formatting issues, and incomplete citations (missing years, inconsistent formatting) detract from polish.
- Some technical definitions (e.g., the exact forms/roles of matrices Pt and Q) are deferred and only described as PSD in the main text; a brief intuition would help.
- Practical guidance for choosing ηj and specific ψ/φ choices for common losses/regularizers could be expanded.

Originality
Moderate to high. PDMM’s combination of:
- a random K-block Jacobi update,
- a dual backward step explicitly compensating the implicit dual ascent,
- sparsity-aware step-size selection (τi, νi depending on di and K),
and the unifying view that recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extremes, appears novel within the multi-block ADMM literature. Related ideas exist (relaxation/over-relaxation in ADMM, proximal Jacobian ADMM, randomized single-block updates as in RBSUMM), but the specific mechanism and analysis for K>1 random parallel updates with constant step sizes is a useful addition.

Significance
Moderate. The method is broadly applicable to linearly constrained, block-separable convex problems, including overlapping-regularized models and RPCA. The sparsity-aware step sizes and K-block sampling provide a practical design knob and theoretical guarantees. That said, the rate is O(1/T), matching standard ADMM, and the empirical section does not yet demonstrate parallel scalability or clear superiority over strong modern baselines across diverse large-scale tasks. With a stronger empirical section (true parallel implementation, larger datasets, more baselines such as linearized multi-block ADMM and primal-dual hybrids like PDHG/SPDHG), impact would increase.

Strengths
- Clear intuition for and principled design of the dual backward step; connects and unifies sADMM and PJADMM.
- Convergence with constant step sizes for randomized K-block parallel updates, with explicit dependence on A’s sparsity.
- Unified treatment of exact and inexact subproblems via Bregman linearization.
- Practical application to overlapping groups yielding favorable step sizes (e.g., τ=0.5 when di=2).
- Empirical results are encouraging and analyze the effect of K.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: sequential MATLAB only; no demonstration of wall-clock speedups on multicore/cluster, which is central to the paper’s motivation.
- Baseline fairness: sADMM appears to be handicapped by using public code without a dual update; the paper notes this but still reports it. More careful reimplementation or corrected baseline is needed. Comparisons to linearized multi-block ADMM, over-relaxed ADMM, or primal-dual (PDHG/SPDHG) methods are missing.
- Some clarity/polish issues (typos, incomplete references, limited practical guidance for selecting ηj and ψ/φ).
- Theoretical rate matches standard ADMM (no non-ergodic or accelerated guarantees).
- The dependence on di suggests limited advantage when A is dense (di≈J), which could be discussed more explicitly.

Questions for the authors
- Provide concrete recipes for ψ, ϕ, and ηj for common models (e.g., logistic loss with overlapping group lasso) and discuss sensitivity.
- Clarify the role and construction of Pt and Q in the analysis; any practical impact?
- How does PDMM perform when A is dense (di≈J), where τi≈1/J? Is there still a practical edge over sADMM/PJADMM?
- Compare with modern primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., randomized PDHG/SPDHG) on overlapping/composite problems.
- For RPCA, please include a corrected sADMM baseline with the appropriate dual update and document parameter tuning for all methods.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good theory, limited but suggestive experiments.
- Clarity: moderate; readable with room for polish and more practical guidance.
- Originality: moderate to high within multi-block ADMM literature.
- Significance: moderate; potentially impactful with stronger empirical validation.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying baselines and improving empirical comparisons; otherwise borderline. The theoretical contribution and unifying perspective are valuable for the community.
