This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea – leveraging randomization in block updates to potentially overcome convergence issues in multi-block ADMM, coupled with a novel ‘backward step’ on the dual update – is quite intriguing. The authors attempt to connect PDMM to existing Jacobian-based ADMM methods and establish convergence guarantees. The application to RPCA and overlapping group lasso provides some empirical validation.

The strength of this work lies in its novelty. The proposed PDMM offers a fresh perspective on addressing the challenges of multi-block ADMM, particularly the potential for divergence. The randomized block update strategy, combined with the dual backward step and its theoretical justification, constitutes a significant contribution. Connecting PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable insights. The attempt to analyze the impact of problem structure (sparsity of A) on the algorithm's parameters is commendable.  The convergence analysis, while possibly dense, provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The applications chosen are relevant and demonstrate potential utility.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The presentation is somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. While the authors claim PDMM addresses the convergence issues of multi-block ADMM, the empirical results presented do *not* convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over carefully tuned implementations of existing methods (GSADMM, sADMM). The comparison to GSADMM appears slightly unfair, as PDMM is inherently parallelizable while GSADMM is presented sequentially. The claim that PDMM is faster than GSADMM needs stronger justification, perhaps through a parallelized GSADMM implementation.  The parameter tuning details for competing methods aren't fully transparent, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.  The convergence rate, while established, appears similar to standard ADMM – a key selling point of the proposed method is missing.  The connection to sADMM and PJADMM, while theoretically interesting, doesn't translate into a clear practical advantage demonstrated in the experiments. The section on overlapping group lasso, while interesting, doesn’t show overwhelming performance improvements. The experiments, while present, feel somewhat limited in scope and parameter exploration. The role of Bregman divergence feels a little forced – its impact on performance isn’t clearly articulated or demonstrated. The notation is often heavy and makes understanding the core ideas more difficult.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The core idea of randomized block updates with a dual backward step is a significant contribution.
*   Theoretical Contribution: The convergence analysis provides a solid foundation for the method.
*   Potential Impact:  A successful randomized ADMM variant could have broad applicability in large-scale optimization.
*   Connections to Existing Work: The bridging of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is insightful.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited Empirical Validation: The experimental results don't definitively demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods.
*   Dense Presentation: The paper is difficult to follow in places, hindering understanding.
*   Lack of Clear Advantage: The claimed benefits over standard ADMM (convergence, speed) aren't consistently shown.
*   Not significantly faster.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards acceptance, but with a strong caveat. This paper presents a novel idea with theoretical grounding, which is a strong positive. The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly convincing, are sufficient to warrant further investigation. The method’s theoretical promise and novelty outweigh the current lack of strong empirical validation.  However, the authors *must* address the concerns about the empirical comparisons in a rebuttal, ideally providing more compelling evidence of PDMM's advantages. If the rebuttal offers a more thorough comparison, including parallelized baselines, and highlights a clear practical benefit, my recommendation would strengthen. Right now it is a valuable contribution due to novelty, but not a slam dunk.
