Summary
The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex, linearly constrained problems with block-separable objectives. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks each iteration. Two key design choices distinguish PDMM: (i) a “dual backward step” that damps the dual ascent to counteract the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term in parallel primal updates; and (ii) per-dual-block step sizes that depend on the sparsity pattern of A (via di, the number of nonzeros in each row-block) and the number of updated primal blocks K. An inexact variant uses Bregman proximalization to linearize difficult terms. The authors show that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM (corresponding to different choices of damping/proximalization), prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with explicit constants depending on K and di, and provide experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates PDMM among multi-block ADMM variants: Gauss–Seidel (GS-ADMM/BSUMM), Jacobian/proximal variants (PJADMM), and randomized single-block updates (RBSUMM). It correctly notes divergence issues with parallel Jacobi ADMM, and that recent works ensure convergence by (i) variable splitting to two-block ADMM (sADMM) or (ii) adding proximal terms (PJADMM), often with o(1/T)–O(1/T) rates. The proposed dual backward step is closely related to relaxation/damping in ADMM and primal-dual methods; the per-row di-dependent steps are novel and intuitively appealing, leveraging sparsity for larger safe steps. The unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM is useful.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Clear diagnosis of why naive Jacobi ADMM fails (implicit dual ascent), and a principled fix via a backward dual step with problem-dependent step sizes. The dependence of τi, νi on K and di is insightful and practically relevant for sparse A.
- Generality: Handles exact and inexact updates in a unified Bregman framework; supports arbitrary K parallel updates; applies to overlapping group structures after standard splitting.
- Theory: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard convex assumptions, with explicit constants showing trade-offs in K and sparsity. The residual definition ties directly to the optimality conditions for both exact and inexact cases.
- Connections: The mapping to sADMM and PJADMM clarifies when one should use strong dual damping versus proximalization; this perspective can guide practitioners.
- Empirical indications: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive, and sometimes faster, than the baselines considered.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Experimental validation of “parallel” claims is weak: all runs are sequential Matlab. No multicore/distributed results, no communication/computation analysis, and no scaling studies. Since the main selling point is parallelizability and dependence on K, the lack of actual parallel speedups is a significant gap.
- Fairness and strength of baselines: The sADMM code used lacks a dual update (τi=0), which is acknowledged; comparing to this implementation is not meaningful. PJADMM is discussed but not evaluated. For RBSUMM, only a narrow set of schedules is tried; lack of convergence within 5000 iterations could be due to tuning. Accelerated proximal methods (PA-APG, S-APG) are included but their tuning is not described.
- Parameter tuning vs theory: Although τi, νi are given by theory, experiments also report “best” τi, νi that deviate from the prescribed values. It is unclear whether convergence is still guaranteed under these settings; more systematic sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Clarity and notation: There are many typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “seperable,” “defiend”), inconsistent use of ρ and factors of 1/2 in the augmented term (eq. (2) vs later expressions), and heavy notation (Acj/Ari/Aij, z variables, Pt/Q) with limited intuition in the main text. Pseudocode would help.
- Theoretical presentation: The main text defers crucial details (definitions of Pt and Q, proof intuition for the key lemmas) to the supplement. The role of the backward step is nicely motivated, but a brief proof sketch explaining where the di- and K-dependent constants arise would strengthen the paper.
- Scope of applications: Only two (mostly synthetic) problems; no real datasets for RPCA (e.g., video background subtraction) or structured sparsity; no comparison on problems where di varies widely, to demonstrate the benefit of sparsity-aware steps.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a clean mapping from PDMM’s τi, νi to standard over-relaxation/damping parameters in ADMM and Chambolle–Pock/PDHG? Is PDMM’s backward step equivalent to a known relaxation in a transformed space?
- How sensitive is convergence to mis-specification of τi, νi? Can adaptive rules (e.g., backtracking on the dual step) be used to enlarge steps while preserving convergence?
- What are Pt and Q concretely in the residual and Lyapunov function? Are they easy to compute or conservative surrogates?
- How does PDMM behave when A is rank-deficient or poorly conditioned? Any preconditioning strategies?
- For inexact PDMM, what practical guidance can you give for choosing ϕjt/ψjt and ηjt beyond the Lipschitz/strong convexity conditions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add multicore/distributed experiments demonstrating wall-clock speedups vs K, including communication costs and sparsity patterns; report parallel efficiency.
- Correct the sADMM implementation or reimplement it faithfully; include PJADMM with appropriately large proximal terms; ensure fair parameter tuning and report primal/dual residuals per ADMM best practices.
- Include ablations showing the impact of di and K on convergence and the benefit of sparsity-aware τi.
- Provide algorithm pseudocode and a clearer, consistent notation (fix ρ/1/2 factors); collect key definitions in one place.
- Add a short proof sketch in the main text showing how the backward step and sparsity-dependent step sizes enter the descent inequality.
- Discuss asynchronous variants (you briefly mention an extended work); even preliminary results would be valuable.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Theoretical development appears sound and standard; the novelty lies in the dual damping and sparsity-aware steps. Empirics suggest promise but are limited and include an unfair sADMM baseline.
- Clarity: Moderate; the main ideas are understandable, but the paper would benefit from cleaner notation, pseudocode, and more self-contained proofs.
- Originality: The backward-step perspective and per-row sparsity-aware step sizes are new to my knowledge in the ADMM literature; the unification with sADMM/PJADMM is insightful.
- Significance: Potentially useful for large sparse problems; however, the lack of real parallel experiments and stronger baselines reduces immediate impact.

Pros
- Clear motivation and elegant fix (dual backward step).
- Theory with explicit dependence on K and sparsity.
- Unifies existing methods as special cases.
- Practical inexact variant via Bregman linearization.

Cons
- No actual parallel implementation results.
- Baseline comparisons (sADMM, RBSUMM, PJADMM) are incomplete/unfair.
- Some clarity/notation issues and heavy reliance on supplement.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has solid and interesting ideas with correct theory, but the experimental section does not yet convincingly demonstrate practical advantages in parallel settings, and some comparisons are not fair. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarity would make this a strong submission.
