This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems, a common formulation in machine learning applications like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors aim to improve upon existing ADMM variants by allowing for random selection of blocks for updates, leading to potential parallelization benefits. The paper thoroughly connects PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, providing theoretical insights into their relationships. The authors establish convergence guarantees and provide empirical validation on two applications.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem setting is well-defined and relevant to a wide range of machine learning applications. The introduction effectively motivates the need for efficient algorithms for this class of problems.
* **Thorough Literature Review:** The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the related work in ADMM and its variants.  The connections to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM) are clearly explained and contribute to the novelty of the proposed approach.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The convergence analysis is a significant strength. Establishing global convergence and an iteration complexity bound (O(1/T)) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method. The parameter tuning guidance based on problem structure (sparsity of A) is particularly valuable.
* **Insightful Design:**  The introduction of the backward step on the dual update and the consideration of block sparsity are clever ideas that address limitations of existing approaches.  The ability to handle arbitrary numbers of blocks randomly updated is also a significant contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to existing methods. The detailed comparisons and parameter tuning discussion are helpful.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The notations are clearly defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides guidance on setting parameters τi and νi, the dependence on the sparsity pattern of A (di) might require significant pre-processing to determine accurately, potentially adding overhead.  A more practical, automated method for determining K could be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental section focuses on two specific applications.  Expanding the evaluation to other problem domains or datasets could strengthen the claims of generality.  For instance, testing on problems with different levels of block overlap or different types of regularization would be useful.
* **Runtime Comparison Caveats:** The claim that PDMM is faster than GSADMM needs clarification. The paper acknowledges that GSADMM cannot be easily parallelized.  A fairer comparison might involve implementations of parallel GSADMM or a more detailed analysis of the computational cost per iteration.
* **Assumptions for Convergence:**  Assumption 1, while standard for ADMM, requires the existence of a KKT point.  A discussion of the implications if this assumption is not met, or strategies for dealing with such cases, would enhance the paper's robustness.
* **Bregman Divergence Choices:** While the paper mentions using Bregman divergences for inexact updates, the selection of a specific Bregman divergence and its impact on performance are not fully explored. More details on this aspect would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-supported algorithm (PDMM) for a common and important optimization problem.
* The connections to existing ADMM variants provide valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior and design choices.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and establishes strong theoretical guarantees.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM on relevant applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The parameter tuning complexity and limited experimental scope could hinder the practical applicability of the method.
* The comparison with GSADMM requires further clarification regarding parallelization aspects.
* The assumption of a KKT point may limit the applicability in certain scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NeurIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding parameter tuning complexity and providing a more nuanced comparison with parallelized GSADMM.  

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more automated approach for determining the optimal number of blocks (K) to update.
* Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of problem domains and datasets.
* Clarify the comparison with GSADMM by considering parallelized implementations or analyzing the computational cost per iteration.
* Discuss the implications of the KKT point assumption and potential strategies for handling cases where it is not met.
* Provide more details on the selection of Bregman divergences and their impact on performance.
