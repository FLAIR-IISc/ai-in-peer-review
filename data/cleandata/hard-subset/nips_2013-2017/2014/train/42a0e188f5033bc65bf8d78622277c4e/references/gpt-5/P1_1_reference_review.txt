Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex, block-separable objectives with linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks at random and solves their primal subproblems in parallel against an augmented Lagrangian, while updating all dual blocks with a steplength τi that depends on the number of updated blocks and the sparsity pattern of A. A key design feature is a “dual backward step”: the primal updates use a damped dual iterate ŷt = yt − νi ρ (Axt − a), which moderates the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty. The paper provides an “inexact” variant by adding Bregman proximal terms so that updates can be made closed-form; this unifies exact and proximal-Jacobian variants. The authors also formalize connections showing that two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are special cases at extreme parameter choices, thereby clarifying the role of the backward step and the dual steplength.

Under standard convexity and feasibility assumptions, the paper establishes global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant stepsizes; the rate matches classical ADMM rates. The analysis accounts for row-block degrees di = |N(i)| and the number of updated blocks K, yielding larger τi for sparser A and larger K. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso illustrate that PDMM can be faster than sADMM and RBSUMM; GSADMM sometimes requires fewer iterations but is sequential and lacks general convergence guarantees.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The algorithmic idea—separating implicit and explicit dual ascent via a backward step—and the stepsize schedule that leverages sparsity and K are well motivated. The theory gives global convergence and O(1/T) rates in expectation, aligning with the ADMM literature. That said, several technical details are under-specified in the main text (e.g., the definitions and roles of Pt and Q in the residual and Lyapunov function), and the augmented Lagrangian coefficient appears inconsistent between equations (2) and later updates (ρ vs ρ/2). Empirical evaluation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso is relevant, but limited: all runs are sequential (despite the method’s parallel motivation), datasets are modest, and fairness of baselines is not fully convincing (e.g., sADMM code without dual updates, step-size tuning asymmetry).
- Clarity: The paper’s high-level narrative is clear, and the backward-step intuition is helpful. However, the presentation suffers from numerous typos and grammatical errors, heavy notation, and missing algorithmic pseudocode. Some important quantities (Pt, Q, γi) are introduced abruptly and only partially defined. The connection to sADMM/PJADMM is deferred to an appendix without a succinct in-paper instantiation, which would strengthen understanding.
- Originality: Multi-block ADMM variants, randomized block coordinate schemes, and proximal Jacobian methods are active topics; the paper’s main novelty is the explicit dual backward step plus an analysis that ties stepsizes to both K and sparsity (di), along with a unifying view that recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extremes. To my knowledge, this specific combination and its analysis are new and interesting. Related randomized primal-dual/block-ADMM works are cited (RBSUMM, PJADMM), though a broader comparison to other randomized primal-dual methods could be strengthened.
- Significance: The contribution is practically relevant for large-scale structured problems (e.g., overlapping groups, RPCA) where parallel block updates are attractive. The theoretical results are of interest to the optimization-for-ML community. Empirical evidence suggests gains, but the lack of true parallel experiments and limited tuning/engineering for baselines dampens the practical impact demonstrated.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic insight: the dual backward step explains why naive Jacobi updates diverge and how to stabilize them.
- Stepsize rules that incorporate sparsity and number of updated blocks; interpretable extremes that recover sADMM and PJADMM.
- Convergence guarantees with O(1/T) complexity under constant stepsizes for both exact and inexact (Bregman) variants.
- Useful application to overlapping group lasso with an argument for larger τi via di = 2.

Weaknesses
- Clarity issues: inconsistent constants in augmented Lagrangian (ρ vs ρ/2), missing definitions (Pt, Q), and lack of a concise algorithm box.
- Experimental limitations: no parallel implementation, modest problem sizes, partial/favorable settings (e.g., sADMM implementation lacking dual update; GSADMM reported as “best” without systematic tuning across methods; RBSUMM parameterization not fully standardized).
- Theoretical exposition relies on supplemental material for key details; main text could better communicate sufficient conditions and intuition (e.g., why τi = K/[K̃i(2J−K)]).
- Some references are incomplete or misformatted; related randomized primal-dual literature could be discussed more comprehensively.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the augmented Lagrangian scaling: eq. (2) uses ρ‖Ax − a‖^2, while later updates and derivations use (ρ/2)‖·‖^2. Which convention holds in proofs and code?
- Can you provide explicit definitions of Pt and Q in the main text (or a short lemma summarizing their construction and bounds) and explain their roles?
- How sensitive is PDMM to misspecification of di and K (e.g., if di varies across rows or is estimated)? Can adaptive τi, νi be used?
- Could you report true parallel wall-clock results (e.g., multi-core) to substantiate the “parallel” advantage?
- For sADMM and GSADMM, can you ensure fair tuning, including dual steps when applicable, and provide per-iteration cost breakdowns?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a compact pseudocode algorithm with inputs (ρ, τi, νi, K), options for exact/inexact updates, and termination criteria.
- Include an ablation on τi and νi showing stability/convergence regimes and the effect of sparsity di, beyond the brief empirical tuning.
- Expand experiments to larger-scale data and provide parallel runtimes; include more recent baselines where possible.
- Proof-polish: unify constants, define all matrices in the main text, and streamline the Lyapunov argument for readability.
- Clean up typos/grammar and fix reference formatting.

Decision
Recommendation: weak accept.

Rationale: The paper offers a principled and practical modification to parallel multi-block ADMM with a novel dual backward step and a clean unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM. The convergence analysis and sparsity-aware stepsizes are technically interesting and relevant to NeurIPS. While clarity and experimental rigor can be improved, the core idea and theory are solid enough to merit acceptance, provided the authors address clarity issues and strengthen empirical validation.
