

# Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel algorithm called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints, specifically addressing the limitation of existing ADMM variants for multiple blocks. The authors tackle the challenge of parallelizing ADMM while maintaining convergence guarantees, which has been a significant open problem in the literature. PDMM is a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM that updates any number of blocks in parallel, using a dual backward step to make conservative progress on the dual variable. The step sizes are carefully chosen based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks being updated. The authors establish that existing methods like sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) are special cases of PDMM, providing a valuable theoretical unification. The paper demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where previous methods either fail or are inefficient.

This work builds on the extensive literature on ADMM (e.g., [2, 12, 20]) and randomized block coordinate methods (e.g., [16, 18]), addressing a key gap in the literature regarding parallelizable ADMM variants for multiple blocks with convergence guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for analyzing parallel block coordinate ADMM variants. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, which is a major improvement over existing methods like RBSUMM that require decreasing step sizes or strict local error bounds.

2. **Novel Algorithm Design**: PDMM's dual backward step is a clever and well-justified mechanism to address convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates. The careful derivation of step sizes based on sparsity of A and number of blocks being updated is a strong point, and the paper provides a clear theoretical justification for these choices.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates the practical utility of PDMM on real-world problems (robust PCA and overlapping group lasso) where previous methods either fail or are inefficient. The experiments show significant runtime improvements over existing methods, particularly for problems with overlapping structures.

4. **Clear Connections to Prior Work**: The authors properly situate their work in relation to existing literature, showing how sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. This provides a valuable theoretical unification of several existing methods, which is particularly insightful for understanding the role of the dual backward step.

5. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The experimental section is well-designed, with careful comparison to multiple baselines across two different problem domains. The analysis of how the number of blocks K affects performance is particularly insightful, revealing that PDMM can achieve comparable or better performance with fewer blocks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Methods**: While the paper compares to sADMM and RBSUMM, it does not compare to more recent methods like the parallel multi-block ADMM variants proposed in [10] (which the authors reference) or the more recent work on randomized ADMM. This makes it harder to fully assess the relative novelty and performance improvements.

2. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The convergence analysis relies on Assumption 1, which requires the existence of a KKT point for the Lagrangian with ρ=0. While this is standard for ADMM, it would be useful to discuss how restrictive this is in practice for the types of problems the authors consider.

3. **Experimental Details**: The experimental section could benefit from more details about the implementation choices (e.g., how exactly the random block selection is implemented, what specific values of ρ were used for each method, etc.). The paper mentions "best" results for some methods but doesn't clarify how these were selected.

4. **Theoretical Complexity Analysis**: While the paper establishes O(1/T) iteration complexity, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the per-iteration computational complexity of PDMM compared to other methods. This is particularly relevant for the experimental results where PDMM with different K values shows varying performance.

5. **Scalability Considerations**: The paper demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness for problems of moderate size (e.g., m=1000, n=5000 for RPCA), but it doesn't discuss how the algorithm scales to extremely large problems (e.g., m=10^6, n=10^6), where communication costs might become a bottleneck.

## Evaluation

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is thorough and the convergence proofs are rigorous. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing PDMM outperforms baselines in terms of runtime. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the trade-off between number of blocks and subproblem complexity). The paper presents a complete piece of work, not just a position paper.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work in the literature. The theoretical analysis is presented in a logical manner. However, some of the technical details (e.g., the exact form of the Bregman divergence terms) could be made clearer for readers unfamiliar with this technique. The experimental section is well-organized and easy to follow.

### Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution by introducing PDMM as a unified framework for parallel block coordinate ADMM variants. The dual backward step mechanism is a key innovation that addresses a known convergence issue with direct Jacobi updates. While there are related works (like RBSUMM), PDMM's ability to handle arbitrary numbers of blocks in parallel with constant step size and convergence guarantees is novel. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM as special cases is particularly insightful.

### Significance
The results are significant for the optimization community and practitioners working on large-scale machine learning problems. The ability to efficiently solve block-separable convex problems with overlapping structures (which are common in structured sparsity applications) is valuable. The O(1/T) convergence rate with constant step size is important for practical applications where tuning step sizes can be challenging. The runtime improvements demonstrated in experiments suggest that PDMM could be a valuable tool for practitioners.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, particularly for large-scale problems with block-separable structures. The theoretical contributions are significant, the experimental results are compelling, and the algorithm has clear practical relevance.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with very recent methods and some missing details in the experimental setup. However, these do not significantly detract from the paper's overall quality.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel algorithm with rigorous theoretical guarantees
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature on parallelizable ADMM variants
- Demonstrates practical performance improvements over existing methods
- Provides valuable theoretical unification of existing methods
- Well-written with clear experimental evaluation

## Arguments against Acceptance
- Limited comparison with the most recent methods in the field
- Some missing implementation details in experiments
- Theoretical assumptions might be restrictive for some applications

The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh these weaknesses, and the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in parallel optimization methods and provides valuable insights for both theoretical and practical applications.
