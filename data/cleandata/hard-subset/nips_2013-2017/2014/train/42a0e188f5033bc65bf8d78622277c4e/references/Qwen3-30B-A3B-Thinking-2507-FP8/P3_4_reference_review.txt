

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key limitation of existing multi-block ADMM variants (both Gauss-Seidel and Jacobian) by introducing a dual backward step that enables parallel updates of multiple blocks while maintaining convergence guarantees. PDMM generalizes two existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases, and the authors establish global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity under fairly standard assumptions.

The paper is well-positioned within the NeurIPS literature on optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning. It builds on the extensive ADMM literature (e.g., Boyd et al. 2011, Hong et al. 2016) while addressing a specific gap in multi-block optimization methods that has been noted in recent work (Chen et al. 2016, Hong et al. 2017).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a unified framework for parallel block coordinate ADMM with constant step size. The dual backward step mechanism is well-motivated and provides a clear explanation for why direct Jacobi updates often fail.

2. **Novel Connections**: The demonstration that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is particularly valuable. This provides new theoretical insights into the relationship between these methods and the role of the dual backward step.

3. **Practical Algorithm**: The algorithm is designed with practical considerations in mind, taking into account the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks being updated. This is a thoughtful touch that enhances the algorithm's real-world applicability.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are well-designed and show consistent improvements over existing methods. The authors carefully tune parameters and provide meaningful comparisons with relevant baselines.

5. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear notation and logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm to theory to experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper compares with sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM, it doesn't include comparisons with more recent methods like the parallel multi-block ADMM with O(1/k) convergence (Deng et al., 2016) or other recent advances in asynchronous ADMM.

2. **Lack of Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis showing how PDMM's cost scales with the number of blocks or problem size. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

3. **Limited Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity**: While the paper tunes parameters for experiments, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how sensitive PDMM is to the choice of K (number of blocks updated per iteration) beyond the experimental results.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The convergence guarantees rely on the existence of a KKT point (Assumption 1), which is standard but worth noting as a limitation for some non-convex extensions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the approach.

The paper advances the state of the art in multi-block ADMM by providing a unified framework that explains and generalizes existing methods while addressing their limitations. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is particularly insightful and adds value beyond just proposing a new algorithm.

The main limitation is the lack of comparison with some of the most recent multi-block ADMM variants, but this doesn't significantly undermine the paper's core contributions. The theoretical and empirical results are robust, and the paper is well-written with clear contributions.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning. The theoretical framework is sound, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of practical advantages. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions relative to existing work.

The minor weaknesses noted (limited comparison with very recent work and lack of detailed complexity analysis) do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The authors have made a valuable contribution to a problem that is increasingly important as machine learning models grow in scale and complexity.

The paper's significance is particularly strong because it provides a practical algorithm that can be applied to a wide range of important machine learning problems (RPCA, overlapping group lasso, etc.) while offering strong theoretical guarantees. This bridges the gap between theoretical optimization and practical machine learning applications.

I recommend acceptance to NeurIPS. The paper would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings and would likely be of interest to a broad audience in the machine learning community.
