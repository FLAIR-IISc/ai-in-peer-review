Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses minimization of block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. The authors build on the augmented Lagrangian framework and ADMM. Direct Jacobi (fully parallel) ADMM is known to diverge for multi-block problems; Gauss–Seidel variants (GSADMM/BSUMM) have partial guarantees under restrictive conditions, and randomized versions like RBSUMM typically update one block and require local error bounds and decreasing stepsizes. Proximal-Jacobi ADMM (PJADMM) adds proximal terms to ensure convergence, achieving o(1/T) under sufficiently large proximal terms.

The proposed Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) is a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that can update an arbitrary number K of blocks per iteration. Its distinguishing features are: (i) a “dual backward step” that counteracts the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty in the primal subproblem; (ii) stepsizes for dual ascent (τi) and backward step (νi) that depend on the sparsity pattern of A via di (the number of nonzeros in row-block i) and on K; and (iii) support for exact or inexact primal updates via an added Bregman divergence term, unifying exact PDMM, proximal variants, and enabling closed-form updates for otherwise costly subproblems. The authors show that sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM are limiting cases of PDMM (small dual step with exact primal updates vs. large proximal terms with full dual step).

Under standard convexity assumptions and existence of KKT points, the paper proves global convergence in expectation and an O(1/T) ergodic rate for objective suboptimality and feasibility violation with constant stepsizes τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, where K̃i = min{di, K}. Experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso suggest PDMM attains favorable iteration/runtime performance compared to GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM in a sequential MATLAB implementation. The effect of K and of stepsizes (τi, νi) is explored.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design is well-motivated: the dual backward step addresses over-aggressive dual motion caused by implicit ascent in the primal update. The dependence of τi and νi on di and K is intuitive and yields larger steps when A is sparse or when fewer blocks are updated. Theoretical results (global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic complexity) are consistent with the state of the art for convex, multi-block methods. The distance/potential function h(·) and residual R(·) are defined, with monotonic decrease in expectation. However, several technical quantities (e.g., the precise definitions/choices of the PSD matrices Pt and Q used in R and h) are only referenced as “some positive semidefinite matrix” and deferred to the supplement; having at least one explicit admissible choice in the main text would improve verifiability.
- Assumptions are mild (closed, proper, convex; existence of a KKT point). The inexact framework via Bregman divergence is standard and the conditions on strong convexity/Lipschitz gradient to ensure Bϕ ≥ (1/η)Bψ are reasonable.
- Claims about sADMM and PJADMM as special cases appear plausible given the parameterization, but full equivalence is relegated to the appendix. Including a concise derivation for the two-block splitting case in the main text would strengthen the exposition.
- Empirics: The experiments are indicative but limited. All methods are run sequentially, which does not demonstrate the parallel advantage suggested by the algorithm’s design. Comparisons sometimes rely on “best” parameter choices without detailing the search protocol. The sADMM implementation used lacks a dual update (τi = 0), which is noted, but this weakens the comparison. No multi-threaded or distributed wall-clock speedups are shown.

Clarity
The paper clearly states the problem, algorithm, and high-level intuition for the dual backward step and stepsize rules. The connection to existing ADMM variants is helpful. However, clarity is hampered by:
- Numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “addded,” “defiend,” “convegence,” “seperable”; Acj vs Ajt; mixing superscripts and subscripts).
- Some opaque notation (stacked z variables, Pt and Q) without concrete instantiation in the main text.
- Occasional dense passages that would benefit from a brief derivation or example (e.g., derivation of τi, νi; a worked mini-example of overlapping groups mapping to A with di = 2).

Originality
The contribution is incremental but nontrivial: PDMM unifies and generalizes multi-block ADMM variants by introducing a dual backward step with data-dependent stepsizes and allowing multiple randomized block updates per iteration. Prior randomized methods typically update one block; prior Jacobi methods rely on proximal regularization or splitting. The explicit dependence of steps on sparsity (di) and the interpretation of sADMM and PJADMM as extremes within a single framework appear to be novel.

Significance
Given the broad applicability of multi-block convex programs in ML (RPCA, structured sparsity), a convergent, parallelizable, randomized multi-block ADMM with constant stepsizes is valuable. The O(1/T) ergodic rate aligns with current standards. The practical impact would be strengthened by demonstrations of real parallel speedups and larger-scale studies; currently, results are promising but not definitive.

Pros
- Conceptually sound fix (dual backward step) to stabilize Jacobi ADMM in multi-block settings.
- Stepsize formulas exploit sparsity and number of updated blocks; intuitive and potentially large practical benefit.
- Unifying view: sADMM and PJADMM as special cases; accommodates exact and inexact updates via Bregman terms.
- Convergence guarantees under standard assumptions; O(1/T) rate with constant stepsizes.
- Empirical evidence on two representative applications; exploration of K and stepsize effects.

Cons
- Main text omits critical details (explicit Pt, Q choices; full equivalences to sADMM/PJADMM), relying on supplement.
- Experimental evaluation is sequential and limited in scope; no parallel or distributed benchmarks; some baselines disadvantaged (sADMM implementation lacking dual step).
- Presentation issues: typos, notation inconsistencies, and occasional ambiguity.
- Convergence results are in expectation and ergodic; no non-ergodic rates or high-probability bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit admissible Pt and Q in the main text and a brief derivation of τi, νi to clarify their dependence on di and K.
- Include a concise, self-contained derivation showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.
- Expand experiments: implement genuine parallelism (multi-core or distributed), report speedups vs. thread/core count, and add more datasets. Ensure fair baselines (sADMM with proper dual update; PJADMM with tuned proximal terms).
- Add ablations isolating the effect of the backward step (vary νi) and stepsizes (vary τi) beyond the current limited tuning.
- Improve writing and notation consistency.

Overall assessment
This work offers a principled and practically motivated variant of multi-block ADMM with solid convergence guarantees and a unifying perspective. The theoretical contribution is sound and moderately novel; the empirical section, while promising, is not yet commensurate with the algorithm’s parallel claims. With stronger experimental validation and improved clarity, the paper would be a strong contribution. As it stands, it is a borderline but positive submission on technical grounds.
