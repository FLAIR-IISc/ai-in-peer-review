### NIPS Review

**Paper Summary:**
This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a well-studied and important problem class in machine learning. The key challenge in this area is that the direct parallelization (Jacobi-style update) of the popular Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) often fails to converge for more than two blocks. The authors identify the cause as an overly aggressive "implicit dual ascent" step within the primal update.

The core idea of PDMM is to counteract this by introducing a "dual backward step," which makes the dual variable updates more conservative. Crucially, the step sizes for both the forward dual update and the backward step are intelligently adapted based on two factors: the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (`K`) being updated in parallel at each iteration. This allows the algorithm to flexibly trade off between fully sequential and fully parallel updates. The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are extreme cases of their more general framework, providing a valuable unifying perspective. The method's utility is demonstrated on robust PCA and, notably, on overlapping group lasso, which they frame as a randomized block coordinate descent method for a problem that is challenging for standard RBCD.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of introducing an adaptive "dual backward step" to stabilize parallel ADMM is both simple and profound. It directly addresses a known failure mode of naive parallelization with clear intuition. This is not an incremental tweak but a fundamental new mechanism for designing parallel ADMM-style algorithms.

2.  **Unifying Framework:** The paper's demonstration that sADMM (for exact primal updates) and PJADMM (for inexact updates) are special cases of PDMM is a significant contribution. It elegantly connects the degree of inexactness in the primal step with the required conservatism in the dual step. This provides the community with a much deeper understanding of the design space for parallel primal-dual methods.

3.  **Algorithmic Flexibility and Adaptivity:** PDMM is not a single algorithm but a flexible framework. The ability to choose the number of parallel blocks (`K`) is a powerful feature for practitioners. More impressively, the automatic adaptation of step sizes based on constraint matrix sparsity (`d_i`) is a sophisticated feature that allows the algorithm to exploit problem structure for potentially faster convergence.

4.  **Bold Application:** Applying PDMM to the overlapping group lasso problem and framing it as a solution to an open problem for randomized block coordinate descent is a strong point. It shows that the framework is not just for canonical ADMM problems but can be cleverly adapted to solve other structured optimization challenges.

5.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide proofs for global convergence and an O(1/T) rate, which is the standard for this class of methods. This places the new algorithm on firm theoretical ground and is on par with the guarantees for classic ADMM.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of True Parallel Implementation:** The experiments are conducted sequentially in Matlab. While this is sufficient to validate the convergence properties (e.g., number of iterations) and compare per-iteration costs, it doesn't demonstrate the practical wall-clock time speedups achievable in a true parallel computing environment. This is a minor weakness, as the main contribution is the algorithm and its analysis, but a parallel implementation would have made the experimental section more compelling.

2.  **Comparison with Baselines:** In the RPCA experiment, a well-tuned sequential GSADMM converges in fewer iterations than PDMM. While the authors correctly note that PDMM can be parallelized and has a slightly lower per-iteration cost, leading to potential runtime gains, the advantage is not overwhelming in the presented sequential simulation. The strength of PDMM lies in its parallel nature, which is not fully captured by the experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a significant and novel concept (the adaptive dual backward step) to solve a long-standing problem in parallel optimization.
*   Provides a new, unifying perspective that deepens the understanding of several existing algorithms.
*   The proposed method is flexible, adaptive, and theoretically well-supported.
*   Addresses a difficult problem (overlapping group lasso) in a novel way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical validation could be stronger, particularly with a real parallel implementation to showcase practical speedups.

**Recommendation:**
This paper is an excellent candidate for acceptance. Its primary value lies in its high degree of novelty and the significant conceptual contribution it makes to the understanding of parallel primal-dual algorithms. The introduction of the adaptive dual backward step is a genuinely new and powerful idea. The unification of prior work and the flexible nature of the algorithm are significant strengths. While the experimental validation is not perfect (lacking a true parallel implementation), this is a minor flaw that is easily outweighed by the paper's theoretical and conceptual strengths. This work presents a bold new direction and is likely to inspire follow-up research in large-scale optimization. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
