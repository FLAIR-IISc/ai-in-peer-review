This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems, a common framework in machine learning applications like RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors aim to improve upon existing ADMM-based methods, particularly in handling multiple blocks where convergence can be challenging. PDMM proposes a randomized block coordinate update scheme with a ‘backward step’ on the dual update, dynamically adjusting step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomly selected blocks. The paper establishes convergence guarantees and demonstrates the method's effectiveness through experiments.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on ADMM and its variants. It correctly identifies the challenges of extending ADMM to multiple blocks and positions PDMM as a generalization of existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, offering a unifying perspective. The authors appropriately reference relevant work in optimization, sparse recovery, and machine learning, including foundational papers on ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011], RPCA [Candes et al., 2011], and related algorithms. The problem formulation is standard and well-motivated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of randomized block coordinate updates with a dual backward step, dynamically adjusting step sizes based on problem structure, is a novel contribution. The demonstrated connection to sADMM and PJADMM provides further insight into the algorithm’s behavior and design choices.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides convergence analysis with established iteration complexity bounds. The assumptions are reasonable and aligned with standard conditions for ADMM convergence. The theoretical framework appears rigorous, although detailed proofs are relegated to the supplement.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly consistent, and the algorithmic description is clear. The motivation for each design choice (backward step, dynamic step size) is adequately explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM in RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems, showing improvements over existing ADMM variants.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Detail:** While the experiments demonstrate PDMM's performance, the experimental setup lacks some detail. For instance, the parameter tuning process for competing methods isn’t fully described. The claim that “best” results were chosen for GSADMM and RBSUMM requires further clarification – what metrics were used, and how many trials were performed?
*   **Complexity of Parameter Selection:** Although the paper provides formulas for τi and νi, the practical implications of choosing K (the number of randomly updated blocks) aren’t thoroughly discussed. The experiments offer some guidance, but a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs involved would be valuable.
*   **Supplement Reliance:** Crucial details, specifically the full proofs of the theoretical results, are in the supplement. While this is standard practice, it hinders a complete assessment within the page limit. The main paper could benefit from including key lemmas or a more detailed sketch of the proof strategy.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to two applications. Extending the evaluation to a broader range of problems and datasets would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Relation to existing stochastic methods:** The method is positioned as a new approach, but the relation to standard stochastic optimization techniques could be clarified. How does PDMM relate to, e.g., SAGA or SVRG in terms of convergence guarantees and practical performance?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a widely applicable optimization problem.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing state-of-the-art algorithms.
*   The paper provides valuable insights into the behavior of ADMM variants and offers a unifying perspective on existing approaches.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and is limited in scope.
*   The reliance on the supplement for critical details hinders a complete assessment of the paper's contributions.
*   The discussion of practical parameter selection and the connection to related stochastic optimization techniques could be more thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While some aspects of the experimental setup and parameter selection could be clarified, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more detailed experimental settings and expanding the discussion of parameter selection.
