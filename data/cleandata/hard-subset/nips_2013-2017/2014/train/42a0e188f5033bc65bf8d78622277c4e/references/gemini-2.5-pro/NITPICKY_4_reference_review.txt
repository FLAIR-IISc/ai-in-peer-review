This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block-coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core idea is to update a randomly selected subset of `K` primal blocks in parallel. The method's key features include a "backward step" on the dual variable to control its progress and a novel step-size selection scheme that adapts to the constraint matrix's block sparsity and the number of blocks `K` being updated. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing an O(1/T) convergence rate. They also argue that two existing Jacobian ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, thereby providing a unified framework. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Algorithmic Contributions:** The proposed PDMM framework is elegant. The integration of a dual backward step with step sizes (`τi`, `νi`) that depend on both problem structure (sparsity `di`) and algorithmic choice (parallelism degree `K`) is a novel and well-motivated contribution. This provides a principled way to tune step sizes, which is often a challenge in multi-block ADMM methods.

2.  **Theoretical Unification:** The paper's claim to unify sADMM and PJADMM as two extremes of the PDMM framework is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides valuable insight into the role of the dual backward step and the trade-off between the accuracy of the primal update and the aggressiveness of the dual update.

3.  **Strong Application Case:** The application of PDMM to the overlapping group lasso problem is compelling. By reformulating the problem and leveraging the sparsity-aware step sizes, the authors demonstrate a significant performance advantage over methods that ignore this structure, such as sADMM. This highlights the practical utility of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper, despite its strong core ideas, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and experimental evaluation, which collectively undermine the confidence in its results.

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Algorithm and Theory:**
    *   **Missing Definition:** The algorithm's description in equations (5)-(7) is critically incomplete. The primal update (5) depends on an intermediate dual variable `ŷt`, but its definition is absent from the algorithm block. A definition (`ŷt = yt − νρ(Axt − a)`) is mentioned informally later in the text, but this crucial step must be explicitly stated as part of the algorithm's iterative process.
    *   **Typographical Errors in Key Equations:** Equation (8) presents the formula for the backward step parameter as `νi = 1− K̃i`. This is incorrect, as `K̃i` is an integer count of blocks; the text and theory sections later imply the formula should be `νi = 1− 1/K̃i`. Such a fundamental typo in a core equation is a serious issue.
    *   **Vague Theoretical Definitions:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is difficult to follow and verify due to vague and ill-defined terms. The residual `R(xt+1)` in Eq. (19) and the Lyapunov function `h(v*,vt)` in Eq. (20) depend on matrices `Pt` and `Q` that are only described as "positive semi-definite" without any concrete definition. Furthermore, the expressions for `γi` in Eq. (21) and `ζi` in Lemma 1 appear to have significant formatting errors, rendering them uninterpretable. The parameter `βi = KJ/K̃i` used in the complexity theorem also seems suspect, potentially containing an extraneous factor of `J`. These issues make the theoretical claims unverifiable.

2.  **Inadequate Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The paper's title and core contribution relate to a *parallel* method. However, all experiments were run sequentially. Without any experiments or even simulations demonstrating parallel performance (e.g., speedup curves), the primary motivation for the algorithm remains unsubstantiated.
    *   The presentation of the RPCA results is confusing. In one part, the fully parallel PDMM3 is faster than the randomized variants, while in the "parameter tuning" section, the randomized PDMM1 is the fastest. This inconsistency is not adequately explained.
    *   The authors' note that the public code for sADMM uses `τi = 0` suggests they may be comparing against a misconfigured baseline. For a fair comparison, sADMM should be implemented correctly, especially since it is a special case of their own framework.

3.  **Sloppy Scholarship and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript is riddled with minor but frequent formatting and grammatical errors. Examples include the inconsistent and non-standard notation for summations (`J∑ j=1`), inconsistent citation formatting in the bibliography, and awkward phrases ("the converge rate," "local error bounds to be hold").
    *   Crucial proofs connecting PDMM to sADMM and PJADMM are relegated to an appendix of the authors' own ArXiv preprint [25]. For a peer-reviewed submission, key theoretical claims should be supported by proofs in a supplement, not by reference to a non-peer-reviewed version of the same paper.
    *   The paper makes speculative claims, such as in Remark 1(b) regarding BSUMM's convergence, without providing justification.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a promising and conceptually interesting extension of ADMM, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The lack of clarity in the algorithm's definition and the theoretical section is a major barrier to verifying the paper's technical soundness. The experimental evaluation is weak, particularly concerning the absence of any results that speak to the algorithm's parallel performance. Finally, the numerous typographical and formatting errors throughout the manuscript suggest a lack of care in its preparation.

The ideas in this paper have significant potential. However, in its current state, the work does not meet the high standards of quality and clarity expected for NIPS. The sheer number of minor and major flaws prevents me from recommending acceptance.

**Recommendation: Reject**
