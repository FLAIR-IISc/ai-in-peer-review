Summary
The paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of the method of multipliers for convex problems of the form min_x ∑_j f_j(x_j) subject to Ax=a. PDMM updates K randomly selected primal blocks in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian with optional Bregman proximal terms, and performs a “dual backward step” (ŷ) that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. A key contribution is to set block-wise dual step sizes τ_i and the backward parameter ν_i as explicit functions of both the number of selected blocks K and the row-sparsity of A via d_i, yielding larger steps when A is sparse. The authors show that (i) two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) and (ii) proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are recovered as limiting cases, and prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convex assumptions. Empirically, PDMM is tested on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, showing favorable iteration/time performance versus GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM, and standard ADMM in the considered settings.

Relation to prior work
The work sits among multi-block ADMM variants (GSADMM, PJADMM, sADMM), randomized block-coordinate methods (RBCD, RBSUMM), and proximal/Bregman ADMM variants. The “dual backward step” can be viewed as a relaxation/correction to counteract the implicit dual ascent from the augmented term, which is consistent with stability devices used in multi-block ADMM (e.g., damping, back substitution). The explicit dependence of τ_i and ν_i on K and on degrees d_i is a distinguishing design choice, and the ergodic O(1/T) rate matches standard ADMM guarantees.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides a clear potential-function style analysis with a residual R(·) and Lyapunov h(v*,v^t), proving monotone decrease in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic rates under basic convexity/KKT existence. The parameterization τ_i=K/(K̃_i(2J−K)), ν_i=1−1/K̃_i with K̃_i=min{d_i,K} is interesting and leverages sparsity. However, several technical details critical for reproducibility are deferred or not fully specified in the main text:
  - P^t and Q are only described as positive semidefinite; explicit forms and how they arise in the descent are needed in the main paper (not just the supplement).
  - The exact derivation/intuition of the τ_i, ν_i formulas is only sketched. A brief proof sketch or inequality that dictates these constants would help.
  - For inexact PDMM, the assumptions on ψ_j (Lipschitz gradient) and ϕ_j (strong convexity) are mentioned but the constants and their role in tuning η_j are not made explicit. Practical guidance on selecting η_j is missing.
  - Convergence is ergodic; non-ergodic rates or primal-dual gap guarantees are not discussed.
- Experiments: The studies (RPCA; overlapping group lasso via standard splitting) are relevant. Strengths include exploring the effect of K and showing benefits when A is sparse (group lasso setting). However:
  - All runs are sequential, despite the method’s core motivation being parallelization. Wall-clock parallel speedups or distributed experiments would significantly strengthen the empirical case.
  - The sADMM comparison is unfair in one place: the public implementation used τ_i=0 (no dual update). Since the paper claims sADMM is a special case of PDMM for τ_i=1/J, ν_i=1−1/J, the authors should implement that baseline properly.
  - RBSUMM is reported to not converge within 5000 iterations under various schedules; a more systematic tuning or citing its recommended parameter policies would improve fairness. Also include the number of passes through data as a metric for randomized methods.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with good high-level intuition (implicit vs explicit dual ascent; role of sparsity and K). Notational load is high but standard for this area. There are several typos and minor grammatical issues (“block-seperable,” “defiend,” “convegence,” “contast”); a careful proofread is needed. The main text should define P^t and Q and include at least one worked-out proximal choice for a common loss (beyond the brief logistic example) to aid reproducibility.

Originality
The main novelty lies in (i) the explicit “dual backward step” combined with (ii) step-size rules that depend on row degrees d_i and on K, within a randomized parallel multi-block setting; and (iii) unifying sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of the same framework. While the broad idea of damping/relaxation and proximalization in multi-block ADMM is not new, embedding these choices into per-row-step sizes driven by sparsity and allowing K>1 randomized updates appears to be a useful synthesis. The mapping to existing methods is valuable, though much of it is in the appendix.

Significance
For practitioners solving linearly constrained composite problems with sparse A or structured overlaps, PDMM offers a flexible alternative to sADMM/PJADMM with provable guarantees and potentially better constants when A is sparse. The ability to update K>1 blocks at each step (unlike RBSUMM) is practically relevant. That said, the theoretical rate is standard O(1/T), and the empirical evaluation lacks true parallel results. The impact would increase with a stronger empirical study and clearer guidance on parameter choices.

Questions/requests for authors
1) Please define P^t and Q in the main paper and briefly justify the τ_i, ν_i choices (a derivation or tight inequality).
2) Provide practical guidance for choosing η_j and the Bregman kernels for common losses/regularizers; include sensitivity plots.
3) Implement a fair sADMM baseline with the claimed parameters; otherwise, remove the comparison that uses τ_i=0.
4) Include parallel/distributed experiments demonstrating wall-clock speedups and scaling with K and number of workers.
5) Discuss non-ergodic convergence (if any) and convergence of the primal-dual gap.
6) Clarify how the grouping/partitioning scheme for randomized updates affects τ_i and convergence (beyond simple cyclic permutations).

Pros
- Clear framework unifying sADMM and PJADMM; insightful “dual backward step.”
- Step-size design that leverages A’s sparsity and number of updated blocks.
- Global convergence and O(1/T) rate under standard assumptions without diminishing steps.
- Applicable to overlapping group problems; simple reduction provided.
- Empirical indications of improved iteration/runtime in tested tasks.

Cons
- Key technical definitions (P^t, Q) missing from main text; limited derivation for step sizes.
- No true parallel experiments; sequential MATLAB runs weaken the practical claim.
- Some baselines are not fairly implemented/tuned (notably sADMM).
- Parameter selection guidance for inexact PDMM is limited.
- Writing needs polishing; several typos.

Overall
This is a solid and timely contribution on randomized parallel multi-block ADMM-like methods with a useful perspective on stabilizing the dual updates and exploiting sparsity. I lean toward a weak accept contingent on improving fairness of comparisons, adding at least one parallel experiment, and clarifying key theoretical elements in the main text.
