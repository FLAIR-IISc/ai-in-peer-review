Summary
The paper proposes PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex problems with linear constraints and block-separable objectives. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks each iteration. The key design is a “dual backward step” ŷ = y − νρ(Ax − a) to counteract the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian, coupled with a dual ascent step y ← y + τρ(Ax − a). The step sizes τi and νi depend on the number of updated blocks K and the sparsity pattern of A via the per-row degree di, allowing larger dual steps when A is sparse. PDMM also admits inexact primal updates via Bregman divergences, unifying exact and proximalized variants. The authors show that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases (extremes) of PDMM, prove global convergence with O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard assumptions, and provide experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicating improved empirical performance.

Relation to prior work
Multi-block ADMM can diverge; prior fixes include Gauss–Seidel variants with safeguards, variable-splitting-based two-block reductions (sADMM), and Jacobian/proximal variants (PJADMM). RBSUMM gives randomized updates of a single block with stringent assumptions and no known complexity. PDMM’s novelty is: (i) explicit dual backward step to temper the implicit dual ascent; (ii) per-row-degree- and K-aware step-size rules; (iii) randomized parallel updates of any number of blocks; and (iv) a unified inexact framework via Bregman distances. The claimed O(1/T) ergodic rate matches classic ADMM rates, but now for a randomized parallel multi-block method with constant step sizes and without local error bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The “dual backward step” is a clean, interpretable fix for the instability of naive Jacobi ADMM. The observation that the quadratic term performs implicit dual ascent is insightful, and the backward step is a principled way to balance primal and dual progress.
- Step-size design that adapts to the sparsity structure (degrees di) and K is elegant and practically useful; it helps explain why multiple-block parallel updates can remain stable and effective.
- Unifying view: sADMM and PJADMM appear as extremes of PDMM, clarifying the role of primal progress vs. dual step sizes and providing a broader lens on multi-block ADMM design.
- Theory: Global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions, with constant step sizes, for randomized K-block Jacobi-style updates. The optimality residual is carefully defined; the analysis accounts for randomness over block selections.
- Practicality: The inexact PDMM via Bregman divergence can yield closed-form updates for common losses/regularizers. The overlapping-group lasso formulation demonstrates PDMM’s applicability to overlapping structures where classic RBCD struggles.

Weaknesses
- Relation to existing primal–dual methods could be strengthened. The backward step resembles under-relaxation/extragradient ideas known in the PDHG/Chambolle–Pock family and relaxed ADMM; a clearer comparison (including empirical baselines) would help establish distinctiveness.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and largely sequential in Matlab, despite PDMM’s parallel motivation. Parallel or distributed implementations and scaling studies (varying K, di, and processor counts) would better support the practical value.
- Baseline fairness: The sADMM implementation used reportedly lacks a dual update; this undermines the comparison. More careful tuning of ρ and inclusion of properly implemented sADMM/PJADMM would be appropriate.
- No improvement in worst-case complexity beyond O(1/T); the contribution is qualitative (stability/parallelizability) rather than a stronger rate. Any linear-rate results under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity, full rank) would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: The paper is dense and has several typos (e.g., “seperable,” “convegence”). Some notation and the role of Pt, Q, βi, γi could be streamlined for readability. The practical guidance for choosing ηjt and Bregman generators deserves more space.

Quality
The technical development appears sound at a high level: the stability issue is correctly diagnosed, the step-size rules are derived with attention to sparsity, and the analysis framework is coherent. However, the proofs are in the supplement; some steps (e.g., the specific constants in τi, νi) would benefit from intuition or sketches. Empirical results support the claims but would be stronger with truly parallel experiments and fairer baselines.

Clarity
Generally well organized but heavy. Adding a figure or table summarizing the design space (K, τ, ν, η) and how it specializes to sADMM/PJADMM would aid comprehension. Proof sketches and practical tuning advice would help practitioners. Fix typos and polish exposition.

Originality
Moderately high. While related to known ideas (relaxation/extragradient, proximal Jacobian ADMM), the specific dual backward step, the degree-aware step sizes, and the unifying view across sADMM and PJADMM with randomized K-block updates constitute a meaningful new direction.

Significance
Good. The approach addresses a known pain point—stable and efficient parallelization of multi-block ADMM—on a broad class of problems, including overlapping structures. If adopted, the step-size rules and backward step may influence practice.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please compare to PDHG/Chambolle–Pock and relaxed ADMM (over-/under-relaxation) both conceptually and empirically; is the backward step equivalent to a known relaxation or extragradient step?
- Provide ablations isolating the impact of the backward step (vary νi) and the degree-aware τi; include denser vs. sparser A.
- Include properly implemented sADMM and PJADMM with tuned ρ, and report sensitivity to τ, ν.
- Demonstrate parallel speedups on multicore/distributed systems and report scaling with K and processors.
- Discuss potential linear convergence under additional assumptions (strong convexity, error bounds).

Pros
- Novel, principled mechanism (dual backward step) with degree-aware step sizes.
- Unifies several ADMM variants; randomized K-block updates with theory.
- Practical inexact updates via Bregman divergences; solid applicability.

Cons
- Limited and partially unfair empirical comparisons; no parallel speedups.
- Overlap with existing relaxed/extragradient ideas not fully clarified.
- No rate improvement; presentation could be clearer.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-leaning positive). The paper introduces a fresh, useful idea with a unifying perspective and solid theory. Despite some experimental and exposition shortcomings, the conceptual contribution is strong enough to merit inclusion and is likely to spur follow-up work and practical adoption.

Confidence
Medium.
