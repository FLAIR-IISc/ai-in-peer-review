Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for convex optimization with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. PDMM updates K randomly chosen primal blocks in parallel and performs a modified dual update comprising an explicit ascent step with step size τi and a “backward” correction νi that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian. Step sizes depend on both the number of updated blocks K and the sparsity pattern of A via di (the number of nonzero block-columns connected to each dual block). The authors also include an inexact variant using Bregman divergences, unifying exact PDMM with proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) as one extreme (large proximal terms, τ=1, ν=0) and two-block ADMM with variable splitting (sADMM) as another (small τ, large ν). They provide global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard convex assumptions with constant step sizes. Experiments on synthetic robust PCA and overlapping group lasso suggest PDMM is competitive or faster than sADMM, RBSUMM, and (in some cases) GSADMM/ADMM.

This work builds on extensive literature on ADMM and its multi-block variants, including GSADMM [He et al.; Hong & Luo], Jacobian/proximal variants [Deng et al.], and randomized block methods [Richtárik & Takáč; Hong et al.’s RBSUMM]. The paper’s core novelty is the dual backward step coupled with step sizes that account for A’s sparsity and the randomly selected K, and a unifying perspective linking sADMM and PJADMM.

Strengths:
- Conceptual insight: clear identification of the implicit dual ascent inside augmented Lagrangian block updates and the use of a backward dual step to stabilize parallel updates.
- Generality: handles nonsmooth separable objectives, arbitrary number K of updated blocks, and overlapping group structures via splitting.
- Theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes; step sizes adapt to sparsity (di) and K. Unifies exact and inexact updates via Bregman terms.
- Connections: shows sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, clarifying when larger dual steps are safe (with proximal terms) versus when conservative steps are needed (exact updates).
- Empirics: on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM compares favorably to RBSUMM and sADMM, and is competitive with GSADMM/ADMM; analysis of K’s effect is useful.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data and sequential Matlab; no truly parallel/distributed runs, which is central to the claimed advantages.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: sADMM implementation used τ=0 due to public code limitations; results may understate sADMM performance. RBSUMM tuning may not be exhaustive.
- Clarity: some technical definitions (Pt, Q, βi) and their roles in residuals and Lyapunov functions are only briefly described; many details are deferred to the supplement.
- Scope of theory: only ergodic O(1/T) rates; no nonergodic rates or linear convergence under stronger assumptions (common in recent ADMM literature).
- Practical guidance: limited advice on choosing ηjt and Bregman generators for inexact updates; sensitivity to ρ is not discussed.

Quality:
Technically sound at a high level: the algorithmic design is well-motivated (implicit vs explicit dual ascent), and the convergence/complexity statements align with the ADMM literature. The step-size rules τi = K/[K̃i(2J−K)] and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i that account for K and di are a nice contribution and plausibly justified by the analysis (though proofs are in the supplement). The unification with sADMM/PJADMM strengthens the conceptual contribution. Experiments support claims qualitatively, but stronger empirical evidence (including real datasets, wall-clock on multicore/cluster, and better-matched baselines) would bolster the case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, but notation is heavy. The dual backward step and its intuition are well explained. However, several objects (Pt, Q, βi, ζi) and conditions appear abruptly; more intuition or a brief proof sketch would help. Typos and minor grammar issues (e.g., “seperable,” “addded,” “convegence”) should be corrected. A small table summarizing special cases (K=1, K=J, sparse A with small di, exact vs inexact) and the corresponding τ, ν would aid readers.

Originality:
Moderately high. While multi-block ADMM, randomized coordinate updates, and proximal/Bregman techniques are established, the particular combination—parallel randomized block updates with a calibrated dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes—appears novel. The explicit connection unifying sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM is insightful.

Significance:
Potentially significant for large-scale structured optimization in ML, especially when A is sparse and parallelism is available. The ability to tune K and exploit sparsity may yield practical speedups. The theoretical contribution (constant-step O(1/T) with new stepsizes) is incremental but meaningful.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Baselines: Re-run sADMM with a correct dual update (τ>0) to ensure fair comparison; if code limitations prevented this, implement the minimal change needed. For RBSUMM, clarify tuning protocol and report a few strong configurations.
- Parallel experiments: Include multicore/shared-memory or distributed experiments to validate parallel scalability and communication patterns (e.g., cost of computing Aix and global residuals).
- Practical guidance: Provide heuristics for ρ and ηjt selection; discuss sensitivity across problems. Clarify when to linearize fj vs the quadratic term and how to pick ψjt, ϕjt.
- Theory: Brief proof sketch in the main paper for Theorem 1 (the descent inequality and role of ν, τ), and define Pt and Q more concretely. Comment on whether nonuniform sampling of blocks or cyclic selection preserves convergence (experiments used cyclic for some cases).
- Stronger results: If feasible, add nonergodic convergence or linear rates under strong convexity/Lipschitz gradient assumptions, in line with recent ADMM analyses.
- Overlapping groups: The di=2 observation and τ=ν=0.5 for K=J are nice; consider adding a complexity comparison to standard ADMM under this structure.
- Minor: Fix typos, unify notation (Acj vs Aj), and ensure all references are complete.

Pros:
- Solid, well-motivated algorithm with a clear new idea (dual backward step).
- Sparsity/K-aware step sizes; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.
- General framework including inexact updates; global convergence with O(1/T).
- Empirical gains on two representative problems; analysis of K’s effect.

Cons:
- Limited and partially unfair empirical comparisons; no parallel runs.
- Some clarity issues and heavy reliance on supplement for proofs.
- Only ergodic O(1/T); no stronger rates.
- Limited guidance on practical parameter choices.

Overall recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and novel perspective on stabilizing parallel multi-block ADMM with theoretically grounded, sparsity-aware stepsizes, and provides a unifying view of existing methods. Strengthening empirical validation and clarifying some technical details would further improve the paper.
