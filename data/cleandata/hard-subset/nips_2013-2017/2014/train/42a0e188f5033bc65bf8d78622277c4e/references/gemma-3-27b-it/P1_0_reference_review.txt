## Peer Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate descent algorithm for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the limitations of existing ADMM variants, particularly for problems with a large number of blocks, by proposing a method that randomly updates *any number* of blocks in parallel.  Key contributions include a backward step on the dual update to promote conservative progress, consideration of matrix sparsity to optimize step size, and a theoretical analysis establishing convergence and iteration complexity. The authors position PDMM as a generalization of existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM and demonstrate its effectiveness on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso problems.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established foundation of ADMM and its extensions.  It correctly identifies the challenges in scaling ADMM to a large number of blocks (cite [15, 10, 14]), and addresses the potential for non-convergence in such scenarios.  The authors appropriately reference and contrast their work with Gaussian-Seidel ADMM, Jacobian variants like sADMM and PJADMM, and RBSUMM. The connection drawn between PDMM and these existing methods is insightful. The work acknowledges the parallelization efforts in randomized block coordinate descent [16, 18] and extends them to the augmented Lagrangian framework.  

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of randomly updating *any number* of blocks in parallel, coupled with the backward step and sparsity-aware step size selection, is a significant contribution. This offers a flexible approach to balancing parallelization and convergence stability.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a convergence analysis with established iteration complexity. Demonstrating O(1/T) convergence with a constant step size is a valuable theoretical result, especially considering the often strict conditions required for convergence in multi-block ADMM variants.
* **Generalization:**  Positioning sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM provides a unifying framework and deeper understanding of the impact of different design choices.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM and compare its performance favorably against existing methods. The ablation study investigating the effect of ‘K’ (number of blocks updated) is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for a new algorithm, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and how PDMM addresses them.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides guidelines for setting  τ<sub>i</sub> and ν<sub>i</sub>, the interplay between these parameters, K, and the specific problem structure could require considerable tuning in practice. The experiments present a simplified setting and a more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the presentation.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to other methods in the RPCA experiments lacks detailed parameter tuning information for those methods.  While the authors mention 'best' results, detailing the parameter search process would add credibility.  Also, the claim of superior performance needs to be backed by statistical significance tests.
* **Notation:** The notation, especially concerning block indexing and matrix partitioning, is dense and occasionally difficult to follow. A more consistent and potentially visual explanation of the indexing scheme would improve readability.
* **Discussion of Bregman Divergence:** The section on inexact PDMM utilizing Bregman divergences feels somewhat rushed. While the idea is sound, the explanation of its application and the specific choices of φ<sub>j</sub> could be more detailed.
* **Slightly Repetitive:** Some of the explanations and connections between PDMM and other methods are reiterated across different sections, creating a feeling of redundancy.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging optimization problem.
* The contribution of PDMM, generalizing existing methods and offering a more flexible parallelization strategy, is significant.
* The empirical results, although could be further strengthened, provide promising evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and contributes to a better understanding of ADMM convergence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The potential complexity of parameter tuning and the limited sensitivity analysis raise concerns about practical applicability.
* The comparison to other methods could be more thorough and statistically rigorous.
* The notation and some explanations could be improved for clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems. While there are areas for improvement in clarity and experimental detail, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

*   **Parameter Tuning Discussion:** Expand the discussion on parameter tuning, including recommendations for practical settings and potential strategies for automating the process.
*   **Experimental Detail:** Provide more detailed information on the parameter tuning process for all compared methods in the experiments. Include statistical significance tests to support performance claims.
*   **Notation Clarity:**  Revise the notation, potentially incorporating visual aids, to improve readability and reduce ambiguity.
*   **Bregman Divergence Explanation:**  Expand the explanation of the Bregman divergence section, providing more concrete examples and justifying the choices of functions.
*   **Reduce Redundancy:** Streamline the paper by removing repetitive explanations and connections.
