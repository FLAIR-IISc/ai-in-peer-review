### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), for minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to a general linear constraint. This is a canonical problem in machine learning and distributed optimization. PDMM is a parallel, randomized block-coordinate method. At each iteration, it randomly selects a subset of K primal variable blocks and updates them in a parallel, Jacobi-like fashion.

The key technical contributions that distinguish PDMM from prior work are threefold. First, it introduces a "dual backward step" to temper the dual variable update, which the authors argue is crucial for the convergence of parallel multi-block ADMM variants. Second, it proposes an adaptive step-size scheme for both the standard dual ascent and the backward step, where the step sizes (`τi`, `νi`) depend on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (K) updated in parallel. Third, the framework is general enough to unify two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, showing them to be two extreme cases of PDMM. This provides a valuable, unified perspective on these Jacobian-style ADMM algorithms.

The paper situates itself well within the literature on multi-block ADMM, which is largely divided into sequential Gauss-Seidel variants (like BSUMM) and parallel Jacobian variants. The authors correctly point out the known convergence difficulties of direct Jacobi updates and the often restrictive convergence conditions or unknown complexity of existing methods like RBSUMM. PDMM offers a flexible parallel approach with a proven O(1/T) convergence rate under standard assumptions, on par with classic ADMM. The paper also demonstrates an interesting application to overlapping group lasso problems, which are challenging for standard block-coordinate descent methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Unification:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel and well-motivated. The combination of randomized parallel updates with an adaptive dual backward step is a significant contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is particularly insightful, clarifying the trade-off between the exactness of the primal update and the aggressiveness of the dual update. This provides a deeper understanding of the design space for parallel ADMM-style methods.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Generality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. The assumptions required are standard for this class of problems. The framework is quite general, accommodating an arbitrary number of parallel block updates (K) and allowing for inexact primal updates via Bregman divergence, which enhances its practical applicability.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation for the dual backward step is explained intuitively by analyzing the failure of direct Jacobi updates. The connections to related work are thoroughly discussed, and the key differences are highlighted effectively.

4.  **Practical Potential:** The algorithm addresses a fundamental and important optimization problem. Its flexibility in choosing K allows practitioners to balance the amount of parallel work per iteration against the total number of iterations, potentially tuning the algorithm for specific hardware architectures. The experiments, especially on the overlapping group lasso problem, demonstrate promising performance against several relevant baselines.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Experimental Evaluation of Parallelism:** The primary motivation for a "parallel" method is speedup in wall-clock time on parallel hardware. However, all experiments were implemented and run sequentially in Matlab. While this is sufficient to compare iteration counts and per-iteration complexity, it does not demonstrate the real-world performance gains from parallelization. The claim that "PDMM can run in parallel but GSADMM only runs sequentially" is a key advantage that remains unsubstantiated by the experiments.
    *   **Suggestion:** To make the paper's claims more compelling, I strongly recommend adding experimental results from a true parallel implementation (e.g., using OpenMP or MPI). A plot of wall-clock time versus the number of processors/cores for different values of K would be highly valuable and would directly support the central premise of the paper.

2.  **Fairness of Comparisons:** The experimental comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment raises some concerns. The authors state that the public code for sADMM uses `τi = 0` (no dual update), which explains its slow convergence. They note that with the correct `τi = 1/3`, sADMM would be identical to their PDMM3. The current presentation makes sADMM look unnecessarily poor.
    *   **Suggestion:** For a fairer comparison, the authors should either implement sADMM with its proper dual update step or be more explicit in the plot's caption about this limitation of the specific implementation used. The latter is less preferable, as it still represents a weak baseline.

3.  **Intuition on Step-Size Choice:** The paper presents the specific formulas for the step sizes `τi` and `νi` in Equation (8) without much intuition in the main text. While the derivation is presumably in the supplement, the reader is left wondering why these specific, somewhat complex forms are necessary.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief, high-level explanation in Section 2 for the choice of these step sizes would greatly improve the paper's clarity. For instance, explaining how this choice precisely balances the progress made by the K parallel primal updates in the Lyapunov function used for the convergence proof would be very helpful.

4.  **Practical Guidance on Choosing K:** The paper shows empirically that the choice of K (the number of blocks to update) has a significant impact on performance. However, it offers little guidance on how a user should select K in practice. This is a crucial hyperparameter for the proposed method.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on the trade-offs involved in choosing K. For example, how does K relate to communication overhead in a distributed setting versus computational cost? Are there any problem-dependent heuristics for selecting a good value for K?

### Decision

This paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated algorithm for a fundamental class of optimization problems. The unification of existing methods is a strong conceptual contribution. The main weaknesses lie in the experimental section, which does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed parallel approach and contains a potentially unfair comparison. However, these weaknesses do not invalidate the core technical and theoretical contributions of the work, which are of high quality and will likely be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community. The suggestions above, particularly regarding a parallel implementation, would substantially strengthen the paper for a final version.

**Recommendation: Accept**
