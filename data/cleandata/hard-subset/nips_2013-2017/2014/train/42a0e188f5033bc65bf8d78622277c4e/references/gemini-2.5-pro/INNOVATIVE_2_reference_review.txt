This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core challenge the paper addresses is the known divergence of naive parallel (Jacobi-style) ADMM. The authors introduce a clever "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm, allowing for an arbitrary number of blocks to be updated in parallel at each iteration.

A key contribution is that the step sizes for the dual update and the backward step are not fixed but are intelligently adapted based on two factors: the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (`K`) being updated in parallel. This adaptivity is a significant departure from prior work. The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, which is on par with standard ADMM.

Conceptually, the work is very strong. It elegantly unifies two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, by showing they are extreme cases of PDMM (corresponding to exact primal updates with a backward step, and inexact primal updates with no backward step, respectively). This connection provides a new and insightful perspective on the role of primal update accuracy and dual step conservativeness in parallel optimization. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates how PDMM can be applied to solve composite minimization with overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion, which the authors claim is an open problem for standard randomized block coordinate descent methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The central idea of a "dual backward step" to enable convergent parallel updates is both novel and insightful. It directly addresses a fundamental instability in Jacobian-style ADMM. This is not an incremental improvement but rather a new mechanism that fundamentally alters the algorithm's dynamics for the better. The formulation of adaptive step sizes based on problem structure (`d_i`) and algorithmic choice (`K`) is also highly original.

2.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is a major strength. It moves beyond simply proposing a new algorithm and provides a deeper understanding of the entire class of parallel ADMM methods. This unifying view clarifies the trade-offs between different algorithmic choices and is likely to influence future research in this area.

3.  **Flexibility and Potential Practical Impact:** PDMM offers a flexible framework where the user can choose the degree of parallelism (`K`) to suit the specific computational architecture and problem structure. This provides a spectrum of algorithms, from fully sequential (`K=1`) to fully parallel (`K=J`), all under a single convergent framework. For large-scale machine learning, where parallelization is critical, this is a highly desirable property.

4.  **New Application:** The application to overlapping group lasso is an excellent demonstration of the method's power. By reformulating the problem and leveraging PDMM's structure, the authors provide a new, provably convergent randomized coordinate method for a problem that was difficult for previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Preliminary Experimental Validation of Parallelism:** While the experiments demonstrate strong convergence behavior on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems, they were conducted sequentially in Matlab. The true benefit of a parallel algorithm lies in its wall-clock time speedup on a multi-core or distributed system. The current experiments show that updating more blocks can reduce iteration count, but they don't directly validate the practical speedup from parallel execution. This is a validation gap.

2.  **Minor Issues in Experimental Comparison:** The comparison with sADMM in the RPCA experiment seems to be based on an implementation where the dual update step size was set to zero (`τ_i = 0`), leading to poor performance. The authors acknowledge this and note that sADMM would be equivalent to PDMM3 with the correct step size. While this doesn't invalidate PDMM's performance, a more carefully tuned baseline would have made the comparison more compelling.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The core ideas presented in this work are highly novel, conceptually significant, and address a long-standing challenge in parallelizing ADMM. The introduction of the dual backward step and the adaptive step-size rule is a bold and effective approach. While the experimental validation of true parallel speedup is incomplete, this is a minor flaw that I am willing to overlook given the strength and importance of the conceptual contribution. The paper provides a new, flexible, and provably convergent family of algorithms and deepens our understanding of existing methods by unifying them under a single framework. This is precisely the kind of work that pushes the field forward and is likely to be highly influential. The weaknesses are related to the completeness of the empirical study, not the soundness of the core ideas, and can be addressed in future work.
