This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors aim to extend ADMM to a parallel randomized block coordinate setting, addressing limitations of existing approaches like GSADMM and Jacobian variants. The key ideas include a backward step on the dual update to promote convergence, incorporating sparsity of the constraint matrix A, and enabling random updating of multiple primal blocks concurrently. Theoretical convergence guarantees with O(1/T) iteration complexity are established, and the method is evaluated on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, proposed method, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. The introduction effectively establishes the context, outlining the problem and its relevance to various machine learning applications. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing ADMM variants and highlights the shortcomings PDMM aims to address. The mathematical derivations are generally clear and well-explained, though a deeper understanding might require referring to the supplementary material. The connection established between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a valuable contribution, providing insights into the behavior of these algorithms. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the performance of PDMM compared to other methods, showing its effectiveness on the chosen problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The parallel randomized block coordinate approach with the backward step and consideration of sparsity is a novel contribution to the ADMM family of algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis and iteration complexity results provide a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and derivations.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of existing literature, accurately positioning their work within the field.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides guidelines for choosing parameters (τi, νi), the optimal settings might still require careful tuning for specific problems. The sensitivity analysis could be improved.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is limited to two applications. Exploring a wider range of problems would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
*   **Overlapping Block Lasso Setup:** The setup for the overlapping group lasso is not fully explained, and the benefits of PDMM in that scenario could be elaborated on more clearly. The statement that it solves the problem in a randomized block coordinate fashion is a bit hand-wavy and needs more explanation.
*   **Discussion of Practical Parallelism:** While the paper emphasizes the parallel nature of PDMM, it lacks a discussion of practical considerations for implementing it on parallel computing architectures (e.g., communication overhead, synchronization issues).
*   **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art:** The comparison with PA-APG and S-APG is limited, stating these are slower than PDMM and ADMM without fully exploring the reasons why.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a widely applicable class of optimization problems.
*   The method addresses limitations of existing ADMM variants and offers potential advantages in terms of convergence and parallelizability.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM on relevant benchmark problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope, and the practical aspects of parallel implementation are not fully addressed.
*   The parameter tuning process and the advantages of PDMM in the overlapping block lasso scenario could be clarified.
*   The overall gain is incremental rather than a breakthrough, with the main benefit being an improved understanding of ADMM variants and a better framework for choosing step sizes.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of optimization, offering a valuable extension to the ADMM framework. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are encouraging. While some aspects could be further improved, the overall quality of the work is high, and the paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, potentially with minor revisions. It would be beneficial to add more diverse experiments, elaborate on practical implementation details, and provide more insight into the parameter sensitivity.
