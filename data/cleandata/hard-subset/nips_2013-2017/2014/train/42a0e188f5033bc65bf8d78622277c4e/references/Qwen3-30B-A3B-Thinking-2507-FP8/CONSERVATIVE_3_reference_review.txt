

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors claim their method can randomly update any number of blocks in parallel, incorporates a dual backward step, and takes sparsity of the constraint matrix into account when setting step sizes. They establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, and claim PDMM includes sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a theoretically sound approach with clear convergence guarantees. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is well-explained, and the theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) appear technically correct. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good explanation of the dual backward step mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its incremental contribution that lacks sufficient validation against established baselines. While the authors claim PDMM is "the first" to allow random selection of any number of blocks to update in parallel, this appears to be a natural extension of existing work rather than a significant innovation. RBSUMM [14] already considers randomized block updates (though limited to one block), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how random selection of multiple blocks provides meaningful advantages over existing methods.

The experimental validation is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The authors compare PDMM to only a few methods (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on two applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). They don't compare against more recent or state-of-the-art methods for these problems. For instance, in the RPCA experiments, they don't compare to methods like the accelerated ADMM variants [27, 9] that are specifically designed for this problem.

The paper claims PDMM is faster than GSADMM in terms of runtime but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity. While they note that "the computation per iteration in GSADMM is slightly higher," they don't provide a detailed comparison of computational costs, which would be essential to support their runtime claims.

The paper's claim that PDMM solves the "open problem" of RBCD for overlapping blocks is unsubstantiated. The authors state that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" but don't provide evidence that their method actually solves this problem in a way that RBCD cannot. The experimental results on overlapping group lasso don't demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods that handle overlapping groups.

The most concerning aspect is the lack of rigorous validation of the claimed advantages. The paper states that "PDMM can be faster than GSADMM in terms of runtime" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The experiments show PDMM1 (updating one block) converging faster than RBSUMM, but this is expected since RBSUMM also updates only one block. The paper doesn't demonstrate that updating multiple blocks (PDMM2, PDMM3) provides significant advantages over updating one block.

The authors claim their method "inherits the merits of the method of multipliers and can solve a fairly large class of problems, including nonsmooth functions," but this is a property shared by many existing methods, including ADMM and its variants. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why PDMM is better than these established methods for nonsmooth problems.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents an interesting extension to existing ADMM variants, but the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The theoretical results are sound, but the experimental validation is limited and doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the authors' claims of superior performance. The paper fails to demonstrate that PDMM offers substantial advantages over well-established methods for the problems it claims to solve.

The paper would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive experiments against a broader range of baselines, particularly state-of-the-art methods for RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors should also provide a more thorough discussion of how their method differs from existing randomized block coordinate approaches and why their specific dual backward step is advantageous.

While the paper is technically sound, it lacks the novelty and significant empirical validation required for acceptance at NIPS. The contribution appears to be an incremental improvement to existing methods rather than a substantial advance that would interest the broader NIPS community. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the proposed method offers significant advantages over established baselines for real-world problems.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper makes an incremental contribution without sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The claimed advantages over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and the theoretical results don't provide enough evidence of practical significance.
