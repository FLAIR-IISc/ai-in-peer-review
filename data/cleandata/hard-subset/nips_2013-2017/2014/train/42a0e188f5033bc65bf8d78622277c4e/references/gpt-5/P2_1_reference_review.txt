Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate method for minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. It works with the augmented Lagrangian and updates K randomly selected primal blocks in parallel, combined with a carefully designed “dual backward step.” The key technical elements are:
- A dual update with per-row-block step sizes τi and a backward correction νi that temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty in the primal subproblems. These steps depend on the sparsity of the constraint matrix (di, the number of nonzeros in row-block i) and on K, the number of blocks updated per iteration.
- An inexact variant using Bregman divergences to linearize problematic terms (e.g., logistic loss or the quadratic penalty), yielding closed-form updates while preserving convergence.
- Connections that show sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) are limiting cases of PDMM: exact primal updates imply conservative dual steps (small τi, large νi), while strongly proximalized primal updates permit larger dual steps (τi = 1, νi = 0).

The paper proves global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions and existence of KKT points. Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM against GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

This situates PDMM among multi-block ADMM variants (Gauss–Seidel and Jacobian), randomized block-coordinate methods, and proximal linearization techniques. Relative to RBSUMM (randomized BSUMM) which updates one block and requires local error bounds/decreasing steps, PDMM allows arbitrary K, uses constant step sizes, and achieves O(1/T).

Quality
Strengths:
- The algorithmic design is well-motivated: recognizing the implicit dual ascent in Jacobi ADMM and countering it via a backward step is insightful, and the dependence of τi, νi on di and K is a principled way to exploit sparsity.
- Theoretical results appear technically solid: the paper defines a Lyapunov function h(v*, vt), shows monotone decrease in expectation, and derives O(1/T) ergodic bounds on both objective suboptimality and feasibility violation. Assumptions are standard (closed, proper, convex; KKT existence).
- The inexact PDMM framework with Bregman divergences is general and subsumes popular linearization strategies, with clear conditions (ϕ strongly convex; ψ smooth) for validity.

Weaknesses:
- Some key derivations and the proofs of claims such as sADMM/PJADMM being special cases are deferred to an external technical report [25], not just the supplementary material. For a NeurIPS paper, essential arguments should be in the main or official supplement, not another paper.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and does not demonstrate actual parallel speedups. All methods are implemented sequentially in Matlab; wall-clock improvements are therefore driven by per-iteration costs and number of iterations, not by parallel scalability, which is the central promise of PDMM.
- Fairness/robustness in comparisons: several baselines are tuned to their “best” behavior, but PDMM also benefits from particular τi, νi choices; additionally, some experiments use cyclic selection instead of random selection (theoretical guarantees are for random), creating a theory-experiment mismatch.
- Important related ideas such as over-relaxed or corrected ADMM (and their relation to the backward step) are not explicitly discussed; placing PDMM in that broader context would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
Strengths:
- The high-level motivation and the roles of τi, νi, K, and matrix sparsity are explained clearly. The discussion of implicit dual ascent is particularly helpful.
- The algorithm is specified unambiguously, including the inexact variant.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Writing and notation could be tightened: there are typos (e.g., “block-seperable”), occasional inconsistencies, and a heavy reliance on symbols introduced mid-stream. A diagram or pseudocode block would help.
- Explicitly summarize in the main text the mapping that makes sADMM and PJADMM special cases (currently relegated to [25]).
- Clarify practical guidance on choosing K and the trade-off between per-iteration cost and convergence, and provide more intuition for τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i.
- Reference formatting is inconsistent (missing years/venues).

Originality
- Allowing randomized updates of an arbitrary number of blocks (K ≥ 1) with sparsity-aware dual steps and a backward correction appears novel relative to existing multi-block ADMM variants. Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are endpoints of a single framework is conceptually unifying.
- The Bregman-based inexact framework is not new per se, but the way it is integrated into this randomized multi-block ADMM with concrete step-size prescriptions adds value.
- Related work is broadly covered (Gauss–Seidel, Jacobian/proximal, randomized BSUMM), though discussion of over-relaxation and recent corrected multi-block ADMM could be expanded.

Significance
- Theoretical: Clear, general convergence guarantees with constant steps for multi-block, randomized, parallel updates are valuable. The sparsity-aware step sizes are practically relevant.
- Practical: The targeted applications (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) are important. However, the current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate benefits in real parallel/distributed settings. Stronger empirical validation (multi-core/GPU/distributed implementations, larger-scale problems, and broader baselines) would enhance impact.

Pros
- Solid and well-motivated algorithmic idea (dual backward step) with sparsity- and K-aware step sizes.
- General framework unifying sADMM and PJADMM; supports exact and inexact updates.
- Global convergence and O(1/T) rate under standard assumptions with constant steps.
- Empirical results suggest competitiveness and potential advantages over existing methods.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is limited, sequential, and partly mismatched with the theory (cyclic vs random).
- Key technical details deferred to an external report; clarity and reference formatting issues.
- Limited discussion of closely related over-relaxed/corrected ADMM literature.
- Practical guidance on parameter choices and implementation-level costs/communication is sparse.

Recommendation
Overall, the paper meets the quality and originality criteria well, is moderately clear, and has potentially significant impact, though the empirical evidence is not as strong as it could be. I lean toward a weak accept, conditional on improving clarity (self-contained connections to sADMM/PJADMM, better writing) and, ideally, strengthening the experimental section to reflect parallel gains.
