Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks each iteration. A key design element is a “dual backward step”: because the primal updates implicitly perform a dual ascent via the augmented term, PDMM shrinks the explicit dual update using parameters τi and νi to achieve conservative dual progress. Notably, the dual step sizes are adapted to (i) the number of updated blocks K and (ii) the sparsity pattern of A through di, the number of nonzero blocks per dual row, yielding τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i with K̃i = min{di, K}.

An inexact variant is handled via Bregman divergences to linearize problematic terms, unifying exact and proximalized updates. The paper shows that two known multi-block ADMM variants are special cases: sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) corresponds to PDMM with τi = 1/J and νi = 1 − 1/J when all blocks are updated; PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) corresponds to PDMM with sufficiently large proximal/Bregman terms, enabling τi = 1 and νi = 0. Theoretical results establish global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes under standard convexity assumptions. Applications include RPCA and overlapping group lasso; experiments compare against GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

This work builds on a substantial literature on ADMM and its multi-block extensions and associated difficulties (e.g., non-convergence of naive multi-block ADMM), including sADMM, PJADMM, and randomized/upper-bound-based methods (RBSUMM/BSUMM). The claimed novelty lies in parallel randomized multi-block updates with dual backward steps whose magnitudes depend on sparsity and K, and in recovering sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithm is clearly specified. The paper motivates the dual backward step via the implicit dual ascent inside the primal update. It provides a Lyapunov function h and a residual R that together imply convergence to KKT points. Theorems give monotonic decrease in expectation and an O(1/T) ergodic rate; assumptions (closed, proper, convex functions; existence of KKT point) match standard ADMM analyses. The inexact/Bregman treatment follows known techniques and appears consistent. Some detailed proofs are relegated to the supplement; the main text includes key lemmas and parameter settings, lending plausibility.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments (Matlab, sequential) on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggest PDMM improves over sADMM and RBSUMM and is competitive with GSADMM/ADMM. Effects of K and (τ,ν) are explored. However, given the paper’s emphasis on parallelism, lack of multi-core/distributed experiments weakens practical validation. Some comparisons rely on heuristic tuning of τ, ν; also one sADMM implementation reportedly lacked a dual update, complicating fairness.
- Limitations: No non-synthetic datasets; limited breadth of tasks; no ablation isolating the dual backward step apart from step-size scaling; no per-iteration complexity analysis beyond qualitative comments.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well-structured. The motivation for the backward dual step and the dependence of step sizes on sparsity and K are clearly articulated. Notation is heavy but coherent. There are typographical issues, formatting inconsistencies, and incomplete references (missing years and details) that should be fixed. Some claims (equivalences to sADMM/PJADMM) are deferred to the supplement, which is acceptable but would benefit from brief in-main-text sketches.

Originality:
- The combination of (i) randomized parallel updates of multiple blocks, (ii) an explicit dual backward step to counter implicit dual ascent, and (iii) step-size formulas adapting to sparsity and K, appears to be a novel and nontrivial extension beyond sADMM (all blocks via splitting), PJADMM (all blocks with proximal terms), and RBSUMM (one block with conditions and no rate). Positioning sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases within PDMM provides conceptual unification.

Significance:
- Theoretical significance is moderate to strong: establishing O(1/T) with constant steps for randomized multi-block parallel updates while leveraging sparsity is valuable. Practical significance is promising (e.g., overlapping groups) but under-demonstrated due to lack of actual parallel implementations and broader empirical coverage. Nonetheless, the ability to select K flexibly and exploit sparsity may be compelling for large-scale applications.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear algorithmic idea (dual backward step) grounded in analysis of implicit dual ascent.
- Convergence guarantees with explicit step-size rules depending on sparsity and K; O(1/T) rate.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, clarifying relationships among methods.
- Handles inexact updates via Bregman divergences; applicable to overlapping group models.
- Empirical results indicate improvements over relevant baselines in iteration/time on tested problems.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Empirical evaluation lacks parallel/distributed experiments; all results are sequential Matlab.
- Comparisons may be affected by implementation choices (e.g., sADMM code without dual update) and tuning; fairness and reproducibility need strengthening.
- Limited problem diversity and absence of real datasets; minimal analysis of sensitivity to τ, ν beyond a few settings.
- Writing/presentation issues: typos, incomplete references, and reliance on supplement for key derivations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide multi-core and/or distributed experiments demonstrating wall-clock gains and scalability with K and sparsity.
- Ensure fair, consistent implementations of baselines (especially sADMM with proper dual updates) and report sensitivity to ρ, τ, ν.
- Include per-iteration complexity and communication analyses; discuss practical guidelines for choosing K.
- Expand empirical evaluation to real datasets and additional problem classes; provide ablations isolating the dual backward step.
- Correct and complete all references; improve exposition and notation consistency.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to parallel multi-block ADMM methods, with clear theoretical guarantees and a unifying perspective. The main weakness is the limited empirical validation of parallel benefits. With strengthened experiments and presentation polish, the work would be a solid addition. As is, it meets the bar on theory and novelty, with moderate empirical support.
