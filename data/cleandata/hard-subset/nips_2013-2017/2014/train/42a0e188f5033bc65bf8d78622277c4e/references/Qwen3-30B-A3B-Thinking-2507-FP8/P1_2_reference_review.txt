

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a gap in the literature regarding parallelizable extensions of ADMM for multiple blocks, where existing methods either suffer from convergence issues (like direct Jacobi updates) or lack theoretical guarantees. PDMM introduces a backward step on the dual update and strategically selects step sizes based on matrix sparsity and the number of blocks being updated in parallel. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates applications to robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems. The work connects several existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases of PDMM, providing a unified theoretical framework.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis under reasonable assumptions. The authors clearly establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, which is a significant contribution given the theoretical challenges of multi-block ADMM variants. The analysis is well-structured and builds logically from the proposed algorithm.

2. **Novel Contributions**: The key innovations—particularly the dual backward step (equations 6-7) and the step size selection based on sparsity (equation 8)—are well-motivated and address real convergence issues with existing approaches. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases provides valuable theoretical insight.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates significant empirical advantages on two important machine learning applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). The experimental results show PDMM outperforms existing methods in runtime while maintaining convergence guarantees, with the added benefit of parallelization.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical presentation is clear, the connections to related work are well-explained, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The authors effectively use notation consistently throughout.

5. **Addressing an Important Gap**: The paper directly addresses a significant limitation in the literature—parallelizing ADMM for multiple blocks while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The application to overlapping groups is particularly valuable as it's a problem where randomized block coordinate descent remains an open challenge.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Parallel Methods**: While the paper compares PDMM with several ADMM variants, it doesn't provide sufficient comparison with other parallel optimization methods for multiple blocks (e.g., [10, 26] which are mentioned but not thoroughly contrasted). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address how to choose the optimal number of blocks K to update in parallel (K is a parameter in the algorithm). While the experiments show that K=20 is near-optimal for the overlapping group lasso problem, the paper lacks guidance on how to select K for new problems.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges some limitations but could provide a more thorough discussion of when PDMM might not be advantageous. For example, for problems where the matrix A is dense (di = J for all i), the step size selection (equation 8) reduces to τi = 1/J, which is the same as sADMM.

4. **Missing Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites [10, 26] but doesn't explicitly contrast PDMM with the recent parallel multi-block ADMM work in [10] beyond noting it's a special case. A more detailed comparison would help position the contribution.

5. **Experimental Analysis Depth**: While the experiments demonstrate PDMM's advantages, they could provide more detailed analysis of the parallel speedup and how the algorithm scales with problem size, especially for very large-scale problems.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed and parallel optimization. The paper's main innovation—PDMM—provides a unified framework for several existing methods while offering practical advantages in both convergence and runtime. The theoretical guarantees are solid, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature regarding parallelizable multi-block ADMM variants. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable theoretical insight that could guide future algorithm design. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly noteworthy as it addresses a problem where existing randomized methods are still open.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of convex optimization with important theoretical and practical implications for machine learning practitioners dealing with large-scale problems.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pro:**
- Strong theoretical contributions with clear convergence guarantees
- Novel connection between existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) through PDMM
- Practical advantages demonstrated in experiments
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Addresses an important gap in parallel optimization methods

**Con:**
- Limited comparison with other parallel optimization methods
- Could provide more guidance on selecting K (number of blocks to update)
- Some limitations of the approach aren't fully explored

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it makes a meaningful contribution to the field that will be valuable to both theoretical and applied researchers.
