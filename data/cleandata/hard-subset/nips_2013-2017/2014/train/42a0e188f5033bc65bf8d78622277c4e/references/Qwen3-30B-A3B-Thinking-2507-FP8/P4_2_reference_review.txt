

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. The problem formulation generalizes many machine learning applications including robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors position PDMM as a unified framework that connects existing approaches: they show that sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) are special cases of PDMM. The paper extends the literature on multi-block ADMM variants, which has been an active area in recent NIPS conferences (e.g., [7, 10, 14, 15, 17, 20, 26]). Previous work has struggled with convergence guarantees for parallel updates, with some methods requiring strict conditions or decreasing step sizes.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size. The authors carefully define the necessary assumptions (Assumption 1) and develop appropriate Lyapunov functions (h(v*,vt)) to prove convergence. The theoretical framework is well-constructed, with clear connections between the dual backward step and convergence behavior. The proofs in the supplement appear technically sound.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation, algorithm description, and theoretical results are presented logically. The paper effectively explains the intuition behind the dual backward step (Section 2.1) and demonstrates how sparsity and random block selection affect step size determination (Section 2.2). The connection to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is clearly articulated, providing valuable context for the field.

**Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel unified framework for parallel ADMM variants that addresses a significant gap in the literature. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from existing methods (GSADMM, RBSUMM, sADMM, PJADMM) by emphasizing three key aspects: the dual backward step, consideration of matrix sparsity, and arbitrary parallel block selection. The connection between existing methods through PDMM provides deeper theoretical understanding of the role of the dual backward step.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's practical advantages over existing methods. The paper shows that PDMM can achieve faster convergence than RBSUMM, sADMM, and GSADMM in terms of runtime while maintaining convergence guarantees.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Explanation of Dual Backward Step Necessity:** While the paper provides intuition for why the dual backward step is needed (Section 2.1), it doesn't offer a rigorous explanation of why the direct Jacobi updates (3)-(4) fail to converge. A more detailed analysis of the failure modes of direct Jacobi updates would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Experimental Comparison Depth:** The experimental section compares PDMM with several methods but could provide more detailed analysis of computational complexity per iteration. For example, the paper mentions that GSADMM has "slightly higher computation per iteration" than PDMM3 but doesn't quantify this difference, which would be valuable for practitioners.

**Overlapping Group Lasso Connection:** The connection to overlapping group lasso (Section 2.3) is somewhat superficial. While the paper shows how the problem can be formulated as (14), it doesn't elaborate on how the block structure is defined for this specific problem, which would help readers understand how to apply PDMM to similar problems.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a unified theoretical framework that connects existing ADMM variants with convergence guarantees
- Establishes O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size (a significant improvement over methods requiring decreasing step sizes)
- Demonstrates practical effectiveness on important machine learning problems
- Carefully considers practical aspects like matrix sparsity and random block selection
- Well-written and clearly organized with sufficient detail for reproduction

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited explanation of why the dual backward step is theoretically necessary for convergence
- Some experimental comparisons lack depth in terms of computational complexity analysis
- The connection to overlapping group lasso could be more detailed

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on ADMM variants for multi-block optimization. The theoretical results are sound and advance the state of the art by providing convergence guarantees with constant step size for a parallel randomized block coordinate method. The practical experiments validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. While the paper could strengthen its theoretical explanation of the dual backward step and provide more detailed experimental comparisons, these are minor limitations relative to the paper's substantial contributions.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel unified framework (Originality), and addresses an important problem with significant practical implications (Significance). The work is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS as it advances the field with both theoretical and practical contributions.
