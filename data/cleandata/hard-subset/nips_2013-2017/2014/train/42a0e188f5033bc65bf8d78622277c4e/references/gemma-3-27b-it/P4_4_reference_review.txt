This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for solving linearly constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea is to update a randomly chosen subset of blocks in parallel, aiming for improved efficiency compared to existing methods like GSADMM and sADMM. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, proposed algorithm, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. It effectively positions PDMM within the existing landscape of ADMM variants, highlighting its advantages in terms of potential parallelism and convergence properties. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is valuable, providing a deeper understanding of the interplay between different dual update strategies. The mathematical notation is generally clear, though some equations could benefit from slightly more descriptive variable definitions. The inclusion of a detailed convergence analysis is a significant strength, establishing theoretical foundations for the proposed method. The experimental section provides a reasonable comparison with other algorithms, though the parameter tuning process could be discussed more thoroughly.

Strengths:

* **Novelty:** The introduction of PDMM, with its randomized block updates and backward step on the dual update, represents a novel contribution to the ADMM family. The ability to randomly update any number of blocks offers flexibility and potential for parallelization.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis is a key strength. Establishing global convergence and iteration complexity with a constant step size is a significant result. The analysis clearly identifies assumptions and provides a rigorous justification for the algorithm's behavior.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the algorithm is clearly explained, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a logical order.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively connects PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, providing a broader context and highlighting the unique aspects of the proposed approach.

Weaknesses:

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper establishes theoretical convergence, the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the number of blocks (K) in the experiments is highlighted.  The rationale for choosing specific values of K and the impact on performance (beyond runtime) are not thoroughly discussed.  A more systematic exploration of K’s influence would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Depth:** The experimental section, while demonstrating competitive performance, could be expanded. Comparing with more state-of-the-art methods or exploring a wider range of datasets and problem sizes would strengthen the empirical validation.  A more detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process, and a justification for the chosen parameters, would be helpful. The claim that “tuning parameters” was done without detailed explanation feels weak.
* **Assumptions:** The convergence analysis relies on Assumption 1, which requires the existence of a KKT point. The implications of this assumption and potential strategies for dealing with cases where a KKT point doesn't exist are not addressed.
* **Bregman Divergence Discussion:** The discussion around the Bregman divergence seems slightly disconnected from the main flow of the paper. While its role in enabling inexact updates is mentioned, a more intuitive explanation of its practical benefits and the impact of choosing different Bregman divergences would be valuable.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a class of important optimization problems.
* The convergence analysis provides strong guarantees for the algorithm's behavior.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods.
* The connection to existing ADMM variants provides valuable insights into the interplay between different dual update strategies.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental section could be more comprehensive and explore the parameter sensitivity in greater detail.
* The discussion of the Bregman divergence and the underlying assumptions could be further clarified.
* The impact of the number of blocks (K) is only superficially addressed.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and demonstrates promising empirical performance. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
