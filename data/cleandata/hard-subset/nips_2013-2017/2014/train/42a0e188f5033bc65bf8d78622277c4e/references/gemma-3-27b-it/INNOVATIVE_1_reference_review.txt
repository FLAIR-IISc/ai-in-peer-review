This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM, designed for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The central idea is to allow for parallel updates of a randomly chosen *number* of blocks, going beyond previous work that primarily focused on cyclic (Gauss-Seidel) or single-block randomization. The authors establish connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, framing them as special cases of PDMM, and provide convergence guarantees with O(1/T) iteration complexity. The application to RPCA and overlapping group lasso is a reasonable choice to demonstrate the method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core concept of PDMM – randomly updating *K* blocks in parallel, where *K* can vary and doesn't necessarily equal one – is a genuinely novel contribution. This extends the flexibility of ADMM beyond existing parallel implementations.  The theoretical connection establishing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases adds to the paper’s value.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** Providing convergence guarantees (O(1/T) complexity) is a significant strength. The conditions assumed for convergence appear reasonable and align with standard ADMM assumptions.
* **Potential for Scalability:** The randomized block updates, coupled with the analysis considering block sparsity, suggest potential for efficient parallelization and scalability to large-scale problems.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing ADMM variants for multi-block problems and motivates the development of PDMM.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The authors do a good job of placing PDMM within the landscape of existing ADMM methods, highlighting its advantages and how it generalizes previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Validation is Limited:** While experiments are conducted on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, the comparison to baseline methods feels somewhat incomplete. The justification for parameter tuning of GSADMM is not very clear. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons with state-of-the-art solvers specifically designed for these problems, would strengthen the paper.  The discussion lacks detail on hardware used and actual parallel speedups observed, a critical aspect given the paper's focus.
* **Complexity of Parameter Choice:**  The formulas for τi and νi, while theoretically motivated, seem somewhat complex and may require significant tuning in practice. A discussion on practical guidelines for choosing *K* and understanding the trade-offs between different parameter settings would be helpful.
* **Lack of Insight into *K* Selection:** The experimental results on overlapping group lasso show performance varying with K, but the paper doesn’t provide strong guidelines on how to choose *K* in practice based on problem characteristics (sparsity, block sizes, etc.). The discussion around *K* feels somewhat ad-hoc.
* **Bregman Divergence:** While theoretically sound, the justification for using a Bregman divergence feels a bit forced.  The explanation of how it enables inexact updates is present, but the benefits beyond that are not fully articulated.
* **Notations:** The notation is dense and sometimes difficult to follow. The use of multiple indices (i, j) can be confusing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The idea is novel and represents a meaningful extension of ADMM, addressing a known limitation of existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a foundation for understanding the convergence properties of PDMM.
* The potential for improved scalability and parallel performance is significant.
* The connection to existing methods provides valuable insight and highlights the generality of PDMM.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the practical advantages of PDMM.
* The complexity of parameter tuning and the lack of clear guidance on choosing *K* could hinder adoption.
* The paper could benefit from a more streamlined presentation and clearer explanations of certain concepts.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper warrants acceptance despite the identified weaknesses. The novelty of the approach and the solid theoretical foundation outweigh the shortcomings in experimental validation.  The authors have identified an important problem and proposed a promising solution that expands the capabilities of ADMM. The potential impact of PDMM, particularly for large-scale applications, justifies its inclusion in NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses raised in the review through more thorough experimentation and a clearer presentation in a future version. I’d rate this as a **weak accept**. The paper presents a significant *idea*, and that's what NIPS should prioritize.
