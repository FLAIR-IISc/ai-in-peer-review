Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives under linear equality constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate method built on the augmented Lagrangian. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks, introducing a “dual backward step” that dampens the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. Crucially, the dual step sizes τi and the backward-step parameters νi depend on the sparsity pattern of A via di (number of nonzero blocks in row i) and the number of updated blocks K. The method encompasses two prominent Jacobian ADMM variants as extremes: sADMM (exact primal solves, small dual step) and PJADMM (proximalized primal solves, full dual step), thereby unifying them. Theoretical results establish global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions with constant step sizes. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate favorable convergence versus sADMM, GSADMM, and RBSUMM.

This sits within a rich literature on ADMM and block-coordinate methods: two-block ADMM [Boyd et al.] is well understood, while multi-block extensions can diverge without safeguards [Chen et al.]. Proximal/Jacobi ADMMs [Deng & Yin; He et al.; Wang et al.] and randomized block methods [Richtárik & Takáč; Hong et al.] provide related tools. PDMM’s backward step is related to relaxation/under-relaxation in ADMM and to the effect of added proximal terms.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Clear interpretation of how the augmented Lagrangian causes implicit dual ascent and how a backward step stabilizes parallel Jacobi updates. The mappings showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM help position the method.
- Sparsity-aware parameterization: Making τi, νi depend on di and K is intuitive and practically relevant; it provides larger steps when each dual block is coupled to fewer primal blocks.
- Theoretical guarantees: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant steps; conditions are standard (closed, proper convex fj and existence of KKT point), and the framework covers exact and inexact (Bregman-proximal) updates.
- Applicability to overlapping groups: The formulation for composite regularization with overlaps is natural, and the specialization di=2 yields simple τi, νi.
- Empirical promise: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive or faster than strong ADMM baselines in iteration/time on synthetic data.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing relaxation/proximal schemes: The “dual backward step” resembles known ADMM relaxation/damping (over-/under-relaxation) and proximal Jacobian schemes. The paper’s specific dependence τi, νi on di and K is original, but the conceptual novelty may be incremental unless the derivations/benefits are made more explicit.
- Theoretical presentation gaps: Key matrices and constants in the bounds (Pt, Q, βi, γi) are introduced tersely; their construction, roles, and tightness are not discussed enough. Only ergodic O(1/T) is shown; no non-ergodic rates or linear convergence under stronger assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation is limited:
  • All runs are sequential MATLAB; no parallel/multi-core or distributed tests despite the method’s motivation.
  • Mostly synthetic data; no large real datasets (e.g., video RPCA, large-scale structured sparsity).
  • Comparisons involve some non-uniform tuning (e.g., sADMM code without dual update; PDMM tuned τi, νi beyond the formula in one table), clouding fairness.
  • No ablations isolating the effect of the backward step νi or the sparsity-aware scaling; limited exploration of K beyond qualitative plots.
- Claims around overlapping block coordinate descent being “open” are overstated; there exist randomized coordinate methods for partially separable/overlapping structures (e.g., proximal gradient/FISTA with overlapping prox, and coordinate methods under partial separability assumptions). The paper should nuance this claim and cite broader related work on overlapping regularization.
- Clarity/polish: Notational overload (row/column/block indices), some typos, and missing details (e.g., how ηj, ψj, ϕj are selected in practice; how σ is known/estimated) make reproduction harder.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the idea and analysis align with augmented Lagrangian and randomized block frameworks. However, the presentation of proofs (delegated to supplement) relies on constructs (Pt, Q) that are not sufficiently explained in the main text. Experimental methodology needs stronger baselines, fairness, and parallel evaluations.

Clarity
Readable overall, but the paper would benefit from:
- A concise algorithm box with all parameters defined.
- Clear derivation/intuition for τi, νi from the analysis.
- Expanded related work on relaxation in ADMM and overlapping optimization.
- Proof sketches giving the role of sparsity in the constants.

Originality
Moderate. The sparsity- and K-dependent step sizing and the unifying view are interesting. The backward step per se and the inexact Bregmanization are known ideas; the contribution is in their particular synthesis and analysis.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners facing multi-block, sparse-coupled constraints, especially when parallelism is available. Current empirical evidence is promising but not yet sufficient to claim state-of-the-art practical impact.

Specific questions/suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise derivation for τi = K/[K̃i(2J−K)] and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, including where tightness/looseness arises and how sparsity enters the proof. Can τi be adaptively tuned based on observed coupling?
- Define Pt and Q explicitly in the main text and discuss how to compute or bound them in practice. Report constants in Theorem 2 for the tested problems to contextualize rates.
- Include an ablation: PDMM without backward step (νi=0), with fixed τi, and with sparsity-unaware τ to quantify contributions.
- Add parallel experiments (multi-threaded or distributed) reporting wall-clock speedup versus K and versus di, and larger real datasets (e.g., background subtraction videos for RPCA; real overlapping group lasso benchmarks).
- Ensure fair baselines: use sADMM with proper dual updates; report tuned parameters for all methods; include proximal gradient/FISTA (with overlapping prox) and Chambolle–Pock/PDHG as additional baselines.
- Clarify practical guidance for ηj, ψj, ϕj selection and σ estimation; give concrete choices per experiment.
- Discuss relation to relaxed ADMM (over-relaxation parameter) and show how PDMM’s νi maps to standard relaxation.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general algorithmic framework with convergence guarantees.
- Insightful sparsity-aware step sizing and connection between sADMM and PJADMM.
- Applicable to important classes (RPCA, overlapping groups).

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to known relaxation/proximal ADMM ideas.
- Limited and partly unfair empirical validation; no parallel results.
- Missing clarity on constants and practical parameter selection.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The ideas are promising and could be impactful with stronger empirical validation and clearer theoretical exposition. I encourage the authors to address the evaluation and clarity issues; with those improvements, PDMM would be a strong candidate.
