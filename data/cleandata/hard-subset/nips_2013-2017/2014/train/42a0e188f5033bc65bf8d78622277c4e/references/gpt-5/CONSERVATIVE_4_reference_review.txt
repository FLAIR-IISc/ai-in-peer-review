Summary
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization. PDMM updates any K randomly chosen primal blocks in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian and introduces a “dual backward step” via an intermediate dual variable ŷ to counteract the implicit dual ascent created by the quadratic penalty. The method prescribes per-row dual stepsizes τi and backward factors νi that depend on (i) the row-sparsity di of A and (ii) the number of updated blocks K. An inexact variant adds Bregman terms to enable closed-form primal updates. The authors show that sADMM and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) arise as extreme cases of PDMM. They provide global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard convexity/KKT assumptions. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso compare PDMM against GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work
Multi-block ADMM is known to be delicate; Gauss–Seidel variants may diverge; Jacobian variants typically require proximal damping or splitting and often come with O(1/T) ergodic rates. Randomized block-coordinate methods (RBCD) for composite objectives and randomized ADMM-like schemes (e.g., RBSUMM) exist, typically updating one block. The “backward step” here is conceptually akin to under-relaxed/over-relaxed ADMM and proximal Jacobian ADMM; the main novelty is the explicit τi, νi schedule that accounts for di and K, and allowing K>1 randomized blocks.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-motivated gap: randomized parallel multi-block ADMM with K>1 blocks and convergence guarantees.
- Clean conceptual clarification of why naive Jacobi ADMM diverges (implicit dual ascent) and how a backward step mitigates it.
- Connections showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases help situate PDMM and explain parameter roles.
- Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under mild convex assumptions; handles nonsmooth separable terms with inexact (Bregman) updates.
- Practical angle: step-size rules use sparsity (di) and K; experiments include overlapping groups where di=2 yields larger step sizes.

Weaknesses
- Evidence of practical impact is limited: all experiments are sequential Matlab; no parallel/multicore or distributed evaluation, so the claimed parallel benefits are speculative.
- Baseline tuning and fairness are questionable. sADMM is reported using public code with τ=0 (no dual update), which is not a fair implementation; comparisons should include proper sADMM/PJADMM with recommended parameters. RBSUMM tuning appears ad hoc and negative results are inconclusive.
- The prescribed τi, νi are sometimes overridden by tuning in experiments, which weakens the link between theory and practice. No adaptive or data-driven guidance is provided.
- The complexity results are standard (ergodic O(1/T) in expectation). No non-ergodic bounds, high-probability guarantees, or linear rates under additional assumptions, despite an emphasis on improved step sizes.
- Theory is dense and key definitions (e.g., Pt, Q, γi) and their roles are not well motivated in the main text; many crucial details are deferred to the supplement.
- Communication/synchronization costs and the effect of heterogeneity in di across rows are not modeled; guidance on choosing K is mostly qualitative.
- Clarity issues: numerous typos and notation overload; an algorithm box/pseudocode would help.

Quality
Technically, the framework is plausible and generally consistent with established analyses for randomized and Jacobian ADMM variants. However, the novelty of the analysis (beyond parameter choices tied to di and K) is incremental, and the empirical evidence does not convincingly substantiate practical advantages over well-implemented baselines. The mismatch between theoretical step sizes and tuned parameters in experiments raises concerns.

Clarity
The paper is organized, but the presentation is heavy and not always self-contained. Important constructs (Pt, Q, βi, ζi) appear abruptly; assumptions for inexact updates (Lipschitz/strong convexity constants) are only sketched. Several typos and grammatical errors remain. An explicit algorithm listing and clearer guidance on parameter selection would improve readability.

Originality
Moderate. The backward step is closely related to known under/over-relaxation and proximal damping in multi-block ADMM. Allowing randomized updates with K>1 and linking τi, νi to di appears to be the main new element, along with unifying sADMM/PJADMM as extremes.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners dealing with sparse A and overlapping structures; however, without true parallel experiments and stronger baselines, the practical impact is uncertain. The theoretical rate matches prior work and does not advance the state of the art in convergence guarantees.

Questions for the authors
- Please justify the derivation of τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i more transparently in the main text. Are these tight or conservative? Can they be adapted online?
- How sensitive is PDMM to mis-specified τi, νi? In the experiments where you tuned them away from (8), what ranges worked?
- Provide fair sADMM and PJADMM baselines with proper dual updates and proximal terms, and include recent multi-block ADMM variants with known convergence.
- Can you provide non-ergodic or high-probability convergence statements, or linear rates under strong convexity/regularity?
- What is the communication pattern and cost when di is heterogeneous? How should K be chosen in practice to balance parallel efficiency and convergence?
- Please include actual parallel (multicore/distributed) timing.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and clear identification of why Jacobi ADMM fails.
- Convergent randomized K-block Jacobian scheme with explicit step sizes using sparsity.
- Unifying view of sADMM/PJADMM; handles inexact updates via Bregman terms.

Cons
- Empirical evidence is weak (sequential code; suboptimal baselines; parameter tuning not aligned with theory).
- Theoretical contributions are incremental; rates match standard O(1/T) and are in expectation.
- Presentation issues and heavy reliance on supplement.

Recommendation
Reject in the current form. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the paper lacks convincing empirical validation against strong, fairly implemented baselines and does not provide sufficiently novel theoretical advances. A revised version with fair baselines (proper sADMM/PJADMM), real parallel experiments, clearer analysis, and practical guidance on choosing τi, νi, and K would substantially strengthen the case.
