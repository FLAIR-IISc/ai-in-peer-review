This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a new algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. This is a general and important problem formulation in machine learning. The key motivation is to overcome the limitations of existing methods based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Standard Gauss-Seidel ADMM for multiple blocks is inherently sequential, while direct parallel (Jacobian) versions often diverge.

The proposed PDMM algorithm introduces a flexible, randomized block-coordinate approach where an arbitrary number of blocks, `K`, can be selected and updated in parallel at each iteration. The main technical innovations are a "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm and a novel step-size rule. This rule adaptively sets the dual learning rate (`τ_i`) and the backward step parameter (`ν_i`) based on the total number of blocks (`J`), the number of parallel updates (`K`), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix `A`. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, proving global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. Conceptually, the paper provides a unifying framework that shows two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM. The practical utility of the method is demonstrated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where it shows strong performance against several baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm for a fundamental class of optimization problems. The idea of a randomized, parallel block update with an adaptive, stabilizing dual step is a significant contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is particularly insightful, providing a deeper understanding of the role of primal update accuracy versus dual step aggressiveness in parallel ADMM-type methods.
2.  **Flexibility and Practicality:** The ability to choose the number of parallel blocks (`K`) is a powerful feature. It allows practitioners to navigate the trade-off between the computational cost per iteration and the total number of iterations required for convergence, adapting the algorithm to the available parallel resources. The step-size rule's dependence on the constraint matrix sparsity is a clever and practical innovation that can lead to much larger, more effective step sizes, as demonstrated convincingly in the overlapping group lasso experiment.
3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) convergence rate in expectation. The assumptions are standard, and the analysis appears sound. This places PDMM on firm theoretical footing, comparable to that of standard ADMM.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively highlight the benefits of PDMM. The application to overlapping group lasso is a strong point, as it showcases how PDMM can naturally handle a problem structure that is challenging for standard randomized block coordinate descent methods. The results show a clear performance advantage over several relevant and state-of-the-art methods.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** The presentation of the algorithm's updates in equations (5)-(7) is slightly confusing. Equation (5) uses `ŷ^t` for the primal update at iteration `t+1`, while equation (7) defines `ŷ^{t+1}`. The paper should explicitly state how `ŷ^t` is computed from the variables at iteration `t` (presumably `ŷ^t = y^t - νρ(Ax^t - a)`). Including a formal algorithm box with pseudocode would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the method.
2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** In the RPCA experiment, the comparison against sADMM seems unfair. The authors note that the public sADMM code they used has a zero dual step size (`τ_i = 0`), which deviates from the theory and leads to poor performance. For a fair comparison, sADMM should be implemented with its theoretically justified step size (`τ_i = 1/J`). While the authors acknowledge that with the correct step size sADMM would be equivalent to PDMM3, the presented plot is misleading. This comparison should be corrected or more clearly caveated in the main text.
3.  **Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The number of blocks to update in parallel, `K`, is a key hyperparameter. The experiments show that its choice significantly impacts performance. However, the paper provides no guidance on how to select `K`. While a theoretical treatment may be difficult, a discussion of the practical trade-offs (e.g., computation vs. communication/synchronization costs, convergence rate vs. per-iteration work) would be very valuable for practitioners wishing to apply PDMM.
4.  **Details on Baselines:** The paper mentions that RBSUMM failed to converge for the overlapping group lasso problem. While this is a valid empirical result, providing more details on the parameter ranges tried for RBSUMM would strengthen this claim and help rule out the possibility of a suboptimal implementation or parameter search.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, flexible, and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to presentation and experimental details, which can be addressed in a revision. The work is significant and likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners working on distributed and parallel machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
