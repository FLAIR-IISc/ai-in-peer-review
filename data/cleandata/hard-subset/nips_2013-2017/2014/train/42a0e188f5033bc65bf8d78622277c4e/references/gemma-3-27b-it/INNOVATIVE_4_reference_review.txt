This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM designed for solving linearly-constrained block-separable convex optimization problems. The core idea is to update a randomly selected set of blocks in parallel, offering a balance between full Jacobi updates (which can be unstable) and single-block updates (potentially slow). The paper establishes connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, framing them as special cases of PDMM, and provides convergence guarantees with a clear O(1/T) iteration complexity. The application to RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates the practical efficacy of the approach.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While ADMM and its variants are well-explored, PDMM’s strategic randomization and the theoretical grounding linking it to other methods provide a fresh perspective. The dual backward step, motivated by the potential instability of full Jacobi updates, is a particularly insightful contribution. The extension to overlapping group lasso is also valuable, addressing an open problem related to randomized block coordinate descent in this context. The authors convincingly demonstrate that PDMM bridges a gap between existing methods and presents a flexible framework for parallel optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PDMM algorithm itself and the theoretical connection to sADMM and PJADMM are genuinely novel.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis, establishing O(1/T) complexity, is a strong point. The careful consideration of step size parameters (τi, νi) based on problem structure is commendable.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso are well-chosen and demonstrate the algorithm’s applicability to important problems. Empirical results indicate PDMM performs competitively.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The rationale behind the dual backward step is well explained and addresses a known issue with Jacobi updates.
*   **Well-structured:** The paper is logically organized, progressing from problem formulation to algorithm description, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are informative, they are limited in scope. Comparing PDMM against state-of-the-art solvers beyond ADMM variants would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The discussion of parameter tuning (especially the impact of K) could be more detailed. While the paper acknowledges the trade-off, a more systematic analysis of how to choose K in practice would be beneficial.
*   **Bregman Divergence:** The treatment of the Bregman divergence feels somewhat cursory. While the authors mention its utility, a more in-depth exploration of different choices and their impact on performance would be valuable.
*   **Notation:** The notation, especially around block indices (Aij, Ari, Acj), is dense and could be made more accessible.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound algorithm (PDMM) for solving a class of important optimization problems.
*   The connection to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable insight and unifies the field.
*   The convergence guarantees and empirical results support the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   The work addresses an open problem related to randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping group lasso.
*   The potential for parallelization is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
*   The discussion of parameter tuning and the Bregman divergence could be more detailed.
*   The notation is occasionally dense.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The novelty and theoretical grounding of PDMM, coupled with its practical applicability, make it a compelling submission. While the experimental evaluation could be expanded and some aspects clarified, these are minor weaknesses that do not detract significantly from the paper's overall quality. I recommend **acceptance** with a strong encouragement to the authors to expand their experimental validation and provide more detailed guidance on parameter selection in a future version. The innovative spirit and potential impact of this work outweigh its minor shortcomings.
