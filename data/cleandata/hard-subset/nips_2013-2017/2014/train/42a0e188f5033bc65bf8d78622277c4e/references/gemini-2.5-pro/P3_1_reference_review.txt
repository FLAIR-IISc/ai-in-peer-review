### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The method is a variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) that allows for parallel, randomized block coordinate updates of the primal variables. The key challenge in designing such parallel (Jacobian-style) ADMM is that direct parallelization often leads to divergence. The authors identify that this is due to overly aggressive dual updates. To remedy this, PDMM introduces three core ideas: (1) a "backward step" on the dual variable to make the updates more conservative; (2) a randomized scheme where an arbitrary number of blocks `K` can be selected and updated in parallel at each iteration; and (3) adaptive step sizes that depend on the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of parallel blocks `K`.

The paper provides a unified framework that connects two existing parallel ADMM methods, sADMM and PJADMM, showing they are special cases of PDMM. This connection provides valuable insight into the trade-off between exactness in the primal update and the aggressiveness of the dual update. The authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with constant step sizes, under standard assumptions. The practical utility of the method is demonstrated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, where PDMM shows competitive or superior performance compared to existing sequential and parallel ADMM variants.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of effectively parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems is of high practical importance in large-scale machine learning. The failure of naive parallelization is a well-known issue. This paper provides a novel and well-motivated solution. The combination of the dual backward step, randomized block selection, and sparsity-aware adaptive step sizes is a significant and original contribution to the ADMM literature. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM is also a strong conceptual contribution that deepens the understanding of this class of algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous. The paper provides a full convergence proof and establishes an O(1/T) rate for the ergodic average of the iterates, which is the standard and expected rate for this type of first-order method. The assumptions are minimal and standard for ADMM analysis. The derivations leading to the step size rules in equation (8) appear sound within the provided theoretical framework.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation for the dual backward step is explained intuitively, making the core idea accessible. The connections to related work are clearly articulated, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are conducted on relevant and challenging machine learning problems (RPCA and overlapping group lasso). The comparison with appropriate baselines, including sequential (GSADMM), parallel (sADMM), and randomized (RBSUMM) methods, is comprehensive. The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM, particularly the flexibility afforded by the parameter `K`. The study on the effect of `K` in the overlapping group lasso experiment is particularly insightful.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Intuition behind Step Sizes:** While the proof validates the choice of step sizes (`τi`, `νi`) in equation (8), the paper provides limited intuition for their specific functional form. A paragraph explaining *why* these forms arise from the analysis (e.g., how they balance certain terms in the Lyapunov function decrease) would greatly improve the reader's understanding and make the method feel less like a "black box" where the parameters are chosen solely to make a proof work.

2.  **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The primary motivation for the algorithm is parallelism. However, all experiments are run sequentially, reporting wall-clock time on a single machine. While this demonstrates the convergence behavior in terms of iterations and sequential time (reflecting per-iteration complexity), it misses the crucial aspect of actual parallel speedup. A discussion of communication costs (e.g., synchronization needed for the dual update `Aix^(t+1)`) and potential scalability bottlenecks, or even a small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about being a "parallel" method.

3.  **Theoretical Comparison:** Remark 2 notes that `J=K=1` gives the fastest convergence rate, but this corresponds to the non-decomposed method of multipliers, which defeats the purpose of the algorithm. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-off between the convergence constant (which depends on `J` and `K`) and the per-iteration cost would be valuable. Does the theoretical rate constant improve with smaller `J` or larger `K` in a way that quantitatively justifies the empirical findings?

4.  **Claims about RBSUMM:** The paper states that for the overlapping group lasso problem, RBSUMM did not show convergence within 5000 iterations. This is a very strong negative result for a competing method. It would be more convincing and scientifically sound to provide more details about the parameter settings that were tried for RBSUMM, or to temper the claim, as it could be due to an issue with implementation or parameter tuning.

5.  **Self-Containment:** The paper refers to an external arXiv paper [25] for the detailed derivations showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases. While appendix material is limited, a brief sketch of these arguments in an appendix would make the submission more self-contained and easier to evaluate.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on distributed and parallel optimization. It tackles an important, well-known problem with a novel and theoretically sound method. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, and its ability to unify and generalize existing methods is a significant insight. The experimental results are promising and support the theoretical claims. Despite some weaknesses, primarily the lack of true parallel experiments and the need for more intuition on the step-size selection, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality, significance, and technical quality are substantial. The work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire further research in this area.

**Recommendation: Accept**
