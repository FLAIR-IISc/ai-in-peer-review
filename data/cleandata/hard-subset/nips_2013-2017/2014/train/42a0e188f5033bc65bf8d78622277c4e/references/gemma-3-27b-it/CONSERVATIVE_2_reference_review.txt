This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method (PDMM) for solving linearly-constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems, a common formulation in various machine learning applications. The authors attempt to improve upon existing ADMM variants by introducing a backward step on the dual update and allowing for random selection of blocks for parallel updates. The theoretical analysis establishes convergence and iteration complexity, and experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are presented.

While the problem setting is relevant and the potential for parallelization is appealing, this paper falls short of convincing me of the significance of the proposed method. My primary concern lies with the incremental nature of the contribution. The authors position PDMM as an improvement over existing ADMM variants, but the improvements – a backward step and randomized block selection – feel largely heuristic and lack a strong theoretical justification beyond what is already known. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM, while interesting, doesn’t fundamentally alter the understanding of these methods, and framing them as extreme cases of PDMM doesn’t necessarily establish PDMM as a superior general approach.

The theoretical results, while present, don't offer a significant leap forward. Establishing convergence with a constant step size is commendable, but the O(1/T) iteration complexity is typical for first-order methods and doesn’t immediately highlight a substantial advantage over existing techniques. Furthermore, the assumptions required for convergence (Assumption 1) are standard for ADMM but should be explicitly addressed in relation to the specific applications presented.

The experimental evaluation is limited. While the authors compare against several ADMM variants, the parameter tuning seems cursory. The claim that “PDMM performs better” is often qualified by statements like “PDMM can converge faster” or “PDMM is faster in terms of runtime,” suggesting the improvements are not consistently demonstrated. The choice of parameters for GSADMM and sADMM appears somewhat arbitrary, potentially skewing the comparison. The experiments also lack a more comprehensive benchmark against highly optimized solvers commonly used in these applications. The sensitivity analysis on the number of blocks K is useful but doesn't fully explain the observed performance differences. The fact that the results for RBSUMM were not included due to convergence issues raises concerns about the robustness of the comparison.

The paper suffers from some clarity issues. The notation is dense, and the motivation behind several design choices, particularly the specific formulas for τi and νi, feels underdeveloped. While the authors attempt to connect these parameters to the sparsity of A, the rationale isn't entirely clear, and the impact of different sparsity patterns isn’t thoroughly investigated. The connection between the backward step and the convergence behavior isn’t fully elucidated.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to many machine learning applications.
*   The potential for parallelization is a positive aspect.
*   The theoretical analysis provides some level of rigor.
*   The paper attempts a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks a comprehensive benchmark.
*   The clarity of the paper could be improved, especially regarding the motivation behind key design choices.
*   The parameter tuning appears cursory.
*   The benefit of PDMM is not conclusively demonstrated compared to well-established methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem and proposes a potentially useful algorithm.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a degree of confidence in the method’s convergence.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The incremental nature of the contribution.
*   The limited experimental evaluation.
*   The lack of a clear demonstration of superiority over existing methods.
*   The clarity issues in the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper isn't fundamentally flawed, it doesn't present a sufficiently compelling contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The improvements over existing ADMM variants are incremental and lack a strong theoretical or empirical justification. The paper needs significantly more thorough experimentation, clearer explanations, and a more convincing demonstration of its advantages to be considered for publication. The authors should consider focusing on specific scenarios where PDMM demonstrably outperforms existing methods and providing a more rigorous analysis of its behavior in those scenarios.
