

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving linearly-constrained optimization problems with block-separable convex functions. The key innovation is a method that can randomly update any number of blocks in parallel (not just one block like RBSUMM or all blocks like sADMM/PJADMM), while providing a unified framework that shows sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases of PDMM. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, where step size selection cleverly incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks being updated.

The paper makes a compelling case for its novelty. While existing methods either update a single block (RBSUMM) or all blocks (sADMM, PJADMM), PDMM offers a flexible middle ground that can update any number of blocks in parallel. This flexibility is particularly valuable for large-scale problems where parallelization is crucial. The introduction of the dual backward step (Equations 6-7) is a key insight that addresses convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates. The paper's theoretical analysis is sound, with clear connections to existing work and a well-structured proof of convergence.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, it provides a unifying perspective that connects existing methods through PDMM, which is valuable for understanding the landscape of ADMM variants. Second, the step size selection (Equation 8) that incorporates the sparsity of A and the number of blocks K is both theoretically grounded and practically useful. Third, the experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, particularly showing that PDMM with moderate K (not just K=1 or K=J) can be the fastest approach. The paper also successfully applies PDMM to the challenging problem of overlapping group lasso, where RBCD is still an open problem.

The main weakness is that the experimental evaluation, while solid, is limited to only two applications. More diverse experiments would strengthen the case for broad applicability. Additionally, the paper could better elaborate on the practical trade-offs between different values of K (number of blocks to update), particularly in terms of communication overhead versus convergence speed.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper's contribution highly significant. The ability to randomly update any number of blocks in parallel with a theoretically grounded step size selection represents a genuinely new direction in ADMM variants. The paper establishes a clear conceptual bridge between existing methods while offering a more flexible framework that can be tuned to problem-specific sparsity patterns.

The theoretical results are well-supported, and the paper makes a strong case for the significance of the work by demonstrating practical improvements in convergence speed on real-world problems. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides a deeper understanding of the role of the dual backward step, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization with clear practical implications. The ability to flexibly choose the number of blocks to update in parallel could significantly impact the design of distributed optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning problems. The paper's theoretical contribution (O(1/T) complexity with constant step size) is comparable to existing methods but with greater flexibility.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on ADMM variants (e.g., "Parallel multi-block ADMM with O(1/k) convergence" by Deng et al., which focuses on a specific parallel variant), this paper offers a more general framework with a novel perspective on the dual updates. The key differentiator is the ability to update any number of blocks in parallel, which is not present in prior work.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization and would be a valuable addition to NIPS. The novelty of the approach, the solid theoretical foundation, and the practical improvements demonstrated in experiments make it a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Strengths:**
- Novel algorithm that generalizes existing ADMM variants
- Theoretical convergence guarantees with constant step size
- Practical insights on step size selection incorporating problem sparsity
- Demonstrated improvements over existing methods in experiments
- Clear connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to two experimental applications
- Could provide more detailed analysis of K trade-offs
- Theoretical analysis assumes convexity, which is standard but worth noting

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to ADMM variants that offers significant flexibility in parallelization while providing a unifying theoretical framework. The contribution is substantial enough to outweigh the minor limitations in experimental scope.
