Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints. It proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM that updates any K randomly selected primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks, with two key ideas: (i) a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty, and (ii) step sizes τi and νi that depend on both the sparsity pattern of A (via di, the number of nonzeros per dual row block) and the number K of blocks updated per iteration. The method accommodates exact and inexact primal updates through a Bregman divergence term, allowing closed-form updates for difficult subproblems. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convexity assumptions and connect PDMM to two families: sADMM with splitting variables and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM), which appear as extreme cases depending on the aggressiveness of the primal update and dual step. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso show empirical gains over sADMM, RBSUMM, and some accelerated proximal baselines.

Relative to prior work, the paper is well-situated. It acknowledges challenges of multi-block ADMM (e.g., divergence, Chen et al.), the BSUMM/RBSUMM line (Hong et al.), proximal Jacobian ADMM (Deng et al.), and randomized block-coordinate descent (Nesterov; Richtárik–Takáč). The dual backward step resonates with over-relaxation/extragradient ideas in primal–dual methods (e.g., PDHG/Chambolle–Pock; Mirror-Prox), though these connections are not explicitly cited. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is conceptually appealing.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: clear diagnosis of why naive Jacobi ADMM may fail (implicit dual ascent) and a simple, interpretable “backward step” to control it.
- Adaptive steps: explicit formulas for τi, νi that leverage both sparsity (di) and the number of updated blocks (K); this is practically meaningful and theoretically motivated.
- Generality: covers exact and inexact updates via Bregman linearization, enabling closed-form updates for common losses/penalties.
- Theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes, with conditions aligned with standard ADMM assumptions. The residual definition and potential function h(v*, v^t) are carefully constructed.
- Unification: shows sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases, aiding understanding of when strong proximal terms vs. cautious dual steps are appropriate.
- Applications: overlapping groups handled naturally within the same framework; empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are promising.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited and partly unfair: all experiments are sequential (no parallel or distributed results), while the main selling point is parallel randomized updates. sADMM is reported using public code “without dual update,” which biases the comparison; fair tuning with proper dual steps/relaxation should be provided. RBSUMM is reported with step rules that may not satisfy its assumptions; failing to see convergence within 5000 iterations may reflect setup mismatch rather than a fundamental deficiency.
- Missing ablations: the effect of sparsity di on performance is asserted but not systematically studied; synthetic experiments varying di and K would strengthen the story.
- Theoretical exposition in the main text is heavy on notation but light on intuition for the choice of τi and νi; their derivation is deferred and readers must consult the supplement/arXiv. Proof sketches or clearer high-level arguments would improve accessibility.
- Connections to broader primal–dual literature (e.g., Chambolle–Pock, Mirror-Prox, over-relaxation) are not discussed; the “dual backward step” is reminiscent of extragradient corrections and could be contextualized and contrasted.
- No non-ergodic convergence guarantees and no results under inexactness beyond Bregman linearization conditions; it would help to clarify assumptions on Lipschitz/strong convexity and how constants affect the rates.
- Clarity/presentation: several typos and notational glitches (“seperable”, “convegence”); algorithmic pseudocode is missing; some matrices (P^t, Q) are introduced without intuition; many results are relegated to appendices with only terse references in the main text.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with standard assumptions and a plausible potential-based analysis yielding O(1/T). However, key parameter choices and auxiliary matrices are not motivated in the main text, and empirical comparisons have fairness issues and lack parallel scaling studies.

Clarity
Moderate. The paper is dense and notation-heavy; readability would benefit from algorithm pseudocode, a clearer summary of assumptions, and more intuition for the backward step and step-size rules. Some editorial issues and missing proof sketches hinder accessibility.

Originality
Good. While the components (randomized block updates, proximal terms, over-relaxation) are known, combining them with a principled dual backward step, sparsity-aware step sizes, and a unifying view of sADMM/PJADMM appears novel.

Significance
Potentially significant for large-scale, structured problems where parallel block updates are attractive. The ability to handle overlapping groups and to tune steps via sparsity is useful. Significance would be stronger with convincing parallel/distributed results and broader benchmarks.

Pros
- Clear idea addressing a known pathology of Jacobi ADMM
- Solid convergence analysis with constant step sizes
- Unifies related ADMM variants; practical step-size guidance
- Handles inexact updates and overlapping groups
- Empirical gains on two representative problems

Cons
- No parallel or distributed experiments; limited benchmarks
- Comparisons to baselines are not fully fair or comprehensive
- Derivations and parameter rules not sufficiently explained in the main text
- Limited discussion of related primal–dual/extragradient methods
- Only ergodic O(1/T); no non-ergodic rates or sharper constants

Suggestions for improvement
- Add parallel and/or distributed experiments demonstrating wall-clock scalability with varying K and di, including speedup vs. cores.
- Ensure fair baselines: implement sADMM with proper dual updates/relaxation; include modern primal–dual (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG with over-relaxation), linearized ADMM, and recent multi-block convergent schemes.
- Provide ablations varying di and K on synthetic problems to validate the sparsity-aware step-size advantage; report sensitivity to τi, νi.
- Include algorithm pseudocode and a high-level derivation/intuition for τi, νi in the main text; explain the roles of P^t and Q.
- Discuss connections to extragradient methods and over-relaxed ADMM, clarifying similarities/differences and when PDMM is preferable.
- Clean up typos/notation and clarify the setup for inexact updates (assumptions on Lipschitz/strong convexity and how to choose Bregman generators).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The conceptual contribution and theory are compelling, but the empirical validation (especially parallel scalability and fair baselines) and clarity fall short of NeurIPS standards. With strengthened experiments, clearer exposition of parameter derivations, and broader comparisons, this would become a strong submission.
