

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors claim PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, handles overlapping group structures, and achieves O(1/T) convergence with constant step size. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear connection between existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) and introduces a unified framework (PDMM) that encompasses them. The theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions. The experimental results demonstrate practical efficiency improvements over existing methods, particularly for overlapping group lasso.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Errors
1. **Typographical errors**: The paper uses "block-seperable" (p.1) instead of "block-separable" and has inconsistent spacing in equations (e.g., "ρ 2" in Eq. 9 instead of "ρ/2").

2. **Mathematical notation**: In Eq. (8), "νi = 1− K̃i" should be "νi = 1 - K̃i" for proper formatting. The notation "K̃i" is inconsistently defined (sometimes as "min{di,K}" but used as a variable without clear definition).

3. **Critical mathematical error**: In Section 2.3, the paper states "Uj ∈ Rb×L is the columns of an identity matrix" when it should be "Uj ∈ Rb×b" (the identity matrix is b×b, not b×L). This is a fundamental error that would mislead readers.

4. **Equation formatting**: In Eq. (19), "ρ ‖zt+1 − zt‖2Pt" uses "Pt" as a matrix but writes it without proper notation (should be "P_t" or "P_t" with subscript).

### Theoretical Issues
1. **Convergence rate claim**: The paper claims "PDMM converges at the same rate as ADMM and its variants" but doesn't specify the exact rate (O(1/T) vs o(1/T)) or how it compares to specific variants like PJADMM.

2. **Missing details in proofs**: The paper references "Appendix B in [25]" and "Appendix C in [25]" for connections to sADMM and PJADMM, but these appendices are not provided in the submitted version. This undermines the theoretical claims.

3. **Theoretical assumptions**: The paper relies on Assumption 1, which is standard for ADMM, but doesn't discuss how this assumption compares to assumptions in other methods (e.g., BSUMM requires additional local error bounds).

### Experimental Issues
1. **Experimental setup ambiguity**: The paper refers to "PDMM1", "PDMM2", and "PDMM3" but doesn't clearly define what the number signifies (e.g., number of blocks updated in parallel, or number of blocks in the problem). This makes it difficult to reproduce the results.

2. **Comparison method**: The paper claims "sADMM should be the same as PDMM3 if τi = 1/3" but then states "sADMM does not have dual update, i.e., τi = 0" (p.11), creating a contradiction.

3. **Missing baseline**: The paper compares PDMM to RBSUMM, sADMM, and GSADMM, but doesn't include a comparison to the randomized block coordinate descent (RBCD) method [16, 18] that the paper claims is not applicable to overlapping blocks (Section 1).

### Conceptual Issues
1. **Parallelism claim**: The paper claims "PDMM can randomly update arbitrary number of primal blocks in parallel" but the algorithm (5)-(7) updates the selected blocks sequentially (not truly in parallel), which contradicts the paper's claim.

2. **Practical relevance**: While the paper claims "PDMM can achieve the fastest convergence by setting J = K = 1" (p.12), this reduces to the method of multipliers, which is not parallel and may be computationally infeasible for large problems.

## Recommendation
While the paper introduces an interesting unified framework connecting existing methods and has some promising experimental results, the numerous technical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and conceptual ambiguities significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The fundamental error in Section 2.3 (misdefining Uj) is particularly concerning as it would mislead readers about the overlapping group lasso formulation.

The paper's theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by the presented analysis, and the experimental comparisons are not as clear as they could be. The authors have made a good attempt at a novel contribution but have not executed it with the precision expected at NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the significant technical issues that prevent the reader from confidently assessing the paper's contribution. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.
