

# Peer Review: "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel (rather than sequentially as in Gauss-Seidel ADMM or one block at a time as in RBSUMM), and introduces a dual backward step to ensure convergence. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, showing that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The paper demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, showing improved performance over existing methods. This work builds on the rich literature of ADMM variants (e.g., [2, 10, 14, 15, 17]) while addressing the gap in parallel randomized block coordinate approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis with global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity guarantees. The unified framework that connects PDMM to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is particularly insightful and clarifies the role of the dual backward step.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Approach**: PDMM's ability to randomly update any number of blocks in parallel represents a significant advancement over existing methods. The paper demonstrates how step sizes can be adapted to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated, which is a practical and theoretically sound contribution.

3. **Practical Impact**: The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso clearly demonstrate PDMM's advantages in terms of both convergence speed and runtime. The paper successfully addresses the open problem of randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping groups, which previous work [16, 18] has not solved.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear motivation, thorough theoretical development, and convincing experimental validation. The connections to existing work are well-explained, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Inexact Update Analysis**: While the paper discusses inexact updates using Bregman divergences, the theoretical convergence guarantees primarily focus on the exact case (η_j = 0). A more comprehensive analysis of the inexact update regime would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper provides a theoretically justified choice of τ_i and ν_i, but the experiments don't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of PDMM to these parameters. It would be valuable to see how performance changes with different step sizes.

3. **Scalability to Very Large Problems**: The experiments use moderate-sized problems (e.g., m = 1000, n = 5000), but the paper doesn't demonstrate PDMM's performance on truly massive datasets where parallelism would provide the most significant benefits. A scalability analysis would strengthen the practical relevance.

4. **Comparison with RBSUMM**: The comparison with RBSUMM is limited, as the authors note they couldn't get RBSUMM to converge within 5000 iterations with various parameter settings. A more thorough comparison would better establish PDMM's advantages.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by convincing experimental results. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., the trade-off between number of blocks and subproblem complexity) and provide complete proofs in the supplement. The paper represents a complete, well-executed piece of work.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The mathematical notation is consistent, the organization is logical, and the figures and tables effectively communicate the results. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The core contribution—PDMM as a parallel randomized block coordinate method for ADMM—represents a significant advance over existing work. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides new theoretical insights. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work and thoroughly references related contributions.

**Significance**: The results address an important class of optimization problems that arise frequently in machine learning. The O(1/T) convergence rate is comparable to existing methods, but with the added benefit of parallelism, which is crucial for large-scale problems. The demonstrated improvements in runtime and convergence speed suggest PDMM could be widely adopted in practice.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, the experimental results are compelling, and the work addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper provides both a novel algorithm and a unifying theoretical framework for understanding existing methods. While there are some limitations in the experimental comparison and theoretical analysis of inexact updates, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.

PDMM advances the state of the art in parallel optimization methods for machine learning and has the potential to impact a broad range of applications. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The results are important enough that I believe this paper will be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Novel parallel randomized block coordinate approach to ADMM
- Rigorous convergence analysis with O(1/T) complexity
- Unified framework connecting existing methods
- Strong experimental results on real-world problems
- Addresses an open problem (randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping groups)

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Limited analysis of inexact updates
- Limited scalability demonstration on very large problems
- Some parameter sensitivity not thoroughly explored
- Comparison with RBSUMM could be more comprehensive

Overall, this paper represents a significant contribution to optimization for machine learning that meets all the criteria for acceptance at NeurIPS.
