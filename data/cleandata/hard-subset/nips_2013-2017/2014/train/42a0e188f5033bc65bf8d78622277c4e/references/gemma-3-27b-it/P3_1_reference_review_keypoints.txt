- Summary: Introduces PDMM — a randomized block-coordinate ADMM with a dual “backward step” and adaptive step sizes (based on A’s sparsity and chosen blocks), with O(1/T) convergence and experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso; connects sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.  
- Strength — relevance: Tackles the important problem of scaling ADMM to multi-block settings.  
- Strength — novelty: Combines randomized block updates, a backward dual step, and sparsity-aware step sizes; unifies sADMM and PJADMM.  
- Strength — theoretical rigor: Provides convergence analysis with O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions.  
- Strength — empirical validation: Competitive results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, with well-designed experiments.  
- Strength — clarity: Well-written, organized, consistent notation, and helpful connections to related methods.  
- Weakness — parameter sensitivity: Performance likely depends on careful tuning of τ, ν, and K, with insufficient exploration of sensitivity.  
- Weakness — random selection overhead: Lacks discussion/analysis of computational cost and trade-offs of random block selection at large scale.  
- Weakness — limited comparisons: Comparisons to GSADMM, RBSUMM, sADMM are incomplete and parameter choices for baselines are not fully justified.  
- Weakness — Bregman divergence role: Inclusion feels tacked on; practical impact and effect of divergence choice are not well demonstrated.  
- Weakness — scalability: Does not demonstrate scalability to truly large-scale problems, a key motivation for randomized methods.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, valuable contribution with solid theory and encouraging experiments; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Pro-accept arguments: Addresses a significant challenge, presents a novel/theoretically sound method, shows competitive empirical performance, unifies existing methods, and is clearly written.  
- Con-accept arguments: Needs better treatment of parameter sensitivity, analysis of random-selection cost, more comprehensive comparisons, and large-scale scalability evidence.  
- Rating: Accept (with minor revisions).
