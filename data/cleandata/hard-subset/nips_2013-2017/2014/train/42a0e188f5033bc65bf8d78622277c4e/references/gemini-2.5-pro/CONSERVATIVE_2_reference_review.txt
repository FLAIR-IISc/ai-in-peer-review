**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear equality constraints. The method is a randomized block coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Its main contribution is the introduction of a "dual backward step," which aims to stabilize the parallel updates that are known to cause convergence issues in naive Jacobian-style ADMM. The algorithm randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks for parallel updates at each iteration. Notably, the step sizes for the dual update and the backward step are adaptively chosen based on the number of selected blocks (`K`) and the block-wise sparsity of the constraint matrix. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity. They also show that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, can be seen as special cases of PDMM. The method's performance is evaluated on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Sound Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-known and practical challenge in large-scale optimization: the difficulty of effectively parallelizing multi-block ADMM. The motivation for the proposed method is clearly articulated, stemming from an intuitive observation about the instability caused by aggressive "implicit" and "explicit" dual updates.
2.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The inclusion of a formal convergence analysis is a significant strength. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate under standard assumptions, which is on par with related ADMM methods. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm.
3.  **Unifying Perspective:** The paper provides a valuable contribution by positioning PDMM as a generalized framework that unifies two existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM. This connection enhances the understanding of the trade-offs between primal update accuracy and dual update step-sizing in parallel ADMM variants.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness of this paper lies in the experimental evaluation, which is not sufficiently rigorous to substantiate the claims of the method's superiority.
    *   **Limited Scope:** Experiments are confined to only two applications (RPCA, Overlapping Group Lasso) and appear to use a single data configuration for each. A new general-purpose optimizer should be tested on a more diverse set of problems to demonstrate its robustness and general applicability.
    *   **Flawed Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison against sADMM, a key competitor, seems flawed. The authors state that the public code they used had a zero step size (`tau_i = 0`) and that a correct implementation "should be the same as PDMM3". This implies they did not compare against a proper implementation of sADMM as described in the original work. A fair comparison requires implementing the baseline correctly, and the current result against sADMM is therefore not credible.
    *   **Dismissal of Baselines:** The claim that RBSUMM "could not see the convergence" on the overlapping group lasso problem is a very strong one. This could be due to implementation issues or insufficient effort in parameter tuning. Without further details, this dismissal weakens the empirical study.
2.  **Lack of True Parallelism Demonstration:** Despite the method being named "Parallel DMM," all experiments were run sequentially. The reported runtime improvements are based on per-iteration complexity on a single core. The practical benefits of parallelization, considering communication and synchronization overheads, are not demonstrated. A proper evaluation would require experiments on a multi-core or distributed system to validate the claimed performance benefits in a parallel setting.
3.  **Clarity of Novelty:** While the dual backward step is presented as the core innovation, its relationship to other stabilization techniques like under-relaxation is not discussed. The derivation of the specific step-size rule in equation (8) is also not intuitively explained in the main text, making it appear somewhat ad-hoc without consulting the supplement. The key insights feel more like clever algebraic manipulation than a fundamentally new optimization principle.

**Detailed Comments for the Authors**

*   The most critical issue to address is the empirical comparison. To make a convincing case for PDMM, you must compare against correctly implemented versions of key baselines, particularly sADMM. The comparison should also be broadened to include more datasets and problem types.
*   The claim regarding the difficulty of applying RBCD to overlapping groups is interesting but requires a stronger justification or citation. Please clarify why existing methods fail, as this bolsters the significance of your contribution in Section 2.3.
*   While I understand space is limited, providing more intuition behind the specific choice of `tau_i` and `nu_i` in the main paper would significantly improve readability and the reader's understanding of why the method works.
*   To truly justify the "Parallel" in the paper's title, experiments demonstrating speedup on a parallel architecture are necessary. Sequential runtime comparisons can be misleading and do not capture the full picture of an algorithm designed for parallel execution.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound extension of ADMM for parallel block-coordinate updates. The theoretical contribution, particularly the unifying framework for sADMM and PJADMM, is noteworthy. However, the work is let down by an insufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation. The comparisons to established baselines like sADMM are not conducted fairly, and the lack of experiments in a true parallel environment makes it difficult to assess the method's practical significance. While the ideas are promising, the paper in its current form does not provide the extensive evidence and thorough validation required to endorse this new method over existing, well-established ones. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time. A revised version with a substantially improved experimental section could be a strong contender for acceptance.
