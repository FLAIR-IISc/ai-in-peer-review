Summary
The paper tackles convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM that can update any number K of primal blocks per iteration. The key design is a “dual backward step”: instead of using the current dual iterate y in the primal subproblems, PDMM uses a corrected ŷ = y − νρ(Ax − a), thereby tempering the combined effect of the implicit dual ascent from the quadratic penalty and the explicit dual ascent. The step sizes τi (for dual ascent) and νi (for the backward step) depend on the sparsity of A via di (the number of nonzeros in dual row-block i) and on K via K̃i = min{di, K}: τi = K/(K̃i(2J − K)), νi = 1 − 1/K̃i. The method also admits inexact updates via a Bregman divergence term to linearize difficult parts of the subproblems.

The authors show that two prominent multi-block ADMM variants are special cases: two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) when τ = 1/J, ν = 1 − 1/J (exact updates), and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) when sufficiently large proximal terms are used (allowing τ = 1, ν = 0). They provide global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions, with constants depending on di and K. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate PDMM can be faster than sADMM and RBSUMM, and competitive in runtime with GSADMM, despite GSADMM converging in fewer iterations in some settings.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned among multi-block ADMM methods: Gauss–Seidel variants (including GSADMM and BSUMM), Jacobian/proximal Jacobian methods, and randomized updates (RBSUMM). It connects PDMM’s parameters to these methods and to the structure of A, and argues the dual backward step mitigates divergence observed in direct Jacobi ADMM. The inexact/Bregman approach is aligned with linearized ADMM and proximal majorization frameworks.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The dual backward step is a clear and useful insight that explains why naive Jacobi updates diverge and how to stabilize them. The dependence of τi, νi on both sparsity (di) and K is novel and practically motivated.
- Unifying view: Showing sADMM and PJADMM are extreme cases of PDMM helps position and interpret a range of algorithms within one framework.
- Theoretical guarantees: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes for randomized parallel block updates under standard convex assumptions.
- Practical scope: Handles nonsmooth functions and overlapping groups; the inexact Bregman framework leads to closed-form updates in common cases.
- Empirical observations: Sensible exploration of the effects of K, τ, ν and di; improvements over sADMM and RBSUMM in the reported settings.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
Quality
- Theory: The rate matches known O(1/T) results; there is no demonstrated rate improvement or nonergodic bounds. The constants and matrices P^t, Q, βi are only sketched (positive semidefinite, etc.), which limits interpretability. Please specify them explicitly and provide bounds that highlight dependence on di and K.
- Novelty vs known corrections: The backward step resembles relaxation/correction steps (e.g., over-relaxed/under-relaxed ADMM, extragradient primal–dual methods). A more precise comparison to relaxed ADMM and Chambolle–Pock-type algorithms would strengthen positioning.
- Experiments: All implementations are sequential MATLAB. The main empirical claim is parallelizability, yet no actual parallel or distributed results or communication/latency analysis are shown. The comparisons depend on tuning τ, ν for PDMM, while the sADMM implementation reportedly lacks a dual update, which is unfavorable to sADMM. The RBSUMM tuning may not be exhaustive. Provide:
  - A fair sADMM baseline (with dual updates) and PJADMM.
  - Parallel wall-clock scaling on multi-core or distributed systems, with K varied and di exploited.
  - Clear per-iteration complexity and memory/communication costs.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy. Key objects (P^t, Q, βi) and their roles should be defined clearly in the main text, not only in the supplement.
- Several typos/grammar and minor inconsistencies: “seperable”→“separable”, “defiend”→“defined”, “convegence”→“convergence”, “contast”→“contrast”, “negligable”→“negligible”. Equation-level issues: e.g., in Sec. 2.3 the phrase “sADMM uses τi = 1/J 0.5” is confusing; please fix. A concise algorithm box with default parameter choices would help.

Originality
- The idea of tempering the dual step in Jacobi ADMM is interesting and the explicit formula tying τi, νi to di, K seems new. However, the relation to prior stabilized multi-block ADMM (e.g., Gaussian back substitution, proximal/linearized variants, relaxed ADMM) should be made more explicit to delineate what is genuinely new versus a reparameterization.

Significance
- If supported by stronger empirical evidence and clearer theory constants, PDMM could be a practical addition for multi-block, structured problems (e.g., overlapping regularizers, networked constraints), especially where A is sparse and K can be tuned. As it stands, the significance is promising but somewhat underdemonstrated due to limited, non-parallel experiments.

Questions for the authors
- Are the τi, νi choices optimal (in some sense) for given di, K? How sensitive is convergence speed to these choices?
- Can you provide explicit expressions for P^t and Q and discuss their magnitudes?
- How exactly does PDMM relate to over-relaxed ADMM (relaxation parameter α) and to extragradient primal–dual methods?
- Can K be selected adaptively during iterations? Any heuristic or theoretical guideline?
- For overlapping groups (Sec. 2.3), please provide a concise derivation of τi = νi = 0.5 when K = J and di = 2.
- Any extensions to strongly convex settings (linear convergence) or to certain nonconvex problems?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, useful conceptual stabilization of Jacobi ADMM via dual backward step.
- Unifying framework linking sADMM and PJADMM; handles overlapping groups.
- Solid convergence analysis under standard assumptions; simple step formulas leveraging sparsity.
- Empirical gains on representative convex problems.

Cons for acceptance
- No actual parallel experiments; fairness and breadth of baselines could be improved.
- Clarity issues and missing explicit constants/definitions in the main text.
- Incremental rate guarantees; limited discussion vis-à-vis closely related stabilized/relaxed ADMM literature.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper has a good idea and promising theory, but needs clearer exposition of the analysis, stronger and fairer empirical validation (especially true parallel results), and a tighter positioning with respect to relaxed/extragradient ADMM variants. With these addressed, it would be a strong candidate. Confidence: medium.
