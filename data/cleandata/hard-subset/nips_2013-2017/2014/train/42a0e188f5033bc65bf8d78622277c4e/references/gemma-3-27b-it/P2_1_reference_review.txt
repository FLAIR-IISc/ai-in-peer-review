## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent variant of ADMM for solving linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization problems. The authors address the challenges of parallelizing ADMM for multiple blocks, where naive Jacobi updates often fail to converge. PDMM tackles this by randomly selecting a subset of blocks to update in parallel, employing a backward step on the dual update to promote stability, and adapting step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix *A*. The paper establishes convergence guarantees and demonstrates empirical performance on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso problems, positioning PDMM as a flexible and potentially efficient alternative to existing ADMM variants.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Efficiently solving large-scale linearly constrained optimization problems is crucial in many machine learning applications. Parallelizing ADMM, a popular approach, is a natural and important extension.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of randomized block selection, the backward step on the dual variable, and sparsity-aware step size adjustment offers a genuinely novel contribution.  The authors clearly articulate the intuition behind each component.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, even if under fairly standard assumptions, is a significant strength.  The analysis seems rigorous (though detailed proof verification would require examining the supplement).
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM provides valuable insight and contextualizes the proposed method within the broader ADMM landscape. This connection helps clarify the roles of different components and parameters.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on RPCA and Overlapping Group Lasso are encouraging, showing PDMM performs favorably compared to other ADMM variants and, in some cases, state-of-the-art methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard and the algorithm is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the results are positive, the experiments are limited to two applications. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a broader range of problem types and datasets would strengthen the claims of general applicability.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention tuning parameters for comparisons, but detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity for PDMM itself is lacking. A sensitivity analysis investigating the impact of *K* (number of randomly selected blocks) and other parameters on performance would be valuable.
* **Overlapping Group Lasso Experiment Detail:** The description of the overlapping group lasso experiment is somewhat brief. More detail regarding the data generation process and the specific parameter settings used for comparison would enhance reproducibility and facilitate a more thorough evaluation.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper does not mention details regarding the implementation (e.g., programming language, libraries used for sparse matrix operations, parallelization strategy). Knowing these details is important for evaluating the feasibility and scalability of PDMM.
* **Lack of Comparison to Fully Parallel Methods:** While comparisons to sequential methods are made, the paper doesn’t compare PDMM’s performance against *truly* parallel implementations of algorithms designed from the ground up for distributed computation, which could provide a more relevant benchmark.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The convergence analysis seems reasonable, and the empirical results provide supporting evidence. However, a detailed review of the supplemental material is necessary to fully assess the correctness of the theoretical claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.  The motivation and intuition behind the proposed method are clearly explained.
* **Originality:**  The approach is original, combining existing techniques in a novel way to address a significant challenge in parallel optimization. The connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:**  The potential for PDMM to accelerate ADMM-based optimization for large-scale problems is significant.  If the empirical results generalize to other applications, this could have a tangible impact on research and practice.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem in large-scale optimization.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Provides theoretical convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results.
* Clearly positions the work within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical evaluation.
* Lack of detailed parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Lack of comparison to truly parallel algorithms designed for distributed computation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization.  The proposed PDMM algorithm is a promising approach for parallelizing ADMM, and the theoretical and empirical results are encouraging.  While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

*   Expand the empirical evaluation to include a broader range of problem types and datasets.
*   Provide a more detailed sensitivity analysis of the key parameters of PDMM.
*   Include more details about the implementation of PDMM, including programming language, libraries used, and parallelization strategy.
* Consider adding a comparison to methods specifically designed for massively parallel computation.
