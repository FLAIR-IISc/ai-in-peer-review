This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block-coordinate algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to generalize ADMM-style methods to allow for a random subset of `K` primal variable blocks to be updated in parallel at each iteration. To ensure convergence, the authors introduce a crucial "dual backward step" and derive step sizes for both the standard dual update (`τ_i`) and this new backward step (`ν_i`) that cleverly depend on the number of blocks being updated (`K`) and the sparsity of the constraint matrix (`d_i`).

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a flexible algorithmic framework that bridges the gap between fully parallel Jacobian-style updates (`K=J`) and single-block randomized updates (`K=1`). Second, it establishes a valuable theoretical connection by showing that two existing methods, the splitting-based sADMM and the proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM), are special cases of PDMM corresponding to exact and inexact primal updates when all blocks are selected (`K=J`). This unification provides deeper insight into the role of the dual backward step. Third, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. Finally, the utility of PDMM is demonstrated on two relevant machine learning problems: robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and important contribution to the literature on distributed and parallel optimization. The idea of a randomized parallel update with an adaptive dual-step mechanism is both elegant and practical. It offers a new degree of freedom (`K`) for practitioners to trade off per-iteration computational cost, communication, and overall convergence speed. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly compelling, as this problem structure is notoriously difficult for standard block coordinate descent methods, and PDMM provides a principled way to parallelize the updates.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The introduction of the dual backward step is the key innovation that enables convergence for parallel updates, addressing a known instability of direct Jacobi-style ADMM. The derivation of the step sizes (`τ_i`, `ν_i`) as a function of `K` and `d_i` is non-trivial and demonstrates a deep understanding of the algorithm's convergence dynamics. The convergence proofs seem solid and follow a Lyapunov-based argument, which is standard for this class of methods.

3.  **Unifying Perspective:** A major strength is the unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework. This is more than just a curiosity; it clarifies the relationship between the accuracy of the primal update and the necessary "conservatism" of the dual update. PDMM shows that an exact primal step (like in sADMM) requires a more conservative dual update (a significant backward step), while an inexact/proximal primal step (like in PJADMM) allows for a more aggressive dual update (no backward step). This provides a valuable conceptual contribution to the field.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained by analyzing the failure of direct Jacobi updates. The connections to related work are thoroughly discussed, placing PDMM appropriately within the landscape of ADMM variants.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental evaluation, while positive, has a few weaknesses.
    *   In the RPCA experiment, the authors state that the public code for sADMM has no dual update (`τ_i = 0`), which explains its slow performance. This is not a fair comparison. For a direct comparison, sADMM should have been implemented according to its theoretical specification (which corresponds to PDMM with K=J). The current result might be misleading about the relative performance of sADMM.
    *   The primary motivation for PDMM is *parallelism*. However, all experiments were run sequentially. While sequential runtime (combining iteration count and per-iteration cost) is a reasonable proxy, the paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by even a small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine demonstrating actual wall-clock time speedups as `K` is varied. This would provide direct evidence of the practical benefits of the proposed parallel scheme.

2.  **Intuition behind Step Sizes:** The formulas for `τ_i` and `ν_i` in equation (8) are presented without much intuition. While the proof in the supplement presumably validates them, the main paper would benefit from a brief, high-level explanation of why these specific forms arise. For example, why does `τ_i` increase and `ν_i` decrease as `K` decreases? A sentence or two explaining the intuition would improve readability and understanding.

3.  **Choice of K:** The paper shows the effect of varying `K` in one experiment but provides little general guidance on how to choose `K` in practice. The choice of `K` involves a trade-off between the degree of parallelism (higher `K` is better) and the convergence rate per iteration (lower `K` appears to have better constants, as noted in Remark 2 for K=1). A more detailed discussion of this trade-off, perhaps considering communication overhead in a distributed setting, would enhance the paper's practical impact.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound algorithm for a broad and important class of optimization problems. The unification of existing methods is a significant conceptual contribution. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental section and could be addressed in a final version. The strengths, particularly the originality of the method and the quality of the theoretical analysis, far outweigh the weaknesses.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and flexible randomized parallel ADMM variant (PDMM).
*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation with global convergence and an O(1/T) rate.
*   Offers a valuable unification of sADMM and PJADMM, enhancing the understanding of related methods.
*   Addresses important ML problems, including the challenging case of overlapping regularizers.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison with sADMM may be unfair due to an implementation issue.
*   The paper lacks experiments demonstrating actual parallel speedups, which is the main motivation for the algorithm.

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper presents a solid and significant contribution to the optimization toolkit for machine learning. The authors should be encouraged to strengthen the experimental section as suggested above for the final camera-ready version.
