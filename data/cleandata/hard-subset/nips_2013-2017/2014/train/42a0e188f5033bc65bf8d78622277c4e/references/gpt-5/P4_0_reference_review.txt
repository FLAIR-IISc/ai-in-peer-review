Summary
The paper studies convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives under linear constraints and proposes a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM, termed Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The method updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and all dual blocks each iteration. Key features are: (i) a “dual backward step” that damps the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term, implemented via an intermediate dual variable ŷ and parameters {τi, νi}; (ii) step-size rules for {τi, νi} that depend on the sparsity pattern of A (through di, the number of nonzeros per dual row block) and the number K of updated blocks; and (iii) support for inexact primal updates via Bregman proximal terms to enable closed-form updates. The authors show that two existing parallel multi-block ADMM variants are special cases of PDMM: sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) when τi = 1/J and νi = 1 − 1/J, and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) when ηj is sufficiently large enabling τi = 1 and νi = 0. For theory, under standard convexity/KKT assumptions, they establish global convergence in expectation and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes, with a Lyapunov function tailored to the randomized updates and the sparsity-aware step sizes. Empirically, PDMM is evaluated on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, showing faster convergence than RBSUMM and sADMM and competitive or better runtime than GSADMM/ADMM, with additional analysis of the impact of K and of τi, νi.

Relation to prior work: ADMM and its two-block variants are well established; multi-block Gauss–Seidel ADMM can diverge, and convergence is typically guaranteed via proximal terms, back substitution, or restrictive conditions. Jacobian/parallel variants (e.g., PJADMM) and randomized block variants (RBSUMM) exist but either rely on proximal regularization with tuned parameters or have weaker/conditional guarantees. PDMM unifies sADMM and PJADMM through explicit damping and proximalization and adds randomized K-block updating with sparsity-aware step sizes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theoretical development is plausible and consistent with the literature on randomized block-coordinate augmented Lagrangian methods. The use of a Lyapunov potential h(v*, vt), the expectation over random block choices, and the ergodic O(1/T) rate align with known ADMM analyses. The sparsity-aware step sizes τi = K/[K̃i(2J − K)] and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i are motivated by controlling implicit dual ascent and appear conservative. However, some definitions are underspecified in the main text (e.g., Pt, Q in the residual and Lyapunov terms), and full proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate advantages over RBSUMM and sADMM, and competitive runtime vs. GSADMM/ADMM. The effect of K and τi, νi is explored. However, all implementations are sequential Matlab; no true parallel/distributed experiments are shown. Some comparisons are potentially confounded (e.g., sADMM code without dual update; later tuning is mentioned but not fully standardized across methods). Parameter choices for ηj and proximal functions are not fully specified.
- Reproducibility: Algorithms are described via update equations, but a pseudocode algorithm, explicit defaults for ηj/Bregman choices, and more complete hyperparameter selection procedures would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but suffers from typographical errors, notational inconsistencies, and incomplete definitions:
- Several symbols (e.g., Pt, Q) are used before being clearly defined; Ajc vs. Acj notation sometimes varies.
- The intuition for the dual backward step is clear, but a concise algorithm box with all steps, parameter settings, and options (exact/inexact) would help.
- Citations are inconsistently formatted; some references lack years or complete details.

Originality
The combination of (i) randomized K-block parallel updates; (ii) a principled dual backward step with sparsity- and K-dependent damping; and (iii) guarantees with constant stepsizes without local error bounds appears novel relative to sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM. The unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM provides useful conceptual insight. While damping/relaxation ideas have precedents in ADMM, calibrating τi, νi via di and K and analyzing the resulting randomized parallel scheme seems new.

Significance
The approach is relevant for large-scale block-structured problems in machine learning, especially when A is sparse and blocks can be updated in parallel. Theoretical guarantees at O(1/T) with constant steps and explicit dependence on sparsity are valuable. Practical significance would be strengthened by demonstrations on parallel hardware, larger-scale datasets, and comparisons with other recent stabilized multi-block ADMM and over-relaxed variants.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear formulation of a parallel randomized multi-block ADMM with a well-motivated dual backward step.
- Sparsity-aware and K-aware step-size rules; unifies sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.
- Convergence and ergodic O(1/T) rate with constant steps under standard assumptions; no local error bounds required.
- Empirical improvements over RBSUMM and sADMM; competitive with GSADMM/ADMM.
- Handles inexact updates via Bregman terms, broadening applicability to nonsmooth/nonquadratic losses.

Cons:
- Clarity issues: missing definitions (Pt, Q), inconsistent notation, and some grammatical/typo problems.
- Experimental setup is sequential only; no true parallel/distributed scaling results to validate the main motivation.
- Some baseline comparisons appear uneven (e.g., sADMM implementation details); hyperparameter selection for ηj and Bregman functions is not fully specified.
- Guarantees are ergodic and in expectation; no non-ergodic bounds; no discussion of communication costs or asynchrony.
- Limited discussion of relation to over-relaxed/damped ADMM beyond sADMM/PJADMM; broader positioning could strengthen novelty claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complete algorithm box with all variables, default parameter choices (τi, νi, ηj), and recommended Bregman divergences for common f.
- Clarify and define Pt and Q and the role they play in the residual and Lyapunov function.
- Standardize and document parameter tuning across baselines; ensure sADMM/GSADMM implementations are fully representative.
- Include parallel/distributed experiments and scalability analyses; report per-iteration communication and compute costs, and study heterogeneity in di.
- Discuss connections to over-relaxed ADMM and other stabilized multi-block ADMMs; position PDMM relative to these methods.
- Improve notation consistency and citation formatting.

Overall evaluation
The paper offers a technically sound and useful contribution to parallel multi-block ADMM with a unifying perspective and practical step-size guidance that leverages sparsity. While clarity and experimental validation can be improved—especially via real parallel scaling—the core ideas and analysis are significant and original enough to merit consideration.
