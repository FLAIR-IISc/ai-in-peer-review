

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the limitations of existing Jacobian ADMM variants by introducing a dual backward step, considering the sparsity of the constraint matrix, and allowing arbitrary numbers of blocks to be updated in parallel. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates empirical superiority over existing methods on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by extending the ADMM framework to a more flexible parallel setting. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, establishing connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) as special cases. The experimental results are well-designed and demonstrate PDMM's practical advantages, particularly in terms of runtime efficiency. The paper identifies an important gap in the literature regarding randomized block coordinate methods for overlapping structures.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Notation and Formatting Errors
The paper contains numerous minor but significant technical notation inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:

- "block-seperable" appears multiple times instead of "block-separable" (Introduction, Abstract, Section 1)
- Inconsistent notation for column blocks: "Acj" in some places vs "A_{c_j}" elsewhere
- Equation (2) incorrectly omits the 1/2 factor in the quadratic penalty term: should be ρ/2||Ax-a||²₂
- Equation (8) has inconsistent formatting: τi = K/K̃i(2J−K) should be τ_i = K/(K̃_i(2J-K))
- In the abstract: "J∑ j=1" should be "\sum_{j=1}^J"
- In the abstract: "xj ∈ Rnj×1" should be "x_j ∈ \mathbb{R}^{n_j \times 1}"

### 2. Mathematical Inconsistencies
The paper contains several confusing mathematical formulations:

- In equation (5), the notation "x t k 6=j" is unclear and should be "x_{t,-j}"
- The paper states "Bφjt (xjt ,x t jt )" but should use consistent subscripting as "B_{\phi_{j_t}}(x_{j_t}, x^t_{j_t})"
- The connection between PDMM and sADMM (Section 2.2) claims τ_i = 1/J, but the paper writes "τi = 1J" which is ambiguous
- In the overlapping group lasso experiment, the paper claims J=101 blocks, but the problem formulation only has 100 groups plus the variable w, making J=101 correct, but this should be explicitly stated

### 3. Ambiguous Claims and Explanations
The paper makes several claims that are unclear or potentially misleading:

- "RBCD [16, 18] is used to solve (2) exactly, but leading to a double-loop algorithm" - RBCD is not necessarily a double-loop algorithm; this is inaccurate and misleading
- "PDMM can also do randomized dual block coordinate ascent in an extended work [25]" - the paper should provide a brief explanation of this extension rather than just mentioning it
- The statement "PDMM3 is faster than two randomized version of PDMM since the costs of extra iterations in PDMM1 and PDMM2 have surpassed the savings at each iteration" is vague and lacks quantitative support

### 4. Experimental Section Issues
The experimental section has several weaknesses:

- The notation "PDMM1, PDMM2, PDMM3" is confusing as it implies K=1,2,3 but the paper doesn't clarify whether K refers to the number of blocks or the number of blocks updated in parallel
- The paper claims PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm but doesn't provide sufficient evidence for this claim (e.g., concrete iteration counts or runtime comparisons)
- The reference to "RBSUMM" in the experiments is not properly explained in the main text, though it's cited in Section 2.2

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a technically sound extension to the ADMM framework with potentially useful theoretical and practical contributions, the numerous minor technical errors, inconsistent notation, and ambiguous claims significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too pervasive to overlook, as they suggest a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail that is expected in NIPS submissions.

The paper's main contribution - a parallel randomized block coordinate method for ADMM - is novel and potentially valuable. However, the minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper raise concerns about the thoroughness of the authors' review process. In a field like optimization where precise notation and mathematical correctness are paramount, these issues are particularly problematic.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, I would recommend acceptance if the authors could address the numerous minor errors and inconsistencies. However, as currently written, the paper's technical flaws make it difficult to assess the full contribution with confidence.

**Recommendation: Reject** (due to numerous minor technical inconsistencies that undermine the paper's credibility, despite the potentially valuable contribution)
