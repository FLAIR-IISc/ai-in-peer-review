- Proposes PDMM: randomized, parallel multi-block ADMM variant that updates K primal blocks and uses a “dual backward step.”
- Key ideas: dual backward step, sparsity- and K-dependent dual step sizes (τi, νi using di), and inexact primal updates via Bregman divergences.
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM are extremes of PDMM, clarifying the backward step’s role.
- Proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convexity/KKT assumptions.
- Lyapunov-like function h(v*,vt) and monotone expected decrease with explicit τi, νi formulas; proofs in supplement.
- Inexact/unified treatment via Bregman divergences is technically clean and aligns with prior ADMM analyses.
- Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM can outperform RBSUMM/sADMM and match GS-ADMM in runtime, exploiting sparsity benefits.
- Experimental limitations: sequential Matlab runs only (no parallel/distributed evaluation), modest-scale datasets, and some baselines not run in their strongest forms.
- Reproducibility: algorithm description mostly clear with concrete τi, νi, but Pt/Q matrices and some constructions are deferred or unspecified; pseudocode would help.
- Clarity: well organized and insightful on implicit dual ascent, but contains typos, heavy notation, and lacks explicit pseudocode and clearer block-selection description.
- Originality: novel randomized multi-block Jacobian updates with sparsity-aware steps and principled backward step; unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM.
- The inexact PDMM via Bregman is consistent with prior ideas but novel in this randomized multi-block context.
- Significance: practically relevant algorithmic design and step-size guidance, but theoretical rate matches existing best-known O(1/T) so novelty is more algorithmic than asymptotic.
- Pros: clear intuition for backward step, simple sparsity-aware step rules, unified exact/inexact framework, provable convergence with constant stepsizes, useful application to overlapping group lasso.
- Cons: limited empirical evaluation (no parallel/large-scale), some suboptimal baseline comparisons, clarity issues, and incremental theoretical novelty.
- Suggested improvements: add pseudocode and Pt/Q construction, give practical guidance for ηjt/ϕjt/ψjt, provide parallel/distributed experiments, compare to GS-ADMM with back substitution and proximal linearized ADMM, test robustness to di mis-specification, and include real datasets.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — solid algorithmic contribution and analysis but weaker experimental validation.
