

# NIPS Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the limitations of existing ADMM variants for multiple blocks, particularly the convergence issues of Gauss-Seidel ADMM and the restrictive step size choices of Jacobian ADMM methods. PDMM introduces a dual backward step, considers the sparsity of the constraint matrix, and allows for parallel updates of any number of blocks. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, connects PDMM to existing methods (showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases), and demonstrates empirical superiority on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results are well-motivated and supported by rigorous proofs (provided in the supplement). The authors establish convergence under reasonable assumptions, including the existence of a KKT point, which is standard in the ADMM literature. The O(1/T) iteration complexity matches the best-known rates for ADMM variants, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The algorithmic design is sound, with the dual backward step properly justified through analysis of the implicit dual ascent problem in standard Jacobi updates.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and relevant, comparing PDMM to multiple baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on two important applications. The results consistently show PDMM outperforms existing methods, particularly when using a moderate number of blocks (K=20-61) in the overlapping group lasso problem. The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that while PDMM achieves O(1/T) complexity, the constant factors depend on problem structure and block selection.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivates the proposed method. The algorithm description (Section 2) is precise, with the dual backward step and sparsity considerations well-explained. The connections to existing work (Section 2.2) are particularly helpful for understanding the novelty. The theoretical results (Section 3) are presented logically, with the key lemmas and theorems properly positioned.

The paper would benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the step size selection (τi, νi) in Section 2.3, as these parameters can be somewhat abstract. The notation is consistent, though the dense mathematical expressions in the theoretical sections might challenge readers unfamiliar with ADMM. The supplementary material provides necessary technical details without overwhelming the main paper.

## Originality

PDMM makes a significant contribution to the literature on ADMM variants. While recent work has explored parallel ADMM methods (e.g., sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM), PDMM introduces a unified framework that encompasses these methods as special cases while offering a more flexible approach to block selection and step size determination. The dual backward step is particularly novel, addressing the convergence issues of direct Jacobi updates that have been observed in prior work.

The paper properly situates its contribution relative to recent NIPS papers on ADMM, including Deng et al. (2015), Hong et al. (2015), and Wang et al. (2015). The key distinction is that PDMM provides a principled way to choose step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated, which is not present in prior methods. The application to overlapping group lasso is also novel, as the authors note that RBCD for overlapping blocks remains an open problem.

## Significance

The problem addressed (block-separable convex optimization with linear constraints) is fundamental in machine learning, with applications in robust PCA, group lasso, and many other problems. The paper demonstrates that PDMM can provide significant practical improvements over existing methods, particularly for problems with sparse constraint matrices. The empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show consistent speedups, with PDMM being the fastest method in both applications.

The theoretical guarantees (O(1/T) convergence) are important for practitioners, as they provide confidence in the method's reliability. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable theoretical insights that help understand the role of dual backward steps in ADMM variants. The paper's focus on practical implementation considerations (sparsity-aware step sizes, randomized block selection) makes it particularly relevant to practitioners.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with global convergence and O(1/T) complexity
- Novel dual backward step that addresses a known convergence issue in Jacobi updates
- Practical step size selection based on sparsity and block count
- Demonstrated empirical superiority on real-world problems
- Clear connections to and generalization of existing methods

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of parallel speedup and computational complexity
- The parameter tuning for τi, νi is somewhat ad hoc, though the authors provide a principled way to choose them
- The paper references a companion paper [25] for some details, which might make it slightly harder to follow without access to that work

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of convex optimization for machine learning. It addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The theoretical results are rigorous, the algorithm is novel, and the empirical evaluation is convincing. The paper meets all the NIPS criteria at a high level.

The paper's main contribution is the PDMM framework that unifies and extends existing ADMM variants for multiple blocks with a randomized parallel approach. The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that this approach can provide both theoretical guarantees and practical benefits.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a solid advance in the area of distributed optimization methods for machine learning, with clear significance for both theory and practice.
