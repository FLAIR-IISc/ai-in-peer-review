

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a gap in existing literature by developing a method that can randomly select *any number* of blocks to update in parallel (unlike RBSUMM which only updates one block, or sADMM/PJADMM which update all blocks). PDMM incorporates three key innovations: (1) a dual backward step to ensure convergence, (2) step size determination that accounts for the sparsity of the constraint matrix A, and (3) the ability to randomly update arbitrary numbers of blocks. The authors establish global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity and demonstrate the method's effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proofs are well-structured and build upon standard ADMM theory. The authors properly address the relationship between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM), showing they are special cases of PDMM. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines on two well-established machine learning problems. The authors are careful to note limitations (e.g., that updating only one block may require more iterations despite lower per-iteration cost), demonstrating intellectual honesty.

The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including precise algorithmic steps, theoretical conditions, and experimental parameters. The supplemental material appears to contain the necessary technical proofs. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, with PDMM consistently outperforming RBSUMM and often matching or exceeding the performance of sADMM and GSADMM.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and distinguishes the paper's contribution from prior work. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The algorithm description is precise, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The discussion of the dual backward step and how it addresses convergence issues with direct Jacobi updates is particularly clear and insightful.

The authors provide excellent context for their method by explaining how it relates to existing approaches (Section 2.2), which helps readers understand both the novelty and the theoretical significance. The experimental section is well-organized, with clear figures and appropriate performance metrics (iterations, runtime, residual).

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. While there has been work on ADMM variants for multiple blocks (Gauss-Seidel vs. Jacobian approaches), PDMM is novel in its ability to randomly select *any number* of blocks for parallel updates rather than being limited to updating one or all blocks. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides new theoretical insights into the role of the dual backward step, which is particularly valuable.

The paper distinguishes itself from RBSUMM by enabling arbitrary block selection (not just single-block updates) and from sADMM/PJADMM by providing a unified framework with principled step size determination. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly relevant, as the authors correctly note that RBCD struggles with overlapping blocks.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning: efficient distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm offers practical advantages for parallel computing environments, which is increasingly crucial as datasets grow larger. The experimental results demonstrate that PDMM can achieve faster runtime than sequential methods (GSADMM) despite running in parallel, which is a significant practical contribution.

The theoretical guarantees (O(1/T) convergence rate) match those of other ADMM variants, ensuring that the parallelism does not come at the cost of theoretical performance. The paper shows that for overlapping group lasso, PDMM achieves superior performance compared to existing methods, which is significant given the prevalence of overlapping group structures in modern machine learning applications.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel algorithm that generalizes existing Jacobian ADMM methods
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive experimental validation on two important applications
- Clear explanation of how PDMM relates to and improves upon existing methods
- Practical insights into step size selection based on problem structure

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could provide more detailed discussion about why RBCD struggles with overlapping groups while PDMM succeeds
- Some of the experimental comparisons (e.g., with RBSUMM) could be more thorough with additional parameter exploration
- The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed beyond the runtime measurements

## Recommendation

This paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for machine learning. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the practical significance is evident. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by providing a unified framework that encompasses existing methods while offering new capabilities (arbitrary block selection). The connections between PDMM and other methods provide valuable theoretical insights that will benefit the optimization community.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is of high quality, clearly written, sufficiently original, and addresses a significant problem with meaningful contributions. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Final Assessment

The paper is technically strong, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical results are properly supported by experiments, and the presentation is excellent. The authors have successfully addressed the limitations of prior work while maintaining the theoretical guarantees of existing methods. This paper represents a solid contribution to the optimization literature that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning.
