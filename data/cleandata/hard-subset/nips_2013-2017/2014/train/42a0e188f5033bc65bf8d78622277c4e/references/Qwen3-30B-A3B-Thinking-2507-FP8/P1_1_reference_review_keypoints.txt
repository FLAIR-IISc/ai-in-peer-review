- Proposes PDMM, a randomized block-coordinate ADMM that updates any number of blocks in parallel for block-separable convex problems with linear constraints.  
- Uses a dual backward step and step sizes chosen from constraint-matrix sparsity and number of updated blocks to ensure conservative, convergent updates.  
- Unifies existing methods by showing sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.  
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso where prior methods struggle or are inefficient.  

- Theoretical contribution: proves global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size.  
- Algorithmic novelty: dual backward step is a well-motivated fix for Jacobi-style divergence and step-size choices are theoretically justified.  
- Practical relevance: experiments show significant runtime improvements, especially for problems with overlapping structure.  
- Literature clarity: situates PDMM relative to prior ADMM and randomized block-coordinate work and provides useful theoretical unification.  
- Experimental design: comprehensive comparisons and analysis of how the number of blocks K affects performance.  

- Lacks comparison to some recent parallel multi-block ADMM and newer randomized ADMM variants, limiting assessment of novelty and performance.  
- Convergence relies on Assumption 1 (existence of a KKT point for ρ=0); paper should discuss how restrictive this is in practice.  
- Experimental details are insufficient (e.g., exact random block selection procedure, specific ρ values, how “best” results were chosen).  
- No detailed per-iteration computational complexity analysis to contextualize runtime/ K trade-offs.  
- Scalability to extremely large problems (e.g., m,n ~ 10^6) and communication costs are not addressed.  

- Quality: technically sound with rigorous proofs and experiments supporting claims.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, though some technical definitions (e.g., Bregman terms) could be clearer for non-experts.  
- Originality: novel in enabling arbitrary parallel block updates with constant step size and convergence guarantees; connects to and generalizes prior methods.  
- Significance: meaningful for optimization and large-scale ML, particularly for structured-sparsity problems; practical O(1/T) rate is valuable.  

- Recommendation: accept for NIPS.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel algorithm with rigorous guarantees, fills a key gap, shows practical runtime gains, and unifies existing methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited recent-method comparisons, missing experimental specifics, and potentially restrictive theoretical assumptions.
