

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a new algorithm called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. PDMM is framed as a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM that generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size, and demonstrate its performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems. The paper claims PDMM can randomly update arbitrary numbers of blocks in parallel, with step size parameters chosen based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear theoretical connection between existing ADMM variants (sADMM and PJADMM) and the proposed PDMM, showing how these methods are special cases of the more general framework. The theoretical analysis is technically sound and builds upon established convergence results for ADMM variants. The paper also addresses an interesting problem (overlapping group lasso) where previous randomized block coordinate methods have not been successfully applied.

## Weaknesses
Despite the theoretical contributions, this paper suffers from significant weaknesses in experimental validation and practical significance that undermine its claim of being a meaningful advancement over established methods.

1. **Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The paper only evaluates PDMM on two applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso), which is inadequate for a method claiming to be a general solution for block-separable convex optimization. The experimental section lacks thorough comparison with a wider range of baselines (e.g., standard ADMM with different step sizes, other randomized block coordinate methods) and doesn't include statistical significance testing of the results.

2. **Lack of Comprehensive Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares PDMM primarily with sADMM, RBSUMM, and GSADMM, but fails to show how PDMM compares to other established methods for the specific problem domains. For example, in RPCA, the paper doesn't compare with state-of-the-art methods like the one proposed by Candes et al. (2011) or other efficient solvers for this problem.

3. **Unconvincing Practical Benefits**: The paper claims PDMM is faster than existing methods, but the runtime improvements are modest (e.g., "PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm" without quantifying the speedup). The experimental results show that PDMM3 (updating 3 blocks) is faster than PDMM1 (updating 1 block), contradicting the paper's claim that "PDMM1 is the fastest algorithm." The paper also doesn't provide clear evidence that the theoretical O(1/T) convergence rate translates to meaningful practical improvements.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper claims to have a principled way to choose step sizes τi and νi, but the experiments show that the "best" results for PDMM require manual tuning of these parameters, which undermines the paper's claim of a theoretically justified step size selection. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how sensitive PDMM's performance is to these parameters.

5. **Lack of Parallel Speedup Analysis**: The paper claims PDMM can be used in parallel, but the experiments are run sequentially. There's no analysis of the parallel speedup, scalability with the number of processors, or communication overhead that would be critical for a truly parallel method.

6. **Theoretical Contribution vs. Practical Value**: The paper establishes convergence results that match the O(1/T) rate of existing methods, but doesn't demonstrate how PDMM achieves this with better constants or under weaker assumptions. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is interesting but doesn't provide substantial new theoretical insights.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an incremental extension to existing ADMM variants with some theoretical connections and modest experimental improvements. However, as a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes rigorous validation against established baselines, I find the paper's claims overstated. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that PDMM offers meaningful advantages over well-established methods in practical applications. The theoretical results are sound but not particularly novel, as they match the convergence rates of existing methods. The experimental validation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.

The paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments across multiple problem domains, a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and a clearer demonstration of how the theoretical improvements translate to practical speedups. Without these, the paper's contribution appears marginal compared to the extensive body of work on ADMM variants.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an incremental extension of existing ADMM variants with insufficient experimental validation against established baselines. The claimed advantages over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and the practical significance of the proposed method is questionable. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its novelty or importance for the NIPS community.
