

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces a novel parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The authors address the problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, which is common in machine learning applications. The key insight is that PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM while introducing a dual backward step that enables more aggressive dual updates when blocks are updated in parallel.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it proposes a unified framework for parallel ADMM variants by introducing a dual backward step that accounts for the sparsity structure of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks updated in parallel. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing work, as it provides a theoretical explanation for why certain step size choices work better than others. Second, the paper establishes the global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity of PDMM with constant step size, which is a notable theoretical contribution. Third, the authors demonstrate how PDMM can solve overlapping group lasso problems, which is a significant application since randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks was previously an open problem.

The paper's novelty is particularly strong. While previous work has focused on either Gauss-Seidel (cyclic) or Jacobi (parallel) variants of ADMM for multiple blocks, PDMM provides a continuous spectrum between these two extremes. The paper elegantly shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a deeper theoretical understanding of these methods. This connection alone would make the paper valuable, but the authors go further by establishing convergence guarantees and demonstrating practical advantages in experiments.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to the interplay between the sparsity of the constraint matrix, the number of blocks updated, and the step size choices. The paper correctly identifies the key issue with direct Jacobi updates (overly aggressive dual updates) and proposes a principled solution with the dual backward step.

In terms of experiments, the authors evaluate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso problems, comparing it with ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM. The results show that PDMM consistently outperforms the other methods, particularly in terms of runtime. The paper also demonstrates that PDMM can achieve significant speedups by updating multiple blocks in parallel, with the optimal number of blocks depending on the problem structure.

The main strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. It doesn't just propose a new algorithm; it provides a unified framework that connects existing methods and offers a deeper understanding of the underlying principles. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS values. The paper successfully bridges theory and practice, with clear theoretical insights that translate to practical performance improvements.

The minor weaknesses are that the experimental results are limited to two problems, and the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the performance on dense problems (though the theory covers this case). However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong conceptual contribution.

In terms of significance, PDMM addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization with real-world applications in machine learning. The parallel nature of the method makes it particularly relevant for modern distributed computing environments. The paper provides a new direction for parallelizing ADMM variants that could influence future work in optimization for machine learning.

Overall, this paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. It presents a novel algorithm that generalizes existing methods, provides a deeper theoretical understanding of the underlying principles, and demonstrates practical advantages in experiments. The paper's novelty and conceptual clarity outweigh any minor methodological limitations, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Strengths:**
- Conceptual novelty in unifying ADMM variants through the PDMM framework
- Clear theoretical insights about dual backward steps and step size selection
- Strong convergence guarantees with constant step size
- Practical performance improvements demonstrated on real problems
- Connection to open problems (overlapping group lasso)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental scope (only two problems)
- Minor theoretical analysis of dense problem cases
- Some details about the implementation could be more thorough

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field with its novel conceptual framework for parallel ADMM variants. The theoretical insights and practical advantages demonstrated in experiments make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The novelty and bold approach of the paper align perfectly with NIPS's interest in innovative algorithmic directions.
