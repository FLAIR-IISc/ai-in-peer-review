

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size. PDMM generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases. The paper demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

## Strengths
- The paper presents a well-motivated extension of ADMM that handles parallel randomized block updates.
- The theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and iteration complexity with constant step size, which is a significant contribution.
- The connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provide valuable insights into the dual backward step mechanism.
- The experimental results demonstrate PDMM's effectiveness on real-world problems compared to existing methods.

## Weaknesses and Critical Issues

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that significantly hinder readability and professional presentation:

1. **Mathematical notation**: Inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts (e.g., "xj" vs "x_j", "x t k" vs "x_t^k"), missing spaces in equations (e.g., "J∑ j=1" instead of "∑_{j=1}^J"), and inconsistent parentheses usage.

2. **Typos and grammatical errors**: "block-seperable" (p.1), "defiend" (p.8), "x t+1 jt" (p.5), "K̃i = min{di,K}" (p.5) where "K̃i" should be "K̃_i", "x t+1 j" (p.6), "Bφjt" (p.5) should be "B_{φ_{j_t}}".

3. **Equation formatting**: Inconsistent formatting of equations throughout the paper (e.g., equation (8) should be "τ_i = K/(K̃_i(2J-K))" rather than "K K̃i(2J−K)").

### Technical Concerns

1. **Lack of clear justification for step size selection**: The paper states τ_i and ν_i are chosen based on "sparsity of A and the number of randomized blocks" but provides no formal justification for the specific values in equation (8). The claim that "a low di will lead to bigger step sizes τ_i" is not mathematically justified.

2. **Inadequate explanation of dual backward step**: The paper attributes the failure of Jacobi updates to "implicit dual ascent" but does not provide a clear mathematical argument showing why the dual backward step (7) resolves this issue. The connection to sADMM (Section 2.2) is mentioned but not thoroughly explained.

3. **Unclear connection to RBCD**: The paper claims "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem" but does not clearly articulate how PDMM solves this problem or how it differs from RBCD.

4. **Incomplete experimental comparison**: The paper claims PDMM1 (updating one block) is the fastest algorithm but does not provide sufficient explanation for why this is the case. The comparison with RBSUMM is incomplete as the paper states "could not see the convergence of the objective within 5000 iterations" for RBSUMM but doesn't provide any results for it.

### Theoretical Concerns

1. **Convergence conditions**: The paper assumes "a KKT point of the Lagrangian (ρ = 0 in (2)) of Problem (1) exists" (Assumption 1), which is a strong condition. The paper should discuss how this assumption holds for the specific applications (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) and how it compares to assumptions in related work.

2. **Iteration complexity**: The paper claims "PDMM converges at the same rate as ADMM and its variants" but doesn't clearly explain how the specific step sizes in PDMM lead to this rate.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper proposes a theoretically interesting extension to ADMM with potential practical benefits, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, unclear explanations, and lack of rigorous justification for key design choices significantly undermine the paper's quality. The technical contributions are promising but are obscured by poor presentation and insufficient theoretical justification.

The paper contains multiple typos and formatting errors that make it difficult to follow the mathematical arguments. For example, the paper repeatedly uses inconsistent notation for the same concepts (e.g., "xj" vs "x_j"), which is unacceptable in a technical paper. The explanation of the dual backward step is particularly weak, with only heuristic arguments provided.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS, but the presentation issues are severe enough that the paper would not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS. The authors have made a good conceptual contribution but have not presented it in a clear, rigorous, and professional manner.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper contains a potentially interesting contribution to the optimization literature, the numerous formatting errors, inconsistent notation, and lack of clear theoretical justification for key design choices make it difficult to evaluate the technical merits of the work. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
