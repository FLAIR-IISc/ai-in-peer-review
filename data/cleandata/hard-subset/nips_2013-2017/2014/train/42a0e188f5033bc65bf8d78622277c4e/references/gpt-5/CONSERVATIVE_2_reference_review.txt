Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper considers linearly constrained convex optimization with block-separable objectives. It proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of ADMM that (i) performs primal block updates in parallel for a randomly chosen subset of blocks, (ii) introduces a “dual backward step” (a damping/correction on the dual update), and (iii) sets per-row dual stepsizes τi and backward parameters νi that depend on the sparsity pattern of A (via di) and the number of updated blocks K. An inexact variant uses Bregman proximal terms to linearize difficult components. The authors relate PDMM to existing two extremes: sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM), which they show are special cases under certain τi, νi and proximal choices. They provide global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard convexity assumptions, and report experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

The work connects to a broad literature on ADMM for multi-block problems: Gauss–Seidel multi-block ADMM (with back substitution), proximal Jacobian ADMM, randomized block methods (e.g., RBCD, RBSUMM), and over-relaxed/damped ADMM variants. The main claimed novelty is a principled, sparsity-aware choice of dual stepsizes and a unifying view that recovers sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.

Quality:
- Theory: The convergence statements (global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate) are in line with established ADMM results for convex problems. However, the manuscript leaves several key technical elements opaque: Pt and Q are only described as positive semidefinite without explicit construction; the choice of τi, νi is asserted with little intuition beyond heuristics; and the conditions for inexact updates (strength of ϕjt vs. Lipschitz constant of ψjt) are stated but not operationalized in terms of verifiable problem constants. The “dual backward step” is closely related to well-known damping/relaxation in ADMM; the theoretical novelty beyond existing proximal/damped Jacobi ADMM is limited.
- Empirics: Experiments are small to medium scale and run sequentially, despite the paper’s central claim being parallel efficiency. Several baselines are either missing or not implemented fairly (e.g., sADMM is reported with τ=0 due to public code rather than a proper implementation; other convergent multi-block Jacobian/proximal ADMM variants are not included). Hyperparameter tuning details are sparse (“best” results are reported without clear selection protocol). No sensitivity analysis for ρ, τi, νi (beyond a small illustration) nor ablation isolating the effect of the backward step.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable but suffers from numerous typographical issues, inconsistent notation, and missing details that would hinder reproducibility. Important definitions (Pt, Q, βi) and the precise stopping criterion (“residual”) are under-specified. Figures/tables referenced are not included here. The connection to relaxation/damping in classical ADMM could be explained more explicitly.

Originality:
The idea of stabilizing parallel Jacobi ADMM with proximal regularization and/or damping is established; PJADMM and related works already add proximal terms to ensure convergence with parallel updates. The “dual backward step” appears to be a restatement of dual damping/over-relaxation, and the unification with sADMM/PJADMM is interesting but incremental. The sparsity-aware stepsize formulas depending on di and K are a nice twist, though it is unclear how tight or practically impactful they are beyond being conservative bounds.

Significance:
If validated thoroughly, a parallel, randomized multi-block ADMM that is easy to tune would be useful. However, the current empirical evidence is limited and largely sequential; there is no demonstration on large-scale parallel hardware, nor comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., other convergent multi-block Jacobi/proximal ADMM, recent stochastic ADMM variants). The theoretical rate matches standard O(1/T) and does not improve the state of the art.

Strengths:
- Addresses a well-motivated instability of Jacobi multi-block ADMM via a principled damping scheme.
- Stepsizes account for sparsity (di) and randomization level (K), which is intuitively appealing.
- Unifying view that recovers sADMM and PJADMM clarifies relationships between methods.
- Provides convergence guarantees with constant stepsizes and an inexact framework via Bregman terms.
- Applications to overlapping group lasso are natural; the handling of overlaps in a randomized scheme is of interest.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental relative to proximal/damped Jacobi ADMM; the “backward step” closely resembles known relaxation.
- Theory lacks detail in key places (definitions of Pt, Q; verifiable conditions for inexact updates; derivation and tightness of τi, νi).
- Empirical evaluation is limited, sequential, and includes questionable baseline implementations; lacks large-scale parallel results and robust tuning protocols.
- No ablations convincingly isolate the contribution of the backward step vs. proximal terms vs. randomization.
- Writing and references need polishing; some claims (e.g., PJADMM o(1/T)) are stated without careful contextualization.

Questions/suggestions:
- Provide explicit constructions for Pt and Q, and a clearer derivation of τi, νi with bounds showing why these are safe and non-overly-conservative.
- Implement sADMM and other proximal Jacobian ADMM fairly with proper dual updates; include recent convergent multi-block parallel ADMM baselines.
- Show strong-scaling/weak-scaling results on multi-core/GPU/cluster to substantiate “parallel” claims.
- Add ablations: with/without backward step; varying K; sensitivity to ρ, τi, νi; effect of di-aware stepsizes vs. uniform stepsizes.
- Clarify stopping criteria; report primal/dual residuals as in standard ADMM diagnostics; release code.

Pros:
- Clear motivation; unifying perspective.
- Sparsity-aware stepsize scheme.
- Theoretical guarantees under standard assumptions.

Cons:
- Incremental novelty; close to established damped/proximal Jacobi ADMM.
- Insufficient and partly unfair empirical validation; no true parallel results.
- Missing technical details and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). With stronger, fair empirical validation on parallel hardware, clearer theory, and tighter positioning relative to existing damped/proximal Jacobian ADMM, this could be a solid contribution.
