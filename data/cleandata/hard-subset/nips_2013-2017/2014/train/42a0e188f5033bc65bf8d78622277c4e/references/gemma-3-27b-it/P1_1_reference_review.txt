## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate descent variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly select and update *K* blocks in parallel, combining the benefits of parallelization (like dual ascent) with the convergence properties of augmented Lagrangian methods. The authors highlight three key aspects of PDMM: a backward step on the dual update for conservative progress, consideration of sparsity in the constraint matrix *A* for step size selection, and the ability to update an arbitrary number of blocks concurrently. They establish connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, positioning PDMM as a unifying framework, and prove global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity. Empirical evaluation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates competitive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundation of ADMM and its variants. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of multi-block ADMM algorithms, differentiating itself from Gauss-Seidel approaches (GSADMM, BSUMM) and Jacobian variants (sADMM, PJADMM). The authors acknowledge the challenges of convergence with multiple blocks and address them through the proposed backward step and randomized updates.  The connection drawn to sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM is a valuable contribution, offering a more complete understanding of these methods and their underlying principles. The references to RBCD and its challenges with overlapping blocks are relevant and highlight the niche PDMM aims to fill.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM presents a genuinely new approach to multi-block ADMM, offering a flexible framework that combines the strengths of existing methods. The randomized block updates and adaptive step size based on sparsity are particularly innovative.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis, establishing global convergence and a reasonable iteration complexity. This is a significant strength, as convergence guarantees are often lacking in randomized optimization algorithms.
* **Unifying Framework:** Positioning sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM provides a valuable conceptual link and deeper understanding of these related algorithms.
* **Practical Considerations:** The incorporation of sparsity information into step size selection demonstrates an awareness of practical considerations for large-scale problems.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provide evidence of PDMM's competitive performance compared to established methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the algorithm and theoretical results. The notation, while dense, is consistently used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper briefly mentions tuning τi and νi, but lacks a detailed discussion of how to choose the optimal K, or how sensitive performance is to these parameters. A more in-depth analysis of parameter selection, perhaps with a small sensitivity study, would strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity Analysis Limitations:** While the O(1/T) complexity is encouraging, it doesn't explicitly account for the cost per iteration, which can be substantial for large-scale problems. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, considering the cost of parallel updates, would be beneficial.
* **Empirical Scope:**  The empirical evaluation is limited to two applications. While these are relevant, broader testing on a wider range of problem instances would increase confidence in the generalizability of PDMM.  The comparison with state-of-the-art methods appears limited, particularly in the overlapping group lasso experiment.  The comparison with PA-APG and S-APG seems to be only briefly mentioned.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks crucial implementation details.  Is the parallelization done with threads, MPI, or another approach?  This information is important for reproducibility.
* **Some notation can be improved:** The notation regarding blocks and indices is slightly cumbersome and could benefit from simplification.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for solving a widely applicable class of optimization problems.
* The convergence analysis and iteration complexity results are significant contributions.
* The unification of sADMM and PJADMM under the PDMM framework is insightful.
* The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate promising performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed parameter tuning guidelines and complexity analysis limits the practical impact of the work.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The implementation details are insufficient for reproducibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** with minor revisions.  The core idea of PDMM is novel and well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – specifically, providing more detailed parameter tuning guidelines, a more thorough complexity analysis, and expanding the empirical evaluation – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. The contribution is significant and aligns well with the scope of NIPS.  The paper advances the state-of-the-art in ADMM-based optimization methods and has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working on large-scale, block-separable convex optimization problems.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
