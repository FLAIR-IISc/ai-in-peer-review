**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel, randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The key contributions are threefold: 1) It introduces a "backward step" on the dual variable update to control its progress, which the authors argue is crucial for the convergence of parallel (Jacobian-style) updates. 2) The step sizes for the dual updates are dynamically determined by the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks (`K`) randomly selected for update at each iteration. 3) The algorithm allows for an arbitrary number of blocks (`K`) to be updated in parallel, from one to all blocks. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. They also claim that two existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, thus providing a unifying framework. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel contribution to the literature on ADMM-type methods. The core idea of adapting the dual update and backward step parameters (`τi`, `νi`) based on both the problem structure (sparsity `di`) and the algorithmic choice (number of parallel blocks `K`) is elegant and appears to be effective. This provides a principled way to set step sizes, moving beyond heuristics or overly conservative global bounds.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate in an ergodic sense under standard assumptions. This matches the known rates for ADMM and its variants and provides strong theoretical justification for the method. The use of a Lyapunov function to prove convergence is standard but properly executed.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates that PDMM is competitive with and often outperforms several state-of-the-art methods, including GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM. The results on the overlapping group lasso problem are particularly impressive, where PDMM significantly outperforms specialized solvers and other ADMM variants, highlighting the benefit of its sparsity-aware step sizes. The empirical study on the effect of `K` also offers valuable practical insights.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite its promising contributions, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to major problems with verifiability.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper's most critical flaw is its reliance on an external ArXiv preprint (`[25]`) for key theoretical claims. The connections to sADMM and PJADMM, which are presented as a central contribution for understanding the role of the dual backward step, are not proven in the manuscript or its supplement but are deferred to "Appendix B in [25]" and "Appendix C in [25]". This is unacceptable for a peer-reviewed conference submission. All claims must be verifiable from the submitted materials. Without these proofs, the claimed unification is unsubstantiated.

2.  **Clarity and Methodological Details:**
    *   There are numerous typographical errors and grammatical issues (e.g., "block-seperable" in the first sentence of the introduction, "In contast" in Section 2.2).
    *   The "Notations" section is placed awkwardly at the end of the introduction, making the initial text harder to parse.
    *   In Section 2.3, Remark 1(b) contains a speculative claim about the convergence of BSUMM ("BSUMM should converge... although the theory of BSUMM does not include this special case"). Such unsubstantiated statements should be avoided.
    *   The explanation of the inexact updates in Section 2.1, particularly the choice `φjt = ϕjt − 1/ηjt ψjt`, is dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation for the non-expert reader.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The comparison against `sADMM` in the RPCA experiment is confusing. The authors state that the public code for `sADMM` uses `τi=0` and is thus slow, and that `sADMM` *should* be the same as `PDMM3`. A fair comparison requires implementing the baseline algorithm as described in its original paper, not relying on a potentially flawed third-party implementation. This casts doubt on the validity of that specific comparison.
    *   A primary motivation for PDMM is its parallel nature, yet all experiments were conducted sequentially. While the runtime improvements over the sequential GSADMM are noted, the paper does not empirically validate the method's scalability or parallel speedup, which remains a claim.
    *   The presentation of results could be improved. The figures use generic axis labels like "Residual" without specifying which residual is being measured. The use of a raw URL as a footnote for experimental setup is unprofessional.

4.  **Formatting and Professionalism:** The manuscript lacks the level of polish expected for a top-tier conference. The combination of typos, awkward phrasing, unconventional structure (notation section), and unprofessional citations (raw URL) detracts from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core algorithmic idea of PDMM is novel and significant.
*   The method is supported by a solid O(1/T) convergence theory.
*   The empirical results, particularly for overlapping group lasso, are strong and demonstrate a clear advance over existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is not self-contained, with key proofs deferred to an external preprint, making a thorough review impossible.
*   The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish, including numerous typos, formatting issues, and unclear statements.
*   The experimental comparison with at least one key baseline (sADMM) is questionable.
*   The central claim of parallel performance is not empirically validated with parallel execution.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a promising and well-motivated algorithm with strong potential, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level required for NIPS. The lack of self-containment is a fatal flaw for the review process. Combined with the numerous minor issues in writing clarity, experimental methodology, and overall polish, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The authors have a solid foundation for an excellent paper, and I would encourage them to address the issues raised here—especially by incorporating all necessary proofs into the supplement—and resubmit to a future venue.
