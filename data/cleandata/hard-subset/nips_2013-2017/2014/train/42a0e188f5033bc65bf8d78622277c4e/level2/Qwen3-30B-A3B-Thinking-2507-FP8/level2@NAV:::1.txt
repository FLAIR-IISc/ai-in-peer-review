

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving block-separable convex optimization with linear constraints. The main contributions are: (1) a unified framework for parallel block updates with global convergence guarantees and O(1/T) iteration complexity; (2) a connection showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM; and (3) demonstration that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks in group lasso problems.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in multi-block ADMM extensions. The theoretical analysis is sound, establishing convergence under reasonable assumptions and providing a clear connection to existing methods. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's superiority over state-of-the-art methods (sADMM, RBSUMM, GSADMM) in both convergence speed and runtime, with the added benefit of parallelism. The paper correctly references key works in the field (ADMM, RBCD, group lasso) and establishes the novelty of PDMM through its unified framework and handling of overlapping blocks.

Strengths: The theoretical contribution is significant, providing the first randomized parallel block coordinate method for multi-block ADMM with constant step size convergence guarantees. The experimental validation is thorough, with appropriate baselines and multiple runs. The connection to existing methods clarifies the role of dual backward steps.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the sensitivity of performance to parameter choices (τi, νi) beyond the theoretical settings. The experimental section could better explain why PDMM1 (updating one block) outperforms others in some cases despite the parallelism advantage. While the paper acknowledges the trade-off between block count and computational cost, it lacks deeper analysis of this trade-off.

The work makes a meaningful contribution to optimization methods for machine learning, with clear practical utility for large-scale problems requiring parallel computation. The significance is high as it addresses a well-known challenge in multi-block optimization with applications to structured sparsity. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a compelling case for PDMM as a versatile and efficient optimization method.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and well-supported approach that advances the state of the art in distributed optimization. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the experimental validation is convincing. The paper would be a valuable addition to the conference program. I recommend acceptance.
