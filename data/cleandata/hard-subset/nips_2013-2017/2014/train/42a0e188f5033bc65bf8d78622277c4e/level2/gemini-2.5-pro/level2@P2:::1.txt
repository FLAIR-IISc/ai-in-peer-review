This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of multi-block convex optimization with linear constraints, a formulation that captures many problems in machine learning. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for the two-block case, its generalization to more than two blocks has proven difficult, with direct extensions often failing to converge or requiring restrictive assumptions. The authors propose a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which is a parallel, randomized block coordinate method designed to overcome these limitations.

The core ideas of PDMM are threefold. First, to counteract the aggressive dual updates that cause instability in naive parallel ADMM, it introduces a "dual backward step", which uses a conservative intermediate dual variable in the primal update step. Second, it proposes an intelligent step-size rule for both the main dual update and the backward step. These step sizes, `τi` and `νi`, cleverly incorporate the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated in parallel at each iteration. Third, the method is flexible, allowing for any number of blocks `K` (from 1 to J) to be randomly selected and updated in parallel.

The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. A key insight of the paper is showing that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, emerge as special cases of PDMM, which helps to unify and understand the role of the dual backward step. The practical utility of the method is demonstrated on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where PDMM is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known bottleneck in a widely used class of optimization algorithms. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel and its core components—the dual backward step and the adaptive, sparsity-aware step-size rule—are a significant contribution. This provides a principled and effective way to parallelize ADMM for the multi-block case, which is of great interest to both the optimization and machine learning communities.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The convergence analysis is a major strength. The paper provides a rigorous proof of global convergence and establishes an O(1/T) rate, which is the standard rate for this class of first-order methods. The analysis is clean and the result holds with a constant step size, which is practically desirable. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is elegant and provides valuable insight into the design of parallel ADMM algorithms.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the algorithm is clearly presented by first discussing the failure of direct Jacobi-style updates. The roles of the key parameters (`τi`, `νi`, `K`) are explained, and the connections to prior work are carefully articulated.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM. The comparison against relevant baselines like GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM on challenging problems like RPCA and overlapping group lasso shows that PDMM is not only theoretically sound but also practically superior in many cases. The ablation study on the effect of `K` (the number of parallel blocks) provides useful practical guidance. The successful application to overlapping group lasso, framed as an open problem for randomized block coordinate descent, is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Intuition on Step-Size Derivation:** While the choice for `τi` and `νi` in equation (8) is justified by the subsequent theoretical analysis, the paper could benefit from providing more intuition in the main text as to *why* these specific forms are chosen. For instance, explaining how this choice balances the progress from the `K` updated primal blocks with the dual update across all `I` dual blocks would strengthen the reader's understanding.

2.  **Parallelism in Experiments:** The main promise of PDMM is its parallel nature. However, the experiments were run sequentially, comparing methods based on single-core runtime. While this is acceptable for comparing iteration counts and per-iteration complexity, the paper's claims would be significantly bolstered by including even a small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine that demonstrates actual wall-clock speedup as `K` increases. This would provide direct evidence of the algorithm's practical parallel performance.

3.  **Clarity on Randomization Scheme:** In Section 2, the authors suggest a practical sampling scheme: "permutate the J blocks and choose K blocks cyclically." This is a form of random permutation sampling without replacement. However, the theoretical analysis appears to rely on an expectation taken over the random choice `Jt` at each step, which usually implies i.i.d. sampling with replacement. It would be good to clarify whether the theoretical guarantees hold for the cyclic sampling scheme used in the experiments. If they do, a brief note on this would be helpful; if not, the discrepancy should be acknowledged.

4.  **Minor Clarification on sADMM Connection:** In the RPCA experiment (J=3), the paper states that PDMM3 corresponds to `(τi, νi) = (1/3, 1/3)`. However, the discussion in Section 2.2 suggests that sADMM (which is a special case of PDMM with K=J) corresponds to `τi = 1/J = 1/3` and `νi = 1 - 1/J = 2/3`. This creates a slight confusion. Clarifying that the parameters used in the experiment are valid choices derived from the theory, even if they differ from the specific sADMM instantiation, would avoid this potential confusion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically-sound algorithm for a fundamental problem. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core contributions. The work is significant, original, and likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
