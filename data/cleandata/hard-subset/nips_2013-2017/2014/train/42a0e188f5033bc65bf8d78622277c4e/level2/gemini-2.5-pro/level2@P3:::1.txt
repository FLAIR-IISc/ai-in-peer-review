**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a block-separable convex function subject to linear constraints, a canonical form for many machine learning problems. The work focuses on extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting (more than two blocks), where direct generalizations are known to have convergence issues. The proposed method, Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), is a parallel randomized block coordinate method. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks and updates them in parallel.

The key technical contributions are a novel "dual backward step" to stabilize the algorithm, and a step-size selection rule that adapts to both the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated in parallel. The paper provides a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. Furthermore, the work shows that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, can be viewed as special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective. The method is also shown to be applicable to problems with overlapping variable groups, a known challenge for block coordinate methods. The effectiveness of PDMM is demonstrated empirically on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where it outperforms several state-of-the-art methods.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are significant, providing a full convergence and complexity analysis for a randomized, parallel multi-block ADMM variant. The assumptions required for the theory are standard in the ADMM literature. The claims of the paper are well-supported by both the theoretical analysis and the experimental results. The experiments are conducted on relevant, non-trivial problems and include comparisons to appropriate baselines, demonstrating the practical advantages of the proposed method.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides excellent context, clearly motivating the problem and situating the work within the existing literature on multi-block ADMM. The core ideas of PDMM, particularly the dual backward step and the adaptive step size, are explained effectively. The connection to related work is a strong point, clarifying how PDMM unifies and generalizes previous Jacobian-style ADMM methods.

For potential improvement, the paper could benefit from more intuition behind the specific formulation of the step sizes `τ_i` and `ν_i` in Equation (8). While the derivation is presumably in the supplement, a brief, high-level explanation in the main text of why this particular form is chosen and how it relates to the convergence proof would enhance the reader's understanding.

**Originality**

The work is original and makes several novel contributions. While building upon the well-established ADMM framework, the proposed PDMM algorithm is a new combination of randomized block selection, parallel updates, a dual backward step, and an adaptive step-size rule. This combination successfully addresses the known instability of direct parallel extensions of ADMM. The insight to connect problem structure (sparsity of `A`) and algorithmic choice (`K`) to the step size is a notable contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is also a valuable conceptual advance. Finally, demonstrating a convergent randomized block-coordinate approach for optimization with overlapping groups is a significant contribution, as this has been an open problem.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to have a substantial impact. The need for efficient, scalable, and parallelizable optimization algorithms is a central theme in modern machine learning. Multi-block ADMM is a powerful tool, but its practical use has been hampered by convergence issues and the sequential nature of Gauss-Seidel variants. PDMM provides a flexible, theoretically-grounded, and parallel-by-design algorithm that directly addresses these limitations. Practitioners and researchers working on large-scale optimization problems, particularly those with separable structure or overlapping regularizers, are likely to find this method highly useful. The work advances the state of the art in distributed and parallel optimization and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of developing a convergent parallel multi-block ADMM.
*   The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, flexible (tunable degree of parallelism), and theoretically well-founded with a complete convergence and rate analysis.
*   Provides a valuable conceptual unification of existing parallel ADMM methods, deepening the understanding of the field.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against relevant baselines on important machine learning tasks.
*   The application to overlapping group regularization solves a standing problem for block-coordinate methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   The choice of step-size parameters, while given by a formula, appears complex. The experiments suggest that further tuning beyond the formula can improve performance, which might slightly reduce the out-of-the-box utility of the proposed rule. This is a minor point, however, as providing a theoretically-sound default is already a strong contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in terms of algorithm design, theory, and practical application. It is a clear advance for large-scale optimization and is highly suitable for publication.
