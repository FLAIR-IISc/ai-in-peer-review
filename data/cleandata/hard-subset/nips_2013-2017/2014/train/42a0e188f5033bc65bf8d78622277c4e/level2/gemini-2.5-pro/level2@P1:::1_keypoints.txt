- Problem: Minimizing a block-separable convex function with linear constraints; multi-block ADMM extensions often suffer convergence issues or require restrictive assumptions.  
- Proposed method: PDMM — randomized parallel block-coordinate method that updates K randomly chosen blocks each iteration.  
- Key innovations: a "dual backward step" for conservative dual updates and adaptive primal/dual step sizes based on K, J, and constraint sparsity (d_i).  
- Theory: Global convergence with an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate using a constant step size.  
- Applicability: Naturally handles overlapping blocks where standard block coordinate descent fails.  
- Empirical results: PDMM outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art ADMM variants on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.  
- Strength — significance/originality: Tackles a important, challenging problem; PDMM is novel and unifies sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.  
- Strength — technical quality: Strong theoretical analysis and non-trivial convergence proof.  
- Strength — clarity: Well-written presentation; Section 2 effectively explains design choices.  
- Strength — practical relevance: Parallel design suits large-scale ML and overlapping-group problems; experiments are convincing.  
- Weakness — step-size intuition: Limited high-level explanation for the specific functional forms of τ_i and ν_i in the main text.  
- Weakness — parallelism experiments: Experiments were sequential and do not demonstrate actual multi-core/distributed wall-clock speedups.  
- Weakness — choosing K: Lacks practical guidance on selecting K and discussion of communication/synchronization trade-offs.  
- Weakness — baseline comparison: sADMM baseline used a non-standard τ_i=0 implementation; comparing to a theory-consistent sADMM (e.g., τ_i=1/J) would be fairer.  
- Overall: Strong contribution with solid theory and empirical support; recommend acceptance.
