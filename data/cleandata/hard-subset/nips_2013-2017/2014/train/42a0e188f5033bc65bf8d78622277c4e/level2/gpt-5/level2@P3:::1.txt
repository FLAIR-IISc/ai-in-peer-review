Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) objectives subject to linear constraints, a setting widely addressed by augmented Lagrangian and ADMM-type methods. While two-block ADMM is well understood [2], multi-block extensions can diverge [7] or require stringent conditions [14]. The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant that updates K primal blocks per iteration and all dual blocks with a “dual backward step.” Step sizes τi and νi are chosen as functions of the number of updated blocks K and the sparsity profile di of the constraint matrix A. The paper establishes global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions, for both exact and inexact (Bregman-linearized) primal updates. It also shows that (i) two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) [17,26] and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) [10] are special (extreme) cases of PDMM, and (ii) PDMM naturally handles overlapping groups by introducing equality constraints, achieving favorable step sizes when di = 2. Empirical comparisons on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate improved convergence (iterations and/or time) versus GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM [14], with a study of the effect of K.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Identifies the source of instability in parallel Jacobi ADMM as implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty, and counteracts it via an explicit dual backward step. This is a clear, useful insight.
- Unifying view: Derives sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases of PDMM (small vs large proximal/Bregman regularization), clarifying how primal progress and dual step sizes should be coupled.
- Step-size design informed by sparsity: Dependence of τi and νi on di and K is principled and leads to larger, safe dual steps for sparse A; this is particularly appealing for overlapping group models where di = 2.
- Theory: Global convergence with constant step sizes and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard assumptions, for both exact and Bregman-linearized updates. The potential function and residual definitions are explicit.
- Practicality: The randomized parallel block updates can exploit parallel hardware. The paper provides guidance on choosing K and discusses group sampling.
- Empirical evidence: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants are competitive or superior in both iterations and runtime, and the study of K gives actionable guidance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Essential
- Empirical fairness and scope: All experiments are sequential Matlab runs. Since PDMM’s primary appeal is parallelism, at least a modest multi-core experiment (e.g., OpenMP/MEX or thread-based BLAS) would substantiate the claimed advantage. Reporting speedups versus number of threads for different K would strengthen the case.
- Baseline implementation details: The sADMM code used lacks a dual update (τi = 0), making the comparison unfavorable. A corrected variant with τi = 1/J (as discussed) should be implemented; otherwise, sADMM results should be labeled as ablated. Similarly, PJADMM [10] with tuned proximal weights (yielding o(1/T)) should be included for completeness.
- Sampling scheme clarity: The analysis assumes an expectation over Jt. Specify whether sampling is with or without replacement, and whether the “grouped cyclic” variant is covered by the theory or only the i.i.d. random subset case. If the grouped/cyclic version is used in experiments, clarify theoretical guarantees for that variant.

Optional but valuable
- Parameter sensitivity and tuning: Provide guidance on choosing ρ and ηjt in practice. The theory gives admissible τi, νi, but performance may vary with ρ and the choice of Bregman generators; include a brief sensitivity analysis and default recommendations.
- Complexity per iteration: Include a breakdown of per-iteration costs for PDMM versus GSADMM/sADMM/PJADMM (e.g., number of proximal evaluations, matrix-vector multiplications, and synchronization for Ax). This helps interpret time vs iteration curves.
- Non-ergodic behavior: The paper defines a residual R and proves ergodic rates; reporting last-iterate convergence behavior empirically would be informative.
- Relation to primal-dual methods: Briefly position the dual backward step relative to primal-dual hybrid gradient/Chambolle–Pock and extragradient techniques, to aid readers familiar with those frameworks.

Quality
- The algorithmic design is sound, the proofs appear careful, and the assumptions align with prior ADMM analyses [2,22]. The inexact/Bregman update framework is standard [23], and the dependence of τi, νi on K and di is well-motivated.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with helpful notation. The paper would benefit from a concise algorithm box summarizing PDMM (sampling, primal update with/without Bregman term, dual ascent, dual backward step) and a short table mapping special cases (PDMM, sADMM, PJADMM) to τi, νi, ηjt.

Originality
- The specific combination of randomized parallel block updates, dual backward step with analyzable step sizes depending on sparsity, and a unifying view of sADMM/PJADMM appears novel relative to [10,14,17,26].

Significance
- Addresses an active and important area where naive multi-block ADMM may diverge. The design principles and guarantees are likely to influence practice for structured problems, especially overlapping group models and sparse constraints.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear insight into why parallel Jacobi ADMM fails and how to fix it.
- Principled, analyzable step-size scheme exploiting sparsity.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with constant step sizes.
- Unifies and clarifies relations among prominent ADMM variants.
- Strong empirical performance and practical guidance on K.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of parallel implementations in experiments weakens claims about scalability.
- Comparisons to sADMM/PJADMM could be more comprehensive/fair.
- Some practical tuning details (ρ, Bregman choices) are not fully elaborated.

Minor comments
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “seperable” → “separable”, “convegence” → “convergence,” “precesion” → “precision”).
- Ensure all references have complete bibliographic information.
- In Section 2.3, clarify the definition of U and dimensions to avoid confusion.
