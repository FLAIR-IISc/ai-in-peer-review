

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel extension of ADMM for multi-block convex optimization with linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by developing a parallel randomized block coordinate method that updates multiple blocks simultaneously, unlike existing methods that typically use cyclic (Gauss-Seidel) or single-block updates. PDMM introduces a dual backward step to ensure convergence, carefully tunes step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomly selected blocks, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity for constant step sizes.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a unified framework that connects existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM, offering valuable theoretical insights into their relationships. Second, it demonstrates how PDMM can handle overlapping groups in optimization problems, a case where standard block coordinate methods struggle. Third, the theoretical results are well-developed, with careful analysis of the convergence properties and step size selection. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of runtime efficiency.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection to prior work. The authors properly situate their contribution within the broader context of ADMM variants, citing relevant literature from NIPS and other venues. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental design is sound, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. While the authors claim PDMM "can do randomized block coordinate descent on overlapping blocks," they provide only empirical evidence for this claim, without theoretical guarantees for the overlapping case. The connection to the recently proposed "Randomized Block Coordinate ADMM" (RBCADMM) [1] is not adequately discussed, and it's unclear how PDMM differs from this method in theory or practice. The experimental section could be strengthened by including more recent benchmarks and a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond those cited.

The theoretical results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the dual backward step (νi) is necessary. The motivation provided in Section 2 is somewhat technical, and a more intuitive explanation would help readers understand this key innovation. Additionally, the paper mentions that "PDMM can also be used to solve overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion" but doesn't provide a formal convergence guarantee for this specific case, which is a significant omission given the importance of overlapping structures in modern machine learning.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in large-scale optimization. The ability to parallelize multi-block optimization while maintaining convergence guarantees is valuable for distributed computing environments. The paper's practical results demonstrate real-world applicability, particularly for problems like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso that are increasingly important in machine learning.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and practically effective method for multi-block optimization. The main strengths are the elegant unification of existing methods, the clear convergence guarantees, and the strong empirical results. The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical guarantees for the overlapping groups case, insufficient comparison with very recent methods, and some technical details that could be clarified for better accessibility.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing theoretical guarantees for the overlapping groups case
2. More thoroughly comparing with the most recent methods in the field
3. Clarifying the necessity of the dual backward step with more intuitive explanations
4. Including a more detailed discussion of how PDMM differs from RBCADMM

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature. The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical findings are convincing. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper represents a significant step forward in multi-block optimization methods and deserves consideration for acceptance at NIPS.
