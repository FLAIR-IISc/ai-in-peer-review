This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address the challenges of extending the well-studied two-block ADMM to multi-block scenarios. PDMM introduces a randomized block coordinate update strategy coupled with a backward step on the dual update, aiming to improve convergence and parallelism. The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrates empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant problem in optimization, particularly relevant to machine learning. The connection to existing work, especially ADMM and its variants (GSADMM, sADMM, PJADMM), is thoroughly discussed, and the authors successfully position PDMM within this landscape.  The theoretical contributions – convergence analysis and iteration complexity – are substantial. The inclusion of connections to other methods, showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, is valuable for understanding the method's properties.  The experimental evaluation on two practical problems is a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues, particularly in the notation and flow of the technical arguments. The notation is dense and could benefit from clearer definitions and more consistent use. For example, the meaning of indices (i, j, k) and sets (Jt, N(i)) is sometimes difficult to track. While the authors attempt to explain the intuition behind the dual backward step and the choice of step sizes, the explanation could be more accessible to a broader audience.  The discussion relating the sparsity of A to the step size selection is valuable but could be expanded; it’s not immediately clear *how* sparsity directly impacts the optimal step size.

A significant weakness is the lack of a clear comparison to highly optimized, state-of-the-art solvers. While the paper compares to ADMM, sADMM and RBSUMM, mentioning PA-APG and S-APG, and then only briefly presenting results, feels incomplete. A more detailed comparison with these established methods would strengthen the claim of improved performance.  The empirical section feels somewhat fragmented. The reasons for choosing specific parameter settings aren’t always clearly justified, and there’s limited analysis of the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices.  The results on RPCA are presented as figures, but lack accompanying tables with quantitative data, making detailed comparisons more difficult.  The claim that "PDMM can do randomized block coordinate descent on overlapping blocks" is somewhat overstated. While the application demonstrates *an* overlapping problem, it doesn’t fundamentally address the challenges of overlapping blocks in the general case beyond applying the algorithm.

Originality: The idea of combining randomized block coordinate descent with ADMM and incorporating a backward step on the dual update is novel. While the individual components (randomized coordinate descent, ADMM) are known, their combination, and the theoretical analysis, constitute a significant contribution.

Significance: The paper addresses a core optimization problem with widespread applications. If the method proves robust and scalable in practice, it has the potential to impact areas like RPCA, sparse recovery, and machine learning with structural sparsity. The theoretical guarantees and the insights into the interplay between the dual backward step and sparsity are valuable contributions to the field. The demonstrated performance, while needing more rigorous comparison, is encouraging.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques (randomized block coordinate descent, ADMM, backward step).
*   Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees and iteration complexity.
*   Demonstrated performance on practical problems (RPCA, overlapping group lasso).
*   Connections to existing methods, providing insights into their relationships.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity issues in notation and technical explanations.
*   Limited comparison to highly optimized, state-of-the-art solvers.
*   Insufficient quantitative analysis in the experimental section.
*   Overstated claim regarding overlapping blocks beyond the demonstrated application.



**Recommendations:**

*   **Improve Clarity:** Revise the notation and provide more detailed explanations of the key ideas, particularly the dual backward step and the effect of sparsity.
*   **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:**  Include a more thorough comparison with established solvers (e.g., PA-APG, S-APG).  Provide quantitative results alongside the figures. Analyze the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices.
*   **Refine Discussion of Overlapping Blocks:** Clarify the specific advantages of PDMM for overlapping block problems beyond the demonstrated application.
*   **Minor Edits:** Proofread carefully for typos and grammatical errors.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a potentially significant contribution. Addressing the clarity and empirical evaluation concerns would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
