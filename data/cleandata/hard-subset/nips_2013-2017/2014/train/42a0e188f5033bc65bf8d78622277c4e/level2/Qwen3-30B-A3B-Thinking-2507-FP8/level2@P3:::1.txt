

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The work addresses a gap in the literature regarding effective generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks, which has been poorly understood despite preliminary work. PDMM updates blocks in parallel using a randomized block coordinate approach, establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates connections to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM as special cases). The paper shows PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions. The convergence results are particularly valuable as they provide guarantees for a parallel method that can handle large-scale problems, which is lacking in much of the existing literature on multi-block ADMM variants. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) provides important theoretical insights into the role of dual backward steps in these algorithms.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and distinguishes the proposed method from prior work. The notation is consistent throughout, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail to understand the contributions. The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate baselines (ADMM, GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) and two meaningful applications that demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by proposing a parallel randomized block coordinate method that updates multiple blocks simultaneously, addressing a limitation of RBSUMM which only updates one block. The paper demonstrates that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks in a way that is not possible with standard randomized block coordinate descent, which is an important practical consideration for many machine learning applications. The theoretical analysis of how sparsity and the number of blocks affect step size selection is novel and valuable.

The significance of this work is evident in both the theoretical advances and practical performance. The method provides a more efficient solution for large-scale optimization problems with multi-block structure, which is increasingly important as machine learning applications grow in scale. The experimental results consistently show PDMM outperforming existing methods in terms of runtime while achieving comparable or better accuracy, which demonstrates practical relevance.

Some minor improvements could be made to enhance the paper. The discussion of the sparsity of A in Section 2.2 could be strengthened with a concrete example of how the step size selection affects performance in a specific application. While the paper mentions that PDMM can be used for overlapping groups, a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the overlapping case compared to the non-overlapping case would be valuable. Additionally, the paper could more explicitly address the trade-off between parallelism (number of blocks updated simultaneously) and the cost per iteration, particularly in the context of distributed computing environments.

The paper avoids the common pitfalls of superficial reviews by engaging deeply with the technical content. The claims about convergence and complexity are well-supported by theoretical results, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The comparison with existing methods is fair and well-justified, with appropriate baselines included.

The paper is clearly within scope for NIPS, addressing a fundamental optimization problem with important applications in machine learning. The theoretical contribution is significant, and the practical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for machine learning. The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated on important applications. The work advances the state of the art in multi-block optimization and provides practical benefits for large-scale problems. The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the value of the proposed method.
