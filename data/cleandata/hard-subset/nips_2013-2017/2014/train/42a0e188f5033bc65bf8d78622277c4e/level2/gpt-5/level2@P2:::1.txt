Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses convex optimization with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of ADMM that updates K blocks of primal variables in parallel per iteration and performs a “dual backward step” (damped dual update). The key ideas are: (i) replacing the standard Jacobi primal updates with updates evaluated at a “backstepped” dual ŷt to mitigate the well-known instability of naive parallel multi-block ADMM; (ii) choosing dual step sizes τi and backward parameters νi as explicit functions of the per-row sparsity di of A and the number of updated blocks K; (iii) allowing inexact updates via Bregman proximalization, which unifies exact PDMM and proximal-Jacobian ADMM. The paper proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard convexity assumptions, and shows that sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) and PJADMM are special cases at the extremes of the parameterization. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate favorable performance.

This work sits in the line of research on multi-block ADMM, where Gauss–Seidel variants (e.g., BSUMM) need strong assumptions or small/decreasing steps and may lack known iteration complexity, and Jacobian/proximal variants (e.g., PJADMM) add proximal terms to guarantee convergence. RBSUMM allows randomized single-block updates but requires local error bounds and decreasing steps with no rate. PDMM contributes a Jacobi-style, randomized, multi-block algorithm with constant step sizes, leveraging problem sparsity to select τi and νi, and a clean O(1/T) rate.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper defines an appropriate Lyapunov/potential h(v*, vt) and shows monotone decrease in expectation, yielding global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant steps, matching best-known rates for convex ADMM-like methods. The role of the dual backward step is well-motivated via the “implicit dual ascent” viewpoint. The parameter choices τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i reflect both sparsity (di) and parallelism (K) and recover sensible limits (e.g., νi → 0 when K=1). The inexact/Bregman framework is standard and well-justified.
- Empirics: The experiments are on two representative tasks, including a setting with overlapping groups where PDMM’s sparsity-aware stepsizes give an advantage. Results indicate faster convergence than sADMM and RBSUMM, and competitive runtime with (sequential) GSADMM while offering inherent parallelism.

Some technical aspects could be clearer. The paper introduces matrices Pt and Q in the potential but defers their exact constructions to the supplement; the reader would benefit from at least a summary of what determines them and how they relate to step-size choices. Assumptions are standard (existence of KKT point), but it would be helpful to spell out any requirements on A (e.g., rank conditions) that are implicitly used to ensure bounded dual sequences or to define di meaningfully. The analysis is in expectation; a high-probability or almost-sure statement would further strengthen the guarantees, though is not strictly required.

Clarity:
The high-level narrative is clear and the motivation for the dual backward step is compelling. The presentation is dense; including a pseudocode box for PDMM, a concise table mapping PDMM parameter choices to sADMM/PJADMM, and a short proof sketch of the main descent inequality would improve readability. Some notation (Pt, Q, γi, βi) could be consolidated and briefly interpreted in the main text.

Originality:
Introducing a dual backward step calibrated by sparsity and the number of randomized blocks, together with a unified inexact framework, is a novel and useful design for multi-block ADMM. Establishing O(1/T) rates with constant steps for randomized multi-block updates that can handle overlapping structures is a meaningful theoretical and algorithmic contribution beyond prior RBSUMM/PJADMM/sADMM work.

Significance:
Multi-block ADMM is widely used, and instability of naive Jacobi updates is a practical pain point. PDMM provides a principled way to run parallel randomized updates with convergence and rate guarantees, and the sparsity-aware step sizes can materially improve performance in structured problems (e.g., overlapping group lasso). The connections to sADMM and PJADMM clarify the landscape and may influence algorithm selection in practice.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contribution: global convergence and O(1/T) rate with constant steps for randomized multi-block ADMM with overlapping blocks.
- Clear algorithmic innovation: dual backward step plus sparsity- and K-dependent step sizes.
- Unifying view showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.
- Empirical evidence on two representative applications, with sensible ablations on K.

Cons and suggestions:
- Fairness and completeness of experiments: The sADMM baseline appears to use a public implementation that omits the dual update (τi=0); this is a serious fairness issue. Please reimplement sADMM with proper dual updates, and also include PJADMM with tuned proximal parameters as it is a direct comparator. Consider larger-scale datasets and report parallel wall-clock on multi-core/multi-node systems to substantiate the “parallel” advantage.
- Parameter sensitivity: While the recommended τi, νi formulas are attractive, provide sensitivity analyses across ρ, τi, νi and K for both problems. An ablation that turns off the backward step (νi=0) while keeping other components fixed would isolate its effect.
- Practical guidance: Offer heuristics for choosing K in practice given computation/communication costs, and discuss nonuniform sampling (e.g., sampling blocks proportional to di) and how τi, νi adapt.
- Theory exposition: Summarize the definitions and roles of Pt and Q; state explicitly any assumptions on A (e.g., di≥1 for all rows, feasibility). If possible, add a brief discussion on almost-sure convergence and whether stronger rates are obtainable under strong convexity.
- Overlapping blocks: Provide more details on the construction for overlapping group lasso (memory overhead of splitting, effect on di) and any proximal choices used for efficient updates.

Minor comments:
- Fix small typos (e.g., “seperable”→“separable”, “defiend”→“defined”, “convegence”).
- Clarify the remark comparing PDMM (τi=0.5) vs sADMM in the overlapping case; the inequality signs appear inconsistent.
- Add pseudocode and a table of parameter choices for common scenarios.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a substantive contribution to randomized, parallel multi-block ADMM with sound theory and promising empirical results. Addressing the fairness of baselines and strengthening the experimental section would further improve the paper.
