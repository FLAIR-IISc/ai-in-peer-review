This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a block-separable convex function subject to linear constraints. This is a canonical problem formulation in machine learning and optimization. The authors note that while the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for two-block problems, its generalization to the multi-block case is not straightforward, with many direct extensions suffering from convergence issues or requiring restrictive assumptions.

To address this, the paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate method. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks and updates them in parallel. The key innovations are twofold: first, a "dual backward step" is introduced to make the dual variable updates more conservative, which helps stabilize the algorithm. Second, the step sizes for the primal and dual updates are not fixed but are adaptively chosen based on the number of blocks selected (`K`), the total number of blocks (`J`), and the sparsity structure of the constraint matrix `A`. The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size. The paper also shows how PDMM can naturally handle problems with overlapping blocks, a setting where standard block coordinate descent is not applicable. Finally, the authors demonstrate empirically that PDMM outperforms or is competitive with several state-of-the-art ADMM variants on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known, important, and challenging problem: the convergence of multi-block ADMM. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel and elegant. The core idea of using a dual backward step, with step sizes that intelligently adapt to the problem structure (`d_i`) and the degree of parallelism (`K`), is a significant contribution. The unification of prior methods like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM provides valuable insight into the landscape of parallel ADMM algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong. Proving global convergence and establishing an O(1/T) rate for a randomized, parallel multi-block method with a constant step size is a non-trivial achievement. The analysis appears sound and relies on standard assumptions for this class of problems.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the algorithm is presented in a structured manner. The discussion in Section 2, which breaks down the roles of the dual backward step, sparsity, and randomization, is particularly helpful for understanding the method's design.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithm is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning. Its parallel nature makes it suitable for modern computing environments. The demonstration on overlapping group lasso is a compelling use case that highlights the flexibility of the proposed framework over methods that require strict block-separability. The experimental results are convincing and show clear improvements over relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Intuition for Step Sizes:** While the step size rules in equation (8) are central to the algorithm's convergence guarantees, the paper provides limited intuition for their specific functional form in the main text. A brief, high-level explanation of why these particular settings for `τ_i` and `ν_i` balance the primal and dual updates to ensure convergence would further improve the paper's clarity.

2.  **Parallelism in Experiments:** A key motivation for PDMM is its parallel nature. However, the experiments were conducted sequentially. While runtime comparisons on a single core can suggest the potential for speedups (by showing that cheaper, more numerous iterations can be faster overall), they do not demonstrate the true benefits of parallelism. An experiment on a multi-core machine or a distributed system showing actual wall-clock time speedups as the number of parallel workers increases would make the paper's practical claims much more compelling.

3.  **Guidance on Choosing K:** The number of blocks to update in parallel, `K`, is a new hyperparameter introduced by the method. The experiments on overlapping group lasso (Figure 2, right) nicely illustrate the effect of `K` on performance. However, the paper could be improved by providing some practical guidance or discussion on how to select `K`. This choice likely involves a trade-off between the convergence rate (which seems to improve with `K`) and system-level costs like communication and synchronization, which are not captured in the current experiments.

4.  **Comparison to Baselines:** In the RPCA experiment, the results for sADMM seem unusually slow. The authors note that the public code for sADMM they used had `τ_i = 0`. This seems to be a non-standard implementation, as sADMM is essentially a special case of PDMM with a specific non-zero `τ_i`. Comparing against a version of sADMM that more closely matches the theory (i.e., with `τ_i = 1/J`) would provide a fairer and more informative comparison.

Despite these minor points, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed method is novel, the theory is solid, and the results are significant. It addresses a long-standing challenge in a principled and effective way. I recommend acceptance.
