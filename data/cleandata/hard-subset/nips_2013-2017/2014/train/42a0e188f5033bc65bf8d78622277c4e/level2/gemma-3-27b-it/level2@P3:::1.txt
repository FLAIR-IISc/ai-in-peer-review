This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, generalizing the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks. The core idea is to randomly update blocks in parallel, drawing inspiration from randomized block coordinate descent. The paper establishes convergence guarantees and demonstrates improved performance in RPCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

The work addresses a significant challenge in extending ADMM to multi-block problems, where convergence is not always guaranteed with naive approaches like Gauss-Seidel updates. The proposed PDMM method, with its randomized block updates and backward step on the dual update, offers a promising alternative. The theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, is a strong point. Connecting PDMM to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM provides a valuable understanding of the landscape of multi-block ADMM variants. The experimental results, while limited to two applications, support the claims of improved performance.

However, several areas could be strengthened. While the paper explains the motivation for the backward step and the sparsity considerations, a more intuitive explanation of *why* randomized block updates improve convergence would be beneficial. The assumption that a KKT point exists is standard, but its implications for practical application, particularly when dealing with ill-conditioned problems, could be discussed. The parameter tuning process for the experiments is not fully detailed; specifying the method used to select ρ and other relevant parameters would enhance reproducibility.  The connection between the step size parameters τi and νi and the properties of the problem (sparsity, block size) is discussed but could be further explored.  For example, a more thorough analysis of how the optimal K varies with problem size and structure would be valuable.  The computational cost analysis is missing and would be helpful to understand the advantages/disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to other methods.  The empirical evaluation is limited – a wider range of datasets and comparisons with more state-of-the-art solvers would bolster the claims of improved performance.  Specifically, comparing PDMM to more recent and optimized solvers for RPCA and overlapping group lasso is important. The statement in the conclusion that PDMM is "faster than existing methods" seems strong given the limited experimental results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to multi-block ADMM with theoretical guarantees.
*   Clear connection to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) offering new insights.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating improved performance.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed parameter tuning information.
*   Missing computational cost analysis.
*   Intuition behind the randomized updates could be strengthened.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of optimization, offering a new and theoretically sound method for solving multi-block convex problems. While the experimental evaluation is limited, the theoretical results and connection to existing work are compelling.  Addressing the identified weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper.  The paper is currently borderline; acceptance is contingent on addressing the review comments.  Specifically, extending the experimental evaluation and providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity are crucial.
