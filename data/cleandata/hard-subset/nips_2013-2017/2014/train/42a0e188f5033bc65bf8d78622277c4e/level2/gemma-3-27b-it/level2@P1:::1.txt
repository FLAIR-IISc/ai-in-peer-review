This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints, termed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). The authors address the challenges of extending the well-studied Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, where convergence is not always guaranteed. PDMM randomly updates blocks in parallel, offering a potential speedup. The paper provides convergence analysis and demonstrates empirical performance on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in applying ADMM to problems with many blocks. The introduction effectively outlines the problem and positions PDMM within the existing literature, clearly highlighting the limitations of previous approaches (GSADMM, Jacobian variants). The proposed method appears intuitively reasonable, and the inclusion of a backward step on the dual update is a key contribution, attempting to address the convergence issues of parallel Jacobi-type updates.  The connections drawn to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) are valuable, providing a better understanding of the parameter choices and the role of the backward step.  The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, is a strong point. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are a good starting point, demonstrating the performance of PDMM compared to state-of-the-art methods.

However, the paper has some weaknesses.  The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly with the numerous indices and block definitions. While the authors attempt to explain the rationale behind the step size choices (τi, νi), the explanation feels somewhat hand-wavy and could be more intuitive. The reliance on existing work for implementation details (e.g., RBSUMM) means reproducibility isn’t fully guaranteed. While the experiments are encouraging, the comparison to existing methods is not always comprehensive. For example, the GSADMM implementation seems to be a standard one, but the parameter tuning details aren’t clearly explained.  The discussion around the effects of K (number of randomly selected blocks) in the overlapping group lasso experiment is intriguing, but the explanation of why the runtime decreases after K=61 is missing.  The reference to a pre-print [25] for extended work is not ideal; key details should be included in the main paper or the supplementary material. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of PDMM. Under what conditions might PDMM *not* perform well? Are there specific problem structures that would make it unsuitable?

**Strengths:**

*   Technically sound, with convergence guarantees and iteration complexity analysis.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.
*   Provides a novel approach (PDMM) with a clear motivation and connection to existing work.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Dense and sometimes difficult-to-follow notation.
*   Explanation of step size choices could be more intuitive.
*   Lack of comprehensive comparison to all relevant methods.
*   Reliance on a pre-print for key details.
*   Limited discussion of limitations.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for solving a challenging optimization problem.
*   The convergence analysis provides strong guarantees for the method's performance.
*   The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of PDMM.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The notation and presentation could be improved for clarity.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The reliance on a pre-print raises concerns about reproducibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, addressing a significant challenge with a novel and theoretically sound approach. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (improving notation, providing more detailed experimental comparisons, and including key details from the pre-print) would significantly strengthen the paper. The potential impact of PDMM on applications involving large-scale, block-separable optimization problems is substantial.
