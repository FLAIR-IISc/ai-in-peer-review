Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, Jacobi-style primal-dual algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. PDMM updates an arbitrary number K of primal blocks in parallel and introduces a dual “backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian. Step sizes depend on the sparsity pattern of A (via di) and on K. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity under standard convex assumptions, unify existing multi-block ADMM variants by showing sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases, and demonstrate applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso. This work connects to a substantial literature on ADMM for multi-block problems (e.g., non-convergence results of Chen et al.; sADMM, PJADMM; BSUMM), and to randomized block coordinate descent (Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč).

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated algorithmic innovation (dual backward step and sparsity/K-aware step sizes) addressing known instability of Jacobi multi-block ADMM.
- Solid theory: global convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant step sizes; conditions align with ADMM standards; insightful role of τi, νi.
- Unifying perspective that clarifies when small/large dual steps are appropriate; ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized fashion.
- Empirical results on two representative applications show promising improvements over sADMM and RBSUMM, and competitive performance versus GSADMM.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Empirics are limited: Matlab, sequential implementation, mostly synthetic data; no true parallel/distributed experiments to validate the primary appeal of PDMM (parallel updates) or communication/synchronization costs.
- Baselines: PJADMM is discussed theoretically but not compared empirically; other modern primal-dual baselines (e.g., linearized ADMM, Chambolle–Pock variants) are absent.
- Practical guidance for choosing K and mapping sparsity to τi, νi could be expanded; constants (e.g., Q, Pt) and tuning implications are opaque for practitioners.
- Some presentation issues (typos, dense notation) and missing algorithmic pseudocode impede reproducibility despite a full supplement.

Quality: The theory appears technically sound and carefully positioned; experimental support is positive but not comprehensive. Clarity: Generally good but could be improved with pseudocode and a parameter-selection section. Originality: High—novel step-size design and unification of sADMM/PJADMM. Significance: Potentially strong for large-scale constrained ML, but current empirical scope underplays impact.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add parallel/multi-core and distributed evaluations with wall-clock speedups and communication analysis; report scalability vs. K and sparsity.
- Include PJADMM and additional primal-dual baselines with tuned parameters.
- Provide pseudocode and a practitioner’s guide for τi, νi, K; consider adaptive schemes.
- Include real datasets for RPCA and group-structured models; release code.

Pros for acceptance: conceptual novelty; solid convergence with constant steps; unifying view; promising results; relevance to NeurIPS optimization. Cons: limited empirical validation of parallelism; missing baselines; practical guidance gaps. Overall, promising and worth consideration with stronger experiments.
