

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address the gap in generalizing ADMM from two-block to multiple-block problems, which has been a challenging problem in optimization literature. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size, and shows that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a dual backward step in the dual update, which prevents overly aggressive progress on the dual variable when updating multiple blocks in parallel. This is a key insight that distinguishes PDMM from direct Jacobi updates, which have been shown to fail to converge. The authors also provide a principled way to determine step sizes by considering the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of randomized blocks to update.

The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous analysis. The paper establishes global convergence (Theorem 1) and iteration complexity (Theorem 2) under reasonable assumptions. The authors carefully explain how PDMM relates to existing methods, providing valuable theoretical connections between sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM. The experimental section demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, showing it outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both convergence speed and runtime.

The paper is well-written with clear organization and consistent notation. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and places the work in context with previous ADMM variants. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (Section 2.2) is particularly insightful and helps readers understand the role of the dual backward step. The experimental setup is thorough, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines and careful parameter tuning.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature by providing a unified framework for multiple-block ADMM variants. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation is convincing. The authors effectively demonstrate how to handle the sparsity of the constraint matrix when setting step sizes, which is a practical concern for large-scale problems.

I have a few suggestions for improvement. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of computational complexity per iteration between PDMM and existing methods. While the experimental results show PDMM's runtime advantages, a more explicit analysis of the computational cost of the dual backward step would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the paper mentions that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, but the connection to the overlapping group lasso application could be made more explicit in the main text (it's currently in Section 2.3).

The paper addresses an important problem in optimization with clear theoretical contributions and practical applications. The proposed method fills a gap in the literature regarding multi-block ADMM variants, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental optimization problem with relevance to machine learning applications. The technical quality is strong, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization methods for machine learning applications.

Strengths:
- Novel dual backward step that enables convergence for multiple blocks
- Unified framework showing connections between existing ADMM variants
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with global convergence and iteration complexity results
- Practical demonstration of performance improvements on real machine learning problems
- Clear exposition of the relationship between sparsity, step size, and convergence

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity per iteration
- Some aspects of the connection to overlapping group lasso could be made more explicit
- The paper could better articulate the trade-offs between block size and convergence speed

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, the experimental validation is thorough, and the proposed method has practical value for large-scale machine learning problems. The paper is well-written and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
