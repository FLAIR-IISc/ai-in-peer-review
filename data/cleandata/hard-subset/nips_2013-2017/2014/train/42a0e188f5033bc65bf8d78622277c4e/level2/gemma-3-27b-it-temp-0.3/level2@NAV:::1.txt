## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints, a common framework for problems like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors address a key challenge in extending the successful Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, where convergence isn’t guaranteed with naive parallelization. PDMM tackles this by randomly updating blocks in parallel and introducing a backward step on the dual update, aiming for improved convergence and efficiency.

The paper clearly articulates the problem and its relevance, building upon the extensive work on ADMM and its variants (GSADMM, Jacobian variants). The proposed PDMM is well-motivated, and the authors effectively highlight how it generalizes existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM. The theoretical contributions, specifically the convergence analysis with O(1/T) iteration complexity, are a significant strength. The connection established between PDMM and existing methods provides valuable insight into the behavior of these algorithms.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the practical implications of the parameters (τi, νi) are not fully explored. The explanation of how sparsity impacts these parameters feels somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from more detailed justification. The experimental section, while demonstrating PDMM’s performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, lacks a thorough comparison with highly optimized implementations of existing methods. The claim of superior performance needs stronger evidence, particularly given the computational advantages of existing libraries. The discussion of the effect of K (number of randomly chosen blocks) is insightful, but the reasoning behind the observed performance curve could be further elaborated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on relevant applications.
*   Clear connection to and generalization of existing ADMM variants.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity and practical implications.
*   Insufficiently rigorous comparison with highly optimized baselines.
*   Experimental results could be more comprehensive and insightful.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical novelty and potential for practical impact are significant. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning, a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art implementations, and a deeper analysis of the experimental results. The authors should also clarify the practical guidance for choosing the number of blocks (K) based on problem characteristics.
