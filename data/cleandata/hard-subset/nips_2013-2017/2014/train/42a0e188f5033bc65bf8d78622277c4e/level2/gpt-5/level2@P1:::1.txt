Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing a sum of convex (possibly nonsmooth) block-separable functions subject to linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM chooses K blocks to update in parallel and introduces a “dual backward step” via an intermediate dual variable. The step sizes for the explicit dual update and the backward step are functions of (i) the number of updated primal blocks K and (ii) the row-wise block sparsity di of the constraint matrix A. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes. They also show that two existing Jacobian-style ADMM variants are special cases: sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) when primal updates are exact and dual steps are conservative, and PJADMM when proximal terms make primal progress small and full dual steps are used. They further illustrate that PDMM supports randomized updates for overlapping blocks (via standard duplication and linear constraints) and apply it to RPCA and overlapping group lasso, reporting favorable performance over sADMM, GSADMM, and RBSUMM.

This work is situated among recent efforts to generalize ADMM beyond two blocks (e.g., Gauss–Seidel multi-block ADMM, PJADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) and to provide convergence guarantees under constant step sizes. The “dual backward step” is related to damped/over-relaxed dual updates considered implicitly in splitting-variable formulations; here it is made explicit and calibrated to sparsity and parallelism.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: the identification of “implicit dual ascent” in the primal quadratic term and the resulting stabilization via a backward dual step is insightful and explains the instability of naive Jacobi multi-block ADMM.
- Generality: PDMM allows randomized parallel updates of an arbitrary number of blocks K and can accommodate overlapping groups through standard splitting, where the step-size rules become particularly favorable (τi = 0.5 for all blocks).
- Theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity with constant step sizes; the special-case reductions to sADMM and PJADMM help unify existing methods and clarify when conservative vs. aggressive dual steps are appropriate.
- Practical guidance: step-size formulas τi and νi that incorporate both K and the per-row sparsity di, with an intuitive discussion of their behavior as K and di vary.
- Empirical results: on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM compares favorably to sADMM and RBSUMM; the study of how K affects runtime is informative.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Clarity of technical definitions: the Lyapunov function and residual use Pt, Q, βi; these are only stated as PSD matrices/constants, with specifics deferred to the supplement. It would improve readability to give explicit forms or at least tighter descriptions in the main text and brief intuition for how they arise.
- Parameterization and tuning: while τi and νi are prescribed by formulas, the Bregman terms (ηjt, choice of φjt) for inexact PDMM are problem-dependent. More guidance on choosing ηjt and concrete defaults would help practitioners. Sensitivity analyses for ρ, τi, νi (beyond a few settings) are limited.
- Experimental scope and fairness: all implementations are sequential MATLAB. Since the key selling point is parallelism, a multi-threaded or distributed experiment (or simulated speedup) would strengthen claims. For RPCA, comparisons to strong proximal/nuclear-norm solvers would add context. For RBSUMM, the paper notes non-convergence within 5000 iters under some schedules; including a best-performing schedule and wall-clock comparisons (even if slower) would be fairer.
- Novelty vs. known variants: while the dual backward step is well-motivated and the sparsity-aware step-size rule is new to my knowledge, the overall framework builds on familiar ingredients (splitting, proximal regularization). The originality lies in the combination and the explicit calibration to di and K with proof of O(1/T) under constant steps.
- Overlapping blocks: the formulation via duplication and equality constraints is standard; the statement that randomized BCD on overlapping blocks is “open” could be nuanced by citing proximal gradient approaches that avoid block separability assumptions (the authors do compare to S-APG/PA-APG).

Quality:
- The theoretical results appear technically sound and align with contemporary ADMM analyses; the interpretation of τi, νi is convincing. Some key auxiliary quantities and proof sketches should be surfaced in the main text for self-containment.
- Experiments support the claims but would benefit from larger scales and parallel runs.

Clarity:
- Overall well-organized and readable. Suggestions:
  - Define Pt, Q explicitly or provide simple bounds in the main text.
  - Summarize the proof idea behind the τi, νi formulas (beyond intuition), even briefly.
  - Unify the discussion of “best” settings across methods to avoid confusion about tuning.

Originality:
- Moderate. The dual backward step plus sparsity- and K-aware step sizes, and the unification of sADMM/PJADMM, provide a useful perspective and practical algorithmic guidance.

Significance:
- Practical significance is promising, particularly for sparse constraints and overlapping structures, and for parallel environments. Theoretical significance is solid but incremental relative to the two-block ADMM literature; nevertheless, multi-block convergence with constant steps and parallel randomized updates is valuable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual contribution (dual backward step) with principled step-size calibration.
- Unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM.
- Convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant step sizes.
- Empirical improvements on representative problems and informative study of K.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited parallel experiments; mostly sequential MATLAB results on moderate scales.
- Some theoretical components deferred to the supplement, reducing self-containment.
- Parameter sensitivity and practical guidance for inexact PDMM could be expanded.
- Comparisons could be broader and more standardized across baselines.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Provide concrete defaults and heuristics for ηjt and φjt in common settings; add a short sensitivity study for ρ, τi, νi.
- Move explicit forms of Pt, Q, βi (or their roles) into the main text; add a brief derivation sketch for τi, νi.
- Include at least one parallel/multi-core experiment or simulated parallel speedup to substantiate the parallel advantage.
- Expand experimental baselines for RPCA (e.g., proximal gradient/nuclear norm solvers) and report robust tuning protocols for all methods.
- Clarify the “open problem” statement on overlapping BCD and relate PDMM’s benefits over proximal-gradient alternatives.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and principled variant of multi-block ADMM with clear intuition, convergence guarantees under constant steps, and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity and experimental breadth concerns would further strengthen its impact.
