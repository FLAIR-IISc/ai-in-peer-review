This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. This is a canonical problem formulation in machine learning, and the paper rightly points out that generalizing the highly successful two-block Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting is an important and challenging open problem. The core idea of PDMM is to combine the principles of randomized block coordinate descent with ADMM. At each iteration, the algorithm randomly selects a subset of K (out of J) primal variable blocks and updates them in parallel. A key contribution is the introduction of a "dual backward step" that makes the subsequent dual variable update more conservative, which proves crucial for ensuring convergence. The paper connects PDMM to existing parallel ADMM variants, showing that methods like sADMM and PJADMM can be seen as special cases. Theoretically, the authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with a constant step size, which matches the standard rate for ADMM.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the significant problem of efficiently solving multi-block linearly constrained optimization problems, a topic of great interest to the NIPS community. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and elegant synthesis of randomized coordinate descent and ADMM. The introduction of the dual backward step, and the analysis of how to set the step sizes (τi, νi) based on block sparsity and the number of parallel updates (K), is a strong technical contribution. The ability to handle overlapping blocks through a simple reformulation is also a significant advantage, as this is a difficult setting for standard block coordinate descent methods.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions for ADMM. The proofs appear correct and the theoretical results strongly support the algorithm's design. The empirical evaluation is also a strength. The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are well-designed and compare PDMM against a relevant set of state-of-the-art methods (GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM). The results convincingly demonstrate that PDMM can be significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, even in a sequential implementation, showcasing the efficiency of the update rules.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the problem is clearly laid out, and the proposed algorithm is explained in a clear, step-by-step manner. The discussion in Section 2, which breaks down the roles of the dual backward step, block sparsity, and randomization, is particularly insightful for understanding the algorithm's mechanics. The connection to prior work (sADMM, PJADMM) is clearly articulated and helps to position PDMM within the existing literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The primary motivation and name of the algorithm centers on its parallel nature. However, all the experiments reported are run sequentially on a single machine. While the superior runtime in a serial setting is promising (suggesting that the per-iteration cost savings and faster convergence outweigh the cost of more iterations), the paper does not provide direct evidence of speedup from a parallel implementation. A small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine demonstrating scalability with respect to the number of workers (or blocks K) would have made the paper's central claim much stronger.

2.  **Parameter Tuning:** Compared to standard two-block ADMM, which primarily requires tuning the penalty parameter ρ, PDMM introduces additional parameters. While the paper provides a principled way to set the step sizes τi and νi based on K (the number of blocks to update), K itself becomes a hyperparameter that requires tuning. The experiments show that the choice of K has a significant impact on performance. This adds a layer of complexity for the practitioner, and the paper offers limited guidance on how to choose K optimally.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known and important problem: the effective generalization of ADMM to the multi-block setting.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated parallel randomized algorithm (PDMM) with a key technical insight (the dual backward step).
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees, including global convergence and an O(1/T) rate, matching the performance of standard ADMM.
*   Demonstrates compelling empirical performance on relevant machine learning problems, outperforming several competing methods in sequential runtime.
*   The paper is very clearly written and provides valuable insights into the landscape of parallel ADMM-type methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The central claim of being a "parallel" method is not empirically validated with experiments on a parallel architecture.
*   The algorithm introduces an additional tuning parameter (K, the number of blocks to update in parallel) whose selection is critical for performance but not fully addressed.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. The strengths, particularly the elegant algorithm design, strong theory, and convincing sequential performance, outweigh the weaknesses. While the absence of parallel experiments is a notable shortcoming, the paper provides a strong foundation for future work in this direction. I recommend acceptance.
