This paper proposes PADMM/PDMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant for linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization. Each iteration samples K primal blocks, updates them in parallel (optionally with a proximal/Bregman term to simplify subproblems), and then performs a conservative dual update via a backward step. The method explicitly sets blockwise dual stepsizes as a function of the number of selected blocks and the sparsity pattern of A, thereby enabling larger steps than Jacobi ADMM while maintaining stability. The authors prove global convergence and an ergodic iteration complexity O(J/(T K)) for J blocks and K updates per iteration, matching the O(1/T) rate of ADMM but allowing parallelism. They position the method relative to prior multi-block ADMM work: GSADMM can be fast but lacks general guarantees; sADMM uses splitting and small steps; PJADMM adds proximal terms; RBSUMM randomizes a single block under restrictive conditions. PDMM unifies these by showing sADMM and PJADMM are limiting cases and extends to overlapping blocks. Experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show improved time-to-accuracy over sADMM and RBSUMM and competitive performance with GSADMM.

Quality: The analysis is careful and technically sound. The backward dual step is well motivated; the proofs establish global convergence with explicit stepsize rules, and the complexity bound scales sensibly with K. Experiments are relevant and consistent with the claims, though they are sequential only.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Suggestions: include pseudocode and a practical guide for choosing K, τ, ν, and proximal parameters; add a brief communication/synchronization discussion for distributed settings.

Originality: The combination of randomized parallel block updates with a dual backward step and sparsity-aware stepsizes is novel and clearly distinguished from sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM.

Significance: Enabling parallel ADMM-like updates with guarantees for multi-block problems is important. The approach is likely to be adopted for large-scale applications with structured constraints and overlaps.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theory (global convergence; O(J/(T K)) complexity)
- Clear unification with sADMM and PJADMM; ability to use larger stepsizes
- Full parallelization of the primal step; handles overlapping blocks
- Promising empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso

Cons for acceptance:
- No parallel (multicore/GPU/distributed) implementation results
- Requires tuning three parameters; limited guidance and sensitivity analysis

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed contribution that advances multi-block ADMM with both theoretical and empirical support.
