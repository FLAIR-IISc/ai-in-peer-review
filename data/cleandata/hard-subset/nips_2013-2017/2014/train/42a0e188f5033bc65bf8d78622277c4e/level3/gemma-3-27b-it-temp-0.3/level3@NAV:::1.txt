## Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly update K primal blocks in parallel, coupled with a backward dual step, aiming to improve convergence and parallelism compared to existing ADMM variants. The authors prove sublinear convergence and demonstrate the algorithm generalizes several existing ADMM methods. This work builds upon the extensive research into ADMM, particularly its extensions to multi-block problems, addressing the challenges of convergence and scalability. Recent NIPS proceedings have featured numerous works on accelerated ADMM variants (e.g., proximal Jacobian methods), and PDMM presents a distinct approach within this landscape.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical contributions – the convergence analysis and the demonstration of PDMM’s generality. The connection drawn between PDMM and existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable insight into the role of the dual backward step and parameter selection. The proposed method appears promising for parallel implementation, a crucial aspect for large-scale problems. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provide initial validation, though limited in scope.

However, there are areas for improvement. The algorithm description in steps (5) and (6) lacks clarity. Specifically, it's unclear whether `y^{t+1}` and `\hat{y}^{t+1}` update *all* coordinates or only the selected block, and how the index `i` is selected for the dual update. This needs clarification. Furthermore, Theorem 2’s proof contains a missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" which should be addressed. 

The discussion surrounding Theorem 2 could be significantly expanded. The authors should demonstrate how the convergence rate relates to known ADMM variants by appropriately choosing parameters K and J. Guidance on optimal ways to split the transformation matrix A would also be beneficial. Finally, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of tensor completion applications, given the inherent multi-block structure of such problems, and an indication of which ADMM variant (including PDMM) might be optimal in that context. Minor typos exist: a missing space after "min" in (9) and an extra space before "Section" at line 217.

**Overall Recommendation:** I am inclined to **accept** this paper. Despite the clarity issues and areas for expanded discussion, the theoretical contributions are significant, and the proposed method offers a potentially valuable addition to the toolkit for solving large-scale optimization problems. Addressing the reviewer comments will further strengthen the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Novel approach to parallel ADMM.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated generality encompassing existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Clarity issues in algorithm description.
*   Limited experimental validation.
*   Lack of detailed discussion regarding parameter selection and application-specific performance.
