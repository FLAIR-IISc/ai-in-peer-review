This paper tackles convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints, a setting encompassing robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors propose PDMM, a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of ADMM. At each iteration, K block coordinates are selected at random and updated in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian with an optional Bregman proximal term; the dual is then updated with a “backward” correction that counteracts the aggressive implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty. The step sizes for the dual ascent and the backward step are chosen as functions of both the number of updated blocks (K) and the sparsity pattern of A via the per-row degrees di, enabling larger steps on sparse problems. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes; the bounds scale as O(J/(KT)) in the suboptimality term, consistent with randomized block updates. The framework unifies and clarifies prior multi-block ADMM variants: sADMM and PJADMM arise as extreme cases depending on the size of the proximal terms, explaining when full dual steps are safe. Empirically, on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM attains target accuracy in fewer CPU seconds than sADMM and RBSUMM and is competitive with/adaptively better than GSADMM, while offering a parallelizable design.

Relation to prior work: Two-block ADMM is well-understood [2,12], but direct multi-block Gauss–Seidel ADMM can diverge [7], and Jacobian variants typically need proximal stabilization or variable splitting [10,17,26]. RBCD methods [16,18] motivate random block updates but do not address constrained coupling, especially with overlapping blocks. PDMM contributes a principled Jacobian-style, randomized multi-block method with a dual backward step and step-size schedule that leverages sparsity; it bridges sADMM (smaller dual steps when primal progress is larger) and PJADMM (larger proximal terms allow full dual steps), offering conceptual clarity.

Quality: The algorithm is clearly specified, with exact and inexact updates unified via Bregman divergences. The dual backward step is well-motivated by rewriting the primal subproblem to expose the implicit dual ascent. Convergence guarantees (global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic rates) are derived under standard convexity and KKT existence assumptions, matching known rates for ADMM-type methods. The explicit dependence of τi, νi on K and di is a useful and well-argued design choice. The analysis considers overlapping blocks via reformulation and yields concrete step sizes (e.g., τi = 0.5 for di = 2), explaining gains over sADMM’s conservative choices. Proof sketches are coherent, though heavy; full proofs are deferred to the supplement. Experiments are carefully set up and include meaningful baselines (GSADMM/BSUMM, sADMM, RBSUMM, APG variants). One limitation is that all experiments run sequentially; the practical parallel speedups, communication costs, and scalability are not demonstrated.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the backward step, the role of K and sparsity in step-size selection, and the connections to sADMM and PJADMM are clearly explained. Notation is heavy but consistent; a compact algorithm box would aid readability. The assumptions for inexact PDMM (Lipschitz gradients for linearized terms and strong convexity of the surrogate) are stated, but more guidance on choosing ηjt, φjt, and ψjt in practice would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Moderately high. Randomized parallel block updates for multi-block ADMM with a principled dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes are novel and practically relevant. The unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM is insightful. The application to overlapping groups via constrained reformulation and randomized updates is a useful contribution relative to open questions in RBCD with overlap.

Significance: High practical potential. Many large-scale problems fit the considered template; enabling safe, parallel block updates with constant step sizes and providing O(1/T) rates is valuable. The experimental gains (especially on overlapping group lasso where τi can be much larger) suggest PDMM can improve time-to-accuracy. Demonstrating actual wall-clock parallel speedups and larger-scale deployments would further strengthen the case.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: randomized parallel block updates with a dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes.
- Solid theory: global convergence and O(J/(KT)) ergodic complexity with constant step sizes; handles exact and inexact updates under unified analysis.
- Conceptual unification: sADMM and PJADMM emerge as special cases with an interpretable trade-off between primal progress and dual step size.
- Relevant applications: robust PCA and overlapping group lasso; competitive to superior runtime versus strong baselines.
- Handles overlapping blocks via constrained reformulation and randomized updates, addressing a challenging setting for RBCD.

Weaknesses
- No parallel implementation results; all runs are sequential, so scalability and communication costs remain untested.
- Multiple hyperparameters (ρ, τi, νi, ηjt) require tuning; although formulas are provided, practical guidance and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Empirical scope is modest (two applications, synthetic or standard setups); broader benchmarks and ablations (e.g., effect of K, ηjt, and backward step) would be valuable.
- Comparisons to state-of-the-art multi-block proximal Jacobian methods could include stronger tuning and wall-clock fairness discussions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a parallel/multi-core or distributed implementation with scaling results and discussion of communication and synchronization.
- Include sensitivity studies for τi, νi, ρ, and ηjt; offer default heuristics and robustness analyses.
- Add ablations isolating the impact of the backward step versus proximal terms, and the effect of K on convergence and runtime.
- Expand experiments to additional datasets and problem classes; include memory/throughput metrics.
- Add an algorithm box and a brief practitioner’s guide for picking K and step sizes given di.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely challenge in multi-block constrained optimization with a principled, parallelizable method.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with interpretable step-size rules leveraging sparsity.
- Unifies prior ADMM variants and clarifies when conservative versus full dual steps are appropriate.
- Promising empirical results and practical applicability to overlapping group problems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of parallel implementation and scaling evidence reduces practical impact at this stage.
- Parameter tuning burden and limited guidance may hinder adoption.
- Experimental suite is relatively narrow.

Overall, this is a well-executed, insightful contribution that advances understanding and practice of multi-block ADMM. With added parallel experiments and practical guidance, it would be a strong addition to the program.
