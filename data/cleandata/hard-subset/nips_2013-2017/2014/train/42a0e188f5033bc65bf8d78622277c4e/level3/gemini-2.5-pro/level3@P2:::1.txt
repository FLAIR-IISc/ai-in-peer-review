### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear equality constraints, a common formulation in machine learning. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is effective for two-block problems, its generalization to multi-block settings has been challenging, with many variants lacking convergence guarantees or requiring restrictive assumptions.

The authors propose a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects a subset of K primal variable blocks and updates them in parallel. The key contributions of the method are: 1) A "dual backward step" that stabilizes the dual update and guarantees convergence, a problem that plagues direct Jacobi-style ADMM extensions. 2) Adaptive step sizes for the dual update that leverage the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. 3) A solid theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, under standard assumptions.

Conceptually, the paper provides a unified framework that encompasses existing parallel ADMM variants like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, offering a clearer understanding of their connections. The effectiveness of PDMM is demonstrated on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where it shows superior or competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis appears sound, providing a convergence rate guarantee that matches standard ADMM. The claims are well-supported by the provided proofs in the supplement and the experimental results. The work is a complete contribution, presenting an algorithm, its theory, and empirical validation. One minor correction seems to be needed in the proof of Theorem 2 (detailed below), but this does not appear to affect the validity of the main result.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem and related work, clearly positioning the contribution. However, the description of the core algorithm contains a critical ambiguity that hinders reproducibility. In equations (6) and (7), the updates for the dual variables `y_i` and `ŷ_i` are indexed by `i`. It is not specified whether these updates are performed for all row blocks `i = 1, ..., I` in parallel at each iteration, or for a single selected index `i`. This must be clarified.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While several multi-block ADMM variants exist, the proposed PDMM introduces a novel combination of parallel randomized updates and a carefully designed dual backward step. The idea of adapting step sizes based on matrix sparsity and the size of the random block subset is also novel and practically important. Furthermore, the unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is a significant conceptual contribution that deepens the understanding of this class of algorithms. The application of this framework to solve the overlapping group lasso problem in a randomized block coordinate fashion is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. ADMM is a cornerstone of large-scale optimization in machine learning, and developing a provably convergent and efficient parallel version for the multi-block case is an important and challenging problem. PDMM provides a flexible and powerful tool for this purpose. Researchers and practitioners working on problems like distributed optimization, sparse and low-rank modeling, and graphical models are likely to find this work useful and build upon it. The provided analysis offers new insights into designing and understanding parallel optimization algorithms.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Algorithmic Clarity:** Please clarify the dual updates in equations (6) and (7). Are all `I` blocks of the dual variables `y` and `ŷ` updated at every iteration `t`, or is there a selection rule for the index `i`? If all are updated, this should be made explicit (e.g., using a "for i = 1 to I" statement).

2.  **Proof Correction:** In the proof of Theorem 2 provided in the supplement, it appears a summation `∑_{i=1}^I` is missing in the second line of the derivation. Please double-check and correct this if necessary.

3.  **Discussion of Theorem 2:** The iteration complexity result in Theorem 2 is quite general. To enhance the paper's impact, I suggest adding a discussion that shows how this general result recovers the known convergence rates for special cases like sADMM (by setting K=J) and PJADMM. This would beautifully illustrate the unifying power of your framework.

4.  **Practical Guidance:** Could you provide some practical guidance or intuition on how one should choose the number of blocks `J`? The performance of PDMM depends on the trade-off between the cost of subproblems and the overall convergence rate (as noted in Remark 2). A discussion on good strategies for partitioning the matrix `A` would be extremely valuable for practitioners.

5.  **Application to Tensor Completion:** Many tensor decomposition and completion problems can be formulated as multi-block optimization problems. It would be insightful if you could comment on how PDMM could be applied here and perhaps speculate on which ADMM variant (e.g., PDMM with small K, PDMM with K=J, or GSADMM) might be most effective for such problems.

6.  **Minor Typos:**
    *   In equation (9), there should be a space after "min", i.e., `argmin_{x_j \in X_j}`.
    *   In the paragraph before Section 2.1, there is an extra space: "...as shown in Section 2.3".

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes PDMM, a novel, well-motivated, and flexible parallel algorithm for multi-block convex optimization.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees, including global convergence and an O(1/T) rate, addressing a key gap in the ADMM literature.
*   Offers a significant conceptual contribution by unifying several existing parallel ADMM methods within a single framework.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on relevant machine learning problems.
*   The work is likely to have a high impact on both the research and practitioner communities.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The current description of the algorithm's update rules is ambiguous, which is a major drawback for reproducibility. (This is easily fixable).
*   The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the implications of its theoretical results and more practical guidance for users.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarity and can be addressed in a revision. The paper presents a significant and elegant contribution to an important area of research. I am positive about this paper and recommend acceptance.
