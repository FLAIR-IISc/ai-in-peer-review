

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel extension of ADMM for solving multi-block convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while two-block ADMM has been well-studied, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM differs from previous approaches by randomly selecting K blocks to update in parallel (instead of sequentially), incorporating a dual backward step for conservative dual updates, and considering block sparsity and number of blocks to determine step sizes. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. The paper demonstrates that PDMM enables randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping groups, which was previously an open problem.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contribution is significant: it provides convergence guarantees for a parallel randomized multi-block method with constant step size, addressing an important open problem in the literature. The dual backward step (equations 6-7) is a key innovation that enables convergence, and the authors clearly explain its necessity through the implicit dual ascent observation (equation 9). The step size selection (equation 8) that considers block sparsity (di) and number of blocks (K) allows for larger step sizes than competing methods (e.g., sADMM uses τi = 1/J while PDMM can use τi = 1/di), which is crucial for practical performance. The paper also provides valuable connections between PDMM and existing methods, clarifying the role of dual backward steps. Empirically, PDMM outperforms sADMM, RBSUMM, and GSADMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, achieving better accuracy with fewer iterations and less runtime. The results are well-documented with clear comparisons and parameter tuning experiments.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The most significant omission is the lack of actual parallel implementation results: while the paper claims PDMM enables parallelization, it only reports sequential runtime comparisons, making it impossible to assess the claimed parallel speedup. This is particularly problematic given the paper's central motivation. Additionally, PDMM requires tuning three parameters (τi, νi, ηjt), which is less convenient than traditional ADMM (which typically requires only one parameter ρ). The paper provides some guidance for parameter selection (equation 8) but doesn't offer clear practical advice for setting these in real applications. The comparison to other parallel methods (e.g., distributed ADMM variants in [10]) is limited, and the implementation details for overlapping group lasso (Section 2.3) could be more thorough.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly addresses the problem. It makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of multi-block optimization methods. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate practical advantages over competing methods. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides valuable theoretical insight that wasn't previously available. While the parameter tuning complexity and lack of parallel implementation results are notable limitations, they don't undermine the core contribution, which is a theoretically sound and practically useful algorithm that enables parallelization of multi-block optimization.

The paper's significance is high: it solves an important open problem in multi-block optimization, provides a method that can be applied to a wide range of machine learning problems (as shown in the RPCA and overlapping group lasso applications), and establishes convergence guarantees matching ADMM's rate. The problem is well-motivated by the increasing need for scalable optimization methods in large-scale machine learning, and the paper makes a clear contribution to solving this problem.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in multi-block optimization. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical validation is convincing, and the paper is well-written. The minor weaknesses (lack of parallel results, parameter tuning complexity) are outweighed by the significant theoretical and practical contributions. I recommend acceptance.
