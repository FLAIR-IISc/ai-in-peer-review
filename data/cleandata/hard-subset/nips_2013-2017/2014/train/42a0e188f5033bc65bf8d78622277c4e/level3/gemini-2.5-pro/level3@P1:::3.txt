This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear equality constraints. The standard Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for two-block problems, but its generalization to the multi-block setting has proven challenging, with direct extensions often failing to converge. PDMM addresses this by introducing a randomized scheme where, at each iteration, a subset of K primal variable blocks are selected and updated in parallel.

The key innovations of PDMM are twofold. First, it introduces a "backward step" for the dual variable update. This step makes the dual update more conservative, which the authors argue is critical for ensuring convergence, especially when many blocks are updated in parallel. Second, the step sizes for the dual update and the backward step (`τi` and `νi`) are intelligently adapted based on the total number of blocks (J), the number of randomly selected blocks (K), and the block-sparsity of the constraint matrix A. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, which matches the standard rate for ADMM. Furthermore, the paper provides a valuable unifying perspective by showing that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM. The method is also shown to be applicable to problems with overlapping blocks, such as the overlapping group lasso.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and difficult problem of developing a provably convergent, parallel multi-block ADMM. The proposed algorithm is novel, particularly in its use of the dual backward step and the sparsity-aware, adaptive step-size rule. This represents a significant conceptual advance over existing Jacobian-style ADMM methods. The ability to handle any number of parallel block updates (from K=1 to K=J) within a single convergent framework is a major strength.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong. The paper provides a rigorous proof of global convergence and establishes an O(1/T) complexity rate under standard assumptions, which is the best one can expect for this class of first-order methods. The analysis elegantly handles the randomized selection of blocks and the inexact primal updates. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is insightful and enhances the understanding of the field.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained, the algorithm is presented precisely, and the connections to a wide range of related work are thoroughly discussed. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and the overall narrative is easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are compelling. On a sequential machine, PDMM demonstrates superior performance in terms of wall-clock time compared to several relevant baselines, including sADMM and RBSUMM. This is particularly impressive as it shows that even without actual parallel execution, the faster convergence (in iterations) and algorithmic structure of PDMM can lead to significant speedups.

### Weaknesses

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The primary motivation and name of the algorithm centers on its parallel nature. However, all experiments were conducted on a sequential machine. While the sequential results are promising and suggest potential for parallel speedups, the paper would be significantly stronger if it included experiments on a multi-core or distributed system to demonstrate real-world parallel performance gains. Without this, the practical benefits of the parallel design remain speculative.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The algorithm introduces new parameters `τi` and `νi`. Although the paper provides a principled formula (Eq. 8) for setting them based on K, J, and the matrix sparsity, the experimental results in Table 1 indicate that the "best" results are achieved by manually tuning these parameters away from the theoretical suggestions. This implies that practitioners might face a more challenging tuning problem compared to standard ADMM, which only has the penalty parameter `ρ`. A discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters and guidance on how to set them in practice would be beneficial.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel, provably convergent algorithm for the important problem of multi-block parallel ADMM.
2.  The theoretical analysis is sound, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) rate.
3.  Offers a valuable unifying view of related methods like sADMM and PJADMM.
4.  Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a sequential machine against state-of-the-art competitors.
5.  The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The central claim of parallel performance is not directly substantiated with experiments on parallel hardware.
2.  The practical application of the method may require tuning more parameters than traditional ADMM, potentially complicating its use.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The theoretical development is elegant and the sequential empirical results are strong. The weaknesses, while notable, do not undermine the core contribution of the work. The paper provides a solid foundation for future research in parallel optimization, and the proposed PDMM algorithm is likely to be influential and built upon by others. I recommend acceptance.
