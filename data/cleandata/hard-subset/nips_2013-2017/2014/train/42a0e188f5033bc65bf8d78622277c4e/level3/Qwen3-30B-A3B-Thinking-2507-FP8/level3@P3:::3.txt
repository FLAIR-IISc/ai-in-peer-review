

**Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"**

This paper proposes PADMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers), a randomized parallel ADMM variant for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The key innovation is a backward dual step that makes the dual update more conservative, enabling global convergence when randomly updating K blocks in parallel per iteration. The paper establishes global convergence and O(J/(TK)) iteration complexity with constant step size, where J is the number of blocks, T is iterations, and K is blocks per iteration. PADMM generalizes existing methods: sADMM and PJADMM are shown to be special cases, providing theoretical insight into their relationships.

The paper addresses a critical gap in multi-block ADMM extensions, where previous methods either lack convergence guarantees (e.g., direct Jacobi updates) or have limited parallelization (e.g., Gauss-Seidel ADMM). PADMM's backward dual step resolves the convergence issues of direct Jacobi updates by making dual progress more conservative. The method leverages constraint matrix sparsity to determine step sizes, allowing larger steps than competing methods. The paper demonstrates PADMM's ability to handle overlapping group structures—a problem where standard randomized block coordinate descent remains open.

**Strengths:** The theoretical contributions are robust, with clear global convergence and iteration complexity results under standard assumptions. The backward dual step is a clever technical innovation that resolves a fundamental convergence challenge. The connection between PADMM and existing methods provides valuable theoretical insight. Empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods (sADMM, RBSUMM, GSADMM), achieving target accuracy with less computation time. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant shortcoming is the absence of parallel implementation results. Without demonstrating actual speedup in a parallel setting, the claimed parallelization benefits remain theoretical. Additionally, PADMM requires tuning three parameters (τi, νi, ηjt), which is less convenient than traditional ADMM's single or dual parameter tuning. The paper could better clarify how to select these parameters in practice.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported empirical results. The authors carefully address limitations, including parameter tuning complexity and lack of parallel results. The convergence analysis is comprehensive and correctly establishes the method's guarantees.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with logical progression from problem formulation to method, analysis, and experiments. Notation is consistent, and the relationship to prior work is well-explained. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Originality:** PADMM represents a significant advancement over existing multi-block ADMM variants. The backward dual step is novel and resolves a key convergence issue. The method's ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized fashion addresses an open problem in the literature. The paper properly references related work and clearly articulates its contributions relative to prior methods.

**Significance:** This work solves an important problem in large-scale optimization, enabling full parallelization of the primal step while maintaining convergence guarantees. The empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages in two important applications, suggesting broad utility for practitioners. The method matches ADMM's convergence rate while enabling parallelization—a crucial practical advantage for large-scale problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art, novel backward dual step, well-written and organized, addresses an important open problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of parallel implementation results, increased parameter tuning complexity.

Overall, this is an excellent contribution to the literature on distributed optimization. The theoretical advances are significant, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages. While the absence of parallel implementation results is a notable limitation, it doesn't undermine the core theoretical contribution. The paper makes a valuable addition to the field, matching ADMM's convergence rate while enabling parallelization—a critical practical advantage for modern large-scale problems. I recommend acceptance.
