This paper addresses the important problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, a formulation that captures many problems in machine learning. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is a powerful tool for the two-block version of this problem, its generalization to the multi-block setting remains an active area of research. Standard sequential (Gauss-Seidel) extensions are not guaranteed to converge, and parallel (Jacobian) extensions often struggle with convergence.

The main contribution of this paper is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block-coordinate algorithm that enables parallel updates for the multi-block problem. The core idea is to replace the sequential primal updates of ADMM with parallel updates over a randomly selected subset of blocks. The key innovation that enables convergence is the introduction of a "dual backward step." This step effectively tempers the dual variable update, compensating for the fact that parallel primal updates are calculated using stale information from other blocks. The authors establish the global convergence of PDMM and derive an O(1/T) iteration complexity, which matches the rate of standard ADMM. The paper also provides a valuable unification of existing work, showing that other parallel ADMM variants like sADMM and PJADMM can be viewed as special cases of PDMM. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated experimentally on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and difficult problem: the efficient parallelization of ADMM for multi-block optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and elegant solution. The introduction of the dual backward step is a particularly clever technical contribution that directly addresses the instability of Jacobian-style updates in this context, allowing for provable convergence under general conditions. This work provides a valuable new tool for the distributed optimization toolkit.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound. The authors provide a rigorous proof of global convergence and an O(1/T) rate for a constant step size under standard assumptions. This is a strong theoretical result, as proving convergence for multi-block ADMM variants is often challenging.

3.  **Clarity and Unification:** The paper does an excellent job of placing PDMM within the landscape of existing augmented Lagrangian methods. By showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases, the authors provide a unifying framework that clarifies the relationship between these methods. This connection offers a deeper understanding of the role of the primal and dual step sizes in ensuring the convergence of parallel decomposition methods.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are well-executed and convincing. They clearly demonstrate that PDMM is competitive with, and often superior to, several state-of-the-art methods, including sequential ADMM (GSADMM) and other randomized variants (RBSUMM), in terms of overall runtime.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Explanation of Counterintuitive Results:** The empirical results are strong, but some aspects are counterintuitive and would benefit from deeper discussion. Specifically, in Table 1 (RPCA), the randomized, single-block PDMM1 converges in fewer iterations than the sequential, fully-updated GSADMM. This is surprising, as Gauss-Seidel methods leverage the most recent information at each block update and are thus typically expected to converge in fewer iterations than Jacobian-style or randomized single-block methods. While the runtime improvement is expected due to lower per-iteration cost, the improvement in iteration count is not. The authors should elaborate on why this might be the case. Is it an artifact of the specific problem structure, the randomization strategy, or the specific parameter settings? A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between information freshness (in GSADMM) and the update structure of PDMM would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and provide valuable insights for practitioners.

2.  **Clarity on Parameter Selection:** The choice of step sizes τi and νi is crucial. While Equation (8) provides the formulas, a more intuitive explanation for *why* these specific forms arise from the analysis would improve the paper's clarity. For example, a brief discussion on how the dual backward step (controlled by νi) needs to increase in magnitude as more blocks are updated in parallel (as K increases) would help the reader build a more conceptual understanding of the algorithm's mechanics.

3.  **Parallel Implementation:** The authors rightly note that PDMM is designed for parallel execution while GSADMM is inherently sequential. However, the experiments were conducted on a sequential machine. While this is acceptable for demonstrating the algorithm's convergence properties, the claims about practical speedup would be even more compelling if backed by results from a true parallel implementation, even on a multi-core machine. This is a suggestion for future work rather than a prerequisite for acceptance.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective algorithm for a significant problem in large-scale machine learning. The unification of prior work is an additional valuable contribution.
*   **Con:** The paper could be strengthened by providing a more detailed discussion and intuition behind some of the surprising empirical results concerning iteration counts.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM method is both novel and useful, and the theoretical and empirical support is strong. I recommend acceptance.
