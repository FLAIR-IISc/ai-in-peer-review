

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been successfully applied to two-block problems, generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates blocks in parallel rather than sequentially, introducing a dual backward step that compensates for the limited information propagation in Jacobian updates. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. Empirical results on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM's superiority over state-of-the-art methods.

The paper excels in technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proofs are comprehensive, addressing both exact and inexact updates through a unified Bregman divergence framework. The dual backward step (Equations 6-7) is particularly novel and well-justified, explaining why Jacobian updates can converge when naive implementations fail. The connection to existing methods (Section 2.2) provides valuable theoretical insights, clarifying the relationship between sADMM, PJADMM, and PDMM. The experimental results are thorough, with careful comparisons against relevant baselines and detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity (Tables 1, 2).

PDMM makes significant contributions to the literature on distributed optimization. The key innovation—using a dual backward step to enable convergence of Jacobian updates—addresses a fundamental limitation in multi-block ADMM variants. This approach provides a principled way to balance parallelism and convergence guarantees, which is especially valuable for large-scale problems. The paper's theoretical results (Theorems 1-2) match the convergence rates of ADMM and its variants, establishing PDMM as a theoretically sound alternative to sequential methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with helpful explanations of the dual backward step and its relationship to existing methods. The connection between the sparsity of A and the step size parameters (Equation 8) is particularly elegant and practical. The experimental section provides strong evidence of PDMM's effectiveness, with runtime comparisons that account for parallelism (unlike most baselines).

The main weakness is the somewhat counterintuitive nature of PDMM's performance: Jacobian methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel approaches, yet PDMM outperforms sequential methods. While the paper provides a good theoretical explanation, the reviewer requests more intuitive explanation for why the dual backward step enables this improvement. The experimental comparison with GSADMM (which has no convergence guarantees) might be slightly misleading, as the authors note that GSADMM can be faster in iterations but slower in runtime due to sequential implementation.

Regarding the four criteria:
- Quality: High. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-justified. The convergence proofs are technically sound and address important cases (exact/inexact updates).
- Clarity: High. The paper is well-structured with clear explanations of the key innovations. The connection to existing methods (Section 2.2) is particularly helpful for readers.
- Originality: High. The dual backward step is novel, and the paper establishes a unified framework that clarifies the relationship between multiple ADMM variants.
- Significance: High. PDMM provides a practical, theoretically sound approach for parallel optimization with linear constraints, addressing a key gap in the literature. The applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear practical value.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence guarantees
- The dual backward step resolves a key limitation in multi-block ADMM
- Empirical results demonstrate clear performance advantages
- Provides theoretical unification of existing methods
- Addresses an important practical problem in distributed optimization

Arguments against acceptance:
- The counterintuitive performance (Jacobian outperforming Gauss-Seidel) needs more intuitive explanation
- Some readers may question the practicality of the step size parameters
- The connection to sADMM (which the authors acknowledge is not well-tuned in the public code) could be more thoroughly addressed

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, making a significant contribution to the theory and practice of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are solid, the experimental validation is thorough, and the method has clear potential for application in large-scale machine learning problems. The dual backward step is particularly elegant and could inspire further research in parallel optimization methods. The paper's connections to existing literature (sADMM, PJADMM) provide valuable context and help clarify the broader landscape of multi-block ADMM variants.

PDMM represents a meaningful advancement in the field, offering both theoretical insights and practical improvements over existing methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear significance for both theoretical and applied optimization. While the counterintuitive performance merits further explanation, the paper's strengths substantially outweigh this minor weakness. The authors have made a significant contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
