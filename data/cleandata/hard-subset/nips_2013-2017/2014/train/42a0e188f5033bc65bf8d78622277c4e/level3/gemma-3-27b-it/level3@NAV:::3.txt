## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant designed for multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The core idea – randomly selecting *K* blocks per iteration for parallel update, followed by a “backward” dual update – is a compelling approach to tackle the challenges of full parallelization and convergence observed in standard multi-block ADMM methods. The authors convincingly argue that this backward dual step is crucial for ensuring global convergence, a point often overlooked in parallel ADMM implementations. The ability to incorporate a proximal term within the primal update offers increased flexibility and can simplify certain subproblems, broadening the method's applicability.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with established global convergence guarantees and an iteration complexity of O(J/(T*K)) after T iterations, relating performance directly to the number of blocks and the randomized selection parameter K. Empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods like sADMM, PJADMM, and GSADMM, showcasing faster convergence to target accuracy. The connection drawn between PADMM, sADMM, and PJADMM, clarifying the role of the backward dual step, is insightful. 

However, the paper does have weaknesses. Notably, the lack of reported results for a *parallel* implementation of PADMM is a significant omission, given the method’s core motivation. While the authors detail the parallelizable steps, demonstrating actual speedup on a multi-core architecture would strengthen the claims substantially. Furthermore, tuning the three PADMM parameters (K, ρ, and potentially parameters within the proximal term) might be less convenient than traditional ADMM, which relies primarily on the penalty parameter. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PADMM offers a novel approach to parallelizing ADMM, addressing convergence issues prevalent in other multi-block variants.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds provide a solid mathematical foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates improved performance on relevant benchmark problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, effectively conveying the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The absence of empirical evidence showcasing actual parallel speedup is a crucial shortcoming.
*   **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** Three parameters may require more careful tuning compared to standard ADMM.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-written contribution that presents a valuable addition to the landscape of parallel ADMM methods. Despite the missing parallel implementation results, the solid theoretical foundation, insightful analysis, and promising empirical findings warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the parallel implementation aspect in future work. I believe the work advances the state-of-the-art in parallel optimization and could be beneficial to researchers and practitioners working with large-scale, block-separable problems.
