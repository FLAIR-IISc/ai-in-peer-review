This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. It addresses the challenges of parallelizing ADMM for multiple blocks, proposing a method that randomly updates K blocks per iteration with a backward step for the dual update to ensure convergence. The paper builds upon extensive prior work in ADMM, including two-block ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011], extensions to multiple blocks [Chen et al., 2013; He et al., 2015; Deng et al., 2016], and Jacobian variants like sADMM [Wang et al., 2014] and PJADMM [Deng et al., 2016]. The authors clearly position PADMM within this landscape, highlighting its advantages over existing methods.  Related work in randomized coordinate descent [Nesterov, 2012; Richtarik & Takac, 2014] is also appropriately referenced.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined algorithm and a rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proof and iteration complexity bound (O(J/(T*K))) are significant contributions. The authors thoughtfully address potential convergence issues inherent in multi-block ADMM by introducing the backward dual step. Empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PADMM, showing improved performance compared to sADMM, PJADMM, and GSADMM.  The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the lack of a parallel implementation and the tuning of three parameters.  The connection established between PADMM, sADMM and PJADMM strengthens the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are clear. However, the explanation of the intuition behind the backward dual step could be more accessible. A more intuitive explanation of why a conservative dual update is crucial for convergence would benefit the reader.  The connection between the step size parameters τi and νi and the sparsity of the matrix A is clearly explained.

**Originality:** PADMM presents a novel combination of randomized block coordinate descent with ADMM. While the individual components are well-established, the specific approach of randomly selecting K blocks and employing the backward dual step is original. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, is also a significant contribution. The extension to handle overlapping block problems adds to the originality.

**Significance:** The results are important as they address a key challenge in parallelizing ADMM for large-scale problems. Enabling full parallelization of the primal step and allowing for larger step sizes than competing methods makes PADMM a potentially valuable tool for practitioners. The empirical results, demonstrating faster performance on relevant applications like RPCA and overlapping group lasso, further bolster the significance of the work.  The theoretical guarantees and matching ADMM’s convergence rate while enabling parallelization are crucial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Parallelization:** PADMM enables full parallelization of the primal step, a key advantage for large-scale problems.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds.
*   **Improved Performance:** Empirical results demonstrate faster performance compared to existing methods.
*   **Flexibility:** The method allows for inexact primal updates through the use of Bregman divergences.
*   **Connection to Existing Methods:** The authors clearly show how sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PADMM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation:** The absence of a parallel implementation limits the demonstrated benefits of the algorithm.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** Tuning the three PADMM parameters (K, ρ, and the parameters controlling the Bregman divergence if used) may be less convenient than traditional ADMM.
*   **Intuition:** The explanation of the backward dual step could be more intuitive.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to parallelizing ADMM.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithm.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the practical benefits of PADMM.
*   The connection to existing methods strengthens the contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a parallel implementation prevents a full demonstration of the algorithm’s potential.
*   The parameter tuning process may be less convenient than traditional ADMM.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-written paper with solid theoretical and empirical support. It addresses an important problem in the optimization community – parallelizing ADMM – and proposes a novel and effective solution. Despite the limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
