- Proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block coordinate method for multi-block linear-constrained optimization.  
- Addresses gap: extending ADMM from two-block to multi-block settings with unclear generalizations.  
- Updates blocks in parallel and introduces a novel dual backward step to enable convergence of Jacobian updates.  
- Establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size.  
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.  
- Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods.  
- Strong technical quality with rigorous, comprehensive convergence proofs for exact and inexact updates.  
- Uses a unified Bregman divergence framework to handle both exact and inexact updates.  
- The dual backward step (Eqs. 6–7) is particularly novel and well-justified, explaining why naive Jacobian updates fail.  
- Clear theoretical connections to existing methods (Section 2.2) that clarify relationships among multi-block ADMM variants.  
- Experiments are thorough, include parameter sensitivity and runtime comparisons that account for parallelism.  
- Practical insight: relates sparsity of A to step size parameters (Eq. 8), which is elegant and useful.  
- Main weakness: counterintuitive claim that Jacobian PDMM can outperform Gauss–Seidel needs more intuitive explanation.  
- Comparison with GSADMM may be misleading since GSADMM lacks convergence guarantees and is sequentially implemented.  
- Review rates Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance as high.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory, dual backward step resolving a key limitation, strong empirical validation, and unification of methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of more intuitive explanation for performance, potential concerns about practicality of step-size tuning, and sADMM comparison issues.  
- Overall recommendation: well-suited for NIPS—technically solid, practically relevant, and likely to inspire further research.
