Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex functions subject to linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K primal blocks at random and updates them in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian with an optional Bregman proximal term; it also performs a “dual backward step,” i.e., it uses an intermediate ŷ that effectively shrinks the implicit dual ascent created by the quadratic penalty. The authors derive per-row adaptive dual step sizes τi and backward factors νi that depend on (i) the number of selected blocks K and (ii) the row-wise block sparsity di of A. They show global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes, and they clarify that sADMM and PJADMM are two limiting cases of PDMM, helping explain when small-vs-large dual steps are appropriate. They further show how PDMM can be applied to overlapping group-structured problems (e.g., overlapping group lasso) by rewriting them with equality constraints, enabling randomized updates even with overlapping blocks.

The work relates to a substantial literature on ADMM and multi-block extensions. Compared to GSADMM/BSUMM and randomized variants such as RBSUMM, PDMM provides convergence with constant step sizes and an explicit rate; compared to proximal/Jacobian ADMM (e.g., PJADMM), it gives a unifying view via the backward step and the role of sparsity-aware step sizes. The experimental evaluation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso shows empirical improvements over sADMM and RBSUMM, with GSADMM sometimes competitive in iteration count but inherently sequential.

Strengths:
- Principled, simple algorithmic idea (dual backward step) that stabilizes parallel multi-block updates, with clean dependence of τi, νi on K and di.
- Unifying perspective: recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extremes; clarifies the tradeoff between exact vs proximal primal progress and dual step size.
- Solid theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant steps; conditions match standard convex assumptions used in ADMM.
- Practical relevance: enables randomized parallel updates even with overlapping blocks; easy to implement.
- Empirical results on two important applications demonstrate competitive to superior performance versus strong baselines.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Clarity: Algorithm steps (5)–(7) are ambiguous about whether y^{t+1} and ŷ^{t+1} update all dual coordinates or only selected ones. The text later says “(6) and (7) update all dual blocks,” but this should be stated prominently in the algorithm description. Also clarify how index i is selected/looped and how xt+1 aggregates updated vs non-updated primal blocks.
- Proof detail: In the proof of Theorem 2 there appears to be a missing “∑_{i=1}^I”; please add it at the relevant step to make the bound precise.
- Discussion of rates: Theorem 2 would benefit from explicit corollaries that (a) recover known ADMM rates when choosing K and η appropriately (e.g., K=J and η=0 yields sADMM-like behavior; large η with ν=0 yields PJADMM-like behavior), and (b) quantify how τi, νi change with di and K to guide practice.
- Guidance on splitting A: Provide practical recommendations on how to choose the block partition (J) and K. For example, discuss tradeoffs between fewer, larger blocks (harder subproblems but larger τi) versus many small blocks (easier subproblems but smaller τi), and how the distribution of di across rows should influence grouping. Any heuristic (e.g., balance di across rows, cluster columns by row-support overlap) would be valuable.
- Experiments: All experiments run sequentially; since PDMM’s main benefit is safe parallelism, a parallel runtime study (even limited) would strengthen the case. An ablation on the backward step (e.g., vary νi) would also illustrate its necessity. Finally, tensor completion is a canonical multi-block application—please discuss how PDMM maps to common tensor factorizations/completion formulations and which ADMM variant (sADMM/PJADMM/PDMM with which K) is likely to be best there.
- Minor: Sensitivity to τi/νi is hinted at in RPCA; provide guidance or defaults beyond (8) when A is moderately dense or when di is highly variable.

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears good; assumptions align with standard convex settings. The rate and convergence arguments are consistent with ADMM literature; please fix the missing summation in Theorem 2’s proof and clarify the update scope for y, ŷ.
- Experiments are appropriate and reproducible; adding parallel timing and more ablations would further substantiate the claims.

Clarity:
- Generally well written, but notation-heavy. Include a concise pseudocode box specifying: sampling of J^t; computation of xt+1 with mixed updated/non-updated blocks; full updates of y and ŷ; the role of η and choice of Bregman terms.
- Minor typos: missing space after “min” in (9); remove the extra space before “Section” around line 217.

Originality:
- The dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes for fully parallel randomized multi-block updates are novel and insightful. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM parameters is also original and useful.

Significance:
- Addresses a known gap: stable, convergent, parallel multi-block ADMM with constant steps and rate guarantees. Likely to influence practitioners working on large-scale, structured convex problems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual contribution (backward step) with theory and practice.
- Unifying framework connecting multiple ADMM variants.
- Applicable to overlapping groups; fills an open gap vs RBCD.
- Solid experimental evidence on two representative tasks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Some presentation ambiguities (algorithm updates) and a proof typo.
- Limited exploration of parallel runtime and broader applications (e.g., tensors).
- Limited practical guidance on block partitioning and parameter selection.

Recommendation: Accept (clear contribution with solid theory and promising empirical results; revision can address clarity and minor issues).

Reviewer confidence: High.
