- Addresses minimization of block-separable convex functions with linear equality constraints and proposes PDMM.  
- PDMM generalizes ADMM from two-block sequential (Gauss–Seidel) updates to multi-block parallel (Jacobian) updates.  
- Key innovation is a "dual backward step" that tempers aggressive dual updates caused by parallel primal updates.  
- Provides rigorous theory: global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size.  
- Unifies prior parallel ADMM variants by showing sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.  
- Empirical validation on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso shows PDMM is competitive or superior to several baselines.  
- Strength — originality/significance: solves a well-known multi-block parallel ADMM convergence challenge enabling randomized parallel updates.  
- Strength — theoretical quality: strong convergence proofs under standard assumptions.  
- Strength — conceptual clarity: clarifies relationships and parameter roles across augmented Lagrangian methods.  
- Strength — empirical: competitive wall-clock performance against sADMM, RBSUMM, and sometimes GSADMM.  
- Weakness — unexpected sequential runtime advantage over GSADMM needs explanation (iteration count vs per-iteration cost).  
- Weakness — lacks true parallel (multi-core/distributed) experiments to demonstrate practical parallel speedups.  
- Weakness — needs more intuition for step-size rules (τi, νi) and their dependence on sparsity, total blocks J, and sampled blocks K.  
- Pro summary: novel, theoretically sound, practically effective algorithm with a crucial dual backward step.  
- Con summary: empirical claims vs sequential methods are counterintuitive and the main parallel advantage is not empirically demonstrated.  
- Overall: high-quality, influential work recommended for acceptance, with suggested clarifications and parallel experiments.
