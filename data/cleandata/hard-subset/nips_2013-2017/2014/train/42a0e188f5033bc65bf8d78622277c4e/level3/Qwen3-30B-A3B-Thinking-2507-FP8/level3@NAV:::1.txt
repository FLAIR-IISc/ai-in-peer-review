

This paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors generalize ADMM variants by introducing a backward dual step and enabling parallel primal updates of randomly selected blocks, establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity. The work connects to prior ADMM literature by showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing theoretical insight into their relationships.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) it introduces a unified framework for randomized block coordinate methods with a backward dual step, (2) it proves convergence for a broader class of problems than previous work, and (3) it demonstrates practical advantages through experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear connections to existing methods and appropriate generalization of convergence results.

The paper's main weaknesses require attention in revision. The algorithm description (steps 5-7) lacks clarity on whether dual variables update all coordinates or a single block, and how the index i is selected in the dual updates. Theorem 2's proof requires adding the missing summation term \sum_{i=1}^I. The authors should expand Theorem 2 to explicitly show consistency with known ADMM variants' convergence rates by parameter choices, and provide guidance on optimal matrix splitting for A. The experimental section should address tensor completion applications to demonstrate PDMM's utility in multi-block structures.

Minor issues include a missing space after "min" in equation (9) and an extra space before "Section" at line 217. The paper is well-written overall, with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproduction.

The work demonstrates strong technical quality, originality in generalizing ADMM variants, and significant practical relevance for large-scale optimization. The theoretical contributions advance the state of the art in parallel optimization methods. With the requested clarifications and expansions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the conference.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The minor issues noted are easily addressable in revision, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
