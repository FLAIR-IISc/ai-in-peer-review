This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PADMM), a randomized block coordinate ADMM variant for solving multi-block separable convex optimization problems. The core idea is to randomly select K out of J blocks at each iteration, update them in parallel, and then perform a dual update. This work fits into a well-established line of research aimed at generalizing the highly successful two-block ADMM to the multi-block setting, addressing the limitations of sequential methods like Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM) and potentially inflexible parallel schemes like sADMM. The key innovation of PADMM is a "backward step" in the dual update, which makes the update more conservative and is shown to be crucial for proving global convergence.

The paper has several significant strengths. On the theoretical side, it provides a solid analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity, which matches the standard rate for ADMM while enabling parallelization. This is a strong result. The framework is also flexible, allowing for proximal terms in the primal update to simplify subproblems. The claim that PADMM unifies and generalizes prior work like sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable insight into the mechanics of parallel ADMM-style methods. On the empirical side, the experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are promising, demonstrating that PADMM can reach a target accuracy with less computation time than several state-of-the-art methods.

However, the paper has two notable weaknesses. The most significant shortcoming is the lack of a true parallel implementation. While the algorithm is designed for parallel execution and its per-iteration cost is lower in sequential simulations, the claimed advantages of parallelization are not directly validated. A demonstration on a multi-core or distributed system would have made the paper’s practical contributions much more compelling. Secondly, the method introduces additional hyperparameters (τi, νi) beyond the standard ADMM penalty parameter ρ, and the choice of K blocks adds another tuning dimension. This makes the algorithm less convenient to use in practice compared to traditional ADMM, potentially hindering its adoption.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically promising algorithm for a significant problem in large-scale optimization. The strengths in its theoretical contributions and strong sequential performance outweigh the weakness of not having a parallel implementation tested. The work advances the state-of-the-art and is likely to be influential for researchers working on distributed optimization.
