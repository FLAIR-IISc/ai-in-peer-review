This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly update *K* primal blocks in parallel at each iteration, coupled with a novel backward step in the dual update. The authors establish global convergence and a sublinear iteration complexity, and demonstrate that existing ADMM variants are special cases of PDMM. The method is evaluated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing promising performance.

The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization, particularly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications. Extending ADMM to multiple blocks effectively remains a challenging area, and this work offers a compelling contribution. The proposed PDMM appears well-motivated, building on the strengths of both dual ascent and ADMM while addressing their limitations. The theoretical analysis is a major strength, providing convergence guarantees and establishing connections to existing methods. The experimental results demonstrate that PDMM performs competitively, and often outperforms, state-of-the-art alternatives.  The generalization of existing ADMM variants as special cases of PDMM provides valuable insight into the interplay between different update strategies and parameter choices.

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. The presentation of the algorithm, specifically steps (5) and (6), lacks clarity. It’s unclear whether `y^{t+1}_i` and `\hat{y}^{t+1}_i` update all dual coordinates or just a single block, and how the index *i* is selected in relation to the randomly chosen blocks *Jt*. A more precise explanation or a step-by-step breakdown of the update process would significantly improve readability.

I noticed a minor technical issue in the proof of Theorem 2. The summation appears to be missing an “∑_{i=1}^I” term, which should be corrected.

Furthermore, while the theoretical results are strong, expanding the discussion around Theorem 2 would be beneficial. Specifically, it would be valuable to demonstrate how the established convergence rate aligns with those of known ADMM variants (e.g., sADMM, PJADMM) by strategically selecting parameters like *K* and *J*. This would provide a clearer understanding of the trade-offs involved and assist practitioners in choosing appropriate settings. Also, a discussion guiding optimal ways to split the transformation matrix *A* – perhaps based on its sparsity structure or computational cost of multiplying by different blocks – would significantly enhance the practical applicability of the method.

The empirical evaluation, while solid, could be broadened. Given the multi-block structure, exploring the application of PDMM to tensor completion problems would be highly relevant and demonstrate its versatility. Furthermore, discussing which ADMM variant might be optimal for tensor completion, and providing a comparison, would strengthen the practical contribution of the work.

Finally, there are minor stylistic issues: a missing space after "min" in equation (9), and an extra space before "Section" on line 217.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to multi-block ADMM, offering a unique combination of parallel updates and a backward dual step.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis and establishes connections to existing methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on relevant benchmark problems.
*   **Generality:** The framework generalizes existing ADMM variants, providing a unified perspective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The algorithm description lacks some precision, particularly regarding the dual updates.
*   **Discussion:** The theoretical discussion could be expanded to provide more practical guidance and connections to existing results.
*   **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation could be broadened to include additional applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor concerns regarding clarity and discussion, I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. I am inclined to **accept** the paper with the recommendation that the authors address the aforementioned clarity and discussion points during revision. A minor correction in the proof of Theorem 2 is also required. Addressing these issues would further enhance the impact and accessibility of this work.
