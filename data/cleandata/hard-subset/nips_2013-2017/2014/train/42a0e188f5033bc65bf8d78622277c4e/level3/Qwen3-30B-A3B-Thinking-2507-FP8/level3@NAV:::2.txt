

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. PDMM generalizes ADMM by replacing its sequential Gauss-Seidel updates with parallel Jacobian updates while introducing a dual backward step to compensate for limited information propagation in parallel updates. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates comparable to ADMM, demonstrate that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, and provide empirical evidence of PDMM's superior performance in robust PCA and overlapping group lasso applications.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The dual backward step represents a significant technical contribution that elegantly resolves convergence challenges in parallel updates. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions. The connection between PDMM and existing methods clarifies the relationship among augmented Lagrangian decomposition techniques. The empirical results are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate PDMM's advantages in both convergence speed and runtime efficiency.

However, the paper's claim that Jacobian updates can outperform Gauss-Seidel updates (typically slower) requires deeper justification. While the authors establish convergence rates, they do not sufficiently explain why PDMM's parallel updates overcome the typical convergence slowdown of Jacobian methods. The counterintuitive result merits more detailed theoretical and empirical explanation to strengthen the paper's contribution.

The paper is well-written, clear, and properly contextualized within existing literature. The significance is high, as PDMM provides a theoretically grounded alternative for distributed optimization that enables parallel computation without sacrificing convergence guarantees. The connections to related work are comprehensive, and the experimental evaluation is thorough.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization by providing a theoretically sound and practically effective method for multi-block problems. The minor weakness regarding the counterintuitive convergence behavior does not undermine the paper's strong theoretical and empirical results. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the explanation of the Jacobian convergence advantage in the final version.
