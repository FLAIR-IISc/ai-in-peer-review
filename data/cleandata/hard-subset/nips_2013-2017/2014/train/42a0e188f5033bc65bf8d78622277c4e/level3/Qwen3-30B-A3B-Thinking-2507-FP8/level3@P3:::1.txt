

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates K primal blocks in parallel (randomly selected), uses a backward dual step, and proves sublinear convergence with constant step size. The paper establishes that PDMM generalizes several existing ADMM variants, including sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, and can handle overlapping blocks in a way that RBCD cannot.

The main contribution is a theoretically sound and practically efficient method that addresses key limitations in parallel ADMM approaches. The paper builds on prior work by [2, 10, 14, 17, 26] while making several important improvements. Unlike existing methods that either update blocks sequentially (GSADMM) or randomly but only one block at a time (RBSUMM), PDMM enables parallel updates of multiple blocks with a unified convergence analysis. The introduction of the backward dual step (equation 7) is particularly insightful, explaining why direct Jacobi updates often fail and providing a principled way to control dual updates.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical results with clear convergence guarantees. Theorem 1 establishes global convergence, while Theorem 2 provides the O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size. The authors provide a helpful discussion of how PDMM generalizes existing methods through parameter choices, though this could be expanded as noted below. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of runtime efficiency when parallel updates are possible.

The paper is generally well-written and clear, with a logical structure. The technical content is sound, though several points need clarification:

1. Algorithm steps (5) and (6) are unclear regarding whether updates apply to all coordinates or a single block. The paper should explicitly state whether y^{t+1} and \hat{y}^{t+1} update all dual coordinates or just a single block, and clarify the selection process for index i. This is critical for reproducibility.

2. In Theorem 2's proof, a missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" should be added to ensure correct indexing.

3. The paper should expand Theorem 2 to explicitly show how PDMM's convergence rate connects to known ADMM variants by choosing specific parameters (e.g., K, J). For instance, how does the rate reduce to known results for sADMM (K=J) or PJADMM (η_j>0)?

4. The paper lacks guidance on optimal ways to split the transformation matrix A, which is crucial for practitioners. The authors should provide insights on how to choose block sizes for different problem structures.

5. For tensor completion applications (which have multi-block structure), the paper should provide a comparison or insight into which ADMM variant may be optimal. This would strengthen the practical relevance.

Minor issues include a missing space after "min" in equation (9) and an extra space before "Section" at line 217.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature for distributed and parallel computing. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. The connection between PDMM and existing ADMM variants provides important theoretical insights that advance our understanding of these methods.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims, clearly written with sufficient detail for reproduction, original in its unified framework for multiple ADMM variants, and significant in advancing the state of the art for parallel optimization methods.

The only concerns are the clarifications needed in the algorithm description and the expansion of the theoretical discussion. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the literature.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The authors have made an important contribution to the field of distributed optimization, providing both a theoretically grounded method and practical evidence of its effectiveness. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant gap in the literature on parallel ADMM methods.
