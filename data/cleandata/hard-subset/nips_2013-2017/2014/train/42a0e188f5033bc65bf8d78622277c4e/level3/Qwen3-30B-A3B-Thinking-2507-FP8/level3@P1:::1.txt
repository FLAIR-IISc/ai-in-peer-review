

The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as while two-block ADMM is well-established, generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM randomly updates K blocks in parallel, introduces a dual backward step to ensure convergence, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity under constant step size. The paper demonstrates that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified framework that clarifies the relationship between these methods. The authors also show PDMM can solve overlapping group lasso problems, which is particularly noteworthy as randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks was previously an open problem.

The paper makes significant contributions to the optimization literature. The theoretical results are technically sound, with careful attention to the convergence analysis and step size selection that considers matrix sparsity and number of blocks. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity, which is a substantial improvement over existing randomized methods like RBSUMM that require decreasing step sizes. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods, with PDMM achieving faster convergence rates and better scalability.

The paper's strengths are evident in its technical contribution and empirical validation. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical framework that connects several existing ADMM variants under a single umbrella, demonstrating the flexibility and generality of PDMM. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with the authors carefully addressing the challenges of parallel updates that have plagued previous attempts at multi-block ADMM. The experimental section is thorough and well-justified, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

However, there are some clarity issues that need addressing. The description of Algorithm (5)-(7) is ambiguous regarding whether the dual updates (6)-(7) apply to all dual blocks or a single block, and how index i is selected in these updates. The paper should clarify whether y^{t+1} and \hat{y}^{t+1} update all dual blocks simultaneously or in a block coordinate fashion. Additionally, a missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" in the proof of Theorem 2 requires correction.

The paper could benefit from expanded discussion of Theorem 2's implications. Specifically, the authors should demonstrate how the convergence rate aligns with known ADMM variants by choosing specific parameter values (e.g., K=1, J=2), and provide guidance on optimal ways to split the transformation matrix A for different problem structures. The experimental section should also include a comparison for tensor completion applications, which naturally exhibit multi-block structures, to illustrate which ADMM variant (PDMM, sADMM, or PJADMM) would be optimal in such settings.

The paper adheres to the NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in optimization with applications across machine learning. The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community as it provides both theoretical advances and practical algorithms for large-scale optimization problems. The problem formulation (1) is well-motivated by several important machine learning applications, and the authors effectively connect their method to these applications through the RPCA and overlapping group lasso experiments.

The paper meets all four evaluation criteria at a high level. The quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation. The clarity is generally good, though the algorithm description needs improvement. The originality is significant, as PDMM provides a unified framework that generalizes existing methods and solves the previously open problem of randomized block coordinate descent for overlapping blocks. The significance is substantial, as multi-block optimization is a critical problem in large-scale machine learning with wide-ranging applications.

The paper's main weakness is the unclear description of the dual update steps, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding of the algorithm. The proof error in Theorem 2's proof also needs correction. However, these are fixable issues that do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.

In conclusion, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature with a novel method that generalizes several ADMM variants, establishes strong theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates empirical superiority on important machine learning problems. The minor issues identified are not substantial enough to outweigh the paper's significant contributions. The work is well-positioned to advance the field of large-scale optimization for machine learning applications. I recommend acceptance.
