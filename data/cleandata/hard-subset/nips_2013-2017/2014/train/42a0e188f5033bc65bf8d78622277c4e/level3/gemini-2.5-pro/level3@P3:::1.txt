This paper addresses the important problem of multi-block convex optimization with linear constraints, a formulation common to many machine learning tasks. While the two-block Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is well-understood, its generalization to three or more blocks has faced theoretical and practical challenges, with direct extensions often failing to converge. This work introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM. The key ideas of PDMM are to update a random subset of K primal variable blocks in parallel at each iteration, and to stabilize the algorithm by introducing a "backward step" on the dual variable update. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing an O(1/T) sublinear convergence rate with a constant step size. A significant contribution is showing that PDMM provides a unifying framework that generalizes several existing ADMM variants, including splitting-based ADMM (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM), as special cases. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm for a challenging and highly relevant problem. The combination of randomized parallel primal updates with a carefully controlled dual update (via the backward step) is a clever approach to overcoming the instability of direct Jacobian-style ADMM for multiple blocks. The algorithm is particularly significant for large-scale machine learning, where parallelism is essential.
*   **Quality of Theoretical Contribution:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The paper provides a rigorous proof of global convergence and establishes an O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions. Furthermore, by showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, the work provides valuable new insights into the connections between these methods and clarifies the role of the dual update parameters. This unification is a strong conceptual contribution.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation, situates the work within the existing literature, and methodically presents the algorithm, theory, and experiments.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be addressed to improve its quality and impact.

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the update steps could be more precise. In the algorithm description (equations 5-7), it is not immediately clear how the indices relate. Specifically:
    *   Do the dual updates in (6) and (7) for `y^{t+1}` and `ŷ^{t+1}` happen for all dual blocks `i = 1, ..., I`, or only for a subset related to the selected primal blocks `Jt`? The text states "all dual blocks" are updated, but this should be made explicit in the algorithm's notation.
    *   The relationship between the primal block indices `jt ∈ Jt` and the dual block index `i` needs clarification. Clarifying this would make the algorithm easier for readers to understand and implement correctly.

2.  **Proof Correction:** In the proof for Theorem 2, there appears to be a minor error in the main inequality. A summation term `∑_{i=1}^I` seems to be missing in the numerator of the first term on the right-hand side. The authors should carefully check and correct this.

3.  **Need for Deeper Discussion:** The paper's theoretical contributions could be strengthened with more discussion:
    *   **Connection to Existing Rates:** The paper claims to unify several ADMM variants, which is a great contribution. To make this point more concrete, the analysis of Theorem 2 should be expanded to explicitly show how PDMM's convergence rate recovers the known rates of methods like sADMM and PJADMM when the corresponding parameters (e.g., setting K=J for sADMM) are substituted into the formula.
    *   **Guidance on Block Partitioning:** The performance of PDMM heavily depends on the initial choice of how to partition the constraint matrix `A` into row and column blocks. The paper currently offers no guidance on this crucial step. A discussion on practical strategies for block partitioning based on problem structure would significantly enhance the paper's utility for practitioners.

4.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are well-executed. However, the multi-block structure of PDMM makes it a natural fit for other important problems.
    *   Could the authors provide some discussion or insight on applying PDMM to tensor completion or decomposition? These problems have a natural multi-block structure and represent a challenging and relevant application area. A brief comparison of which ADMM variant (e.g., PDMM1 vs. PDMM with K=J) might be most suitable for such problems would be a welcome addition.

**Minor Typos:**

*   In equation (9), there is a missing space after "min".
*   On line 217, there is an extra space before "Section".

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for large-scale machine learning. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, practical, and supported by a strong theoretical foundation that elegantly unifies several related methods. The weaknesses identified above are primarily related to clarity and the need for more detailed discussion, all of which are addressable in a revision. The strengths of the paper far outweigh these issues.

I am positive about this paper and recommend acceptance.
