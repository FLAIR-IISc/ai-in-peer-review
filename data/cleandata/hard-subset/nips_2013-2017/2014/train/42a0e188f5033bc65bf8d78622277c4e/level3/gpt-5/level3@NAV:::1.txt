This paper proposes PDMM, a parallel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM for linearly constrained, block-separable convex optimization. At each iteration PDMM randomly selects K primal blocks to update in parallel and employs a “backward” dual step that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the quadratic penalty. The step sizes depend on both the degree di of row-block sparsity in A and the number K of updated blocks. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes, show that sADMM and PJADMM arise as limiting cases, and provide empirical evidence on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicating favorable runtime versus GSADMM/RBSUMM and sADMM.

Relation to prior work is clear: the paper positions PDMM among two-block ADMM, multi-block Gauss–Seidel ADMM with back substitution, proximal Jacobian ADMM, and randomized BSUMM, and connects to randomized coordinate descent. The backward dual step and sparsity-aware step sizes help explain why Jacobi-style updates often diverge, offering conceptual value.

Quality: The analysis appears technically sound and the assumptions match standard ADMM settings. However, there are correctable issues: (i) Algorithm clarity—Steps (5)–(7) should explicitly state whether y^{t+1} and ŷ^{t+1} update all dual coordinates at each iteration or a single block, and how the index i is selected; a short pseudocode box would help. (ii) Theorem 2 has a missing “∑_{i=1}^I” that should be added. (iii) The discussion after Theorem 2 should be expanded to demonstrate consistency with known convergence rates by choosing parameters (e.g., K, J) to recover sADMM/PJADMM bounds and to give guidance on how to split A to optimize β_i and step-size choices.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, but notation-dense. Please fix minor typos: add a missing space after “min” in (9) and remove the extra space before “Section” at line 217.

Originality: The backward dual step combined with sparsity- and K-aware step sizes, and the unifying view that includes sADMM and PJADMM as extremes, are novel and insightful.

Significance: Multi-block ADMM remains practically important. The results should interest both theory and systems communities, especially for parallel implementations. It would strengthen the paper to add discussion/experiments for tensor completion (multi-block structure) and indicate which ADMM variant (PDMM vs. sADMM/PJADMM) is preferable there.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical contributions with O(1/T) rate and unifying framework
- Practical, parallelizable algorithm with promising empirical results
- Insightful explanation of Jacobi instability via backward dual step

Cons:
- Ambiguities in algorithm description
- Minor proof/typo issues
- More guidance on parameter choices and matrix splitting needed; missing discussion on tensor completion

Overall recommendation: Accept.
