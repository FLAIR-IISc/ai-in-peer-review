## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate descent method for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors aim to generalize ADMM to multiple blocks in a way that allows for parallel updates and guarantees sublinear convergence. The approach builds upon existing ADMM variants, offering a flexible framework that encompasses sADMM and PJADMM as special cases. Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field, offering a potentially valuable tool for large-scale optimization problems, and I am inclined to accept it.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing multi-block ADMM methods. The proposed PDMM is presented logically, with a strong theoretical foundation including convergence analysis and iteration complexity bounds. The connection drawn between PDMM and existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM is insightful, providing a unified perspective and a better understanding of the dual backward step. The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are promising, demonstrating the efficacy of PDMM compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

However, several areas require clarification. Specifically, steps (5) and (6) of the algorithm are unclear. It's not immediately obvious whether `y^{t+1}` and `ŷ^{t+1}` update *all* coordinates or a single block, and the method for selecting index `i` in the dual update (6) is not specified. Adding this detail would significantly improve clarity. 

Furthermore, while Theorem 2 establishes sublinear convergence, it would be beneficial to expand the discussion to show how these convergence rates relate to those of known ADMM variants (e.g., by carefully choosing K and J). Providing guidance on optimal strategies for splitting the transformation matrix A would also enhance the practical applicability of the method. 

Regarding applications, a discussion regarding tensor completion problems (which inherently have multi-block structure) and an indication of which ADMM variant might be optimal in that context would strengthen the paper.

Finally, there are a few minor typos: a missing space after "min" in equation (9), and an extra space before "Section" on line 217. These are easily remedied.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to multi-block ADMM.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (convergence and iteration complexity).
*   Clear connection to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM).
*   Promising experimental results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in algorithm steps (5) and (6).
*   Limited discussion of convergence rate relationship to existing methods and A splitting strategies.
*   Missing application-specific insights (e.g., tensor completion).
