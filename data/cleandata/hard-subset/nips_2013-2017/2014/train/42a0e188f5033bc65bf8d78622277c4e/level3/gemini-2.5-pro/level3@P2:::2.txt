This paper addresses the problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear equality constraints, a formulation that covers a wide range of problems in machine learning. The central contribution is a novel algorithm called the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM). This method extends the well-known Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) from its standard two-block, sequential (Gauss-Seidel) update scheme to a multi-block, parallel (Jacobian) one. Generalizing ADMM to multiple blocks in a provably convergent parallel manner has been a significant challenge, and this paper makes substantial progress on this front.

The key innovation of PDMM is the introduction of a "dual backward step." The authors insightfully observe that direct parallelization of primal updates can lead to overly aggressive steps on the dual variable, causing divergence. The proposed backward step effectively tempers the dual update, compensating for the fact that parallel primal updates do not incorporate the latest information from each other within the same iteration. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with a constant step size, which is on par with standard ADMM. Furthermore, the work provides a valuable conceptual contribution by showing that existing parallel ADMM variants, such as sADMM and PJADMM, can be viewed as special cases of PDMM. This unifies these methods within a single framework and clarifies their underlying connections. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of ADMM—its difficulty in handling more than two blocks, especially in a parallel setting. The proposed PDMM algorithm, with its novel dual backward step, is a significant and original contribution that allows for provably convergent, randomized, parallel updates across multiple blocks. This opens up new possibilities for designing efficient distributed and parallel optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning problems.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong. The authors provide a proof of global convergence and establish an O(1/T) rate, which is the standard for this class of first-order methods. The analysis is conducted under general assumptions common in the ADMM literature.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** A major strength of the paper is its unification of previous work. By demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases, the authors provide a clearer understanding of the landscape of augmented Lagrangian methods. This connection helps to explain the role of different parameter choices (e.g., exact vs. inexact primal solves) in relation to the dual update step size.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are well-executed and demonstrate that PDMM is competitive with or superior to several state-of-the-art methods, including sADMM, RBSUMM, and in some cases, even the sequential Gauss-Seidel ADMM in terms of wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Jacobian vs. Gauss-Seidel Performance:** The experimental results, particularly in Table 1, show that PDMM can be faster in runtime than GSADMM (a sequential, Gauss-Seidel method). This is somewhat counterintuitive, as Gauss-Seidel methods typically require fewer iterations to converge because they use the most recently updated information at each block update. While a parallel method is expected to be faster on parallel hardware, the experiments here were run sequentially. Could the authors please elaborate on this finding? Is the runtime advantage due to a lower computational cost per iteration (as hinted at for PDMM3), or are there other factors at play? A more detailed discussion of the trade-off between iteration count and per-iteration complexity would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Lack of Parallel Experiments:** The primary motivation for a Jacobian-style method like PDMM is its amenability to parallelization. While the sequential experiments are useful for comparing per-iteration costs, they do not demonstrate the algorithm's main practical advantage. Including even a small-scale experiment on a multi-core machine to showcase the actual speedups from parallel execution would make the paper's contribution significantly more compelling.
*   **Intuition on Step-Size Rules:** The step-size rules for τi and νi in equation (8) are central to the algorithm's convergence. While the formulas are provided, the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why these specific forms, which depend on sparsity (di), the total number of blocks (J), and the number of sampled blocks (K), are the correct choices.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective parallel algorithm for a broad and important class of optimization problems. The dual backward step is a clever and crucial innovation. The work also provides significant conceptual clarification by unifying related methods.
*   **Con:** The empirical advantage over sequential methods is counterintuitive and needs more discussion. The lack of true parallel experiments leaves the main selling point of the algorithm undemonstrated.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is elegant, and its theoretical and empirical validation is solid. While the paper could be improved by providing more intuition on certain results and including parallel experiments, these are not fatal flaws. The work is original, significant, and likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on distributed optimization. I recommend acceptance.
