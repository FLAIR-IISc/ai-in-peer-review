Paper summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes PDMM, a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable convex objectives with linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K primal blocks to update in parallel, and performs a “backward” step on the dual variable to counteract the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. The method incorporates the sparsity of A and the number of updated blocks K into per-row-block dual step sizes (τi) and backward coefficients (νi), and accommodates inexact primal updates via Bregman divergences. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes, and show that sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM—capturing them as extremes of exact vs heavily proximalized primal updates. Empirically, PDMM outperforms several ADMM variants on RPCA and overlapping group lasso. The work builds on and connects to a rich literature on ADMM for multi-block problems (e.g., Deng et al. 2013; He et al. 2012; Hong et al. 2013; Boyd et al. 2011; Richtárik & Takáč 2014), addressing known pitfalls of naive Jacobi multi-block ADMM via the backward dual step.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper is well-motivated, technically solid, unifies several strands of ADMM research, and offers practically useful guidance for parallel randomized block updates.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A clean, parallel randomized framework with a principled backward dual step; per-row-block step sizes adapt to sparsity (di) and K.
- Theory: Global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant steps. Clarifies when sADMM and PJADMM emerge as special cases.
- Scope: Handles nonsmooth objectives and overlapping blocks via an equivalent constrained formulation.
- Empirics: Competitive to superior performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, with insightful ablations on K and (τi, νi).

Weaknesses and clarifications:
- Algorithm clarity: Steps (5)–(7) are ambiguous. Please state explicitly that:
  • The primal update (5) updates only the sampled blocks j ∈ J^t (in parallel).
  • The dual updates (6) and (7) update all dual blocks i = 1,…, I each iteration (unless using the randomized dual-coordinate variant in [25]); make the index ranges explicit and provide a short pseudocode block.
  • Clarify how J^t is sampled (uniform without replacement? partitioned groups?) and whether the sampling is independent across iterations.
- Proof correction: In the proof of Theorem 2, one inequality lacks the outer summation over i. Please add “∑_{i=1}^I” where the bound aggregates the per-row-block residual/dual terms (the context suggests the bound for E[∑ βi ||Ari x̄_T − ai||^2]).
- Theorem 2 discussion: Expand to make the consequences more transparent.
  (a) Show explicit consistency with known rates by parameter choices:
     • K = J, η = 0 recovers sADMM-like behavior (τi = 1/J, νi = 1 − 1/J), with O(1/T) rate.
     • Large η (proximalized primal) allows τi = 1, νi = 0 (PJADMM), also O(1/T) here; comment on the o(1/T) refinement in [10] relative to your constants.
     • K = 1 yields τi = 1/(2J − 1), νi = 0; relate to randomized one-block ADMM variants.
  (b) Offer guidance on splitting A and choosing K:
     • Keep di (the number of nonzeros per row block) small to allow larger τi = K/[K̃i(2J − K)] and smaller νi = 1 − 1/K̃i; this favors partitions that align with sparsity patterns (e.g., per-constraint neighborhoods).
     • Balance per-iteration cost vs rate: larger K improves constants in Theorem 2 but raises per-iteration work; provide a heuristic for choosing K given compute resources and subproblem cost.
     • For overlapping constraints where di ≈ 2 (as in Sec. 2.3), highlight that τi = 0.5 is attainable with K = J, which can be much larger than sADMM’s 1/J.
- Experiments:
  • Parallelism: Results are sequential; a parallel/multicore experiment (or a discussion of expected speedups and communication costs, especially the aggregation Aix) would strengthen the empirical case for “parallel” PDMM.
  • Tensor completion: Please discuss how PDMM would fare for tensor completion/robust tensor PCA with multi-block constraints (e.g., per-mode unfoldings or factor matrices). Which variant (PDMM with moderate K and backward step vs PJADMM-like with large η) is preferable when subproblems require SVDs? Map typical formulations to di to justify expected step sizes and performance.
  • Parameter guidance: Provide practical defaults for (τi, νi) beyond Eq. (8) when ηjt > 0, and a rule-of-thumb for ηjt selection tied to Lipschitz/strong convexity constants.

Quality:
The analysis is careful and, modulo the minor summation fix, appears sound. The backward dual step is well-motivated and the dependence of (τi, νi) on di and K is insightful. The convergence and complexity results match the state-of-the-art O(1/T) rates for related ADMM variants, with clear constants and structural dependence on sparsity.

Clarity:
Generally well-written, but the algorithm description would benefit from explicit index ranges, a compact pseudocode, and a clearer explanation of sampling. The connections to sADMM and PJADMM are valuable; consider adding a small table mapping parameter choices to methods.

Originality:
While related ideas exist (sADMM, PJADMM, RBSUMM), the unifying PDMM framework with randomized parallel updates, backward dual step, and sparsity-aware step sizes is novel and useful. The overlapping-block randomized treatment is also a nice contribution.

Significance:
Likely to be used by practitioners facing multi-block constraints and overlapping structures. The framework clarifies when and how to safely parallelize ADMM-style methods and how to set stepsizes in terms of structural sparsity.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theory with practical algorithmic insights
- Unifies and clarifies relationships among ADMM variants
- Handles overlapping blocks; sparsity-aware stepsizes
- Good empirical performance and ablations

Cons against acceptance:
- Minor correctness/clarity issues (algorithm description; missing summation)
- No parallel runtimes; limited application breadth (no tensor experiments)
