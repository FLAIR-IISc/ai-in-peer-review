- Proposes PDMM: a randomized parallel block-coordinate ADMM with a dual backward step and optional Bregman proximal term for block-separable convex problems with linear constraints.  
- At each iteration PDMM samples K primal blocks, updates them in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian, and applies a shrinking dual update ŷ to stabilize dual ascent.  
- Derives adaptive per-row dual step sizes τi and backward factors νi that depend on K and row-wise block sparsity di.  
- Proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes.  
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases of PDMM, clarifying when small vs large dual steps are appropriate.  
- Demonstrates PDMM on overlapping group-structured problems by reparameterizing with equality constraints to enable randomized updates.  
- Situates PDMM relative to prior ADMM work: provides constant-step convergence and explicit rate vs randomized BSUMM/RBSUMM and unifies PJADMM via the backward step.  
- Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show improvements over sADMM and RBSUMM; GSADMM is sometimes competitive but sequential.  
- Strength: principled and simple dual backward step with clear dependence of τi, νi on K and di.  
- Strength: unifying perspective that recovers sADMM and PJADMM and explains tradeoffs between primal progress and dual step size.  
- Strength: solid theoretical guarantees under standard convex assumptions.  
- Strength: practical relevance—enables randomized parallel updates with overlapping blocks and is easy to implement.  
- Strength: empirical evidence on two representative applications supports claims.  
- Weakness: Algorithm steps ambiguous about whether y and ŷ update all dual coordinates or only selected ones; algorithm box should state this clearly.  
- Weakness: Proof of Theorem 2 appears to miss a summation (∑_{i=1}^I) and needs correction for precision.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2 should include explicit corollaries recovering known ADMM rates for specific K and η choices and quantify τi, νi dependence on di and K.  
- Weakness: Lacks practical guidance on choosing block partition J and K; needs heuristics for tradeoffs between block sizes and di distribution.  
- Weakness: Experiments run sequentially; a parallel runtime study and an ablation on the backward step (vary νi) are needed to demonstrate PDMM’s parallel advantages.  
- Weakness: Does not discuss mapping PDMM to tensor completion/factorization problems or recommend which ADMM variant and K to use there.  
- Weakness: Sensitivity to τi/νi is hinted at; provide guidance or defaults for moderately dense A or highly variable di.  
- Quality: Technical soundness is good; assumptions and rates align with ADMM literature, pending the minor proof fix and update-scope clarification.  
- Clarity: Generally well written but notation-heavy; recommends a concise pseudocode showing sampling, primal aggregation, full dual updates, and Bregman/η role.  
- Minor edits: fix typos (e.g., missing space after “min” in (9); extra space before “Section” near line 217).  
- Originality: Dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes for fully parallel randomized multi-block updates are novel and the unification of sADMM/PJADMM via PDMM is useful.  
- Significance: Fills a gap by delivering stable, convergent, parallel multi-block ADMM with constant steps and rate guarantees, likely impactful for large-scale structured convex problems.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear conceptual contribution, theory and practice, unifying framework, applicability to overlapping groups, and solid experiments.  
- Cons against acceptance: presentation ambiguities, a proof typo, limited parallel runtime and application exploration, and limited guidance on partitioning and parameter selection.  
- Recommendation: Accept, subject to revisions addressing clarity, the proof typo, parallel experiments/ablations, and practical guidance.
