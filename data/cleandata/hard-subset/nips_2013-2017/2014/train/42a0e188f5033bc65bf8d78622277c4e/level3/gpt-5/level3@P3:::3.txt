Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel variant of ADMM for block-separable convex optimization with linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks uniformly at random, updates the selected primal blocks in parallel, and then performs a conservative dual update via a “backward step.” The method also allows adding a proximal (Bregman) term to each selected primal subproblem, which can turn otherwise expensive updates into simple closed-form steps, yielding an inexact but analyzable variant.

A central insight is that fully Jacobi ADMM can implicitly take overly aggressive dual steps (because the augmented term induces an implicit dual ascent), which harms convergence. PDMM counteracts this with a backward dual step ŷ = y − νρ(Ax − a) that makes the subsequent explicit ascent more conservative; the step sizes τi (ascent) and νi (backward) are chosen per dual row block i to exploit both the sparsity pattern of A (via di, the number of nonzeros in row-block i) and the number K of updated primal blocks. The paper shows that two popular Jacobian multi-block ADMM variants are extremes of PDMM: sADMM (small dual steps, ν ≈ 1) and PJADMM (large proximal terms permit full dual steps, ν = 0).

Theoretical results establish global convergence under standard convex assumptions and an ergodic iteration complexity of O(J/(KT)), where J is the total number of primal blocks and K is the number updated per iteration. This matches the O(1/T) rate of ADMM while quantifying how more parallelism (larger K) improves the bound. PDMM also supports randomized updates for overlapping blocks, illustrated by overlapping group lasso.

Empirically, on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM achieves target accuracy faster (in time and/or iterations) than sADMM and RBSUMM, is more flexible than PJADMM (no need for very large proximal terms), and is more parallelizable than GSADMM. A limitation is that experiments are sequential MATLAB implementations; no parallel speedups are reported.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithm is clearly specified, including default τi and νi that depend on K and di. The Lyapunov function and descent lemmas are carefully constructed, yielding global convergence and expected O(1/T) ergodic bounds. The analysis covers both exact and inexact (proximal) updates. The intuition for the backward dual step is compelling and well connected to why naive Jacobi ADMM fails.
- Claims vs. support: The convergence and complexity claims are supported by proofs. The empirical comparisons on two representative tasks are consistent with the claims of faster performance than sADMM and greater flexibility than PJADMM/GSADMM. However, the paper does not include a parallel implementation, so claims about parallel scalability remain qualitative.
- Completeness and care: The paper is a complete piece of work with theory, algorithmic connections (showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases), and experiments. Minor typos aside, the evaluation is honest about conditions and limitations.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The intuition behind the backward step and the dependence of stepsizes on sparsity and K are helpful. The role of η and the Bregman terms is explained with concrete examples.
- Suggestions: Include a compact algorithm box with default parameter choices; provide a short “practical guide” on selecting K, τi, νi, and ηjt. A small glossary for the block notation (di, K̃i, etc.) near the algorithm would further aid readability.

Originality
- The combination of randomized parallel block updates with an explicit backward dual step and block-dependent step sizes that leverage sparsity is novel. The unifying view that recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extremes is insightful. The randomized overlapping-block update capability is also a useful contribution relative to prior RBCD and ADMM variants.

Significance
- Multi-block ADMM with provable convergence and practical parallelizability is important across ML and signal processing. The theoretical guarantees and the simple default stepsize rules make PDMM a promising tool. While the lack of parallel experiments limits immediate practical impact, the contribution is still significant and likely to influence subsequent algorithms and implementations.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear fix (backward dual step) for Jacobi instability, with rigorous analysis.
- Fully parallelizable primal update; step sizes exploit sparsity (di) and K, enabling larger and more effective dual steps than sADMM.
- Flexible proximalization of subproblems; unifies exact/inexact variants.
- Solid convergence theory matching ADMM’s O(1/T) rate; complexity decreases with K as O(J/(KT)).
- Strong empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso; robust to K and demonstrates benefits of larger K.

Weaknesses
- No reported results from an actual parallel implementation (multi-core/distributed/GPU). Claims of wall-clock speedups due to parallelism remain unsubstantiated.
- Three parameters (τi, νi, ηjt) to tune; while defaults are provided, practical guidance and sensitivity analyses are limited compared to standard ADMM.
- Experimental baselines omit an explicit PJADMM implementation; including it would strengthen claims of improved flexibility/performance.
- Theoretical constants (Q, βi, etc.) are not easy to interpret; some guidance on expected magnitudes would help.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide parallel (multi-threaded and/or distributed) experiments to quantify scalability and communication costs, especially the impact of di on synchronization.
- Add an ablation on τi, νi, and ηjt (including adaptive or line-search variants) and a heuristic for choosing K given di and problem size.
- Include PJADMM baseline and report sensitivity to proximal strength to substantiate the “extremes” narrative.
- Clarify the grouping strategy when K does not divide J and its effect on convergence.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and challenging problem with a principled, novel solution.
- Strong and clean theory with intuitive insights; unifies and clarifies relationships among existing methods.
- Empirical results are promising and relevant.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing parallel implementation weakens the main motivation of parallelism.
- Parameter tuning burden and limited guidance may hinder adoption.

Overall assessment
An interesting, well-written contribution with solid theoretical underpinnings and promising empirical evidence. It matches ADMM’s convergence rate while enabling randomized parallelization and larger, better-informed stepsizes. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that adding parallel experiments and practical tuning guidance would substantially strengthen the paper.
