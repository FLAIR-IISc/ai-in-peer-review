Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks uniformly at random and updates their primal variables in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian plus an optional Bregman (proximal) term; it then performs a dual ascent step with a “backward” correction. The backward step tempers the dual’s implicit ascent induced by the quadratic penalty in the primal subproblems, which otherwise causes divergence for Jacobi-style multi-block ADMM. The dual step sizes τi and backward factors νi depend on the number of updated blocks (K) and the row-block sparsity of A through di, allowing larger steps on sparse problems. The authors establish global convergence and an ergodic O(1/T) rate with constants that scale as J/K (so more parallelism yields better constants). They show that two known methods are extremes within their framework: sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting) corresponds to exact primal updates with small dual steps, while PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) corresponds to heavy proximal terms enabling full dual steps. They also show how PDMM yields a randomized scheme for overlapping composite problems (e.g., overlapping group lasso), where classical randomized block coordinate descent is problematic.

The work is well-situated in the literature on ADMM and its multi-block extensions: Gauss–Seidel multi-block ADMM (and back-substitution variants), Jacobian/proximal ADMM, randomized BSUMM (RBSUMM), and RBCD. The paper correctly notes convergence issues of naive multi-block ADMM and the lack of iteration complexity guarantees for some randomized variants.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: the diagnosis of implicit dual ascent explains the divergence of Jacobi ADMM and motivates the backward dual step.
- Generality and flexibility: supports exact and inexact (proximal/Bregman) primal updates; can update any K blocks; accommodates overlapping groups via simple equality constraints.
- Theoretical guarantees: global convergence under standard convex assumptions and O(1/T) ergodic rates with explicit dependence on K and sparsity di. The links to sADMM and PJADMM offer unifying insight.
- Practical step-size rules: τi and νi exploit row sparsity (di) and K, yielding larger steps than sADMM in sparse/overlapping settings.
- Empirical evidence: on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM achieves target accuracy faster than sADMM and RBSUMM, and is competitive with (or faster than) GSADMM in wall-clock time despite GSADMM’s sequential updates.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- No parallel implementation is reported. Claims of parallel scalability are plausible, but speedups and communication/synchronization costs (dual updates require aggregating Ax) are not validated experimentally.
- Parameterization burden: PDMM introduces τi, νi, and ηj. While default rules are provided, practitioners may need guidance on sensitivity and adaptivity. Some tuning is reported; more systematic guidance would help.
- Empirical scope: comparisons omit a tuned PJADMM baseline in the overlapping case, and larger-scale or distributed experiments would strengthen the practical case.
- Presentation: notation is heavy; clearer guidance on how to set τi, νi in practice (e.g., when di varies widely) and a concise algorithm box would improve clarity.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Please clarify implementation details for computing di and K̃i in practice when A is large and sparse, and whether di can change if problem reformulations (e.g., overlapping groups) vary.
- In the inexact variant, the assumptions on ψjt (Lipschitz gradient) and ϕjt (strong convexity) are standard; a short table of recommended choices for common losses/regularizers would be useful.
- The rate constants in Theorem 2 include J/K and βi = J K̃i/K; a short corollary that explicitly states the O(J/(KT)) dependence in suboptimality for the uniform-di case would improve readability.
- For the overlapping group lasso, PDMM uses τi = νi = 0.5 with K = J due to di = 2; please add an ablation showing the effect of using the sADMM step sizes versus the proposed ones.
- Could the backward step be adaptively scaled (e.g., using residual balancing) to further reduce parameter tuning?
- Discuss the cost of forming and maintaining Ax and partial sums across blocks; PDMM relies on reusing sums—this is an important engineering detail for performance.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The algorithm is technically sound with clear derivations and proofs of global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity. The choice of τi, νi is well-motivated by the implicit dual ascent argument and sparsity. Experiments, while sequential, are carefully conducted and support the claims.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with good intuition for the backward step and connections to sADMM/PJADMM. The notation is dense; adding a concise algorithmic summary and a parameter-setting guide would help.
- Originality: The dual backward step within a randomized Jacobi ADMM framework and the explicit exploitation of sparsity to set dual steps are novel and insightful. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM is a nice conceptual contribution.
- Significance: Addresses a well-known limitation of multi-block ADMM and offers a practical, parallelizable alternative with theory. Likely to be used and extended for large-scale convex problems with structure, including overlapping regularization.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a central, timely problem (parallel multi-block ADMM) with a principled fix (backward dual step).
- Solid theory with clear practical implications (larger step sizes on sparse problems; better constants with more parallelism).
- Unifies and clarifies relationships among existing ADMM variants.
- Promising empirical results on representative applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of parallel/distributed implementation and scaling experiments.
- Parameter tuning complexity may hinder adoption without further guidance.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader (e.g., PJADMM baselines and larger data).

Overall evaluation:
An interesting and well-executed contribution that advances our understanding and practice of parallel multi-block ADMM. With minor clarifications and, ideally, added parallel experiments, this work would make a strong addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
