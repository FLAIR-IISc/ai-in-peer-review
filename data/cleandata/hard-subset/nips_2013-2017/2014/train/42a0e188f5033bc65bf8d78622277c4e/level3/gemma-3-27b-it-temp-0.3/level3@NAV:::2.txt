## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core idea is to replace the Gauss-Seidel updates of ADMM with Jacobian (parallel) updates, addressing a known limitation of extending ADMM to multi-block problems. A novel “dual backward step” is introduced to compensate for the limited information propagation inherent in these parallel updates, effectively controlling the dual step size. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates, comparable to ADMM, and interestingly demonstrate that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying framework. Experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show empirical improvements over ADMM and its variants.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in augmented Lagrangian methods and decomposition techniques, referencing key papers from NIPS and related venues (Boyd et al., Candes et al., Hong et al., Wang et al.). It correctly positions PDMM within the landscape of existing multi-block ADMM variants like GSADMM, sADMM, and PJADMM. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates, is a significant contribution, and the clarification of relationships between different methods is valuable. The experimental validation on standard problems provides initial evidence of PDMM’s effectiveness.

However, the central claim – that parallel Jacobian updates can outperform sequential Gauss-Seidel updates – is somewhat counterintuitive, as Jacobian methods typically suffer from slower convergence due to reduced information propagation. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* PDMM overcomes this limitation. The role of the dual backward step is crucial here, and while explained, a more intuitive explanation of its impact on convergence would be helpful. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM is well-established, but the practical implications of this relationship – i.e., when to choose PDMM over these simpler alternatives – could be further explored. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PDMM algorithm and the dual backward step are novel contributions.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Unifying Framework:** Demonstrating sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM is insightful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experiments show promising results on relevant problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Counterintuitive Result:** The improved performance of parallel updates requires more detailed explanation.
*   **Practical Guidance:** Limited discussion on when to prefer PDMM over existing methods.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The sensitivity of performance to parameter settings (particularly K) could be explored further.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with potential for significant impact in distributed optimization.
*   The unifying framework provided by PDMM is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The experimental results, while preliminary, are encouraging.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The counterintuitive nature of the main result requires more thorough justification.
*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of practical considerations and parameter tuning.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the counterintuitive result and provide more practical guidance for using PDMM. A more intuitive explanation of the dual backward step would also be beneficial. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization and has the potential to be highly impactful.
