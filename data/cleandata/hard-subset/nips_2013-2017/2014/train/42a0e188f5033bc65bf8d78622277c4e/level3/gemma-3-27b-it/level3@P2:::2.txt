This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to extend the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks by adopting a parallel, randomized block coordinate update scheme, reminiscent of Jacobian ADMM, but incorporating a novel "dual backward step" to improve convergence. The authors establish global convergence with an O(1/T) iteration complexity and demonstrate through experiments on Robust PCA and overlapping group lasso that PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

The paper builds directly upon the well-established foundation of ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011] and its extensions to multi-block problems [He et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016]. It correctly identifies the challenges of generalizing ADMM to multiple blocks, particularly the potential for divergence when employing purely parallel (Jacobian) updates. The proposed dual backward step is a clever attempt to address this issue, effectively controlling the dual variable update and preventing overly aggressive progress. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM [Wang et al., 2017], and PJADMM [Deng et al., 2016] – demonstrating the former encompasses the latter as special cases – is a valuable contribution to understanding the landscape of augmented Lagrangian decomposition methods.  The authors appropriately reference related work and contextualize their contribution within the broader literature.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical rigor and practical relevance. The convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate, is reassuring. The clear explanation of the dual backward step and its motivation is well-presented. The experimental results, though limited to two applications, provide empirical evidence supporting the method’s efficacy. The extension to overlapping block problems is a notable addition, addressing a scenario where standard block coordinate descent struggles. The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written, though some notation could benefit from more explicit definition (e.g., the meaning of 'K̃i').

However, there are some weaknesses. The experimental section lacks a detailed comparison of computational costs per iteration. While PDMM is shown to converge faster in terms of iteration count, a comparison of runtime per iteration would be informative, especially given the parallel nature of the method. The statement that the results are counterintuitive because Jacobian methods *typically* converge slower than Gauss-Seidel is accurate and raises a critical question that, while addressed in the discussion of the dual backward step, could be more thoroughly investigated. A more detailed analysis of how the step sizes (τi, νi) impact performance in different scenarios would strengthen the paper.  The choice of parameters for the comparison algorithms (e.g., RBSUMM) isn’t fully detailed, making it harder to assess the fairness of the comparison. Finally, the writing sometimes leans towards technical jargon without sufficient explanation for readers less familiar with the ADMM literature. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The dual backward step is a genuinely novel contribution that addresses a key challenge in extending ADMM to multiple blocks.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis provides strong theoretical guarantees for the method.
*   **Practical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that PDMM outperforms existing methods on relevant benchmark problems.
*   **Generality:** The method can handle overlapping block problems, expanding its applicability.
*   **Clarification of Relationships:** Demonstrating sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM enhances understanding of the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to two applications.
*   **Lack of Per-Iteration Cost Analysis:** The runtime comparison would benefit from analyzing computational cost per iteration, given the parallel nature of the algorithm.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Further analysis of parameter sensitivity, specifically τi and νi, is needed.
*   **Occasional Technical Jargon:** The writing style could be more accessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. The novelty of the dual backward step and the clarification of relationships between different ADMM variants are particularly noteworthy. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically concerning a more detailed analysis of computational costs and parameter sensitivity within the experimental section.  Addressing these points would further solidify the paper’s impact and contribution.
