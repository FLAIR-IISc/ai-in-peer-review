This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key challenge in extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, where convergence is not guaranteed with naive parallel updates. PDMM introduces a randomized block coordinate approach with a backward dual step and provides convergence guarantees under mild assumptions, achieving a sublinear convergence rate of O(1/T). The work also establishes connections to existing ADMM variants like sADMM and PJADMM, positioning PDMM as a unifying framework. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in large-scale optimization and building upon established work in the field.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The theoretical results, including convergence analysis, are presented with sufficient detail (with full proofs in the supplement). The assumptions made are standard for ADMM-type methods. The experimental results, though limited in scope, provide some evidence supporting the efficacy of PDMM compared to other methods. The authors acknowledge potential limitations and directions for future work. The paper is a complete piece of work and demonstrates careful consideration of both strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation and overview of related work. However, steps (5) and (6) of the PDMM algorithm are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is not immediately obvious whether `y^{t+1}` and `\hat{y}^{t+1}` update all coordinates or only the coordinates corresponding to the randomly selected blocks `J_t`. Additionally, the selection of the index `i` in step (6) is not explicitly explained—is it iterating through all rows of A, or a subset? Clarifying these points would significantly improve readability.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel combination of ideas – randomized block coordinate descent, a backward dual step, and a unifying framework for different ADMM variants. While the core building blocks (ADMM, randomized coordinate descent) are not new, the PDMM algorithm and its theoretical analysis represent a significant contribution. The connection established between PDMM, sADMM and PJADMM, providing a more holistic understanding of these methods, further strengthens the originality of the work.

**Significance:** The proposed PDMM algorithm has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications involving large-scale, block-separable optimization problems. The demonstrated performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso suggests its practical applicability. The theoretical results, establishing convergence and iteration complexity, provide a solid foundation for further research and development. The generalizability to overlapping blocks is also a significant advantage. The potential for parallelization further enhances the significance of the work, especially in modern computing environments.

**Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Algorithm Clarity:** As mentioned above, clarify steps (5) and (6). Specify whether `y^{t+1}` and `\hat{y}^{t+1}` are updated over all coordinates or just those related to the selected blocks. Explain how index `i` is chosen in step (6).
* **Proof Correction:** In Theorem 2's proof, a missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" should be added.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** Theorem 2 would benefit from further discussion. (a) Elaborate on how the convergence rates of known ADMM variants can be recovered by appropriately choosing the parameters K and J. (b) Provide guidance on how to optimally split the transformation matrix A to maximize the performance of PDMM, considering the sparsity structure of A.
* **Experimental Evaluation:**  The paper would be strengthened by providing a comparison or insight regarding tensor completion applications. Tensor completion naturally leads to multi-block structures. Discuss which ADMM variant (including PDMM) might be optimal in this setting and why.
* **Minor Typos:** Correct the missing space after "min" in equation (9). Remove the extra space before "Section" on line 217.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to extending ADMM to multiple blocks.
* Strong theoretical guarantees (convergence and iteration complexity).
* Promising experimental results on relevant applications.
* Clear connection to existing ADMM variants.
* Potential for significant impact in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Algorithm steps (5) and (6) lack sufficient clarity.
* Limited experimental evaluation.
* Further theoretical discussion could strengthen the results.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I am inclined to accept this paper. While there are minor issues regarding clarity and a desire for more extensive experimental evaluation, the technical contributions, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work are significant. Addressing the points raised above in a revision would further strengthen the paper and make it an even more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
