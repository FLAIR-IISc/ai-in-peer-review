This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core idea is to replace the Gauss-Seidel updates of standard ADMM with parallel, randomized Jacobian updates. A novel “dual backward step” is introduced to compensate for the limited information propagation inherent in these parallel updates. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates, demonstrate connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, and provide empirical validation on RPCA and overlapping group lasso problems.

The paper tackles a significant problem in optimization – scaling ADMM to multi-block scenarios. While ADMM’s success with two blocks is well-established, generalizing it effectively to more blocks remains challenging. The proposed PDMM offers a compelling approach, and the theoretical results providing convergence guarantees are valuable. The clarification of the relationships between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a notable contribution, offering a unifying perspective on these related decomposition methods. The experimental results provide evidence of PDMM’s effectiveness, though, as the reviewer noted, the outcomes are somewhat counterintuitive.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The Jacobian-based parallel updates in PDMM, combined with the dual backward step, represent a novel contribution to multi-block ADMM methods.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Establishing O(1/T) convergence rates provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Unifying Framework:** Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM clarifies the landscape of augmented Lagrangian decomposition methods and enhances understanding of the dual backward step's role.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso showcase PDMM’s competitive performance.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it accessible to researchers familiar with ADMM. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Counterintuitive Results:** The reviewer’s point about the counterintuitive convergence behavior – Jacobian methods typically converging slower than Gauss-Seidel – is crucial. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth explanation of *why* PDMM overcomes this expectation. The current explanation (dual backward step) feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks sufficient intuition.  A more detailed analysis, possibly linking the step size selection to the spectral properties of the problem, would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates convergence with constant step sizes, the optimal step size selection (τi, νi) appears somewhat complex and problem-dependent. A discussion on practical guidelines for choosing these parameters in different scenarios would be valuable.  The experiments only show the best-performing parameter settings; sensitivity analysis is missing.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to two applications. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a broader range of problem instances would strengthen the claims of PDMM’s general effectiveness.
* **Lack of Comparison to First-Order Methods:** It's unclear how PDMM compares to standard first-order methods (e.g., FISTA) *without* block decomposition.  Such a comparison could provide further context for its performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in distributed optimization: scaling ADMM to multiple blocks.
* The proposed PDMM method is novel and theoretically sound.
* The unifying framework connecting PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a valuable contribution.
* The empirical results, while needing further exploration, demonstrate promising performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The counterintuitive convergence behavior requires a more thorough explanation.
* The parameter sensitivity and limited scope of experiments raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
*  The absence of comparison to baseline first-order methods hinders comprehensive assessment.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the experimental results are promising. However, the counterintuitive convergence behavior and limited experimental scope warrant further investigation. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  The authors should focus on providing a more intuitive explanation for PDMM’s convergence properties, conducting a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis of the parameters, and expanding the empirical evaluation to a wider range of problems.  Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
