This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel extension of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly select a subset of blocks at each iteration, update them in parallel, and employ a backward step in the dual update to promote stability and convergence. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for global convergence and establish an iteration complexity of O(J/(T K)), where J is the total number of blocks, T is the number of iterations, and K is the number of randomly selected blocks per iteration. Empirical evaluations on robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso demonstrate promising performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods like sADMM and RBSUMM.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in ADMM and its variants. The authors correctly position PDMM within the landscape of existing multi-block ADMM methods, differentiating it from Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM), Jacobian variants (like sADMM and PJADMM), and randomized block coordinate methods like RBSUMM. They highlight the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the potential for non-convergence in multi-block settings and the challenges associated with scaling to large problems. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is particularly insightful, revealing that these methods can be viewed as special cases within the broader PDMM framework. The authors appropriately cite relevant work, including foundational papers on ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011], proximal algorithms [Parikh & Boyd, 2014], and related techniques for sparse optimization [Chen et al., 2008].

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the proposed PDMM algorithm offers a compelling approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems, a crucial aspect for tackling large-scale applications. The randomized block selection and the backward dual step appear to be key innovations that address the convergence issues often encountered in parallel ADMM implementations. The theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity, provides a strong foundation for understanding the algorithm's behavior. The empirical results, while limited to two applications, are encouraging and demonstrate PDMM's potential to outperform existing methods in terms of both runtime and accuracy. The clear and concise writing style enhances the paper's readability and makes the technical details accessible to a broad audience. The authors adequately address the challenges and limitations of their approach.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis provides convergence guarantees, the assumptions required for these guarantees (Assumption 1) should be discussed further in relation to practical applications. It would be beneficial to provide guidance on how to verify these assumptions in real-world datasets. The empirical evaluation, although promising, lacks a comprehensive comparison with a wider range of competing methods, particularly those specifically designed for RPCA and overlapping group lasso. Furthermore, the paper does not present results for a genuinely *parallel* implementation of PDMM. The experiments are conducted sequentially, which limits the assessment of the algorithm's scalability and performance gains in a truly parallel computing environment. The authors acknowledge that tuning three parameters in PDMM might be less convenient than traditional ADMM, and demonstrating the sensitivity of performance to these parameters could have strengthened the analysis. Additionally, the notation, while generally clear, could benefit from more consistent use of subscripts and superscripts.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing ADMM.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in solving large-scale optimization problems.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for global convergence and iteration complexity.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating improved performance on two relevant applications.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the algorithm and its analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a parallel implementation demonstrating scalability.
*   Limited empirical evaluation compared to a broader range of competing methods.
*   Potential sensitivity to parameter tuning, requiring further investigation.
*   Assumptions for theoretical guarantees may not always hold in practice.



**Overall assessment:** This is a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, presenting a well-motivated and theoretically grounded algorithm for parallel ADMM. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. The potential for significant performance gains in large-scale applications makes PDMM a promising technique for further research and development. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the comments regarding parallel implementation, broader empirical evaluation, and discussion of the theoretical assumptions.
