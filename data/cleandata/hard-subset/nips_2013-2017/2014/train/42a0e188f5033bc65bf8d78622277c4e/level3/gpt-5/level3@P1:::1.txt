This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives subject to linear constraints. PDMM generalizes ADMM to the multi-block setting by (i) randomly selecting K primal blocks to update in parallel, (ii) introducing a backward step on the dual variable to temper the implicit dual ascent inherent in the augmented Lagrangian’s quadratic term, and (iii) choosing dual step sizes that depend explicitly on the number of updated blocks and the sparsity structure of A via di, the number of nonzero blocks in each row block. The method admits exact and inexact variants using Bregman linearization, shows that sADMM (two-block with splitting variables) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are limiting cases of PDMM, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) ergodic rate under standard convexity assumptions. Empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show favorable performance against GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work: Multi-block ADMM remains challenging; GS variants can diverge and typically require safeguard steps, while Jacobian variants often rely on strong proximal regularization or restrictive conditions. PDMM contributes a principled randomized, parallel Jacobian framework with constant steps and a unifying perspective connecting sADMM and PJADMM through the dual backward step. It also addresses overlapping groups—an area where randomized BCD remains subtle—by reformulating with auxiliary variables and constraints and exploiting di = 2 for larger dual steps.

Quality:
- Theory is largely sound and matches the stated goals. The backward dual step is well-motivated by the “implicit dual ascent” observation, and the step-size rules τi = K/[K̃i(2J−K)] and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i sensibly incorporate both K and sparsity via di. The convergence and O(1/T) rate under constant steps are standard and useful.
- The inexact PDMM framework using Bregman linearization is technically clean and practical.
- One correction: in the proof of Theorem 2, an outer summation over i is missing in a displayed expression. Please add the missing “∑_{i=1}^I” as appropriate.
- Suggest strengthening the theory by adding corollaries that recover known ADMM rates under specific parameter selections, e.g., K = J with dense A (di = J) gives τi = 1/J and νi = 1 − 1/J (sADMM), while large proximal terms with νi = 0 and τi = 1 reduce to PJADMM. This will help readers verify consistency with prior results.

Clarity:
- Algorithmic steps (5)–(7) need clarification. Do y^{t+1} and ŷ^{t+1} update all dual blocks i = 1,…,I each iteration, or only those coupled to the selected primal blocks? The text later says all dual blocks are updated, but the notation could be made explicit. Also please state clearly how indices i are selected and whether dual updates can be performed in a block-coordinate manner (or if that is reserved for the extended work).
- Notation mixes Acj, Ajt, and Ari; consider a small table of symbols for row/column blocks and block indices.
- Minor typos: add a space after “argmin” in (9); remove the extra space before “Section” at line 217. There are a few punctuation/spelling nits (e.g., “addded”, “convegence”).

Originality:
- The dual backward step combined with sparsity-/K-aware dual steps is a novel, insightful stabilization of Jacobian multi-block ADMM. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as extremes of PDMM is conceptually valuable. Allowing randomized parallel updates for overlapping groups in a principled way adds to the contribution.

Significance:
- Multi-block constrained problems are common in ML; providing a parallelizable, scalable algorithm with convergence guarantees is impactful. The empirical results are promising, though stronger evidence of wall-clock speedups in actual parallel settings (beyond sequential Matlab) would further increase significance.

Suggestions and questions:
- Expand the discussion around Theorem 2 with guidance on splitting A: practitioners need heuristics to choose blockings that balance per-iteration cost and convergence speed. For example: (a) favor partitions minimizing max_i di to increase τi; (b) merge tightly coupled variables when the induced subproblems remain tractable; (c) balance block sizes to keep linear algebra costs similar across blocks. A short empirical sensitivity study would be helpful.
- Provide explicit corollaries demonstrating that PDMM recovers the rates/behaviors of sADMM and PJADMM under the stated parameter choices. This will make the “PDMM as a unifier” message more concrete.
- Tensor completion: This is a natural multi-block application (e.g., low-rank tensor models with separable regularizers or unfolding-based constraints). Please discuss how to form A and blocks for CP/Tucker formulations, typical di in those designs, and which PDMM specialization (exact/inexact; K; τi, νi) you expect to work best. A brief comparison or at least a reasoned recommendation versus sADMM/PJADMM in that setting would be valuable (e.g., when proximal terms are needed to keep subproblems simple, PJADMM-like PDMM; when unfoldings are sparse per row block, exploit small di for larger τi).
- Experiments: Since GSADMM runs sequentially and PDMM is parallelizable, consider reporting simulated parallel runtimes (or actual multicore runs) to substantiate the claimed scalability. Also include an ablation isolating the backward step: compare PDMM with νi = 0 vs. νi per (8) to demonstrate its stabilizing effect.

Pros:
- Well-motivated algorithm that unifies and extends multi-block ADMM variants
- Solid convergence analysis with constant steps; clear dependence on sparsity and K
- Practical inexact updates via Bregman linearization
- Good empirical performance on RPCA and overlapping group lasso

Cons:
- Some ambiguity in algorithm description (dual updates and indexing)
- Missing summation in Theorem 2 proof; minor typos
- Limited guidance on block design/splitting; no tensor completion study
- Experiments are sequential; parallel speedups not demonstrated

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a meaningful advance in multi-block ADMM with both theoretical and practical contributions. Addressing the clarity issues, the minor proof fix, and adding guidance/case discussion (especially for tensor problems) would further strengthen the final version.
