- Proposes PDMM: a randomized, parallel, multi-block ADMM variant updating K randomly chosen blocks in parallel with augmented Lagrangian + optional Bregman proximal terms and a dual “backward” correction.
- Chooses dual ascent and backward step sizes τi, νi as functions of K and per-row degrees di to enable larger steps on sparse problems.
- Proves global convergence in expectation and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes; bounds scale as O(J/(K T)).
- Unifies prior multi-block ADMM variants: sADMM and PJADMM appear as extreme cases, explaining when full dual steps are safe.
- Analysis handles overlapping blocks via constrained reformulation and provides concrete step-size examples (e.g., τi = 0.5 for di = 2).
- Empirical results: achieves target accuracy faster (CPU seconds) than sADMM and RBSUMM and is competitive or better than GSADMM on RPCA and overlapping group lasso.
- Experiments were all sequential; no demonstrated parallel speedups, communication costs, or scalability results.
- Algorithm is clearly specified, unifying exact and inexact updates via Bregman divergences; dual backward step is well-motivated by analysis.
- Proof sketches are coherent though heavy; full proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Notation is dense; a compact algorithm box would aid readability.
- Assumptions for inexact PDMM are stated, but practical guidance on choosing ηjt, φjt, and ψjt is limited.
- Originality: moderately high — novel randomized Jacobian-style multi-block method with sparsity-aware dual steps and a principled backward correction.
- Significance: high practical potential for large-scale constrained problems given safe parallel updates and interpretable step-size rules.
- Strength: solid theoretical guarantees and interpretable, sparsity-leveraging step-size design.
- Strength: conceptual clarification connecting and explaining sADMM and PJADMM trade-offs.
- Strength: addresses overlapping-group problems with randomized updates, filling a gap in RBCD literature.
- Weakness: multiple hyperparameters (ρ, τi, νi, ηjt) require tuning and lack extensive sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: limited empirical scope (two applications) and absence of ablation studies on K, ηjt, and the backward step.
- Weakness: comparisons could be strengthened with fair wall-clock tuning and more baseline variants.
- Suggestion: provide a parallel/multi-core or distributed implementation with scaling and communication analysis.
- Suggestion: include sensitivity studies, default heuristics for hyperparameters, and ablation isolating the backward step’s impact.
- Suggestion: expand experiments to more datasets/problem classes and report memory/throughput metrics.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, principled and parallelizable method, strong theory, unifies prior work, and promising empirical gains.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing parallel scaling evidence, tuning burden, and relatively narrow experimental validation.
- Overall: a well-executed, insightful contribution that would be strengthened by parallel experiments and more practical guidance.
