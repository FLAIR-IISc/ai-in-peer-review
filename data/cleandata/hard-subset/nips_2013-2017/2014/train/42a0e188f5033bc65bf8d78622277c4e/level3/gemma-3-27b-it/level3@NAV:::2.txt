## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. The core idea is to replace the Gauss-Seidel updates of ADMM with Jacobian (parallel) updates, addressing a known issue with extending ADMM to multi-block problems. A key innovation is the introduction of a dual backward step, designed to compensate for the limited information propagation inherent in parallel Jacobian updates, effectively controlling the dual step size. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates, matching ADMM’s performance, and importantly, demonstrate that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, offering a unifying perspective. Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso showcase PDMM’s effectiveness.

The paper builds upon the extensive work on ADMM and its variants (references [2, 10, 14, 15, 17, 26] are relevant). It acknowledges the difficulties in generalizing ADMM to multiple blocks and positions PDMM as a solution. The connection drawn between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM is a valuable contribution, clarifying the relationship between these algorithms. The theoretical convergence analysis is a strong point, assuring the method’s soundness. The experimental evaluation provides supporting evidence of PDMM’s performance.

A significant strength lies in the novel dual backward step, which appears crucial for achieving convergence under more general conditions, particularly when the parallel updates might otherwise lead to divergence. The paper convincingly argues that this step addresses the information propagation issue. PDMM’s potential for enabling alternatives and possibilities for distributed optimization is also notable.

However, the reported empirical results are somewhat counterintuitive. The authors acknowledge that Jacobian methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel, yet PDMM demonstrably outperforms ADMM and related methods. While the experiments offer evidence, the *reason* for this unexpected behavior isn't fully elucidated. A more detailed explanation, perhaps linking it to the specific problem structures (RPCA, overlapping group lasso) and the impact of the dual backward step, would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the discussion of parameter tuning could be expanded. While the authors mention choosing parameters according to (8), the sensitivity to these parameters and the practical considerations for selecting K in different scenarios aren’t thoroughly addressed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to multi-block ADMM with theoretical guarantees.
*   Insightful connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM).
*   Empirically effective performance on challenging applications.
*   The dual backward step is a significant and potentially broadly applicable innovation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Counterintuitive empirical results require more detailed explanation.
*   Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical considerations.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. While a deeper exploration of the empirical results and parameter tuning would be beneficial, the theoretical soundness, novelty, and demonstrated effectiveness of PDMM make it a worthy candidate for acceptance.
