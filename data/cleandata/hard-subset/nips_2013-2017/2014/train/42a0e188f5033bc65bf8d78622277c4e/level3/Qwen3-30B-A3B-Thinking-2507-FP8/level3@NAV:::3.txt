

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel ADMM variant that updates K randomly selected blocks per iteration. The key innovation lies in the backward dual step, which ensures conservative dual updates crucial for global convergence, and the ability to incorporate proximal terms to simplify subproblems. The authors establish global convergence and O(J/(TK)) iteration complexity, with theoretical guarantees that match ADMM's convergence rate while enabling parallelization.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions by positioning PDMM as a unifying framework that encompasses sADMM and PJADMM as special cases. This connection provides valuable insights into the role of dual backward steps. The empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are compelling, showing PDMM achieves target accuracy with less computation time than competing methods, particularly when using larger step sizes.

Regarding quality, the theoretical analysis is technically sound with comprehensive convergence proofs, though the absence of parallel implementation results is a notable gap. The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections, though the parameter tuning complexity (three parameters to adjust) is a practical drawback compared to traditional ADMM. The originality is strong, as PDMM addresses an important gap in multi-block ADMM generalizations with a novel randomized parallel approach that handles overlapping blocks effectively. The significance is high, as the method enables efficient parallelization for large-scale optimization problems in machine learning.

The main weaknesses are the lack of reported parallel implementation results, which undermines the claimed parallelizability advantage, and the parameter tuning complexity that may hinder practical adoption. The paper effectively addresses related work but could better discuss how PDMM compares to other parallel ADMM variants beyond the mentioned methods.

Overall, this is a well-executed contribution that makes a meaningful advancement in parallel optimization. The theoretical and empirical evidence is solid, and the method offers a promising path for scalable optimization. While the missing parallel implementation results are a concern, the paper's technical quality, clarity, and significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The authors have clearly demonstrated a novel approach that advances the state of the art in parallel optimization algorithms.
