The paper "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers" proposes a novel optimization algorithm, PDMM, for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The authors replace the Gauss-Seidel primal updates in ADMM with Jacobian updates, which enables parallelization and improves convergence rates. A key innovation is the introduction of a dual backward step that compensates for the limited information propagation in Jacobian updates, effectively reducing the dual step size.

The authors establish the global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity of PDMM with constant step size, similar to ADMM. They also show that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, clarifying the relationships among augmented-Lagrangian decomposition methods. Experimental results demonstrate PDMM's empirical effectiveness compared to ADMM and other variants in two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

The reviewer finds the results counterintuitive because Jacobian methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel, and requests elaboration. The authors' response highlights the importance of the dual backward step in achieving convergence under general conditions. The reviewer notes that PDMM provides additional alternatives and possibilities for distributed optimization.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is a complete piece of work, not merely a position paper.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results. However, some minor suggestions for improvement could be made. For example, the notation could be more clearly defined, and some of the technical proofs could be included in the main text rather than the supplement.

Originality: The problems and approaches are new, and the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions and adequately reference related work. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance: The results are important, and other researchers and practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build upon them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The authors provide unique data and conclusions on existing data, as well as a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach.
