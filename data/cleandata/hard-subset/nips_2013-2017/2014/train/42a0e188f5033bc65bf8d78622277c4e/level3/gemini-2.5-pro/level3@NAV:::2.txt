This paper addresses the challenging problem of extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting for block-separable convex optimization. This is a well-known problem in the optimization and machine learning communities, with prior work often focusing on sequential (Gauss-Seidel) updates or struggling with the convergence of parallel (Jacobian) schemes. The authors propose the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which cleverly enables parallel updates for multiple primal blocks. The key innovation is a novel dual backward step, designed to compensate for the limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian updates.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Quality & Originality:** The introduction of the dual backward step is a novel and theoretically sound contribution. It provides a mechanism to control the dual ascent, preventing the divergence issues that typically plague direct parallel extensions of ADMM. The theoretical analysis is solid, establishing an O(1/T) convergence rate, which matches that of the classic two-block ADMM.
**Significance:** This work is highly significant for a few reasons. First, it provides a principled and convergent parallel algorithm for a wide class of problems, offering a valuable new tool for distributed optimization. Second, the authors make an important conceptual contribution by showing that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. This unification clarifies the relationships within the family of augmented Lagrangian methods. The empirical evaluation on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso further demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, there is one key point that could be clarified to strengthen the paper.
**Clarity:** The achievement of an O(1/T) convergence rate is somewhat counterintuitive. Methods based on Jacobian updates, which use "older" information from the previous iteration for parallel blocks, typically converge slower than their Gauss-Seidel counterparts, which use the most recently updated information. While the proofs in the supplement likely validate this claim, the main paper would benefit from a more detailed intuitive explanation of how the dual backward step precisely compensates for the lack of sequential updates to retain the same convergence rate as standard ADMM. This would help the reader better appreciate the mechanism's power.

In summary, this is a strong and well-executed paper presenting a novel, theoretically-backed, and practically useful algorithm. The unification of prior methods is an excellent contribution. Addressing the intuition behind the convergence rate would further improve its impact.
