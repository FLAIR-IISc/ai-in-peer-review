## Review of Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems subject to linear constraints. The authors address a known challenge in extending the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks: the potential for divergence when using parallel (Jacobian) updates, as opposed to the sequential (Gauss-Seidel) updates common in two-block ADMM.  PDMM tackles this issue by introducing a novel "dual backward step" that adjusts the dual variable update, effectively reducing the step size and promoting convergence. The paper establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity and demonstrates, through experimental results on Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso, that PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods. 

The work builds upon a significant body of research in ADMM and its variants.  The authors correctly position their contribution within the existing literature, citing key papers on ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011], its extensions to multiple blocks [Chen et al., 2013; Deng et al., 2016; He et al., 2015], and related methods like BSUMM [Hong et al., 2016] and RBSUMM [Hong et al., 2016]. Importantly, the paper highlights the theoretical limitations of some of these existing approaches, particularly regarding convergence guarantees and iteration complexity.  The connection made between PDMM, sADMM [Wang et al., 2016], and PJADMM [Deng et al., 2016] is a significant contribution, providing a unifying framework for understanding these methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The dual backward step is a genuinely innovative idea. It directly addresses the convergence issues that can arise when using parallel updates in multi-block ADMM, a problem identified in prior work [Chen et al., 2013].
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide a rigorous analysis of PDMM, establishing global convergence and proving an O(1/T) iteration complexity, which is comparable to that of ADMM. 
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The demonstration that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM clarifies their relationship and provides a more comprehensive understanding of these algorithms. This is a valuable contribution for researchers working in this area.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate that PDMM performs well in practice, outperforming other ADMM variants. The inclusion of these practical evaluations is crucial for demonstrating the usefulness of the proposed method.
* **Potential for Parallelism:** The inherent parallelizability of PDMM makes it well-suited for large-scale optimization problems, a key advantage in modern machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper provides guidelines for setting parameters (τi, νi), the sensitivity of PDMM’s performance to these parameters could be further investigated.  A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies in different application contexts would be beneficial.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The notation and some of the theoretical explanations are dense and could benefit from greater clarity, especially regarding the derivation of the step sizes τi and νi.  Providing more intuitive explanations of these choices would enhance readability.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to two applications. Expanding the evaluation to other problem domains would strengthen the evidence for PDMM’s general applicability.
* **Comparison with First-Order Methods:** A comparison with state-of-the-art first-order methods (e.g., proximal gradient methods) for solving the considered problems would help to better assess the advantages and disadvantages of PDMM.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm addresses a crucial challenge in extending ADMM to multiple blocks and provides a practical and theoretically sound solution. The novelty of the dual backward step, the rigorous analysis, and the empirical validation all contribute to the high quality of this work.  While some areas could benefit from further elaboration and experimentation, the paper is well-written and presents a compelling case for the effectiveness of PDMM.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important open problem in the field of distributed optimization.
* The proposed algorithm, PDMM, is novel and theoretically well-justified.
* The experimental results demonstrate that PDMM outperforms existing methods.
* The connection between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM provides valuable insights.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental evaluation.
* Some aspects of the theoretical analysis and parameter tuning could be clarified.




**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should consider addressing the clarity concerns and expanding the experimental evaluation to further strengthen the paper.
