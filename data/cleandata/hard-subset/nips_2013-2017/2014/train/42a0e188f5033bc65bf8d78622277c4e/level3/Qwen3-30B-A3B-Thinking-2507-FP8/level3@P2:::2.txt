

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel approach for solving optimization problems with block-separable convex objectives and linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM introduces a parallel randomized block coordinate method that updates multiple blocks simultaneously, contrasting with the Gauss-Seidel approach of standard ADMM.

PDMM's key innovation is a dual backward step that compensates for the limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian (parallel) updates. By introducing an intermediate variable ŷ^t, PDMM achieves conservative dual progress, enabling convergence under general conditions. The paper establishes that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying framework that clarifies their relationships. This theoretical connection is particularly valuable as it explains the role of dual backward steps in these methods.

The paper makes strong theoretical contributions, establishing global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size. The authors carefully consider the sparsity of constraint matrices and the number of randomized blocks to determine step sizes, making PDMM adaptable to various problem structures. The theoretical analysis is thorough and builds on established results in the ADMM literature, including the work of Boyd et al. (2011) and Hong et al. (2013).

Empirically, PDMM demonstrates significant advantages over existing methods in two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experiments show that PDMM converges faster than ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, with the fastest variants (PDMM1, PDMM2) outperforming others in runtime. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of how the number of blocks (K) affects performance, showing that PDMM can achieve speedups by updating multiple blocks in parallel without sacrificing convergence guarantees.

Strengths:
1. The dual backward step is a clever and novel solution to the convergence challenges of parallel ADMM variants.
2. The theoretical connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable insights into the design space of augmented Lagrangian methods.
3. The paper establishes convergence rates comparable to ADMM while offering parallelism.
4. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate clear empirical advantages.
5. The approach for handling overlapping blocks in group lasso is particularly noteworthy as this problem has been challenging for block coordinate methods.

Weaknesses:
1. The reviewer finds the empirical results somewhat counterintuitive since Jacobian methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel methods. The authors could provide more detailed analysis of why PDMM overcomes this typical limitation.
2. The paper could more explicitly discuss the computational trade-offs between the number of blocks updated (K) and the per-iteration cost, especially for distributed implementations.
3. The comparison with other parallel methods (e.g., the work of Deng et al. on parallel multi-block ADMM) could be more extensive.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical contributions accessible. The notation is consistent, and the relationship to prior work is thoroughly discussed. The authors correctly cite relevant NIPS papers, including those on ADMM variants (e.g., Bregman ADMM by Wang and Banerjee, 2012).

The significance of this work is high. PDMM provides a theoretically grounded approach to parallel optimization that addresses a well-known gap in the literature. The method has broad applicability to distributed machine learning problems with linear constraints, and the unifying framework it provides will likely influence future research in optimization methods.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and empirical results. The authors are careful to address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to experiments.

Originality: The dual backward step and the unified framework for Jacobian ADMM variants represent significant conceptual advances over previous work.

Significance: The method has clear practical value for large-scale distributed optimization, and the theoretical insights will advance the field's understanding of multi-block optimization.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contribution (dual backward step) that solves a significant open problem
- Unified framework connecting existing methods
- Strong theoretical convergence guarantees
- Empirical evidence of superior performance
- Clear significance for distributed optimization

Arguments against acceptance:
- The empirical results may require more explanation to reconcile with typical Jacobian method behavior
- Limited comparison with the most recent parallel ADMM variants

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to optimization theory with clear practical implications. The dual backward step is particularly clever and addresses a long-standing challenge in multi-block ADMM. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and well-organized. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
