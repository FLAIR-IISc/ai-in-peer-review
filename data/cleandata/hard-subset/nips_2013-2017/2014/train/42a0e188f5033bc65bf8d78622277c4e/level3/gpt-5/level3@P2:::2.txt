Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex functions subject to linear constraints. PDMM is a randomized, parallel, multi-block variant of augmented Lagrangian methods that updates K blocks of the primal variables in parallel (Jacobian-style) and augments the dual update with a “dual backward step.” The step sizes τi for dual ascent and νi for the backward step depend on the number of blocks updated K and on the per-row block sparsity di of A, yielding conservative dual progress that compensates for limited information propagation in parallel updates. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes, and show how PDMM subsumes two notable Jacobian ADMM variants as special cases: two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM). PDMM also supports randomized updates for overlapping blocks via an equality-constrained reformulation.

Relative to prior work on ADMM (Boyd et al.) and its multi-block extensions, the paper addresses known convergence issues of Gauss–Seidel multi-block ADMM and of naive Jacobian updates, which can diverge. It complements recent randomized block methods (e.g., RBSUMM) by allowing multiple parallel block updates per iteration with constant step sizes and by avoiding restrictive error-bound assumptions. The link that PDMM provides between sADMM and PJADMM clarifies the role of the dual backward step and proximalization strength. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show competitive-to-superior empirical performance.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The dual backward step with sparsity-aware step-size selection is a clear and useful idea that explains and fixes the instability of naive Jacobian ADMM.
- Theoretical guarantees: Global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant step sizes for randomized parallel multi-block updates under standard convex assumptions.
- Unification: Deriving sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases of PDMM provides insight into how primal progress (exact vs proximal/inexact) should be balanced with dual step sizes.
- Practical scope: The framework covers nonsmooth objectives, allows overlapping blocks via a simple reformulation, and accommodates inexact primal updates via Bregman linearization.
- Empirical results: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants compare favorably to strong baselines in both iterations and runtime under the reported settings.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Counterintuitive empirical behavior: Jacobian updates are typically slower than Gauss–Seidel; here PDMM sometimes outperforms GS-ADMM. The paper should elaborate more on when and why the backward step yields faster practical convergence (beyond stability), e.g., how di and K influence contraction in practice.
- Parameter guidance: τi and νi depend on di and K; ηjt depends on Lipschitz/strong convexity constants. More explicit guidance (or adaptive rules) would help practitioners choose τ, ν, η, and K, including how to estimate di and how sensitive performance is to misspecification.
- Proof exposition: The main text references matrices Pt and Q used in the potential and residual but does not define them explicitly; key definitions and intuition for these terms (and for βi, γi) should be surfaced from the supplement.
- Inexact PDMM conditions: The requirements for ψjt and ϕjt (Lipschitz gradient and strong convexity) are only briefly stated; it would help to spell out common choices and corresponding closed forms, and to clarify whether global O(1/T) still holds uniformly for inexact updates under these conditions.
- Experimental scope: All experiments are single-threaded Matlab; given PDMM’s appeal is parallelism, a small-scale parallel/distributed evaluation (or at least a communication/computation complexity analysis per iteration) would strengthen claims. Real datasets and stronger baselines with tuned parameters would also add credibility. RBSUMM tuning appears limited; more systematic tuning and reporting would be fair.
- Reproducibility details: Exact parameter settings (ρ, τ, ν, η), stopping criteria per method, and code/data availability should be included.

Quality
- The analysis appears technically careful, with appropriate convexity assumptions and standard ADMM-style ergodic bounds. The dependence of τi and νi on K and di is both theoretically motivated and intuitive. However, the main text should provide more self-contained definitions and a high-level proof sketch to improve readability.
- The experimental methodology is reasonable but somewhat limited for a methods paper emphasizing parallel randomized updates.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with helpful motivation for the dual backward step. Notation is heavy; adding a concise algorithm box and a table summarizing parameters would help. There are several typos (“seperable,” “defiend,” “contast,” “negligable,” “convegence”) that should be corrected.

Originality
- The dual backward step tailored to multi-block Jacobian updates and the sparsity-aware step sizes are, to the best of my knowledge, novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within PDMM is also original and insightful.

Significance
- The contribution is significant for large-scale and distributed optimization where multi-block, parallel updates are desirable. The O(1/T) rate with constant steps under broad conditions and the practical recipes for τi, νi are likely to be adopted and extended.

Questions for the authors
- Can τi and νi be adapted online (e.g., via residual balancing) without losing guarantees?
- How sensitive is PDMM to inaccurate di (e.g., when A is not explicitly block-sparse)?
- Do the O(1/T) constants improve as di decreases, and can this be made explicit?
- For inexact updates, is there a simple rule for choosing ηjt beyond Lipschitz constants?
- Can you provide a brief communication complexity analysis per iteration under a distributed setting?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea (dual backward step) with theory and practice.
- Unifies and clarifies relationships among important ADMM variants.
- Broad applicability, including overlapping groups.
- Solid convergence guarantees with constant steps.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation of parallel scalability; all results are single-threaded.
- Some theoretical details deferred to the supplement hinder readability.
- Parameter selection guidance and adaptivity could be improved.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a valuable, well-justified contribution to randomized parallel ADMM for multi-block problems, with both theoretical and practical interest. Strengthening exposition, adding parameter guidance, and expanding empirical evaluation (especially parallelism) would make it even more compelling.
