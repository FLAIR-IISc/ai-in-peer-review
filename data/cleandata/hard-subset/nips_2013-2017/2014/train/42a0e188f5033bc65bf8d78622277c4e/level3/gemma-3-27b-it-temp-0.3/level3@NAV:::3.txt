## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly select K blocks per iteration, update them in parallel, and then perform a backward step for the dual update – a crucial element for ensuring global convergence. The authors demonstrate theoretical convergence guarantees with an iteration complexity of O(J/(T*K)) after T iterations, where J is the total number of blocks. 

The work builds upon the extensive literature surrounding ADMM, addressing the challenges of parallelization in multi-block settings. Existing methods like GSADMM and Jacobian variants (sADMM, PJADMM) are discussed, and PADMM is positioned as offering full parallelization of the primal step and the ability to use larger stepsizes. Importantly, the authors establish connections between PADMM and sADMM/PJADMM, providing a unifying perspective. The paper then validates PADMM’s performance through empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showcasing faster computation times compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and a rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proof is a significant contribution, and the empirical results are promising, demonstrating practical benefits. The connection drawn between PADMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is insightful and enhances understanding. The ability to handle overlapping blocks is also a valuable feature. The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:** A notable limitation is the lack of reported results for a *parallel* implementation of PADMM. While the algorithm is designed for parallelization, the experiments are conducted sequentially, leaving a key aspect of its potential unrealized. Furthermore, the method introduces three parameters that require tuning, which might be less convenient than traditional ADMM. While acknowledged by the authors, this could hinder adoption.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong and well-written contribution that addresses an important challenge in ADMM – effective parallelization for multi-block problems. The theoretical guarantees and empirical support are solid, and the method appears to offer significant advantages over existing approaches. Despite the lack of parallel implementation results and the parameter tuning complexity, the benefits of PADMM, particularly its potential for scalability, make it a valuable addition to the optimization toolbox. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation with proven convergence.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating improved performance.
*   Novel approach to parallelizing ADMM with a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   Insightful connection to related work (sADMM, PJADMM).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of results demonstrating the benefits of a parallel implementation.
*   Increased parameter tuning complexity compared to standard ADMM.



I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parallel implementation aspect, perhaps through a discussion of expected speedups or a preliminary analysis.
