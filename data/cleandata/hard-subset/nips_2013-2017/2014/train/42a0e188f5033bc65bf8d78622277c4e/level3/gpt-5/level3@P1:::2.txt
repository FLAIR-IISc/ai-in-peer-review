This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate augmented-Lagrangian method for minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex objectives under linear constraints. PDMM departs from multi-block ADMM variants by: (i) performing Jacobi (parallel) primal updates on a random subset of blocks; (ii) introducing a “dual backward step” (a conservative correction of the dual variable used inside the primal subproblems) to counteract the instability of naive Jacobi ADMM; and (iii) setting dual step sizes adaptively based on the number of updated blocks and the sparsity pattern of the constraint matrix. The method admits exact and inexact (proximal/Bregman-linearized) primal updates within one analysis, recovers sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases, and comes with global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity guarantees. Empirically, PDMM outperforms several ADMM baselines on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

Relation to prior work: Two-block ADMM is well-established (Boyd et al., Found. Trends ML, 2011), and multi-block extensions have mixed theory: Gauss–Seidel updates may diverge (e.g., Chen et al., 2013), while Jacobian/proximal variants (e.g., Deng et al., 2014; Hong et al., BSUMM/RBSUMM) require strong conditions or have limited rates. PDMM clarifies where sADMM with variable splitting (Wang et al.) and PJADMM (Deng et al.) sit within a single framework via the dual backward step and proximalization. The randomized block-coordinate viewpoint builds on RBCD theory (Richtárik & Takáč), but in a constrained augmented-Lagrangian setting with per-row-block degrees di shaping dual steps, which to my knowledge has not been made explicit before.

Core ideas and contributions:
- Algorithm: For a random set of K blocks, update xjt via augmented Lagrangian with a Bregman term, using a “backward” dual ŷt = yt − νiρ(Aixt − ai). Then update yi with a shrunk dual ascent step size τi depending on K and the row-sparsity di. The choices τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i stabilize the Jacobi updates.
- Interpretation: The augmented term implicitly performs a dual ascent step inside the primal updates; the backward step reduces this implicit dual step so that overall dual progress is conservative. The dependence on di leverages sparsity: when constraints are sparse (small di), larger τi are admissible.
- Theory: Under standard convexity and existence of a KKT point, PDMM enjoys global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rates with constant stepsizes, matching best-known rates for (multi-block) ADMM-type methods without resorting to decaying steps or error bounds.
- Unification: sADMM and PJADMM emerge as special cases (all-block updates with exact vs strongly proximalized primal steps, respectively), illuminating how dual step sizes should shrink as primal progress becomes more “aggressive.”
- Practice: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM variants converge faster in wall-clock time than RBSUMM and sADMM; on overlapping groups where di=2, PDMM can use τi=νi=0.5 (vs τi≈1/J in sADMM), explaining its strong performance.

Quality: The algorithmic design is sound and well-motivated by the instability of naive Jacobi ADMM. The theoretical results are clearly stated, with the main proofs in the supplement, and the assumptions align with prior ADMM work. The experiments are careful and include ablations on K and on τi,νi; they support the claims, although all runs are sequential MATLAB implementations (limiting the demonstration of parallel gains).

Clarity: The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The role of the dual backward step and its interaction with the implicit dual ascent is an important and insightful explanation; however, the derivation of τi and νi (their dependence on K and di) could be given more intuition in the main text. The notation z, Pt, Q, and the residual R could be condensed or intuitively summarized before the technical section.

Originality: The backward dual step for stabilizing Jacobi augmented-Lagrangian updates, combined with sparsity-aware step sizing and randomized parallel block selection, is novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within this scheme is also a useful conceptual contribution.

Significance: PDMM adds a practically relevant option for distributed/parallel optimization with provable guarantees in settings where multi-block ADMM can be fragile. The dependence on constraint sparsity is particularly appealing for large-scale structured problems (e.g., overlapping groups). The O(1/T) rate is standard, but the ability to obtain it under constant stepsizes and with parallel randomized updates broadens applicability.

Addressing the “counterintuitive” Jacobian vs Gauss–Seidel concern: PDMM counters the usual slowdown/instability of Jacobi updates via (a) the dual backward step that cancels excessive implicit dual ascent in the augmented Lagrangian, and (b) larger admissible dual steps when constraints are sparse (small di), which reduces the performance gap with Gauss–Seidel. Randomized block selection further mitigates cyclic error accumulation. Thus, while pure Jacobi ADMM may diverge, PDMM’s tailored dual control yields both stability and competitive speed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide more intuition for τi and νi beyond the formulas, including a short derivation sketch and bounds showing how di and K change effective conditioning.
- Add ablations isolating the effect of the backward step (e.g., νi=0) and of sparsity (vary di synthetically) to corroborate the theory.
- Include true parallel/distributed experiments to demonstrate wall-clock gains and communication patterns (e.g., per-row aggregation of Aixt).
- Report sensitivity to ρ and to the Bregman parameters ηjt; discuss adaptive strategies for K and τi,νi.
- Compare with additional primal–dual methods where applicable (e.g., linearized ADMM/proximal ADMM), clarifying scope differences.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and novel idea (dual backward step) that stabilizes Jacobi updates.
- Solid theory with O(1/T) rate under constant stepsizes and standard assumptions.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM and clarifies their behavior.
- Practical relevance for overlapping groups and sparse constraints; strong empirical results.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experiments are limited in breadth and lack parallel/distributed implementations.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary proofs; some step-size intuition missing in main text.
- Comparisons could be broader (other stabilized multi-block ADMM and primal–dual baselines).
- Sequential MATLAB timings only partially reflect the claimed parallel advantages.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper advances our understanding of multi-block augmented-Lagrangian methods, offers a principled and implementable algorithm, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence. With minor clarifications and broader experiments, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
