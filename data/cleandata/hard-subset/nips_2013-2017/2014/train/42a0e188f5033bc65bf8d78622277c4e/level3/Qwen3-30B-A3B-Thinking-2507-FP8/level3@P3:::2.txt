

# Review: Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: the lack of effective generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks. While ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, multi-block extensions have proven challenging, with most existing methods either lacking convergence guarantees or requiring restrictive conditions.

PDMM makes three key contributions. First, it replaces ADMM's sequential Gauss-Seidel updates with parallel Jacobian updates, enabling true parallelism. Second, it introduces a novel dual backward step that effectively reduces the dual step size, compensating for limited information propagation in Jacobian updates. This is particularly significant because, as the authors note, direct Jacobi updates (which PDMM generalizes) are known to fail to converge. Third, PDMM establishes O(1/T) convergence rates with constant step size, matching the theoretical guarantees of ADMM.

The paper establishes important connections to existing methods: it shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unified framework that clarifies the relationships among augmented-Lagrangian decomposition methods. The authors also demonstrate how PDMM can handle overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion, an important extension for problems like overlapping group lasso where traditional block coordinate descent methods struggle.

The theoretical contributions are strong. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under relatively mild assumptions. The key innovation is the dual backward step, which is carefully analyzed and justified through an intuitive explanation of the "implicit dual ascent" problem in standard Jacobi updates. The paper provides detailed technical conditions for the step size parameters, taking into account the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks updated in parallel.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates PDMM's practical advantages over ADMM, sADMM, PJADMM, and RBSUMM in two important applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The results show that PDMM can achieve faster convergence than competing methods, particularly when using multiple blocks in parallel. The authors provide insightful analysis of how different values of K (number of blocks updated in parallel) affect performance, showing that there's an optimal trade-off between iteration cost and convergence rate.

I find the paper's main theoretical contribution—the dual backward step—to be particularly elegant and well-motivated. The counterintuitive nature of the results (that parallel updates with this modification can achieve the same convergence rate as sequential updates) is addressed effectively through the detailed analysis. The paper's connection to existing methods through the unified PDMM framework is a significant contribution that clarifies the landscape of multi-block ADMM variants.

However, the reviewer notes that the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the dual backward step enables convergence. While the authors provide the mathematical justification, a more intuitive explanation of how the backward step reduces the "aggressive progress" on the dual variable would help readers better grasp this key insight. The paper's claim that PDMM can achieve the same O(1/T) rate as ADMM despite using parallel updates (which typically converge slower) is counterintuitive and deserves more explicit discussion.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with sufficient technical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines and careful parameter tuning. The authors properly acknowledge limitations and provide clear connections to prior work.

Strengths:
- Novel dual backward step that enables convergence for parallel updates
- Strong theoretical guarantees (global convergence and O(1/T) complexity)
- Clear connections to existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM)
- Empirical results demonstrating practical advantages
- Unified framework that clarifies relationships among multi-block ADMM variants

Weaknesses:
- Could provide more intuitive explanation for why the dual backward step works
- The counterintuitive convergence results would benefit from additional discussion
- Some technical details in the convergence analysis could be made more accessible

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization by providing a theoretically grounded and empirically effective method for multi-block convex optimization. It resolves an important open problem in the extension of ADMM to multiple blocks while providing a unified perspective on existing methods. The authors' insights into the dual backward step and its role in enabling parallel updates are particularly valuable. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in optimization with both theoretical and practical significance. I recommend acceptance.
