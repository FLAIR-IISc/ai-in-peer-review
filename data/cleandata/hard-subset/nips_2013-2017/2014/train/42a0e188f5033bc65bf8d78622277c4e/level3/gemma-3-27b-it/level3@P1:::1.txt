This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key challenge in extending the widely-used Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to multiple blocks, where convergence can be problematic. PDMM leverages randomized block coordinate updates, a backward step in the dual update, and allows for parallel computation. The paper presents convergence analysis establishing sublinear iteration complexity and demonstrates connections to existing ADMM variants, showing that they can be viewed as special cases of PDMM. The method is evaluated on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing promising performance.

The proposed approach builds upon the foundations laid by Boyd et al. (2011) on ADMM and extends it to address the difficulties observed with multi-block generalizations as discussed in Chen et al. (2017). The authors correctly identify the potential for divergence in direct Jacobi updates (Chen et al., 2017) and propose a backward step on the dual update to mitigate this issue.  The connection to existing methods like sADMM (Wang et al., 2017) and PJADMM (Deng et al., 2017) is a valuable contribution, providing a unifying framework and insights into the role of different components.  The theoretical results – specifically, the established convergence and iteration complexity – are significant, offering guarantees for the method's behavior. The experiments demonstrate practical effectiveness in relevant applications like robust PCA (Candes et al., 2011) and overlapping group lasso (Zhao et al., 2006).

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis with a proven sublinear convergence rate.
*   **Novelty:** PDMM offers a novel approach to multi-block ADMM, addressing the challenges of convergence and parallelism. The use of randomized block updates and the backward dual step are valuable contributions.
*   **Generalization:**  Demonstrating that existing ADMM variants are special cases of PDMM provides a unifying perspective and deeper understanding of these methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provide evidence of the method’s practical effectiveness.
*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing multi-block ADMM approaches and provides a strong motivation for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Algorithmic Clarity:** The description of the algorithm, specifically steps (5) and (6), could be made more precise. It’s unclear whether y^{t+1} and \hat{y}^{t+1} update all dual coordinates or a subset corresponding to the randomly selected blocks. Also, how is the index *i* selected in the dual update (6)? Clarifying this in the revision would greatly improve readability.
*   **Proof Technicality:** In the proof of Theorem 2, a missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" should be added to ensure the mathematical correctness of the result.
*   **Limited Parameter Guidance:** While the theoretical results establish convergence, the paper lacks detailed guidance on how to choose the parameters (e.g., *K*, *J*) to achieve optimal performance and consistency with the convergence rates of known ADMM variants. Expanding Theorem 2 to explicitly show this connection would strengthen the contribution.
*   **A Decomposition Matrix Discussion:** Further discussion on optimal ways to split the transformation matrix *A* into blocks would be beneficial. The performance of PDMM could be highly sensitive to the choice of block structure.
*   **Application Scope:** The experiments focus on two specific applications.  Expanding the evaluation to other relevant problems, such as tensor completion (which naturally exhibits a multi-block structure), would strengthen the empirical validation.  Discussing which ADMM variant (including PDMM) might be most suitable for tensor completion would be insightful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important open problem in the optimization community: extending ADMM to multiple blocks while guaranteeing convergence.
*   The proposed PDMM method offers a novel and theoretically sound solution.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the method's effectiveness in practical applications.
*   The connection to existing ADMM variants provides a valuable unifying perspective.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed parameter guidance and discussion of block structure optimization might limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The clarity of the algorithm description could be improved.
*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation could raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The proposed PDMM method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for solving large-scale optimization problems. I recommend **acceptance** with the request for minor revisions to address the clarity and parameter guidance issues outlined above. Specifically, clarify the block update procedure in the algorithm description, correct the missing summation in the proof of Theorem 2, and expand the discussion to provide guidance on parameter selection and block structure optimization.
