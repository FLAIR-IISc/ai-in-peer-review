This paper addresses the important problem of multi-block convex optimization with linear constraints, a structure that appears in numerous machine learning applications. The authors propose a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which extends the popular Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key idea of PDMM is to perform parallel, randomized block coordinate updates on the primal variables. To ensure convergence, which is often problematic for naive parallel extensions of ADMM, the authors introduce a "backward step" on the dual variable update. The step sizes for both the standard dual update and the backward step are intelligently chosen based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel (K). The paper provides a theoretical analysis establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. Furthermore, it demonstrates that two existing parallel ADMM variants, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, thereby providing a unifying framework. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed PDMM framework is a novel and significant contribution to the literature on ADMM-style methods. The direct extension of ADMM to multiple blocks is known to have convergence issues, and this work provides a principled way to achieve parallelism through randomized updates while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The introduction of the dual backward step and the adaptive step-size rule are key innovations.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) complexity rate, which is standard for this class of algorithms. The analysis appears to be technically sound and covers both exact and inexact updates of the primal variables.

3.  **Unifying Framework:** A major strength of the paper is its ability to unify and generalize existing methods. Showing that sADMM and PJADMM are two extreme cases of PDMM provides valuable insight into the relationship between these algorithms and clarifies the role of the dual update steps. This conceptual contribution enhances our understanding of multi-block optimization methods.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm is well-motivated by the need for scalable solvers for large-scale machine learning problems. The parallel nature of the primal updates makes it suitable for modern distributed computing environments. The experiments on RPCA and, notably, overlapping group lasso (which the authors frame as a challenging case for standard block coordinate methods) demonstrate strong empirical performance against relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be clarified or expanded upon to increase its impact.

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the algorithm updates in equations (5)-(7) could be clearer. In step (5), a random subset of blocks `Jt` is updated. However, steps (6) and (7) use an index `i` for the dual variable blocks `y_i`. It is not immediately obvious if these steps update *all* dual blocks `i=1,...,I` at each iteration, or only a subset corresponding to the updated primal blocks. The text states "(6) and (7) update all dual blocks", but the notation and flow could be made more explicit to avoid ambiguity for the reader trying to implement the algorithm.

2.  **Discussion of Theoretical Results:** Theorem 2 presents the main iteration complexity result. This result could be strengthened with more discussion.
    *   **(a)** Since the paper claims to generalize sADMM and PJADMM, it would be highly instructive to show how the convergence rate in Theorem 2 reduces to or compares with the known rates for these methods by setting the parameters (e.g., K, J, sparsity) appropriately. This would solidify the "unifying framework" claim.
    *   **(b)** The complexity depends on the number of blocks, `J`. This implies that the way the problem is partitioned (i.e., how matrix `A` is split) affects performance. The paper would benefit from a discussion offering practical guidance on how to best partition `A` to optimize the convergence bound.

3.  **Scope of Applications:** The paper applies PDMM to RPCA, which has a 3-block structure. A natural and challenging application for multi-block methods is tensor completion/decomposition, which can involve many more blocks. A brief discussion on how PDMM might be applied to such problems, and whether the authors have any insight into which ADMM variant (e.g., PDMM with small K, PDMM with large K, or GSADMM) would be most suitable, would broaden the paper's perceived impact.

4.  **Minor Proof Correction:** In the supplementary material's proof of Theorem 2, there appears to be a missing summation term `\sum_{i=1}^I` in the derivation of the bound on the primal sub-optimality. This seems to be a minor oversight but should be corrected for technical accuracy.

**Minor Typos:**
*   Line 115: There should be a space after "argmin" in the equation display (rewritten from (3)).
*   Line 217: There is an extra space before "Section".

**Overall Recommendation:**

I am positive about this paper and lean towards acceptance. It presents a well-motivated, novel, and theoretically sound algorithm for a very relevant problem in machine learning. The unification of prior work is a significant conceptual contribution, and the empirical results are promising. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and the depth of discussion, all of which are addressable in a revision. Addressing these points would further strengthen an already solid paper.
