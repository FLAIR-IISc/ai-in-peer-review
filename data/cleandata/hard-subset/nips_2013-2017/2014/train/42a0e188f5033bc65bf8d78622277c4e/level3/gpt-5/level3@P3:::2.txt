Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex objectives with linear constraints. PDMM replaces the two-block, Gauss–Seidel-type primal updates of ADMM with randomized Jacobian (parallel) updates over K blocks per iteration, while introducing a novel “dual backward step” that shrinks the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. The method accommodates exact and inexact primal updates via a Bregman term, yields global convergence, and establishes O(1/T) ergodic rates under standard assumptions (same as ADMM).

Conceptually, PDMM sits between two known Jacobian-style generalizations of ADMM: (i) two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM), which the authors show is a special case of PDMM with τi = 1/J and νi = 1 − 1/J, and (ii) proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM), recovered from PDMM by using sufficiently large proximal terms (ηjt > 0), allowing full dual steps (τi = 1) and no backward step (νi = 0). This unification clarifies when conservative dual steps are needed: exact, substantive primal progress calls for smaller dual steps; heavy proximalization permits larger dual steps.

A central contribution is the design of per-row dual step sizes τi and backward factors νi that depend jointly on the number of updated blocks K and the sparsity profile di of the constraints (number of nonzero blocks in row i). The analysis shows that τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, where K̃i = min{di, K}, are sufficient for convergence and lead to faster practical progress when A is sparse.

The paper also shows how PDMM naturally handles overlapping group structures by transforming composite problems into equality-constrained forms; notably, the induced sparsity (di = 2) permits much larger τi than in sADMM. Empirically, on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM compares favorably to GSADMM/ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analysis is careful and technically sound, with a Lyapunov function h(v*, vt) that decreases in expectation, and an O(1/T) ergodic rate on both objective suboptimality and constraint residuals. Assumptions match standard ADMM assumptions (closed, proper, convex fj; existence of a KKT point). The role of the dual backward step is well-justified by the observation that the augmented term induces implicit dual ascent; without damping, a direct Jacobi scheme tends to diverge.
- Algorithms: The exact and inexact PDMM variants are unified via a Bregman framework. The choice of ψjt to linearize problematic terms is sensible and leads to closed-form subupdates when needed.
- Experiments: RPCA and overlapping group lasso are appropriate testbeds. Results are consistent with the theory and show practical benefits, especially when exploiting sparsity. However, all runs are in sequential Matlab; there is no demonstration of wall-clock speedups in actual parallel/distributed settings.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The intuition for the dual backward step (implicit dual ascent from the augmented term) is a strong explanatory device. Suggestions:
- Provide a brief pseudocode for PDMM to fix notation (x̂ vs ŷ, when Bregman is used).
- Earlier highlight that τi, νi depend on sparsity (di) and K, and offer a short “rule of thumb” for practitioners choosing K.
- Collect definitions (K̃i, di, N(i), z variables) in a compact box to reduce back-and-forth.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “seperable”).

Originality
- The dual backward step (explicitly undoing a portion of implicit dual ascent) is, to my knowledge, novel and essential for making Jacobian multi-block updates converge under standard conditions with constant stepsizes.
- The unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases is useful to the community and clarifies design trade-offs.
- The dependency of step sizes on constraint sparsity is insightful and practically important.

Significance
- Provides a viable, theoretically grounded Jacobian alternative to multi-block ADMM with O(1/T) guarantees, widening the design space for distributed optimization.
- The ability to exploit sparsity for larger dual steps can yield tangible speedups.
- The handling of overlapping groups via PDMM is compelling for modern structured sparsity problems.

Addressing the “counterintuitive” aspect (Jacobian vs Gauss–Seidel)
The results are plausible when one recognizes that:
- The augmented term adds implicit dual ascent proportional to A(xt − a). In Gauss–Seidel, information propagates within the sweep, naturally damping instability. In pure Jacobi, this implicit step accumulates across blocks without intermediate feedback, which is why direct Jacobi often diverges.
- PDMM’s backward step explicitly offsets the implicit ascent, and the per-row τi, νi scale with K and di so that the net dual progress matches what would be stable under the information propagation attainable in that iteration. This “calibrated damping” reconciles Jacobian updates with stable progress, hence the comparable O(1/T) rate.
- The analysis formalizes this via a Lyapunov function that accounts for both primal change and constraint residuals; the constants demonstrate how sparsity allows more aggressive steps.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Practical guidance on K: Given a fixed parallel budget and communication costs, how should K be chosen? Can you provide a simple heuristic or auto-tuning scheme?
- Sensitivity: How robust is convergence speed to deviations from the theoretically suggested τi, νi? Any ablations?
- Strongly convex case: Do linear rates hold with additional assumptions (e.g., strong convexity and full column rank of A)? Any insights from your proof technique?
- Distributed/asynchronous: Can the backward step be adapted to asynchronous or stale updates? What changes in τi, νi would be needed?
- Communication/computation trade-offs: In truly distributed settings, how does exploiting di reduce communication per iteration?
- Experiments: Include parallel/distributed wall-clock results; add comparisons to PJADMM with tuned proximal terms; report the effect of varying di (synthetic sparse A).

Pros
- Novel and well-justified dual backward step enabling stable Jacobian multi-block updates.
- Clear unification of sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.
- Solid O(1/T) theory under standard assumptions, with sparsity-aware stepsizes.
- Empirical gains on relevant problems; handles overlapping groups elegantly.
- Expands the toolkit for distributed optimization.

Cons
- No true parallel/distributed experimental validation.
- Some constants and parameters (τi, νi) could use more practical guidance.
- Presentation could be streamlined; typos and notation density in the theory sections.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is technically sound, original in its dual backward step and unifying perspective, and likely to impact practice and follow-on research in distributed convex optimization. Strengthen the empirical section with parallel runs and add more implementation guidance for K, τi, and νi to maximize utility for practitioners.
