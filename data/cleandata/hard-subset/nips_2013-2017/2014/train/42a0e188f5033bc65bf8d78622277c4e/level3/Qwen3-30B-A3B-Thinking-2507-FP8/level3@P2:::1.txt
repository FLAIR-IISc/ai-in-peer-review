

# Review for "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

## Summary

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving convex optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address an important gap in the literature: while ADMM for two-block problems is well-established, generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM randomly updates K blocks in parallel, employs a dual backward step to ensure convergence, and incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix A in determining step sizes. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size. Notably, the authors show that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing theoretical insight into their relationship. The method is evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art methods.

This work builds upon and extends several key NIPS contributions, including ADMM (Boyd et al., 2011), randomized block coordinate descent (Nesterov, 2012), and recent multi-block ADMM variants (Hong et al., 2013, Deng et al., 2013). The paper also relates to overlapping group lasso work (Zhao et al., 2009) and Bregman ADMM (Wang & Banerjee, 2013).

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity results are well-established under reasonable assumptions. The proof techniques are solid, and the connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides valuable theoretical insight. The experimental section demonstrates clear empirical advantages of PDMM over competing methods in two important applications. The authors carefully address the trade-offs between parallelism and convergence rate.

*Minor concern*: In the proof of Theorem 2, a missing $\sum_{i=1}^I$ should be added to ensure correctness.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though there are some clarity issues with the algorithm description. Algorithm (5)-(7) is somewhat ambiguous regarding whether updates apply to all dual blocks or a single block, and how index i is selected in the dual updates. The authors should clarify these points in the revision. Additionally, there's a missing space after "min" in equation (9) and an extra space before "Section" at line 217.

## Originality

PDMM represents a meaningful contribution to the literature. The randomized parallel update strategy with dual backward step is novel, and the paper clearly articulates how it differs from previous work. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM provides new theoretical understanding of these methods. The ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized block coordinate fashion is particularly significant, as the authors note that "the corresponding problem for RBCD [16, 18] with overlapping blocks is still an open problem." This addresses an important gap in the literature.

## Significance

The results are significant for both theory and practice. The O(1/T) convergence rate with constant step size is valuable for practitioners, as it avoids the need for diminishing step sizes. The method's ability to handle overlapping blocks in a parallel fashion addresses a practical challenge in large-scale machine learning. The experimental results demonstrate clear performance improvements over existing methods in two important applications, suggesting that PDMM could be widely adopted.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical contribution: Global convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant step size
- Practical contribution: Parallel updates with dual backward step, handles overlapping blocks
- Connection to existing methods: sADMM and PJADMM as special cases
- Empirical validation: Clear improvements in two important applications

**Weaknesses:**
- Algorithm description (5)-(7) needs clarification regarding dual updates
- Theorem 2 proof requires correction (missing summation)
- More discussion needed on optimal parameter selection (K, J) for different problem structures
- Limited discussion of application to tensor completion problems (which have natural multi-block structure)

## Arguments for Acceptance

- The paper addresses a well-motivated problem (multi-block optimization) with clear theoretical and practical contributions
- The method is theoretically sound with rigorous convergence guarantees
- Empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- The connection between existing methods provides valuable theoretical insight
- The paper is well-written and well-organized

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Some algorithmic details need clarification (dual updates)
- Theorem 2 proof contains a minor error
- More guidance on parameter selection would strengthen the paper
- Limited discussion of potential applications beyond the two evaluated

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and practical benefits. The minor issues with clarity and the proof correction are easily fixable in revision. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on distributed optimization and ADMM variants, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and proof correction.
