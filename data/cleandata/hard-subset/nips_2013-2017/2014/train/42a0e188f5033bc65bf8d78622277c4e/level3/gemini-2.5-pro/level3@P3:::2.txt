This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints. This is a fundamental problem formulation that captures numerous applications in machine learning, such as robust PCA and structured sparse regression. The proposed method builds upon the well-known Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key challenge in generalizing ADMM from two blocks to multiple blocks is a well-documented problem, with direct extensions often failing to converge. The standard multi-block ADMM variants typically employ a sequential, Gauss-Seidel update scheme. This paper's main contribution is to propose a parallel, Jacobian-style update for the primal variables, which is more amenable to distributed computation.

The critical innovation that enables convergence for this parallel scheme is the introduction of a "dual backward step." This step effectively dampens the dual variable update, compensating for the fact that parallel primal updates use less current information compared to sequential ones. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, establishing an O(1/T) convergence rate, which matches that of standard ADMM. The paper further strengthens its contribution by showing in the supplement that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM can be viewed as special cases of PDMM, thus providing a unifying perspective on this class of algorithms.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of creating a provably convergent, parallel, multi-block ADMM. The proposed dual backward step is a simple yet powerful idea that directly addresses the known instability of Jacobian-style augmented Lagrangian methods. This provides a valuable new tool for large-scale and distributed optimization.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The work is technically sound. The authors provide rigorous proofs for global convergence and establish an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size, under standard assumptions. This places the algorithm on a firm theoretical footing.

3.  **Unifying Framework:** A significant strength is the connection established between PDMM, sADMM, and PJADMM. This analysis clarifies the relationships between different augmented Lagrangian methods and provides insight into the trade-off between exactness in the primal update and the allowable step size in the dual update.

4.  **Practical Effectiveness:** The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are convincing. They demonstrate that PDMM is competitive with or outperforms other state-of-the-art methods, including sequential ADMM variants and other parallel approaches, in terms of convergence speed.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Intuition Behind Jacobian Convergence:** The primary positive result—that a Jacobian-style update can be made to converge at the same rate as a Gauss-Seidel one—is somewhat counterintuitive. Gauss-Seidel methods are generally expected to converge faster (in terms of iterations) because they incorporate the most recent information immediately within the same iteration. Jacobian methods, which use information from the previous iteration `t` for all parallel updates at `t+1`, suffer from delayed information propagation. While the mathematical proofs are sound, the paper could be strengthened by providing a more detailed intuitive explanation of *how* the dual backward step precisely compensates for this information lag. A deeper discussion on this aspect in the main body would greatly benefit the reader.

2.  **Practical Guidance on Parameter `K`:** The algorithm has a key hyperparameter `K`, the number of blocks to update in parallel. The experiments show that the choice of `K` significantly impacts performance. While the theory accommodates any `K`, the paper offers little practical guidance on how to set this parameter. A discussion of the trade-off between the degree of parallelism (computation saved per iteration for larger `K`) and convergence rate (fewer iterations needed for smaller `K`) would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Parallel Implementation:** A core motivation for PDMM is its parallel nature. However, the experiments were run sequentially, comparing runtime on a single machine. While this demonstrates the computational efficiency per iteration, the true benefit of a parallel algorithm lies in its wall-clock time reduction in a distributed setting. Including even a small-scale experiment or a more detailed discussion on the expected speedups and communication costs in a true parallel environment would make the paper's practical significance more compelling.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and validated by solid experimental results. It represents a complete and polished piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established, the proposed algorithm is presented precisely, and the main ideas are communicated effectively.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the ADMM framework, the introduction of the dual backward step to stabilize a parallel Jacobian update is a novel and impactful idea. The unification of related methods is also a valuable original contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. Given the prevalence of large-scale optimization in machine learning and the need for effective distributed algorithms, PDMM offers an important and practical new approach. Researchers and practitioners working on distributed optimization are very likely to use these ideas or build upon them.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the optimization literature and the NIPS community. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily suggestions for improving the exposition and experimental validation. The core ideas are sound, interesting, and practically relevant.
