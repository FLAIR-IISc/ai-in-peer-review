

# Review of "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers"

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate method for solving optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The authors address a gap in the literature regarding effective generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks. While ADMM for two blocks is well-established, extensions to multiple blocks face convergence challenges. PDMM updates K random blocks in parallel at each iteration, incorporating a dual backward step that makes the dual update more conservative. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes, demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) It provides a unified perspective on existing Jacobian ADMM variants through the PDMM framework; (2) It introduces a principled way to determine step sizes based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of blocks K; (3) It enables parallelization of the primal update step, which is a significant practical advantage; (4) It extends to handle overlapping blocks, which is an open problem for randomized block coordinate descent.

The theoretical analysis is thorough and carefully presented, with clear assumptions and well-structured proofs. The authors establish the global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions that match those required by ADMM. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides valuable theoretical insight into the role of the dual backward step.

The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are compelling, showing clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods in terms of runtime while maintaining accuracy. The paper demonstrates that PDMM can converge faster than GSADMM (which has no convergence guarantees) and sADMM, with the best performance observed when updating multiple blocks in parallel.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The technical sections are logically structured, with clear explanations of the algorithmic components and their theoretical underpinnings.

The main strengths of the paper are:
- Solid theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees
- Clear connection to and unification of existing methods
- Practical advantages through parallelization
- Strong empirical results demonstrating the method's effectiveness
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

The main weaknesses are:
- No reported results for a parallel implementation of PDMM (only sequential results are shown)
- Tuning parameters (τi, νi) require careful selection, which may be less convenient than traditional ADMM
- The paper doesn't discuss how to choose K (the number of blocks to update) in practice

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, providing sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.
- Originality: While building on ADMM, the paper introduces a novel randomized parallel variant with important theoretical insights, distinguishing it from existing work.
- Significance: The method addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization (parallelization) while maintaining theoretical guarantees, making it valuable for practitioners and researchers.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between the theoretical understanding of ADMM and practical parallelization needs, providing both a theoretical framework and empirical validation. The method's ability to handle overlapping blocks also expands the applicability of block coordinate methods to important structured sparsity problems.

While the lack of parallel implementation results is a notable omission, the paper's theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial. The method's convergence rate matches ADMM's while enabling parallelism, which is a significant practical advantage. The paper addresses an important problem in optimization with clear theoretical and practical contributions.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contributions with global convergence and iteration complexity guarantees, clear connection to existing methods, compelling empirical results showing faster convergence than state-of-the-art methods, and practical advantages for large-scale problems.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited discussion of parameter tuning and no parallel implementation results, which might be important for practitioners evaluating the method.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning by providing a theoretically sound and practically efficient method for parallelizing ADMM. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution that would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
