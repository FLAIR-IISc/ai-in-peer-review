

This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant designed for problems with multiple blocks. The key innovation lies in its per-iteration selection of K random blocks for parallel primal updates, followed by a conservative dual update via a backward step. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this backward step is critical for global convergence, as it prevents divergence by ensuring dual updates remain sufficiently conservative. Additionally, PADMM supports proximal terms in the primal update, facilitating efficient solutions for specific subproblems. Theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and an iteration complexity of O(J/(T K)), where J is the total number of blocks, T is the number of iterations, and K is the number of blocks updated per iteration. This rate matches standard ADMM's convergence behavior while enabling parallelism.

The primary advantages of PADMM are its full parallelization of the primal step and its ability to utilize larger step sizes compared to standard ADMM variants. Specifically, PADMM outperforms sADMM (which requires smaller step sizes for convergence) and offers greater flexibility than PJADMM while maintaining higher parallelism than GSADMM. The authors validate these claims empirically on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, reporting significant speedups in achieving target accuracy relative to sequential methods. The results are compelling, showing clear computational efficiency gains.

However, the evaluation has notable gaps. The paper does not present results for PADMM under parallel implementation (e.g., runtime scaling with increasing processors), which is essential for assessing its practical parallel benefits. Without this, the claimed parallel advantages remain theoretical. Furthermore, PADMM introduces three parameters (ρ, θ, τ) for convergence control, and tuning them is less straightforward than for standard ADMM, potentially complicating practical adoption. The authors should address this trade-off more explicitly.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with solid theoretical and empirical components. The convergence analysis is rigorous, and the experimental design appropriately targets relevant problem classes. The connection between the backward step and convergence guarantees is particularly strong, addressing a common weakness in parallel ADMM methods. The comparison with existing approaches (sADMM, PJADMM, GSADMM) is thorough and highlights PADMM’s niche in balancing parallelism, convergence, and flexibility.

Minor suggestions for clarity: The phrase "handle palatalization" is a clear typo (likely "multiple blocks"). The convergence rate notation should be standardized as O(J/(T K)) for consistency with optimization literature. The claim that PADMM "allows relatively large update step size" should specify "compared to standard ADMM" to avoid ambiguity.

Overall, PADMM makes a meaningful contribution to scalable ADMM for multi-block problems. It successfully bridges the gap between theoretical convergence guarantees and practical parallel efficiency, offering a viable path for large-scale distributed optimization. While the lack of parallel runtime data and parameter tuning discussion slightly weakens the empirical impact, the core algorithmic advances are significant and well-supported. The paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS, provided minor clarifications are made in the final version.
