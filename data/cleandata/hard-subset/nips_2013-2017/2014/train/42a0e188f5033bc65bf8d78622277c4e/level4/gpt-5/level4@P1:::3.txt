This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks at random and updates their primal variables in parallel using an augmented Lagrangian with an optional proximal/Bregman term; it then performs a “backward” step on the dual variables to temper the implicit ascent induced by the quadratic penalty. The authors derive step sizes that depend on both the number of updated blocks and the sparsity pattern of A, and they establish global convergence and an ergodic O(1/T) rate with constant step sizes. In particular, when there are J total blocks and K are updated per iteration, the ergodic optimality gap scales as O(J/(KT)). PDMM recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases (exact vs heavily proximal primal updates), and it supports randomized updates over overlapping blocks, which is nontrivial for standard RBCD.

Relation to prior work: Two-block ADMM is well understood; extensions to many blocks include Gauss–Seidel ADMM (GSADMM) and Jacobian variants, as well as sADMM with splitting variables and PJADMM with proximal terms. Some multi-block ADMM variants lack convergence guarantees or require restrictive assumptions (e.g., error bounds, diminishing steps). PDMM contributes a unified randomized parallel scheme with explicit, sparsity-aware dual step sizes and a dual backward step that connects to sADMM/PJADMM, providing intuition and theory absent in prior randomized methods such as RBSUMM.

Technical assessment (Quality):
- The analysis is careful and technically sound under standard assumptions (closed, proper, convex fj; existence of a KKT point). The backward dual step addresses over-aggressive implicit ascent in Jacobi-style updates and appears essential for convergence with multiple parallel block updates.
- The global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes are in line with ADMM variants, but PDMM’s constants explicitly reflect K and the per-row sparsity di, clarifying the parallelism–stepsize trade-off.
- The inexact PDMM using Bregman divergences is well motivated and unifies exact and proximal updates. The connections showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases are valuable.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear algorithmic descriptions and notation. The intuition for the backward step as counteracting implicit dual ascent is helpful. Some guidance could be added on practical selection of K, grouping strategies, and how to set τi, νi, and η in practice beyond the formulas (e.g., sensitivity and heuristics).

Originality:
- The combination of randomized parallel block updates, sparsity-aware dual step sizes, and a backward dual step, together with a unifying view connecting sADMM and PJADMM, is novel and informative. The ability to handle overlapping blocks in a randomized fashion is a notable design point compared to standard RBCD.

Significance:
- Multi-block constrained optimization is pervasive (e.g., RPCA, overlapping group lasso). A parallelizable algorithm with constant-step convergence and interpretable step-size rules is practically useful. The theoretical insights into how sparsity and K affect allowable steps are likely to inform future designs.

Empirical evaluation:
- Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM outperforming sADMM and RBSUMM and being competitive with or faster than GSADMM in wall-clock time, despite GSADMM sometimes using fewer iterations. The overlap setting highlights PDMM’s advantage from larger τ due to di = 2.
- All results are sequential Matlab; no parallel implementation is reported, which undercuts the central claim of parallel efficiency. Baselines are appropriate, but the sADMM comparison should ensure proper dual updates and parameter tuning for fairness.

Pros:
- Randomized parallel updates with provable global convergence and O(1/T) rate using constant step sizes.
- Clear, sparsity- and K-dependent step-size rules (τi, νi) with sound intuition via the backward dual step.
- Unifying framework: sADMM and PJADMM arise as special cases; supports overlapping groups.
- Empirical results on two representative applications show strong performance.

Cons:
- No experiments demonstrating actual parallel speedups; all runs are sequential.
- Practical tuning is more involved than classical ADMM (ρ, τi, νi, and optional η); limited guidance on sensitivity.
- Some baseline comparisons could be strengthened (e.g., fully tuned sADMM with dual updates, broader datasets).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include parallel implementations (multi-core or distributed) to demonstrate wall-clock speedups versus GSADMM/sADMM/PJADMM at similar accuracy.
- Provide a brief tuning guide and sensitivity study for τi, νi, K, and η, and ablations showing the effect of the backward step.
- Clarify grouping strategies for randomized updates and discuss asynchronous or stale updates, if applicable.
- Ensure all baselines are fairly configured (e.g., sADMM with proper dual updates) and consider additional real-world datasets.

Recommendation: A solid and interesting contribution that advances randomized, parallel ADMM for multi-block problems with a clean theory–algorithm–practice connection. The lack of parallel experiments is the main weakness, but the technical content and empirical trends are strong. I lean toward acceptance.
