

This paper introduces PDMM, a parallel direction method of multipliers for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. Unlike ADMM—which updates primal variables in a Gauss-Seidel fashion limited to two blocks—PDMM employs Jacobian-style updates for multiple blocks. The key innovation is the dual backward step, which compensates for the reduced information propagation inherent in Jacobian updates by adaptively reducing the dual step size. This mechanism enables convergence guarantees under general conditions, establishing O(1/T) iteration complexity similar to ADMM.  

The supplementary materials demonstrate that two prior methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, clarifying their relationships within the broader framework of augmented Lagrangian-based decomposition methods. Experimental results show PDMM outperforms ADMM and its variants in tested scenarios. However, the empirical findings are somewhat counterintuitive: Jacobian-type methods typically exhibit slower convergence than Gauss-Seidel counterparts due to reduced information flow. The authors should elaborate on why PDMM achieves faster convergence despite this, as this is a critical point requiring justification.  

The dual backward step appears to be the novel core contribution enabling PDMM’s convergence under general conditions, where standard Jacobian updates would otherwise fail. This insight is significant, as it resolves a long-standing limitation in parallelizable decomposition methods. The paper successfully positions PDMM as a flexible alternative for distributed optimization, expanding the toolkit for large-scale convex problems.  

While the theoretical contribution is sound and the experimental validation is thorough, the counterintuitive convergence behavior merits deeper analysis. The authors should address: (1) whether the dual backward step’s adaptive step size fundamentally alters the convergence dynamics compared to standard Jacobian methods, and (2) if the observed speedup is consistent across problem classes or dependent on specific problem structures. Without this, the empirical advantage remains partially unexplained.  

The writing is generally clear, but minor grammatical improvements are needed: "compensate the limited information propagation" → "compensate for reduced information propagation"; "give better understanding" → "provide clearer understanding"; "results are a little counter intuitive" → "results are somewhat counterintuitive." The supplementary material’s role in contextualizing prior work is well utilized, strengthening the paper’s theoretical narrative.  

Overall, PDMM presents a meaningful advancement in parallelizable optimization. The dual backward step is a compelling solution to a known challenge, and the method’s flexibility for distributed settings is well motivated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the convergence behavior discrepancy with classical Jacobian methods, as this is pivotal to the paper’s credibility and impact. The current results suggest a promising direction, but the theoretical justification for the empirical performance gap needs reinforcement.  

The contribution is novel within the context of multi-block decomposition methods, and the technical presentation is rigorous. With the suggested clarification on convergence dynamics, this work would make a valuable addition to the literature on distributed optimization. The authors should ensure the discussion of the counterintuitive results directly addresses the theoretical basis for PDMM’s superior empirical performance.
