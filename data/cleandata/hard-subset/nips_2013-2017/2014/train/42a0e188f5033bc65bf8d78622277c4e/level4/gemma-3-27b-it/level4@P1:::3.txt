This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM to handle optimization problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PDMM (Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers) randomly selects K blocks, updates the corresponding primal variables, and then updates the dual variables using a backward step. The authors highlight the importance of this backward step, as it promotes a more conservative dual update, contributing to global convergence. The algorithm also allows the incorporation of a proximal term in the primal update, simplifying the solution of certain optimization problems. Theoretical analysis establishes the algorithm’s global convergence and iteration complexity. With a total of J blocks and K randomly selected blocks per iteration, the convergence rate is O(1/T) – independent of J and K, and thus comparable to standard ADMM.

The primary advantages of PDMM are (1) its ability to fully parallelize the primal update step and (2) its potential for larger update stepsizes compared to other methods. Specifically, PDMM can outperform sADMM by allowing for larger step sizes. It offers greater flexibility than PJADMM and better parallelizability than GSADMM. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's performance on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, achieving promising experimental results. The algorithm achieves the desired accuracy with competitive computational time. However, the authors do not report performance with a parallel implementation, which would be a natural extension given the algorithm’s design. Tuning the three parameters of PDMM (τi, νi, and ηjt) may also be less straightforward compared to traditional ADMM.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to solving ADMM-type problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm achieves a convergence rate comparable to ADMM while offering opportunities for parallelization and exhibiting strong practical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical and empirical components are robust.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems, addressing a known challenge in the field.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis, establishing both global convergence and iteration complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate PDMM’s competitive performance on relevant benchmark problems (RPCA and overlapping group lasso).
*   **Flexibility:** The inclusion of a proximal term and the adjustable step sizes enhance the algorithm's versatility.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions PDMM within the existing literature, demonstrating its relationship to sADMM and PJADMM.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The absence of results demonstrating actual speedup from parallelization is a missed opportunity.
*   **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** The algorithm requires tuning three parameters, which may be less convenient than standard ADMM.
*   **Convergence Rate:** While comparable to ADMM, the O(1/T) rate isn’t necessarily a significant improvement and could benefit from further analysis or techniques to potentially accelerate convergence.
