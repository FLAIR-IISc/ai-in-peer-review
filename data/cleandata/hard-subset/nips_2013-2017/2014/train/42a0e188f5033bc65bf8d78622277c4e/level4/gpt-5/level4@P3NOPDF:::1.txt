Summary:
The paper studies convex optimization with block-separable objectives under linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM. At each iteration, PDMM updates K randomly selected primal blocks in parallel and applies a backward step to refine the dual variable. The authors establish a sublinear convergence rate and show that PDMM unifies several existing ADMM variants.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written, technically sound, and addresses a relevant problem with a method that is both parallelizable and general. The theoretical analysis appears careful, and the unifying perspective is valuable. I recommend acceptance, with the following clarifications and minor revisions to improve readability and completeness.

Major comments and questions:
1) Algorithm specification (Eqs. (5)–(6))
- It is unclear whether all coordinates (blocks) of y are updated at iteration t or only a single dual block i. The notation y^{t+1} and ĥy^{t+1} suggests full dual updates, but the text hints at blockwise updates. Please clarify:
  - Precisely which blocks of the dual are updated at each iteration, and in what order relative to the primal updates.
  - How the block(s) i of the dual are selected (same random subset as the primal? independently sampled? deterministically?).
  - Whether updates are synchronous across the K selected primal blocks and how the dual “backward step” aggregates their effects.
  - Provide explicit pseudocode that defines the sampling scheme S_t ⊂ {1,…,J} with |S_t|=K, the dual update set (if blockwise), and the exact formulas for y^{t+1} and ĥy^{t+1}.
  - If the dual update is full (all blocks), explain how step sizes are scaled with K to ensure stability.

2) Theorem 2: missing summation
- In the proof of Theorem 2, it appears that ∑_{i=1}^I is missing immediately after a left brace “{”. Please correct this typographical error and check related expressions for consistency.

3) Interpreting and specializing Theorem 2
- Since PDMM generalizes several ADMM variants, please discuss how Theorem 2 specializes to known convergence results:
  - For classic 2-block ADMM, randomized block ADMM, and other variants you claim to recover, identify the parameter choices (e.g., K, J, step sizes, relaxation) and show that the resulting rate and constants match known bounds.
  - Clarify whether your rate is ergodic (e.g., O(1/t) on averaged iterates) or non-ergodic, and how expectations over the sampling enter the guarantees.
  - Provide guidance on parameter selection (penalty, backward-step parameters) that is sufficient for convergence and near-optimal in practice.

4) Splitting of A and choice of K
- The theorem’s constants presumably depend on the block partition of A and possibly on quantities such as block Lipschitz constants or spectral norms. Please discuss:
  - How to choose the block partition of A to optimize the rate constants (e.g., balance block sizes, minimize maximum block spectral norm, align with sparsity).
  - Trade-offs between larger K (more parallelism) and the resulting step-size/scaling in the dual backward step; when is increasing K beneficial in wall-clock time?

5) Relation to tensor completion
- The tensor completion setting (“Tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data,” 2012) has a natural multi-block structure. Please comment on:
  - Which ADMM variant (including PDMM with different K) is theoretically preferable for this problem under your framework.
  - Any empirical evidence or guidance on block partitioning and parameter choices for tensor completion, even if limited to a brief discussion or supplemental experiment.

Additional clarity requests:
- Precisely define the “backward step” for the dual: is it an extragradient/proximal correction? How does it relate to over-relaxation?
- State assumptions used in Theorem 2 (convexity, smoothness/nonsmoothness, full row rank of A, etc.) in one place and clarify to which variables they apply.
- Describe how residuals are maintained efficiently when only K primal blocks are updated (e.g., incremental updates to Ax−b), especially in parallel/distributed settings.

Minor issues:
- In (9), insert a space after “min”.
- Line 217: remove the space before “Section”.
- Check equation references (e.g., (5)–(6)) and notation for hats/superscripts for consistency throughout.

Recommendation:
Accept. The contributions are solid and relevant. Addressing the clarification points—especially the precise algorithm specification around (5)–(6) and a more thorough discussion of Theorem 2’s specialization and practical guidance—will significantly strengthen the paper.
