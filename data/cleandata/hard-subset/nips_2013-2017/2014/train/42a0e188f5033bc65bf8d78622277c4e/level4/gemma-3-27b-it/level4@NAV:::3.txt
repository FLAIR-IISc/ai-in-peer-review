This paper introduces a randomized, parallelized version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for problems with multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) operates by randomly selecting *K* blocks in each iteration, updating the corresponding primal variables, and then performing a backward step for the dual update. The authors convincingly demonstrate the benefit of this backward step, highlighting its role in creating a more conservative dual update that facilitates global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm’s inclusion of a proximal term in the primal update simplifies the solution process for certain optimization problems. A thorough theoretical analysis accompanies the algorithm, proving global convergence and establishing an iteration complexity of O(J/(TK)) after *T* iterations, where *J* is the total number of blocks.

The key strengths of PADMM lie in (1) its potential for full parallelization of the primal update step and (2) its ability to accommodate larger step sizes compared to other methods. Specifically, the authors show PADMM can outperform sADMM due to its larger stepsize capabilities, and it offers greater flexibility than PJADMM and improved parallelizability over GSADMM. The algorithm's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, yielding promising results indicating reduced computation time while achieving the required accuracy. However, a reported performance evaluation utilizing a parallel implementation is currently absent. Tuning PADMM's three parameters presents a slightly increased complexity compared to standard ADMM. 

In conclusion, this work presents a valuable contribution to the field of ADMM-based optimization, particularly for constrained problems. The algorithm achieves a convergence rate comparable to traditional ADMM while enabling parallel computation and showing strong empirical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and experimental validation are robust.
