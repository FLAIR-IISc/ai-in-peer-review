This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm designed to minimize block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. In contrast to the classic Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which updates primal variables sequentially in a Gauss-Seidel fashion and is typically limited to two blocks, PDMM performs parallel updates for multiple blocks in a Jacobian-like manner. The central contribution of the paper is the introduction of a "dual backward step," which cleverly compensates for the limited information propagation inherent in parallel Jacobian updates by effectively reducing the step size for the dual variables. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate for PDMM, placing it on par with the theoretical guarantees for standard ADMM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed method is well-motivated and addresses a significant limitation of ADMM, namely its inherent sequentiality, which can be a bottleneck in modern parallel and distributed computing environments. The "dual backward step" appears to be a novel and critical component that ensures convergence under conditions as general as those for ADMM. This mechanism is the key to making the parallel update scheme viable.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is solid, providing convergence guarantees that match those of well-established methods. This gives practitioners confidence in the algorithm's stability and reliability.

3.  **Unification of Existing Methods:** A significant strength, detailed in the supplementary materials, is the unification of previous methods. By demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, the authors provide a clearer understanding of the relationships within the broader landscape of augmented Lagrangian-based decomposition methods.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of PDMM in comparison to ADMM and other variants.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

One point that merits further discussion is the strong empirical performance of the method. The results are somewhat counter-intuitive, as Jacobian-style iterative methods are generally known to converge more slowly than their Gauss-Seidel counterparts, which leverage the most recently updated information within each iteration.

The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide more intuition or a deeper explanation for why PDMM circumvents this typical drawback. Is the "dual backward step" solely responsible for closing this performance gap, or are there other properties of the tested problems or the algorithm's structure at play? Elaborating on this aspect would not only address a natural question from readers familiar with classical numerical methods but also provide a more profound understanding of the algorithm's power.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable and practical extension to the family of ADMM-like algorithms. The proposed PDMM offers a compelling new alternative for large-scale and distributed optimization, and the introduction of the dual backward step is a clever and essential contribution to enabling parallel updates while maintaining rigorous convergence guarantees. The work is a solid contribution to the field.
