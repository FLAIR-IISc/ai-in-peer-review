

This paper introduces a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. Unlike ADMM, which updates primal variables via Gauss-Seidel iteration (limited to two blocks), PDMM employs Jacobian-style updates across multiple blocks. The core contribution is the dual backward step, which addresses restricted information flow in Jacobian updates by adaptively adjusting the dual step size. This mechanism enables convergence guarantees under general conditions, analogous to ADMM, with O(1/T) convergence rates established for PDMM.  

The supplementary material demonstrates that two prior methods—sADMM and PJADMM—are special cases of PDMM, clarifying their relationships within the broader landscape of augmented Lagrangian-based decomposition techniques. Experimental results show PDMM outperforms ADMM and other variants in tested scenarios. However, the observed efficiency of the Jacobian approach here is somewhat counterintuitive, as Jacobian-type methods typically exhibit slower convergence than Gauss-Seidel variants in practice. The authors should elaborate on why this discrepancy arises in their setting, particularly regarding the role of the dual backward step in overcoming standard convergence limitations.  

The dual backward step appears novel and essential for establishing convergence without restrictive assumptions, distinguishing PDMM from existing parallel methods. This work significantly expands the toolkit for distributed optimization by providing a theoretically grounded framework that accommodates arbitrary block partitions. The method’s flexibility in handling multiple blocks while maintaining convergence is a notable advancement.  

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on decomposition methods. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation supports the claims. To strengthen the manuscript, the authors should: (1) explicitly address the counterintuitive convergence behavior in the main text, (2) clarify how the dual backward step specifically mitigates the typical drawbacks of Jacobian updates, and (3) discuss practical implementation considerations (e.g., communication overhead in distributed settings). The connection to sADMM and PJADMM is valuable but could be expanded to highlight how PDMM generalizes their limitations.  

The writing is generally clear, though minor phrasing improvements would enhance readability (e.g., "compensate" → "compensates," "effectively reducing" → "effectively adjusts"). The technical arguments are well-structured, and the supplementary material adequately supports the claims. With the suggested clarifications, this work would be a strong addition to the field.
