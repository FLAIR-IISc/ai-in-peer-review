This paper proposes PADMM, a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for optimization problems with a multi-block structure. The core idea is to randomly select a subset of K blocks in each iteration, update the corresponding primal variables in parallel, and then perform a "backward step" to update the dual variables. The authors effectively illustrate the importance of this backward step, which makes the dual update more conservative and is key to enabling global convergence. The framework also flexibly incorporates a proximal term in the primal update, which can simplify the resulting subproblems.

The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, establishing its global convergence and deriving its iteration complexity. For a problem with J total blocks, where K blocks are randomly selected per iteration, the authors establish a convergence rate of O(J/(TK)) after T iterations.

**Strengths:**

The main advantages of the PADMM algorithm are its strong theoretical underpinnings and practical design.
1.  **Parallelization:** The algorithm is inherently parallel, as the updates for the K selected primal blocks can be executed simultaneously. This makes it well-suited for modern multi-core and distributed computing environments.
2.  **Improved Convergence Properties:** Compared to other stochastic or randomized ADMM variants, PADMM's design allows for a relatively large update step-size, which can translate to faster practical convergence. The authors make a convincing case that it can be faster than sADMM, more flexible than PJADMM, and more parallelizable than GSADMM.
3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The algorithm is evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results are quite promising, showing that PADMM achieves the desired accuracy in less computation time than several competing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of True Parallel Experiments:** A significant limitation of the evaluation is that the authors have not reported the performance of a true parallel implementation. While the algorithm is designed for parallel execution, the experiments appear to have been run serially. Demonstrating empirical speed-up on a multi-core or distributed architecture would substantially strengthen the paper's practical claims.
2.  **Parameter Tuning:** Compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM introduces additional parameters. This increased complexity can make the algorithm less convenient to tune in practice. A more detailed discussion or heuristic for setting these parameters would be a valuable addition.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this paper makes an interesting and valuable contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The proposed PADMM algorithm provides a well-motivated method for solving multi-block ADMM-type problems, offering the significant benefit of parallelization while maintaining a convergence rate comparable to standard ADMM. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and the empirical validation are solid. Despite the lack of experiments on a true parallel system, the work presents a promising algorithm that is likely to be of high interest to the optimization and machine learning communities.
