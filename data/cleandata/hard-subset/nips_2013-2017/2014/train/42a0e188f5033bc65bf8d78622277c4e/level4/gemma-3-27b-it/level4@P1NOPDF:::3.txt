This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM, designed to handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, the proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) algorithm randomly selects *K* blocks, updates the primal variables for those blocks, and then updates the dual variables using a backward substitution step. The authors highlight the importance of this backward step, explaining that it results in a more conservative dual update, which is crucial for ensuring global convergence. The algorithm also incorporates a proximal term into the primal update, simplifying the solution process for a range of optimization problems. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating the algorithm’s global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, with a total of *J* blocks and PADMM randomly selecting *K* blocks per iteration, the convergence rate after *T* iterations is O(*J* / (*T* *K*)).

The primary advantages of PADMM are (1) its ability to fully parallelize the primal update step and (2) its allowance for relatively larger step sizes compared to other methods. Notably, PADMM can outperform sADMM due to these larger step sizes. It offers greater flexibility than PJADMM and improved parallelizability compared to GSADMM. The authors evaluate the algorithm on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems. The experimental results are promising, showing that PADMM achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computation time. However, the authors do not report performance results obtained with a parallel implementation of the algorithm. Compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM introduces three parameters that require tuning, making it slightly less convenient to configure. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to solving ADMM-type problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm maintains the same convergence rate as standard ADMM while enabling parallelization and demonstrating promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation are solid.
