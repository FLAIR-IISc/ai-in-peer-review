

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM designed for solving optimization problems with multiple blocks of linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as while two-block ADMM has been extensively studied, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates K randomly selected blocks in parallel at each iteration, incorporating a dual backward step that makes dual updates more conservative, which the authors demonstrate is crucial for convergence. The algorithm also allows for proximal terms in the primal updates, providing flexibility for different problem structures. Theoretical contributions include establishing global convergence and O(J/(TK)) iteration complexity with constant step size, where J is the total number of blocks and T the number of iterations. The paper shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective on these methods.

The paper demonstrates the practical utility of PDMM through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where PDMM consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods. Notably, PDMM achieves faster convergence than sADMM and RBSUMM, and its parallel nature enables efficient implementation, particularly when K is appropriately chosen. The authors also establish connections between PDMM and existing methods, showing how the algorithm's parameters (τi, νi) can be optimized based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish global convergence and iteration complexity under reasonable assumptions, which is a significant contribution given the lack of theoretical guarantees for many multi-block ADMM variants. The proofs are well-structured and the theoretical results are clearly presented. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines (sADMM, RBSUMM, GSADMM) and thorough evaluation across two distinct applications. The authors carefully address the trade-offs between convergence rate and computational complexity, particularly regarding the choice of K (number of blocks updated in parallel).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within existing literature, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) is particularly well-explained, providing valuable insight for readers. The experimental section clearly presents results with appropriate metrics (objective value, residual, runtime), and the figures effectively illustrate convergence behavior. Minor suggestions for improvement include providing more details on the parameter selection process for different applications and including convergence plots for the overlapping group lasso problem.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by generalizing ADMM to handle multiple blocks in a parallel randomized fashion. While randomized block coordinate methods exist, PDMM's key innovation is its incorporation of the dual backward step, which enables convergence with larger step sizes. The connection between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM) provides a unifying theoretical framework that advances our understanding of these approaches. The application to overlapping group lasso is particularly novel, as the authors demonstrate that PDMM can handle this problem in a randomized block coordinate fashion, while the corresponding RBCD problem remains open.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications where parallelization is essential. The ability to handle multiple blocks in parallel while maintaining convergence guarantees addresses a critical bottleneck in distributed optimization. The practical performance improvements demonstrated in the experiments (faster convergence, reduced runtime) show that the theoretical advances translate to real-world benefits. The algorithm's flexibility in handling different problem structures (through proximal terms) and its applicability to a range of constrained optimization problems make it broadly useful.

**Strengths:** 
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with global convergence and iteration complexity guarantees
- Clear connections to existing methods that provide deeper theoretical understanding
- Practical implementation with promising experimental results
- Effective parallelization capability that enables faster computation
- Applicable to a range of problems including overlapping group lasso

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not report results with parallel implementation, making it difficult to assess the true parallel speedup
- Parameter tuning (τi, νi) requires more detailed guidance for different problem structures
- The theoretical analysis does not fully explore the trade-offs between convergence rate and computational cost when varying K
- The experimental section could provide more insight into when PDMM is most advantageous compared to other methods

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. It successfully addresses a significant gap in multi-block ADMM methods with both theoretical rigor and practical utility. The authors have clearly demonstrated the advantages of their approach through comprehensive experiments. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in scalable optimization. While the lack of parallel implementation results is a minor drawback, the theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial and would be valuable to the NIPS community. The paper meets all criteria for high-quality scientific work and makes a meaningful advancement in the field.
