This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), designed to efficiently solve block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints, particularly those with multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) operates by randomly selecting *K* blocks at each iteration, updating their primal variables, and then updating the dual variables with a novel backward step. This backward step is motivated by the authors' observation that aggressive dual updates can hinder convergence, and it aims to promote more conservative progress. PDMM also incorporates a Bregman divergence term, enabling the inclusion of proximal terms for solving a broader range of optimization problems. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating global convergence and establishing an iteration complexity of O(1/T). They highlight that the algorithm’s performance is influenced by the number of blocks *J* and the number of randomly selected blocks *K*, with a convergence rate related to *J/(TK)*.

The primary strengths of PDMM lie in its potential for parallelization – enabling efficient primal updates – and its ability to utilize relatively large step sizes compared to other methods. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that PDMM can outperform sequential ADMM variants like sADMM (by allowing larger step sizes) and offers greater flexibility than PJADMM while exhibiting better parallelizability than GSADMM. The algorithm's connection to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM as special cases) provides valuable insight into the roles of the proposed backward step and parameter choices. The empirical evaluation on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso demonstrates promising performance, with PDMM achieving desired accuracy in competitive runtimes.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the authors mention the potential for parallel implementation, they do *not* present any results demonstrating actual speedup on parallel hardware. Including such results would significantly strengthen the paper and better justify the algorithm’s name. The method introduces three parameters (τi, νi, ηjt) which, while theoretically grounded, may require more tuning effort compared to standard ADMM, and a discussion of practical guidelines for selecting these parameters would be beneficial. Additionally, the experimental section could be expanded. For example, it would be helpful to see how PDMM performs on problem instances with varying degrees of block overlap in the overlapping group lasso setting. Finally, the notation, while generally clear, could be improved – for example, defining variables like 'di' more explicitly earlier in the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed PDMM algorithm presents a novel approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems, incorporating a theoretically motivated backward step.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate competitive performance.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:**  Establishing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases of PDMM provides valuable context and insight.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The algorithm’s parallel nature has the potential to significantly improve performance on large-scale problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The absence of experimental results demonstrating speedup on parallel hardware weakens the claim of parallel efficiency.
*   **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** The introduction of three parameters may make the algorithm less convenient to use in practice.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:**  Expanding the experimental section with more diverse datasets and problem settings would enhance the paper’s impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that proposes a promising new algorithm for solving a challenging class of optimization problems. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the lack of parallel implementation results and the potential for parameter tuning complexity are concerns, the novelty and potential benefits of PDMM outweigh these weaknesses. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the demonstration of actual parallel performance.
