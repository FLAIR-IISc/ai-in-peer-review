This paper introduces a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed to handle problems with multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) iterates by randomly selecting *K* blocks, updating the corresponding primal variables, and then performing a backward step to update the dual variables. The authors highlight the importance of this backward step, demonstrating that it promotes a more conservative dual update, which is crucial for ensuring global convergence. The algorithm’s flexibility is further enhanced by allowing the inclusion of a proximal term in the primal update, simplifying the solution of certain optimization problems. A theoretical analysis accompanies the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and providing an iteration complexity bound. Specifically, after *T* iterations, with a total of *J* blocks and a block selection size of *K*, the convergence rate is shown to be O(*J* / (*T* *K*)).

The key benefits of PADMM are its ability to (1) fully parallelize the primal update step and (2) utilize relatively large step sizes compared to existing methods.  Notably, PADMM can outperform sADMM due to its allowance of larger stepsizes. It offers greater flexibility than PJADMM and improved parallelizability compared to GSADMM. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, and the experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating that PADMM achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computational cost. However, the authors do not present results from a parallel implementation of PADMM.  Furthermore, PADMM introduces three parameters that may require more careful tuning than traditional ADMM. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of ADMM-based optimization, particularly for problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm achieves a convergence rate comparable to that of ADMM, while simultaneously enabling parallelization and exhibiting strong empirical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and the experimental validation are sound.
