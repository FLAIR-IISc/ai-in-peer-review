Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel block-coordinate variant of augmented Lagrangian methods. PDMM updates a random subset of primal blocks in parallel (Jacobi-style) and introduces a dual “backward step” to temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented term. The method sets per-row dual step sizes τi and backward-step factors νi as functions of the sparsity pattern of A (via row degrees di) and the number of blocks K updated per iteration. The paper establishes global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic complexity with constant step sizes, and shows how to incorporate Bregman proximal terms to obtain efficient inexact updates. It further demonstrates that two known multi-block ADMM variants—two-block ADMM with splitting (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM)—are special cases of PDMM, clarifying their relationship via the backward-step mechanism. Applications to RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate empirical gains over ADMM variants including GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

This work builds on a rich literature on ADMM and multi-block extensions. While two-block ADMM is well understood, naive multi-block Gauss–Seidel ADMM can diverge and often requires additional assumptions (e.g., BSUMM) or proximal regularization (PJADMM). Randomized block updates have been studied (e.g., RBSUMM), but typically only one block is updated per iteration and convergence guarantees can require error bounds or diminishing steps. PDMM contributes a randomized, truly parallel multi-block scheme with constant steps and explicit dependence on sparsity, alongside a principled dual backward step to stabilize Jacobi updates.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea: the dual backward step counteracts the implicit dual ascent from the quadratic penalty, making Jacobi-style primal updates stable even with multiple blocks.
- Theoretical guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard convexity assumptions and constant step sizes; coverage of exact and inexact (Bregman) updates in a unified framework.
- Conceptual unification: sADMM and PJADMM emerge as limiting cases of PDMM, illuminating when small dual steps (with exact primal progress) vs. larger dual steps (with proximalized primal updates) are preferable.
- Practical relevance: explicit use of A’s sparsity (row degrees) and the number of updated blocks K to set τi and νi; applicability to overlapping group models where standard RBCD remains challenging.
- Empirical results: on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM shows competitive or superior convergence in iterations and wall-clock time relative to several ADMM variants.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality:
- The derivation and role of τi and νi, especially equation (8), deserve more intuition in the main text. An ablation comparing PDMM with and without the backward step, and with different K and di, would strengthen the case that the backward step is the key stabilizer for Jacobi updates.
- The complexity results are ergodic; a brief discussion of non-ergodic rates or residual decay would help position PDMM among recent ADMM analyses.
- The inexact PDMM section relies on conditions linking the curvature of ϕjt and the Lipschitz constant of ψjt. It would help to state these assumptions explicitly in the main text and give concrete choices for common losses/penalties.

Clarity:
- The paper is notation-heavy. A concise algorithm box with all steps (x-update, dual ascent, backward step) and the default choices for τi and νi would aid readability.
- The definition of the row neighborhood N(i), degree di, and K̃i is crucial for the step sizes; consider moving these to the main text early and providing a small illustrative example.
- Several typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “seperable,” “defiend,” “contast,” “permutate”) should be corrected. Some equations could be spaced and labeled more consistently.

Originality:
- While PDMM builds on existing ADMM variants, the dual backward step combined with randomized multi-block parallel updates and steps tied to sparsity appears novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is conceptually useful.

Significance:
- The ability to safely perform randomized parallel Jacobi updates with multiple blocks and constant steps is valuable for large-scale and distributed settings, particularly with sparse A and overlapping structures. The paper can influence practice by offering practical step-size rules and highlighting the stabilizing role of the backward step.

Experimental evaluation:
- Experiments are sequential Matlab implementations. Given the emphasis on parallelism, a distributed or multi-threaded evaluation would better reflect PDMM’s advantages and the communication/synchronization implications of Aixt+1 aggregation.
- Clarify tuning: how ρ, τi, νi, and proximal parameters were chosen for all baselines; ensure fairness in parameter sweeps. Report primal/dual residuals and feasibility metrics alongside objective values.
- The observation that GSADMM can converge in fewer iterations but be slower in time is interesting; a breakdown of per-iteration costs would make this more convincing. Also, include a comparison between random vs cyclic selection for PDMM and RBSUMM, since selection strategy affects convergence.
- Expand sensitivity analyses: vary K, di (via synthetic sparsity patterns), and problem sizes; report how τi = K/[K̃i(2J−K)] and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i affect stability and speed.

Pros:
- Novel and well-motivated dual backward step enabling stable Jacobi-style multi-block updates.
- Convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant step sizes under standard assumptions.
- Explicit exploitation of sparsity and block count in step-size rules.
- Unifying view of sADMM and PJADMM; applicability to overlapping groups.
- Solid empirical performance on two representative applications.

Cons:
- Heavy notation and some presentation issues; key intuitions (for τi, νi) are not emphasized in the main text.
- Lack of parallel/distributed experiments; limited analysis of communication and synchronization overhead.
- Ergodic complexity only; limited discussion of non-ergodic measures and practical stopping criteria.
- Some baselines (e.g., GSADMM) are reported as sequential; fairness in tuning and selection (random vs cyclic) could be clarified.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances multi-block ADMM-style methods by making randomized parallel Jacobi updates both principled and practical. Strengthening the exposition around the backward step, expanding the empirical analysis to parallel environments, and clarifying parameter selection would further improve the paper.
