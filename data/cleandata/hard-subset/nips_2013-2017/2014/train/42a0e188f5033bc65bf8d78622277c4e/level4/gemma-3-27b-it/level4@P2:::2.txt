This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. Existing Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) approaches struggle with efficient generalization to more than two blocks, often relying on Gauss-Seidel updates which can be slow to converge. This work proposes a Jacobian-style update – updating multiple primal blocks in parallel – coupled with a novel “dual backward step” designed to compensate for the reduced information propagation inherent in this parallel approach. The authors establish global convergence with an O(1/T) iteration complexity, similar to ADMM.

The paper positions PDMM within the broader landscape of ADMM variants, demonstrating (in the supplementary materials) that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of the proposed framework. This provides a unifying perspective and deeper understanding of the relationships between these algorithms. Experimental results on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso demonstrate PDMM’s effectiveness compared to ADMM and its variants. While the superior performance of a Jacobian-style method compared to Gauss-Seidel approaches is somewhat counterintuitive, the authors argue the dual backward step is key to achieving convergence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The dual backward step appears to be a novel contribution and a crucial component for ensuring convergence in a multi-block, parallel setting.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides convergence analysis with a standard O(1/T) rate, bolstering its theoretical foundation.
*   **Unifying Framework:**  Demonstrating that existing methods are special cases of PDMM offers valuable insight and context.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso provide evidence of PDMM’s practical effectiveness.
*   **Potential for Distributed Optimization:** The parallel nature of PDMM makes it a promising candidate for distributed optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Counterintuitive Results:** The superior performance of a Jacobian method compared to Gauss-Seidel requires more detailed explanation. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of why the dual backward step overcomes the typical convergence limitations of such methods.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of step sizes (τi and νi) is crucial.  The paper details how these are calculated, but a sensitivity analysis or discussion of practical guidelines for tuning these parameters would be helpful.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be streamlined in certain sections, particularly regarding the indexing of blocks and variables.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important challenge in multi-block ADMM – efficient parallelization – with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The convergence guarantees and empirical results suggest that PDMM is a valuable addition to the toolkit of optimization algorithms.
*   The unification of existing methods within the PDMM framework provides useful insights.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed explanation regarding the surprisingly good performance of the Jacobian-style update is a minor concern.
*   The paper assumes some familiarity with ADMM variants and could benefit from a more accessible introduction for readers less versed in the field.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that proposes a novel and theoretically justified algorithm for solving a challenging optimization problem. The dual backward step appears to be a significant contribution, and the experimental results are encouraging.  While some aspects could benefit from further clarification, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**.
