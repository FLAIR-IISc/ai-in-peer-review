This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), referred to as PADMM, which can efficiently handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM randomly selects K blocks, updates the block primal vector, and then updates the dual vector via a backward step. The authors emphasize the importance of using a backward step, as it makes the dual update more conservative, enabling global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm allows for the addition of a proximal term to the primal update, making certain optimization problems easier to solve.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, when there are J blocks in total, and the PADMM algorithm randomly selects K blocks at each iteration, the convergence rate of the algorithm is O(J/(TK)) after T iterations of updates. This theoretical guarantee is a significant contribution, as it provides insight into the algorithm's behavior and performance.

The main advantages of the PADMM algorithm are twofold. Firstly, it allows for full parallelization of the primal step, which can significantly reduce computation time. Secondly, it permits relatively large update step sizes compared to other methods, making it more efficient. In particular, PADMM can be faster than the stochastic ADMM (sADMM) algorithm, as it allows for greater step sizes. Additionally, PADMM is more flexible than the parallelizable Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more parallelizable than the Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM).

The authors evaluate the algorithm on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating that PADMM achieves the desired accuracy with less computation time. However, the authors do not report the performance of PADMM with parallel implementation, which would further highlight its benefits.

Compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM has three parameters that require tuning, which may be less convenient. Nevertheless, this is a minor drawback considering the algorithm's advantages.

Overall, this paper makes an interesting contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are present. The algorithm has the same convergence rate as ADMM but allows for parallelization and exhibits promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical and empirical parts are solid.
