This paper proposes a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) to minimize block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. In contrast with ADMM, which updates primal variables using a Gauss-Seidel manner but is limited to two blocks, this work proposes updating the primal blocks in the Jacobian manner with multiple blocks. The main result of the paper is the introduction of the dual backward step, which compensates for the limited information propagation in the Jacobian updates by effectively reducing the step size for the dual updates. Similar to ADMM, O(1/T) type convergence results are established for PDMM in this paper.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the context of ADMM and its variants. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the connections between PDMM and existing methods, including sADMM and PJADMM, which are shown to be special cases of the proposed method. This provides a better understanding of the relationship between different methods in the landscape of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and results. The technical analysis and proofs are relegated to the supplement, making the main text more accessible to a broader audience. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the PDMM method compared with ADMM and other variants.

One aspect that may seem counterintuitive is the faster convergence of PDMM compared to Gauss-Seidel type methods, as Jacobian type methods typically exhibit slower convergence. However, the dual backward step in PDMM appears to be a key innovation that enables convergence under general conditions, similar to ADMM. The proposed method provides more alternatives and possibilities for distributed optimization.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of the dual backward step, which is a novel contribution to the field of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian.
* The establishment of O(1/T) type convergence results for PDMM, which is comparable to ADMM.
* The demonstration of the effectiveness of PDMM compared with ADMM and other variants through experimental results.
* The provision of a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and results, making the paper accessible to a broader audience.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The limited discussion of the implications of the dual backward step and its potential applications in other contexts.
* The lack of a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the number of blocks and the efficiency of each subproblem in PDMM.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel contribution to the field of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared with ADMM and other variants.
* The paper provides a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may benefit from a more detailed analysis of the implications of the dual backward step and its potential applications in other contexts.
* The paper could be improved by providing a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the number of blocks and the efficiency of each subproblem in PDMM.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian and demonstrates the effectiveness of PDMM compared with ADMM and other variants. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper is well-organized and provides a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and results.
