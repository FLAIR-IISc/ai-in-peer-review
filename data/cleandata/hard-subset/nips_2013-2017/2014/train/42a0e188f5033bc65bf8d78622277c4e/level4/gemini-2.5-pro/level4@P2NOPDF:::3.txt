This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed for optimization problems with a multi-block structure. In each iteration, the proposed algorithm randomly samples K blocks of variables, performs parallel updates on the corresponding primal variables, and then updates the dual variables via a crucial backward step. The authors effectively demonstrate that this backward step introduces a more conservative dual update, which is key to ensuring the algorithm's global convergence. Furthermore, the method allows for the inclusion of a proximal term in the primal update, which can simplify the resulting subproblems for certain classes of optimization tasks.

The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, formally establishing its global convergence and deriving its iteration complexity. Specifically, for a problem with a total of J blocks where K blocks are sampled in each iteration, the convergence rate is shown to be O(J/(TK)) after T iterations.

The primary advantages of PADMM are twofold: (1) it enables full parallelization of the primal variable updates, and (2) it can accommodate a larger update stepsize compared to related methods. The authors draw favorable comparisons to existing work, arguing that PADMM can be faster than stochastic ADMM (sADMM) due to its larger allowable stepsize, more flexible than PJADMM, and more amenable to parallelization than Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM).

The algorithm's efficacy is evaluated on two standard problems: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating that PADMM can achieve the desired solution accuracy with less computational time compared to competing methods.

Despite these strengths, there are areas for improvement. A significant weakness is the lack of experimental results from a true parallel implementation. While the algorithm is designed for parallel settings, the reported performance gains are based on serial simulations of the parallel process. Evaluating the method's practical speedup on parallel hardware would be essential to fully validate its claims. Additionally, compared to the traditional two-block ADMM, PADMM introduces more parameters, which could make tuning more challenging in practice.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization and ADMM-type methods for multi-block problems. The proposed algorithm achieves a convergence rate comparable to standard ADMM while offering the significant benefits of parallelization. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and empirical validation are sound. It represents a solid piece of work with promising practical implications.
