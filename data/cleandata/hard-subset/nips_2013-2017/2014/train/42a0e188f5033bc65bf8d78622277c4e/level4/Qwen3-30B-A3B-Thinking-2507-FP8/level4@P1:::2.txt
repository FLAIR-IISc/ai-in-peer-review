

This paper introduces a parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving optimization problems with block-separable convex objectives subject to linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates multiple primal blocks in parallel (in a Jacobian fashion) rather than sequentially (Gauss-Seidel fashion), and introduces a novel dual backward step that ensures convergence under general conditions. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size, showing that two existing methods—sADMM and PJADMM—are special cases of their framework. This connection provides valuable theoretical insight into the relationship between different ADMM variants. The paper also demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness in handling overlapping blocks, a challenging scenario where standard block coordinate methods fail.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the dual backward step is a key innovation that compensates for limited information propagation in parallel updates, addressing a fundamental challenge in Jacobian-style methods. Second, the authors carefully consider the sparsity structure of the constraint matrix and the number of randomly updated blocks when determining step sizes, which is crucial for practical implementation. Third, the theoretical analysis establishes convergence under relatively mild assumptions, and the experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods.

The technical quality is strong, with clear theoretical results supported by appropriate assumptions. The convergence proofs are rigorous, and the connection to existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) is well-explained, enhancing the paper's significance by placing it within the broader context of ADMM variants. The experimental section is thorough, comparing PDMM with relevant baselines including ADMM, sADMM, GSADMM, and RBSUMM, and demonstrating consistent performance improvements. The authors provide detailed parameter settings and clear convergence metrics, making the results reproducible.

The paper excels in originality. While Jacobian variants of ADMM have been studied before, the dual backward step introduced here is novel and directly addresses the convergence issues that have plagued previous approaches. The extension to overlapping blocks via randomized block coordinate descent is particularly noteworthy, as this is an open problem for standard block coordinate methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, including the recent RBSUMM method, which requires restrictive assumptions and lacks known iteration complexity.

I find the experimental results somewhat counterintuitive: Jacobian methods typically converge more slowly than Gauss-Seidel methods due to less efficient information propagation, yet PDMM outperforms sequential methods like GSADMM in runtime. The authors do not sufficiently explain this phenomenon, which is a notable gap. I would have liked to see a more detailed discussion of why parallel updates with the dual backward step can overcome the typical convergence disadvantages of Jacobian approaches. This is particularly important since the paper claims to provide "better understanding of dual backward step" through the connection between sADMM and PJADMM.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation. The supplementary materials provide additional technical details that support the main claims. The writing is clear and accessible to readers familiar with optimization methods, though some of the theoretical derivations could be more intuitively presented.

In terms of significance, PDMM offers a practical and theoretically grounded approach to distributed optimization with multiple blocks, which is increasingly important for large-scale machine learning problems. The method's ability to handle overlapping blocks opens new applications in structured sparsity problems. The convergence rate of O(1/T) matches that of standard ADMM variants, making it competitive with existing methods while offering greater flexibility in parallelization.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The dual backward step is a clever and effective solution to a well-known problem, and the theoretical results are sound. The experimental results confirm the practical advantages of the approach. The only notable weakness is the lack of sufficient explanation for why PDMM's parallel updates outperform sequential updates in practice, which is a critical point for readers to understand the method's advantages. With this minor clarification, the paper would be highly suitable for NIPS.
