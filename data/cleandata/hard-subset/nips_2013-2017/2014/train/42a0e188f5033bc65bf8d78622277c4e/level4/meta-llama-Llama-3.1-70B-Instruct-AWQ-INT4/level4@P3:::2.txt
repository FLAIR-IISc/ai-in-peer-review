This paper proposes a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) to minimize block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The work is closely related to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which has been widely used to solve optimization problems with two-block linear constraints. However, extending ADMM to multiple blocks is still an open problem. The authors attempt to address this issue by proposing a parallel randomized block coordinate method, PDMM, which randomly updates some blocks in parallel, behaving like parallel randomized block coordinate descent.

The paper establishes the global convergence and iteration complexity of PDMM with constant step size. The authors also show that PDMM can do randomized block coordinate descent on overlapping blocks, which is an open problem for traditional randomized block coordinate descent (RBCD). Experimental results demonstrate that PDMM performs better than state-of-the-art methods in two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of the dual backward step, which compensates for the limited information propagation in the Jacobian updates by effectively reducing the step size for the dual updates. This is a key innovation that allows PDMM to converge under general conditions, similar to ADMM. The authors also show that two previous methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, providing a better understanding of the relationship between different methods in the landscape of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of PDMM compared to ADMM and other variants. However, it would be beneficial to elaborate on the counterintuitive result that Jacobian-type methods, such as PDMM, can converge faster than Gauss-Seidel-type methods, such as ADMM, in certain scenarios. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the two approaches would be helpful.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the potential of PDMM in solving large-scale optimization problems. The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning.
