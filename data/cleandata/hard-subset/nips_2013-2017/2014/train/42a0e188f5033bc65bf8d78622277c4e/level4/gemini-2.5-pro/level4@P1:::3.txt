This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed to solve multi-block convex optimization problems with linear constraints. At each iteration, the algorithm randomly selects a subset of K blocks, updates their corresponding primal variables in parallel, and then updates the dual variables. A key feature of the method is a "backward step" in the dual update, which makes the update more conservative and is shown to be crucial for ensuring global convergence. The framework is flexible, accommodating inexact primal updates via Bregman divergence terms, which can simplify the resulting subproblems. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing the global convergence and an O(J/(TK)) iteration complexity rate for the algorithm with a constant step size, where J is the total number of blocks.

The primary strength of PDMM lies in its design for parallel computation. By updating a subset of blocks independently, the primal update step is fully parallelizable. Furthermore, the analysis shows that PDMM can accommodate larger dual step sizes compared to some competing methods (e.g., sADMM), which contributes to its fast practical convergence. The paper clearly positions PDMM relative to related work, highlighting its improved parallelizability over sequential methods like GSADMM and its greater flexibility compared to PJADMM. These advantages are convincingly demonstrated through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, where PDMM outperforms several state-of-the-art methods in terms of computation time.

Despite these strengths, there are a couple of points worth noting. A significant weakness is the lack of experimental validation on a true parallel computing architecture. While the algorithm is designed for parallelism and its name implies this, the reported experiments were run sequentially. Demonstrating empirical speedups in a parallel environment would have made the practical benefits of the proposed method much more compelling. Secondly, while the authors provide a principled way to set the step-size parameters (τi, νi), the introduction of these additional parameters compared to the standard ADMM could make the algorithm more difficult to tune in practice for new problems.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and interesting contribution to the literature on ADMM-type algorithms for large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm successfully combines ideas from randomized block-coordinate descent with the ADMM framework to yield a method that is highly parallelizable and exhibits promising empirical performance. It achieves a convergence rate comparable to standard ADMM while offering a clear path to practical speedups on multi-block problems. The paper is well-written, and its theoretical and empirical sections are both solid.
