This paper addresses the problem of minimizing block-separable functions subject to linear constraints. The authors propose a Primal-Dual Fixed Point Method based on the Majorized ADMM (PDMM), a variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers designed for problems with multiple blocks. The proposed method randomly selects a subset of K blocks of the primal variable to update at each iteration, which can be performed in parallel, followed by a backward step to update the dual variable. The authors establish a sublinear convergence rate for the proposed method and show that it generalizes several existing ADMM variants.

The paper is well-written, and the contributions are clearly presented. The generalization of several ADMM variants into a single framework is a valuable contribution. I am supportive of accepting this paper and believe it will be of interest to the optimization community. My detailed comments and suggestions for improvement are provided below.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Algorithmic Clarity:** The description of the dual variable update in equations (5) and (6) could be clarified. It is unclear whether the updates to `y^{t+1}` and `\hat{y}^{t+1}` apply to all coordinates or only to a specific block `i`. My interpretation is that only a single block of the dual variable is updated, but the mechanism for selecting the block index `i` is not specified. A more precise explanation of this step is needed in the revised manuscript to ensure the algorithm is reproducible.

2.  **Discussion and Implications of Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 presents the paper's main theoretical result—the convergence rate of PDMM. While the proof is sound, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its implications.
    *   **Consistency with Prior Work:** A key contribution of PDMM is its generalization of several ADMM variants. A natural and important question is whether the convergence rate in Theorem 2 is consistent with the known rates of these specialized methods when the parameters (e.g., K, J) are set accordingly. Demonstrating that your general result recovers existing ones would provide a strong validation of the theoretical framework.
    *   **Guidance on Block Partitioning:** The method relies on splitting the constraint matrix `A` into multiple blocks. This raises a practical question: does the analysis provide any insight into the optimal way to partition `A`? For instance, does the convergence rate favor a particular block structure (e.g., blocks of equal size, or blocks that minimize inter-block coupling)? A discussion on this would provide valuable guidance for practitioners applying this method.

3.  **Potential Application and Comparison:** I am curious about the performance of PDMM on problems with a natural multi-block structure, such as the tensor completion task described in "Tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data" (Liu et al., 2012). While a new experiment is not required, it would be interesting to see a discussion on this. Given the variety of ADMM variants generalized by your work, does your analysis provide any clues as to which variant might be optimal for such a problem?

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

1.  In Theorem 2, it appears that a summation term `\sum_{i=1}^I` is missing before the curly brace `{`. Please verify this in the proof.
2.  In equation (9), a space is missing after "min".
3.  On Line 217, there is an extraneous space before "Section".

In summary, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance, and I look forward to seeing a revised version that incorporates a more detailed discussion of the main theorem's implications.
