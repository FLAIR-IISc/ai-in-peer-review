## Review of “Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, a common formulation in numerous machine learning applications like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. The authors propose a novel method, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), designed to effectively handle multi-block constraints – a known difficulty for standard ADMM. PDMM introduces a randomized block coordinate update scheme coupled with a carefully designed backward step for the dual variable, enabling parallel computation. The theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and provides an iteration complexity bound. Importantly, the paper positions PDMM as a generalization of existing ADMM variants, offering a unified perspective on these algorithms.

This is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The motivation is strong, the proposed method is interesting, and the theoretical results are valuable. The experimental validation demonstrates promising performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. I recommend acceptance. However, several points could be addressed to further strengthen the contribution.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The core idea of PDMM is to address the limitations of existing multi-block ADMM methods by introducing randomness into the block coordinate update. Unlike Gauss-Seidel approaches which cycle through blocks, or Jacobian methods which update all blocks simultaneously (often leading to instability), PDMM randomly selects a subset of blocks to update in parallel at each iteration. This random selection, combined with a backward step on the dual update, is shown to improve convergence properties. The authors demonstrate connections to existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM, framing them as special cases of PDMM. The theoretical analysis provides convergence guarantees and establishes an iteration complexity, and empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso showcase its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The randomized block coordinate update strategy is a valuable contribution, addressing a known challenge in multi-block ADMM.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including proofs of global convergence and iteration complexity. This is crucial for establishing the reliability of the proposed method.
* **Generality:** Positioning PDMM as a generalization of existing ADMM variants provides a unifying framework and enhances the understanding of these algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM and its competitive performance against existing methods.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the proposed method and its theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Algorithm Description Clarity:** The description of the algorithm in equations (5) and (6) could be more precise. Specifically, it's unclear whether the update in (6) applies to all blocks of the dual variable *y* or only to blocks indexed by *i*. Explicitly stating how *i* is selected (e.g., randomly, cyclically) would improve clarity.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** While Theorem 2 provides a theoretical convergence rate, the discussion surrounding its implications is somewhat limited. A deeper exploration of how the convergence rate depends on parameters like *K* and *J*, and a comparison to the known convergence rates of the ADMM variants that PDMM generalizes, would be highly valuable.
* **Block Splitting Strategy:** The optimal strategy for splitting the matrix *A* into blocks isn’t discussed in relation to the theorem. Providing guidelines or insights on how the block structure impacts performance and convergence would be beneficial.
* **Application Scope:** While the experiments are solid, exploring the performance of PDMM on other applications with multi-block structure, such as tensor completion, would further demonstrate its versatility. Discussing which ADMM variant might be optimal for tensor completion (and *why*) would add valuable insight.

**Specific Comments:**

* **Line 109:** A space is missing after “min”.
* **Line 217:** Remove the space before “Section”.
* **Theorem 2:** The summation symbol "\sum_{i=1}^I" appears to be missing in the initial term within the curly braces.
* **Theorem 2 Discussion:** As mentioned above, a more in-depth discussion of the theoretical implications of Theorem 2, particularly its relationship to convergence rates of generalized ADMM variants and optimal block splitting strategies, is desirable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for solving a challenging optimization problem.
* The proposed method demonstrates competitive performance in experimental evaluations.
* The paper provides a unifying framework for understanding various ADMM variants.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the algorithm description could be improved.
* The discussion surrounding the theoretical results could be more extensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed PDMM algorithm offers a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for multi-block constrained problems. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution. I confidently recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
