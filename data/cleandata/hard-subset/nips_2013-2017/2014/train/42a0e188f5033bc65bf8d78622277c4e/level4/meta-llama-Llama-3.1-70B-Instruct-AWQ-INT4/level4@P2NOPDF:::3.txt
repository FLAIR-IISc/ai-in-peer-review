This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), referred to as PADMM, which can efficiently handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM randomly selects K blocks, updates the block primal vector, and then updates the dual vector via a backward step. The authors emphasize the importance of using a backward step, as it renders the dual update more conservative, thereby enabling global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm allows for the addition of a proximal term to the primal update, making certain optimization problems easier to solve. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, when dealing with J blocks and randomly selecting K blocks at a time, the convergence rate of the algorithm is O(J/(TK)) after T iterations of updates.

The primary advantages of the PADMM algorithm are twofold. Firstly, it enables full parallelization of the primal step, and secondly, it allows for relatively large update step sizes compared to other methods. Notably, PADMM can be faster than the stochastic ADMM (sADMM) due to its ability to accommodate greater step sizes. Additionally, PADMM is more flexible than the parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more parallelizable than the Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors have evaluated the algorithm on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, yielding promising experimental results. The PADMM algorithm achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computation time. However, the authors have not reported the performance of PADMM with parallel implementation. Compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM is less convenient to tune due to the presence of three parameters.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are present. The algorithm exhibits the same convergence rate as ADMM but allows for parallelization and demonstrates promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, with both the theoretical and empirical components being solid.
