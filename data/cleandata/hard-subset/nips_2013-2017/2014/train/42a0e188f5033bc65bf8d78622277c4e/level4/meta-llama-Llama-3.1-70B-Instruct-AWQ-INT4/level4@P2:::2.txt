This paper proposes a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) to minimize block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The method updates primal variables in a Jacobian manner with multiple blocks, in contrast to ADMM, which uses a Gauss-Seidel manner but is limited to two blocks. The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of the dual backward step, which effectively reduces the step size for the dual updates to compensate for the limited information propagation in the Jacobian updates. Similar to ADMM, the authors establish O(1/T) type convergence results for PDMM.

The paper also shows that two previous methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of the proposed method. This provides a better understanding of the relationship between different methods in the landscape of decomposition methods based on the augmented Lagrangian. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the PDMM method compared to ADMM and other variants. However, the results are somewhat counterintuitive, as Jacobian-type methods typically exhibit slower convergence compared to Gauss-Seidel-type methods. Further elaboration on this point would be beneficial.

The dual backward step in PDMM is a key innovation that ensures convergence under general conditions, similar to ADMM. This proposed method provides more alternatives and possibilities for distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM in two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The results show that PDMM performs better than state-of-the-art methods in these applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

The originality of the paper lies in the introduction of the dual backward step and the extension of ADMM to multiple blocks. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions, and they adequately reference related work.

The significance of the paper is that it provides a new method for distributed optimization, which is an important problem in machine learning and other fields. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM in two applications, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their method can be used in other applications.
