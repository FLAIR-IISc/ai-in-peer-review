This paper presents a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), designed to efficiently handle problems decomposable into multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) operates by randomly selecting *K* blocks in each iteration, updating the corresponding primal variables, and then performing a backward step for dual variable updates. A key insight highlighted by the authors is the importance of this backward dual update; it promotes a more conservative update, crucial for ensuring global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm readily incorporates a proximal term into the primal update, simplifying the optimization process for a wide range of problems. 

The authors provide a robust theoretical analysis, establishing both the global convergence of PADMM and its iteration complexity. Specifically, with a problem divided into *J* blocks and PADMM selecting *K* blocks randomly per iteration, the algorithm achieves a convergence rate of O(*J* / (*T* *K*)) after *T* iterations. 

PADMM’s strengths lie in its ability to enable full parallelization of the primal update step and accommodate relatively large step sizes compared to existing methods. The experimental results demonstrate that PADMM can outperform sequential ADMM (sADMM) by leveraging these larger step sizes. It is also presented as more flexible than Parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more parallelizable than Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors validate PADMM’s effectiveness through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, reporting promising results showcasing its ability to achieve desired accuracy with reduced computational cost.

However, a notable limitation is the absence of reported performance results from a parallel implementation of PADMM. While the theoretical benefits of parallelization are clear, empirical confirmation of speedup is missing. Additionally, compared to standard ADMM, PADMM introduces three parameters that require tuning, potentially adding to the complexity of practical application. This increased parameter sensitivity should be further discussed.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of ADMM-based optimization, particularly for problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm maintains a comparable convergence rate to traditional ADMM while offering significant advantages in terms of parallelization and practical performance. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. Both the theoretical analysis and the empirical evaluation are sound and support the authors’ claims. 

**Strengths:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Clear explanation of the backward dual update and its importance.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods on relevant problem instances.
*   Well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of experimental results demonstrating the benefits of parallel implementation.
*   Increased parameter tuning complexity compared to standard ADMM.



**Potential questions for the authors:**

*   Could you provide some guidance on strategies for selecting appropriate values for the three parameters of PADMM?
*   What is the anticipated overhead of the backward dual update step, and how does it affect performance in practice?
*   Do you have plans to release an implementation of PADMM to allow for further evaluation and comparison?
*   How does the performance of PADMM vary with different values of *K* (the number of blocks updated per iteration)?
