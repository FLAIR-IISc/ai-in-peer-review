

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate method for solving convex optimization problems with multi-block linear constraints. The work addresses a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, generalizing it to multiple blocks remains challenging. PDMM updates primal variables in a parallel (Jacobian) fashion rather than the sequential Gauss-Seidel manner, with a key innovation being the dual backward step that compensates for limited information propagation in parallel updates. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, showing that PDMM includes two existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM) as special cases, thereby providing new theoretical insights into their relationship.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the dual backward step is a novel mechanism that enables convergence for Jacobian-style updates, which have generally been problematic for multi-block ADMM variants. This insight is particularly valuable as it explains why Jacobian methods might outperform Gauss-Seidel methods in certain settings. Second, the paper demonstrates that PDMM can handle overlapping blocks, addressing an open problem in randomized block coordinate descent. Third, the theoretical framework unifies various existing approaches under a single algorithmic umbrella, with the step size parameters explicitly accounting for sparsity in the constraint matrix and the number of randomly selected blocks.

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-motivated assumptions and clear proofs for global convergence and iteration complexity. The experimental results on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso demonstrate clear performance advantages over state-of-the-art methods, including ADMM variants and randomized block coordinate descent. Notably, PDMM with K=1 (updating one block randomly) outperforms RBSUMM, and PDMM with K=3 achieves faster convergence than GSADMM despite being a parallel method. The authors effectively address the counterintuitive performance of Jacobian methods by showing that the dual backward step enables faster convergence through conservative dual updates.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection between PDMM and existing methods is particularly well-explained, making the contribution accessible to readers familiar with ADMM variants. The supplementary materials provide additional technical details that would be valuable for implementation.

Regarding originality, the paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work. While some previous papers (e.g., [10, 14, 17]) have explored multi-block ADMM variants, PDMM introduces the dual backward step as a general mechanism that enables convergence under milder conditions. The paper also establishes a more comprehensive theoretical framework that accounts for the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including the recent NIPS papers on multi-block ADMM [14, 17, 26].

The significance of this work is substantial. The proposed method addresses a practical challenge in distributed optimization where parallelism is crucial for large-scale problems. The ability to handle overlapping blocks expands the applicability to important structured sparsity problems. The experimental results demonstrate clear computational advantages, with PDMM achieving faster convergence than existing methods in both runtime and iteration count. The paper's insights about the dual backward step provide a theoretical foundation for future work in distributed optimization.

The paper has minor weaknesses. The experimental section could better explain why PDMM with K=1 outperforms RBSUMM, which also updates a single block. While the paper notes that PDMM's dual backward step enables this improvement, a more detailed comparison of the underlying mechanisms would strengthen the analysis. Additionally, the paper does not fully explore the trade-off between the number of blocks K and communication costs in distributed settings, which would be valuable for practitioners.

In summary, PDMM makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The dual backward step is a novel and important theoretical insight that enables convergence for parallel updates, and the method demonstrates clear practical advantages. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The connection between PDMM and existing methods provides a unifying perspective that advances our understanding of ADMM variants. While the experimental analysis could be slightly more detailed, the paper's theoretical contributions and practical results make it a valuable addition to the literature.

Pro: Novel dual backward step enables convergence for Jacobian multi-block updates; rigorous theoretical analysis with O(1/T) complexity; clear experimental validation showing performance advantages; unifies existing methods under a single framework; handles overlapping blocks, addressing an open problem.

Con: Experimental comparison with RBSUMM could be more detailed; limited discussion of communication costs in distributed settings.
