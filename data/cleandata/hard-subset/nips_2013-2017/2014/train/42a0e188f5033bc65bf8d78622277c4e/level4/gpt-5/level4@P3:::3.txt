Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles convex optimization with block-separable objectives and linear constraints, a class encompassing problems such as robust PCA and overlapping group lasso. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for multi-block constraints. At each iteration, PDMM selects K blocks uniformly at random and updates their primal variables in parallel using the augmented Lagrangian with an optional Bregman proximal term; it then performs a dual update augmented with a “backward step” to temper implicit dual ascent. The step sizes for dual ascent and backward step (τi, νi) are chosen as functions of the number of updated blocks K and the block sparsity of A (via di, the number of nonzeros in the i-th row block), enabling larger and more stable steps than naïve Jacobi ADMM.

The paper provides global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity under standard convexity assumptions. It further shows that two established multi-block ADMM variants are special cases of PDMM: (i) two-block ADMM with splitting variables (sADMM) corresponds to small dual steps when primal updates are exact; (ii) proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) corresponds to large dual steps when sufficiently strong proximal terms are added. PDMM also supports randomized updates on overlapping blocks and applies effectively to composite regularization.

Empirically, on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM achieves the target accuracy in fewer wall-clock seconds than sADMM and RBSUMM, and is competitive with or faster than GSADMM, while offering straightforward parallelism (experiments are run sequentially in MATLAB).

Quality:
- Theory: The analysis is careful and builds on standard assumptions for ADMM-type methods. The O(1/T) rate matches the best-known rates for related methods, and the constants highlight how K and the sparsity pattern di affect progress. The backward step is well-motivated by the implicit dual ascent effect of the quadratic penalty.
- Algorithms: The inexact variant, via Bregman proximal terms, is practical and broadens applicability (e.g., to logistic losses). The unifying perspective that recovers sADMM and PJADMM is insightful.
- Experiments: The benchmarks (RPCA and overlapping group lasso) are relevant and clearly set up. Comparisons include strong baselines (sADMM, RBSUMM, GSADMM, ADMM, and accelerated proximal methods). Results generally support the claims.

Clarity:
The paper is readable and well-structured, with useful discussions of τi and νi, the role of sparsity, and the effect of K. There are minor typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., occasional “PADMM”, “palatalization”), but they do not impede comprehension. More guidance on parameter selection in practice would further improve clarity.

Originality:
The combination of randomized multi-block parallel updates, a principled dual backward step with sparsity-aware step sizes, and the unifying view that interpolates between sADMM and PJADMM appears novel. The treatment of overlapping blocks within this framework is also valuable.

Significance:
PDMM addresses scalability and practical deployment of multi-block ADMM by enabling safe, effective parallel updates with constant step sizes, and by offering inexact updates for difficult subproblems. The contributions are likely to be useful to practitioners working on large-scale structured convex problems and to researchers studying ADMM variants.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled algorithmic design (randomized K-block updates; dual backward step).
- Solid theory: global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with explicit dependence on K and di.
- Unification of sADMM and PJADMM provides conceptual clarity and practical guidance.
- Applicability to overlapping groups and support for inexact updates via Bregman divergence.
- Empirical results show favorable runtime and iteration behavior on two representative applications.

Weaknesses:
- No true parallel/distributed experiments; all results are sequential MATLAB runs. Demonstrating speedups on multicore or distributed systems would significantly strengthen the paper.
- Parameter sensitivity and tuning guidelines for τi, νi, ρ, and proximal weights ηjt are not fully explored; automated selection or heuristics would aid adoption.
- Comparisons to GSADMM note faster iteration convergence but do not quantify total computation per iteration across methods in a controlled setting; fairness could be discussed in more depth.
- Some implementation details are specific (e.g., sADMM code without dual update), potentially biasing comparisons.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide practical guidelines for selecting τi, νi, ρ, and ηjt, possibly with adaptive schemes or defaults based on di and K.
- Include parallel or distributed experiments to validate wall-clock gains under realistic execution models and communication costs.
- Report sensitivity analyses for K and for the choice of proximal functions to illustrate robustness.
- Discuss applicability to asynchronous updates and whether the analysis can be extended to that setting.
- Clarify computational overhead for computing/maintaining di and the impact of sparsity patterns on communication and synchronization.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated algorithmic ideas with rigorous analysis.
- Unifying perspective on existing ADMM variants, yielding practical insight.
- Demonstrated performance benefits on relevant applications.
- Clear path to impactful use in large-scale convex optimization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of parallel/distributed implementation results weakens the practical impact claim.
- Limited guidance on parameter tuning may hinder reproducibility and adoption.
- Some experimental comparisons could be made more balanced and comprehensive.

Overall evaluation:
This is a strong and timely contribution to multi-block ADMM methods. The paper is theoretically sound, clearly written, and empirically promising. While parallel experiments and more tuning guidance would strengthen the work, the core ideas and analysis are solid and of interest to the NIPS community.

Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions focused on clarity and practical guidance).
