

This paper introduces PADMM, a randomized parallel ADMM variant designed for problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM randomly selects K blocks, updates their primal variables, and employs a backward step for the dual update. The authors demonstrate that the backward step ensures conservative dual updates, enabling global convergence guarantees. Additionally, PADMM incorporates a proximal term in the primal update, facilitating efficient solution for certain subproblems. Theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and an iteration complexity of O(J/(K T)) after T iterations, where J is the total number of blocks and K is the number of blocks updated per iteration.  

The primary advantages of PADMM are (1) full parallelization of the primal step and (2) the ability to use larger step sizes compared to related methods. Specifically, PADMM outperforms sADMM by allowing greater step sizes and offers greater flexibility than PJADMM while achieving higher parallelism than GSADMM. Empirical validation on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso demonstrates significant computational efficiency, achieving target accuracy with reduced runtime.  

However, the paper omits critical evaluation of PADMM’s performance under parallel implementation, which is central to its design. Without such results, the claimed parallel speedup remains unverified. Furthermore, PADMM requires tuning three parameters (e.g., ρ, η, γ), making it less user-friendly than standard ADMM, which typically involves fewer hyperparameters.  

Overall, PADMM presents a valuable contribution to distributed optimization of ADMM-type problems with multiple constraints. It achieves the same convergence rate as standard ADMM while enabling parallelism and delivering strong empirical performance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are well-supported. The paper is clearly written, with a balanced presentation of both theoretical and empirical aspects. While the missing parallel implementation results and parameter tuning complexity are notable limitations, they do not undermine the core novelty and technical merit of the work. This paper would be a strong fit for NIPS, offering both theoretical depth and practical relevance to the optimization community.
