This paper addresses the problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, a common formulation in machine learning. The authors propose a novel parallel randomized block coordinate method, termed the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), to tackle the multi-block case, a challenging area for ADMM. PDMM randomly selects blocks of the primal variable for updating and incorporates a backward step for dual variable refinement, enabling parallel implementation. The authors establish a sublinear convergence rate and demonstrate that PDMM generalizes several existing ADMM variants.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology and theoretical contributions. The motivation for developing PDMM is well-articulated, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches for multi-block problems. The connection to related work, particularly different ADMM variants, is appropriately discussed. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the efficacy of PDMM. Overall, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, and I recommend acceptance.

However, several points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper:

1) **Clarification of Dual Variable Update:** The algorithm description in equations (5) and (6) lacks clarity regarding the update of the dual variables. Specifically, it’s unclear whether all coordinates of *y<sup>t+1</sup>* and *ŷ<sup>t+1</sup>* are updated, or only a selected block indexed by *i*. The paper should explicitly state how the index *i* is chosen for updating the dual variables, as this is crucial for understanding the algorithm's implementation.

2) **Typographical Error:** In Theorem 2, a summation symbol appears to be missing. The expression should likely be “∑<sub>i=1</sub><sup>I</sup>” before the opening brace.

3) **Discussion of Theorem 2:** While Theorem 2 establishes the convergence rate, a more in-depth discussion of its implications would be beneficial. Given that PDMM generalizes several ADMM variants, it’s natural to ask whether the convergence rate achieved by PDMM (with appropriately chosen parameters *K* and *J*) is consistent with the known convergence rates of those specialized methods. Furthermore, exploring the optimal strategy for splitting the matrix *A* into blocks, based on the theoretical results, would add significant value.

4) **Broader Applicability:** The authors might consider discussing the potential of PDMM for other applications with a multi-block structure, such as tensor completion. Exploring which ADMM variant might be optimal for tensor completion and providing insights based on PDMM's framework would demonstrate the broader impact of this work.

**Minor comments:**

1) A space is missing after "min" in equation (9).
2) Line 217: Remove the space before "Section."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** PDMM introduces a novel approach to handling multi-block ADMM, addressing a known challenge in the field.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence guarantees.
* **Generalization:** PDMM generalizes existing ADMM variants, providing a unified framework.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM on relevant applications.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** (None significant enough to warrant rejection, but addressing the points above would strengthen the paper.)

* The algorithm description could be more precise.
* A more detailed discussion of the theoretical results is needed.
