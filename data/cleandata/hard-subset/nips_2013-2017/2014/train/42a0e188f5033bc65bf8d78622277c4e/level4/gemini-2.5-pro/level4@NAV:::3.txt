This paper introduces a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM, named PADMM, designed to handle optimization problems with multiple blocks. The algorithm's core procedure involves randomly selecting K blocks per iteration, performing a primal vector update for these blocks, and subsequently updating the dual vector through a backward step. The authors effectively argue that this backward step is crucial, as it leads to a more conservative dual update, which in turn secures global convergence. Furthermore, the framework accommodates an optional proximal term in the primal update, which can simplify the solution process for certain subproblems.

The theoretical contributions are substantial, with the authors providing a rigorous analysis that establishes the algorithm's global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, for a problem with J total blocks where K blocks are chosen at each step, the convergence rate is shown to be O(J/(TK)) after T iterations.

The primary strengths of the PADMM algorithm are twofold: (1) it enables full parallelization of the primal update step, and (2) it permits a larger stepsize compared to competing methods. The authors position PADMM favorably against related algorithms, noting it can outperform sADMM due to its larger allowable stepsize, offers greater flexibility than PJADMM, and is more amenable to parallelization than GSADMM.

To validate the method, the algorithm was evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso tasks. The empirical results are compelling, indicating that PADMM achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computational time. However, a limitation is the absence of performance results from an actual parallel implementation. Another minor drawback is that tuning the three hyperparameters of PADMM is less straightforward than tuning the single parameter of conventional ADMM.

In conclusion, this work is a valuable contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, especially those characterized by multiple constraints. The proposed algorithm achieves a convergence rate comparable to standard ADMM while offering the significant advantages of parallelization and promising practical performance. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with both the theoretical analysis and the empirical validation being solid.
