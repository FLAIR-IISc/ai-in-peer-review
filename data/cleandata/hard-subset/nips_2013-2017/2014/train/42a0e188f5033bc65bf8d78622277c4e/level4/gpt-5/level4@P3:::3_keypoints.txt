- Paper proposes PDMM: a randomized parallel multi-block ADMM that updates K blocks uniformly at random with optional Bregman proximal terms and a dual "backward step."  
- Step sizes τi and νi are chosen based on K and block sparsity di, allowing larger, more stable steps than naïve Jacobi ADMM.  
- Provides global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity under standard convexity assumptions.  
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, unifying prior multi-block ADMM variants.  
- Supports overlapping-block updates and inexact (Bregman) primal updates, widening applicability (e.g., logistic loss).  
- Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show faster wall-clock convergence than sADMM and RBSUMM and competitive performance vs. GSADMM.  
- Theory is careful, matches best-known rates, and quantifies how K and di affect progress; backward step is well-motivated.  
- Inexact/Bregman variant is practical and broadens problem classes PDMM can handle.  
- Experiments use relevant benchmarks and strong baselines, generally supporting claims.  
- Paper is readable and well-structured, with clear discussion of parameters, sparsity, and K’s effect; minor typos/notational inconsistencies noted.  
- The combination of randomized parallel updates, sparsity-aware backward step, and unifying perspective appears novel and valuable.  
- PDMM improves scalability and practical deployment of multi-block ADMM by enabling safe parallel updates and inexact solves.  
- Strengths: principled algorithm, solid theory, unification of prior methods, support for overlapping groups and inexact updates, and promising empirical results.  
- Weaknesses: no true parallel/distributed experiments, limited parameter-tuning guidance, and some potentially biased implementation details in comparisons.  
- Suggested additions: practical/adaptive rules for τi, νi, ρ, ηjt; parallel/distributed experiments; sensitivity analyses for K and proximal choices; discussion of asynchronous extensions.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, well-motivated algorithm with rigorous analysis, unifying view of ADMM variants, and demonstrated benefits on applications.  
- Rejection arguments: lack of parallel implementation results, insufficient parameter-tuning guidance, and some imbalanced experimental comparisons.  
- Overall: strong, timely contribution with solid theory and empirical promise; recommend accept with minor revisions for clarity and practical guidance.
