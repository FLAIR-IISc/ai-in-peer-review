

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel approach for solving convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives and linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM for two-block problems has been extensively studied, generalizations to multiple blocks remain poorly understood. PDMM introduces a parallel randomized block coordinate method that updates multiple blocks simultaneously, using a backward step in the dual update to ensure convergence. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, and demonstrates that several existing ADMM variants (including sADMM and PJADMM) are special cases of PDMM.

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by addressing the multi-block ADMM problem, which has been largely unexplored. The theoretical results are comprehensive, providing global convergence and iteration complexity analysis that builds upon previous work while offering new insights. The connection between PDMM and existing ADMM variants provides valuable theoretical understanding of the dual backward step's role. The experimental evaluation on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrates clear performance advantages over state-of-the-art methods, with PDMM consistently outperforming alternatives like sADMM, RBSUMM, and GSADMM in both convergence speed and runtime.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Strong theoretical foundation with well-supported convergence analysis
2. Practical algorithm that effectively handles large-scale problems
3. Clear connections to existing work, with PDMM unifying several ADMM variants
4. Comprehensive experimental validation on two important applications
5. Well-written exposition that makes complex concepts accessible

Minor weaknesses requiring clarification:
1. The algorithm description in Equations (5)-(7) needs refinement. The paper should specify whether dual updates apply to all blocks or a single block, and how the block selection is determined. The current formulation suggests all dual blocks are updated simultaneously, but it's unclear how the specific block "i" is selected.
2. The paper could provide more guidance on optimal block partitioning strategies. While PDMM handles overlapping blocks, the manuscript doesn't discuss how to optimally partition the problem into blocks for different structures.
3. The comparison to tensor completion problems (which have multi-block structure) could be expanded to better contextualize PDMM's applicability.
4. In Theorem 2, the summation notation appears incomplete, and the paper should verify all mathematical expressions for consistency.

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
Pros:
- Addresses an important gap in multi-block optimization literature
- Theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported
- Practical method with clear experimental advantages
- Well-written with strong connections to prior work
- Demonstrates broader applicability to overlapping block structures

Cons:
- Some algorithmic details require clarification
- Lack of guidance on optimal block partitioning
- Comparison to tensor completion could be more comprehensive
- Theorem 2 notation needs verification

The paper represents a high-quality contribution to the optimization literature with significant theoretical and practical value. The technical content is sound, the writing is clear, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The minor weaknesses noted can be addressed in a revision, making this an excellent candidate for NIPS. The authors have successfully bridged a gap in the literature while providing both theoretical insights and practical benefits.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
1. Clarify the dual update process in Equations (5)-(7), explicitly stating whether all dual blocks are updated or only a single block, and how the block selection is determined.
2. Add a discussion of optimal block partitioning strategies based on the theoretical results, particularly for problems with different sparsity structures.
3. Expand the comparison to include tensor completion as a potential application, discussing how PDMM might compare to other methods on this problem.
4. Verify the summation notation in Theorem 2 and ensure all mathematical expressions are complete.
5. Discuss how PDMM's convergence rate compares to existing methods when specialized to known ADMM variants.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to optimization theory and practice, with clear relevance to machine learning applications. The theoretical results are strong, the writing is clear, and the experimental validation is thorough. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall quality. I strongly recommend acceptance.
