This paper presents a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. Existing methods like ADMM update primal variables in a Gauss-Seidel fashion, typically limited to two blocks. PDMM extends this by updating primal blocks using a Jacobian-based approach, allowing for parallel updates with multiple blocks. The core contribution of this work is the introduction of a “dual backward step” designed to compensate for the potentially limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian updates, effectively regulating the dual update step size. The authors establish convergence results of the order O(1/T), mirroring those achieved with ADMM.

The supplementary material demonstrates that existing methods, specifically sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of the proposed PDMM framework, offering valuable insight into the relationships between different augmented Lagrangian decomposition methods. Empirical results showcase the effectiveness of PDMM when compared to ADMM and other variants. These results are somewhat surprising, as Jacobian-based methods are often associated with slower convergence compared to Gauss-Seidel approaches. A more detailed discussion of this discrepancy would strengthen the paper. Specifically, it would be beneficial to understand *why* PDMM overcomes the typical convergence challenges of Jacobian methods in this context. 

The dual backward step appears to be a novel and crucial component of PDMM, enabling convergence under fairly general conditions comparable to those of ADMM. This is a significant achievement. The method's flexibility offers greater options and potential for distributed optimization strategies. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PDMM algorithm, and particularly the dual backward step, represent a novel contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
*   **Generality:** The method generalizes existing algorithms like sADMM and PJADMM, providing a unifying framework.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The O(1/T) convergence guarantee provides a solid theoretical basis for the method.
*   **Potential Impact:** The algorithm offers more choices for distributed optimization, potentially impacting large-scale machine learning and related fields.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Explanation of Convergence Behavior:** The counterintuitive convergence performance (faster than expected for a Jacobian method) is not fully explained. Elaboration on this point is crucial. The authors should discuss the role of the dual backward step in addressing potential convergence issues.
*   **Lack of Detailed Comparison:** While results are presented against ADMM and variants, a deeper analysis of the conditions under which PDMM *specifically* outperforms these methods would be valuable. Are there problem structures or parameters where PDMM consistently shines?



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more in-depth explanation of why PDMM achieves good convergence despite employing a Jacobian-based update scheme? What is the precise mechanism by which the dual backward step overcomes the typical limitations of Jacobian methods?
*   Are there specific problem characteristics (e.g., strong convexity, smoothness, block structure) where PDMM is expected to perform significantly better than ADMM?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the dual backward step? Does it introduce a significant overhead compared to standard ADMM updates?
*   How does the performance of PDMM scale with the number of blocks? Are there limitations to its scalability?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. Addressing the concerns regarding the convergence behavior and providing a more detailed performance analysis would further enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewer’s comments and questions.
