This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. Unlike classical two-block ADMM, which uses Gauss–Seidel updates and does not directly extend to many blocks, PDMM performs randomized, parallel (Jacobi-style) updates of an arbitrary number of primal blocks per iteration. A key innovation is a “dual backward step” that tempers the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian, thereby stabilizing Jacobi updates. The authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes, and show how the dual step sizes depend on both the sparsity pattern of A (via row-block degrees) and the number of updated blocks. They also demonstrate that sADMM (two-block ADMM with variable splitting) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM) arise as limiting cases of PDMM. Experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show favorable performance compared with ADMM variants and first-order baselines.

Relation to prior work: Two-block ADMM is well understood, but its naive multi-block extensions may diverge. Prior multi-block approaches include Gauss–Seidel schemes (with additional safeguards), Jacobian variants with proximal terms (e.g., PJADMM), and randomized single-block methods (e.g., RBSUMM). PDMM contributes a principled Jacobi-style, multi-block, randomized framework with constant step sizes and explicit rates, and unifies sADMM and PJADMM through the backward dual step.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: the backward dual step addresses the overly aggressive implicit dual ascent in Jacobi updates, providing a simple, intuitive control mechanism.
- Solid theory: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity are established under standard convex assumptions without decreasing step sizes. The dependence of step sizes on sparsity (row degrees) and the number of updated blocks is novel and practically relevant.
- Unifying perspective: identifying sADMM and PJADMM as special cases helps clarify when small-vs-large dual steps are appropriate (exact vs proximal primal updates).
- Practical reach: PDMM naturally handles overlapping groups by introducing equality constraints, enabling randomized block updates even in overlapping settings where standard coordinate descent is difficult.
- Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are competitive, often outperforming baselines in both iterations and runtime (even in a sequential Matlab setting).

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical setup: all methods are run sequentially; yet PDMM’s main advantage is parallelism. Including results on multi-core or distributed hardware would better demonstrate its practical benefits and communication costs.
- Fairness and tuning: PDMM’s performance depends on τi and νi. While the paper provides rules tied to sparsity and K, more discussion of sensitivity, robustness, and tuning effort versus baselines (which also require parameters) would help.
- Jacobi vs Gauss–Seidel: the reported gains over GSADMM are somewhat counterintuitive given typical advantages of Gauss–Seidel in convergence speed. Additional explanation (e.g., how the backward dual step offsets Jacobi’s weakness, or when sparsity-driven larger τi yields faster progress) and ablations isolating this effect would strengthen the narrative.
- Choice of K and grouping: clearer practical guidance on selecting K, grouping strategies, and the trade-offs between per-iteration cost and iteration count would aid practitioners.
- Overhead and assumptions: computing degree-based step sizes presumes knowledge of nonzero block structure; discussing overhead and extensions to dynamic or approximate sparsity information would be valuable. Also, a brief comment on constants in the complexity bounds and their dependence on problem parameters would contextualize the rates.

Quality: The paper is technically sound; claims are supported by proofs and experiments. The convergence analysis appears careful and comparable to state-of-the-art for ADMM-type methods. The experimental evidence is positive, though broader benchmarks and true parallel evaluations would make the case more compelling.

Clarity: Generally well written, but some sections are dense. Additional intuition for the backward dual step, a concise summary table mapping PDMM parameters to sADMM/PJADMM, and a short “how to set τi, νi, K in practice” guide would improve readability.

Originality: The dual backward step within a randomized Jacobi multi-block ADMM framework, with sparsity- and K-aware step sizes, is a meaningful and original contribution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is insightful.

Significance: PDMM broadens the toolkit for large-scale constrained convex optimization, especially for problems with block structure and overlaps. The ability to update multiple blocks in parallel with provable guarantees and practical step-size rules is likely to be useful.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant mechanism (backward dual step) enabling stable Jacobi multi-block updates.
- Rigorous theory with constant step sizes and O(1/T) rates.
- Unifying perspective linking existing ADMM variants.
- Positive empirical results on representative applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of experiments on actual parallel/distributed platforms leaves open the real-world advantage of PDMM’s parallelism.
- Limited sensitivity analysis and guidance on parameter selection and K.
- Somewhat narrow empirical scope; more diverse datasets and baselines would strengthen conclusions.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances multi-block ADMM methods with a simple, principled idea and supportive theory. With additional empirical depth and practical guidance, it would be even stronger.
