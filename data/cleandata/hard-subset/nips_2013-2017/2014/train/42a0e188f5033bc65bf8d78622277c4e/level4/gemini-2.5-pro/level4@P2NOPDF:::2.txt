This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), an algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. In contrast to the standard Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which updates primal variables sequentially in a Gauss-Seidel fashion and is typically limited to two blocks, PDMM updates all primal variable blocks in parallel, analogous to a Jacobian-style update.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a "dual backward step." This step is designed to compensate for the potentially slower information propagation inherent in parallel Jacobian updates. It achieves this by effectively reducing the step size for the dual variable update, a mechanism that proves crucial for ensuring convergence. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate for PDMM, matching the standard rate for ADMM.

A valuable contribution, detailed in the supplementary materials, is showing that two prior methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of the proposed framework. This unifies these algorithms and provides a clearer understanding of their relationships within the broader landscape of augmented Lagrangian-based decomposition methods.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM, showing its competitive performance against ADMM and other variants. This empirical success is somewhat counter-intuitive, as Jacobian-type methods are generally expected to converge more slowly than their Gauss-Seidel counterparts. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could provide more intuition or analysis on why PDMM overcomes this potential limitation so effectively. Is this strong performance primarily attributable to the dual backward step, or are there specific problem structures where parallel updates are particularly beneficial?

The dual backward step appears to be a novel and key technical innovation that enables convergence under conditions as general as those for ADMM. By facilitating a fully parallel update scheme, the proposed method offers a valuable and practical new alternative for large-scale distributed optimization.
