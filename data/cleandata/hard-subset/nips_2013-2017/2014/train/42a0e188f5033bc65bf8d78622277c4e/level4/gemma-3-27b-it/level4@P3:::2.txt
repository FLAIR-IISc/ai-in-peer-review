This paper proposes a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is well-established, extending it effectively to more than two blocks remains challenging. This work addresses this by proposing a Jacobian-style update of primal blocks – updating multiple blocks in parallel – coupled with a novel “dual backward step.” This step appears crucial for compensating for the limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian updates and ensuring convergence. The authors establish global convergence with an O(1/T) iteration complexity, mirroring results for ADMM.

The paper effectively positions PDMM within the existing landscape of multi-block ADMM variants. The demonstration (in the supplementary material) that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is a valuable contribution, providing a unifying framework and deeper understanding of the interplay between these methods. The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to ADMM and related techniques. The observation that PDMM outperforms methods traditionally expected to converge more slowly (Gauss-Seidel) is noteworthy and deserving of further discussion.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The dual backward step is a key innovation, potentially enabling convergence in scenarios where standard Jacobian-based methods might struggle.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence analysis, establishing an O(1/T) rate, provides strong theoretical support for the method.
*   **Unifying Framework:**  The demonstration that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM offers valuable insight and streamlines understanding.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on two relevant applications support the effectiveness of PDMM.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and the motivation for PDMM is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Counterintuitive Results:** The experimental finding that a Jacobian-style method outperforms Gauss-Seidel-based methods warrants more in-depth explanation. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of *why* this occurs in the specific problem settings tested. Is it due to the specific sparsity patterns in the problem, the choice of parameters, or a fundamental property of PDMM?
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper does not provide extensive details on parameter tuning. While the authors mention the use of (8) for τi and νi, discussing the sensitivity of performance to these parameters and providing guidance for practical implementation would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to existing methods, a broader evaluation against more recent or highly optimized solvers could further strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Notations:** The notation is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more gradual introduction and explanation. For example, defining variables like 'di' earlier in the text would improve readability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   PDMM offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to multi-block ADMM, addressing a known challenge in the field.
*   The unifying framework provided by relating PDMM to sADMM and PJADMM is a significant contribution.
*   The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on relevant applications.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The counterintuitive experimental results require further investigation and a more detailed explanation.
*   The lack of extensive parameter sensitivity analysis limits the practical guidance provided.
*   Minor improvements in clarity and notation could enhance the overall accessibility of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm, with its novel dual backward step, offers a promising approach to solving multi-block constrained optimization problems. While the counterintuitive experimental results and limited parameter tuning analysis are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper, particularly its theoretical foundations and unifying framework, outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should expand on the explanation of the experimental results and provide more guidance on parameter selection.
