- Problem and contribution: Studies convex block-separable optimization with linear constraints and proposes PDMM, a randomized parallel multi-block ADMM with a dual "backward step."

- Algorithmic detail: PDMM selects K primal blocks per iteration to update in parallel and uses sparsity-aware stepsizes tied to A and K.

- Theoretical results: Proves global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate under constant stepsizes, and handles overlapping blocks.

- Connections to prior work: Shows PDMM recovers sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases and relates to other multi-block ADMM remedies.

- Experiments: Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show favorable performance.

- Strength: Clear motivation and simple algorithmic idea (randomized parallel updates + dual backward step).

- Strength: Theoretically grounded with explicit stepsize rules depending on K and block sparsity di.

- Strength: Unifies sADMM and PJADMM, clarifying appropriate dual-step choices.

- Strength: Practical relevance via overlapping-group handling and strong empirical validation.

- Weakness 1 (clarity): Need clearer description of the dual update—whether y and ŷ are updated for all i or only selected blocks, and how dual indices i are chosen each iteration.

- Weakness 2 (typo): Theorem 2 likely missing a summation ∑_{i=1}^I in the first bound after the opening brace.

- Weakness 3 (discussion after Theorem 2): Request to explicitly state that Theorem 2 recovers O(1/T) rates for sADMM/PJADMM (and relate constants), and to relate K=1 case to RBCD-style rates.

- Weakness 3 (guidance): Ask for practical heuristics on block partitioning—how to choose/group blocks to optimize di, K̃i, τi, and the dual backward step.

- Weakness 4 (application): Suggest discussing tensor completion as an additional application and advising which ADMM variant and K/dual-step settings suit mode-wise sparsity.

- Minor comments: Fix spacing in Eq. (9) and remove extra space before "Section" on line 217.

- Overall evaluation: Paper is clear, technically solid, unifying, practically useful, and recommended for acceptance pending clarifications on dual updates and expanded discussion after Theorem 2.
