- Proposal: PDMM — a randomized, parallel ADMM variant that updates K randomly chosen blocks in parallel using augmented Lagrangian + optional proximal/Bregman term, followed by a backward dual step.  
- Step-size theory: derives τi, νi that depend on K and per-row sparsity di; proves global convergence and ergodic O(1/T) rate with constant steps.  
- Complexity scaling: ergodic optimality gap scales as O(J/(K T)) when J blocks total and K updated per iteration.  
- Special cases: PDMM recovers sADMM (exact updates) and PJADMM (heavily proximal updates) as extremes.  
- Overlapping blocks: supports randomized updates over overlapping blocks, which is nontrivial for standard RBCD.  
- Relation to prior work: fills gaps in randomized multi-block ADMM literature by providing sparsity-aware dual steps and a backward step absent in prior randomized methods.  
- Technical quality: analysis is careful and technically sound under standard convex/KKT assumptions; backward dual step appears essential for convergence with multiple parallel updates.  
- Trade-off clarity: constants explicitly reflect K and di, clarifying the parallelism–stepsize trade-off.  
- Inexact variant: Bregman-divergence-based inexact PDMM is well motivated and unifies exact and proximal updates.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written with helpful intuition for the backward step, but needs more practical guidance for choosing K and tuning τi, νi, η.  
- Originality: novel combination of randomized parallel updates, sparsity-aware dual step sizes, and backward dual step; overlapping-block support is notable.  
- Significance: practically useful for multi-block constrained problems; theoretical insights likely to influence future algorithm design.  
- Empirical results: PDMM outperforms sADMM and RBSUMM and is competitive or faster than GSADMM on RPCA and overlapping group lasso in sequential Matlab runs.  
- Empirical shortcomings: all experiments are sequential (no parallel implementation), weakening the central parallel-efficiency claim.  
- Baseline concerns: some baselines (e.g., sADMM) may need proper dual updates and better tuning for fair comparison; broader datasets would strengthen claims.  
- Pros summary: provable randomized parallel updates, sparsity- and K-dependent step rules, unifying framework including overlapping groups, and strong empirical trends.  
- Cons summary: no demonstrated parallel speedups, more complex practical tuning with limited guidance, and some baseline/configuration weaknesses.  
- Suggested improvements: include parallel implementations and wall-clock speedup comparisons, provide tuning/sensitivity studies and ablations (especially on backward step), clarify grouping strategies and asynchronous/stale-update behavior, and ensure fair baseline tuning and additional datasets.  
- Recommendation: solid and interesting contribution with strong theory–algorithm–practice links; main weakness is lack of parallel experiments — reviewer leans toward acceptance.
