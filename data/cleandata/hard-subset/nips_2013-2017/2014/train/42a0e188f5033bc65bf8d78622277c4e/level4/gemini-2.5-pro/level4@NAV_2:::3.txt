This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions under linear equality constraints. The authors propose a novel algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which extends the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting. The core idea of PDMM is to perform parallel randomized block coordinate updates on the primal variables. At each iteration, a subset of `K` variable blocks is randomly selected and updated in parallel. A key contribution is the introduction of a "backward step" in the dual update, which tempers the dual variable's progress to guarantee convergence, a known issue for direct Jacobi-style parallel ADMM. The method is further distinguished by its step-size rule, which adaptively incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. The authors provide a unifying framework that covers both exact and inexact primal updates via Bregman divergence. This work connects to and generalizes existing Jacobian ADMM methods, showing that sADMM and PJADMM can be viewed as special cases of PDMM. The paper establishes the global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate for PDMM with a constant step size, and demonstrates its novel application to overlapping group regularization problems.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and flexible framework for parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems. The introduction of the dual backward step combined with a sparsity-aware, randomization-aware step size rule is a novel contribution that addresses the known convergence challenges of parallel ADMM. The theoretical unification of sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable insight into the behavior of these methods. Furthermore, applying the method to solve overlapping group problems in a randomized block coordinate fashion addresses a problem that the authors note is open for standard randomized block coordinate descent (RBCD).
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is solid, providing rigorous proofs for global convergence and iteration complexity under standard assumptions. The convergence rate of O(1/T) is on par with standard ADMM, which is a strong result for a parallel and randomized method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained, and the roles of matrix sparsity and block randomization in determining the step size are well-articulated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso are promising. The sequential implementation of PDMM is shown to be competitive with, and often faster than, several state-of-the-art methods, including GSADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** A significant weakness is that while the proposed method is designed for and named after its parallel nature, the experiments are conducted sequentially. The claims of parallelizability would be substantially more compelling if supported by empirical results demonstrating speedups on a parallel computing architecture. Without this, the practical benefits of the parallel updates over an efficient sequential method like GSADMM (which performed well in the RPCA experiment) remain speculative.
*   **Parameter Tuning Discussion:** The paper provides a principled formula for setting the step sizes `τi` and `νi`. However, like all ADMM-type methods, the performance is sensitive to the penalty parameter `ρ`. The experiments simply set `ρ=1` without justification. A more thorough discussion of parameter selection or a sensitivity analysis regarding `ρ` would enhance the practical value of the paper for potential users.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature for large-scale machine learning. The proposed PDMM algorithm is an elegant and theoretically sound extension of ADMM that enables parallel, randomized updates for multi-block problems. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the sequential experiments demonstrate the method's potential. Despite the lack of true parallel experiments, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical results make this a solid and interesting paper.
