

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block-coordinate variant of ADMM designed for solving multi-block convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The authors address a key gap in existing ADMM extensions: while two-block ADMM is well-established, multi-block generalizations often lack convergence guarantees or suffer from slow convergence. PDMM updates K randomly selected blocks in parallel at each iteration, using a dual backward step to ensure conservative dual progress and adaptive step sizes that leverage matrix sparsity and block selection. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, revealing deeper insights into their parameter choices. Theoretical contributions include global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity under constant step sizes, with experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso showing significant speedups over state-of-the-art methods.

The paper’s strengths are substantial. Theoretical analysis is rigorous, establishing convergence under minimal assumptions (Assumption 1) and providing clear connections to existing methods (Section 2.2). The dual backward step mechanism—motivated by the observation that implicit dual ascent in standard Jacobi updates can be overly aggressive—provides a principled solution to a known convergence hurdle. PDMM’s flexibility is notable: it accommodates exact or inexact primal updates via Bregman divergence terms (Section 2.1), handles overlapping blocks (Section 2.3), and achieves larger step sizes than sADMM (e.g., τ_i = 0.5 vs. 1/J when K=J for overlapping group lasso). The empirical results are compelling: PDMM consistently outperforms sADMM, RBSUMM, and GSADMM in both convergence speed and runtime across two challenging applications (Figures 1–2), with PDMM1 (updating one block) achieving the fastest runtime in RPCA despite its simplicity. The theoretical rate O(1/T) matches ADMM variants, but PDMM’s parallelism enables practical gains.

A few weaknesses warrant mention. The authors report sequential results (as the code is implemented in Matlab), but the parallel nature of PDMM suggests runtime comparisons with parallel implementations would strengthen the practical impact. Parameter tuning (τ_i, ν_i, K) requires careful selection (Table 1), though the authors provide clear guidelines via Equation (8). While the paper cites relevant work (e.g., BSUMM [14], PJADMM [10]), it could more explicitly contrast PDMM’s constant-step-size convergence (Theorem 2) with RBSUMM’s reliance on decreasing steps and local error bounds. Additionally, the claim that PDMM "allows greater step sizes" should clarify that this is relative to sADMM’s fixed τ_i=1/J, as PDMM’s step sizes adapt to sparsity (e.g., τ_i=K/(K̃_i(2J−K))).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., Section 2.1’s inexact updates). It directly addresses NIPS’s criteria: 
- **Quality**: Rigorous theory validated by experiments; clear discussion of limitations (e.g., trade-offs in block selection, Section 3, Remark 2).
- **Clarity**: Logical flow from problem setup to algorithm to theory; figures effectively illustrate convergence.
- **Originality**: PDMM fills a gap in multi-block ADMM literature, with the dual backward step and sparsity-aware step sizes being novel contributions.
- **Significance**: Solves a practical problem (large-scale multi-block optimization) with demonstrable speedups, advancing the state of the art in parallel convex optimization.

This work makes a strong contribution to NIPS’s focus on scalable optimization. While the sequential experiments leave room for future parallel implementation studies, the theoretical and empirical results are robust, and the connections to existing methods deepen the field’s understanding. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance.
