- Introduces PDMM: a parallel randomized block-coordinate ADMM that updates K random blocks per iteration with a dual backward step to ensure convergence.  
- Addresses a literature gap: extends ADMM from well-understood two-block cases to multi-block linearly constrained convex problems.  
- Convergence guarantees: proves global convergence and O(J/(T K)) iteration complexity with constant step size (J = number of blocks, T = iterations).  
- Unifies and generalizes prior methods: sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM.  
- Handles overlapping blocks: applicable to problems like overlapping group lasso where traditional block coordinate methods struggle.  
- Key theoretical contribution: provides a unified framework for randomized block coordinate methods under mild assumptions.  
- Novel mechanism: the dual backward step enables parallel updates while maintaining convergence, addressing Jacobi ADMM limitations.  
- Step-size design: step sizes (τi, νi) account for sparsity of constraint matrix A and selected block count K, allowing larger steps than sADMM.  
- Thorough analysis: strong theoretical treatment with clear connections to existing ADMM and block-coordinate literature.  
- Empirical results: on RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM outperforms RBSUMM, sADMM, and GSADMM in runtime; PDMM3 and PDMM1 perform best.  
- K trade-offs: demonstrates smaller K reduces per-iteration cost but may increase iterations; empirical study of K’s effect provided.  
- PDMM1 paradox: PDMM1 often yields fastest runtime empirically, but the paper lacks a thorough analysis explaining why versus PDMM2/3.  
- Experimental limits: parallel implementation performance not reported, limiting practical parallelism claims.  
- Scalability analysis gap: lacks detailed computational-complexity vs. convergence rate comparisons across problem sizes.  
- Non-convex limitation: paper focuses on convex problems and does not address non-convex extensions.  
- Parameter tuning: some parameters require careful tuning in practice.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound with complete convergence proofs and well-designed experiments.  
- Clarity assessment: well-written, consistent notation, and clear exposition of relationships to prior work.  
- Originality assessment: novel algorithm and insights (dual backward step) offering theoretical and practical advances.  
- Significance assessment: addresses important large-scale optimization problems with promising practical impact.  
- Recommendation: overall strong contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel method, solid theory, practical improvements, clear prior-work connections, and good experiments.  
- Rejection/concern arguments: missing parallel performance data, parameter tuning costs, and no exploration of non-convex cases.
