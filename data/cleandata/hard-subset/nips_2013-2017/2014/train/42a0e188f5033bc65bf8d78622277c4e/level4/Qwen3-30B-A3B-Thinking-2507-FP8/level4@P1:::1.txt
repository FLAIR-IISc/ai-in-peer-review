

This paper proposes a parallel randomized block coordinate method called Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving optimization problems involving block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The authors address the gap in effective generalizations of ADMM to multiple blocks by introducing PDMM, which randomly updates K blocks in parallel at each iteration. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step size, and demonstrates its effectiveness on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

The paper makes several important contributions to the literature. First, it provides a unified framework that encompasses two existing Jacobian ADMM variants as special cases: sADMM and PJADMM. This connection offers new theoretical insight into the dual backward step parameter, which is a key innovation of the proposed method. Second, the authors develop a randomized block coordinate approach for overlapping groups, which is particularly significant as this problem remains open for randomized block coordinate descent methods. Third, the paper establishes convergence rates comparable to existing methods while offering greater flexibility in step size selection through consideration of matrix sparsity and the number of blocks being updated.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible to readers familiar with ADMM. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with well-motivated assumptions and a logically developed proof strategy. The authors carefully establish connections to previous work, demonstrating how PDMM generalizes and extends existing methods. The experimental results are thorough and well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines across two challenging applications.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its comprehensive theoretical development and empirical validation. The convergence analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results consistently show PDMM outperforming existing methods in terms of runtime while maintaining comparable or better convergence rates. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the trade-off between the number of blocks and the complexity of subproblems.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on ADMM variants for multi-block problems. While several multi-block ADMM extensions exist, the proposed PDMM framework with its randomized parallel updates and novel step size selection strategy provides a distinct approach that addresses limitations of previous methods. The connection between sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is particularly insightful and advances our understanding of these methods.

The significance of this work is clear, as it provides a practical and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging class of optimization problems with broad applications in machine learning. The ability to handle overlapping groups in a randomized block coordinate fashion is particularly valuable for applications in structured sparsity. The demonstrated speedup in experiments, especially for large-scale problems, suggests the method could be widely adopted by practitioners.

The paper has several strengths that make it compelling for NIPS. The theoretical results are complete and well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the methodological contribution is significant. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of ADMM variants and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches.

I have a few minor concerns that could be addressed to further strengthen the paper. First, the algorithm description in equations (5)-(7) needs clarification regarding the dual variable updates. It's not immediately clear whether the updates apply to all dual blocks or just a single block, and how the block selection is determined. The authors should explicitly state that the dual updates (6)-(7) apply to all dual blocks, while the primal updates (5) apply only to the selected blocks.

Second, Theorem 2 appears to have a missing summation index in the expression for L̃ρ(xt,yt). The authors should verify and correct this notation in the final version.

Third, while the paper establishes convergence rates, it would be helpful to more explicitly discuss how these rates compare to existing methods when PDMM is specialized to sADMM or PJADMM. This would further demonstrate the consistency of the theoretical results.

The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in optimization. The authors have provided a clear, well-structured, and technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art in ADMM variants for multi-block problems. The experimental results are convincing, and the method has clear potential for broader application in machine learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a well-motivated method, solid theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical results. The minor concerns noted above are easily addressable in revision and do not detract from the paper's overall quality.
