This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), called PADMM, designed for optimization problems with a multi-block structure. In each iteration, PADMM randomly samples K blocks of variables, updates the corresponding primal variables in parallel, and then updates the dual variable via a "backward step". The authors highlight the importance of this backward step, which introduces a more conservative dual update that is crucial for ensuring global convergence. The framework also allows for the inclusion of a proximal term in the primal update, which can simplify the resulting subproblems.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis establishing the algorithm's global convergence and iteration complexity. For a problem with J total blocks where K blocks are sampled in each iteration, the convergence rate is established as O(J/(TK)) after T iterations.

The primary advantages of PADMM are its ability to fully parallelize the primal update step and its tolerance for a relatively large stepsize compared to competing methods. Specifically, the authors argue that PADMM can be faster than stochastic ADMM (sADMM) due to its larger allowable stepsize, more flexible than Proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM), and more amenable to parallelization than Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The algorithm was evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results on these problems are promising, demonstrating that PADMM can achieve the desired accuracy in less computation time.

Despite these strengths, there are a few weaknesses. A notable limitation of the empirical evaluation is the absence of results from a true parallel implementation of PADMM. Given that parallelization is a core motivation for the algorithm, this is a significant omission. Another minor drawback is that tuning the three parameters of PADMM may be less convenient than tuning the single parameter of standard ADMM.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and valuable contribution to the literature on ADMM-type optimization. The proposed algorithm matches the convergence rate of standard ADMM while offering the key advantage of parallelization and exhibiting promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, with solid theoretical and empirical components.
