Summary
The paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex objectives subject to linear constraints. At each iteration, PDMM randomly selects K blocks, updates their primal variables in parallel (optionally with a proximal/Bregman term), and updates all dual variables using a “backward” correction that tempers the implicit dual step induced by the augmented Lagrangian. The authors derive parameter choices for the dual step sizes that depend on the number of updated blocks and on the sparsity pattern of the constraint matrix. They prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes; when J total blocks are present and K are updated per iteration, the rate scales like O(J/(KT)). PDMM subsumes two well-known multi-block ADMM variants as special cases: sADMM (with splitting variables) when the primal updates are exact and the dual step is small, and PJADMM when sufficiently strong proximal terms allow larger dual steps. The method can handle overlapping groups by introducing equality constraints, enabling randomized block updates even in that setting. Experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show competitive or improved wall-clock times and iteration counts relative to sADMM, RBSUMM, and (sequential) GSADMM.

Relation to prior work
Two-block ADMM is well-established; its direct multi-block generalizations can diverge, and convergence guarantees often require proximal regularization, back substitution, or restrictive assumptions. PDMM contributes a unifying perspective: by incorporating a dual backward step and step sizes tuned to sparsity and K, it bridges sADMM and PJADMM and generalizes randomized one-block methods like RBSUMM to multiple parallel updates with provable O(1/T) rates under standard convex assumptions.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual innovation: the dual backward step explicitly counteracts the implicit dual ascent from the augmented term, stabilizing parallel Jacobi-style primal updates.
- Generality: supports nonsmooth objectives, linear constraints, and overlapping groups via a clean reformulation.
- Theoretical guarantees: global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes; parameter rules account for matrix sparsity and the number of updated blocks.
- Unifying framework: shows sADMM and PJADMM are extreme instances, clarifying when small vs large dual steps are appropriate.
- Practical performance: strong empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso; PDMM attains target accuracy faster than baselines in several settings.

Weaknesses
- Lack of parallel implementation results: all experiments are sequential, so the claimed parallel scalability is not demonstrated empirically.
- Parameter sensitivity: τi, νi, ρ, and proximal/Bregman parameters require choices that may be less straightforward than standard ADMM; more guidance and ablations would help.
- Experimental comparisons: GSADMM (sequential) sometimes converges in fewer iterations; fairness of step-size tuning across methods could be discussed more explicitly.
- Clarity and readability: the notation and parameter rules (especially dependence on di and K) could be streamlined; a practical “cheat sheet” for choosing K and step sizes would aid adoption.
- Scope: results focus on convex problems; discussion of limitations for nonconvex settings or asynchronous updates is minimal.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound, with clear statements of assumptions (standard convexity and existence of a KKT point), convergence proofs, and iteration complexity. The backward step rationale is insightful and well-motivated.
- Clarity: Generally well written, though heavy notation and many parameters may hinder accessibility. Including a concise algorithm box with recommended default parameters would improve clarity.
- Originality: The dual backward step plus sparsity-aware step sizes for randomized parallel multi-block updates is novel, and the unification of sADMM and PJADMM through PDMM is conceptually valuable.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for large-scale constrained learning problems, especially where overlapping structures and parallel resources are available.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with practical relevance.
- Unifies and clarifies the landscape of multi-block ADMM variants.
- Demonstrates competitive empirical performance on representative applications.
- Enables randomized parallel updates and handles overlapping groups.

Cons against acceptance
- No empirical validation of true parallel speedups.
- Parameter tuning burden and limited guidance for practitioners.
- Some baselines and configuration choices could be reported more transparently.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include parallel (multicore/GPU or distributed) experiments to validate scalability and K-selection trade-offs.
- Provide default parameter selections, sensitivity analyses, and an ablation on the backward step and proximal terms.
- Clarify how sparsity (di) should be estimated and used in practice; include a short practitioner-oriented summary of step-size rules.
- Expand discussion of asynchronous or partially synchronous variants and potential extensions to nonconvex settings.

Overall
This is a strong and timely contribution to multi-block ADMM methods. The paper offers a principled randomized parallel algorithm with clear theoretical guarantees, unifies existing approaches, and shows promising empirical results. With additional guidance on parameter selection and parallel evaluations, it would be even more compelling. I lean toward acceptance.
