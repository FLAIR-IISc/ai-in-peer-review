

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM designed for solving multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while ADMM for two-block problems is well-understood, effective generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM randomly updates K blocks in parallel at each iteration, behaving like parallel randomized block coordinate descent, while incorporating a dual backward step to ensure convergence. The paper establishes global convergence and O(J/(TK)) iteration complexity with constant step size, where J is the total number of blocks and T the number of iterations. The authors also demonstrate that PDMM generalizes existing methods: sADMM and PJADMM are special cases, and PDMM can handle overlapping block structures for problems like overlapping group lasso, where traditional block coordinate descent methods struggle.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework for analyzing randomized block coordinate methods for multi-block problems, establishing convergence under fairly mild assumptions. Second, it introduces a novel dual backward step that enables parallel updates while maintaining convergence, addressing a key limitation of direct Jacobi ADMM variants. Third, the paper carefully considers the sparsity of the constraint matrix A and the number of randomly selected blocks K when determining step sizes, leading to more efficient algorithms. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with clear connections to prior work on ADMM variants and block coordinate descent methods. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of how PDMM relates to sADMM and PJADMM, showing how the dual backward step parameterizes the relationship between these methods.

The empirical evaluation on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso demonstrates PDMM's practical advantages. The results show PDMM converges faster than state-of-the-art methods (including RBSUMM, sADMM, and GSADMM) in terms of runtime, with PDMM3 (updating three blocks) and PDMM1 (updating one block) achieving the best performance. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of how different values of K affect convergence speed, showing that while smaller K values reduce per-iteration cost, they may require more iterations. The paper also demonstrates that PDMM's step size parameters (τi and νi) can be significantly larger than those used in sADMM due to the algorithm's careful consideration of matrix sparsity.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental design is appropriate for the claims made. The connections to related work are well-cited and demonstrate the novelty of the approach. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for careful parameter tuning and the fact that parallel implementation performance wasn't reported.

However, the paper has some limitations that merit consideration. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper doesn't fully explore the trade-offs between parallelism and convergence rate. The authors note that PDMM1 (updating one block) achieves the fastest runtime but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case compared to PDMM2 and PDMM3. The experimental section would benefit from a more detailed comparison of computational complexity versus convergence rate across different problem sizes. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss how PDMM handles non-convex problems, though the authors correctly note their focus on convex optimization.

Regarding the four NIPS criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with thorough theoretical analysis supported by empirical results. The convergence proofs are complete, and the experimental evaluation is well-designed. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent, and the relationship between PDMM and existing methods is well-explained. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM that addresses key limitations of existing methods. The connections to sADMM and PJADMM as special cases provide new theoretical insights into the role of dual backward steps. The application to overlapping group lasso demonstrates practical novelty.

Significance: The problem addressed is important in optimization and machine learning, as multi-block linearly constrained problems arise frequently in applications like RPCA and structured sparsity. PDMM's ability to achieve parallelism while maintaining convergence is valuable for large-scale problems. The experimental results demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The paper presents a novel algorithm with solid theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results. While the parallel implementation performance could have been more thoroughly reported, the theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it advances the state of the art in optimization methods with clear connections to important machine learning applications.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees, practical improvements over existing methods, clear connections to prior work, well-executed experiments, and significant potential impact for large-scale optimization problems.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited discussion of parallel implementation performance, some parameters require careful tuning, and the paper doesn't explore non-convex extensions.

Given the paper's technical soundness, clear novelty, and strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance. The contribution represents a meaningful advancement in the understanding and application of ADMM for multi-block problems, with clear potential for practical impact in machine learning applications.
