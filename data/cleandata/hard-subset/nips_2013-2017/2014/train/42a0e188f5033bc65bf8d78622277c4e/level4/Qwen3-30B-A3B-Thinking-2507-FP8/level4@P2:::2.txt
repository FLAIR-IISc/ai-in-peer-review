

This paper introduces a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for solving optimization problems with block-separable convex objectives subject to linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) has been extensively studied for two-block problems, generalizations to multiple blocks remain poorly understood. PDMM extends ADMM by allowing parallel updates of multiple blocks (rather than sequential Gauss-Seidel updates), incorporating a novel dual backward step to ensure convergence, and establishing O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it introduces a dual backward step that compensates for the limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian-type updates, which had previously been shown to cause convergence issues in parallel ADMM variants. The authors demonstrate that this dual backward step is crucial for convergence under general conditions, unlike previous methods that required restrictive assumptions. Second, they establish that two existing methods—sADMM (splitting ADMM) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobian ADMM)—are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying framework that clarifies the relationships between these approaches. Third, they extend PDMM to handle overlapping blocks in composite minimization problems, which was previously an open problem for randomized block coordinate descent methods.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear convergence guarantees under standard assumptions. The paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity for PDMM with constant step sizes, which matches the best-known rates for ADMM variants. The authors carefully analyze how the step sizes are determined based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated, which is a significant practical contribution. The connection between PDMM and previous methods is well-explained, with detailed mathematical derivations showing how the parameters of sADMM and PJADMM can be recovered as special cases.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates PDMM's effectiveness on two important applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The results show PDMM outperforms existing methods, including ADMM, sADMM, and RBSUMM, in both convergence speed and runtime. Notably, PDMM with K=1 (updating one block at a time) converges faster than RBSUMM, which also updates one block randomly. The authors provide detailed parameter tuning results, showing that PDMM's performance is robust to parameter choices.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature. The connection between PDMM and previous methods provides valuable theoretical insights, while the practical performance improvements demonstrate its utility for large-scale optimization problems. The experimental results are thorough and well-motivated, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

One potential weakness is the counterintuitive nature of the results: Jacobian-type methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel methods, yet PDMM (a Jacobian method) outperforms Gauss-Seidel variants like GSADMM in terms of runtime. The authors briefly acknowledge this but do not fully explain why PDMM achieves this performance advantage. A more detailed discussion of this phenomenon would strengthen the paper, particularly regarding how the dual backward step and parallel updates offset the usual convergence disadvantage of Jacobian methods.

Another minor point is that the paper could more explicitly discuss the trade-offs between the number of blocks K and computational efficiency. While the experiments show that using more blocks generally improves convergence speed, the optimal choice of K depends on the specific problem structure and hardware constraints, which would be valuable for practitioners.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The dual backward step is a novel and crucial component that enables convergence under general conditions, and the unification of previous methods through PDMM provides significant theoretical insight.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel dual backward step resolves convergence issues in parallel ADMM variants
- Establishes O(1/T) convergence with constant step sizes
- Unifies and provides theoretical insights into existing methods (sADMM, PJADMM)
- Demonstrates practical advantages in key applications
- Well-written with clear theoretical and experimental results

Arguments against acceptance:
- Counterintuitive performance results (Jacobian method outperforming Gauss-Seidel) not fully explained
- Limited discussion of practical considerations for choosing K
- Some technical details could be more accessible to a broader audience

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written and clearly organized (Clarity), presents a novel approach to a significant problem (Originality), and demonstrates clear practical impact (Significance). The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization with clear theoretical and practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
