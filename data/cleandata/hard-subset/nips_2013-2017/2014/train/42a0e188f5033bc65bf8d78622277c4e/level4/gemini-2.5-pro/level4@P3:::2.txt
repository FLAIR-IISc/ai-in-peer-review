### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints, a canonical form for many problems in machine learning. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for the two-block case, its generalization to multiple blocks remains an active area of research. Existing multi-block methods are often sequential (Gauss-Seidel style), which can be slow, or parallel (Jacobian style), which can suffer from convergence issues.

The authors propose the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm that updates a random subset of primal variable blocks in parallel at each iteration. The key technical innovation is the introduction of a "dual backward step," which effectively makes the dual variable update more conservative. This step compensates for the lack of immediate information propagation inherent in parallel updates, thereby ensuring convergence. The method also features an adaptive step-size rule that incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing an O(1/T) convergence rate with a constant step size. They also demonstrate that two existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, which provides a unifying perspective on this class of algorithms. The efficacy of PDMM is demonstrated on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of developing a convergent, parallel ADMM-style method for multi-block optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, and its core idea—the dual backward step to stabilize parallel updates—is an elegant and effective contribution. This work provides a valuable new tool for large-scale distributed optimization.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper offers a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. It establishes global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity under standard assumptions, which matches the rate of classic ADMM. The analysis is comprehensive and covers both exact and inexact updates within a unified Bregman divergence framework.

3.  **Unification of Prior Work:** A significant strength is the demonstration (in the supplement) that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. This connection is insightful, clarifying the relationship between different Jacobian-style ADMM variants and providing a deeper understanding of the trade-off between primal update accuracy and dual update step size. It helps position PDMM as a general and flexible framework rather than just another specific algorithm.

4.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are convincing. PDMM is shown to outperform several relevant baselines, including variants of ADMM and specialized solvers. The ability to effectively solve problems with overlapping groups by reformulating them into the paper's framework is another strong practical contribution.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Intuition on Jacobian vs. Gauss-Seidel Performance:** The experimental results are compelling, particularly the finding that a parallel Jacobian-style method can outperform its sequential Gauss-Seidel counterparts (like GSADMM in the RPCA experiment) in runtime. This is somewhat counter-intuitive, as Gauss-Seidel methods leverage the most recently updated information and are often expected to converge in fewer iterations. The authors could strengthen the paper by adding a brief discussion to provide intuition for why PDMM is so effective. Is this primarily due to the reduced computational cost per iteration which outweighs a potentially higher iteration count, the specific problem structures, or the benefits of the carefully chosen step sizes that stabilize the algorithm effectively?

2.  **Practical Guidance on Parallelization:** The experiments were run sequentially to fairly compare iteration counts and single-thread runtime. However, given the method's name and primary motivation, the paper would benefit from a discussion on its practical parallel implementation. A brief comment on expected speedups, potential communication bottlenecks, or even a small experiment on a multi-core system would more directly validate the "Parallel" aspect of the method and its potential for significant real-world performance gains.

3.  **Guidance on Choosing K:** The paper shows the performance of PDMM for different values of K (the number of blocks updated in parallel). The results suggest a trade-off between the degree of parallelism and the convergence rate. It would be helpful to include a short discussion on practical guidance for choosing an optimal K, which likely depends on the problem structure and the underlying hardware architecture (e.g., communication costs vs. computation).

### **Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical proofs supporting its claims. The method is well-motivated, and the work appears complete.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-written and well-organized. The core ideas, particularly the motivation for the dual backward step, are explained clearly. The connection to previous work is insightful.
*   **Originality:** High. The proposed PDMM algorithm, with its dual backward step and adaptive step-sizing for parallel updates, is a novel contribution to the ADMM literature. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM further enhances its originality.
*   **Significance:** High. The work addresses a long-standing and important problem in large-scale optimization. The proposed algorithm is practical, parallelizable, and demonstrates strong performance. It is likely that other researchers and practitioners will use and build upon these ideas, especially for distributed machine learning applications.

### **Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses the important problem of multi-block ADMM with a novel, convergent, and parallelizable method.
    *   Provides strong theoretical guarantees (O(1/T) convergence).
    *   Unifies and provides new insight into existing Jacobian-style ADMM methods.
    *   Demonstrates superior empirical performance against state-of-the-art methods on relevant applications.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The paper could offer more intuition regarding its strong performance compared to sequential methods.
    *   Lacks experiments demonstrating speedups in a true parallel computing environment. (This is a minor point, as the algorithm's design is inherently parallel.)

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature and the machine learning community. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed with small additions to the text. I recommend acceptance.
