This paper presents the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to multi-block linear constraints. The challenge of extending the highly successful two-block Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting is well-known; direct extensions can fail to converge or require restrictive assumptions. The authors address this by proposing a method that, at each iteration, randomly selects a subset of `K` blocks and updates their corresponding primal variables in parallel.

The key innovations of PDMM are twofold. First, it introduces a "backward step" on the dual variable update. This step effectively tempers the dual ascent, preventing the overly aggressive updates that often lead to divergence in parallel (Jacobi-style) ADMM variants. Second, the algorithm incorporates an adaptive step-size rule for the dual updates that depends on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks `K` being updated in parallel. The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, establishing its global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that PDMM provides a unifying framework that includes existing methods like the splitting-variable ADMM (sADMM) and proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) as special cases. The utility of the method is demonstrated on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. The development of a provably convergent, parallel, and randomized ADMM variant for the multi-block case is a noteworthy achievement. The concept of the dual backward step is an elegant solution to the convergence problems plaguing direct parallel extensions of ADMM. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM under the PDMM framework provides valuable insight into the design of this class of algorithms, connecting the conservativeness of the primal update to the aggressiveness of the dual update. The application to overlapping regularizers is also a strong point, showcasing the algorithm's flexibility.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing a global convergence guarantee and an O(1/T) complexity rate, which is standard for first-order methods of this type. The assumptions are reasonable and in line with those used for standard ADMM. The experimental results are convincing, showing that PDMM consistently outperforms or is competitive with several state-of-the-art methods (GSADMM, sADMM, RBSUMM) on non-trivial problems. The ablation study on the effect of the number of parallel blocks (`K`) provides useful insights into the practical behavior of the algorithm.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is strong, and the authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature. The algorithmic steps are laid out clearly, and the intuition behind the key components, such as the dual backward step, is well-explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation:** The primary motivation and name of the algorithm centers on its parallel nature. However, the experiments were run sequentially. While the wall-clock time results suggest that parallelization would be beneficial (e.g., showing that updating a subset of blocks can be faster than updating all blocks or just one), the true performance gains can only be confirmed with an actual parallel implementation. Demonstrating the method's scalability on a multi-core or distributed system would have made the claims of parallel efficiency substantially more compelling.

*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper provides a principled rule (Equation 8) for setting the step-size parameters `τi` and `νi`, which is a theoretical strength. However, the experimental section mentions tuning these parameters to get the "best" results (Table 1), and these tuned values differ from the theoretical ones. This raises a practical question about parameter selection. It would be beneficial for the authors to include a discussion on the performance gap between the theoretically-guaranteed step sizes and the empirically optimal ones. Providing more practical guidance or an analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to these parameters would increase its utility for practitioners.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Proposes a novel, flexible, and provably convergent parallel algorithm for multi-block ADMM, a well-known and challenging problem.
    *   The theoretical contributions, including the convergence and complexity analysis, are strong and sound.
    *   Provides new conceptual insights by unifying related existing methods under a single framework.
    *   Demonstrates impressive empirical performance against relevant baselines on important machine learning tasks.
    *   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The central claim of parallelism is not validated with a parallel implementation, which is a notable omission.
    *   There is a slight disconnect between the theoretical guidance on parameter setting and the practical tuning performed in the experiments.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the optimization and machine learning community. The theoretical and empirical results are solid, and the proposed algorithm is both novel and significant. While the lack of a true parallel implementation is a limitation, the strengths of the paper in terms of its theoretical novelty and empirical performance are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
