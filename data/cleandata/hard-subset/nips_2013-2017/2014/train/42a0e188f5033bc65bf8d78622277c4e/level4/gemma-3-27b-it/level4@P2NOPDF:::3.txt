This paper introduces a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), designed to efficiently handle problems with multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) iteratively selects a random subset of *K* blocks, updates the corresponding primal variables, and then performs a backward step to update the dual variables. The authors highlight the crucial role of this backward step in ensuring a more conservative dual update, which is key to establishing global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm incorporates a proximal term into the primal update, facilitating the solution of a wider range of optimization problems. 

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, proving the algorithm’s global convergence and deriving its iteration complexity. Specifically, with a total of *J* blocks and *K* blocks randomly selected per iteration, the convergence rate is shown to be O(*J* / (*T* *K*)) after *T* iterations.

PADMM’s strengths lie in its ability to (1) fully parallelize the primal update step and (2) accommodate relatively larger step sizes compared to existing methods. This larger step size allows PADMM to potentially outperform sequential ADMM (sADMM). The algorithm is also presented as more flexible than Parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more amenable to parallelization than Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems, achieving promising results. Their empirical evaluation indicates that PADMM reaches the desired accuracy with reduced computation time.

However, a notable limitation is the absence of reported results demonstrating the performance gains achieved through a parallel implementation. Additionally, compared to standard ADMM, PADMM introduces three parameters that require tuning, potentially increasing the complexity of practical application.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ADMM-based optimization, particularly for problems with multiple constraints. While maintaining a convergence rate comparable to traditional ADMM, PADMM offers the significant advantages of parallelization and promising empirical performance. The paper is well-written, with both the theoretical analysis and experimental validation appearing sound and robust.
