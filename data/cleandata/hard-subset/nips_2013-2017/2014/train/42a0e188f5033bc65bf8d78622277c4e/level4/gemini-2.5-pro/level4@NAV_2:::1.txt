This paper addresses the important problem of minimizing block-separable convex functions with linear constraints, a structure common in many machine learning tasks. The authors correctly point out that while the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for two-block problems, its generalization to the multi-block setting remains an active area of research with challenges in ensuring convergence. The central contribution of this work is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM. At each iteration, PDMM updates a randomly selected subset of primal variable blocks in parallel. Crucially, it incorporates a "backward step" in the dual variable update, with step sizes that cleverly adapt to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated. The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity with a constant step size. Furthermore, the work offers valuable insight by showing that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of the proposed PDMM framework.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and difficult problem: the efficient and provably convergent extension of ADMM to multi-block optimization. The proposed solution is both practical and theoretically sound.
*   **Originality:** The combination of parallel randomized updates with an adaptive dual backward step is novel. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM under the PDMM framework is an insightful contribution that clarifies the relationship between these methods.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results, including the proofs for global convergence and iteration complexity, appear to be technically sound. The claims are well-supported by experiments on relevant, large-scale problems like robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, which demonstrate clear performance benefits over state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the proposed algorithm is situated well within the context of related work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong, but could be improved with the following additions and clarifications.

1.  **Algorithm Clarity:** The description of the dual update in equations (6) and (7) is ambiguous. The subscript `i` is used, but the surrounding text states that "all dual blocks" are updated. Please clarify whether the updates for `y` and `ŷ` are performed for all indices `i=1,...,I` at each iteration, or if a single dual block `i` is selected. If the latter, the selection mechanism needs to be specified.

2.  **Discussion of Theoretical Results:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 2, is powerful but its implications are not fully explored. An expanded discussion would significantly strengthen the paper:
    *   Since PDMM generalizes several ADMM variants, it would be highly instructive to show how Theorem 2's convergence rate is consistent with, or relates to, the known rates of these specialized methods (e.g., sADMM) when the parameters `K` and `J` are chosen accordingly.
    *   The convergence rate depends on the blocking of the matrix `A`. Does the theorem provide any insight or practical guidance on how to best partition `A` to achieve the fastest convergence? A brief discussion of the trade-off between the number of blocks (and thus parallelism) and the per-iteration progress as dictated by the bound would be very valuable.

3.  **Experimental Context:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. A minor suggestion would be to briefly comment on the applicability of PDMM to other prominent multi-block problems, such as tensor completion (e.g., Liu et al., "Tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data," 2012), and speculate on its potential advantages in that context.

**Minor Issues:**
*   There appears to be a typo in the statement of Theorem 2. The summation `∑_{i=1}^I` seems to be missing within the main curly braces `{...}`. Please verify this based on the proof in the supplement.
*   In equation (9), a space is missing after "argmin".
*   On Line 217, there is an unnecessary space before "Section".

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective algorithm for a significant class of optimization problems. The theoretical and empirical support is strong. I recommend acceptance and encourage the authors to incorporate the suggested clarifications and discussions to further enhance the impact of their work.
