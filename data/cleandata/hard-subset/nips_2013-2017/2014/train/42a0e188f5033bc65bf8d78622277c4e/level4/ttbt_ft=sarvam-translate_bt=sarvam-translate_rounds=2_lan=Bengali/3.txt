This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM that can handle paralysis with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM selects K number of random blocks, updates the block prime vector, and then updates the dual vector through a backward step. The authors highlight the importance of using the backward step, as it makes the dual update more conservative, enabling global convergence. The algorithm allows for the addition of a proximal term to the prime update, which makes it easier to solve a subset of the optimization problem. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and iteration complexity. When there are a total of J blocks and the PADMM algorithm randomly selects K blocks, the algorithm's convergence rate after T iterations of updates is O(J/(TK)).

The main advantage of the PADMM algorithm is (1) it allows for complete paralysis for the prime step and (2) it allows for relatively large update steps compared to other methods. In particular, PADMM can be faster than SADMM because it allows for larger step sizes. It is more flexible than PJADMM and more paralizable than GSADMM. The authors have evaluated the algorithm in Robert Principals component analysis and overlapping group lasso. Experimental results are quite promising. The PADMM algorithm achieves the desired accuracy in a short computation time. Nevertheless, the authors have not reported on the performance of PADMM in parallel implementations. Compared to traditional ADMM, tuning the three parameters of PADMM is less convenient.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are provided. The algorithm has an ADMM-like convergence rate, but it allows for paralysis and demonstrates promising practical performance. Overall, this is an interesting contribution to solving ADMM-type problems. The paper is well-written. Both the theoretical and empirical parts are challenging.
