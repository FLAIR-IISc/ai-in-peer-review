- Introduces PDMM: randomized, parallel (Jacobi-style) updates for multi-block convex problems with linear constraints and a stabilizing “dual backward step.”
- Dual backward step tempers implicit dual ascent from the augmented Lagrangian, stabilizing Jacobi updates.
- Proves global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant step sizes.
- Step-size rules explicitly depend on A’s sparsity (row-block degrees) and the number of updated blocks.
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM arise as limiting cases of PDMM, unifying prior variants.
- Empirical results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM is competitive or better than ADMM variants and first-order baselines.
- Context: naive multi-block ADMM can diverge; prior fixes include GS schemes, proximal Jacobian (PJADMM), and randomized single-block methods.
- PDMM’s contribution: principled randomized Jacobi framework with constant steps, explicit rates, and unification of sADMM/PJADMM.
- Strength: clear, intuitive algorithmic idea (backward dual step) addressing Jacobi instability.
- Strength: solid theory under standard convex assumptions without diminishing step sizes.
- Strength: unifying perspective clarifies when small vs large dual steps are appropriate.
- Strength: naturally handles overlapping groups via equality constraints enabling randomized updates.
- Strength: favorable empirical performance in iterations and runtime (sequential Matlab).
- Weakness: experiments run sequentially; lacks multi-core/distributed evaluations to demonstrate parallel advantage and communication costs.
- Weakness: sensitivity and robustness to τi and νi need more discussion and empirical analysis.
- Weakness: counterintuitive gains over GSADMM need more explanation and ablation to isolate the backward dual step’s effect.
- Weakness: practical guidance on selecting K, grouping strategies, and per-iteration cost vs iteration count trade-offs is limited.
- Weakness: computing degree-based step sizes assumes known sparsity; overhead and extensions to dynamic/approximate sparsity not addressed; constants in complexity bounds not fully contextualized.
- Quality: technically sound with careful convergence analysis and supportive experiments, but broader benchmarks and true parallel tests would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: generally well written but dense in parts; would benefit from more intuition, a parameter-mapping table, and a practical tuning guide.
- Originality: the dual backward step in a randomized Jacobi multi-block ADMM and sparsity-/K-aware step sizes are meaningful and original contributions.
- Significance: expands tools for large-scale constrained convex optimization with provable parallel updates and practical step-size rules.
- For acceptance: novel mechanism, rigorous theory, unifying view, and positive experiments.
- Against acceptance: missing true parallel experiments, limited sensitivity analysis and practical guidance, and a somewhat narrow empirical scope.
- Overall: solid, timely contribution that would be stronger with additional empirical depth and clearer practical guidance.
