This paper proposes a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM (PADMM) for multi-block problems. In each iteration, the method samples K out of J blocks, updates the selected primal blocks in parallel (optionally with proximal terms), and then performs a conservative “backward” update of the dual variable. The authors argue—and support theoretically—that this backward step is key to ensuring global convergence. They establish global convergence and an iteration complexity of O(J/(TK)) after T iterations, matching the O(1/T) rate of standard ADMM while exposing parallel speedups through the J/K factor.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: randomized block selection with parallel primal updates and a conservative dual backward step that stabilizes multi-block ADMM.
- Solid theory: global convergence guarantees and an explicit iteration complexity bound that cleanly captures dependence on the number of blocks J and the sampling size K.
- Practical flexibility: the inclusion of proximal terms in the primal updates can make subproblems easier and more numerically stable.
- Empirical promise: on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, the method reaches target accuracy with lower computation time than baselines (as reported).

Weaknesses and questions
- Parallel performance is not demonstrated. While the algorithm is inherently parallel in the primal step, the experiments appear to be single-threaded. Without wall-clock results under genuine parallel execution, it is hard to assess the claimed practical advantage.
- Parameter tuning burden. Compared with vanilla ADMM, PADMM introduces additional parameters (e.g., sampling size K, step sizes for primal/dual, and proximal coefficients). The paper notes this but provides limited guidance, which may hinder adoption and fair comparison.
- Comparisons and settings. Claims of faster convergence than sADMM (via larger step sizes), more flexibility than PJADMM, and better parallelizability than GSADMM would be stronger with:
  - Side-by-side sensitivity analyses across a grid of step sizes and proximal strengths.
  - Reporting per-iteration cost and wall-clock times, including communication/synchronization overhead where relevant.
  - Clarifying whether all methods are tuned to their best performance under comparable computational budgets.
- Assumptions and scope. The convergence results would benefit from crisper statements of assumptions (e.g., convexity, closedness, existence of saddle points, Lipschitz continuity or strong convexity where used, bounded variance of randomization). It would help to specify when proximal terms are required for convergence or merely for convenience.
- Randomization details. It is not fully clear whether blocks are sampled uniformly without replacement, with replacement, or via an importance scheme, and how the theory adapts to each. The impact of K on variance and convergence speed (beyond the J/K rate factor) could be discussed more explicitly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide parallel experiments: multi-core or distributed implementations demonstrating speedups versus (i) single-threaded PADMM, (ii) standard ADMM, and (iii) competing multi-block or coordinate-update ADMM variants. Include scaling plots versus number of threads and K.
- Offer practical tuning guidelines: heuristics for choosing K (e.g., proportional to available cores), dual step sizes, and proximal parameters. A small ablation study on sensitivity would be valuable.
- Expand comparisons: include sADMM, PJADMM, GSADMM with matched stopping criteria and best-tuned hyperparameters. Report both iteration counts and wall-clock time.
- Clarify theory and assumptions: state all conditions up front; indicate whether the O(J/(TK)) rate holds in expectation; discuss if stronger assumptions yield faster (e.g., linear) rates; and specify how proximal terms affect constants.
- Discuss implementation details: costs of computing residuals, potential need for preconditioning, and how asynchrony (if any) would affect convergence.

Minor comments
- “palatalization” should be “parallelization.”
- Use “step size” rather than “stepsize.”
- Define acronyms (e.g., sADMM, PJADMM, GSADMM) at first mention and cite canonical references.
- When stating the rate O(J/(TK)), specify whether it characterizes the ergodic primal-dual gap or another measure, and whether it is in expectation.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to scalable optimization with ADMM-style methods. The algorithmic design is simple and appealing, the theory is sound, and the empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are promising. The primary shortcoming is the absence of a true parallel implementation and evaluation, which is central to the paper’s motivation. With stronger empirical evidence of parallel speedups and clearer guidance on parameter selection, the paper would be significantly strengthened. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the experimental and clarity issues above.
