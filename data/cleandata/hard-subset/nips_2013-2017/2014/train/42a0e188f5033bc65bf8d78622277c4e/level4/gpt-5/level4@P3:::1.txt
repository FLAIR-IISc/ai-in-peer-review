Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles convex optimization problems with block-separable objectives subject to linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for multi-block problems. At each iteration, PDMM samples K primal blocks and updates them in parallel using the augmented Lagrangian with an additional dual “backward step” to temper overly aggressive dual progress. The method allows exact or inexact primal updates via Bregman linearization, accommodates overlapping blocks, and sets dual step sizes that depend on the sparsity pattern of A and the number of updated blocks. The authors prove global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes. They demonstrate empirical advantages on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso.

This work builds on extensive literature on ADMM. For two blocks, ADMM is well understood. For multiple blocks, Gauss–Seidel variants (e.g., GSADMM, BSUMM) and Jacobian/proximal variants (e.g., sADMM with variable splitting, PJADMM) have been explored, but convergence and rates often require restrictive conditions or small/decreasing step sizes. RBSUMM randomizes a single block per iteration but its iteration complexity is unclear. PDMM generalizes these methods: sADMM and PJADMM emerge as limiting cases depending on whether the primal update is exact (small dual step) or heavily regularized (full dual step), providing a unifying perspective.

Quality
The technical development is solid. The convergence analysis (global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic rate) under standard convexity assumptions parallels what is known for ADMM-style methods, and the dependence of step sizes on row-block degrees di and K is insightful. The inexact update framework via Bregman divergences is practical and ties to known linearization techniques. The experiments are relevant and indicate improvements over strong baselines, though all implementations are sequential, so the benefits of parallelism are not fully realized or quantified.

A few points need clarification for reproducibility and interpretation: the exact scope of dual updates (all blocks vs. selected blocks), a minor typographical omission in Theorem 2, and a more explicit discussion of how the general rate specializes to existing ADMM variants and how to choose the block partition of A in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The algorithm description would benefit from making explicit which dual coordinates are updated at each iteration and how the sampling interacts with the per-row parameters τi and νi. The notations for block indices and the role of K̃i are well motivated but could be summarized concisely in the algorithm box. Minor typographical issues are listed below.

Originality
The dual backward step, the explicit dependence of step sizes on sparsity and K, and the ability to update multiple blocks randomly in parallel distinguish PDMM from prior work. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM via PDMM’s parameters is an elegant conceptual contribution. Handling overlapping blocks in a randomized parallel fashion is also noteworthy.

Significance
The proposed method advances multi-block ADMM methodology with practical parallelism and provable guarantees under constant step sizes. Given the prevalence of structured sparsity and large-scale linearly constrained problems in machine learning, PDMM is likely to be useful. The empirical gains on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are encouraging. Clearer guidance on parameter/blocking choices would further increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; broad applicability (including overlapping groups).
- Simple, parallelizable updates with a principled dual backward step.
- Global convergence and O(1/T) complexity with constant step sizes; parameters reflect matrix sparsity and sampling rate K.
- Unifies sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, clarifying when small vs. full dual steps are appropriate.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines on two representative tasks.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Algorithm description leaves ambiguity about whether all dual blocks are updated each iteration; selection of dual indices “i” is unclear.
- Limited discussion on how the theoretical rate recovers known rates for sADMM/PJADMM and related methods via parameter specialization.
- No guidance on how to partition A into blocks to optimize convergence, given Theorem 2’s dependence on di and K̃i.
- Empirical comparisons are sequential; parallel wall-clock benefits are not demonstrated.
- Minor typographical/notation issues (see below).

Questions for the authors
1) Algorithm updates: In (5)–(7), do you update all dual blocks yi and ŷi at each iteration (as equations suggest), or only a subset? The text says “(6) and (7) update all dual blocks,” but later you mention a dual block-coordinate variant. Please clarify the default PDMM instance studied in the paper and, if applicable, summarize the dual-coordinate extension and its guarantees.

2) Theorem 2 notation: In the first bound, a summation ∑i=1 I over {…} appears to be missing. Please confirm and correct.

3) Specialization to prior methods: Since PDMM recovers sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, please explicitly show how Theorem 2 specializes to their known O(1/T)-type rates (or o(1/T) in PJADMM with sufficiently large proximal terms). A brief corollary or remark aligning constants and parameter choices (K, τi, νi, ηj) would help.

4) Block partitioning guidance: The rate depends on di and K̃i. What practical heuristics do you recommend for splitting A (number of blocks, grouping by sparsity pattern, balancing di across rows) to accelerate convergence? Can you comment on trade-offs between larger K (fewer iterations, higher per-iteration cost) and smaller K?

5) Tensor completion: For multi-linear/tensor completion formulations with multiple blocks (e.g., nuclear norms on unfoldings), which PDMM parameterization (K, τi, νi, ηj) and block partition would you expect to work best? Any empirical observations or theoretical guidance relative to sADMM or PJADMM?

6) Fair comparisons: You note the public sADMM code lacks a dual update (τi = 0), which hurts its performance. Could you include results with a corrected sADMM implementation (τi = 1/J) to ensure fairness?

Minor comments
- Eq. (9): add a space after “min”.
- Line 217: remove the space before “Section”.
- Please restate in the algorithm box that K̃i = min{di, K} and that τi = K/[K̃i(2J − K)], νi = 1 − 1/K̃i for the analyzed PDMM instance.
- A brief summary table mapping PDMM parameters to sADMM and PJADMM would help readers.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practically relevant extension of ADMM to randomized parallel multi-block settings, unifies prior variants, and shows promising empirical performance. Clarifying the algorithm updates, fixing minor issues, and expanding the discussion around Theorem 2’s specializations and block partitioning would further strengthen the contribution.
