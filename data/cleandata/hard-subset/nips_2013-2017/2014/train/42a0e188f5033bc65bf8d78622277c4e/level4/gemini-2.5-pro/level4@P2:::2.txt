This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear equality constraints. This is a fundamental problem in machine learning and optimization. While the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) is highly effective for the two-block case, its generalization to multiple blocks remains an active area of research. Existing multi-block extensions are often sequential (Gauss-Seidel type), which limits parallelism, or direct parallel (Jacobian type) extensions that can fail to converge.

The main contribution of this work is the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm that performs parallel, randomized block coordinate updates on the primal variables. The key innovation that ensures convergence is the introduction of a "dual backward step." This step effectively tempers the dual variable update, compensating for the potentially destabilizing effects of parallel primal updates that use information from the previous iteration. The authors also propose an adaptive step-size rule for the dual update that cleverly incorporates the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated in parallel.

The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PDMM, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, under standard assumptions similar to those for ADMM. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that PDMM provides a unifying framework for this class of algorithms, showing that two prior methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems, where it shows competitive or superior performance against several ADMM variants.

### Strengths and Positive Aspects

*   **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The core idea of using a dual backward step to guarantee the convergence of a parallel multi-block ADMM is novel and significant. It directly addresses a well-known difficulty with Jacobian-style ADMM extensions and provides a principled solution. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM within the PDMM framework is also an elegant conceptual contribution that improves the understanding of the algorithmic landscape.

*   **Significance and Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is inherently parallel, making it highly suitable for modern distributed and multi-core computing environments. This is a major advantage over the more common Gauss-Seidel (sequential) extensions of ADMM. The ability to solve problems with overlapping regularizers (like overlapping group lasso) in a randomized block-coordinate fashion is another strong, practical contribution, as this is a challenging structure for many existing methods.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. The authors provide rigorous proofs for global convergence and establish an O(1/T) rate, which is the standard expected rate for this class of first-order methods. The analysis appears sound and is conducted under fairly general assumptions.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained, and the connections to related work are well-articulated, placing the contribution in proper context.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the practical advantages of PDMM. The comparison with relevant baselines, including sequential and randomized methods, is convincing and supports the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Intuition on Jacobian vs. Gauss-Seidel Performance:** The experimental results showing PDMM (a Jacobian-type method) outperforming Gauss-Seidel variants are compelling but somewhat counter-intuitive. Gauss-Seidel methods leverage the most recently updated information within an iteration, which typically leads to faster convergence in terms of iteration count. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on this point. Is the superior runtime performance of PDMM primarily due to the potential for parallelization, or do the specific adaptive step-sizes and the dual backward step also contribute to better per-iteration progress than one might expect from a parallel scheme? A deeper intuitive explanation would be very helpful for the reader.

*   **Guidance on Parameter Selection:** The performance of PDMM depends on the number of blocks, `K`, chosen at each iteration. The experiments show this choice has a significant impact on runtime. While the theory holds for any `K`, the paper could provide more practical guidance or heuristics for selecting an optimal or near-optimal value of `K`, perhaps as a trade-off between parallel computation gains and convergence speed.

*   **Discussion of Parallel Implementation:** While the algorithm is designed for parallel execution, the experiments were run sequentially. A brief discussion of the practical overhead in a true parallel setting (e.g., communication costs for synchronization and gathering messages for the dual update) would make the claims about parallel performance more concrete and complete.

### Summary of Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on distributed and large-scale optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and practically relevant. It effectively addresses the convergence issues of parallel ADMM extensions through the clever introduction of a dual backward step. The unification of previous methods and the strong empirical results further underscore the paper's value. The weaknesses identified are minor and mainly relate to providing deeper intuition and practical guidance. I recommend acceptance.
