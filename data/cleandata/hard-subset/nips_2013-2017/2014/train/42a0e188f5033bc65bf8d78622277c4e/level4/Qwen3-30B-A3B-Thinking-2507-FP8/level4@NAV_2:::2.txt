

This paper proposes a Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as while ADMM has been extensively studied for two-block problems, generalizations to multiple blocks remain challenging. The authors introduce a novel dual backward step that enables convergence of Jacobian-style updates for multiple blocks, overcoming a key limitation of previous methods. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel using randomized block coordinate descent, and the paper establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size. The authors demonstrate that two existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective on these approaches. The theoretical results are technically sound, with careful attention to the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) It introduces a dual backward step that compensates for limited information propagation in Jacobian updates, which appears to be the key innovation enabling convergence under general conditions; (2) It establishes connections between PDMM and existing methods, enhancing understanding of the broader landscape of decomposition methods based on augmented Lagrangian; (3) It demonstrates the method's applicability to overlapping block problems, where previous approaches have struggled. The experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso show PDMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of runtime, with PDMM3 and PDMM1 achieving particularly strong results.

Strengths: The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, addressing convergence properties and iteration complexity. The connection to sADMM and PJADMM provides valuable theoretical insight. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate practical advantages. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses: The experimental results are somewhat counterintuitive, as Jacobian-type methods typically converge slower than Gauss-Seidel methods, but PDMM outperforms GSADMM in runtime despite being a Jacobian method. The paper could provide more insight into why this occurs. The discussion of the dual backward step could be strengthened with additional intuition.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution with clear connections to existing work, practical advantages demonstrated through experiments, and significant potential for application in distributed optimization.

Arguments against acceptance: The counterintuitive experimental results could benefit from more thorough explanation.

The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization with clear relevance to NIPS topics. It makes a technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art in distributed optimization, with clear potential for impact in machine learning applications. The work is of high quality, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature.
