This paper introduces a randomized parallel variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed to efficiently address problems with multiple blocks. The proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) operates by randomly selecting *K* blocks in each iteration, updating the corresponding primal variables, and subsequently performing a backward step for dual variable updates. The authors highlight the benefit of this backward step, demonstrating its role in promoting a more conservative dual update and facilitating global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm accommodates a proximal term within the primal update, simplifying the solution process for certain optimization problems. A theoretical analysis accompanies the algorithm, proving its global convergence and establishing its iteration complexity. Specifically, after *T* iterations, with a total of *J* blocks and a block selection size of *K*, the convergence rate is shown to be O(*J* / (*T* *K*)).

PADMM’s key strengths lie in (1) its capacity for full parallelization of the primal update step and (2) its ability to utilize relatively larger step sizes compared to existing methods. Notably, PADMM can outperform sADMM due to its larger permissible step sizes, while offering greater flexibility than PJADMM and superior parallelizability to GSADMM. The algorithm’s effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, yielding encouraging results. PADMM achieves the required accuracy with reduced computational cost. However, the paper lacks performance results from a parallel implementation of the algorithm. Compared to standard ADMM, PADMM introduces a slight increase in complexity due to the need to tune three parameters. 

In conclusion, this work presents a valuable contribution to the field of ADMM-based optimization, particularly for problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm maintains a convergence rate comparable to that of ADMM while enabling parallel computation and exhibiting strong empirical performance. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with both the theoretical analysis and experimental validation presented rigorously.
