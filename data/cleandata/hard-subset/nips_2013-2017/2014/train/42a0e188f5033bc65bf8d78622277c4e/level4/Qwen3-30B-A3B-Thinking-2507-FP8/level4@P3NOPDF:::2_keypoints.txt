- Introduces PDMM, a parallel algorithm for block-separable convex minimization with linear constraints.  
- Uses Jacobian-style parallel primal updates across multiple blocks, unlike ADMM’s two-block Gauss-Seidel updates.  
- Proves PDMM attains O(1/T) convergence rates, matching ADMM while being parallelizable.  
- Key innovation is the dual backward step that adaptively reduces dual step size to compensate limited information propagation.  
- Dual backward step enables convergence under general conditions without restrictive assumptions required by other parallel methods.  
- Shows sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying theoretical perspective.  
- Experimental results demonstrate PDMM often outperforms ADMM and variants in distributed optimization, especially with parallel hardware.  
- Counterintuitive finding: Jacobian PDMM matches or surpasses Gauss-Seidel ADMM, contrary to usual expectations about information flow and convergence.  
- Reviewer requests deeper analysis explaining how the dual backward step compensates for sequential information exchange (e.g., step-size dynamics or small-scale example).  
- Suggests main text should briefly reference supplementary proofs linking PDMM to prior methods to avoid forcing readers to consult appendices.  
- Minor experimental concern: comparisons should explicitly address scenarios where sequential ADMM is impractical and quantify wall-clock speedups from parallelism.  
- Overall assessment: technically rigorous, well-motivated, experimentally supported, and merits acceptance pending clarification of the convergence paradox.
