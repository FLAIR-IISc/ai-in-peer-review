This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. In contrast to classical ADMM, which (in its convergent form) updates two primal blocks in a Gauss–Seidel (sequential) fashion, PDMM updates multiple primal blocks in a Jacobi (parallel) manner. The central innovation is a “dual backward step” that compensates for the reduced information coupling inherent in Jacobi updates by effectively tempering the dual update step size. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates under standard convex assumptions.

A noteworthy contribution is the unification perspective: the supplementary material shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM. This helps situate a number of augmented Lagrangian–type decomposition methods within a single framework and clarifies their relationships.

On the empirical side, the paper reports favorable performance of PDMM relative to ADMM and several variants. These results are somewhat counterintuitive given the common expectation that Gauss–Seidel updates converge faster per iteration than Jacobi updates. The dual backward step appears to be the key mechanism enabling both convergence and good practical performance in the multi-block, parallel setting, offering an attractive option for distributed optimization.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem setting: block-separable convex optimization with linear constraints is central to large-scale and distributed learning.
- Conceptual novelty: the dual backward step is a simple, principled device to stabilize and accelerate Jacobi-style multi-block updates.
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1/T) convergence rates comparable to ADMM under general convex conditions.
- Unification of prior methods: showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases adds clarity to the literature.
- Practical promise: a parallelizable algorithm that empirically competes well with widely used ADMM variants.

Weaknesses and questions
- Intuition for performance: Please elaborate on why PDMM with Jacobi updates can match or surpass Gauss–Seidel-type methods in practice. Some possible angles:
  - In multi-block settings, Gauss–Seidel ADMM can be unstable or require restrictive conditions, whereas PDMM’s dual backward step provides stabilization.
  - Parallel updates reduce wall-clock time even if per-iteration progress is smaller.
  - The backward step may mitigate oscillations and improve primal–dual coupling; an illustrative toy example or eigenvalue/spectral discussion would help.
- Parameter selection: The paper would benefit from practical guidance on choosing the dual backward step size and other algorithmic parameters (e.g., penalty parameters), along with sensitivity analyses. How robust is performance to these choices?
- Assumptions and scope: Please state explicitly the assumptions needed for the convergence theorems (e.g., convexity/closedness, properties of the constraint matrix, existence of saddle points) and contrast them with those required by ADMM, sADMM, and PJADMM.
- Computational and communication complexity: Provide per-iteration costs and communication patterns for distributed settings. How does PDMM trade off communication with local computation relative to ADMM and the special cases it recovers?
- Empirical evaluation details: In addition to iteration counts, report wall-clock time on multi-core or distributed hardware to highlight parallel efficiency. Include problem scales, datasets, stopping criteria, and accuracy metrics. If feasible, add experiments on larger-scale problems to demonstrate scalability.
- Connections to randomness/asynchrony: If PDMM can be combined with randomized block updates or asynchronous execution, a brief discussion or experiment would strengthen the case for practical deployment.

Minor comments
- Use “Jacobi” rather than “Jacobian” when describing parallel coordinate/block updates.
- Clarify the relationship and parameter mappings when recovering sADMM and PJADMM from PDMM in the main text (not only in the supplement), possibly with a concise summary.
- A short, high-level proof sketch of why the dual backward step ensures descent/stability would aid readability.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution to multi-block, distributed optimization. The dual backward step is a simple yet powerful idea that both unifies several existing methods and enables parallel updates with convergence guarantees comparable to ADMM. With additional clarification on intuition, parameter selection, complexity, and empirical reporting (especially wall-clock scaling), the paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above points.
