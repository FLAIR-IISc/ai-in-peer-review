This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM, which can handle optimization with multiple blocks. In each iteration, the proposed Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) picks K random blocks, updates the block primal vector, and then updates the dual vector via a backward step. The authors illustrate the importance of using a backward step, as it makes the dual update more conservative, enabling global convergence. The algorithm also allows adding a proximal term to the primal update, making a part of optimization problems easier to solve.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and its iteration complexity. When there are totally J blocks, and when the PDMM algorithm randomly picks K blocks at one time, the convergence rate of the algorithm is O(1/T), where T is the number of iterations.

The main advantage of the PDMM algorithm is its ability to handle multiple blocks in parallel, allowing for full parallelization of the primal step. Additionally, it allows for relatively large update step sizes compared to other methods. In particular, PDMM can be faster than the state-of-the-art methods such as sADMM as it allows greater step sizes. It is more flexible than PJADMM and more parallelizable than GSADMM.

The authors have evaluated the algorithm on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso. The experimental results are quite promising, with the PDMM algorithm achieving the desired accuracy with less computation time. However, the authors haven't reported the performance of PDMM with parallel implementation. Comparing to traditional ADMM, it is less convenient to tune the three parameters of PDMM.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution to solving the ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are provided. The algorithm has the same convergence rate as ADMM, but it allows parallelization and it exhibits promising practical performance. The paper is well written, and both the theoretical and empirical parts are solid.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel parallel algorithm for solving ADMM-type problems with multiple blocks.
* The algorithm allows for full parallelization of the primal step and relatively large update step sizes.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and its iteration complexity.
* The experimental results are promising, with the PDMM algorithm achieving the desired accuracy with less computation time.

Weaknesses:

* The authors haven't reported the performance of PDMM with parallel implementation.
* Comparing to traditional ADMM, it is less convenient to tune the three parameters of PDMM.

Suggestions for improvement:

* The authors may consider reporting the performance of PDMM with parallel implementation to further demonstrate its efficiency.
* The authors may provide more guidance on how to tune the three parameters of PDMM to make it more convenient for practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient algorithm for solving ADMM-type problems with multiple blocks.
* The algorithm has the same convergence rate as ADMM, but it allows parallelization and it exhibits promising practical performance.
* The paper is well written, and both the theoretical and empirical parts are solid.

Arguments for rejection:

* The authors haven't reported the performance of PDMM with parallel implementation, which may limit its practicality.
