- Proposes PDMM: randomized parallel (Jacobi-style) multi-block updates with a “dual backward step” to stabilize dual updates under augmented Lagrangian.
- Allows exact or inexact primal updates via Bregman proximal terms and derives dual step sizes dependent on A’s sparsity and number of updated blocks K.
- Establishes global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic iteration complexity with constant step sizes under standard convex assumptions.
- Unifies and recovers sADMM and PJADMM as extreme cases, clarifying the trade-off between dual step size and primal update aggressiveness.
- Practical inexact variant enables closed-form updates for common losses/regularizers and handles overlapping group problems by exploiting low row-block degrees.
- Empirical results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso show PDMM is competitive or superior in iterations and runtime versus sADMM, RBSUMM, APG, and sometimes GSADMM.
- Conceptual strength: the dual backward step is simple, well-motivated, and transparently moderates implicit dual ascent.
- Theoretical strength: clean analysis that ties step sizes τi, νi to sparsity and K, with proofs (supplemented) of convergence and complexity.
- Practicality strength: parameter choices leverage problem structure (di=2 for overlaps) to allow larger dual steps and parallel updates.
- Novelty: combining dual backward step, sparsity-aware step sizes, and randomized parallel updates is original and insightful.
- Significance: advances scalable, distributed convex optimization for overlapping-structure problems with provable guarantees.
- Weakness: Counterintuitive claim that Jacobi PDMM outperforms GSADMM lacks deeper empirical ablation isolating the backward step vs. parallelism and per-iteration cost comparisons.
- Weakness: Insufficient practical guidance on choosing τi, νi, and η; no sensitivity analysis or rules of thumb for mis-specification.
- Weakness: Experiments are sequential Matlab runs; missing multi-core/distributed wall-clock speedups and discussion of communication/synchronization costs.
- Weakness: Potential experimental unfairness—public sADMM code may omit appropriate dual updates; tuning protocols for baselines are under-specified.
- Weakness: Some technical quantities in convergence proofs (Pt, Q, βi, γi) need clearer intuition and a concise algorithm box with recommended defaults would aid readability.
- Quality: Technical development appears sound and aligned with ADMM literature; supplement proof deferral is acceptable but high-level sketches would increase accessibility.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and intuitively explains backward step; a simple 2-block toy example would further clarify stabilization mechanism.
- Pros for acceptance: novel algorithmic idea, solid theoretical guarantees, unifying perspective, practical applicability, and strong empirical results.
- Cons for acceptance: limited evidence of parallel execution, need for parameter guidance and deeper GS vs. JS analysis, experimental fairness and missing sensitivity studies.
