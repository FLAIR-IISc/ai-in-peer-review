This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM, termed PADMM, designed to handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM randomly selects K blocks, updates the corresponding block’s primal variable, and then updates the dual variables via a backward substitution step. The authors demonstrate the importance of this backward step, highlighting that it leads to a more conservative dual update, thereby enabling global convergence. The algorithm also accommodates a proximal term in the primal update, simplifying the solution of certain optimization problems. A theoretical analysis is provided, establishing the algorithm’s global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, the convergence rate after T iterations is O(J/(TK)), where J is the total number of blocks and K is the number of blocks selected randomly in each iteration.

The primary advantages of PADMM are (1) its allowance for full parallelization of the primal update step and (2) its ability to utilize relatively large step sizes compared to other methods. Notably, PADMM can achieve faster performance than sADMM due to its larger permissible step sizes. It offers greater flexibility than PJADMM and superior parallelizability to GSADMM. The authors validate the algorithm’s effectiveness through experiments on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, reporting promising results. PADMM achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computation time. However, the authors do not present performance results with a parallel implementation, which would strongly support their claims. Furthermore, compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM introduces three parameters that require tuning, potentially adding to implementation complexity. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to solving ADMM-type problems with multiple constraints. The algorithm maintains the same convergence rate as standard ADMM while enabling parallelization and demonstrating promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation are sound.
