This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of ADMM to address optimization problems with multiple blocks. The core idea is a "Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers" (PDMM) that, at each iteration, randomly selects K blocks for primal variable updates and then performs a dual variable update incorporating a backward step. The authors motivate this backward step as crucial for conservative dual updates and ensuring global convergence. The method also incorporates a Bregman divergence to allow for flexible and potentially efficient primal updates. The paper provides theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, the authors show an O(1/T) convergence rate, where T is the number of iterations and K is the number of randomly selected blocks.

The primary strengths of PDMM lie in its potential for parallelization of the primal step and its ability to accommodate larger step sizes compared to other methods. The authors highlight that PDMM can outperform sADMM due to its larger step sizes and offer greater flexibility than PJADMM, while avoiding the sequential nature of GSADMM. The method’s performance is demonstrated empirically on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, with promising results suggesting improved computational efficiency.

The paper’s weaknesses are relatively minor. While the experiments demonstrate strong performance, they are conducted sequentially. Reporting results with a genuinely parallel implementation would further strengthen the claims of improved efficiency. Additionally, PDMM introduces three parameters (related to step size and the backward step) requiring tuning, which may be less convenient than standard ADMM's parameterization. Further discussion on practical guidelines for selecting these parameters would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to extending ADMM to multi-block problems, addressing limitations of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including proofs of global convergence and iteration complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on RPCA and overlapping group lasso demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM.
*   **Potential Impact:** The parallelizable nature of PDMM could be significant for large-scale applications.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The empirical evaluation is sequential, limiting the demonstration of PDMM’s core benefit—parallelism.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The increased number of parameters may add complexity for practitioners.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, specifically concerning ADMM-type methods for constrained problems. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and presents a promising algorithm with potential for practical impact. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding parallel implementation results and parameter tuning, the strengths of the work outweigh the drawbacks, and I recommend acceptance.
