This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. Unlike the classical two-block ADMM, PDMM updates multiple primal blocks in parallel (Jacobi-style) with randomized block selection. Its key technical ingredient is a “dual backward step” that tempers the aggressiveness of the dual update induced by the augmented Lagrangian, thereby stabilizing Jacobi updates. The method allows exact or inexact primal updates via Bregman proximal terms and derives step sizes for the dual update that explicitly depend on the sparsity pattern of A and the number of updated blocks K. The authors establish global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step sizes. They also show that two known methods, sADMM (two-block ADMM with splitting variables) and PJADMM (proximal Jacobi ADMM), arise as extreme cases of PDMM, offering a unified perspective. Experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso indicate favorable performance relative to ADMM variants.

Relation to prior work: Two-block ADMM is well understood and successful. Direct multi-block Gauss–Seidel ADMM can be effective but may diverge and lacks general iteration complexity guarantees. Jacobian (parallel) variants such as PJADMM add proximal terms to regain stability; RBSUMM randomizes single-block updates but requires local error bounds and decreasing step sizes and has no known complexity. PDMM generalizes these ideas by (i) randomizing the number of blocks updated in parallel, (ii) using a dual backward step to offset the implicit dual ascent introduced by the quadratic penalty, and (iii) choosing dual step sizes τi and backoff νi using the row-block degrees di and K. The paper also addresses overlapping blocks by introducing equality constraints and shows PDMM can update overlapping groups in parallel, where corresponding RBCD results remain open.

Strengths:
- Conceptual contribution: the dual backward step is a simple, clear mechanism to stabilize parallel block updates, with a transparent interpretation (moderating the implicit dual ascent). The explicit dependence of τi, νi on di and K is well motivated.
- Theoretical results: global convergence and O(1/T) ergodic complexity with constant step sizes under standard convex assumptions (closed, proper, convex f, existence of KKT point). The analysis cleanly recovers sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, clarifying when the dual should take small steps (exact, more aggressive primal progress) vs. large steps (inexact, proximal primal progress).
- Practicality: the inexact PDMM variant (via Bregman divergence) enables closed-form updates for common losses/regularizers. The method applies naturally to overlapping group problems by exploiting sparsity (di=2) to use larger τi.
- Empirics: On RPCA and overlapping group lasso, PDMM is competitive or superior in iteration count and runtime to sADMM, RBSUMM, and APG baselines; it can also outperform GSADMM in runtime despite GSADMM’s lower iteration count.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Jacobi vs. Gauss–Seidel performance: Results showing PDMM (Jacobi) competitive with or faster than GSADMM are counterintuitive, as GS updates typically converge faster. The paper attributes PDMM’s advantage to better reuse of computations and parallelism, but a deeper explanation would help. For example, can the authors provide empirical ablations that isolate the effect of the dual backward step vs. parallelization, and quantify the per-iteration cost differences?
- Practical guidance on τi, νi and η: While the theory specifies τi = K/(K̃i(2J−K)) and νi = 1 − 1/K̃i, more guidance is needed for practitioners. How sensitive is convergence to mis-specification? How should η (for inexact updates) be chosen to balance per-iteration cost and convergence speed? Including sensitivity plots and rules of thumb would strengthen the practical impact.
- Parallel efficiency: All experiments are run sequentially in Matlab. Since PDMM’s appeal is parallel randomized updates, a demonstration of wall-clock speedups on multi-core or distributed settings, and a discussion of communication/synchronization costs (computing Aixt+1), would be valuable.
- Experimental fairness and baselines: The sADMM code reportedly omits the dual update (τi=0), making it slower than PDMM; to ensure fairness, either implement the appropriate dual step (τi=1/J where applicable) or clearly justify why the public implementation is representative. Additionally, the “best” parameter choices for GSADMM and RBSUMM are mentioned; please detail the tuning protocol for all methods.
- Clarity and presentation: Several technical quantities (e.g., Pt, Q, βi, γi) that appear in the convergence statements are defined but their roles could be better explained in the main text (intuition for positivity and how they arise). A concise algorithm box (including the backward step, randomization strategy, and recommended default parameters) would aid readability. Also, standardize terminology (“Jacobi” vs. “Jacobian”).

Quality: The technical development appears sound and is aligned with established ADMM analyses; the convergence and complexity results are carefully stated. Much of the proof machinery is deferred to the supplement, which is reasonable, but adding some high-level proof sketches (e.g., how the backward step ensures descent in h(v*,vt)) would improve accessibility.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized. The intuition for the backward step is compelling; a simple 2-block toy example illustrating divergence of naive Jacobi and stabilization via νi would further clarify the mechanism.

Originality: The dual backward step combined with step-size rules that leverage sparsity and randomization is novel and insightful. The unification of sADMM and PJADMM as limiting cases is also valuable.

Significance: PDMM broadens the toolbox for large-scale, distributed convex optimization, especially for problems with overlapping structures. Given ADMM’s popularity, a principled, parallel multi-block variant with provable guarantees is important.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated algorithmic idea (dual backward step) with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Unifying view of existing ADMM variants.
- Practical applicability to overlapping group problems.
- Solid empirical results.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited parallel execution evidence; experiments are sequential.
- Need for clearer practical guidance on parameter selection and deeper analysis of when PDMM outperforms GSADMM.
- Some experimental fairness concerns (sADMM implementation) and missing sensitivity analyses.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. Addressing the questions above—particularly parallel efficiency, parameter guidance, and the Jacobi vs. Gauss–Seidel comparison—would further strengthen the paper.
