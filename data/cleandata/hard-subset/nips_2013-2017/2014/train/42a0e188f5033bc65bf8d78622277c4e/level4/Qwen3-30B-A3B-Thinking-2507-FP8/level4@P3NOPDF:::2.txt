

This paper introduces Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a novel algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. The key contribution lies in its Jacobian-style update scheme for primal variables across multiple blocks—unlike ADMM, which uses Gauss-Seidel updates limited to two blocks. The authors establish that PDMM achieves O(1/T) convergence rates, matching ADMM, despite its parallelizable structure. The critical innovation is the dual backward step, which compensates for limited information propagation inherent in Jacobian updates by adaptively reducing the dual step size. This mechanism enables convergence under general conditions without requiring the restrictive assumptions often needed in other parallel methods.

The paper effectively situates PDMM within the broader landscape of decomposition methods. Supplementary materials demonstrate that two prior approaches—sADMM and PJADMM—are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective that clarifies their relationships and highlights PDMM’s generality. This theoretical connection strengthens the paper’s contribution by showing how PDMM subsumes and extends existing work. The experimental results further validate the approach, showing PDMM outperforms ADMM and its variants in distributed optimization tasks, particularly in scenarios where parallelism is advantageous.

A notable aspect of the results is the counterintuitive finding that PDMM’s Jacobian updates (typically associated with slower convergence in standard methods) achieve comparable or superior performance to ADMM’s Gauss-Seidel updates. This observation warrants deeper discussion, as the conventional wisdom suggests Gauss-Seidel-type methods generally converge faster due to more frequent information exchange between blocks. The authors should elaborate on why the dual backward step overcomes this typical limitation, perhaps by analyzing the interplay between the step size adaptation and the convergence rate. For instance, does the dual backward step effectively mimic the information flow of Gauss-Seidel updates without sequential dependencies? Clarifying this would significantly enhance the paper’s impact, as it addresses a fundamental tension in distributed optimization literature.

The dual backward step appears to be the paper’s most original and crucial element. Unlike prior parallel methods that either sacrifice convergence guarantees or require restrictive conditions (e.g., strong convexity), PDMM’s approach ensures convergence under the same general assumptions as ADMM. This makes the method particularly valuable for real-world distributed systems where problem structure is complex and data is partitioned across multiple nodes. The paper’s theoretical foundation is sound, with clear proofs of convergence and well-motivated algorithm design. The supplementary materials adequately support the claims about the relationship to sADMM and PJADMM, though the main text could briefly reference this connection to avoid readers needing to consult supplements.

One minor concern is the experimental setup: while the results demonstrate PDMM’s effectiveness, the comparison should explicitly address scenarios where sequential ADMM is impractical (e.g., due to communication bottlenecks or large-scale parallel hardware). The paper notes that Jacobian methods are typically slower, but the experiments show PDMM excels in parallel settings—this contrast should be explicitly reconciled. For example, the authors might quantify the speedup from parallelism relative to the theoretical convergence rate, showing that PDMM’s O(1/T) rate translates to practical gains in wall-clock time when leveraging multiple processors.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to distributed optimization. PDMM provides a theoretically grounded, flexible framework that bridges the gap between sequential and parallel decomposition methods. The dual backward step is a clever technical insight that resolves a longstanding challenge in multi-block parallelization. The writing is generally clear, though the counterintuitive convergence result needs more thorough explanation to fully convince readers. If the authors address this point in revision—perhaps with a brief analysis of the step size dynamics or a small-scale example demonstrating the information flow—the paper would be significantly strengthened.

The work is well-motivated, technically rigorous, and experimentally supported. It offers practitioners a practical alternative for distributed convex optimization where parallelism is critical, without sacrificing convergence guarantees. Given its novelty, clarity, and relevance to NIPS audiences, this paper merits acceptance. The minor gap in explaining the convergence paradox is easily resolvable and does not undermine the core contribution. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for scalable machine learning and large-scale optimization.
