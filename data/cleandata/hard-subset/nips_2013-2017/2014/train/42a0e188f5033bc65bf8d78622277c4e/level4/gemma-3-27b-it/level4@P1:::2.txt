This paper proposes a parallel direction method of multipliers (PDMM) to minimize block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. Unlike ADMM, which typically updates primal variables in a Gauss-Seidel manner and has been largely limited to two blocks, this work proposes updating primal blocks in a Jacobian manner, extending this approach to multiple blocks. The central contribution is the introduction of a dual backward step, which addresses the potential for limited information propagation in Jacobian updates by effectively regulating the step size for dual variable updates. Similar to ADMM, the paper establishes O(1/T) type convergence results for PDMM.

The authors demonstrate (in the supplementary materials) that existing methods, sADMM and PJADMM, are special cases of the proposed PDMM framework, offering a unifying perspective on different decomposition methods based on the augmented Lagrangian. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PDMM compared to ADMM and its variants. The results are somewhat counterintuitive, as Jacobian-type methods often exhibit slower convergence than Gauss-Seidel approaches. Elaborating on this point would be beneficial. The dual backward step in PDMM appears novel and crucial for achieving convergence under general conditions comparable to ADMM. The proposed method provides more alternatives and possibilities for distributed optimization.

The paper begins with a clear motivation and relevant background, situating the work within the context of existing literature on ADMM and its extensions. The introduction effectively highlights the challenges of extending ADMM to multi-block problems and positions PDMM as a solution. The technical presentation is generally sound, and the notation is mostly consistent. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, although a deeper understanding would require careful examination of the supplementary materials. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The dual backward step is a key innovation and addresses a critical issue in extending Jacobian-type methods to multiple blocks.
*   **Generality:** PDMM unifies existing approaches like sADMM and PJADMM, providing a broader perspective on the field.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The established O(1/T) convergence rate provides a solid foundation for the method's reliability.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PDMM in relevant applications (RPCA and overlapping group lasso).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Counterintuitive Results:** The experimental findings, showing PDMM outperforming Gauss-Seidel-based ADMM, warrant further investigation and discussion. Providing more insight into why PDMM avoids the typical convergence issues of Jacobian methods would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Presentation:** While technically sound, the paper is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of key concepts, particularly the dual backward step and its impact on convergence.
*   **Limited Discussion of Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions parameter selection (τi, νi) but provides limited guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice for different problem instances.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of distributed optimization.
*   The proposed method is novel and provides a valuable extension to existing ADMM-based approaches.
*   The theoretical analysis and experimental results support the method's effectiveness.
*   The unification of sADMM and PJADMM under the PDMM framework provides a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The counterintuitive experimental results require further explanation.
*   The paper could benefit from improved clarity and a more intuitive presentation of the technical concepts.
*   The lack of detailed guidance on parameter tuning might limit the method’s practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. While the presentation could be improved for clarity, the novelty, theoretical guarantees, and experimental validation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above, particularly a more detailed discussion of the experimental results and parameter tuning.
