This paper addresses the problem of minimizing block-separable functions subject to linear constraints. The authors propose a variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), called PDMM, which is specifically designed to handle the multiple blocks case. In PDMM, K blocks of the primal variable are randomly selected for updates, and a backward step is used to refine the dual variable. This process can be implemented in parallel. The authors also establish a sublinear convergence rate for the proposed method, which generalizes several existing variants of ADMM.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. I am inclined to accept this paper, subject to the authors addressing the following questions and comments:

1. The algorithm description in equations (5) and (6) could be clarified. Specifically, it is unclear whether all coordinates of y^{t+1} and \hat{y}^{t+1} are updated, or only a single block "i". Based on the context, it appears that only a block of the dual variable "y" is updated, but the method for selecting "i" is not explicitly stated. This should be clarified in the revision.

2. In the proof of Theorem 2, it seems that "\sum_{i=1}^I" is missing from the expression.

3. The authors should provide more discussion on Theorem 2, which presents the convergence rate of the proposed PDMM method. Two natural questions arise: Firstly, can Theorem 2 be shown to be consistent with the convergence rates of existing ADMM variants by properly choosing parameters like K and J? Secondly, given that the transformation matrix A is split into multiple blocks, what is the optimal way to split it, according to the theorem?

4. I am curious to see a comparison with the tensor completion problem ("tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data, 2012"), which also exhibits a multiple block structure. Do the authors have any insights into which variant of ADMM is optimal for this problem?

Minor issues:

1. In equation (9), a space is missing after the "min" operator.

2. In line 217, there is an unnecessary space before the word "Section".

Overall, I am positive about accepting this paper, but I expect the authors to provide more discussion after Theorem 2 to address the above questions and comments.
