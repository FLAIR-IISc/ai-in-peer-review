Summary
The paper addresses minimizing block-separable (possibly nonsmooth) convex objectives subject to linear constraints. It proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized, parallel block-coordinate variant of ADMM for multi-block problems. At each iteration, PDMM updates K randomly selected primal blocks in parallel and performs a “backward” step on the dual variable to temper the implicit dual ascent induced by the augmented Lagrangian. The method sets dual stepsizes based on the sparsity pattern of A and the number of blocks updated, and can handle overlapping blocks via a reformulation. The authors establish global convergence and an O(1/T) ergodic rate with constant stepsizes, and show that sADMM and PJADMM arise as limiting cases of PDMM. Experiments on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso show favorable performance compared with ADMM variants and RBSUMM.

Relation to prior work
Two-block ADMM is well understood, but direct multi-block extensions can diverge; Gauss–Seidel-type methods (e.g., BSUMM) need stringent conditions, while Jacobian/proximal variants (e.g., PJADMM) add proximal terms. RBSUMM randomly updates a single block with weaker guarantees. PDMM contributes a principled, parallel randomized scheme for multiple blocks with a clean O(1/T) rate, and unifies sADMM and PJADMM through its parameterization.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and solid connection to important applications (RPCA, overlapping group lasso).
- A simple, implementable algorithm with parallel updates and principled dual “backward” step.
- Convergence analysis with constant stepsizes and explicit dependence on sparsity and K.
- Insightful unification showing sADMM and PJADMM as special cases, aiding interpretation of step-size choices.
- Strong empirical results versus relevant baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Algorithmic clarity (Eqs. (5)–(7)): Please clarify whether all dual blocks yi and ŷi are updated at each iteration or only a selected dual block. The text and equations suggest that all dual blocks are updated after the K primal blocks are updated, but this is not stated explicitly. If only a subset is updated, please specify how the dual block index i is chosen and how this interacts with the randomized choice of primal blocks.

2) Theorem 2 notation: In the first inequality bound, it appears that the summation ∑i=1^I is missing just after the opening brace “{” (consistent with the proof). Please correct the display.

3) Discussion around Theorem 2:
- Consistency with special cases: Since PDMM recovers sADMM and PJADMM under specific choices of K, τi, νi, it would help to state explicitly how Theorem 2 reduces to known convergence rates for those methods. For example, when K=J with exact primal solves (sADMM-like), and when large proximal terms allow τi=1 and νi=0 (PJADMM-like), do the bounds match the best-known O(1/T) (or o(1/T) under strong proximal conditions)?
- Splitting A: The rates depend on di (the number of nonzeros per row block) and K̃i. Can your bounds be used to guide an “optimal” or at least principled partitioning of A into row/column blocks to improve constants (e.g., balancing di, exploiting sparsity, or grouping to increase τi)? A brief guideline or heuristic would be valuable for practitioners.

4) Additional application: Tensor completion (e.g., “tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data,” 2012) also yields multi-block structures. Do your results suggest which variant (PDMM with what K, τi, νi; sADMM; PJADMM) is preferable in typical tensor settings (e.g., low Tucker/Tubal ranks)? Even a qualitative recommendation based on the aggressiveness of the dual step and the cost of primal updates would be helpful.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely and relevant problem with practical impact.
- Conceptually clean algorithm that is easy to parallelize.
- Solid theory with constant stepsizes and clear connections to prior ADMM variants.
- Strong empirical validation on representative tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Minor ambiguities in the algorithm description and theorem presentation.
- Limited discussion on how the theory informs block partitioning and parameter choices in practice.
- No tensor completion experiment despite relevance to multi-block settings.

Minor comments
- Eq. (9): Add a space after “min.”
- Line 217: Remove the extra space before “Section.”

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is well written, technically sound, and makes a useful contribution by unifying and extending ADMM variants for multi-block problems, with both theoretical and empirical support. Clarifying the dual update scope in the algorithm, fixing the missing summation in Theorem 2, and expanding the discussion on special-case consistency and block partitioning would further strengthen the submission.
