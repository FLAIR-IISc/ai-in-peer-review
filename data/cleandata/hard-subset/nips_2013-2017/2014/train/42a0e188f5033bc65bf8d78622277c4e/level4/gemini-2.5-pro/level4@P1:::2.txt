This paper proposes the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), an algorithm for minimizing block-separable convex functions subject to linear constraints. This problem class is of significant interest, but extending the highly successful two-block Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to the multi-block setting has proven challenging. While standard ADMM employs a sequential, Gauss-Seidel update scheme, this work explores a parallel, Jacobian-style update for the primal variables.

The core technical contribution is the introduction of a "dual backward step." This step is designed to compensate for the potentially slower information propagation inherent in parallel Jacobian updates by making the dual variable update more conservative, effectively reducing its step size. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate for PDMM, which is comparable to that of standard ADMM. Furthermore, the paper provides a valuable theoretical contribution by showing that two related methods, sADMM and PJADMM, can be viewed as special cases of the proposed PDMM framework. This unification provides a clearer understanding of the relationships between different augmented Lagrangian-based decomposition methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed dual backward step appears to be a novel and effective mechanism for ensuring the convergence of a multi-block, parallel ADMM variant. This addresses a well-known challenge in the field and provides a new, theoretically-grounded tool for distributed optimization.
*   **Theoretical Unification:** Demonstrating that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM is a strong contribution. It helps to unify a landscape of seemingly disparate methods and provides deeper insight into the role of the dual update.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso are convincing. The method is shown to be highly competitive with, and in some cases superior to, other ADMM variants, including sequential (Gauss-Seidel) approaches.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm's design for parallel and randomized block updates makes it well-suited for large-scale problems and modern computing architectures.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The empirical results are quite strong, and in some ways, counter-intuitive. Generally, Jacobian-style methods are expected to exhibit slower convergence than their Gauss-Seidel counterparts, as they utilize less recent information within each iteration. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of why PDMM performs so well empirically. While the dual backward step is key to ensuring convergence, an elaboration on why the method is not only convergent but also *fast* would be beneficial. Is the performance advantage in the experiments primarily due to the algorithm's suitability for parallel execution (even when run sequentially), or are there specific properties of the test problems that favor this update scheme? A brief discussion on this point would add valuable insight for practitioners.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to the literature on distributed and large-scale optimization. The proposed method is elegant, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the connection to previous work is insightful. The algorithm provides a valuable new alternative for solving multi-block constrained optimization problems.
