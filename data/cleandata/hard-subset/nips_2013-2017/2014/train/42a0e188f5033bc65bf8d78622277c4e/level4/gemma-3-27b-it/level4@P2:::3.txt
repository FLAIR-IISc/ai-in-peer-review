This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a randomized parallel algorithm for solving block-separable convex optimization problems with linear constraints. The core idea is to randomly select and update *K* blocks in parallel at each iteration, combined with a “backward step” on the dual update to promote conservative progress and ensure convergence. The authors highlight the benefits of this approach – enabling full parallelization of the primal update and allowing for potentially larger step sizes compared to existing methods. They establish global convergence and iteration complexity results, and demonstrate promising performance in experiments on robust principal component analysis (RPCA) and overlapping group lasso.

The paper effectively contextualizes PDMM within the existing landscape of ADMM variants. It clearly articulates the limitations of directly extending ADMM to multiple blocks and positions PDMM as a solution that addresses these challenges. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, accurately describing the strengths and weaknesses of methods like GSADMM, sADMM, and PJADMM, and showing how PDMM relates to and improves upon them.  The connection drawn between PDMM and these other methods – positioning sADMM and PJADMM as special cases – provides valuable insight. The inclusion of theoretical analysis, specifically the convergence guarantees and iteration complexity bound, strengthens the contribution.

A key strength of PDMM is its flexibility. The authors demonstrate how the algorithm can adapt to different problem structures, including overlapping group lasso, and how the parameters can be tuned to balance convergence speed and computational efficiency. The experimental results are well-presented and support the claims of improved performance compared to several baseline methods. 

However, the paper has some areas for improvement. While the experiments are compelling, they are conducted sequentially. A crucial test of PDMM’s value proposition – its parallelizability – is missing. Reporting performance with a parallel implementation would significantly bolster the paper’s impact. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge that PDMM has three parameters to tune, compared to the fewer parameters in traditional ADMM, they don’t offer extensive guidance on parameter selection.  A more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical tuning strategies would be helpful for potential users. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PDMM presents a novel approach to parallelizing ADMM for multi-block problems, offering a potentially significant improvement over existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence and iteration complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experimental results demonstrate promising performance on relevant applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical foundations.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** Thoroughly contextualizes PDMM within the existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Parallel Implementation Results:** The absence of parallel performance data weakens the argument for PDMM’s practical benefits.
*   **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** The increased number of parameters compared to traditional ADMM may pose a challenge for users.



**Overall assessment:** This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm addresses a significant challenge in scaling ADMM to multi-block problems. While the lack of parallel implementation results is a drawback, the theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and clear presentation make this a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the comments above, particularly regarding a discussion on parallel implementation considerations and more detailed parameter tuning advice.
