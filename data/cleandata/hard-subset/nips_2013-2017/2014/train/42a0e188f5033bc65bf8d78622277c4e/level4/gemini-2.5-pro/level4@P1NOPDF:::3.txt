This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), designed to handle optimization problems with a multi-block structure. In each iteration, the proposed algorithm, PADMM, randomly selects a subset of K blocks, updates the corresponding primal variables in parallel, and then updates the dual variables via a backward step. The authors illustrate the importance of this backward step, showing that it makes the dual update more conservative, which is crucial for ensuring the algorithm's global convergence. The framework also allows for the inclusion of a proximal term in the primal update, which can make the resulting subproblems easier to solve.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and deriving its iteration complexity. For a problem with a total of J blocks, where K blocks are randomly selected in each of T iterations, the convergence rate is shown to be O(J/(TK)).

The main advantages of PADMM are that it allows for full parallelization of the primal update step and permits the use of a larger step size compared to other methods. In particular, PADMM can be faster than stochastic ADMM (sADMM) because of its larger allowable step size. It is also presented as being more flexible than parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more amenable to parallelization than Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM).

The algorithm was evaluated on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, with promising experimental results. The experiments demonstrate that PADMM can achieve the desired accuracy in less computation time than the baseline methods. Nevertheless, a key limitation is that the authors did not report the performance of a true parallel implementation of PADMM. Thus, it is difficult to assess its practical speedup on parallel hardware. Additionally, compared to traditional ADMM, tuning the three parameters of PADMM may be less convenient.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution to the literature on solving ADMM-type problems, particularly those involving multiple constraints or blocks. The algorithm achieves the same convergence rate as standard ADMM while offering the significant advantages of parallelization and strong empirical performance. The paper is well-written, and both the theoretical analysis and the experimental validation are solid.
