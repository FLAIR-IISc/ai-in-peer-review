This paper introduces the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM) for minimizing block-separable convex objectives under linear constraints. Unlike ADMM, which updates primal variables in a Gauss–Seidel fashion and is typically restricted to two blocks, PDMM updates multiple primal blocks in parallel in a Jacobi-style manner. The central technical contribution is a dual backward step that compensates for the limited information propagation inherent in Jacobi updates by effectively shrinking the dual step size. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence guarantees for PDMM, analogous to classical ADMM results.

The supplementary material shows that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a useful unifying perspective on augmented Lagrangian–based decomposition methods and clarifying how these variants relate. Empirical results indicate that PDMM performs favorably compared with ADMM and several of its variants.

While the experiments are encouraging, the findings are somewhat counterintuitive: Jacobi-type updates often converge more slowly than Gauss–Seidel updates. It would strengthen the paper to explain in more depth why PDMM avoids this slowdown in practice—e.g., by analyzing the role of the dual backward step in mitigating stale or partial information, characterizing regimes (problem conditioning, block structure, sparsity patterns) where PDMM enjoys an advantage, and clarifying the trade-offs between per-iteration parallelism and iteration complexity.

Overall, the dual backward step appears to be a novel and important ingredient that enables convergence under conditions comparable to those required by ADMM, while supporting parallel multi-block updates. The method broadens the toolkit for distributed and parallel convex optimization and helps systematize prior ADMM variants within a common framework.
