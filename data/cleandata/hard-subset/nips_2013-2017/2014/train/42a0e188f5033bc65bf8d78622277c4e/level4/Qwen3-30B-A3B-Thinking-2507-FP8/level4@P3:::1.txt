

This paper proposes Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), a parallel randomized block coordinate variant of ADMM for solving optimization problems with block-separable convex objectives and linear constraints. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while two-block ADMM has been well-studied, generalizations to multiple blocks remain unclear. PDMM updates multiple blocks in parallel using a randomized block coordinate approach, incorporates a dual backward step to ensure convergence, and establishes global convergence with O(1/T) iteration complexity. The paper demonstrates that existing methods like sADMM and PJADMM are special cases of PDMM, providing a unifying perspective. The authors validate PDMM on robust PCA and overlapping group lasso, showing superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes several valuable contributions to the optimization literature. First, it provides a theoretically sound method for parallelizing ADMM with multiple blocks, addressing a key limitation in existing approaches. The dual backward step (Equations 6-7) is particularly insightful, resolving the convergence issues of direct Jacobi updates by making conservative dual progress. Second, the paper establishes a clear connection between PDMM and existing methods, demonstrating how sADMM and PJADMM emerge as special cases when specific parameters are chosen. Third, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-constructed proofs for global convergence and iteration complexity. Finally, the experimental results are convincing, showing PDMM's advantages in both convergence speed and runtime across two important applications.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and positions the work relative to prior research. The notation is consistent and well-motivated. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental section provides a thorough evaluation of the method's performance. The connections to related work (Section 2.2) are particularly strong and help readers understand the paper's contribution in the broader landscape of optimization methods.

Regarding strengths, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for a parallel ADMM variant with constant step size, which is not available for many existing methods. The method's ability to handle overlapping blocks (Section 2.3) is also noteworthy, as this is a challenging case for block coordinate methods. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages, particularly in the overlapping group lasso application where PDMM significantly outperforms alternatives.

I have a few minor suggestions for improvement. The description of the dual update (Equations 5-7) could be clarified to explicitly state that all dual blocks are updated simultaneously (not just a single block), as this was initially confusing. In Theorem 2, the expression should include the summation over all dual blocks (i.e., the missing "\sum_{i=1}^I" mentioned in the draft review). The authors might also consider expanding the discussion of Theorem 2 to explicitly connect the convergence rate to known results for ADMM variants, as this would further emphasize the paper's theoretical contribution. For instance, when K=J (updating all blocks), PDMM reduces to the method of multipliers with the same convergence rate as standard ADMM, while setting K=1 yields a randomized variant that converges at a rate comparable to RBSUMM but with a simpler step size selection.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS as it addresses a fundamental optimization problem with significant applications in machine learning. The method's ability to handle large-scale problems through parallelization aligns with the conference's interest in scalable machine learning. The theoretical results advance our understanding of ADMM variants, and the practical improvements demonstrated in experiments make a strong case for the method's significance.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by both rigorous theory and empirical results. I believe it meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. The minor clarifications suggested would further strengthen the paper, but they do not detract from its overall quality.

In summary, this paper offers a significant advancement in the development of parallel ADMM methods for block-separable optimization problems. The theoretical contributions are solid, the experimental validation is thorough, and the method has clear practical value. I recommend acceptance.
