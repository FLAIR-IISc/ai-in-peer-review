

This paper proposes a randomized parallel variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), named Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), designed to solve optimization problems with multiple linearly constrained blocks. The method randomly selects K blocks to update in parallel at each iteration, performing a primal update followed by a dual update with a backward step. The authors demonstrate that the dual backward step enables conservative dual updates, facilitating global convergence, and the algorithm supports proximal terms in the primal update to simplify certain optimization problems. Theoretical analysis establishes global convergence and O(1/T) iteration complexity with constant step size, where T denotes the number of iterations. The convergence rate depends on the number of blocks J and the number of blocks K updated per iteration, with the algorithm achieving O(J/(TK)) convergence behavior.

The primary strengths of PDMM include (1) full parallelization of the primal step, (2) larger allowable step sizes compared to existing methods, and (3) greater flexibility than proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and superior parallelization potential compared to Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors validate PDMM on two applications: robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, demonstrating significant computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods. Experimental results show PDMM achieves target accuracy with reduced computation time, particularly when updating multiple blocks (K > 1).

The paper has several noteworthy aspects: it establishes connections between PDMM and existing methods (sADMM and PJADMM), provides theoretical justification for the dual backward step, and addresses overlapping block optimization—a problem that has remained challenging for randomized block coordinate descent. However, the authors do not report performance metrics for parallel implementation, which is a key aspect of the proposed method. Additionally, PDMM requires tuning of three parameters (τi, νi, and ηjt), which may present a practical challenge compared to traditional ADMM.

Overall, this is a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization, extending ADMM to handle multiple blocks with parallelism while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The paper is well-written, with solid theoretical and empirical components. PDMM achieves the same convergence rate as ADMM but with the added benefits of parallelization and promising practical performance, making it a valuable addition to the optimization toolkit for large-scale machine learning problems.
