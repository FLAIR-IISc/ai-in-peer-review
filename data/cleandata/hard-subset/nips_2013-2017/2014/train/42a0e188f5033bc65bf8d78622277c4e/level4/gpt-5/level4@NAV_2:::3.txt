This paper introduces a randomized, parallel variant of ADMM for multi-block linearly constrained convex optimization. In each iteration, PDMM samples K blocks, updates the corresponding primal variables in parallel, and then performs a dual update that includes a backward (conservative) step. The authors motivate the backward step as a means to temper the implicit dual ascent introduced by the augmented Lagrangian, thereby enabling global convergence. The method also permits the addition of a proximal/Bregman term in the primal subproblems to ease computation. Theoretical results establish global convergence and an iteration complexity bound; specifically, with J total blocks and K blocks updated per iteration, the convergence rate scales as O(J/(K T)) after T iterations.

The chief benefits of PDMM are: (1) fully parallelizable primal updates, and (2) relatively larger effective dual step sizes than in several alternatives. In particular, by tailoring step sizes to the sparsity pattern of the constraint matrix and the number of updated blocks, PDMM can be faster than sADMM; it is also more flexible than PJADMM and admits more parallelism than GSADMM. Empirical evaluations on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso demonstrate promising performance, with PDMM reaching target accuracies in less wall-clock time. That said, the paper does not report results from an actual parallel implementation, and, compared with standard ADMM, PDMM introduces additional parameters whose tuning may be less straightforward.

Overall, this is a compelling contribution to ADMM-type methods for multi-block linear constraints. The algorithm attains the same O(1/T) convergence rate as ADMM while enabling parallel execution and exhibiting strong empirical behavior. The manuscript is clearly written, and both the theoretical analysis and experiments are solid.
