This paper addresses the multi-block convex optimization problem with linear constraints, a significant generalization of the well-studied two-block problem. The authors propose a new algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which is a randomized block-coordinate variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key ideas of PDMM are to randomly select a subset of `K` primal variable blocks for parallel updates at each iteration, and to introduce a "backward step" on the dual variable update to ensure convergence. The step sizes for the dual updates are intelligently adapted based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks being updated.

The authors situate their work effectively within the existing literature, discussing both Gauss-Seidel (cyclic) and Jacobian (parallel) extensions of ADMM. PDMM is presented as a unifying framework, showing that existing methods like the splitting-variable ADMM (sADMM) and Proximal Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) are special cases. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size, which is a notable result.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. I am in favor of its acceptance. The following comments are intended to help the authors further strengthen the manuscript.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and flexible algorithm that elegantly generalizes ADMM to the challenging multi-block setting. The ability to perform parallel, randomized updates makes it highly suitable for modern large-scale, distributed optimization. The theoretical unification of prior methods like sADMM and PJADMM is a valuable conceptual contribution.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results are strong, providing a sublinear O(1/T) convergence rate with a constant step size, which improves upon related methods that may require diminishing step sizes or lack complexity guarantees (e.g., RBSUMM). The experimental results on Robust PCA and Overlapping Group Lasso are compelling, demonstrating that PDMM outperforms several state-of-the-art methods in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow. The motivation for the dual backward step is clearly explained as a mechanism to temper the aggressive dual updates that can cause instability in parallel schemes.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the dual variable updates in equations (6) and (7) could be more precise. While the text states that "all dual blocks" are updated, the use of a single index `i` in the equations could be interpreted as an update for only one block. To avoid ambiguity and improve reproducibility, I suggest the authors clarify that the update is performed for all `i = 1, ..., I`.

2.  **Deeper Discussion of Theoretical Results:** The paper's main theoretical result, Theorem 2, is powerful, but its implications could be explored in greater detail.
    *   **Connection to Special Cases:** The paper establishes that PDMM generalizes several ADMM variants. It would significantly strengthen this claim to explicitly show how the general convergence rate in Theorem 2 specializes to the known rates for sADMM and PJADMM when the corresponding parameters (K, J, etc.) are chosen.
    *   **Practical Guidance from Theory:** The remark following Theorem 2 briefly touches upon the trade-off in choosing the number of blocks. It would be very useful if the authors could elaborate on whether the theorem provides any deeper insights into the optimal strategy for partitioning the constraint matrix A to achieve the fastest convergence.

3.  **Expanded Experimental Evaluation:** While the current experiments are strong, the paper could be made more impactful by demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on a wider range of applications. A promising candidate would be tensor completion (e.g., "Tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data," Liu et al., 2012), which naturally features a multi-block structure and is a common benchmark for this class of problems.

**Minor Corrections:**

*   In Theorem 2, please double-check the constant term inside the curly braces in the main bound. Based on the definitions in (20) and (21), it appears a summation over `i` might be missing from the `L̃ρ` term or that its definition should be reiterated for clarity.
*   In Equation (9), a space is missing between "min" and "xj".
*   On Line 217 of the submitted PDF, there appears to be an extra space before "Section". Please proofread for similar minor formatting issues.

In summary, this paper makes a solid and significant contribution to the literature on distributed and parallel optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is both theoretically well-founded and practically effective. I recommend acceptance, and I believe the paper will be even stronger if the authors can address the suggestions above, particularly regarding the discussion of their theoretical results.
