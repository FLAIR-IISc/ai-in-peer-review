This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), denoted as PADMM, which can efficiently handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, PADMM randomly selects K blocks, updates the corresponding block primal vector, and then updates the dual vector through a backward step. The authors highlight the importance of incorporating a backward step, as it renders the dual update more conservative, thereby enabling global convergence. Furthermore, the algorithm allows for the addition of a proximal term to the primal update, facilitating the solution of certain optimization problems. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, when dealing with J blocks and randomly selecting K blocks at a time, the convergence rate of PADMM is O(J/(TK)) after T iterations.

The primary advantages of the PADMM algorithm are twofold: (1) it enables full parallelization of the primal step, and (2) it allows for relatively large update step sizes compared to other methods. Notably, PADMM can be faster than the stochastic ADMM (sADMM) due to its ability to accommodate greater step sizes. Additionally, PADMM is more flexible than the parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more parallelizable than the Gaussian-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors have evaluated the algorithm on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso, yielding promising experimental results. The PADMM algorithm achieves the desired accuracy with reduced computation time. However, the authors have not reported the performance of PADMM with parallel implementation. Compared to traditional ADMM, PADMM is less convenient to tune due to the presence of three parameters.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are involved. Although PADMM has the same convergence rate as ADMM, it offers parallelization capabilities and exhibits promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, with both the theoretical and empirical components being solid.
