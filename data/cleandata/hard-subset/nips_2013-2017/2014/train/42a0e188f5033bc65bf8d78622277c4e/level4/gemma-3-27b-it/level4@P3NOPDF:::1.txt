## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission: [Paper Title - Replace with Actual Title]

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing block-separable functions subject to linear constraints. The authors propose a novel variant of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), termed PDMM (Proximal Dual Method with Many blocks), specifically designed to efficiently handle problems with a large number of blocks. PDMM achieves parallelization by randomly selecting a subset *K* of primal variable blocks for update in each iteration, followed by a backward step to refine the dual variable. The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating sublinear convergence, and establishes that PDMM generalizes several existing ADMM variants. Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper, and I recommend acceptance. 

My primary feedback focuses on clarifying algorithmic details and expanding the discussion surrounding the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement and proposed method are presented in a logical and accessible manner.
* **Novelty:** PDMM offers a promising approach to scaling ADMM to problems with numerous blocks, a common scenario in many applications. The random block selection strategy and subsequent dual update appear to be key innovations.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis, establishing a sublinear convergence rate.
* **Generality:** Demonstrating that PDMM generalizes existing ADMM variants adds to its value and broadens its applicability.

**Weaknesses & Questions:**

1. **Algorithm Clarity (Equations 5 & 6):** The description of the algorithm in equations (5) and (6) requires further clarification. Specifically, it is unclear whether the updates to *y*<sup>*t*+1</sup> and *ŷ*<sup>*t*+1</sup> involve all coordinates of the dual variable or only those corresponding to the selected block *i*. My understanding is that only a single block is updated at a time, but the paper does not explicitly state how block *i* is selected. Please clarify this selection process in the revision.

2. **Typographical Error (Theorem 2 Proof):** In the proof of Theorem 2, there appears to be a missing summation symbol. I believe the expression should read “∑<sub>i=1</sub><sup>I</sup> {…}” rather than just “{…}”.

3. **Discussion of Theorem 2 & Connections to Existing Results:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of Theorem 2, the central contribution of this work. 
    * Given that PDMM generalizes several ADMM variants, it would be valuable to demonstrate, through appropriate parameter choices (e.g., *K*, *J*), whether the convergence rate established in Theorem 2 is consistent with the known convergence rates of these existing methods. This would provide a more complete understanding of the theoretical properties of PDMM in relation to its predecessors.
    * Furthermore, the optimal strategy for splitting the transformation matrix *A* into blocks, based on the theoretical results presented, should be discussed. Are there specific block structures that lead to faster convergence or improved performance?  Guidance on this partitioning would greatly enhance the practical utility of the method.

4. **Comparison to Tensor Completion:** I am curious about potential performance comparisons on the tensor completion problem (as referenced, e.g., "tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data, 2012"). Tensor completion naturally exhibits a multi-block structure.  Do the authors have any insights into which variant of ADMM (including PDMM) would be expected to perform best on this type of problem, and why?  Experimental results, even limited, would be helpful here.

**Minor Comments:**

1. Equation (9) is missing a space after the "min" operator.
2. Line 217: Please remove the extra space before “Section”.



In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and potentially highly effective for solving large-scale block-separable optimization problems. Addressing the points raised above, particularly expanding the discussion following Theorem 2, will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I reiterate my positive assessment and recommend acceptance.
