This paper proposes a randomized parallel version of the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which can handle problems with multiple blocks. In each iteration, the proposed Parallel ADMM (PADMM) algorithm picks K random blocks, updates the block primal vector, and then updates the dual vector via a backward step. The authors highlight the importance of using a backward step, as it makes the dual update more conservative, enabling global convergence. The algorithm also allows adding a proximal term to the primal update, making a part of optimization problems easier to solve. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, establishing its global convergence and iteration complexity. Specifically, when there are J blocks in total, and the PADMM algorithm randomly selects K blocks at a time, the convergence rate of the algorithm is O(J/(TK)) after T iterations of updates.

The main advantages of the PADMM algorithm are twofold: (1) it allows full parallelization for the primal step, and (2) it permits relatively large update step sizes compared to other methods. Notably, PADMM can be faster than the stochastic ADMM (sADMM) as it allows greater step sizes. It is also more flexible than the Parallel Jacobian ADMM (PJADMM) and more parallelizable than the Gauss-Seidel ADMM (GSADMM). The authors evaluate the algorithm on robust principal component analysis and overlapping group lasso problems, and the experimental results are quite promising. The PADMM algorithm achieves the desired accuracy with less computational time. However, the authors have not reported the performance of PADMM with parallel implementation. Compared to traditional ADMM, it is less convenient to tune the three parameters of PADMM.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution to solving ADMM-type problems, where multiple constraints are present. The algorithm has the same convergence rate as ADMM but allows parallelization and exhibits promising practical performance. The paper is well-written, with both the theoretical and empirical parts being solid.
