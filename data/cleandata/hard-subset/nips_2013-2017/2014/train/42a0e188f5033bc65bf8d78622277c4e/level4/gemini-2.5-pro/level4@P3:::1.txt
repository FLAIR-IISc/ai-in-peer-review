**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a sum of block-separable convex functions subject to linear equality constraints, a common and important formulation in machine learning and statistics. The authors propose a novel algorithm, the Parallel Direction Method of Multipliers (PDMM), which extends the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to handle the challenging multi-block setting. The core ideas of PDMM are to randomly select a subset of primal variable blocks for parallel updates, while introducing a stabilizing "backward step" for the dual variable update. A key feature is that the step sizes are adaptively determined based on the sparsity of the constraint matrix and the number of blocks selected for update. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing global convergence and an O(1/T) iteration complexity rate with a constant step size. The authors also show that PDMM serves as a unifying framework, encompassing existing parallel ADMM variants like sADMM and PJADMM as special cases.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the important and notoriously difficult problem of developing convergent parallel extensions of ADMM for multi-block optimization. The proposed PDMM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated contribution. The combination of randomized parallel updates with a stabilizing dual backward step is an elegant solution to a problem where direct Jacobian-style updates are known to diverge.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The work is theoretically sound. The authors provide proofs for global convergence and derive an O(1/T) convergence rate in expectation under standard assumptions. Achieving this rate with a constant step size is a strong result for this class of algorithms.

3.  **Unifying Framework:** A major strength is that the paper successfully situates PDMM within the existing literature. By showing that sADMM and PJADMM are special cases, the authors provide the community with a deeper and more unified understanding of these methods.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm is designed for parallel implementation, which is critical for large-scale problems. The application of PDMM to solve overlapping group lasso in a randomized block coordinate fashion is particularly valuable, as this has been a challenging open problem for standard block coordinate descent methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, its impact could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the dual updates in equations (6) and (7) could be made more precise. Although the text clarifies that all dual blocks are updated, the equations themselves are indexed by a single `i`. To avoid any potential ambiguity, I suggest explicitly stating that the updates apply for all `i = 1, ..., I`.

2.  **Discussion of Theoretical Implications:** Theorem 2 presents the main convergence rate, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its consequences.
    *   **Connection to Special Cases:** Since PDMM generalizes other ADMM variants, it would be highly insightful to show how the rate in Theorem 2 recovers or relates to the known convergence rates of those methods when the parameters (e.g., K, J) are set appropriately. This would further strengthen the claim that PDMM is a unifying framework.
    *   **Guidance on Block Partitioning:** Remark 2 correctly identifies the trade-off between the number of blocks and the complexity of the subproblems. It would be very helpful for practitioners if the authors could discuss whether the theoretical rate from Theorem 2 offers any guidance on how to optimally partition the constraint matrix `A` to achieve the fastest practical convergence.

3.  **Broader Applicability:** The experiments on RPCA and overlapping group lasso are convincing. To highlight the method's potential even further, the authors could briefly discuss its applicability to other relevant multi-block problems. For example, tensor completion problems also feature this structure. A brief comment on how PDMM might perform in such settings would underscore the broad utility of the proposed method.

**Minor Corrections:**

*   In equation (9), consider using the standard operator formatting for `argmin` to improve readability.
*   On line 217 of the original manuscript, there appears to be an extra space before "Section".

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the optimization literature. The PDMM algorithm is an elegant, theoretically-sound, and practical solution for an important class of multi-block convex optimization problems. The paper's strengths in originality, theoretical quality, and its role in unifying existing methods are substantial. I am positive about this work and recommend **acceptance**. Addressing the suggestions for improving clarity and expanding the discussion would elevate this from a strong paper to an excellent one.
