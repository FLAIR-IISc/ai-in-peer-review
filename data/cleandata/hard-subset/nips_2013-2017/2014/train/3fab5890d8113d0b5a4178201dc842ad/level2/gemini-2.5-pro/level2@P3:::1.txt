This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling. The central result is the derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). This EPPF describes the marginal probability of a partition of grouped data after integrating out the infinite-dimensional beta process measure. Previously, the absence of such a marginal distribution for the BNBP necessitated inference schemes based on truncation or slice sampling. The derivation presented here enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based models.

The paper's method for deriving the EPPF is novel. It first introduces an auxiliary model where group sizes are treated as random variables, leading to an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that describes the joint distribution of group sizes and their partitions. The desired EPPF, which is conditional on the observed group sizes, is then obtained via Bayes' rule. From this theoretical foundation, the paper derives a simple and intuitive predictive rule for simulating partitions.

As a concrete application, the work develops a BNBP topic model. This model is presented as a compelling alternative to the widely used hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP). A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is derived for this model, and its performance is empirically evaluated against HDP-LDA on several standard text corpora. The experiments suggest that the BNBP topic model's sampler converges more rapidly and mixes better than a comparable HDP-LDA sampler. Furthermore, the results indicate that the BNBP model can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance, often with a more compact (i.e., fewer topics) representation of the data.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The mathematical derivations that lead from the BNBP definition to the ECPF, and subsequently to the EPPF and the predictive rule, are a complete and valuable piece of theoretical work. The claims are well-supported by the derivations presented. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard datasets, a well-established evaluation metric (held-out perplexity), and a strong, relevant baseline (HDP-LDA). The analysis of MCMC sampler convergence and mixing adds compelling evidence for the practical advantages of the proposed inference scheme.

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It effectively motivates the problem by highlighting a key limitation of existing BNBP models. The logical flow from the process definition to the final algorithm is easy to follow. A particular strength is the direct comparison of the predictive update equations for the BNBP topic model, LDA, and HDP-LDA (Equations 12-14). This comparison provides excellent intuition into the distinct shrinkage properties of the BNBP, showing a multiplicative interaction between a topic's global and local popularities, in contrast to the HDP's additive mechanism.

For potential improvement, while the main lemmas are stated clearly, their proofs are deferred to the appendix. The main body of the paper could be strengthened by including a brief, intuitive sketch of the proof for Lemma 3 (the EPPF), particularly explaining how the ECPF is marginalized to obtain the distribution of the group sizes and how this is used in Bayes' rule. This would make the paper's core technical innovation more self-contained.

**Originality**
The work is highly original. The derivation of an EPPF for a non-Dirichlet-based process in the context of mixed-membership modeling is a novel contribution. It expands the toolkit for nonparametric Bayesian modeling beyond the well-trodden ground of Dirichlet-process-based constructions. The approach of randomizing group sizes to facilitate the derivation is an elegant and potentially generalizable technique. The resulting BNBP topic model is distinct from existing models and provides a new perspective on controlling model complexity in topic modeling. The paper does an excellent job of situating this work relative to prior literature on the BNBP, HDP, and EPPFs.

**Significance**
The results presented are important and likely to be impactful. On a theoretical level, this work opens the door to deriving fully collapsed inference schemes for other integer-valued stochastic processes, potentially leading to a more unified framework for mixed-membership modeling, as suggested in the conclusion. On a practical level, the proposed BNBP topic model is a competitive and attractive alternative to HDP-LDA. The faster convergence, better mixing, and ability to find compact representations are highly desirable properties for practitioners. The simplicity of the final Gibbs sampling update equations makes the model accessible and straightforward to implement.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
While the paper is strong, the following suggestions could enhance it further:
1.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The evaluation is based entirely on quantitative metrics. Including a qualitative analysis, such as displaying the top words from topics discovered by both the BNBP model and the HDP-LDA, would provide valuable insight into the practical differences between the models. For example, does the stronger shrinkage of the BNBP lead to more coherent or interpretable topics?
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper thoroughly investigates the effect of the topic-word hyperparameter η. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion or analysis of the sensitivity to the hyperparameters specific to the BNBP prior, such as the concentration parameter `c`. This would give readers a more complete understanding of how to apply the model in practice.
3.  **Expanded Comparison:** The comparison to HDP-LDA is appropriate and well-executed. As a secondary point, a brief comparison to other contemporary nonparametric topic models (such as the discrete infinite logistic normal model cited in the paper) could provide a broader context for the performance results, although this is not essential.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and significant theoretical result: the EPPF for the BNBP for mixed-membership models.
    *   Introduces a new, fully collapsed inference scheme that was previously unavailable for this class of models.
    *   The resulting BNBP topic model is a practical and powerful alternative to the HDP, with empirical evidence showing faster convergence and competitive or superior performance.
    *   The work is clearly presented and well-situated within the existing literature.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The experimental evaluation lacks a qualitative component to help interpret the quantitative results.
    *   The sensitivity to some of the model's own hyperparameters is not fully explored.

These weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. They represent opportunities for refinement rather than fundamental flaws. Overall, this is an excellent paper that advances both the theory and practice of nonparametric Bayesian modeling.
