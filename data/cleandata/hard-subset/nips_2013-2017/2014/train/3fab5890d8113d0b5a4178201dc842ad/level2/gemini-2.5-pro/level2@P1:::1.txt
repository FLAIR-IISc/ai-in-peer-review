This paper addresses the problem of inference in mixed-membership models based on the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). A key challenge for such models has been the lack of a fully collapsed inference scheme, as the marginal distribution of the BNBP governing the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data was unknown. Previous approaches relied on truncating the underlying infinite-dimensional beta process. The main contribution of this work is the derivation of this marginal distribution, which the authors frame as a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF).

The authors achieve this result through a clever construction. They first define an auxiliary model where group sizes are random, and derive the joint distribution of these random group sizes and their partitions, termed an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF). By conditioning on the observed group sizes via Bayes' rule, they then arrive at the desired EPPF for the BNBP. While the EPPF itself has a complex form, it leads to a remarkably simple and intuitive predictive rule, which forms the basis for a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. As a concrete application, the authors develop a BNBP-based topic model and derive its sampler. This work builds upon and significantly extends previous research on the BNBP [10, 11], the general theory of EPPFs [5], and nonparametric topic models like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) [6].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF and the corresponding predictive rule for the BNBP is a major theoretical advance. It provides a principled and complete characterization of the BNBP's partitioning properties in the mixed-membership setting, analogous to how the Chinese restaurant process characterizes the Dirichlet process. This fills a notable gap in the literature on nonparametric Bayesian models based on integer-valued stochastic processes.

2.  **Practical and Efficient Inference:** The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant practical contribution. By marginalizing out the infinite-dimensional beta process, it avoids the approximation errors and implementation complexities of truncation-based methods. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that this sampler converges much more rapidly and exhibits better mixing properties than the widely-used direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA.

3.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the technical arguments are presented with great clarity. The step-by-step derivation from the ECPF to the EPPF is easy to follow. The comparison of the BNBP topic model's update equation (Eq. 12) with that of LDA and HDP-LDA is particularly insightful. The authors clearly articulate the key difference: the BNBP's multiplicative combination of a topic's global popularity and its group-specific popularity, in contrast to the HDP's additive combination. This provides a clear intuition for the BNBP's stronger shrinkage properties.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation on three standard text corpora is thorough. The results compellingly show the practical benefits of the proposed model and sampler. The BNBP topic model is shown to achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance, often providing a more compact representation (i.e., using fewer topics) to achieve a given level of held-out perplexity compared to the HDP-LDA. This suggests the model is less prone to overfitting and may be a preferable choice in many practical applications.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Implications of Group-Size Dependence:** The paper notes that the derived EPPF is "group-size dependent," which distinguishes it from classic EPPFs like the Chinese restaurant process. While this is an interesting and correctly identified property, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of its implications. For instance, how does this property affect the model's behavior on datasets with highly heterogeneous group sizes (e.g., a corpus with both very short and very long documents)? A brief discussion could provide further insight into the model's characteristics.

2.  **Complexity of the EPPF Form:** The final expression for the EPPF (Eq. 9) is quite complex, primarily due to the intractable normalizing constant in the denominator. The authors correctly point out that the simple predictive rule allows one to bypass direct computation of the EPPF for Gibbs sampling. However, it may be helpful to explicitly acknowledge this complexity and briefly comment on whether there is any deeper intuition to be gained from this particular mathematical form.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does a good job of placing priors on several hyperparameters (`r_j`, `gamma_0`). However, the sensitivity to the beta process concentration parameter `c` is not discussed. Was this parameter fixed in the experiments? If so, some commentary on how its value was chosen and how sensitive the model might be to this choice would be welcome.

**Summary:**

This is an excellent paper that makes both a strong theoretical contribution and a valuable practical one. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a novel and significant result. The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model is elegant, efficient, and empirically shown to be highly effective. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the work significantly advances the state of the art in nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for additional discussion that could further strengthen the paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.
