This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), addressing a key limitation in existing BNBP inference methods that require truncation. The authors demonstrate the utility of this EPPF by developing a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, showcasing its fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance.

The work builds upon the established framework of exchangeable random partitions and extends it to the domain of mixed-membership modeling with count data. Prior work in nonparametric Bayesian methods, particularly Dirichlet processes and NRMIs, is well-referenced. The authors correctly identify the gap in existing BNBP methods—the lack of a marginal distribution for exchangeable partitions—and effectively address this by deriving the EPPF. The connection to existing EPPF models and the Chinese Restaurant Franchise is appropriately noted. The paper also properly situates itself within the recent developments utilizing integer-valued stochastic processes for mixed-membership modeling (HDP, gamma-Poisson, GNBP), and clearly articulates how this work moves beyond those approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Deriving the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant contribution, addressing a long-standing limitation. This allows for fully collapsed Gibbs sampling, simplifying inference and avoiding truncation issues.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations, while complex, appear rigorous and are supported by lemmas and a clear presentation of the underlying principles. The use of the augmented beta-gamma-Poisson representation clarifies the connection to mixture modeling.
*   **Practicality:** The derived Gibbs sampler is shown to be computationally efficient and achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard topic modeling datasets. The comparison with HDP-LDA demonstrates the advantages of the BNBP approach. The detailed Gibbs sampling update equations in the appendix greatly enhance the paper's reproducibility.
*   **Clarity:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the key contributions.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, including convergence diagnostics, comparisons with a relevant baseline (HDP-LDA), and analysis of the effects of parameter settings. The convergence plots visually demonstrate the benefits of the BNBP sampler.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors explore a range of η values for the topic model, the sensitivity of the results to the parameters *c*, *a0*, *b0*, *e0*, and *f0* of the beta process and related distributions is not thoroughly investigated. It would be beneficial to include some analysis of how the results change with different values of these parameters.
*   **Complexity of the EPPF:** While the derivation of the EPPF is a major contribution, its explicit form (equation 9) is quite complex and may limit its direct applicability in some scenarios.  More discussion on computational strategies to handle this complexity beyond the Gibbs sampling approach would be valuable.
*   **Lack of Real-World Application:** The evaluation is limited to standard benchmark datasets. Demonstrating the effectiveness of the BNBP topic model on a more complex, real-world application would strengthen the paper's impact.
*   **Minor: Notation:** The notation can be a bit dense. While generally consistent, a table of notations could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a significant problem in nonparametric Bayesian modeling.
*   The derived EPPF and Gibbs sampler are practically useful and achieve state-of-the-art performance.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly evaluated.
*   The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in mixed-membership modeling and topic analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the EPPF and the limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity could hinder wider adoption of the method.
*   The lack of a real-world application limits the demonstration of the method's practical impact.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The novelty of the EPPF derivation, the practicality of the Gibbs sampler, and the empirical results all support acceptance. While the weaknesses noted above could be addressed with further work, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and potentially a discussion of strategies for simplifying the computational complexity of the EPPF.
