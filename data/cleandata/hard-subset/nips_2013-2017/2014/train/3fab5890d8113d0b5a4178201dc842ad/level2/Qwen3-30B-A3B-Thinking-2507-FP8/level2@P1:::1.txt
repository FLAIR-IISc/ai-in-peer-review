

This paper introduces a novel approach to mixed-membership modeling through the development of an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors address a significant gap in the literature by deriving the EPPF for BNBP, which governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, a problem previously unsolved for integer-valued stochastic processes like BNBP. This is a meaningful contribution to the theoretical foundation of nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling, where most existing work (like the hierarchical Dirichlet process) relies on alternative representations such as the Chinese restaurant franchise.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical novelty and practical implementation. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF using a group size dependent model is technically sound and represents a significant methodological advance. The authors provide a clear, step-by-step derivation of the EPPF, prediction rule, and fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, which is a major contribution given the absence of such tools for BNBP in previous literature. The BNBP topic model they construct is both intuitive and practically superior to existing approaches: it converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus compared to HDP-LDA. The experimental results on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS) convincingly demonstrate these advantages, particularly in terms of convergence speed and mixing properties.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical exposition, though the derivation of the EPPF could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the group size dependent approach is necessary. The comparison with HDP-LDA is particularly insightful, highlighting how the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism for topic number differs from HDP's additive mechanism, which explains the observed performance differences. The authors provide sufficient theoretical justification for their approach, connecting it to relevant prior work on EPPFs and integer-valued processes.

Regarding the four key evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The derivations of the EPPF and prediction rule are thorough, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly organized and well-written, though some sections (particularly the EPPF derivation) could be made more accessible to non-specialists through additional intuitive explanations.
- Originality: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by developing the EPPF for BNBP, a process that has been used in mixed-membership modeling but lacked a proper theoretical foundation for exchangeable partitions. The method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling via group size dependent models is a significant conceptual advance.
- Significance: The work addresses an important gap in the literature, and the resulting BNBP topic model offers practical advantages for real-world applications. The method could be extended to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening new avenues for research.

Minor suggestions for improvement: 
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the BNBP's EPPF is dependent on group sizes (as noted in Section 1.1), which is a departure from standard EPPF definitions.
2. While the experimental results are strong, including a comparison with other recent nonparametric topic models (e.g., the discrete infinite logistic normal distribution) would further strengthen the significance claim.
3. The paper mentions "a compact representation of the corpus" but doesn't quantitatively compare the number of topics inferred by BNBP versus HDP-LDA across different datasets; adding this would make the comparison even more compelling.

The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS. It makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods, provides a practical and implementable solution to a significant problem, and demonstrates strong empirical results. The work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers, including those on the beta process and related topics, and advances the state of the art in mixed-membership modeling. The authors have addressed a gap that has been present in the literature for some time, and their solution is both elegant and effective.

The paper's main limitation is its narrow focus on topic modeling, though the authors note that the BNBP framework could be applied to other mixed-membership problems. This limitation is understandable given the scope of the paper, but future work could explore broader applications to demonstrate the framework's versatility.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical advances are significant, the implementation is straightforward, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
