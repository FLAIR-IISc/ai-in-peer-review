Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops the marginal law underlying the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive (i) an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that jointly models random group sizes and their partitions, (ii) a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP by conditioning on observed group sizes, and (iii) a simple prediction rule enabling fully collapsed, truncation-free Gibbs sampling. They show that the implied random count matrix has a Poisson number of i.i.d. columns with sizes following a “digamma” distribution and within-column allocations following a Dirichlet–multinomial distribution. Using this foundation, they construct a fully collapsed BNBP topic model and compare it empirically to HDP-LDA.

Relative to prior work, this fills a notable gap: unlike the Dirichlet process (DP) and its CRP/HDP-CRF marginal representations, analogous marginal EPPFs for integer-valued processes used in mixed-membership settings (BNBP, GNBP, gamma–Poisson) were missing. Previous BNBP inferences relied on truncation or slice sampling; recent works characterize predictive structures for infinite random count matrices (e.g., negative binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet process) but not the grouped-partition EPPF needed for fully collapsed mixed-membership inference. The paper also clarifies how the BNBP mechanism differs from HDP: the BNBP combines global and local popularity multiplicatively in the assignment rule, in contrast to the additive structure in HDP.

Quality:
- Theory: The augmentation via beta–gamma–Poisson, the factorization in Lemma 1, and the finite-dimensional marginalization in Lemma 2 are standard and plausible; the construction of the ECPF and the conditioning to obtain the EPPF are carefully motivated. The count-matrix factorization into digamma + Dirichlet–multinomial is elegant and provides intuition. While the proofs are deferred, the derivations align with known conjugacy and CRM calculus. I encourage authors to highlight assumptions ensuring absolute convergence and measurability when interchanging sums/integrals over countably many atoms.
- Inference: The fully collapsed sampler analytically marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding simple updates and a clear prediction rule. Empirically, the sampler mixes quickly and reaches target topic counts more rapidly than the direct-assignment HDP-LDA baseline.
- Empirics: Experiments on three corpora with multiple η values evaluate convergence, mixing, perplexity vs. number of topics, and runtime. The comparisons are informative, especially the analysis of multiplicative vs. additive shrinkage effects. One concern is fairness: the HDP baseline uses a partially collapsed direct-assignment sampler; a comparison against a fully collapsed CRF sampler would strengthen claims about convergence and mixing. Sensitivity to hyperpriors for γ0, c, and rj is not deeply explored.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The separation between ECPF and EPPF and the conditioning argument is helpful. The prediction rule provides intuition. Some notation (e.g., reuse of “.” subscripts and the digamma distribution) could use a bit more intuition (e.g., brief properties of the digamma distribution and its connection to ψ). A short subsection contrasting sample-size dependent EPPFs with Pitman’s addition rule would help readers unfamiliar with [12].

Originality:
High. Providing a group-size dependent EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling is novel and addresses a long-standing gap relative to the DP/HDP literature. The collapsed sampler that integrates out the beta process is also a notable algorithmic contribution.

Significance:
High. The results unlock truncation-free collapsed inference for an important class of integer-valued processes, with immediate impact on topic modeling and potentially other grouped count models. The empirical findings suggest that BNBP can achieve similar or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA with fewer topics, offering compact representations and faster mixing. The methodology appears extensible to other processes (e.g., GNBP), which could broaden impact.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled derivation of ECPF, EPPF, and prediction rule for BNBP in grouped settings.
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free Gibbs sampler with simple updates and strong mixing.
- Insightful comparison to HDP-LDA, including the multiplicative vs. additive shrinkage interpretation.
- Empirical results across three datasets; code availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Baselines: Include a fully collapsed HDP (CRF) sampler for a fair convergence/mixing comparison; consider Pitman–Yor process topic models as additional baselines.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Report robustness to priors on γ0, c, and rj; provide posterior summaries of rj to substantiate claims about dispersion control.
- Scalability: Provide complexity analysis per token and memory usage; show wall-clock vs. perplexity curves and discuss sparse updates or alias methods actually implemented.
- Theory: Clarify conditions under which the group-size dependent EPPF preserves desirable consistency properties as the number of groups and/or group sizes grow; briefly discuss asymptotics of KJ and cluster sizes via ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c).
- Generality: The introduction claims a “general method” for deriving EPPFs from integer-valued processes; a sketch of requirements and an example beyond BNBP (even partial) would strengthen this claim.

Questions for authors:
1) How sensitive are results to the Gamma priors on γ0, c, and rj? Do you observe multimodality or strong correlations among these parameters?
2) Can you compare against a CRF-based collapsed HDP sampler in terms of both mixing and perplexity at matched topic counts?
3) Are there numerical issues when evaluating ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c) for small c or large r·? Any stabilization tricks?
4) Does the sample-size dependent EPPF admit a projective limit as group sizes vary? Any implications for posterior consistency?
5) Could you report the posterior distribution of KJ and n·k under different η to illustrate the shrinkage behavior more fully?

Reasons to accept:
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (EPPF and prediction rule) enabling fully collapsed inference for BNBP mixed-membership models.
- Practical algorithm with clear advantages in mixing and compactness; solid empirical evidence on multiple corpora.
- Relevance to NeurIPS audience in Bayesian nonparametrics and probabilistic modeling.

Reasons to reject:
- Fairness of empirical comparison could be improved (missing CRF baseline; limited sensitivity analyses).
- Some technical details and assumptions are deferred; readers may benefit from more explicit conditions and guidance on generalization to other processes.

Overall, this is a high-quality submission with substantial theoretical and practical contributions. With strengthened empirical baselines and minor clarifications, it would be a strong addition to the conference.
