

The paper introduces the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) as an integer-valued stochastic process for mixed-membership modeling, developing an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) that had not been previously established for this process. This EPPF enables a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, which the authors apply to topic modeling, creating a BNBP topic model that they claim offers advantages over existing methods. The work extends the theoretical foundation of exchangeable partitions from single-set mixture modeling to mixed-membership modeling, filling an important gap in the literature.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous mathematical derivations. The authors establish the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and derive the EPPF through a group size dependent model, providing detailed proofs in the appendix. The BNBP topic model's fully collapsed Gibbs sampler analytically marginalizes out the topics, topic weights, and infinite-dimensional beta process, resulting in closed-form update equations. The theoretical contributions are well-supported by empirical results on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS12), showing the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA with a more compact representation of the corpus.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature regarding the marginal distribution of integer-valued stochastic processes for mixed-membership modeling. While previous work has focused on single-set mixture modeling (e.g., Chinese restaurant process), this paper generalizes the framework to grouped data. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and fills an important theoretical gap, as the authors correctly note that prior work on BNBP inference required truncation due to the lack of a marginal distribution. The paper's general method for deriving EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling using integer-valued stochastic processes could potentially be extended to other processes like gamma-Poisson and gamma-negative binomial processes.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through practical applications in topic modeling, where the BNBP topic model outperforms HDP-LDA in convergence speed and mixing behavior while maintaining competitive predictive performance. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" is supported by empirical results, though a more comprehensive comparison with recent topic models beyond HDP-LDA would strengthen this claim. The BNBP topic model's multiplicative mechanism for controlling the number of topics differs fundamentally from HDP-LDA's additive mechanism, providing a new perspective on topic modeling.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from theoretical development to practical application. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity, especially regarding scalability to very large datasets. The authors mention sparse computation techniques for LDA could be adapted, but a more thorough analysis of computational requirements would be valuable.

A minor weakness is the limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity beyond the topic Dirichlet smoothing parameter η. While the paper shows how different η values affect performance, it doesn't thoroughly examine the sensitivity to other hyperparameters like c, r_j, or γ_0. A more systematic hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the experimental section.

The paper's contributions are sufficiently novel compared to recent NIPS proceedings. While there is related work on the beta-negative binomial process (e.g., Zhou et al. 2012, Heaukulani and Roy 2014), none of these provide the EPPF or fully collapsed inference for mixed-membership modeling. The paper also distinguishes itself from the Chinese restaurant franchise approach used in HDP by providing a direct derivation of the EPPF.

The BNBP topic model's practical advantages—simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and competitive predictive performance—make it a valuable addition to the nonparametric Bayesian topic modeling toolbox. The paper successfully demonstrates how the BNBP's theoretical properties translate into practical benefits for mixed-membership modeling.

The main recommendation for improvement is to provide more comprehensive experimental comparisons with recent topic models beyond HDP-LDA, particularly those with similar nonparametric approaches. A more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability would also strengthen the paper. The theoretical contributions are sound and significant, and the empirical results support the claims made. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling for mixed-membership problems.
