Summary
This paper develops the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) underlying the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data. The key idea is to first construct a group-size dependent model—randomizing the group sizes—and derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that jointly describes random group sizes and their partitions; then marginalize to obtain the distribution of the group-size vector and use Bayes’ rule to derive the EPPF conditional on observed group sizes. The authors further derive a simple prediction rule that enables a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. Applying this framework to topic modeling yields a new nonparametric Bayesian topic model distinct from HDP-LDA, with multiplicative global-local shrinkage, and a fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process. Experiments on three corpora show fast convergence/mixing and competitive or better held-out perplexity at comparable numbers of topics, often with a more compact representation.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-situated with respect to the DP/NRMI/EPPF literature [1–5], mixed-membership models (HDP) [6], and BNBP/GNBP/gamma–Poisson process connections [7–11]. It clarifies relationships to recent predictive structures for BNBP and related IBP variants [16,17], emphasizing the distinct goal of deriving an EPPF for grouped data with fixed row sums. It also connects to sample-size dependent EPPFs [12] and Poisson–Kingman samplers [13], noting BNBP is outside those classes.

Quality
- Theory: The ECPF (Lemma 2), matrix prior with Poisson number of columns and “digamma” column-size distribution (Eq. 8), EPPF (Lemma 3), and prediction rule (Lemma 4) together form a coherent story. The augmentation via gamma–Poisson (Section 2) is standard and the Poissonization step leading to factorization (Eq. 4) is sound. The group-size dependent EPPF is carefully justified as sample-size dependent (in line with [12]). While the EPPF in Eq. (9) is complex, the prediction rule is simple and practically useful.
- Inference: The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the topic model (Eq. 12) is clean and appears correct. The multiplicative combination of global and local popularity is an interesting and interpretable departure from HDP’s additive form.
- Empirics: The experiments assess convergence/mixing and predictive performance across three datasets and a grid of η. The evaluation protocol (predictive perplexity using posterior samples) is standard. Convergence plots substantiate the faster mixing claim.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally well organized. The motivation for randomizing group sizes to obtain an ECPF, then conditioning to get an EPPF, is clear and valuable.
- Notation is heavy; some readers would benefit from a brief “symbols at a glance” table and a more intuitive explanation of the “digamma distribution.”
- A few typos (e.g., “perdition rule”) and some incomplete references should be fixed.

Originality
- Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting and the associated prediction rule is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The method (ECPF → EPPF via Bayes) is of independent interest and potentially transferable to other count-based processes (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson).
- The fully collapsed sampler that integrates out the beta process and topic weights, with a distinct multiplicative shrinkage mechanism, is novel for topic models.

Significance
- The theoretical contribution advances our understanding of count-based nonparametric priors in mixed-membership contexts, complementing the well-known CRF for the HDP.
- Practically, the sampler is simple, efficient, and competitive in predictive performance while yielding compact topic representations. This could be impactful for large-scale topic modeling and other grouped data applications.

Pros
- Clear and principled derivation of the BNBP EPPF for grouped data, filling a gap vs. prior BNBP work (which relied on truncation/slicing).
- Simple prediction rule enabling a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
- Distinct and interpretable multiplicative global–local shrinkage.
- Strong empirical evidence of fast convergence/mixing; competitive perplexity with fewer topics.
- Potentially generalizable methodology (ECPF → EPPF) to other integer-valued processes.

Cons
- Experimental comparison uses the HDP direct-assignment (partially collapsed) sampler; a comparison to the standard collapsed CRF sampler would be fairer for both mixing and speed.
- η is swept but not inferred; results with learned η (and learned c) would strengthen conclusions.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for γ0 and c; the role of c in the Poisson mean for K and in the prediction rule could be empirically explored.
- The “digamma distribution” is unusual terminology; more intuition and properties (moments, tails) would help readability.
- Some clarity issues and minor typos; several references in the bibliography are incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a comparison to an HDP CRF-based collapsed sampler (or cite results/overheads) to more rigorously benchmark mixing speed and predictive performance.
- Infer η and c (with gamma priors) and report sensitivity; provide posterior summaries for rj to corroborate their role in overdispersion.
- Provide additional intuition for Eq. (10): e.g., show limiting behaviors as c → 0 or r· → ∞, and contrast to HDP’s additive term.
- Report runtime per-token or per-word normalized by number of topics to make speed comparisons robust to KJ.
- Expand discussion contrasting this EPPF to the combinatorial structures in [16,17] and clarify the conditions under which the two frameworks coincide or differ.
- Fix typos and complete references.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong theoretical and methodological contribution with practical impact. Despite some missing experimental baselines and minor clarity issues, the paper advances the state of the art in mixed-membership nonparametrics by providing the missing EPPF for the BNBP and a fully collapsed sampler with compelling performance. I recommend acceptance.
