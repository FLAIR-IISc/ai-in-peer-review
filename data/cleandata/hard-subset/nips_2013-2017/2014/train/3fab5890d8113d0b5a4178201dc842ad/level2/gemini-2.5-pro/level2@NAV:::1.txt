This paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to mixed-membership modeling by deriving the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). This foundational work enables the development of the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based models, overcoming the limitations of previous inference methods that relied on finite truncation or slice sampling. The authors apply this new framework to construct a BNBP topic model and compare it extensively against the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), a cornerstone of nonparametric topic modeling.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and compelling.
**Quality & Originality:** The primary contribution—the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and its simple, elegant prediction rule—is a novel and technically sound achievement. This fills an important gap in the literature and provides a unified, truncation-free inference scheme for this class of models. The resulting multiplicative update mechanism for topic assignment is a clear and interesting departure from the additive mechanism of the HDP, providing a distinct form of shrinkage.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It carefully builds the theoretical argument, starting from the BNBP definition and methodically deriving the ECPF, the random count matrix prior, and finally the EPPF and prediction rule. The comparison with LDA and HDP-LDA samplers in Section 3.1 is particularly insightful and clarifies the key differences.
**Significance:** The practical implications are demonstrated through strong empirical results on three standard corpora. The BNBP topic model shows markedly faster convergence and better mixing properties than the widely-used HDP-LDA sampler. Furthermore, it achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance, offering a more compact corpus representation (fewer topics) for a given level of perplexity, which is highly desirable in practice.

The paper has very few weaknesses. One minor point is that while the prediction rule is simple, the full EPPF expression is quite complex, which may obscure some intuition. However, the authors successfully mitigate this by focusing on the tractable sampling algorithm. The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is well-supported, though it could be slightly nuanced by emphasizing that the model excels at achieving low perplexity with compact representations, while HDP may find lower perplexity with a much larger number of topics in some hyperparameter regimes.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that combines deep theoretical insight with practical algorithmic advances and thorough empirical validation. It presents a complete and significant piece of work that researchers and practitioners in Bayesian nonparametrics and topic modeling are likely to build upon. I strongly recommend acceptance.
