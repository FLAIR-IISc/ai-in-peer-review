

This paper introduces the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) and develops an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling, where data is grouped and each group consists of exchangeable members. The authors derive a novel EPPF for the BNBP that governs the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, which enables fully collapsed Gibbs sampling for a new nonparametric Bayesian topic model. The paper claims this model offers better convergence, mixing, and predictive performance than existing methods like HDP-LDA.

The paper's main strengths lie in its technical contributions. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is a significant theoretical advance, filling an important gap in the literature where previous work on the BNBP had to truncate the number of atoms. The authors provide a clear and rigorous development of the ECPF and EPPF, with well-structured proofs and derivations. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler they develop is a major practical contribution, eliminating the need for tuning parameters like the number of topics K and the Dirichlet smoothing parameter α that are required in LDA and HDP-LDA. The empirical results demonstrate that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves competitive predictive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus than HDP-LDA under common experimental settings.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to readers. The figures (particularly Figures 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the behavior of the BNBP model and its advantages over HDP-LDA. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, properly situating their contribution within the broader context of nonparametric Bayesian methods and mixed-membership modeling.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The claim that the BNBP topic model achieves "state-of-the-art predictive performance" is somewhat overstated. As shown in Figure 3, the BNBP topic model generally has higher perplexity than HDP-LDA for moderate η values, though it uses fewer topics. The paper's conclusion that "the BNBP topic model's perplexity tends to be lower than that of the HDP-LDA if their inferred number of topics are comparable" requires stronger evidence, as the results are sensitive to the choice of η.

The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with the negative binomial Indian buffet process (IBP) [16] and the "ice cream" buffet process [17], which are closely related to the BNBP. The authors claim their approach is distinct from these, but don't clearly articulate the differences. The paper also doesn't sufficiently explain why the BNBP's EPPF derivation is novel compared to existing methods for deriving EPPFs.

While the paper states that "the BNBP topic model is a nonparametric Bayesian algorithm that removes the need to tune these parameters," it still requires tuning of hyperparameters (c, r, γ0), which is a minor but important clarification. The paper also doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the model performs when the group sizes are highly imbalanced, which could be an important practical consideration.

The experimental evaluation is generally solid, but the authors could strengthen their claims by including more datasets or comparing with additional recent nonparametric topic models. The paper's conclusion that "the method to derive the EPPF for the BNBP via a group size dependent model is unique" would be more convincing with a more detailed discussion of why this approach differs from existing methods.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling. The theoretical derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and significant, and the resulting topic model offers practical advantages over existing approaches. While the claims about predictive performance could be more precisely qualified, the paper presents a compelling advance in the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should clarify their claims about predictive performance and better distinguish their approach from related work on the negative binomial IBP.
