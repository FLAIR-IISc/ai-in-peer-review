Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling (grouped data) under the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The core contribution is to derive a sample-size/group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP by (i) introducing a group-size–randomized construction (ECPF) that marginalizes the beta process to yield a finite, tractable joint law for group sizes and partitions; (ii) conditioning back on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to obtain the EPPF on latent, column-exchangeable random count matrices with fixed row sums; and (iii) deriving a prediction rule enabling fully collapsed Gibbs sampling. Using this, the authors build a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding a simple sampler with closed-form updates. Experiments on three corpora compare to HDP-LDA (direct assignment sampler), showing faster convergence/mixing and competitive or better perplexity for comparable numbers of topics, with a more compact representation.

This work connects to and extends multiple strands: CRP/EPPF theory for mixtures (Pitman) to grouped settings; mixed-membership priors such as HDP; and integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP) previously used for counts but lacking explicit marginal laws over partitions of grouped data. The paper relates to recent work on the combinatorial structure of BNBP and negative-binomial IBP-like models for infinite count matrices, but focuses on the fixed-row-sum scenario essential for mixed-membership clustering.

Quality
- Theory: The augmentation to a beta–gamma–Poisson representation is standard and sound, and the derivation of the ECPF (eq. 6) by marginalizing Θ and B is carefully motivated. The induced random count-matrix prior (eq. 7) with Poisson number of columns, digamma-distributed column sums, and Dirichlet–multinomial row allocations is elegant and provides intuition. The EPPF (eq. 9) is admittedly complex but consistent with the logic of conditioning on fixed group sizes; the prediction rule (eq. 10) is simple, interpretable, and aligns with the prior factorization (global popularity times group-specific popularity).
- Correctness: The steps follow standard conjugacies; the “Digam” distribution is correctly attributed. The multiplicative form in the predictive rule is sensible. Full proofs are deferred to the appendix; given the structure, the results appear technically sound.
- Empirics: The topic-model instantiation is clear, and the collapsed sampler (eq. 12) is derived from the same machinery. Diagnostics (trace plots) convincingly show faster convergence/mixing than HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler. Perplexity comparisons across η values and for comparable topic counts are informative. Code is provided.

Caveats
- The HDP baseline uses the direct assignment sampler (partially collapsed). A fairer comparison would include the fully collapsed CRF sampler (albeit bookkeeping-heavy) or at least sampling α and the global base weights r̃, as fixing or weakly updating them can affect both convergence and perplexity. Please clarify whether α and γ0 in HDP were inferred and how.
- The EPPF is explicitly group-size dependent (sample-size dependent), which is nonstandard relative to Pitman’s EPPF definition, though the paper is up front about this and cites Zhou & Walker. This choice is appropriate for mixed-membership, but a brief discussion on implications for exchangeability (within-group vs. across groups) would improve clarity.
- Hyperparameters: The paper lists priors for rj and γ0 but is less explicit about c in experiments. Is c fixed or inferred? Sensitivity to c, γ0, and the rj prior would be useful (these parameters materially affect cluster proliferation).
- Baselines: Beyond HDP-LDA, comparisons to PYP-based topic models (which often yield better heavy-tailed cluster-size behavior) or to NB-process topic models with truncated/parallel block sampling would strengthen the empirical case.
- Evaluation: Perplexity is standard; reporting held-out likelihood under identical inferential budgets and including runtime vs. number of topics would bolster the practical claims.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized. The augmentation, ECPF→EPPF path, and the resulting predictive rule are explained clearly. The intuition behind the multiplicative popularity term is helpful. Some sections could benefit from more intuition (e.g., the role of the digamma distribution, and a brief schematic of the simulate–condition trick). Minor notation overload (e.g., n·k, r·) is manageable. Ensure the appendix provides full derivations and update equations for all hyperparameters used in experiments.

Originality
- Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP and a simple prediction rule for mixed-membership is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work on BNBP’s combinatorial structure and negative-binomial IBP-like models did not provide an EPPF tailored to fixed group sizes.
- The construction provides a potentially general recipe (via ECPF and conditioning) that could extend to other integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP), which is a meaningful conceptual advance.

Significance
- Theoretical: Provides missing marginal laws enabling fully collapsed inference for a widely used class of count processes in mixed-membership settings.
- Practical: The resulting BNBP topic model has a straightforward sampler with appealing convergence/mixing and a compact representation for comparable predictive power. This is attractive for practitioners seeking nonparametric models without the CRF complexity.
- Broader impact: The method could catalyze analogous EPPFs for other processes, impacting Bayesian nonparametrics beyond topic modeling.

Pros
- Clear, principled derivation of ECPF/EPPF and an intuitive prediction rule for BNBP in mixed-membership.
- Fully collapsed sampler; simple updates; code released.
- Strong convergence/mixing behavior; competitive perplexity with fewer topics.
- Conceptual generality to other integer-valued processes.

Cons
- Baseline limited to HDP-LDA direct assignment; lack of fully collapsed HDP comparison.
- Limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis; unclear treatment of c in experiments.
- EPPF is sample-size dependent (nonstandard), with limited discussion on implications.
- Empirical evaluation could include more baselines and runtime vs. performance trade-offs.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Did you sample α and γ0 (and r̃) for HDP-LDA? If not, please do or justify the choice; if yes, provide details and sensitivity.
- How is c handled in practice? Report sensitivity of K and perplexity to c and γ0.
- Can you add a brief comparison to a CRF-based HDP sampler or discuss expected differences?
- Any preliminary results on extending the approach to gamma–Poisson or GNBP?

Overall recommendation
A strong paper. The theoretical contribution fills a real gap, the sampler is practical and well justified, and the empirical evidence is solid though not exhaustive. With minor clarifications and, ideally, a more balanced HDP baseline, this would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
