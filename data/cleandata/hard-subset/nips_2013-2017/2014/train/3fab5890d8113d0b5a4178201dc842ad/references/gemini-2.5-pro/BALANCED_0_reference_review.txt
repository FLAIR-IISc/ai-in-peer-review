### Summary

This paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics for mixed-membership modeling. The authors focus on the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), an alternative to the popular hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP). While the BNBP has been previously proposed for such tasks, it lacked a fully collapsed inference scheme, hindering its practical application. The central contribution of this work is the derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP in the context of grouped data. The authors achieve this through a novel and elegant technique: they first randomize the group sizes to derive an intermediate quantity, the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), and then use Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes to arrive at the desired EPPF. Although the EPPF itself is complex, it leads to a simple, intuitive predictive rule. This rule enables the construction of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model. The authors provide a clear comparison to the HDP, highlighting the BNBP's multiplicative-style update rule, which results in different shrinkage properties. Empirically, they demonstrate on several text corpora that their collapsed sampler converges faster, mixes better, and can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact representation (fewer topics) compared to the HDP.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting is a novel and important result. It provides the missing combinatorial view of the BNBP, analogous to what the Chinese Restaurant Franchise provides for the HDP. This places the BNBP on a more solid theoretical footing for this class of problems and connects it to the rich literature on exchangeable partitions. The technique of using the ECPF as an intermediate step is clever and could potentially be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes, as noted by the authors.

2.  **Practical and Methodological Advance:** The direct outcome of the theoretical work is a simple and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. This is a major practical improvement over previous inference methods for BNBP models, such as finite truncation or slice sampling, which can be slower, more memory-intensive, or introduce approximation errors. The derived sampler makes the BNBP a much more accessible and viable tool for practitioners.

3.  **Clarity and Insightful Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical development is laid out logically, from the model definition to the ECPF, EPPF, and finally the predictive rule. The comparison between the BNBP topic model's update equation (Eq. 12) and that of the HDP-LDA (Eq. 14) is particularly insightful. The authors clearly explain the difference between the BNBP's multiplicative combination of global and local popularity and the HDP's additive combination, providing a clear intuition for their distinct shrinkage behaviors and empirical performance.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough and provides compelling evidence for the proposed method's advantages. The trace plots in Figure 2 clearly demonstrate the superior convergence speed and mixing of the BNBP sampler compared to the HDP-LDA's direct assignment sampler. The results on perplexity vs. the number of topics (Figure 3) convincingly support the claim that the BNBP can achieve competitive predictive power with a more compact model, which is often a desirable property in topic modeling. The experiments are conducted on standard datasets, and the methodology is sound.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion on Generalizability:** The authors suggest that their method of deriving the EPPF via an ECPF is a "general method" that could be applied to other integer-valued processes like the gamma-Poisson process. While this is an exciting possibility, the paper offers little discussion on the conditions or potential challenges of such a generalization. A brief discussion on what properties of the BNBP make this derivation possible, and whether they are shared by other processes, would strengthen this claim and provide a clearer roadmap for future work.

2.  **Sensitivity of Dispersion Parameters `rj`:** The paper correctly identifies the group-specific dispersion parameters `{rj}` as critical for controlling the model's behavior, as illustrated in Figure 1. In the experiments, these are inferred using a Gamma hyperprior. However, there is no analysis of the model's sensitivity to the choice of this hyperprior's parameters (`a0`, `b0`). Given the importance of `rj`, it would be valuable to understand how robust the model's performance is to this choice, or to compare the performance of inferring `rj` versus fixing them to some default value.

3.  **Context within Broader Nonparametric Models:** The comparison is focused almost exclusively on the HDP-LDA. While this is the most natural and important baseline, a brief discussion situating the BNBP topic model relative to other contemporary nonparametric approaches (e.g., models based on the Pitman-Yor process for power-law phenomena, or the discrete infinite logistic normal distribution mentioned in their own references) would have been beneficial for providing a more complete picture of the landscape of mixed-membership models.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   In Section 2.2, the generative story for the random count matrix (Eq. 8) involving a Poisson number of columns, each drawn from a "digamma distribution" and then split via a Dirichlet-Multinomial, is a beautiful and compact characterization. This is a key result and is very well presented.
*   The connection to infinite Poisson factor analysis is briefly mentioned in Section 3. This is an interesting link that could be slightly expanded upon to connect with another community of researchers.
*   The experimental finding that the BNBP topic model achieves better perplexity than the HDP-LDA at a comparable number of topics (right column of Figure 3) is a strong result. It suggests that the BNBP is not just more compact, but is learning "better" topics when the model complexity is constrained to be similar. This point could be emphasized more strongly.

### Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a complete piece of work, from novel theoretical derivations to a practical algorithm with strong empirical backing. It solves an important open problem by providing the first fully collapsed sampler for BNBP mixed-membership models. The work is of high quality, original, clearly presented, and significant for the Bayesian nonparametrics and topic modeling communities. The identified weaknesses are minor and mainly concern avenues for expanding the discussion and analysis, rather than fundamental flaws in the work itself. This paper is a clear contribution to the field and meets the high standards of NeurIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant theoretical derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP.
*   Development of a practically useful and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
*   Clear and insightful comparison with the HDP, clarifying the models' different statistical properties.
*   Convincing empirical results showing faster convergence and competitive performance with more compact models.
*   High clarity in writing and presentation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   None. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's core contributions.
