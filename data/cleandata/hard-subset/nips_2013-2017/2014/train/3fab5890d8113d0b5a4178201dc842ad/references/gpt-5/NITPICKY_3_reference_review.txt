Summary
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling via the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The main theoretical contribution is to derive, for grouped data with observed group sizes, a sample-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP by: (i) introducing a group-size dependent augmentation that randomizes group sizes, (ii) deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) over random sizes and partitions, and (iii) applying Bayes’ rule to obtain an EPPF over column-exchangeable random count matrices with fixed row sums. The authors further: (a) derive a prediction rule that permits collapsed Gibbs simulation of partitions, (b) develop a fully collapsed BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, and (c) present experiments on three corpora comparing to HDP-LDA, claiming faster mixing and competitive or better perplexities for a given number of topics.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: a principled EPPF-like treatment for mixed-membership models based on an integer-valued process (BNBP), with a clear augmentation that connects count and mixture modeling.
- The ECPF (Eq. 6), the random count matrix characterization (Eq. 7–8), and the prediction rule (Eq. 10) are interesting and potentially useful to the community. The Poisson number of columns with Digamma size and Dirichlet-multinomial splits is elegant.
- The fully collapsed sampler for the topic model is attractive; the multiplicative shrinkage term differs substantively from HDP’s additive shrinkage and is insightfully discussed.
- The work relates to prior lines on IBP, negative-binomial IBP, and Poisson factorization, and attempts to unify with partition/EPPF perspectives.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Possible error in the new-topic term in the collapsed sampler (Eq. 12). For a new topic, the Dirichlet predictive for a single token is η/(Vη)=1/V. Hence one expects P(new) ∝ (γ0/(c+r·)) rj × (1/V). The paper gives V·γ0/(c+r·)·rj, which appears off by a factor of V^2. Please clarify the derivation and whether the 1/V factor is accounted for elsewhere.
- Inference for c: c crucially enters all expressions (ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c), denominators in prediction rules). The paper gives no prior, update, or value for c in the main text. If c is fixed, how is it set? If inferred, provide the sampler. This omission undermines reproducibility and may affect comparisons.
- Perplexity estimator: The formula averages across MCMC samples in a nonstandard way (summing numerators and denominators across s before taking logs). The standard approach uses p(v|doc) ≈ (1/S) ∑s ∑k φvk(s) θjk(s)/∑k θjk(s). The presented normalization (summing over v,k,s in the denominator) is unusual and may bias results. Please justify or correct.
- Consistency and terminology for EPPF: You call it an EPPF but allow explicit dependence on m (and J), which deviates from Pitman’s addition rule. While sample-size dependent EPPFs exist, the paper should be more precise about exchangeability properties across groups and what kind of consistency (if any) holds when m changes.
- Notational inconsistencies and editing issues impede readability: K vs KJ; K −ji J vs KJ−ji; “perdition rule” for “prediction”; “disjoint disjoint”; “Poisson-Kigman” for “Poisson-Kingman”; an orphaned fragment “ψ(c+∑j rj)−ψ(c) , and (a) rj=…” inside Sec. 2.2; missing/garbled sentences. Several equations lack clear parameterizations (Gamma scale vs rate). These need careful cleanup.
- Some lemmas and key steps are deferred to the Appendix without sufficient guidance in the main text, e.g., proof ideas for Eq. (6), derivation of Eq. (9), and the construction linking (7–8) to Poisson–Digamma–DirMult columns. Given the centrality of these results, brief derivation outlines would help.

Relation to prior work and positioning:
- The connection to Heaukulani & Roy (combinatorial structure of BNBP) and Zhou et al. (random count matrices from NBP family) is acknowledged but could be sharper: spell out precisely how your EPPF differs from their predictive structures (IBP-like feature allocations vs column-exchangeable count matrices with fixed row sums), and which assumptions enable your fully collapsed sampler.
- Comparisons omit strong nonparametric topic baselines (e.g., HDP-LDA with fully collapsed CRF sampler, Pitman–Yor topic models, or modern inference variants). The chosen HDP baseline is the direct-assignment sampler, which is known to mix worse than CRF; this weakens empirical claims.

Experimental methodology:
- Only perplexity is reported; no uncertainty quantification across runs, no runtime-vs-K plots under identical K, and no investigation of sensitivity to c, γ0 priors, or rj priors. η is grid-searched but not inferred; this can confound fairness and interpretation.
- Claims of “state-of-the-art predictive performance” are not substantiated by breadth of baselines or statistical significance testing. For “compactness,” side-by-side comparisons at matched K are shown qualitatively but need more systematic quantitative support.

Minor issues and formatting:
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues), and some have typos. Ensure complete bibliographic details and correct spelling (“Kingman”).
- Define all distributions precisely with parameterization (Gamma shape/scale vs shape/rate). Verify consistency with NB(r,p) parameterization.
- Figures are referenced but not available; ensure captions are cleanly separated from equations.

Pros
- Conceptually interesting route from integer-valued processes to an EPPF-like object for grouped data with fixed row sums.
- Derivation yields a simple, usable prediction rule enabling fully collapsed sampling.
- Insightful comparison of multiplicative vs additive shrinkage mechanisms.

Cons
- Potential technical mistake in Eq. (12) (new-topic probability factor).
- Missing treatment of c; nonstandard perplexity computation.
- Numerous notational/formatting errors and uneven exposition.
- Empirical comparison is limited and may be unfair to HDP-LDA; overclaims on “state-of-the-art.”

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical direction is promising and could be impactful, but the current draft contains enough technical ambiguities (possibly an error in the new-topic term), missing inference details (c), and presentation/experimental shortcomings that I cannot recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to fix the derivations, strengthen the empirical evaluation (including a properly collapsed HDP-LDA baseline and standard perplexity computation), and thoroughly revise the writing and references.

Confidence: High.
