

# Peer Review: Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling

This paper addresses a significant gap in nonparametric Bayesian modeling by developing a group size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors derive the BNBP's EPPF by first randomizing group sizes, deriving the joint distribution of group sizes and partitions, and then using Bayes' rule to obtain the EPP governing the prior distribution of exchangeable random partitions. They further construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out not only topics and topic weights but also the infinite-dimensional beta process.

## Strengths

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a rigorous theoretical framework for deriving EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling, which has been lacking in the literature. The method to derive the BNBP's EPPF via a group size dependent model is novel and could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. Second, the BNBP topic model is well-motivated and demonstrates clear practical advantages over HDP-LDA: it converges faster (often in <100 iterations vs. >300 for HDP-LDA), mixes better, and achieves comparable or better predictive performance with a more compact topic representation. Third, the paper provides a clear prediction rule (Equation 10) that effectively illustrates how the BNBP controls topic assignments through a multiplicative combination of cluster popularity and group-specific dispersion parameters.

The empirical evaluation on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) is thorough, comparing the BNBP topic model with HDP-LDA under various settings of the Dirichlet smoothing parameter η. The results consistently show that the BNBP model achieves similar or better predictive performance with fewer topics, which is particularly valuable for practical applications where model interpretability matters.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical derivations are technically sound, the paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of the limitations of the BNBP approach. For instance, the paper claims the BNBP "governs a prior distribution for exchangeable random partitions of grouped data," but it doesn't clearly establish how this differs from other similar processes like the gamma-negative binomial process (GNBP) or whether the BNBP has superior theoretical properties (e.g., consistency, convergence rates).

The comparison with HDP-LDA is primarily empirical, and the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of why the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism (Equation 12) leads to better performance than HDP's additive mechanism (Equation 14). A more detailed theoretical comparison of the two models' asymptotic properties would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the BNBP sampler. While it mentions that the BNBP sampler runs in about 2.5 seconds per iteration on the NIPS12 corpus, it doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis or compare it to state-of-the-art LDA implementations that use sparse computation techniques.

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by providing the first EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using the BNBP. This fills a critical gap in the literature, as the authors rightly note that "there is hardly a unified treatment to mixed-membership modeling." The paper's method to derive EPPFs via group size dependent models could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, potentially opening new avenues for research.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through the BNBP topic model, which offers practical advantages over existing models. The paper shows that the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism for topic numbers provides a more effective way to prevent overfitting than the HDP's additive mechanism, which is particularly valuable when η is set to small values (common in practice).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The technical derivations are sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the BNBP topic model demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear figures and intuitive explanations of complex concepts.

The main limitations are the lack of deeper theoretical comparison with HDP-LDA and insufficient discussion of computational complexity. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's overall quality and significance.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, addressing an important gap in mixed-membership modeling with a novel theoretical framework and a practical, well-performing model. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates significant potential for future research and applications in topic modeling and beyond.

The paper's focus on mixed-membership modeling aligns well with NIPS's interest in Bayesian nonparametric methods and their applications. The theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication, and the empirical results provide compelling evidence of the method's practical value.
