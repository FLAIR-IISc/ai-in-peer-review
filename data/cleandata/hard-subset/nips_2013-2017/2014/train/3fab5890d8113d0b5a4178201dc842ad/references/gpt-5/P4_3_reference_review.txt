Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling via the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The core contribution is to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for grouped data and, from it, a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP. Because directly obtaining an EPPF is difficult, the authors first “Poissonize” group sizes to construct a tractable ECPF (Lemma 2), then use Bayes’ rule to condition on observed group sizes and arrive at the EPPF (Lemma 3). They also derive a simple prediction rule (Lemma 4) enabling simulation of exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. Building on these results, they develop a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding closed-form updates (Eq. 12 and Appendix).

The work is positioned relative to Dirichlet processes and NRMIs, EPPFs (e.g., Pitman [5]), HDP and the Chinese restaurant franchise [6], and integer-valued processes for counts and features (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP) [7–11]. It is particularly related to recent BNBP combinatorial characterizations and buffet processes [16,17] and Poisson factorization; however, those focus on infinite random count matrices, whereas this paper develops an EPPF for grouped data with fixed row sums (document lengths), enabling fully collapsed inference in mixed-membership models. The paper also connects to sample-size dependent EPPFs [12] and to column-i.i.d. constructions via digamma and Dirichlet-multinomial distributions [17].

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The augmentation Xj|Θj ∼ PP(Θj), Θj|B ∼ ΓP[rj, B/(1−B)], B ∼ BP(c,B0) and Poissonization of group sizes are standard and yield a factorized joint likelihood (Eq. 4). Marginalizing Θj leads to independent NB(rj, pk) counts per atom (Eq. 5), consistent with BNBP constructions. The ECPF (Eq. 6) and the random count matrix prior (Eq. 7) are coherent with the representation of columns as i.i.d. with sizes sampled from a “digamma” distribution and group allocations Dirichlet-multinomial (Eq. 8). Using Bayes’ rule to recover the EPPF given fixed group sizes is a standard device; the resulting EPPF (Eq. 9) is sample-size dependent, as discussed in [12].
- Prediction rule: The rule in Eq. 10 has an interpretable form combining global popularity n·k and local popularity njk with rj and hyperparameters γ0, c, r·. Its proportionality is consistent with the EPPF and the construction in Section 2.
- Inference: The topic-model sampler (Eq. 12) integrates out Φ, Θ, and B, and mirrors LDA/HDP collapsed updates with a distinctive multiplicative global-local term. Derivations appear consistent with the fully marginalized likelihood.
- Experiments: Perplexity on three corpora, sensitivity to η, and mixing diagnostics are reported. The results suggest faster convergence and comparable or better perplexity for a similar number of topics versus HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler, with more compact representations.

Potential concerns:
- The EPPF is sample-size dependent (does not satisfy Pitman’s addition rule), which is intentional but limits direct comparability with classical EPPFs; the paper cites [12], but clearer discussion of implications (e.g., projective properties) would help.
- Proofs are in the Appendix; some readers may want more sketch-level intuition in the main text.
- The “digamma distribution” is nonstandard terminology; while cited [20], a concise definition and properties in the main text would improve accessibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized: motivation, construction (ECPF→EPPF), prediction rule, and application to topic modeling. Notation is dense but mostly consistent. Suggestions:
- Move definitions of Digam and DirMult into the main text where first used and briefly explain tail behavior and moments.
- Provide a short algorithm box for the collapsed sampler and list all conditional updates (with references to Appendix).
- Fix minor typos (“perdition”→“prediction”), formatting glitches, and ensure figures/captions are clearly referenced.
- Clarify the normalization constants in Eq. 10 and any dependence on ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c), even if they cancel in Gibbs updates.

Originality
Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership modeling and leveraging it for a fully collapsed sampler appear novel. Prior BNBP works [10,11,16,17] and combinatorial analyses have not, to my knowledge, delivered an EPPF geared to grouped data with fixed row sums enabling fully collapsed mixed-membership inference. The multiplicative global-local shrinkage mechanism differs from HDP-LDA’s additive form and is a distinctive modeling contribution.

Significance
Conceptually, the paper advances a unifying EPPF-based treatment for mixed-membership modeling with integer-valued processes and provides practical inference benefits (faster mixing, compact topic usage). The methodology should transfer to other grouped count settings beyond topics. Empirically, the gains are convincing in mixing and, at matched topic counts, in perplexity. The general recipe (Poissonizing group sizes → ECPF → EPPF) could motivate analogous developments for gamma–Poisson or GNBP.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic construction from BNBP to ECPF and EPPF for grouped data.
- Simple, interpretable prediction rule enabling truncation-free, fully collapsed inference.
- Practical sampler with strong mixing and competitive perplexity at comparable topic counts.
- Bridges count modeling and mixed-membership via Poisson augmentation; relates to recent NIPS literature on BNP mixtures and factor models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical comparison uses HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler; adding the Chinese restaurant franchise collapsed sampler baseline (albeit with bookkeeping) would strengthen claims about mixing and perplexity.
- Limited ablations: impact of rj priors, sensitivity to c and γ0, and effect of fixing vs inferring rj.
- Theoretical properties of the sample-size dependent EPPF (e.g., exchangeability within groups vs across sample sizes) could be discussed more explicitly.
- Complexity analysis: provide big-O per-iteration cost and memory, and comments on scalability and sparse updates.
- Clarify relation and differences to Heaukulani & Roy (2014) on the combinatorial structure of BNBP beyond what is stated, to preempt perceived overlap.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution (ECPF/EPPF/prediction rule) enabling fully collapsed mixed-membership BNBP inference.
- Methodologically clean, with interpretable updates and fast, truncation-free sampling.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and compact modeling with competitive predictive performance.
- Likely to influence BNP mixed-membership modeling beyond topic models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical baselines could be stronger (no collapsed HDP-CRF comparison); limited ablations/sensitivity analyses.
- Dense presentation; some definitions and normalizations deferred to appendix may impede reproducibility without the supplement.
- Sample-size dependent EPPF may limit broader theoretical appeal compared to classical EPPFs.

Overall, the paper offers a technically sound and novel contribution with practical significance for BNP mixed-membership modeling. Expanding empirical comparisons and clarifying a few theoretical and presentational points would further strengthen it.
