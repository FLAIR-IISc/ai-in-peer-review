## Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a significant challenge in Bayesian nonparametrics: deriving a tractable framework for mixed-membership modeling using the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While the BNBP has shown promise as an alternative to the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) for such tasks, a key obstacle has been the lack of an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) – a crucial tool for collapsed inference. This work bridges that gap by deriving the BNBP's EPPF for grouped data, demonstrating its utility with a novel topic model and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.  The authors successfully generalize existing techniques from related works on EPPFs and demonstrate practical advantages, particularly in convergence speed and compactness of representation, through experiments on standard text corpora. 

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation in Bayesian nonparametrics, explicitly referencing and extending the work of Ferguson, Lijoi, and Prünster on normalized random measures.  It correctly positions itself within the broader context of EPPF models (Pitman, Teh et al.) and highlights the limitations of existing approaches like HDP for complex mixed-membership structures. The work acknowledges relevant recent developments in BNBP applications (Zhou et al., Heaukulani & Roy) and directly contrasts with methods like HDP-LDA (Teh et al.) and recent advances in collapsed inference for related models (Paisley et al.). The authors accurately situate their contribution as a unified framework that addresses a key missing piece in the BNBP toolkit.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for grouped data is a significant theoretical advancement. This unlocks the possibility of fully collapsed inference, a considerable advantage over truncation-based methods or those relying on approximate inference techniques.
* **Methodological Novelty:** The approach of randomizing group sizes to derive the EPPF is clever and demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying probabilistic principles.  The combination of this with existing techniques for deriving EPPFs is well executed.
* **Practical Implementation:** The development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model is a major strength. The closed-form updates are clearly presented, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The comparison against HDP-LDA on standard datasets is convincing.  The results consistently show faster convergence and, importantly, comparable or better predictive performance with a more compact model representation.
* **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the derivations are (mostly) clear and well-motivated. The authors also provide a clear description of the algorithm and parameter settings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Derivation:** While the overall approach is well-motivated, some of the derivations – particularly those involving the EPPF – are dense and challenging to follow. More intermediate steps and explanations could enhance clarity.  The justification for certain simplifications isn't always immediately apparent.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  While the results on JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS12 are informative, the experimental evaluation could be expanded.  Exploring the sensitivity of the model to different prior settings (e.g., `c` parameter in the beta process) and datasets would further strengthen the findings.  A comparison to other recent nonparametric topic models (e.g., those utilizing stick-breaking processes) would be valuable.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The authors mention the speed of the sampler but do not provide a detailed computational complexity analysis. A comparison to the HDP-LDA in terms of wall-clock time for comparable performance would be useful.
* **Dependency on Group Sizes:** The EPPF is dependent on group sizes. While acknowledged in the introduction, the implications of this dependency (e.g., the impact of highly variable group sizes) are not thoroughly explored.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics.
* The derived EPPF unlocks efficient inference for BNBP-based mixed-membership models.
* The practical demonstration through a topic model and Gibbs sampler is compelling.
* The empirical results are positive and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and addresses a challenging problem in a novel and rigorous manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the derivations may limit accessibility to a wider audience.
* The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive assessment.
* The potential impact of the group-size dependency on the EPPF deserves further discussion.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a significant achievement, and the accompanying topic model and Gibbs sampler provide a practical tool for researchers working on mixed-membership modeling.  While the paper could benefit from some clarifications in the derivations and a more extensive empirical evaluation, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS. The work is technically sound, original, and significant. Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above could further enhance the paper's impact.
