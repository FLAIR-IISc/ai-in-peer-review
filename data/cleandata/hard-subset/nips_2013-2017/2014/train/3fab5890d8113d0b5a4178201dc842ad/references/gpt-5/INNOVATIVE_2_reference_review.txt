Summary
The paper develops a group-size–dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling induced by the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The key idea is to first randomize group sizes and derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then condition on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to obtain an EPPF that governs exchangeable random partitions across groups. From this, the authors derive (i) a simple prediction rule to simulate partitions, (ii) a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes the beta process, topic weights, and topics, and (iii) closed-form updates. They show that the sampler mixes quickly and achieves competitive or better perplexity than HDP-LDA for a comparable number of topics, often with more compact representations.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against DP/NRMIs and their marginalizations (CRP/EPPFs), and against mixed-membership priors such as HDP, gamma–Poisson, BNBP, and GNBP. While the HDP has the Chinese restaurant franchise and rich collapsed samplers, previous BNBP/GNBP developments lacked marginal laws for grouped data partitions and thus lacked fully collapsed inference. The paper builds on recent BNBP-related combinatorial constructions (e.g., negative binomial IBP, “ice-cream buffet,” Poisson factorization) and clarifies how to obtain a grouped-data EPPF for BNBP by randomizing group sizes, yielding a prediction rule and a count-matrix view (digamma for column sizes, Dirichlet-multinomial across groups). The authors also clarify that their construction falls outside Poisson–Kingman families typically used for marginal samplers. Overall, the methodological bridge from integer-valued processes to group-size–dependent EPPFs for mixed-membership is novel and important.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual advance: A general recipe to obtain an EPPF for mixed-membership from an integer-valued process (via ECPF and Bayes) is elegant and appears broadly applicable. For BNBP specifically, the resulting prediction rule is simple and insightful.
- Inference contribution: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes the infinite beta process is nontrivial and practically valuable. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism (global popularity times local contribution) contrasts cleanly with HDP’s additive form and is intuitive.
- Technical depth: The paper provides nontrivial marginalization results (e.g., count-matrix prior with Poisson number of i.i.d. columns; digamma and Dirichlet-multinomial structure) and clear connections to Poisson factor models.
- Empirical behavior: Faster convergence/mixing than HDP-LDA and competitive or lower perplexity at comparable topic counts, suggesting a compact, strong-performing model.

Weaknesses
- Scope of experiments: Baselines are limited to HDP-LDA; comparisons to other BNBP/GNBP-based topic models (e.g., truncated/adaptive truncation samplers), Poisson factorization baselines, or recent sparse LDA samplers are not shown. Perplexity is the only metric; topic quality or interpretability is not assessed.
- Hyperparameter handling and sensitivity: The role and inference of c are under-specified; sensitivity of results to c, gamma0 priors, and rj priors would strengthen the empirical claims. The strong dependence on rj is discussed but not deeply explored empirically (e.g., posterior distributions, robustness).
- Theory presentation: Many proofs are deferred; while acceptable for space, some readers may struggle with the dense notation. The implications of sample-size dependence for exchangeability and coherence across m should be discussed more explicitly.
- Claims: “State-of-the-art” is strong given the narrow set of baselines and metrics. Also, computational complexity is reported qualitatively; a clearer per-iteration complexity and memory comparison would help.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent derivation path (ECPF → EPPF → prediction rule). The collapsed sampler is carefully constructed, and the count-matrix perspective is well justified. Some theoretical details and assumptions (e.g., conditions for exchangeability with sample-size dependence) could be made more explicit.

Clarity
Well-organized but dense. Notation could be lightened; a small running example illustrating ECPF/EPPF construction would help. The definition and use of the “digamma distribution” should be self-contained in the main text (parameters, support, intuition). Explicitly stating which parameters are inferred versus fixed (especially c) would improve readability.

Originality
High. The general method to obtain mixed-membership EPPFs from integer-valued processes, the specific BNBP EPPF and prediction rule, and the fully collapsed BNBP topic sampler appear to be new and useful contributions.

Significance
High for Bayesian nonparametrics and mixed-membership modeling. The method should extend to other negative binomial/gamma–Poisson processes and could catalyze new collapsed samplers and theoretical analyses.

Questions and suggestions
- How is c handled in experiments (fixed or inferred)? Please report sensitivity and posterior behavior if inferred.
- Provide a brief complexity analysis of the collapsed sampler versus HDP-LDA direct-assignment and CRF-based samplers.
- Show sensitivity to priors on rj and gamma0, and report posterior summaries of rj to substantiate their role.
- Add at least one additional baseline (e.g., Poisson factorization or a truncated BNBP/GNBP sampler) and a topic quality metric.
- Discuss whether the ECPF→EPPF recipe extends cleanly to GNBP or gamma–Poisson processes.

Pros
- Conceptually novel and broadly applicable derivation of mixed-membership EPPFs.
- Simple and useful prediction rule; fully collapsed sampler that mixes well.
- Competitive performance with compact models; clear contrast to HDP.

Cons
- Limited baselines and metrics; some hyperparameter handling unclear.
- Dense presentation; some proofs deferred and exchangeability nuances under-explained.

Recommendation
Accept. Strong conceptual novelty and a practical, collapsed sampler outweigh the fairly minor empirical and expository gaps.

Confidence
High (familiar with BNP for mixed-membership and count models).
