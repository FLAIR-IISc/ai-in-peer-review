### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian inference for mixed-membership models. The central problem addressed is the lack of a general framework for deriving fully collapsed inference schemes for many stochastic processes used in this context, a privilege largely reserved for the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) via its Chinese Restaurant Franchise representation. The authors focus on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) and derive, for the first time, its Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for grouped data.

The key technical innovation is a clever two-step method to arrive at this EPPF. Instead of a direct derivation, the authors first consider a model where the group sizes are random. For this model, they derive an Exchangeable *Cluster* Probability Function (ECPF) which describes the joint distribution of the random group sizes and their partitions. Subsequently, by applying Bayes' rule to condition on the observed (fixed) group sizes, they obtain the desired group-size dependent EPPF.

Building on this theoretical result, the paper develops a BNBP topic model and derives a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. This sampler is notable for its simplicity and efficiency, as it analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process, topic proportions, and topics. The authors provide a detailed comparison of their sampler's predictive rule to those of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) and the HDP, highlighting a distinct multiplicative mechanism for sharing statistical strength across groups, in contrast to the HDP's additive one. Finally, empirical results on three standard text corpora demonstrate that the BNBP topic model exhibits faster convergence and better MCMC mixing than the HDP-LDA, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance, particularly when seeking a compact topic representation.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses (Evaluation Criteria)**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations, while dense, appear to be sound and well-founded. The method of using the ECPF as an intermediate step to derive the EPPF is both novel and elegant. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical work and the experimental results. The experiments are thorough, using standard datasets, a strong and appropriate baseline (HDP-LDA), and a proper perplexity evaluation method. Crucially, the authors go beyond just reporting perplexity and include MCMC diagnostics (trace plots), which provide compelling evidence for their claims of faster convergence and better mixing. The work is a complete piece, taking a theoretical idea from its inception to a practical, high-performing algorithm with available code.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem by identifying the gap in the literature regarding collapsed samplers for mixed-membership models beyond the HDP. The distinction between EPPF and ECPF is explained well. The comparison of the final Gibbs sampling update equations (Eq. 12, 13, 14) is particularly insightful, providing a clear and intuitive understanding of the differences between the BNBP, LDA, and HDP models at the algorithmic level. The figures are informative and support the main conclusions of the experimental section. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the contributions and potentially reproduce the results.

#### **Originality**

The work is highly original. While the BNBP itself is not new, the derivation of its EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a novel and significant contribution. The technique of randomizing group sizes to derive the ECPF and then conditioning to get the EPPF appears to be a new methodological tool that could potentially be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes (as the authors suggest in their conclusion). This work clearly distinguishes itself from previous BNBP papers that relied on finite truncation or slice sampling for inference. It also differs from related work on infinite random count matrices by focusing specifically on the fixed-row-sum setting inherent to mixed-membership modeling. The resulting topic model and its multiplicative sharing mechanism offer a genuinely new alternative to the well-established HDP.

#### **Significance**

The results of this paper are significant for several reasons.
1.  **Theoretical Advance:** It provides a key missing piece for the BNBP, placing it on more equal footing with the Dirichlet Process in terms of inferential machinery. The proposed derivation technique may open the door to developing collapsed samplers for a wider class of models.
2.  **Practical Algorithm:** It delivers a practical and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a nonparametric topic model. The reported improvements in convergence speed and mixing over the widely used HDP-LDA are of great interest to practitioners.
3.  **New Modeling Insights:** The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism provides a different modeling bias than the HDP. The results suggest that the BNBP is particularly effective at finding compact representations, achieving strong predictive power with fewer topics. This is a valuable property, as interpretability often suffers when models infer an excessively large number of topics. This work advances the state-of-the-art and provides a compelling alternative to the HDP for mixed-membership modeling tasks.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership context is a fundamental and new result.
*   **New Inferential Machinery:** The paper provides the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, a significant practical advance over previous approximate inference methods.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model demonstrates clear advantages in MCMC convergence and mixing, and highly competitive (often superior) predictive performance compared to the standard HDP-LDA baseline, especially for compact models.
*   **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and lucidly explaining the solution and its implications. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by others in the Bayesian nonparametrics community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical section is dense and may be challenging for readers not already expert in this specific subfield. However, this is inherent to the topic, and the authors have done a good job of providing intuition where possible.
*   The experimental comparison is limited to the HDP-LDA. While this is the most logical and important baseline, including others could have further contextualized the performance. This is a minor point, as the HDP is the dominant model in this space.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a strong combination of novel theory, a practical new algorithm, and compelling empirical results. It solves a well-defined and important open problem in Bayesian nonparametrics. The work is of high quality, original, clearly presented, and significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
