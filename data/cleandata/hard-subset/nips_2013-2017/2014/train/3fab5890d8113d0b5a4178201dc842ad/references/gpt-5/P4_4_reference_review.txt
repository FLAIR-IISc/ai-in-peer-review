Summary
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling with the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The central contribution is to derive the prior law governing exchangeable random partitions of grouped data for the BNBP, which previously lacked an EPPF analog. The authors proceed by (i) introducing a group-size dependent construction that treats group sizes as random via a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, yielding an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) after marginalizing the beta process; (ii) converting the ECPF to an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) conditional on observed group sizes using Bayes’ rule; and (iii) deriving a simple prediction rule that enables a truncation-free collapsed Gibbs sampler.

They further instantiate these results in a BNBP topic model. The sampler marginalizes the infinite-dimensional beta process, per-topic word distributions, and topic weights, yielding a fully collapsed update that combines global and local topic popularity multiplicatively. Experiments on three corpora compare against HDP-LDA: the proposed sampler mixes faster, reaches its stationary distribution quicker, and achieves comparable or lower perplexity at a smaller inferred number of topics, yielding a more compact representation.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on NRMI-based mixture modeling [2,3], EPPFs and exchangeable partitions [5,12], and mixed-membership priors including HDP [6] and integer-valued processes such as gamma–Poisson and BNBP/GNBP [7–11]. Prior work on BNBP focused on feature allocation analogs (IBP variants) or Poisson factorization representations [10,11,16,17], but lacked a group-size dependent EPPF for partitions in mixed-membership settings. The paper also relates to marginal samplers for σ-stable Poisson–Kingman models [13], which do not encompass the BNBP, and to sample-size dependent EPPFs [12]. The derived column-i.i.d. random count matrix representation with Digam and DirMult components follows [17] but is adapted to the conditional-on-group-size setting.

Quality
Technical development appears sound. The augmentation Xj|Θj ∼ PP(Θj), Θj|B ∼ ΓP[rj, B/(1−B)], B ∼ BP(c,B0) is standard; the factorization in Lemma 1 (via marginalizing Θj) yields independent NB draws njk as expected. The ECPF (Eq. 6) correctly integrates out the beta process and composes gamma functions consistent with beta–gamma conjugacy; the induced prior over count matrices (Eq. 7) and the Poisson number of columns with Digam/DirMult column law (Eq. 8) are consistent with random count matrix constructions in [17]. The EPPF (Eq. 9) is intricate but is not needed for sampling due to the predictive rule (Eq. 10), which is simple and interpretable: a product of a global popularity term and a group-specific term, plus a new-cluster probability controlled by γ0, c, r·, and rj.

For the topic model, the fully collapsed update (Eq. 12) follows from integrating out φ and Θ using DirMult and gamma–Poisson conjugacy. The evaluation protocol (held-out perplexity on 50% splits, multiple η values, multiple corpora, averaging over MCMC samples) is standard. Claims about faster mixing and compactness are supported by qualitative trace-plot descriptions; quantitative diagnostics (e.g., ESS or autocorrelation) would strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized: it moves from augmentation to ECPF, to EPPF, to prediction rule, and then to the topic model and experiments. Notation is heavy and occasionally terse (e.g., Digam distribution definition and properties are brief; the role and inference of c receive limited discussion). Proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is acceptable if complete. The description of the random count matrix representation and the connection to DirMult/Digam could be made more intuitive. An algorithm summary of the collapsed sampler would aid reproducibility. Code availability is noted.

Originality
Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling is novel. Prior BNBP works did not provide an EPPF for grouped partitions or a fully collapsed sampler marginalizing the beta process in a topic model. The general method—randomize group sizes, derive ECPF, then condition to obtain an EPPF—appears broadly applicable to other integer-valued processes.

Significance
Methodologically, providing an EPPF and efficient fully collapsed inference for BNBP can impact mixed-membership modeling beyond topic models, enabling nonparametric priors with distinct overdispersion and shrinkage properties compared to HDP. Empirically, the sampler’s faster mixing and compact topic usage are valuable in practice. The empirical scope is limited to perplexity and comparisons mainly with HDP-LDA; broader comparisons (e.g., Poisson factorization with NB processes, NGGP-based models) and additional metrics (topic coherence, wall-clock vs accuracy) would strengthen significance. Nonetheless, the theoretical contribution is substantial and likely to be built upon.

Strengths
- Clear, principled derivation of an ECPF and EPPF for BNBP with grouped, fixed-size data.
- Simple, interpretable prediction rule enabling truncation-free fully collapsed sampling.
- Fully collapsed BNBP topic model that marginalizes the beta process, topics, and weights.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence/mixing and comparable or better perplexity at fewer topics.
- Methodology plausibly generalizes to other integer-valued processes.

Weaknesses
- EPPF is sample-size dependent; implications versus standard addition-rule EPPFs could be discussed more explicitly.
- Limited empirical baselines beyond HDP-LDA; no comparisons to other NB-based Poisson factorization or NGGP/CRMs.
- Quantitative mixing diagnostics and runtime-perplexity trade-offs are limited; complexity analysis is brief.
- Sensitivity analyses for c, γ0, and hyperpriors (a0, b0, e0, f0) are not presented.
- Some notation and distributional definitions (e.g., Digam) may be unfamiliar and are only briefly explained.

Questions/requests for clarification
- How are c and γ0 inferred in practice in the collapsed sampler? Are there closed-form updates or auxiliary sampling steps? Any sensitivity observations?
- Can the authors provide quantitative mixing statistics (e.g., effective sample size, autocorrelation) and wall-clock comparisons?
- How does the method compare against Poisson factorization with NB processes under comparable inference (e.g., collapsed/augmented) and against NGGP/HFPY-based topic models?
- Are there conditions ensuring that the EPPF construction (via conditioning on m) preserves exchangeability within groups and across clusters?
- Is the general ECPF→EPPF recipe applicable to GNBP and gamma–Poisson processes? Any obstacles anticipated?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid theoretical contribution filling a known gap for BNBP in mixed-membership models.
- Practical, fully collapsed sampler with demonstrated advantages in mixing and compactness.
- Clear connections to and differentiation from HDP-based inference.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation could be broader and more quantitative on mixing and runtime.
- Some clarity and exposition issues, particularly around distributional components and hyperparameter handling.
- Limited discussion on generalization to other processes, despite being a stated motivation.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution with practical implications for nonparametric mixed-membership modeling.
