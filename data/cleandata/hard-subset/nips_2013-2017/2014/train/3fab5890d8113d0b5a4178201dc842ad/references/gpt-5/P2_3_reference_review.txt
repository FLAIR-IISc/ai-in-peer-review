Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a group-size–dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in mixed-membership modeling. The key idea is to (i) start from the BNBP’s beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, (ii) “Poissonize” the within-group assignments to obtain a fully factorized likelihood, (iii) derive the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) over random group sizes and partitions by marginalizing out the beta process, and then (iv) condition on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to obtain an EPPF for the column-exchangeable random count matrix of grouped data. From this, the authors derive a simple prediction rule and construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that integrates out topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process. Empirically, the sampler mixes faster than HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler and achieves comparable or better perplexity at similar numbers of topics, often using fewer topics (i.e., a more compact representation).

This work sits at the intersection of Bayesian nonparametrics and mixed-membership modeling. Prior work has rich EPPF theory for single-population mixture models (e.g., CRP and Pitman’s EPPFs), but less for multi-group mixed-membership models. The HDP offers the Chinese restaurant franchise (CRF) as an alternative marginalized representation, but a general EPPF framework for grouped data has been lacking. For BNBP, previous works used truncation or slice sampling and did not provide the marginal partition distributions for grouped data; related processes (e.g., negative-binomial IBP, “ice-cream” buffet) concern infinite random count matrices rather than conditioning on observed group sizes. The paper builds on sample-size–dependent EPPFs and extends them to the grouped case, providing an explicit BNBP ECPF/EPPF and a prediction rule.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations use standard and appropriate tools: the gamma-Poisson augmentation to factorize counts; marginalization of gamma/beta-process components; and conditioning on observed group sizes to get an EPPF. The ECPF/EPPF expressions are intricate but coherent, and the prediction rule is intuitive and easy to implement. The column-wise construction (Poisson number of columns; digamma-distributed cluster sizes; Dirichlet-multinomial allocation across groups) provides a clear generative view and a check on the formulas.
- Claims vs. support: The theoretical claims (existence of an EPPF for BNBP in the grouped setting, prediction rule) are supported by explicit formulas (with proofs relegated to the appendix). The algorithmic claim (fully collapsed Gibbs sampler) is borne out, and the empirical claims (faster mixing and compactness) are consistent with the reported trace plots and perplexity comparisons.
- Empirics: Experiments on three corpora compare to HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler. The study examines sensitivity to η and relates topic count vs. perplexity. While the setup is standard and reasonable, baselines are limited (no CRF-based collapsed HDP comparator, nor other Poisson factorization variants), and evaluation focuses on perplexity. Nonetheless, the results are consistent with the theoretical differences in shrinkage behavior.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but mostly clear for an expert reader. The stepwise path (ECPF → matrix prior → EPPF → prediction rule) is logically organized. The intuitive explanation of the prediction rule (global popularity term times group-specific term; new-cluster probability depending on γ0, c, r·, rj) helps.
- Areas to improve: define the “digamma distribution” more prominently (it is nonstandard nomenclature; include intuition and parameter regimes); add a concise pseudo-code for the sampler; briefly summarize the key steps from the appendix in the main text; clarify the implications of sample-size–dependent EPPFs on projectivity and coherence across datasets of different sizes.

Originality
- The main contribution—deriving a group-size–dependent EPPF for the BNBP and a corresponding prediction rule for mixed-membership modeling—is novel and fills a gap between integer-valued processes and partition-based inference in the grouped setting. The fully collapsed BNBP topic model that integrates out the beta process is also novel relative to earlier BNBP implementations that relied on truncation or slice sampling. The contrast with HDP-LDA’s additive shrinkage vs. BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage is insightful.

Significance
- The EPPF/ECPF framework for BNBP in grouped data provides a principled foundation for collapsed inference beyond CRF-style constructions, and should be of interest to researchers in Bayesian nonparametrics and practitioners building mixed-membership models with count data. The prediction rule and fully collapsed sampler are simple and practical. While the empirical section is modest, the methodological contribution is likely to be reused (e.g., to other negative-binomial–based processes or Poisson factorization models).

Pros
- Clear theoretical advance: an EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership settings, plus an intuitive prediction rule.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that is easy to implement, fast-mixing, and competitive in perplexity with fewer topics.
- Sound derivations leveraging beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation; interpretable global/local popularity terms.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparisons: no CRF-based collapsed HDP sampler or other strong baselines from Poisson factorization literature; evaluation limited to perplexity.
- Heavy reliance on appendix for proofs; some notation and distributions (e.g., “digamma”) could be clarified.
- The generality claim (method extends to other integer-valued processes) is plausible but not demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the normalization and properties of the “digamma distribution” used for n·k; a short intuition and tail behavior would help.
- Discuss projectivity: how does the sample-size–dependent EPPF behave under adding/removing observations/groups? Any implications for predictive use across corpora of differing sizes?
- Provide a brief pseudo-code of the collapsed sampler and complexity per token; compare to sparsity-exploiting LDA implementations.
- Consider adding a CRF-based HDP collapsed sampler as a baseline, or at least discuss practical trade-offs; optionally include a Poisson factorization baseline.
- Analyze sensitivity to priors on rj, γ0, and c; rj’s role is emphasized, so a short ablation would be informative.

Reproducibility
- Code availability is noted. The model and updates are explicit; with the appendix, an expert should be able to reproduce results.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper with a substantial and well-justified theoretical contribution and a practical algorithm that appears to work well. Despite limited baselines and dense presentation, the work meets NeurIPS standards for quality, originality, and significance, and is sufficiently clear for the target audience. I recommend acceptance (leaning accept) provided the authors address clarity and, if possible, strengthen the empirical comparisons.
