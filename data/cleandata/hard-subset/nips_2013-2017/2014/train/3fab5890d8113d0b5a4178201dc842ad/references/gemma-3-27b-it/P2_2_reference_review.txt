## Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of defining a unified framework for mixed-membership modeling, extending beyond the single-dataset focus of traditional nonparametric Bayesian mixture models. Specifically, it focuses on deriving the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) when applied to grouped data.  The authors achieve this by first randomizing group sizes and then using Bayes' rule to obtain the desired EPPF.  They further develop a prediction rule for the BNBP, construct a topic model based on it, and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on standard corpora, showing competitive performance with a compact representation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of nonparametric Bayesian methods, including the Dirichlet Process, Normalized Random Measures with Independent Increments (NRMIs), and the Chinese Restaurant Process.  It acknowledges the limitations of existing methods like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) in providing collapsed inference for processes like the BNBP and GNBP. The work explicitly connects to recent advances in deriving predictive structures for the BNBP (specifically referencing [16, 17]) but differentiates itself by focusing on the EPPF for *grouped* data, rather than infinite random count matrices. The comparison to HDP-LDA ([6, 23]) in the experimental section places the contribution within the current state-of-the-art in topic modeling.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** Deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting is a significant theoretical advancement. It provides a unified framework for understanding and performing inference with the BNBP in this context. The method of first randomizing group sizes is a clever approach.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The derived prediction rule and the resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model are valuable practical contributions.  Collapsed Gibbs sampling offers computational efficiency, a key advantage for nonparametric Bayesian models.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on standard corpora demonstrate that the BNBP topic model achieves competitive performance (perplexity) compared to the HDP-LDA, while potentially offering a more compact representation. The analysis of convergence and mixing properties is also a strength.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The paper generally does a good job of explaining complex concepts, and provides relevant background information.  The connections between the Beta Process, Negative Binomial Process, and their application to mixed-membership modeling are well-articulated.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The detailed comparison with HDP-LDA, highlighting the differences in shrinkage mechanisms and computational properties, is a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The EPPF derived is quite complex, making it difficult to grasp its full implications without carefully studying the equations. While mathematically sound, its practical use beyond the Gibbs sampler might be limited.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  While the authors highlight the benefit of removing the need to tune the number of topics (like in LDA), the performance *does* seem sensitive to the choice of η (as shown in Figure 3), which acts as a prior on the topics.  More discussion on how to choose an appropriate η would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experimental evaluation focuses solely on topic modeling.  While this is a good demonstration, it would be beneficial to showcase the BNBP’s applicability to other mixed-membership modeling tasks (e.g., clustering, community detection).
* **Minor Notation Issues:** There are a few instances where the notation is slightly unclear or inconsistent, requiring careful reading to understand.  For example, the meaning of ‘KJ’ shifts context slightly.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important open problem in nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting is a significant theoretical contribution, and the resulting Gibbs sampler provides a practical tool for inference. The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate competitive performance. While the complexity of the EPPF and the sensitivity to hyperparameters are drawbacks, the overall benefits of the work outweigh these limitations.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical advance in unifying mixed-membership modeling with the BNBP.
* Practical and efficient collapsed Gibbs sampler for a challenging nonparametric model.
* Competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
* Clear and well-written presentation (despite complexity of the topic).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the EPPF may limit its broader applicability.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters (η) requires careful consideration.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation (focused solely on topic modeling).

**Rating:** **Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program. It presents novel theoretical results, provides a practical algorithm, and demonstrates its effectiveness through empirical evaluation.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the suggestions mentioned above.
