Summary
This paper develops a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling induced by the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The core idea is to (i) start from the augmented beta–gamma–Poisson representation of the BNBP, (ii) introduce a model in which group sizes are Poisson random variables linked to the total gamma-process mass, and (iii) marginalize out the beta process to obtain an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint distribution of random group sizes and their partitions. Using Bayes’ rule, the authors then derive the EPPF conditional on observed group sizes. They show the implied prior is a Poisson number of i.i.d. columns whose sizes follow a “digamma” distribution and whose within-column group allocations are Dirichlet-multinomial. From this, they derive a simple prediction rule for fully collapsed sampling.

They instantiate these results in a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process. The resulting collapsed Gibbs sampler differs from HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler: topic popularity is combined via a multiplicative term linking global and local counts, in contrast to HDP’s additive mechanism. Experiments on three corpora indicate faster mixing, fewer topics for comparable perplexity, and competitive or better predictive performance when matching the number of active topics.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to classical EPPFs for mixture models (CRP, Pitman’s EPPF framework) and points out the gap for mixed-membership models. The HDP partly addresses this via the Chinese restaurant franchise, but corresponding marginal partition laws for count-based processes like BNBP/GNBP were previously unavailable. The work builds on BNBP and related developments (Zhou et al.; Heaukulani & Roy; negative binomial IBP) and on sample-size dependent species models (Zhou & Walker) and σ-stable Poisson–Kingman mixtures (Lomelí et al.), noting BNBP lies outside that latter class. The resulting EPPF and prediction rule for grouped data appear novel.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear, principled route from a count process to an ECPF and then an EPPF conditional on observed group sizes. The recipe is of independent interest for other integer-valued processes.
- Fully collapsed inference: The sampler integrates out topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding simple updates and strong empirical mixing.
- Conceptual clarity: The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism (global popularity × group-specific popularity) provides a compelling contrast to HDP’s additive form and explains empirical behavior.
- Empirical evidence: On three datasets, the method tends to reach reasonable topic counts faster and obtains competitive perplexity with fewer topics.
- Practicality: Straightforward implementation; code provided; reasonable runtime.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope: Comparisons are limited to HDP-LDA direct assignment (no CRF sampler; unclear whether HDP concentration hyperparameters are inferred). Additional baselines (e.g., Poisson factor analysis, NB-Dirichlet models, Pitman–Yor HDP) would strengthen claims.
- Evaluation focus: Sole emphasis on perplexity; topic quality (coherence), sensitivity to hyperparameters (c, γ0, a0, b0), and calibration of inferred rj are not explored.
- Terminology and assumptions: The “EPPF” depends on group sizes, which relaxes standard sample-size invariance; while acknowledged and cited, the consequences for projectivity and exchangeability could be discussed more explicitly.
- Clarity: Some derivations (e.g., normalization of the digamma distribution, properties of Q(A) = −Σ ln(1−pk)) are terse and deferred to the appendix; a brief intuitive derivation in the main text would aid accessibility. Occasional typos (“perdition rule”) and incomplete references (missing years).
- Theoretical properties: No discussion of posterior consistency, asymptotic behavior of KJ, or identifiability. Even brief statements or references would help.

Quality
Technically sound with careful constructions: the ECPF and subsequent EPPF derivations are plausible and consistent with the augmented representation; the prediction rule is simple and implementable. The empirical study supports the main claims about mixing and compactness, though broader baselines and hyperparameter inference for HDP would improve fairness.

Clarity
Generally well organized but dense. The main narrative is clear; notation is heavy. Including algorithm pseudocode and a short, self-contained sketch of the key marginalization steps in the main text would improve readability. Fix minor typos and complete references.

Originality
High. Providing a group-size dependent EPPF for mixed-membership via BNBP and a fully collapsed sampler that also marginalizes the beta process is, to my knowledge, new. The general recipe is likely transferable.

Significance
Solid theoretical and practical impact. The bridge from count processes to partition laws for grouped data fills a gap and may catalyze analogous developments for gamma–Poisson or GNBP. The sampler is a useful contribution for practitioners seeking compact topic models with fast mixing.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify whether HDP α (and possibly top-level concentration) is inferred in experiments; if not, please include posterior inference for a fairer comparison.
- Report topic coherence or qualitative topics, and sensitivity to c, γ0, and priors on rj.
- Provide a brief discussion on projectivity and exchangeability implications of group-size dependent EPPFs.
- Add a complexity analysis vs. number of active topics and vocabulary size; include memory footprint.
- Consider adding baselines such as Poisson factor analysis and a CRF-based HDP sampler.
- Fix minor typos and complete bibliographic entries.

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled EPPF/ECPF derivations for BNBP mixed-membership
- Fully collapsed, efficient sampler with clear predictive rule
- Good empirical performance with compact models and faster mixing
- Potentially generalizable methodology

Cons:
- Limited baselines and evaluation metrics
- Some clarity/terminology issues and incomplete references
- Lack of discussion on asymptotic/theoretical properties
- HDP comparison may be disadvantaged if hyperparameters are not inferred

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a substantial, original contribution at the interface of nonparametric Bayesian theory and practical inference, with sensible empirical support. Addressing experimental fairness and clarity would strengthen it further.
