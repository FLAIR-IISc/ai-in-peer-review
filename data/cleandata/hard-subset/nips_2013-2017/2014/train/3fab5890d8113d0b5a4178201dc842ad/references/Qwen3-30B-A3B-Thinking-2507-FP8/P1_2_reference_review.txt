

# Peer Review: "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in nonparametric Bayesian modeling: the lack of a unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling (where data is grouped and each group has a random number of exchangeable data points). While the Dirichlet process and hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) have been widely used for mixture modeling, their extension to mixed-membership modeling has been limited. The authors focus on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), which has been previously applied to mixed-membership problems but lacked a proper marginal distribution governing exchangeable random partitions.

The main contribution is a novel method to derive the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, which had been an open problem. The authors achieve this by first randomizing group sizes to derive a joint distribution, then using Bayes' rule to obtain the EPPF. They also develop a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions and construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out not only topics and topic weights but also the infinite-dimensional beta process. The paper demonstrates that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and produces competitive predictive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus compared to HDP-LDA.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling, filling a significant gap in the literature. The method to derive the EPPF via a group size dependent model is general and could be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2. **Novel Modeling Framework**: The BNBP topic model introduces a distinct mechanism for topic shrinkage (multiplicative vs. HDP's additive) that provides more compact topic representations. This is theoretically well-motivated and empirically validated.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS), demonstrating the BNBP topic model's faster convergence, better mixing, and competitive predictive performance. The comparison with HDP-LDA is particularly convincing.

4. **Technical Rigor**: The mathematical derivations are sound, with proofs provided in the appendix. The paper carefully distinguishes the BNBP from related work (HDP, gamma-Poisson process, etc.) and makes clear theoretical contributions.

5. **Practical Utility**: The BNBP topic model's fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is straightforward to implement and converges faster than HDP-LDA, making it attractive for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: While the paper compares to HDP-LDA, it could more thoroughly compare to other recent nonparametric topic models (e.g., the Hierarchical Gamma Process or recent extensions of the IBP) that also address mixed-membership problems.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Convergence Analysis**: The paper demonstrates empirical convergence speed but lacks theoretical analysis of the BNBP Gibbs sampler's convergence properties, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. **Practical Assumption Limitation**: The paper assumes group sizes are observed rather than random, which may not apply to all mixed-membership scenarios. A discussion of how the method would extend to random group sizes would be valuable.

4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: While the paper tests different values of η (topic Dirichlet smoothing parameter), it could provide more detailed analysis of how the BNBP model's performance varies with the concentration parameter c and the dispersion parameters r_j.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous derivations and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., BNBP's more aggressive topic shrinkage compared to HDP). This is a complete piece of work, not a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key contributions are well-explained. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The only minor issue is that the EPPF derivation (Equation 9) is complex, but this is unavoidable given the nature of the problem.

**Originality**: The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a significant theoretical contribution that fills a gap in the literature. The general method for deriving EPPFs using group size dependent models is novel and could be applied to other stochastic processes. The BNBP topic model's fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is also a new contribution that distinguishes it from previous work.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in nonparametric Bayesian modeling with practical implications for topic modeling and mixed-membership problems. The BNBP topic model provides a more compact representation of corpora with competitive predictive performance, which is valuable for practitioners. The method to derive EPPFs could be generalized to other processes, opening new research directions.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Fills a significant gap in the literature by deriving the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling
- Provides a theoretically sound, general method applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over HDP-LDA in convergence speed, mixing, and compact representation
- Well-written with thorough theoretical and empirical validation
- Contributes to the growing body of work on nonparametric Bayesian modeling for mixed-membership problems

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited comparison to some recent nonparametric topic models
- Lacks theoretical convergence analysis for the BNBP Gibbs sampler
- Assumption of observed group sizes may limit applicability to some mixed-membership scenarios

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in mixed-membership settings. The method is generalizable to other processes, and the BNBP topic model demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches. While there are minor limitations in the scope of comparisons and theoretical analysis, these do not detract from the paper's significant contributions. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful advance in the field.

I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. It represents a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on nonparametric Bayesian modeling and mixed-membership problems.
