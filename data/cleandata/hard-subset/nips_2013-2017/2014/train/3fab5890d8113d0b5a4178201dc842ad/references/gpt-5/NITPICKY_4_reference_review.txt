Summary
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data. The key contribution is to derive a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP by: (i) introducing a Poissonization scheme that treats group sizes as random (via a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation), (ii) deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint law of random group sizes and partitions, (iii) applying Bayes’ rule to condition on observed group sizes and obtain an EPPF over a column-exchangeable random count matrix. The paper further provides a prediction rule for simulating partitions, constructs a BNBP topic model, and develops a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process. Experiments compare the BNBP topic model to HDP-LDA on three corpora, showing faster mixing, competitive perplexity with fewer topics, and a “compact” representation.

Strengths
- Tractable marginalization: The ECPF/EPPF derivation is nontrivial; the use of the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation to move from an infinite-dimensional random measure to a finite combinatorial law is elegant and useful.
- Prediction rule: The simple assignment rule is practically valuable for collapsed inference in mixed-membership settings beyond the HDP/CRF.
- Fully collapsed sampler: Marginalizing both B and Θ (and φ) for the BNBP topic model is technically appealing and leads to good mixing in practice.
- Clear connection between count modeling and mixture modeling, with interpretable roles for rj and c in controlling dispersion and shrinkage.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity
- Ambiguous gamma parameterization throughout. The paper uses Gamma[·, ·] without consistently stating shape/scale vs shape/rate. For example, θjk ∼ Gamma[rj, pk/(1 − pk)] vs θjk|− ∼ Gamma(njk + rj, pk) are ambiguous. This is critical for reproducibility and correctness of (4)–(7), (11)–(12).
- Group-size dependent “EPPF”: By definition, an EPPF typically depends only on partition sizes (and possibly K) and not on sample size; the paper relaxes this (cites [12]) but the terminology can mislead. Please state upfront and consistently that your law is sample-size dependent and explain implications (e.g., addition rule violation).
- Potential inconsistency between the use of ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c) (for KJ’s Poisson mean) and the new-cluster term in the prediction rule (10), which uses γ0 rj/(c + r·) without any ψ term. This discrepancy warrants clarification (derivation in the appendix is claimed but not shown here). If correct, please explain why the ψ terms vanish in the predictive, or correct the expression.
- The “digamma distribution” terminology/definition (8) is nonstandard; although you cite Sibuya, the support and normalizer should be clearly specified and reconciled with n·k ≥ 1 (empty columns removed). Please prove normalization and explain how it emerges from marginalizing B.
- Lemma 1’s equivalence (NBP vs beta–gamma–Poisson) hinges on precise parameterization/measure transformations (B/(1 − B)). It needs a more careful statement of conditions (e.g., B0 finite/continuous, pk ∈ (0,1), measurability).
- The EPPF in (9) includes a denominator that is a sum over all count-matrix decompositions of m. In practice you avoid computing it via the predictive rule, but it would help to state computational intractability explicitly and discuss whether this is an exact EPPF or a formal expression used for proofs.

Modeling/experiments
- c is a central parameter (affecting KJ via ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c)), yet the paper fixes c without stating how it is chosen or whether it is inferred. There is a prior on γ0 and rj, but none on c; sensitivity to c is not evaluated.
- rj prior: Using Gamma(a0, 1/b0) with a0 = b0 = 0.01 is extremely heavy-tailed; no diagnostics/sensitivity study is provided. Since rj strongly affects dispersion and topic shrinkage, omitting this analysis weakens conclusions.
- Comparisons are limited to HDP-LDA (direct assignment), omitting modern baselines or other BNBP inference variants (e.g., slice sampling, truncation-based samplers) in the same setting; “state-of-the-art” is not substantiated.
- Figures are referred to but not shown; no quantitative tables for perplexity, no statistical significance tests, and limited ablations (e.g., effect of c, γ0 priors, rj heterogeneity).
- Runtime claims lack standardized setup (no memory profile, no sparsity tricks for either method), and it is unclear whether the HDP baseline is equally optimized.

Presentation/formatting
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “perdition rule” instead of “prediction rule”; “disjoint disjoint”; “Poisson-Kigman” instead of “Kingman”; stray text intruding into (10) (“ψ(c + ∑ j rj) − ψ(c), and (a) rj = 1, …”), suggesting equation corruption. Please carefully proofread.
- References are often incomplete (missing years, venues, page ranges); some are “to appear” or arXiv with unclear versions; a few URLs are embedded in the main text.
- Notational overload (KJ vs K′), occasional missing vector transposes, and inconsistencies between bold/non-bold notation for vectors/matrices.
- The base measure/space Ω and the mapping to topic parameters φk should be described more carefully (measurability and support); explicitly state that B0 is finite and continuous on Ω consistent with a Dirichlet base for φ.

Quality
- The core derivations are promising but currently obscured by ambiguous parameterization and key dependencies (e.g., c) left unspecified. Several important proofs are deferred to the appendix without sufficient in-paper guidance.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and could be organized more cleanly, with clearer statements of assumptions, parameterizations, and a consistent notation glossary. The predictive rule derivation should be sketched in the main text.

Originality
- Providing a group-size dependent EPPF and a fully collapsed BNBP topic sampler is novel relative to prior BNBP work (finite truncation/slice, negative-binomial IBP). However, the paper should more clearly differentiate from closely related recent works (e.g., Heaukulani & Roy; Zhou et al. on random count matrices) and position its general method for other processes beyond BNBP more concretely.

Significance
- If polished, the general EPPF/ECPF approach and the fully collapsed sampler could be impactful for mixed-membership modeling. As presented, the empirical evidence is suggestive but not definitive.

Pros
- Conceptually interesting route from integer-valued processes to sample-size dependent EPPFs.
- Practical, simple prediction rule and a fully collapsed sampler.
- Clear interpretability of multiplicative shrinkage vs HDP’s additive mechanism.

Cons
- Ambiguous/possibly inconsistent parameterization; unclear handling of c; derivational gaps.
- Limited, partially anecdotal experiments; missing ablations and robust baselines.
- Numerous typos, formatting issues, and incomplete references impede readability.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The ideas are strong, but the accumulation of technical ambiguities, missing details (especially around parameterization and c), presentation errors, and limited empirical validation outweighs the contributions in the current form. With thorough proofreading, clarified derivations, explicit inference for c (or systematic sensitivity analysis), stronger experiments, and improved related-work positioning, this work could be a solid future submission.
