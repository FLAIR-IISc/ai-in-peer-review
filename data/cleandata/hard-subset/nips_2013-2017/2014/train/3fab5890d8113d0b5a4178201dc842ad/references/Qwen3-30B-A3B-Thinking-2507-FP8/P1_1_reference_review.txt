

# Peer Review: "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in mixed-membership modeling by developing a group size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors build upon the limited theoretical understanding of BNBP in mixed-membership contexts, where previous work (e.g., HDP) lacks a unified treatment of exchangeable random partitions. While the BNBP has been used in mixed-membership modeling, its marginal distribution governing exchangeable random partitions was previously unknown. The paper's main contributions include: (1) deriving the BNBP's EPPF through a novel method of randomizing group sizes, (2) developing a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions, and (3) constructing a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process. The authors demonstrate through experiments that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves competitive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus compared to HDP-LDA.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and makes a significant theoretical contribution. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is rigorous, with detailed proofs provided in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from previous work, particularly the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP), by highlighting the multiplicative vs. additive control mechanisms for topic shrinkage. The experimental results are well-designed and support the theoretical claims, demonstrating faster convergence and better mixing of the collapsed Gibbs sampler compared to HDP-LDA. The paper is complete, with theoretical development, algorithm derivation, and empirical validation. The authors are honest about limitations (e.g., the complexity of the BNBP's EPPF compared to HDP's) and provide a clear comparison with related work.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from theoretical development to practical application. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. However, the derivation of the EPPF is quite technical, and the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the BNBP's EPPF has the specific form it does. The experimental section is clear but could include more visualizations of the topic distributions to help readers understand the compact representation achieved by the BNBP. The writing is accessible to experts in Bayesian nonparametrics but may be challenging for readers without background in stochastic processes.

## Originality
This paper makes a strong original contribution by developing the first EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using the BNBP, filling a gap that has existed since the emergence of EPPF models for mixture modeling. The authors' method for deriving the EPPF via randomization of group sizes is novel and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The BNBP topic model and its collapsed Gibbs sampler represent a meaningful extension of previous work on the beta-negative binomial process [10, 11] and the HDP [6]. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing how the BNBP's multiplicative topic control mechanism differs from HDP's additive mechanism, which is a significant theoretical insight.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in mixed-membership modeling that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The BNBP topic model provides a practical alternative to HDP-LDA with demonstrable advantages in convergence speed, mixing properties, and model compactness. The ability to achieve similar or better predictive performance with fewer topics is valuable for practitioners dealing with large-scale document corpora. The method to derive the EPPF could inspire similar derivations for other integer-valued stochastic processes, potentially leading to new models for mixed-membership problems. While the paper focuses on topic modeling, the theoretical framework could be applied to other mixed-membership problems in machine learning.

## Strengths
- Fills a significant theoretical gap in mixed-membership modeling by deriving the BNBP's EPPF
- Develops a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out infinite-dimensional components
- Demonstrates faster convergence and better mixing than HDP-LDA in experiments
- Provides a clear theoretical explanation for the BNBP's multiplicative topic shrinkage mechanism
- Shows practical advantages in achieving comparable performance with fewer topics

## Weaknesses
- The derivation of the EPPF is highly technical and could benefit from more intuitive explanations
- Limited comparison with other recent topic models (e.g., Dynamic Topic Models, other nonparametric models)
- Lacks statistical significance tests for the performance comparisons
- Does not discuss potential limitations for extremely large-scale applications
- Could include more visualization of the topic distributions to illustrate the compact representation

## Pro and Con Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing a significant gap in the literature
- Rigorous mathematical derivation with detailed proofs
- Practical implementation with clear advantages over existing methods
- Well-designed experiments demonstrating concrete benefits
- Clear distinction from previous work with meaningful theoretical insights

**Con:**
- Highly technical content may limit accessibility to broader audience
- Limited comparison with other recent topic models beyond HDP-LDA
- Some claims about performance advantages could be strengthened with statistical tests
- Does not address computational limitations for very large datasets

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to mixed-membership modeling by developing the first EPPF for the BNBP and demonstrating its practical advantages through a topic model application. The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and makes a clear distinction from previous work. While the technical nature of the derivation may limit accessibility for some readers, the practical benefits of the BNBP topic model are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in nonparametric Bayesian modeling for mixed-membership problems and opens avenues for future research on deriving EPPFs for other stochastic processes.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper's theoretical contribution is significant, its practical implementation is well-executed, and the results provide strong evidence of the BNBP's advantages over existing methods. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and makes a clear contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling.

## Suggested Improvements
1. Include more intuitive explanations of the BNBP's EPPF derivation
2. Add statistical significance tests for the performance comparisons
3. Expand the experimental section to compare with additional topic models
4. Provide more visualization of the topic distributions to illustrate the compact representation
5. Discuss computational limitations for extremely large-scale applications
