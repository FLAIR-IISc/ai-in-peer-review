- Major theoretical contribution: first derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in mixed-membership modeling.
- Novel method: derive the BNBP EPPF by randomizing group sizes to get a joint distribution of group sizes and partitions, then use Bayes’ rule.
- Practical tools: derive a prediction rule for exchangeable random partitions and build a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler marginalizing topics and the beta process.
- Technical quality: rigorous theoretical derivations with proofs; careful distinction between group-size-dependent EPPF and traditional EPPFs.
- Empirical results: on JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12 the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and yields comparable or better predictive performance with more compact topics than HDP-LDA.
- Clarity: well-organized and clearly written with consistent notation; some complex derivations (e.g., Lemma 3) could use more intuition.
- Originality: genuinely novel derivation of BNBP EPPF for grouped data and an innovative randomization approach potentially generalizable to other integer-valued processes.
- Significance: advances nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling and offers a practical, efficient alternative with potential for broader applications (e.g., gamma-Poisson).
- Strength: fully collapsed Gibbs sampler marginalizes more components than competing methods.
- Strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating faster convergence and model compactness.
- Strength: clear theoretical contrast to HDP-LDA (multiplicative vs. additive shrinkage).
- Weakness: EPPF formula (Eq. 9) is complex and hard to interpret intuitively.
- Weakness: limited exploration of why BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage gives better practical performance.
- Weakness: no empirical comparison with recent models like the discrete infinite logistic normal (DILN) [26].
- Weakness: computational complexity analysis is insufficiently detailed.
- Recommendation: accept — substantial theoretical contribution, convincing empirical results, and overall strong paper.
- Suggested improvements: add discussion on theoretical advantages of multiplicative shrinkage vs. additive, compare to DILN [26], and provide more detailed computational complexity analysis.
