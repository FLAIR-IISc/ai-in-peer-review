This paper introduces a novel method to derive the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The primary motivation is to extend the unified EPPF framework, commonly used for single-set clustering, to the more complex setting of grouped data, for which priors like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) are dominant. The key challenge addressed is that for many integer-valued stochastic processes like the BNBP, the marginal distribution over partitions is difficult to derive, precluding the development of fully collapsed inference algorithms.

The authors' main technical contribution is a clever two-step procedure to arrive at the EPPF. First, they define an auxiliary model where group sizes are treated as random variables. For this "group size dependent model," they derive the joint probability of the random group sizes and their partitions, which they term the Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF). This is achieved by leveraging the beta-gamma-Poisson representation of the BNBP. Second, by applying Bayes' rule and conditioning on the observed (fixed) group sizes, they derive the desired EPPF for the BNBP.

Building on this theoretical foundation, the paper makes several further contributions. It derives a simple and elegant prediction rule that enables the simulation of partitions from the BNBP's EPPF. This rule forms the basis of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a new BNBP topic model, which is presented as a concrete application. The authors provide detailed experimental comparisons against the widely-used HDP topic model on three text corpora, demonstrating that their proposed model exhibits faster convergence, better MCMC mixing, and competitive predictive performance, particularly when a compact topic representation is desired.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a significant and novel theoretical contribution to Bayesian nonparametrics. The method of using a group-size dependent auxiliary model to derive an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a new and powerful technique. It provides a principled path to developing fully collapsed samplers for a class of models, like those based on the BNBP, where inference has previously relied on less efficient methods like finite truncation or slice sampling. The resulting BNBP topic model is a valuable addition to the literature, offering a clear alternative to the HDP with distinct shrinkage properties (multiplicative rather than additive). The potential for this method to be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, as suggested by the authors, further enhances its significance.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development is presented logically through a series of lemmas. The connection between the BNBP and the group-size dependent model (Lemma 1) is clearly established. The derivations of the ECPF, the random count matrix prior, the EPPF, and the final prediction rule appear correct. A major strength is the derivation of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (Eq. 12), which analytically integrates out the infinite-dimensional beta process, topic parameters, and topic weights. This leads to empirical benefits in convergence and mixing, which are well-supported by the experiments.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the gap in the literature regarding a unified EPPF treatment for mixed-membership models. The step-by-step derivation, while mathematically dense, is easy to follow. The comparison of the BNBP topic model's Gibbs update equation with those of LDA and HDP-LDA (Eqs. 12-14) is particularly insightful, providing a concise and intuitive explanation of the differences in how the models handle topic popularities at the global and local levels.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Derivations:** While the final prediction rule (Eq. 10) is remarkably simple, the intermediate EPPF expression (Eq. 9) is highly complex. The paper relies on the appendix for several key proofs. While this is standard practice, a more intuitive explanation in the main text for why such a complex EPPF leads to such a simple predictive rule would improve the paper's accessibility. The logical leap from the complex ECPF and EPPF to the simple sampler might leave some readers wondering if a more direct derivation exists.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental comparison is solid but could be strengthened. The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" seems slightly overstated. The results in Figure 3 (middle column) show that HDP-LDA often achieves lower perplexity than the BNBP model, albeit by using a larger number of topics. The paper's key empirical strength is that the BNBP model achieves comparable or better perplexity with a *more compact* representation (fewer topics), which is a significant practical advantage. This point is made but could be framed more centrally as the primary performance claim, rather than overall state-of-the-art perplexity. The comparison is made against the HDP's direct assignment sampler; a discussion of how results might differ against a fully collapsed Chinese Restaurant Franchise sampler for the HDP would provide additional context, even if acknowledging the practical difficulties of the latter.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Novel and significant theoretical contribution in deriving an EPPF for a mixed-membership model.
    *   The proposed method provides a new tool for developing fully collapsed samplers for a broader class of Bayesian nonparametric models.
    *   The resulting BNBP topic model is a strong and well-motivated alternative to HDP-LDA.
    *   The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is shown empirically to have superior convergence and mixing properties compared to the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler.
    *   The paper is technically sound and clearly written.

*   **Con:**
    *   The path from the complex EPPF to the simple prediction rule could be better elucidated.
    *   The claim of state-of-the-art predictive performance could be nuanced; the primary advantage appears to be achieving strong performance with a more compact model.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and probabilistic modeling. The theoretical novelty of the EPPF derivation is significant, and the practical application to topic modeling demonstrates its utility. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contributions of the work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to be of interest to researchers working on nonparametric methods, topic models, and mixed-membership models.
