Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling via the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). While the BNBP and related count CRMs have been applied to grouped data (e.g., Zhou & Carin 2015; Zhou et al. 2012), a central gap has been the absence of marginal laws describing the exchangeable random partitions induced on grouped observations—analogous to how DP marginalization yields the CRP. The authors address this by (i) deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the BNBP that jointly models random group sizes and their partitions; (ii) using Bayes’ rule to obtain a group-size-dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for fixed observed group sizes; and (iii) producing a simple prediction rule enabling a truncation-free, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. They then instantiate a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, and give closed-form updates. Experiments on three corpora compare against HDP-LDA, demonstrating faster convergence/mixing and competitive or better perplexities at comparable numbers of topics, typically using more compact representations.

This contribution connects several strands: EPPFs for mixture modeling (Pitman 2006; Lijoi & Prünster on NRMIs), recent marginal samplers for Poisson–Kingman models (Lomelí et al. 2014; which do not include the BNBP), and the BNBP’s count-matrix representations and predictive structures (Heaukulani & Roy 2014; Zhou et al. 2014) that focused on feature allocation or infinite count matrices rather than grouped partitions with fixed totals. The paper builds on the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation to derive finite-dimensional distributions, a digamma law for cluster sizes, and a Dirichlet–multinomial allocation across groups, yielding a Poisson number of i.i.d. columns.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear path from the BNBP CRM to an ECPF and then to a group-size-dependent EPPF and prediction rule. This fills a conceptual and practical gap for mixed-membership modeling with integer-valued processes. The construction is general and likely adaptable to other processes (e.g., gamma–Poisson, GNBP).
- Algorithmic advance: A fully collapsed sampler for the BNBP topic model that integrates out the beta process, topics, and topic weights, with simple Gibbs updates and no truncation. The resulting rule is intuitive: a multiplicative combination of global and local popularity with document-specific dispersion rj.
- Empirical evidence: On three corpora, the sampler converges and mixes notably faster than HDP-LDA’s direct-assignment sampler; at comparable topic counts and non-extreme η, perplexities are typically lower, indicating improved modeling efficiency (similar predictive power with fewer topics). Code is provided.
- Clarity of modeling perspective: The count-mixture viewpoint and the role of rj in controlling overdispersion are well articulated and illustrated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and presentation: Notation is dense and sometimes hard to parse (e.g., reuse of dots for sums, heavy use of subscripts). The EPPF (Eq. 9) is involved; a more guided derivation and a succinct proof sketch in the main text would help. Some equations (e.g., the new-cluster term in the prediction rule) would benefit from explicit normalization constants and clear dependence on c, r·, and γ0; the text mentions digamma functions elsewhere—please ensure consistency and correct factors.
- Evaluation breadth: Baselines are limited to HDP-LDA. Stronger or complementary baselines would include Pitman–Yor process LDA (HDP-PYP), hierarchical NRMI admixtures, and Poisson factorization methods with negative-binomial likelihoods. A comparison with previous BNBP samplers (finite/adaptive truncation, slice) on speed/accuracy would strengthen claims.
- Perplexity evaluation: While you cite Wallach et al. (2009), the reported evaluation samples φ, θ and then averages; the left-to-right sequential estimator is more standard for collapsed models and avoids bias from conditional dependence. Please report LTR perplexities as a robustness check. Also report results at matched topic numbers across methods.
- Sensitivity/ablation: Since rj play a central role, include posterior summaries of rj, sensitivity to their priors, and an ablation with fixed rj to quantify gains. Similarly, discuss/diagnose c and γ0.
- Theoretical scope: The EPPF is explicitly sample-size dependent. While you reference Zhou & Walker (2014), it would help to contextualize implications (e.g., exchangeability properties, addition rule violations, consequences for predictive consistency) and clarify when the group-size dependence is desirable versus a modeling caveat.
- Related work coverage: Please discuss hierarchical NRMI admixture models (e.g., James, Lijoi, Prünster) and exchangeable feature probability functions (Broderick et al.) to better position the contribution relative to other frameworks for grouped partitions/features. Some references are incomplete and should be fixed.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide a concise proof sketch for the ECPF (Eq. 6) and the Poisson law for KJ, or at least a clear pointer to the appendix sections that establish them?  
2) Please clarify the exact form of the new-cluster probability in Eq. (10): does it involve ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c), or only c + r·? Ensuring a consistent derivation between ECPF/EPPF and the prediction rule is important.  
3) What is the per-iteration complexity of the collapsed sampler as a function of nonzero counts and topics, and how does it compare empirically to HDP-LDA at matched K?  
4) How stable are results across η when using the LTR estimator? Do the conclusions about compactness vs. perplexity hold?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and nontrivial theoretical development (ECPF/EPPF and prediction rule) for a widely used count-based CRM.  
- Practical, truncation-free, fully collapsed sampler with clear empirical benefits in convergence/mixing and compact modeling.  
- Potentially broad impact: methodology seems extendable to other integer-valued processes.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical baselines and evaluation methodology; missing standard LTR perplexity and comparisons to PYP/NRMI admixtures.  
- Dense presentation and some ambiguities in equations; heavier reliance on appendices.  
- The implications of sample-size-dependent EPPFs could be discussed more fully.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper makes a meaningful theoretical and algorithmic contribution that addresses a recognized gap and is supported by promising experiments. Strengthening the empirical comparisons, clarifying derivations (especially the prediction rule), and improving presentation would make the work more compelling.
