Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling induced by the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The key idea is to start from the BNBP as an integer-valued process, augment it via a beta–gamma–Poisson construction, and derive (i) an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint distribution of random group sizes and partitions, (ii) a group-size dependent EPPF obtained from the ECPF by conditioning on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule, and (iii) a simple prediction rule enabling Gibbs simulation of exchangeable partitions. Building on this, the authors construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding closed-form updates. Empirically, they compare to HDP-LDA on several corpora, showing faster mixing and comparable or better perplexity at similar topic counts, with a more compact representation.

The work connects and extends several lines of research at NIPS/NeurIPS and beyond: Dirichlet processes and EPPFs/CRP [1,5], hierarchical DP and Chinese restaurant franchise [6], negative binomial processes in mixed-membership/count modeling [7,10,11], and matrix count distributions (e.g., NB-IBP, “ice cream” process) [16,17]. Prior EPPF work largely focused on mixture models for a single set (e.g., Pitman’s EPPF; Poisson–Kingman samplers [13]); here the authors target grouped data with shared clusters and give a unified treatment for the BNBP. The sample-size dependent EPPF relates to recent species sampling models [12] and is outside the σ-stable Poisson–Kingman class.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a general recipe to obtain an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling from an integer-valued stochastic process by randomizing group sizes (ECPF) and conditioning back on observed sizes.
- New prediction rule for BNBP partitions that is simple and interpretable (global popularity × group-specific popularity), analogous in spirit to CRP/CRF rules but multiplicative rather than additive.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes all infinite-dimensional measures. The sampler appears to mix faster than the HDP direct-assignment sampler and is straightforward to implement.
- Empirical evidence across three datasets suggests competitive or better perplexity for a similar number of topics, with more compact representations; convergence/mixing diagnostics are favorable. Code availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Fairness of baselines: The comparison uses the HDP direct-assignment sampler (partially collapsed). A stronger baseline would be a fully collapsed HDP sampler via the Chinese restaurant franchise or modern accelerated CRF variants, and/or a Pitman–Yor HDP. This matters for both mixing and perplexity.
- Scope of empirical evaluation: While perplexity is standard, results emphasize compactness vs. performance trade-offs for a range of η but lack wall-clock time-to-perplexity curves, sensitivity analyses (γ0, c, rj hyperpriors), and ablations (e.g., fixing vs. learning rj). Topic quality/interpretability is not discussed.
- Theoretical positioning: The EPPF is explicitly sample-size dependent. While this is justified by [12], the paper should more clearly discuss implications for consistency/asymptotics, exchangeability properties (within groups and across columns), and how the size dependence affects posterior inference as m grows.
- Relation to concurrent work: Overlaps with Heaukulani & Roy [16] and Zhou et al. [17] on combinatorial structure and random count matrices should be sharpened to delineate novelty (especially the step from ECPF to EPPF for grouped partitions).
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is notation-heavy; some definitions (e.g., the “digamma distribution” over positive integers) are uncommon and deserve more intuition (tail behavior, role in cluster-size prior). A high-level algorithm box and a summary of computational complexity would help. References miss years and some standard details.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The derivations appear technically sound; augmentations and marginalizations are standard and carefully used. Proofs are in the appendix; the main text provides intuition. Empirical methodology is reasonable, but stronger baselines and broader diagnostics would improve the evidential support.
- Clarity: Generally clear but dense. The narrative from ECPF to EPPF to prediction rule is well structured; however, some readers will struggle without more intuition and a concise algorithmic summary. A clearer discussion of the exchangeability assumptions and the size-dependent EPPF would help.
- Originality: Deriving an EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling and a fully collapsed sampler is novel and nontrivial. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism and link to count modeling are insightful.
- Significance: Mixed-membership modeling remains central, and principled nonparametric priors beyond the HDP are valuable. The proposed approach is likely to influence both methodology (collapsed samplers for other integer-valued processes) and practice (compact topic models). That said, empirical gains are moderate; the main impact is methodological.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to a fully collapsed HDP sampler (CRF) and a Pitman–Yor HDP; include runtime vs. perplexity curves and ESS/time metrics.
- Provide ablations: effect of learning rj vs. fixing, sensitivity to c and γ0, and comparison to GNBP or gamma–Poisson alternatives to support the “general method” claim.
- Clarify theoretical properties of the sample-size dependent EPPF: exchangeability, addition rule, and asymptotic behavior; discuss implications for posterior consistency.
- Expand intuition for the digamma distribution prior over cluster sizes and its computational role; provide a brief algorithm box.
- Improve references (years, venues) and tidy typos; add a brief related-work table situating this work among [10,11,16,17] and Poisson–Kingman approaches.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, novel probabilistic construction (ECPF→EPPF) for BNBP mixed-membership modeling with a clean prediction rule.
- Fully collapsed sampler with practical benefits (faster mixing, compact models) and code available.
- Clear connections to and extensions of influential NeurIPS lines of work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical comparison lacks stronger baselines and broader diagnostics; performance gains are not consistently superior.
- Clarity can be improved; theoretical implications of size dependence are under-discussed.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a well-motivated, technically solid contribution with clear methodological novelty and reasonable empirical support. Strengthening the empirical comparisons and clarifying theoretical and presentational aspects would further increase its impact.
