

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary
This paper develops a group size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors derive the EPPF for BNBP, propose a prediction rule, and construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The paper claims to fill a gap in the literature by providing a unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling, which has been largely limited to the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) in prior work.

## Major Technical Concerns
1. **Mathematical Errors in Key Lemmas**: 
   - Lemma 1's derivation of the joint distribution (equation 4) contains a critical error: f(zj, mj |Θj) = f(zj |Θj, mj)Pois(mj, Θj(Ω)) = 1/mj! ∏k θ^{njk}_{jk} e^{-θjk}. This is incorrect; the Poisson PMF should be e^{-Θj(Ω)} Θj(Ω)^{mj}/mj!, not e^{-θjk}. The correct expression should involve the total mass Θj(Ω), not individual θjk terms. This error propagates through subsequent derivations.
   - Lemma 2 (ECPF) contains an incorrect gamma function ratio: Γ(n·k)Γ(c+r·)/Γ(c+n·k+r·) appears instead of the standard Γ(n·k + r·)/Γ(r·) or similar forms found in analogous EPPFs. This suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of the marginalization process.
   - Lemma 4 (Prediction Rule) includes ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c) in the denominator of the new cluster probability, which is unusual and not supported by standard EPPF prediction rules. The digamma function should not appear directly in prediction rules as it does here.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Formatting**:
   - The paper inconsistently uses "n·k" (with a dot) and "n_{\cdot k}" throughout, which is confusing for readers. Mathematical notation should be consistent.
   - The EPPF expression (equation 9) is poorly defined with an ambiguous summation notation ∑∑_{K k=1} n:k=m, which is unclear and not standard mathematical notation.
   - The reference list contains incomplete citations (e.g., "A Bayesian analysis of some nonparametric problems, T.S. Ferguson, Ann. Statist., ,") that omit page numbers and volume information.

3. **Lack of Proper Derivation**:
   - The derivation of the EPPF from the ECPF (equation 9) is not properly justified. The paper claims to use Bayes' rule, but the derivation is not shown with sufficient detail to verify its correctness.
   - The paper claims to generalize [12] but doesn't clearly explain how their "sample size dependent" EPPF differs from the work in [12] or why this generalization is necessary.

## Minor Issues
1. The paper claims to provide "closed-form update equations" for the BNBP topic model, but the Gibbs sampling update (equation 12) still contains the digamma function ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c), which is not computationally closed-form as typically understood in Bayesian inference.

2. The experimental results section (Section 4) lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameter settings (e.g., how c, r, and γ0 were set), making it difficult to reproduce the results.

3. The comparison with HDP-LDA is limited to a single model (HDP-LDA) without comparing with other state-of-the-art topic models.

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding unified treatment of mixed-membership modeling.
2. The BNBP topic model is presented with a clear intuitive interpretation.
3. The experimental results suggest the BNBP topic model converges faster than HDP-LDA, which is a potentially useful contribution.

## Weaknesses
1. The mathematical errors in key lemmas (Lemma 1, 2, 4) undermine the entire theoretical contribution of the paper.
2. The paper's claim of "fully collapsed" inference is questionable due to the presence of the digamma function in prediction rules and update equations.
3. The paper fails to properly connect its BNBP EPPF to the broader literature on exchangeable partitions, particularly how it differs from the "sample size dependent" EPPFs in [12].

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper identifies a potentially interesting gap in the literature and presents a novel topic model, the mathematical errors in the key theoretical contributions are substantial and undermine the paper's technical validity. The paper would need to be completely rewritten to correct these errors before it could be considered for publication. The presence of these errors, combined with the lack of proper derivation and inconsistent notation, makes the paper unsuitable for NIPS.

The authors should carefully re-derive the ECPF and EPPF from first principles, paying special attention to the marginalization process and the correct application of the Poisson and negative binomial distributions. The prediction rule should be verified against standard EPPF forms. The paper should also be carefully proofread for consistent notation and complete references.

While the topic is relevant to NIPS, the technical flaws in the paper's core contributions make it difficult to assess the significance of the proposed method. A corrected version addressing these mathematical errors would be worth considering for future submission.
