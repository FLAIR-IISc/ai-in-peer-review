This paper addresses the problem of deriving the marginal distribution of exchangeable random partitions for mixed-membership models based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While nonparametric Bayesian priors like the Dirichlet process have well-understood marginal representations (e.g., the Chinese restaurant process) that facilitate fully collapsed inference, such a representation has been elusive for the BNBP. Previous work on BNBP-based models has relied on less efficient inference schemes like finite truncation or slice sampling. The main contribution of this paper is the derivation of the group-size dependent Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP. The authors achieve this through a novel technique: they first define a model where group sizes are random variables, derive the joint distribution of these group sizes and their partitions (termed the Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function, or ECPF), and then use Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes, yielding the desired EPPF. From this theoretical result, the paper derives a simple, closed-form prediction rule. This rule is then used to construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, which is demonstrated to be efficient and effective.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivations, while intricate, appear correct and provide a significant step forward in understanding the combinatorial structure of the BNBP. The key theoretical results are presented in a series of lemmas, with proofs referenced in the appendix. The augmentation of the BNBP as a beta-gamma-Poisson process is a standard and useful technique, and the authors leverage it effectively to derive the joint likelihood in (4), which is the starting point for marginalizing out the latent measures. The derivation of the ECPF and the subsequent EPPF via conditioning on group sizes is a clever and complete piece of theoretical work.

The claims are well-supported by the experimental results. The authors compare their proposed BNBP topic model with the widely used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) topic model. The experiments on three standard text corpora compellingly demonstrate the advantages of the fully collapsed BNBP sampler in terms of convergence speed and mixing, as shown in the trace plots in Figure 2. The analysis of held-out perplexity versus the number of topics (Figure 3) provides a nuanced evaluation, showing that the BNBP model can achieve competitive or superior predictive performance with a more compact topic representation, which is a desirable property. The authors are careful to evaluate across a range of hyperparameters and honestly discuss the trade-offs, noting that the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism is stronger than the HDP's additive one.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem by situating the BNBP within the broader context of nonparametric mixture and mixed-membership modeling, contrasting it with the Dirichlet process and HDP. The distinction between the ECPF (for random group sizes) and the EPPF (for fixed group sizes) is explained well and is central to the paper's main contribution. The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations are laid out logically.

A particularly strong aspect of the paper's clarity is the direct comparison of the prediction rules for the BNBP topic model (12), LDA (13), and HDP-LDA (14). This provides an exceptionally clear and intuitive explanation of the differences between the models at the algorithmic level, highlighting the novel multiplicative interaction between a topic's global popularity (`n·k`) and its local popularity (`njk`) in the BNBP model. The description of the experimental setup and evaluation methodology is detailed enough for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the results.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. The primary contribution is the derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide the exact marginal partition distribution for this process, thus enabling fully collapsed inference. The method used to derive it—by first modeling random group sizes to obtain the ECPF and then conditioning—is novel and could potentially be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes, as the authors suggest.

While the BNBP itself and its application to topic modeling are not entirely new, this paper provides a new and superior inference framework. Previous work [10, 11] relied on approximate inference. The resulting BNBP topic model and its fully collapsed sampler are also novel. The work is clearly distinguished from related research on infinite random count matrices [16, 17] by focusing on the partitioning of fixed-size groups, which is the core of mixed-membership modeling. The paper adequately references and builds upon the foundational work in nonparametric Bayes, EPPFs, and the BNBP.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant for both theory and practice. Theoretically, deriving the EPPF for the BNBP fills an important gap, advancing our understanding of the combinatorial structures underlying a key class of nonparametric models beyond the Dirichlet process family. It provides a unified perspective on the BNBP analogous to what the Chinese restaurant process provides for the Dirichlet process.

Practically, the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based model is a highly valuable contribution. Such samplers often exhibit superior mixing and convergence properties compared to partially collapsed or uncollapsed samplers, which the empirical results confirm. The BNBP topic model provides practitioners with a compelling alternative to the HDP, especially when faster convergence and more compact models are desired. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism offers a different modeling assumption that may be better suited for certain datasets. The ideas presented are likely to be used and built upon by researchers working on Bayesian nonparametrics and topic modeling.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is the first to derive the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process, a significant theoretical result that was an open problem.
*   **Practical Advancement:** The derived EPPF leads to a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, which is a major practical improvement over existing inference methods for BNBP models.
*   **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the sampler's faster convergence and mixing, and demonstrate competitive predictive performance against the state-of-the-art HDP-LDA, particularly for achieving compact models.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivating the problem and lucidly explaining its technical contributions and their implications.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   The experimental comparison is strong but limited to HDP-LDA. Including comparisons to other recent nonparametric topic models could have provided a broader context for the performance claims.
*   The generality of the theoretical method (using an ECPF to derive an EPPF) is mentioned as a promising future direction but is not demonstrated for any process other than the BNBP in this work.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor limitations. It presents a complete piece of work with significant contributions to both the theory of Bayesian nonparametrics and the practical application of mixed-membership models.
