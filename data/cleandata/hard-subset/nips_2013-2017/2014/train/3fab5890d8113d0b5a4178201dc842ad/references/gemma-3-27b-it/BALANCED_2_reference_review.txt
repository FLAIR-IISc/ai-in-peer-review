This paper introduces a novel exploration of the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) within the framework of mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP, addressing a gap in the unified treatment of mixed-membership models that exists despite progress in single-dataset mixture modeling. The contribution is significant as it provides a theoretical foundation for using the BNBP in scenarios where data is grouped, and delivers a practical implementation through a topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** Deriving the EPPF for the BNBP is a substantial theoretical achievement. It extends the existing framework of EPPFs to a more complex setting, filling a gap in the literature and enabling principled Bayesian non-parametric modeling of grouped data. The connection made between the BNBP and the ECPF is well-explained and justified.
* **Practical Implementation:** The construction of a BNBP topic model, alongside a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, demonstrates the applicability of the theoretical results.  A fully collapsed sampler is highly desirable for computational efficiency and ease of use.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS12) show competitive performance against the HDP-LDA, particularly highlighting a more compact representation of the corpus. The detailed experimental setup, including comparisons to the HDP-LDA with varying hyperparameter settings, strengthens the conclusions.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written, with clear definitions of key concepts and a logical flow of arguments. The lemmas and derivations are presented in a structured manner.
* **Convergence and Mixing Analysis:** The analysis of convergence and mixing properties of the Gibbs samplers demonstrates a practical advantage of the BNBP topic model over the HDP-LDA, showing faster convergence and better mixing.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of EPPF:** While the derivation of the EPPF is a core contribution, the resulting expression (Equation 9) is quite complex, potentially hindering its practical use for analytical purposes or further theoretical developments.  The reliance on the prediction rule (Equation 10) for simulation is understandable, but the complexity of the EPPF itself remains a drawback.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors explore a range of η values, the sensitivity of the model performance to other hyperparameters (like *a0*, *b0*, *c*, *γ0*, *e0*, *f0*) is not extensively investigated.  A more thorough sensitivity analysis could strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with the HDP-LDA. Including comparisons with other relevant models (e.g., other infinite mixture models or traditional non-Bayesian topic models) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the BNBP topic model's strengths and weaknesses.
* **Repetitive Discussion of Advantages:** The advantages of the BNBP (e.g., faster convergence, compact representation) are reiterated multiple times throughout the paper, sometimes without adding new insights.  This could be streamlined for greater conciseness.
* **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation can occasionally be dense. Consistent use of subscripts and superscripts would enhance readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a theoretical foundation for using the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling.
* The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a valuable contribution, enabling practical application of the model.
* The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance and highlight the model’s ability to achieve a compact representation of data.
* The detailed mathematical derivations and the clear presentation of the results contribute to the scientific rigor of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the derived EPPF may limit its practical utility.
* The scope of the empirical evaluation could be broader.
* A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would enhance the robustness of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian non-parametric modeling. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a substantial theoretical achievement, and the practical implementation through the topic model and Gibbs sampler is well-executed. While the complexity of the EPPF and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the overall quality and originality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, contingent upon minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Streamlining the discussion and reducing redundancy.
2.  Providing a brief discussion of the computational cost of evaluating the complex EPPF.
3.  Consider adding a brief comparison to a non-Bayesian topic model (e.g., LDA) to provide a broader context.
4.  Acknowledging and discussing potential limitations stemming from hyperparameter choices beyond η.
