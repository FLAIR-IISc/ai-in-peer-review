Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership (grouped-data) setting. Because directly marginalizing the beta process to obtain a prior over partitions with fixed group sizes is hard, the authors first construct a group-size dependent model (randomizing the per-group counts), derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint law of group sizes and partitions, and then apply Bayes’ rule to obtain a group-size dependent EPPF that governs a latent column-exchangeable random count matrix whose row sums equal the observed group sizes. They also derive a simple prediction rule enabling collapsed Gibbs sampling, and instantiate these results in a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the infinite beta process. Empirically, they compare to HDP-LDA (direct assignment sampler), reporting faster convergence/mixing and comparable or better perplexity at similar numbers of topics, with a more compact representation. 

This connects to a substantial body of work: EPPFs and CRP/CRF (Pitman; Teh et al.), NRMIs, beta process and IBP variants (Hjort; Thibaux & Jordan), and integer-valued processes for mixed membership (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP). Prior marginalizations for BNBP/related processes over infinite count matrices (e.g., negative-binomial IBP and “ice-cream” buffet) did not provide a mixed-membership EPPF for fixed group sizes. The authors build on sample-size dependent EPPFs (Zhou & Walker) and provide a construction specifically tailored to BNBP.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The augmentation BNBP ≡ beta–gamma–Poisson is standard. Lemma 1 and the factorization trick via conditioning on mj are correct and useful. The ECPF (Eq. 6) obtained by marginalizing the beta process appears consistent with beta-process Lévy measure calculus. The induced random count matrix prior (Eq. 7) with a Poisson number of columns and column-wise Dirichlet–multinomial and “digamma” size distribution (Eq. 8) is well-motivated and matches prior Poisson factorization views. The group-size dependent EPPF (Eq. 9) and the prediction rule (Eq. 10) are plausible and practically valuable; proofs are deferred to the appendix. Overall the derivations look careful and internally consistent.
- Algorithms: The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the topic model (Eq. 12) cleanly combines word likelihood, global topic popularity, and document-specific dispersion. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism is a distinctive feature relative to HDP’s additive form.
- Experiments: Three corpora, multiple η settings, convergence diagnostics, and a perplexity protocol are provided; code is available. Comparisons are primarily to HDP-LDA with the direct assignment sampler. Results support claims on mixing speed and compactness; perplexity comparison is nuanced and discussed.

Points that could be strengthened:
- Baselines: A comparison to the standard collapsed CRF sampler for HDP (marginalizing G̃, with/without table counts) would be a stronger and fairer baseline than the direct assignment sampler, especially for mixing speed and perplexity.
- Sensitivity: Limited analysis of sensitivity to c, γ0 priors, and to learning rj (though rj are inferred). Reporting posterior summaries for these hyperparameters and ablations (e.g., fixed rj) would clarify their practical impact, given the central role of rj highlighted by Fig. 1.
- Complexity: A clearer per-iteration time and space complexity in terms of nonzeros and active topics would improve reproducibility; an algorithm box would help.
- Evaluation protocol: Perplexity estimation is standard but could be complemented by document completion or left-to-right evaluation. Matching the number of topics across methods via η is indirect; an alternative is to calibrate α for HDP to align topic counts and compare perplexities directly.

Clarity
The paper is dense but largely clear. Strengths include: explicit lemmas, a coherent narrative from ECPF to EPPF to prediction rule, and a concrete application. Weaknesses: heavy notation, some concepts (e.g., “digamma” distribution) merit earlier and more intuitive exposition; the EPPF’s sample-size dependence (non-standard vs. Pitman’s EPPF) could be emphasized more prominently to avoid confusion; and several typos/minor formatting issues. A schematic figure contrasting CRF vs. the proposed construction would aid intuition. A concise pseudocode for the sampler and a summary of all conditionals in the main text (not only in the appendix) would help.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first derivation of a group-size dependent EPPF for BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, yielding a practical, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the infinite beta process. The construction (randomize group sizes → ECPF → EPPF via Bayes) is conceptually neat and potentially extensible to other integer-valued processes (GNBP, gamma–Poisson). The prediction rule is simple and useful.

Significance
The theoretical contribution fills a notable gap: an EPPF-based, truncation-free, fully collapsed inference mechanism for BNBP mixed-membership models. Practically, the topic model instantiation is competitive, and the faster mixing/compactness are appealing. The work will likely influence follow-on research on other count processes and mixed-membership priors and provide a tool practitioners can adopt (code provided). The main limitation is that empirical gains are moderate and not uniformly superior in perplexity; however, the methodological advance is substantial.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution: ECPF and group-size dependent EPPF for BNBP; simple prediction rule.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the beta process; straightforward implementation.
- Solid empirical evidence of faster convergence/mixing and compact topic representations; open-source code.
- Potentially generalizable methodology to other integer-valued processes.

Cons
- Baseline comparison omits the collapsed CRF sampler for HDP; comparisons focus on direct assignment.
- Limited sensitivity/ablation studies for key hyperparameters (c, γ0) and the role of rj.
- Notation density and reliance on appendices reduce accessibility; minor clarity issues around the “digamma” distribution and sample-size dependence of the EPPF.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please compare to the collapsed CRF sampler for HDP (and/or report results with tuned α to match topic counts).
- Clarify whether c is inferred; if fixed, provide guidance and sensitivity analysis. Report posterior summaries for γ0 and rj and their effect on topic counts/perplexity.
- Provide a concise algorithm box and complexity discussion in the main text.
- Expand intuition on the sample-size dependent EPPF and the “digamma” distribution; consider adding a small synthetic example illustrating Eq. (10).

Overall recommendation
The paper is technically strong, original, and significant, with a useful practical algorithm. Despite some empirical and clarity limitations, I recommend acceptance (leaning accept).
