Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) as a prior for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data. While classical nonparametric priors such as the Dirichlet process (DP) and broader NRMIs admit EPPFs for single-sample mixture models (e.g., CRP), there has not been a unified EPPF treatment for mixed-membership (multiple exchangeable groups with shared clusters). Prior work on mixed-membership includes the HDP, which attains collapsed inference via the Chinese restaurant franchise (CRF), and several count-based processes (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP) that have appealing augmentation properties but lacked an explicit marginal partition law enabling fully collapsed inference.

The paper’s main contribution is to derive the marginal law of the BNBP that governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. The authors first construct a group-size dependent model, introducing an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint distribution over random group sizes and their partitions, by augmenting the BNBP as a beta–gamma–Poisson hierarchy. They then marginalize the beta process to obtain a finite-dimensional distribution (the ECPF) and, via Bayes’ rule conditioning on observed group sizes, derive a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP. From this, they obtain a simple prediction rule for simulating partitions.

Leveraging this EPPF, the paper constructs a BNBP topic model that analytically marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process to yield a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with closed-form updates. The sampler is compared against the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler across three corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12). The results suggest faster convergence and better mixing for the BNBP sampler, and comparable or lower perplexity than HDP-LDA at similar topic counts, especially for moderate Dirichlet topic priors.

The work is situated relative to: (i) classical EPPFs (Pitman) and sample-size dependent EPPFs, (ii) HDP and its CRF-based collapsed inference, (iii) recent count-matrix priors and negative-binomial IBP variants that focus on infinite count matrices rather than mixed-membership with fixed group sizes, and (iv) Poisson–Kingman samplers that do not encompass the BNBP.

Quality:
- Theory: The augmentation Xj|Θj ∼ PP(Θj), Θj|B ∼ ΓP[rj, B/(1−B)], B ∼ BP(c,B0) is standard and leads to factorization enabling marginalization over Θj. The ECPF expression and the induced prior over count matrices with KJ ∼ Pois{γ0[ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c)]} and column counts drawn from a digamma–Dirichlet-multinomial construction are consistent with known BNBP properties. The derivations appear sound; key proofs are deferred to the Appendix.
- EPPF and prediction rule: The group-size dependent EPPF (not satisfying the addition rule) is appropriate for mixed-membership with observed group sizes, aligning with sample-size dependent EPPF literature. The prediction rule is simple and interpretable, combining a global popularity factor and a group-specific factor multiplicatively.
- Algorithm: The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a clear consequence of the derived EPPF, with closed-form updates for latent assignments and hyperparameters (details in the Appendix). The complexity claims and potential for sparse acceleration mirror LDA practice.
- Experiments: The evaluation uses standard held-out perplexity with posterior sampling. The comparison to HDP-LDA uses the direct assignment sampler (partially collapsed), which is known to mix more slowly than CRF-based collapsed samplers, potentially biasing convergence/mixing comparisons. Nevertheless, the reported perplexities versus number of topics are informative. Hyperparameter sensitivity (notably c, γ0, rj priors) is limited; η is varied extensively.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized. The progression from ECPF to EPPF to prediction rule to sampler is clear. Notation is dense; some equations (e.g., the new-cluster term in the prediction rule) would benefit from clarifying constants and normalizations. Minor typographical issues exist. The introduction and related work appropriately position the contribution. Providing more intuition for the “digamma” distribution and its role would aid readability.

Originality:
- Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting appears novel. The ECPF→EPPF route for integer-valued processes is a useful methodological template.
- The prediction rule and fully collapsed sampler that marginalize the beta process and group-level measures are new in this context.
- The work distinguishes itself from recent combinatorial treatments of BNBP that focus on infinite random count matrices and from Poisson–Kingman frameworks that exclude the BNBP.

Significance:
- The EPPF enables truncation-free, fully collapsed inference for BNBP mixed-membership models, likely to be adopted in topic modeling and other grouped data applications.
- The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism differs from HDP’s additive form and can yield compact models with competitive predictive performance.
- The approach could extend to other integer-valued processes (e.g., gamma–Poisson, GNBP), potentially broadening impact.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical contribution: ECPF and EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership.
- Derives a simple, interpretable prediction rule.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that is fast and mixes well empirically.
- Competitive perplexity with fewer topics on several corpora.
- Code availability improves reproducibility.

Cons:
- Baseline choice: comparison to HDP-LDA uses the direct assignment sampler rather than CRF-based collapsed variants; this may understate HDP’s mixing speed.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (c, γ0, priors on rj).
- Some notation/equation clarity issues (e.g., constants in the new-cluster probability).
- Scalability evaluation is modest; sparse acceleration is suggested but not implemented.
- Empirical comparison to other nonparametric topic models (e.g., PFA/NB process factor models) is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the prediction rule’s new-cluster term and any dependence on ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c) to avoid ambiguity; ensure all normalizing constants are explicit.
- Include (or discuss) results with a CRF-based collapsed HDP sampler to provide a fairer mixing/convergence comparison.
- Add sensitivity analyses for c, γ0, and the Gamma prior on rj; visualize inferred rj to emphasize their role in dispersion.
- Briefly discuss theoretical implications of sample-size dependent EPPFs for coherence when group sizes change.
- Provide complexity per-iteration and memory usage, and, if possible, a sparse implementation experiment on a larger corpus.

Scope:
The paper is in scope for NeurIPS: Bayesian nonparametrics, probabilistic modeling, and scalable inference for mixed-membership models.

Overall recommendation:
Strong contribution on the theory and inference of BNBP for mixed-membership with solid empirical support. Addressing the baseline and clarity issues would further strengthen the work.
