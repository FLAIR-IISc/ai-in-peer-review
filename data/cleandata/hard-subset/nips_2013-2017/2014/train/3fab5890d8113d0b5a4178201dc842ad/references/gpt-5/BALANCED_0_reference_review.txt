Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling via the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). While the BNBP and related count processes (gamma–Poisson, gamma–NBP) have been used for mixed-membership modeling, prior work lacked marginal laws for the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, which prevented fully collapsed inference (in contrast to the HDP’s Chinese restaurant franchise). The authors make this gap precise and provide:

- An exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that describes the joint distribution of random group sizes and their partitions (Eq. 6), derived by augmenting the BNBP with a gamma–Poisson construction and marginalizing the beta process.
- From the ECPF, a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP (Eq. 9) that induces column-exchangeable random count matrices and an explicit generative description in terms of a Poisson number of i.i.d. columns with digamma-distributed sizes and Dirichlet-multinomial allocations (Eq. 8).
- A simple prediction rule (Eq. 10) to simulate exchangeable random partitions.
- A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process (Eq. 12), contrasted with LDA and HDP-LDA samplers.

Empirically, on JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS12 corpora, the BNBP sampler exhibits faster convergence/mixing than an HDP-LDA direct-assignment baseline and achieves comparable or better perplexity when matched on the number of topics, often with a more compact representation.

This work sits at the intersection of EPPFs and mixed-membership models: it extends classical EPPF ideas (Pitman; size-dependent EPPFs in Zhou & Walker) and is complementary to recent marginal samplers for Poisson–Kingman mixtures (Lomelí et al.), which exclude the BNBP. It also relates to combinatorial analyses of the BNBP/IBP family (Heaukulani & Roy; Griffiths & Ghahramani) and prior BNBP inference via truncation or slice sampling (Zhou et al.; Broderick et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A clear pathway from an integer-valued process to an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling via ECPF and Bayes’ rule. This fills a notable gap compared to the HDP’s CRF story.
- Technical solidity and usefulness: The derivations yield a compact prediction rule and fully collapsed Gibbs updates; the column-i.i.d. representation is insightful and practically relevant.
- Practical impact: A collapsed BNBP topic model that is easy to implement, runs fast, mixes well, and yields state-of-the-art perplexity when using a compact topic set.
- Interpretability: The role of dispersion parameters rj in controlling overdispersion and the multiplicative global–local popularity term give transparent modeling intuition distinct from the HDP’s additive form.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical baselines: The primary baseline is HDP-LDA with a direct-assignment sampler. A stronger comparison would include the CRF collapsed HDP sampler; at minimum, sampling/tuning the HDP concentration α should be included to ensure fairness. Please clarify whether α and global weights were inferred.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are limited to perplexity and three standard corpora. Consider adding topic coherence or downstream evaluations and reporting runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs under matched topic counts.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide sensitivity to c and γ0, and summarize the posterior of rj to support the claims about its impact. An ablation showing fixed vs. learned rj would be informative.
- Clarity of presentation: The “digamma distribution” is uncommon; a short intuitive explanation in the main text (not only appendix) would aid readers. Some notation (e.g., the use of dots for sums, KJ notation, and distinctions between columns and clusters) could be streamlined with a small table of symbols.
- Theoretical framing: While the sample-size dependence is acknowledged, it would help to more fully discuss implications (e.g., addition rule) and relate to when size-dependent EPPFs are preferable/harmless in practice. A brief statement on exchangeability properties (within-group token exchangeability; column exchangeability) would strengthen the narrative.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The constructions appear technically sound; the augmentation and marginalizations are standard and carefully executed. The ECPF/EPPF/prediction rule are consistent and lead to implementable samplers. Some proofs are deferred; given the centrality of Lemma 2 and Eq. 9, a more self-contained sketch could help.
- Clarity: Generally clear but dense. The paper could benefit from more intuition around the key distributions, a running toy example, and a compact notation summary.
- Originality: Strong. To my knowledge, this is the first derivation of an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling with the BNBP along with a fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process, complementing the HDP’s CRF story.
- Significance: High. The method opens the door to fully collapsed inference for a broader class of count-based processes in mixed-membership settings and shows tangible empirical benefits.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and general methodology to obtain an EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with strong practical advantages and clear interpretability.
- Solid empirical performance and faster mixing compared to a widely used baseline.
- Potential to generalize to other count processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP).

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical comparison could be stronger (missing CRF-collapsed HDP or tuned α).
- Limited evaluation metrics and sensitivity analyses.
- Some clarity issues in notation and exposition of uncommon distributions.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and practical sampler are significant and relevant to the NeurIPS community. Strengthening the empirical comparisons (especially HDP baselines) and improving clarity would elevate the paper further.
