

# Peer Review: "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on mixed-membership modeling by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While nonparametric Bayesian priors like the Dirichlet process have been well-studied for mixture modeling, their extension to mixed-membership settings (where data is grouped and each group is partitioned into shared clusters) has lacked a unified theoretical treatment. The authors develop a novel method to derive the BNBP's EPPF by first randomizing group sizes, deriving a joint distribution, and then using Bayes' rule to arrive at the desired marginal distribution. They demonstrate this approach through a BNBP topic model that achieves state-of-the-art performance with a compact representation of the corpus. This work builds on previous NIPS contributions like the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) [6] and the beta-negative binomial process [10, 11], but fills a critical theoretical gap that has prevented fully collapsed inference for BNBP-based models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by deriving the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling, which had been an open problem. The authors' method of randomizing group sizes to derive the EPPF is novel and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2. **Technical Rigor**: The derivations are mathematically sound, with careful attention to the properties of the beta process and negative binomial process. The paper properly references related work, including the EPPF framework from [5] and the BNBP's connection to the Indian buffet process [10, 16, 17].

3. **Practical Impact**: The BNBP topic model demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods like HDP-LDA. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler converges faster, mixes better, and achieves comparable or better predictive performance with fewer topics, making it more efficient for real-world applications.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section thoroughly evaluates the model across multiple corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS) with careful attention to parameter sensitivity, convergence diagnostics, and comparison with relevant baselines.

## Weaknesses

1. **Complexity of EPPF**: While the authors successfully derive the EPPF, it is quite complex (Equation 9). This complexity may hinder practical adoption, as the paper does not provide clear guidance on how to efficiently compute or approximate it for large-scale applications.

2. **Limited Theoretical Comparison**: The paper contrasts the BNBP's multiplicative topic popularity control with HDP's additive approach (Equation 12 vs. 14), but doesn't provide deeper theoretical analysis of how this difference affects the model's statistical properties or generalization.

3. **Implementation Details**: While the paper claims the implementation is "straightforward," the computational complexity analysis is limited. It would be valuable to have more details about the scaling behavior with respect to document size, vocabulary size, and number of documents.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that the dispersion parameters {r_j} play a critical role (Figure 1), but doesn't provide guidance on how to set or estimate them in practice beyond the prior choices.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- The paper fills a significant theoretical gap in mixed-membership modeling that has been unaddressed since the introduction of EPPFs for single-group modeling.
- The derived EPPF is mathematically sound and leads to a practical model with clear advantages over existing approaches.
- The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance with a more compact representation of the corpus.
- The method to derive the EPPF could inspire similar developments for other stochastic processes.

**Against Acceptance:**
- The complexity of the EPPF may limit its practical utility despite the model's strong performance.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how to handle the computational challenges that might arise with very large datasets.
- The connection to broader theoretical implications beyond topic modeling is not fully explored.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a well-known gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model provides a clear advance over existing methods in terms of both theoretical grounding and practical performance. While the EPPF is complex, the authors provide a practical prediction rule (Equation 10) that makes the model implementable and demonstrates its effectiveness through thorough experiments.

The paper's focus on mixed-membership modeling aligns well with NIPS' interest in probabilistic modeling and nonparametric Bayesian methods. The authors have carefully positioned their work relative to previous contributions and have provided a compelling case for the novelty and significance of their approach.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, offering both theoretical insights and practical algorithms that could influence future work in mixed-membership modeling. The authors have made a strong case for the importance of their contribution to the field, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of its value.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the EPPF could be approximated for large-scale applications to make the theoretical contribution more practically accessible.

2. Provide more detail on the computational complexity of the BNBP Gibbs sampler relative to HDP-LDA, particularly for very large datasets.

3. Include a brief discussion of how the dispersion parameters {r_j} could be estimated rather than fixed, as this appears to be a critical aspect of the model's behavior (Figure 1).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling and mixed-membership applications.
