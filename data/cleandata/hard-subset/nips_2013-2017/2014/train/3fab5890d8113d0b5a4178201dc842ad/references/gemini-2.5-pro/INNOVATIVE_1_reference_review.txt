**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of deriving the marginal distribution of exchangeable random partitions for mixed-membership models based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While nonparametric priors like the Dirichlet Process have well-understood marginals (e.g., the Chinese Restaurant Process), leading to efficient collapsed inference schemes, such a framework has been elusive for the BNBP. The authors make a significant theoretical breakthrough by deriving the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP in the context of grouped data. Their approach is highly novel: instead of a direct derivation, they introduce an auxiliary model where group sizes are random. This allows them to first derive an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF) for the joint distribution of group sizes and their partitions. By marginalizing out the group sizes and applying Bayes' rule, they successfully recover the desired EPPF, conditioned on the observed group sizes.

Building on this foundational result, the authors derive a simple and elegant prediction rule for the BNBP. This rule enables the construction of the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership models, which they demonstrate by developing a BNBP topic model. This sampler analytically marginalizes out all infinite-dimensional components, including the beta process itself. Experimental results on several text corpora show that the proposed sampler converges faster and mixes better than the popular Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) sampler. Furthermore, the BNBP topic model achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance, particularly when a compact topic representation is desired.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The core contribution—the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—is a substantial and original theoretical advance. This result fills a major gap in the literature, providing a formal basis for collapsed inference in a class of models where it was previously unavailable. The technique of randomizing group sizes to derive the ECPF as an intermediate step is clever, elegant, and potentially generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening up new avenues for research. This is precisely the kind of bold, foundational work that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Practical Impact and A New State-of-the-Art Sampler:** The theoretical derivation is not merely an academic exercise. It directly leads to a simple, intuitive, and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The ability to marginalize out the infinite-dimensional beta process is a significant practical advantage over methods relying on truncation or slice sampling. The resulting BNBP topic model presents a compelling and mechanistically distinct alternative to the HDP. The empirical results strongly support the claims of faster convergence and better mixing, which are critical for practical applications.

3.  **Insightful Model Properties:** The paper provides a clear comparison between the derived sampler's update rule and those of LDA and HDP-LDA. Highlighting the BNBP's *multiplicative* combination of global and local topic popularities, in contrast to the HDP's *additive* mechanism, offers a deep insight into why these models behave differently. This helps explain the BNBP's stronger shrinkage properties and its tendency to find more compact representations, as validated by the experiments.

4.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors guide the reader through a complex derivation with admirable clarity, motivating each step. The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and directly addresses the most important comparisons with the de facto standard, HDP-LDA.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complexity of the EPPF Expression:** While the derivation is a major achievement, the final expression for the EPPF (Eq. 9) is extremely complex and involves a difficult-to-compute normalization constant. Its direct analytical or computational utility is therefore limited. The paper correctly focuses on the resulting simple prediction rule (Eq. 10), which is the real practical payoff. However, it's worth acknowledging that the EPPF itself is more of a theoretical stepping stone than a practical tool.

2.  **Limited Scope of Generality Claim:** The authors suggest that their method for deriving the EPPF could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, such as the gamma-Poisson process. While this is an exciting prospect, the paper does not provide further evidence or a sketch of how this would be accomplished. This is a minor point, as the contribution for the BNBP stands on its own, but it leaves this intriguing claim as purely speculative for now.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant theoretical result by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting.
*   **Pro:** The methodological approach of using an ECPF with random group sizes is a bold and creative solution to a difficult problem.
*   **Pro:** It introduces the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for this model class, which is shown empirically to have superior convergence and mixing properties compared to the widely-used HDP.
*   **Pro:** The BNBP topic model is a new, competitive algorithm that offers a different shrinkage mechanism and excels at finding compact, high-quality representations of data.
*   **Pro:** The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and has the potential to have a major impact on the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and topic modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The derived EPPF formula is analytically intractable for most practical purposes, though this is mitigated by the simple prediction rule it enables.

**Recommendation**

This is an outstanding paper that combines deep theoretical insight with significant practical implications. The novel derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a landmark contribution that solves a long-standing open problem. The resulting fully collapsed sampler is not only elegant but also demonstrably superior to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and mixing. This work provides a powerful new tool for mixed-membership modeling and opens up exciting new research directions. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the importance of the contributions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
