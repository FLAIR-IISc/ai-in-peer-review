Summary
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling and derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for grouped data. The key idea is to randomize group sizes, derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by marginalizing out the beta process, and then use Bayes’ rule to obtain a group-size-dependent EPPF for a latent column-exchangeable random count matrix. The authors also provide a prediction rule for simulating partitions, and build a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process. Experiments on three corpora compare the proposed model with HDP-LDA (direct assignment) using held-out perplexity.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to the Dirichlet process EPPF/CRP framework, NRMIs, and recent integer-valued BNP processes for mixed-membership (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP). Prior BNBP work has given predictive structures (e.g., negative-binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet) and inference via truncation or slice sampling, but not an EPPF for mixed-membership partitions. The paper’s derivation of a size-dependent EPPF and fully collapsed sampler fills a gap relative to HDP’s CRF-based collapse and to recent Poisson–Kingman samplers that do not cover BNBP or mixed-membership.

Strengths
- A principled derivation: The ECPF and the group-size-dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership are carefully constructed via augmentation and marginalization. The prediction rule is simple and intuitive.
- Collapsed inference: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP topic modeling that marginalizes the beta process is technically interesting and practically valuable.
- Conceptual clarity: The augmented beta–gamma–Poisson view and the role of dispersion parameters rj in controlling overdispersion are clearly articulated.
- Empirical indications: On three datasets, the sampler appears to converge and mix faster than the HDP direct assignment sampler; for comparable topic counts, BNBP can achieve similar or lower perplexity with a more compact representation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines and evaluation: The empirical comparison is restricted to the HDP-LDA direct-assignment sampler. The most relevant alternative for a fair comparison is the HDP’s fully collapsed Chinese restaurant franchise sampler (and modern sparse/accelerated implementations). Without this, claims of faster convergence/mixing and state-of-the-art predictive performance are not well substantiated. LDA with tuned K (and modern sparse samplers) and other BNP count models (e.g., GNBP) are also missing.
- Evaluation methodology: Perplexity is computed by randomly splitting words within documents and sampling φ, θ from the posterior. Wallach et al. (2009) argued for left-to-right evaluation to avoid optimistic bias; it is cited but not used. No sensitivity analysis for c, γ0 priors or for rj is reported, despite rj being central to the model’s behavior.
- Theoretical soundness and scope: The EPPF is explicitly sample-size/group-size dependent (following [Zhou & Walker, 2014]). While this is acknowledged, more discussion is needed on implications for exchangeability and additivity (e.g., limitations for predictive use when group sizes vary or expand). The measure-theoretic steps (e.g., integrability conditions ensuring finite expected K, handling of Q(A) = −∑ ln(1−pk)) are deferred to the appendix; the main text would benefit from explicit conditions under which the results hold (on c, γ0, r·).
- Novelty vs. near-contemporaneous work: There is overlap with work on the combinatorial structure of BNBP and random count matrices (e.g., Heaukulani & Roy; Zhou et al. 2014). The paper should sharpen how its EPPF and prediction rule differ from and improve upon those (beyond focusing on mixed-membership rather than feature allocation).
- Practicality and robustness: The algorithmic claims are based on unoptimized Matlab implementations and one CPU; no wall-clock comparisons, memory profiles, or scaling studies are provided. The method’s sensitivity to η and to hyperpriors (a0, b0, e0, f0) is not explored. The figures suggest strong dependence on rj, yet no systematic inference diagnostics for rj are shown.

Quality
- The derivations appear technically solid but hinge on appendix proofs and known identities (digamma/DirMult). The experimental section is not strong enough to validate the claimed advantages; critical baselines are missing and evaluation protocols could be stronger. Claims of “state-of-the-art” are not supported by comprehensive comparisons.

Clarity
- Generally well written with clear motivation and organization. However, the paper is notation-heavy; the EPPF’s dependence on group sizes and its consequences should be highlighted earlier. Including a succinct theorem statement with assumptions would help. The description of the “digamma distribution” could be clarified to avoid confusion.

Originality
- Deriving a group-size-dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership and a fully collapsed sampler are novel and nontrivial. The general recipe (randomize sizes → ECPF → EPPF) may be useful beyond BNBP.

Significance
- Potentially significant for BNP mixed-membership modeling by enabling collapsed inference without CRF-style bookkeeping. However, the current empirical validation is insufficient for strong claims; broader baselines and more rigorous evaluation are needed to establish impact.

Questions for the authors
1) Please compare against the HDP CRF collapsed sampler (and/or modern sparse implementations) in both speed/mixing and perplexity.  
2) Can you provide left-to-right perplexity or document completion evaluation?  
3) What are the precise conditions on (c, γ0, r·) ensuring finiteness of K and validity of the EPPF?  
4) How sensitive are results to priors on rj, c, γ0? Any ablation or posterior summaries of rj?  
5) How does your approach relate technically to Heaukulani & Roy (2014) and Zhou et al. (2014) on random count matrices beyond the application focus?

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution (ECPF/EPPF/prediction rule) for BNBP mixed-membership.  
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free sampler with clear update rules.  
- Intuitive interpretation and connections to gamma–Poisson augmentation.

Cons
- Empirical validation is narrow; key baselines missing.  
- Evaluation protocol could be stronger (left-to-right, sensitivity, scalability).  
- Theoretical assumptions and implications of size-dependent EPPF not fully discussed in main text.

Recommendation
- Overall: Weak reject. The theoretical development is interesting, but the paper does not meet the empirical rigor and comparative validation expected for NeurIPS. Strengthening baselines (HDP CRF), evaluation methodology, and providing clearer theoretical conditions would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: High.
