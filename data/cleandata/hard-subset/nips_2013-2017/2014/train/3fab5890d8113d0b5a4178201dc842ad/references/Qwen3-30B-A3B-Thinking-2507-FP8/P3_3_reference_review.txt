

# Peer Review: Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling

## Summary

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. While previous work has established EPPFs for single-set mixture models (like the Chinese restaurant process), this paper fills a critical gap by providing the first unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling. The authors develop a group size-dependent model to derive the BNBP's EPPF, construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance with a more compact representation of text corpora compared to HDP-LDA.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The authors provide detailed proofs for all key lemmas, including the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and the EPPF, which are carefully derived using Bayes' rule. The theoretical development builds logically on prior work on exchangeable partitions (e.g., [5, 12]) while extending it to mixed-membership modeling. The experimental section is thorough, using three standard text corpora with appropriate evaluation metrics (perplexity) and comparing against relevant baselines (HDP-LDA, LDA). The authors carefully discuss the trade-offs between BNBP and HDP-LDA, particularly regarding topic count control mechanisms.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF, which has been lacking in previous work on BNBP and GNBP. The authors correctly note that while the BNBP has been used for mixed-membership modeling (e.g., [10, 11]), the marginal distribution governing the exchangeable partitions was unknown until now. The derivation method—first randomizing group sizes and then using Bayes' rule to arrive at the EPPF—represents a novel technique that could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to practical implementation. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, though some readers may find the EPPF (equation 9) complex. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 1 showing how dispersion parameters affect partition structures, Figure 2 demonstrating faster convergence of the BNBP sampler). The comparison between BNBP and HDP-LDA in terms of topic control mechanisms (multiplicative vs. additive) is particularly well-explained.

One minor weakness in clarity is the somewhat dense treatment of the augmented representation (Section 2.1), which could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the Poisson process augmentation is necessary for deriving the EPPF. The connection between the BNBP and the topic model could also be made more explicit earlier in the paper.

## Originality

This paper makes a strong original contribution by addressing a significant gap in the literature. While the BNBP has been used for mixed-membership modeling in prior work ([7, 10, 11]), the authors are the first to derive its EPPF, which is essential for understanding the prior distribution of exchangeable random partitions in mixed-membership contexts. The method to derive the EPPF via a group size-dependent model is novel and could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The BNBP topic model itself is a new contribution, distinct from existing nonparametric topic models in its mechanism for controlling topic counts (multiplicative rather than additive).

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly from the HDP-LDA ([6, 22]) and the beta-negative binomial process as used in [10, 11]. The authors cite relevant prior work appropriately, though they could have more explicitly contrasted their approach with the "ice cream" buffet process ([17]).

## Significance

This work has significant implications for nonparametric Bayesian modeling of grouped data. The BNBP topic model offers practical advantages over HDP-LDA, including faster convergence, better mixing properties, and a more compact representation of text corpora with comparable or better predictive performance. The ability to achieve state-of-the-art results without needing to tune hyperparameters like the number of topics (K) or topic smoothing parameter (α) is particularly valuable for practitioners.

The theoretical contribution of deriving the BNBP's EPPF fills a critical gap in the literature and opens the door to further research on mixed-membership modeling using other integer-valued stochastic processes. The method described could be generalized to derive EPPFs for other processes like the gamma-negative binomial process (GNBP), potentially leading to new models with different properties.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. First derivation of the EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership modeling
2. Novel method for deriving EPPFs via group size-dependent models
3. Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out infinite-dimensional beta process
4. Clear theoretical explanation of the multiplicative topic control mechanism
5. Thorough experimental validation across multiple corpora with convincing results

**Weaknesses:**
1. The EPPF (equation 9) is quite complex, which may limit immediate practical adoption
2. Limited discussion of how to select the dispersion parameters rj (though the authors note they should be inferred rather than set heuristically)
3. The paper doesn't explore how the method might generalize to other integer-valued stochastic processes beyond the brief mention in the conclusion
4. Some technical details in the theoretical derivations (e.g., the augmented representation in Section 2.1) could be made more intuitive

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling and provides a practical, well-performing topic model with clear advantages over existing approaches. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF fills a critical gap in the literature, and the resulting BNBP topic model demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with faster convergence and a more compact representation.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a demonstrable advance in the state of the art. While the EPPF is complex, the authors provide a clear prediction rule (Lemma 4) that enables practical implementation. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the BNBP topic model over HDP-LDA.

I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS. This work is both theoretically rigorous and practically valuable, and it addresses an important gap in the literature on mixed-membership modeling. The method to derive the EPPF could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening up new research avenues in the field.
