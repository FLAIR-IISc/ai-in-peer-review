This paper investigates the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The authors aim to provide a unified treatment for this area, analogous to the EPPF’s role in single-dataset mixture modeling. They derive the BNBP’s EPPF, a prediction rule, and demonstrate its application through a topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The work builds upon prior research in nonparametric Bayesian methods, particularly Dirichlet processes, NRMI’s, and the Chinese Restaurant Process, extending these concepts to the BNBP and mixed-membership settings. It also relates to recent work on alternative stochastic processes like the gamma-Poisson and gamma-negative binomial processes, highlighting the BNBP’s unique characteristics.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** Deriving the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant theoretical achievement. This provides a foundational understanding of the BNBP’s behavior in mixed-membership modeling, something previously lacking. The derivation process, starting from randomizing group sizes, is well-motivated.
* **Practical Application:** The development of the BNBP topic model and the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler demonstrates the practical utility of the derived EPPF.  A fully collapsed sampler is highly desirable for efficiency and scalability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, contributions, and connections to existing literature. The mathematical derivations, while complex, are presented with sufficient detail.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on three benchmark corpora demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in scenarios where a compact representation of the corpus is desired. The comparison to HDP-LDA is insightful, revealing trade-offs between model complexity and predictive accuracy. The convergence analysis and mixing tests provide convincing evidence of the sampler’s reliability.
* **Novelty:** The group size dependent EPPF derivation and its application to mixed membership modeling represent a novel contribution. The multiplicative combination of terms in the Gibbs sampler (compared to the additive approach in HDP-LDA) is an interesting distinction.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical derivations and the resulting EPPF are quite complex, potentially hindering wider adoption of the method. While the authors attempt to provide clarity, some readers may find it difficult to fully grasp the intricacies.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance appears sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (η). The discussion of parameter tuning could be more extensive, potentially offering guidance on selecting appropriate values in different scenarios.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the results on the three datasets are promising, the evaluation focuses solely on perplexity. Exploring other evaluation metrics (e.g., topic coherence, qualitative analysis of topics) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s performance.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the Gibbs sampler is “fully collapsed”, the experimental results report a relatively high runtime (2.5 seconds per iteration on the NIPS12 corpus). This might become a bottleneck for larger datasets. Discussion of computational complexity and potential optimization strategies (e.g., parallelization) is limited.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper references previous work on the negative binomial process and its applications to modeling counts and mixed membership. However, the connection to broader research areas such as spatial statistics and disease mapping, where negative binomial models are frequently employed, is not explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contribution of deriving the EPPF for the BNBP is substantial and addresses a gap in the literature.
* The practical application to topic modeling demonstrates the utility of the theoretical results and provides a valuable tool for researchers in NLP and related fields.
* The empirical validation on benchmark corpora provides strong evidence of the model's performance.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical complexity of the EPPF may limit its accessibility and widespread adoption.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameter settings and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation raise some concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
* The computational cost, although reasonable, might become a bottleneck for very large datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a novel and valuable theoretical result. The application to topic modeling demonstrates the practical utility of the method, and the empirical validation is convincing.  While the complexity of the mathematical derivations and the sensitivity to hyperparameters are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper for publication in NIPS.  The authors should consider addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Expand the discussion of hyperparameter selection:** Provide more guidance on choosing appropriate values for the hyperparameters, potentially including a sensitivity analysis.
2.  **Include additional evaluation metrics:**  Supplement the perplexity results with other evaluation metrics, such as topic coherence, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s performance.
3. **Address Computational Complexity**: Discuss further opportunities for computational optimization, such as parallelization techniques.
4. **Clarify limitations:** Explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity.
