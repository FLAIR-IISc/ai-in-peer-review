## Peer Review for "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling. The authors tackle the challenging problem of defining an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) in a grouped data setting. They derive a novel EPPF by first randomizing group sizes and then leveraging Bayes’ rule.  Crucially, they provide a prediction rule for simulating partitions governed by this EPPF and demonstrate its utility by constructing a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The work builds upon the existing literature on EPPFs and integer-valued stochastic processes like the BNBP, filling a gap in unified treatment for mixed-membership models analogous to the unified treatment of mixture models provided by EPPFs.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of nonparametric Bayesian methods. It builds on foundational work by Ferguson (Dirichlet Process), Lijoi & Prünster (NRMIs), Pitman (exchangeable partitions), and Teh et al. (HDP).  The authors acknowledge the existing literature on applying the BNBP to mixed-membership modeling (Zhou et al., Broderick et al.) but highlight the lack of a corresponding EPPF that enables fully collapsed inference, a key motivator for their work. They also appropriately compare their work to existing topic modeling approaches like LDA and HDP-LDA.  The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for grouped data is a significant theoretical result. It provides a principled framework for understanding the exchangeable partitions generated by the BNBP in this context.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The derived prediction rule and fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model provide a computationally efficient and tractable approach for inference. The closed-form update equations are a considerable advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on standard topic modeling datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors show that the BNBP topic model can achieve state-of-the-art performance while often using a more compact representation (fewer topics) than HDP-LDA.
* **Clear Presentation:**  Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The authors clearly define the key concepts and provide sufficient detail for understanding the methodology.
* **Convergence & Mixing:** The demonstrated fast convergence and good mixing properties of the Gibbs sampler are crucial for practical application. The comparisons to HDP-LDA in Figures 2 are particularly convincing.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of EPPF:** While a major achievement, the derived EPPF (Equation 9) appears quite complex, potentially limiting its immediate usability in some applications. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of potential simplifications or approximations could be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of the topic model (and likely other applications) is still dependent on hyperparameters like `c`, `a0`, `b0`, `e0`, and `f0`.  A more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results. Although the authors chose reasonable values, a more detailed exploration of their impact would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on topic modeling. While this is a good starting point, expanding the empirical evaluation to other mixed-membership modeling tasks (e.g., image segmentation, collaborative filtering) would further demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed approach.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Although stating the BNBP sampler is comparable in complexity to HDP-LDA, a more detailed computational analysis would be helpful.  Specifically, a discussion of memory usage and scalability to larger datasets would be valuable.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem in nonparametric Bayesian statistics.
* The derived EPPF and associated algorithms open up new possibilities for inference in mixed-membership models.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its competitiveness with existing methods.
* The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler offers a practical and efficient inference procedure.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the derived EPPF might hinder its widespread adoption.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian statistics. The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a significant theoretical achievement, and the resulting algorithms are both practically useful and computationally efficient.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Provide a brief discussion of potential simplifications or approximations to the EPPF to improve its usability.
* Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Consider expanding the empirical evaluation to other mixed-membership modeling tasks.
* Include a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed approach.
