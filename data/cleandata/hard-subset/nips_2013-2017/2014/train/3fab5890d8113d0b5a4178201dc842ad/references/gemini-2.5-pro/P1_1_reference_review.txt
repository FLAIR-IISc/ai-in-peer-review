This paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling. The central idea is to derive the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), providing a unified framework for understanding the partitioning structure it induces on grouped data. This is analogous to how the Chinese restaurant process and its generalization to EPPFs provided a clear combinatorial view of the Dirichlet process for single-set clustering. The authors achieve this through a novel, indirect method: they first define a model where group sizes are random to derive an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF), and then use Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes, yielding the desired EPPF.

This theoretical result is then leveraged to build a BNBP topic model and derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. This is a key practical advance, as previous inference for BNBP-based models relied on less efficient methods like finite truncation or slice sampling. The resulting sampler analytically integrates out the infinite-dimensional beta process, leading to a simple and elegant prediction rule. The paper compares this model to the widely-used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), highlighting differences in their combinatorial structures and shrinkage properties.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the context of mixed-membership modeling is a novel and important contribution. It fills a notable gap in the literature, as such combinatorial descriptions were previously unavailable for the BNBP, hindering the development of fully collapsed inference schemes. This work provides a deeper understanding of the BNBP's properties and opens the door for similar analyses of other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the ECPF and its subsequent use to obtain the EPPF and the predictive rule is clever and well-executed. The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant practical achievement. It avoids the bookkeeping complexities of the HDP's Chinese Restaurant Franchise and is more statistically efficient than previous BNBP inference methods.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide strong evidence for the claims made. The trace plots in Figure 2 compellingly demonstrate the faster convergence and better mixing of the proposed BNBP sampler compared to the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler. The perplexity results in Figure 3 are presented with nuance and honesty. They show that while HDP-LDA can achieve lower perplexity with a very large number of topics (driven by a very small η), the BNBP topic model can achieve comparable or better perplexity with a more compact representation, which is often more desirable in practice. This highlights a key difference in the shrinkage properties of the two models.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction. The comparison of the BNBP prediction rule (Eq. 12) with those for LDA (Eq. 13) and HDP-LDA (Eq. 14) is particularly insightful, as it crystallizes the difference between the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism and the HDP's additive one. This comparison makes the distinct behaviors of the models intuitive.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Density of Material:** The paper is quite dense, and a full appreciation of the derivations requires substantial background in nonparametric Bayesian statistics. While the logic is sound, more intuition could be provided for the intermediate steps. For instance, a brief intuitive explanation of why randomizing the group sizes is a necessary or useful intermediate step would help the reader grasp the core of the methodological innovation more quickly.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The comparison is exclusively with HDP-LDA. While HDP is the most direct and important benchmark, the paper's claims of state-of-the-art performance would be strengthened by comparison to other recent nonparametric topic models, or at least to other inference schemes for the BNBP (e.g., the slice sampler from Broderick et al. [11]). This would help isolate the benefits of the BNBP model itself versus the benefits of the fully collapsed sampler.

3.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation is based solely on held-out perplexity. While this is a standard metric, it does not always correlate well with human judgment of topic quality. Including a qualitative analysis of the discovered topics or quantitative metrics like topic coherence would provide a more complete picture of the model's performance.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution on both theoretical and practical fronts. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is an elegant theoretical result that advances our understanding of this important process. The development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant practical result that leads to a topic model with fast convergence, good mixing properties, and excellent predictive performance, particularly when a compact model representation is desired. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to adding more intuition and broadening the experimental scope, which does not detract from the core contributions. The work is novel, significant, and of high interest to the NIPS community working on Bayesian nonparametrics and topic modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
