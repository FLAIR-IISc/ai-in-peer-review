

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by developing a group size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors address an important gap in the literature: while EPPFs have been well-studied for single-set mixture modeling, there has been no unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling where data is grouped and each group has its own size. The paper's core contribution is a method to derive the EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using integer-valued stochastic processes, with the BNBP as a specific example.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a technically sound derivation of the ECPF and EPPF for the BNBP, which is a significant theoretical advance. The authors correctly identify the limitation of previous work (e.g., HDP) that relies on the Chinese restaurant franchise representation rather than a fully collapsed inference approach.

2. **Practical Model**: The BNBP topic model is well-motivated, intuitive, and straightforward to implement. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process is a major practical contribution.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The paper demonstrates that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA while using fewer topics. The analysis of how dispersion parameters affect clustering behavior (Figure 1) is particularly valuable.

4. **Clear Comparison**: The comparison with HDP-LDA and LDA (Equations 12-14) clearly articulates the key difference in how the models control topic shrinkage (multiplicative vs. additive), which is an important theoretical insight.

## Weaknesses

1. **Complexity of EPPF**: The EPPF formula (Equation 9) is quite complex and may limit practical adoption. While the prediction rule (Equation 10) is simpler, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the EPPF must be so complicated, which could make it difficult for practitioners to implement or extend.

2. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper cites [17] which also discusses BNBP for random count matrices, but doesn't clearly differentiate its contribution from that work. The relationship between the BNBP topic model and the "ice cream" buffet process mentioned in [17] needs clarification.

3. **Scalability Considerations**: While the paper mentions the computational complexity on the NIPS12 corpus, it doesn't address how the model scales to much larger datasets or how it compares to more recent scalable topic modeling approaches.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper states that the BNBP's EPPF is "dependent on the group sizes" but doesn't fully explore the implications of this dependency compared to traditional EPPFs that are sample-size independent.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by providing a rigorous derivation of the EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using the BNBP. The BNBP topic model demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches, particularly in convergence speed and compact representation. The work fills an important gap in the literature that previous papers on BNBP (e.g., [10, 11, 17]) have not addressed.

The paper meets the quality criteria with sound theoretical derivations and thorough experimental validation. The clarity is generally good, though some complex mathematical expressions could be better motivated. The originality is strong, as the method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling using integer-valued stochastic processes is novel. The significance is high, as the work advances both the theoretical understanding and practical application of mixed-membership models.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Provide more intuition for why the EPPF must be group-size dependent and how this impacts practical applications.

2. Clarify the relationship between this work and [17], particularly regarding the "ice cream" buffet process. The paper should explicitly state how the current contribution differs from and extends this prior work.

3. Add a brief discussion of how the BNBP model scales to very large datasets (e.g., beyond the NIPS12 corpus) and compare computational complexity with other scalable topic models.

4. Consider simplifying the EPPF presentation or providing an alternative representation that might be more accessible to practitioners.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with strong practical implications for mixed-membership modeling. The work addresses an important gap in the literature and provides a novel method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. While the EPPF formula is complex, the prediction rule and practical implementation are well-justified.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling and provides both theoretical insights and practical tools for mixed-membership problems. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has significant potential impact on the field.
