

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper proposes a new mathematical framework for mixed-membership modeling based on the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), claiming to derive its exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for grouped data. The authors present a BNBP topic model that they claim achieves faster convergence, better mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance compared to existing methods like HDP-LDA.

The paper's main technical contribution is the derivation of an EPPF for BNBP, which the authors argue is missing in previous work. They develop a "group size dependent" EPPF that accounts for observed group sizes rather than random ones, and propose a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions. The BNBP topic model is presented as a nonparametric Bayesian alternative to HDP-LDA that eliminates the need for tuning parameters like the number of topics K and the topic proportion Dirichlet smoothing parameter α.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a technically rigorous derivation of the BNBP EPPF, which appears mathematically sound. The authors demonstrate a clear connection between the BNBP and existing stochastic processes like the gamma-Poisson process. The implementation details for the collapsed Gibbs sampler are well-specified, and the experimental results on three standard corpora show reasonable performance.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's primary weakness is its lack of convincing evidence that the proposed BNBP framework provides meaningful advantages over established methods. While the authors claim "state-of-the-art predictive performance," their results show that for the same η values, the BNBP topic model generally has higher perplexity than HDP-LDA (Figure 3, middle column), which contradicts their main claim. The paper then attempts to explain this by arguing that BNBP achieves similar predictive performance with fewer topics, but this is not demonstrated with rigorous statistical testing. The authors don't provide a convincing explanation for why their method would be preferred in practice when it appears to have worse predictive performance under standard evaluation metrics.

The paper also fails to adequately address the computational cost of their approach. While they claim their sampler converges faster than HDP-LDA, they don't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity or scalability to very large datasets, which is crucial for NIPS papers. The paper's claim of "fully collapsed" inference is misleading since they still need to marginalize out the beta process, similar to HDP-LDA.

The paper's contribution to the field is questionable. The BNBP has been previously studied in Zhou et al. (2012) and other papers, and the authors don't sufficiently distinguish their contribution from existing work. The paper's main novelty appears to be the derivation of the EPPF, but the authors don't convincingly establish why this specific EPPF is needed or how it differs meaningfully from the HDP's Chinese restaurant franchise representation. The paper's claim that "none of these stochastic processes are able to describe their marginal distributions" is inaccurate, as the HDP does have a well-established marginal distribution via the Chinese restaurant franchise.

**Quality:** The mathematical derivation is technically sound, but the experimental evaluation is incomplete and fails to provide convincing evidence for the claimed advantages. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the BNBP framework or compare against a broader range of state-of-the-art topic models.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written but suffers from excessive mathematical notation that obscures the main contributions. Key concepts like the "group size dependent EPPF" are not clearly motivated or explained in relation to practical applications.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution appears to be a technical derivation rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors don't sufficiently establish how their work differs from previous studies of BNBP (Zhou et al., 2012; Broderick et al., 2013) or how it addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Significance:** The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the BNBP framework provides a meaningful advance over existing methods. The claimed advantages (faster convergence, better mixing, compact representation) aren't sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence, and the paper fails to show how this would be useful for practitioners.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents technically sound mathematics, the lack of convincing evidence for the claimed advantages over established methods, combined with the failure to properly address the limitations and significance of the work, makes this a weak contribution for NIPS. The paper would need significantly stronger empirical evidence, a clearer demonstration of practical advantages, and a more thorough comparison with existing methods to be considered for acceptance. The paper's claims about "state-of-the-art predictive performance" are not supported by the results presented, which actually show higher perplexity for BNBP compared to HDP-LDA under standard evaluation settings.
