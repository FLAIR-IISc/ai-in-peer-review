**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The primary contribution is a theoretical one: while the BNBP has been used for such models, its marginal distribution governing the random partitions of grouped data was unknown, precluding fully collapsed inference schemes. The authors circumvent the difficulty of direct derivation by first defining a group-size-dependent model and deriving its exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), which describes the joint distribution of random group sizes and their partitions. By marginalizing the group sizes and applying Bayes' rule, they arrive at the desired EPPF. This theoretical development is then applied to construct a BNBP topic model, for which a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is derived. The authors present experimental results on three text corpora, comparing their model to the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), and argue that their sampler exhibits faster convergence and can achieve competitive predictive performance with a more compact topic representation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of an EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting is a novel and important contribution. It bridges a gap in the literature on nonparametric Bayesian models, providing a unified EPPF-based perspective for a model that previously lacked one. This potentially opens the door for similar derivations for other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2.  **Novel Methodological Approach:** The technique of first modeling random group sizes via an ECPF and then conditioning on observed sizes to obtain the EPPF is clever. This provides a generalizable recipe for tackling otherwise intractable marginalization problems.

3.  **Practical Application and Algorithm:** The paper successfully translates the theoretical result into a practical algorithm: a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model. The derivation is sound, and the resulting predictive update rule (Eq. 12) is elegant, revealing a distinct multiplicative interaction between global and local topic popularities, which contrasts interestingly with the HDP's additive mechanism.

4.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, effectively demonstrate the key properties of the proposed sampler, namely its fast convergence and mixing compared to a direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA. The analysis of the trade-off between the number of topics and perplexity provides valuable insight into the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the significant contributions, the paper suffers from a number of minor flaws in its presentation, methodological details, and experimental comparison, which in aggregate detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper is notationally dense and the exposition is extremely fast-paced. Key concepts like the ECPF and EPPF, and the transition between them, could benefit from more intuition and a less abrupt presentation. The sentence "organize z into a random count matrix NJ" is a hand-wavy description of a critical step.
    *   The presentation of the EPPF in Eq. (9) is somewhat misleading. The denominator involves a sum over all partitions of the count vector **m**, which is computationally intractable. While the authors correctly avoid this by deriving a simple predictive rule (Lemma 4), the paper should explicitly state the intractability of Eq. (9) and emphasize that the key practical result is the predictive distribution, not the EPPF formula itself.
    *   In the title, the character "￼" appears, suggesting a formatting or encoding error. Footnotes are used for URLs, which is non-standard for this conference. These are minor points, but they reflect a lack of polish.

2.  **Technical and Methodological Nuances:**
    *   The paper mentions the "addition rule" for EPPFs only to state that it is "not required." The implication of using a sample-size-dependent EPPF, which violates this rule, is a fundamental departure from classic models like the Chinese Restaurant Process. A deeper discussion of the statistical consequences of this dependency is warranted.
    *   The comparison with HDP-LDA in Section 3.1 is insightful but incomplete. The paper contrasts its fully collapsed sampler with the HDP's "direct assignment sampler," noting the latter is only partially collapsed. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison. A truly fair comparison of mixing speed would require comparing against a fully collapsed HDP sampler that uses the Chinese Restaurant Franchise (CRF) representation. While the CRF is more complex, it is the appropriate baseline for claims about improved convergence of a fully collapsed algorithm. The observed faster mixing of the BNBP sampler may be partially attributable to this difference in sampling schemes.
    *   The discussion on the multiplicative vs. additive updates could be expanded. The multiplicative form `(n·k / (c+n·k+r·)) * (njk + rj)` implies that a document's affinity for a topic (`njk + rj`) is scaled by the topic's global popularity. This suggests a stronger shrinkage effect where globally rare topics will struggle to be assigned, even if they are a perfect fit for a specific document. This is a significant modeling assumption that deserves more exploration.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The claims of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" are not fully supported. The middle column of Figure 3 shows that for any given hyperparameter η, HDP-LDA consistently achieves lower (better) perplexity. The paper's claim hinges on the right column, arguing that BNBP can achieve lower perplexity for a *fixed number of topics*. This is a valid and interesting result, demonstrating a better perplexity-complexity trade-off. However, it is not equivalent to superior overall predictive performance, as the HDP can simply use more topics to achieve a better absolute perplexity score. The claim should be rephrased more carefully to reflect this nuance (e.g., "state-of-the-art performance for a given model complexity").
    *   The formula for per-word perplexity in Section 4 appears non-standard. The normalization seems to be happening inside the logarithm over the sum of samples, which is unusual. Clarification is needed to ensure the metric is comparable to those reported in other papers.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Novel and theoretically significant derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP.
*   Provides a new, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for mixed-membership modeling.
*   The BNBP topic model offers an interesting multiplicative alternative to the HDP.
*   Demonstrates fast convergence and mixing properties in experiments.

**Cons:**
*   Numerous minor issues with clarity, notation, and presentation.
*   The experimental comparison to HDP-LDA is potentially unfair (fully vs. partially collapsed sampler).
*   Claims of "state-of-the-art" predictive performance are overstated based on the provided results.
*   Lack of deep discussion on the statistical implications of key model properties (e.g., sample-size dependent EPPF, multiplicative updates).
*   Minor formatting errors.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core theoretical idea of this paper is strong and constitutes a solid contribution, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level expected for NIPS. The accumulation of many small issues—ranging from notational opaqueness and formatting errors to an unfair experimental baseline and slightly overstated claims—prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. The work is promising, and I believe a revised version that addresses the weaknesses in clarity, methodological discussion, and experimental rigor would be a very strong candidate for acceptance.
