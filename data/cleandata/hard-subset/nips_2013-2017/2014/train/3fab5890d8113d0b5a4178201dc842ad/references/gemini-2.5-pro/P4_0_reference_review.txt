### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of mixed-membership modeling from a nonparametric Bayesian perspective. It focuses on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), an integer-valued stochastic process, and aims to derive the marginal distribution that governs the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. This is analogous to how the Chinese Restaurant Process represents the marginal distribution of a Dirichlet Process mixture model. The authors note that a unified treatment for mixed-membership models, akin to the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) framework for single-group mixture models, is largely missing.

The core contribution is a novel method to derive the group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP. As a direct derivation is difficult, the authors introduce a clever intermediate step: they first randomize the group sizes (e.g., document lengths) and derive the joint distribution of these random sizes and their partitions over a shared set of clusters. They term this the Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF). By applying Bayes' rule to condition on the observed, fixed group sizes, they then arrive at the desired EPPF. While the resulting EPPF is complex, it leads to a simple and elegant predictive rule for a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.

The authors demonstrate the utility of this framework by constructing a BNBP topic model. They derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically integrates out the infinite-dimensional Beta process, the topics, and the topic weights. They compare this sampler to that of the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), a standard approach for nonparametric topic modeling. The comparison highlights a key difference in the underlying shrinkage mechanisms: the BNBP employs a multiplicative combination of a topic's global and local popularity, whereas the HDP uses an additive one. Experimental results on three text corpora show that the BNBP sampler converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance, particularly when a compact representation of the data is desired.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivations, particularly the sequence leading from the BNBP definition (Eq. 2) to the ECPF (Lemma 2) and finally the predictive rule (Lemma 4), appear sound and are presented logically. This theoretical development is a complete piece of work, successfully deriving the marginal partition structure of the BNBP for mixed-membership models, which was an open gap. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical analysis and the experimental results. The empirical evaluation is rigorous, using a standard perplexity metric on held-out data and comparing against the most relevant baseline (HDP-LDA). The authors are careful to present a balanced view of the results, noting that while HDP-LDA can achieve lower perplexity with a very large number of topics, the BNBP model offers a better trade-off, achieving comparable or superior perplexity with a more compact model. The inclusion of sampler convergence and mixing diagnostics (Fig. 2) is a strength and provides compelling evidence for the efficiency of the proposed inference algorithm.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly situating the work within the broader context of EPPFs and mixed-membership modeling, and it precisely outlines the paper's contributions. The theoretical development is broken down into manageable steps, with each lemma building on the previous one. A particular strength is the direct comparison of the Gibbs update equations for the BNBP model (Eq. 12), LDA (Eq. 13), and HDP-LDA (Eq. 14). This comparison makes the novel aspects of the BNBP's generative process and its distinct shrinkage properties immediately apparent to the reader. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

#### **Originality**

The work is highly original. While the BNBP has been explored previously for related tasks [7, 10], this paper is the first to derive its exact marginal distribution (EPPF) in the context of mixed-membership modeling with fixed group sizes. The methodological approach of first deriving an ECPF by treating group sizes as random and then conditioning is a novel and clever theoretical device that may be applicable to other integer-valued processes. The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant advance over previous inference methods for BNBP-based models, such as finite truncation or slice sampling. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism that emerges from this derivation is a novel contribution and provides a valuable new tool for Bayesian nonparametric modeling, offering a distinct alternative to the additive mechanism of the HDP.

#### **Significance**

The paper makes significant contributions to both theory and practice. Theoretically, it expands the EPPF framework to a non-trivial mixed-membership model, opening a potential path for deriving similar marginals for other processes like the gamma-Poisson process. This deepens our understanding of the combinatorial structures underlying these models. Practically, it provides a new, competitive nonparametric topic model. The derived fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is simple to implement, converges remarkably fast, and mixes well, addressing common practical issues with MCMC inference in HDP models. The model's tendency to find more compact representations for a given level of predictive accuracy is a desirable feature in many applications where interpretability is important. The availability of code further enhances the paper's potential impact.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, filling a notable gap in the literature.
*   The derivation enables the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, which is a significant practical and theoretical advance over previous inference methods.
*   The resulting sampler is shown empirically to converge faster and mix better than the widely used direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA.
*   The model introduces a new multiplicative shrinkage mechanism, providing a principled and compelling alternative to the additive shrinkage of the HDP.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-motivated, and provides strong intuition for the model's properties and performance.

### **Arguments Against Acceptance**

*   The empirical gains in held-out perplexity are not uniformly superior to HDP-LDA across all hyperparameter settings; the primary advantage is framed as achieving better performance for a given model complexity (number of topics).
*   The primary application, topic modeling, is a standard problem, and the work does not introduce a new application domain.
