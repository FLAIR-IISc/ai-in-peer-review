**Summary**

This paper addresses a key challenge in Bayesian nonparametrics: developing efficient, fully collapsed inference schemes for mixed-membership models beyond the well-known Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP). The authors focus on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), a powerful alternative for modeling overdispersed count data in groups. The central contribution is the derivation of the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting. Since a direct derivation is difficult, the authors introduce a novel and clever technique: they first treat the group sizes as random variables, derive the joint distribution of these sizes and their partitions (termed an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function, or ECPF), and then use Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes to obtain the desired EPPF.

This theoretical result is not merely academic; it immediately leads to a simple, intuitive prediction rule for Gibbs sampling. The authors then apply this machinery to develop a BNBP topic model. Crucially, this results in a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out all infinite-dimensional random measures, a feat not achieved by previous inference schemes for BNBP models. The paper culminates with a strong empirical comparison to HDP-LDA on several text corpora, demonstrating that the proposed BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance with more compact (i.e., fewer topics) representations of the data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership context is a novel and important result. The methodological approach of using a sample-size-dependent auxiliary model (the ECPF) to arrive at the desired conditional distribution is elegant and potentially applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening a new avenue for developing collapsed samplers.
2.  **Practical and Efficient Inference:** The theoretical work directly translates into a practical, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. This is a major advantage over prior work on BNBP models which relied on less efficient methods like finite truncation or slice sampling. The resulting prediction rule (Eq. 10) is simple and interpretable.
3.  **Clear Comparison and Insight:** The paper provides an excellent comparison between the derived BNBP sampler and that of the HDP-LDA (Eq. 12 vs. Eq. 14). The analysis highlights the fundamental difference in how the models combine global and local information (multiplicative for BNBP vs. additive for HDP), offering valuable insight into their differing shrinkage properties and behavior.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The trace plots clearly show superior convergence and mixing properties compared to HDP-LDA's direct assignment sampler. Furthermore, the perplexity results demonstrate that the BNBP topic model is not just a theoretical curiosity but a state-of-the-art method that can outperform the HDP, particularly when a compact model representation is desired.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Complexity of the EPPF Formula:** The final expression for the EPPF (Eq. 9) is quite complex, with a normalization constant that involves a sum over all possible partitions of the data. While the authors correctly show that the simple prediction rule obviates the need to ever compute this constant for Gibbs sampling, the intractability of the EPPF itself might limit its use for other inference methods (e.g., variational inference) or for direct theoretical analysis of the partition structure.
2.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are well-executed but focus exclusively on topic modeling and a comparison with HDP-LDA. While HDP is the most relevant baseline, it would have strengthened the paper to include comparisons with other nonparametric mixed-membership models or to showcase the BNBP's utility in a different application domain where overdispersed counts are common (e.g., modeling user ratings or genomic data).

**Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality: Excellent.** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work, from theory to algorithm to empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by derivations and experiments. The authors are honest about the model's properties, for instance, by showing cases where HDP achieves lower perplexity with a much larger number of topics, while correctly framing their model's strength in achieving competitive perplexity with greater compactness.

*   **Clarity: Excellent.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the theoretical development is logical, and the connection between the EPPF and the final collapsed sampler is explained very effectively. The use of lemmas to structure the derivation and the explicit comparison of the update equations in Section 3.1 greatly enhance readability and understanding.

*   **Originality: High.** The core contribution—the derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP for mixed-membership modeling and the technique used to achieve it—is highly original. While BNBP models and EPPFs exist, their combination in this setting to produce a fully collapsed sampler is a novel and significant advance. It clearly distinguishes this work from previous papers on the BNBP and HDP.

*   **Significance: High.** This work is likely to have a substantial impact. It provides the community with a powerful and practical alternative to the HDP for mixed-membership modeling. The BNBP topic model, with its fast-mixing sampler and strong empirical performance, is a tool that researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt. Furthermore, the derivation technique itself is an important methodological contribution that could inspire further research into developing collapsed samplers for a wider class of Bayesian nonparametric models.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that combines novel theory with practical application and compelling empirical results. It solves an important open problem and provides a valuable new tool for the machine learning community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
