Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a group-size dependent EPPF for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. The key idea is to (i) augment the BNBP via a beta–gamma–Poisson construction, (ii) randomize group sizes via a Poisson link to obtain an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint law of group sizes and partitions, and (iii) condition back on observed group sizes to derive the EPPF. From this, the authors obtain a simple prediction rule enabling fully collapsed Gibbs sampling. They instantiate a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, document weights, and the beta process, and compare against HDP-LDA.

This work connects to several lines: EPPFs and exchangeable partitions (Pitman), NRMI/Poisson–Kingman marginal samplers (Regazzini et al., Lomelí et al.), and integer-valued processes (gamma-Poisson, BNBP, GNBP) for mixed membership (Zhou & Carin). It complements Heaukulani & Roy and Zhou et al. on BNBP/negative-binomial IBP-like structures by focusing on the conditional EPPF for grouped data with fixed sizes, which prior work did not provide. The proposed recipe (randomize counts, derive joint law, then condition) is general and potentially portable to other count processes.

Quality
- Theory: The augmentation and factorization to obtain the ECPF are carefully done. Lemma 1 correctly uses the Poissonization trick to factor the likelihood and integrate out Γ-process atom masses. The ECPF (Eq. 6) and the induced prior on random count matrices (Eq. 7) are plausible and consistent with the resulting digamma and Dirichlet–multinomial column generative mechanism (Eq. 8). The EPPF (Eq. 9) is sample-size (group-size) dependent, aligning with Zhou & Walker’s framework. The prediction rule (Eq. 10) has an intuitive multiplicative form combining global popularity n·k and local popularity njk with dispersion rj, and a new-cluster term depending on γ0, c, and r. Some constants in the new-cluster probability are hard to parse in the main text; ensuring the normalization factor involving ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c) is unambiguous would help.
- Algorithm: The fully collapsed sampler is a significant contribution; it marginalizes topics, document weights, and the beta process, yielding a simple update that is easy to implement. Closed-form updates for remaining parameters are promised in the appendix.
- Experiments: The comparison to HDP-LDA shows faster convergence/mixing and competitive or better perplexity for comparable numbers of topics, with BNBP achieving more compact representations due to stronger shrinkage. The evaluation protocol is standard (held-out perplexity), and code is provided.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally clear for an expert. The derivations are well motivated. However, there are several presentation issues:
- Some equations and constants around the prediction rule are difficult to read (possible typesetting artifacts); explicitly state the exact new-cluster proportionality constant.
- Minor typos (“perdition rule”, “disjoint disjoint”), missing years in references, and footnotes embedded in text break the flow.
- The role of c (fixed vs inferred) should be clarified in the main text, with its update if inferred.

Originality
- The main novelty is the derivation of a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting via the “randomize-then-condition” strategy, and the resulting fully collapsed sampler. To my knowledge, prior work on BNBP/negative-binomial IBP focussed on infinite random count matrices or predictive structures, not on an EPPF conditional on group sizes enabling fully collapsed inference. This is a substantive theoretical and algorithmic contribution.
- The multiplicative shrinkage interpretation versus HDP’s additive mechanism is insightful.

Significance
- The method provides a principled collapsed inference algorithm for a widely used nonparametric count prior, which is valuable beyond topic models. The general recipe could extend to other integer-valued processes (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson) to derive new EPPFs and collapsed samplers.
- In topic modeling, the sampler is simple, fast, and competitive, making it attractive in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness of comparison: HDP-LDA is evaluated with a (partially) collapsed direct-assignment sampler. The standard fully collapsed CRF sampler exists (albeit with bookkeeping). A comparison against a well-tuned collapsed HDP-LDA (or modern efficient variants) would strengthen claims about mixing and predictive performance.
- Sensitivity: The influence of hyperparameters (γ0, c) is not fully explored. Are c and γ0 inferred? If so, provide their posteriors/updates; if fixed, justify choices and sensitivity analyses.
- Scope of baselines: No comparison to Pitman–Yor topic models or other NB-process-based topic/factor models that often capture power-law behavior and can improve perplexity.
- Empirics: Only perplexity is reported. Qualitative topic coherence or sparsity analyses would support the claim of compact representations. Scalability results (runtime vs KJ and corpus size) beyond a single CPU anecdote would be useful.
- Notational/measure-theoretic details: The Lévy measure of the beta process is given without an explicit c factor in front; please ensure conventions are consistent and clarify whether c multiplies B0 or appears as written. The digamma distribution is uncommon; a brief intuition and its moments would help.

Pros
- Clear theoretical advancement: ECPF and EPPF for BNBP with grouped data.
- Simple, fully collapsed prediction rule and sampler.
- Intuitive multiplicative shrinkage mechanism; compelling interpretation.
- Evidence of faster convergence and competitive perplexity with fewer topics.
- Code available.

Cons
- Comparison lacks a fully collapsed HDP-LDA baseline and broader baselines.
- Some ambiguity in constants in the prediction rule; clarity around c/γ0 inference.
- Minor presentation issues and typos.
- Limited empirical analyses beyond perplexity and topic count.

Questions for authors
1) Is c inferred? If yes, provide the sampler; if no, how sensitive are results to c?
2) Please provide the exact new-cluster probability in Eq. (10), including normalization and ψ terms.
3) Can your recipe extend to GNBP or gamma–Poisson to yield analogous EPPFs/prediction rules? Any obstacles?
4) How does the method behave on corpora with very long documents or extreme overdispersion? Any failure cases?
5) Can you compare against a collapsed CRF sampler for HDP-LDA, or justify why such a comparison is impractical here?

Minor comments
- Fix typos and missing years in references.
- Clarify the “log-beta” random variable Q(A) role; a short intuition would help.
