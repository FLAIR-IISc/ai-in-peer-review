This paper introduces a novel framework for mixed-membership modeling based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). The primary contribution is the derivation of an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP that is suitable for grouped data, a setting where traditional EPPFs are not directly applicable. This work builds upon the established theory of EPPFs for mixture models [5] and extends the concept of sample-size dependent EPPFs [12] to the more complex mixed-membership scenario. While previous work on the BNBP [10, 7] and related models has explored its use in this setting, it has generally relied on approximate inference methods like finite truncation or slice sampling. This paper is the first to derive the exact marginal distribution governing the random partitions, which in turn enables a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.

The authors' key technical innovation is an elegant two-step derivation. Instead of deriving the EPPF directly for fixed group sizes, they first introduce a "group size dependent model" where the number of data points in each group is treated as a random variable. For this augmented model, they derive the joint distribution of the random group sizes and their partitions, which they term the Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF). By subsequently applying Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes, they arrive at the desired EPPF for the BNBP. Although the resulting EPPF expression is complex, it leads to a remarkably simple and intuitive predictive rule for assigning data points to clusters. This rule is then used to construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, which is empirically compared against the well-known Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) topic model [6].

### Quality
The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations presented in the main text and detailed in the appendix appear correct and rigorous. The progression from the BNBP definition to the ECPF (Lemma 2), the EPPF (Lemma 3), and the final predictive rule (Lemma 4) is logical and well-founded. The connection drawn between the BNBP and a generative process for a random count matrix with a Poisson number of columns, where column sums follow a Digamma distribution and counts within a column follow a Dirichlet-Multinomial, is insightful and adds to the theoretical understanding of the process.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and supports the paper's claims. The experiments are conducted on three standard text corpora, and the comparison with HDP-LDA, a state-of-the-art baseline, is appropriate. The authors are careful in their evaluation, using held-out perplexity and analyzing convergence, mixing properties, and the trade-off between model complexity (number of topics) and predictive performance. The results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed fully collapsed sampler, particularly its faster convergence and better mixing compared to the direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly situates the work within the context of nonparametric Bayesian models for mixture and mixed-membership modeling, effectively motivating the need for an EPPF-based treatment of the BNBP. The core theoretical contributions are presented as a sequence of lemmas, which aids readability and comprehension. The explicit comparison of the BNBP topic model's predictive update rule (12) with those of LDA (13) and HDP-LDA (14) is particularly effective at highlighting the novel multiplicative mechanism for combining global and local cluster popularities. The figures are informative, especially the trace plots in Figure 2, which provide a clear visual demonstration of the superior mixing properties of the proposed sampler. The work provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the derivations and potentially reproduce the results.

### Originality
The work is highly original. The central idea of deriving an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling by first randomizing group sizes to obtain an ECPF and then conditioning is a novel and powerful technique. While the BNBP itself is not new, this paper provides a new and fundamental understanding of its marginal properties, leading to a new class of inference algorithms. The resulting BNBP topic model and its fully collapsed Gibbs sampler are novel contributions. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work, including prior work on the BNBP [10, 11], HDP [6], and recent work on the combinatorial structures of related processes [16, 17].

### Significance
The paper makes significant contributions to both theory and practice in nonparametric Bayesian machine learning.
1.  **Theoretical Significance:** It provides a new method for analyzing integer-valued stochastic processes in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The suggestion that this technique could be generalized to other processes, such as the gamma-Poisson process, opens up a promising avenue for future research and could lead to a more unified EPPF-based framework for this important model class.
2.  **Practical Significance:** The derived fully collapsed Gibbs sampler offers tangible benefits over existing methods. Faster convergence and better mixing are critical for practical applications, as they lead to more reliable inference in less time. The experimental results, which show that the BNBP topic model can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact representation of the data (fewer topics), will be of great interest to practitioners. The straightforward and easy-to-implement nature of the final sampling algorithm further enhances its practical appeal.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical method for deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting.
*   Develops the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for this model class, a significant practical advancement.
*   The resulting sampler is shown empirically to converge faster, mix better, and achieve competitive or superior predictive performance compared to the widely used HDP-LDA.
*   The work is well-written, technically sound, and clearly situates its contributions within the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The claims of providing a "unified treatment" may be slightly overstated, as the method is demonstrated only for the BNBP, with generalization to other processes left as future work.
*   The experimental comparison is against the direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA. While a standard baseline, a comparison with other HDP inference methods (e.g., one based on the Chinese restaurant franchise) could have provided a more complete picture, although the authors justify their choice by noting the complexity of such alternatives.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant novel contributions in both theory and methodology. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and importance of the work. It advances our understanding of nonparametric processes for mixed-membership modeling and provides a practical algorithm with clear advantages over existing approaches.
