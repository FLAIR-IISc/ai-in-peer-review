### Review

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of mixed-membership modeling, a cornerstone of modern machine learning for which the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) has long been the dominant nonparametric Bayesian approach. The authors argue that while other integer-valued stochastic processes, like the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), are suitable for this task, they have been hampered by the lack of a fully collapsed inference scheme analogous to the HDP's Chinese Restaurant Franchise.

The central contribution of this work is a significant theoretical breakthrough: the derivation of the group-size dependent Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP. The authors achieve this through a novel and clever two-step procedure. They first randomize the group sizes to derive an Exchangeable *Cluster* Probability Function (ECPF) for the joint distribution of group sizes and their partitions. Subsequently, using Bayes' rule, they condition on the observed group sizes to obtain the desired EPPF. From this, they derive a simple and elegant Gibbs sampling prediction rule, which enables the first fully collapsed sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership models. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework by developing a BNBP topic model, which they show converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance with more compact representations compared to the HDP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Originality:** This work represents a fundamental contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics. For years, the HDP has been the de facto standard for mixed-membership modeling, partly due to the elegance of its collapsed representation. This paper successfully challenges that monopoly by providing a complete, principled, and tractable collapsed inference framework for the BNBP. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing model; it is a foundational piece of work that makes an entire class of models (BNBP-based) fully practical for this problem setting for the first time. The method used to derive the EPPF via the ECPF is itself a highly original and powerful idea that may be applicable to other stochastic processes.

2.  **Significant Conceptual and Practical Impact:** By deriving a fully collapsed sampler, the authors unlock significant practical benefits. The resulting sampler is shown to converge much more rapidly and mix better than the widely-used direct assignment sampler for the HDP. This is a major practical advantage. Furthermore, the paper provides clear intuition about the distinct shrinkage properties of the BNBP, highlighting its multiplicative combination of global and local popularities, which contrasts with the HDP's additive mechanism. This leads to models that are empirically shown to be more compact while maintaining strong predictive power. This work is likely to inspire a new wave of research into non-HDP models for mixed-membership tasks and will almost certainly be built upon by others.

3.  **Clarity and Technical Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The authors navigate a series of complex derivations with precision, leading to a surprisingly simple and interpretable final prediction rule (Eq. 10). The theoretical progression from the beta process to the ECPF and finally to the EPPF is laid out logically. The experiments, while not exhaustive, are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's main claims regarding convergence, mixing, and the model's ability to learn compact representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Complexity of the EPPF:** The final form of the EPPF (Eq. 9) is considerably more complex than the classic EPPFs associated with the Dirichlet or Pitman-Yor processes. While this might appear aesthetically unappealing, it is a minor point, as the authors rightly show that what matters for inference is the resulting prediction rule, which is simple and efficient. The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating that such a marginal law exists and can be used to derive a tractable sampler.

2.  **Performance Trade-offs:** The empirical results show that while the BNBP topic model often achieves better perplexity for a given number of topics, the HDP can sometimes achieve a lower absolute perplexity by using a much larger number of topics (e.g., with very small η). This is an interesting trade-off, and the paper could perhaps discuss the implications more fully. However, given the focus on the novel derivation and the clear benefits in convergence and model compactness, this is a minor critique. The current experiments are more than sufficient to validate the paper's core thesis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper solves a major open problem: deriving a collapsed representation for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting.
*   The proposed derivation technique (via the ECPF) is a novel contribution in its own right and could be highly influential.
*   It introduces a compelling and practical alternative to the HDP, backed by strong empirical evidence of superior mixing, convergence, and model compactness.
*   The work is a rare example of a deep theoretical contribution that also has immediate and obvious practical benefits.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None of significance. The work is a complete and impactful contribution.

**Recommendation:**
This paper is an outstanding example of the kind of ambitious, foundational research that NIPS should champion. It is a bold departure from the incremental improvements on existing models that are all too common. The authors tackle a difficult theoretical problem and, in solving it, provide the community with a powerful new tool for mixed-membership modeling that has demonstrable advantages over the long-standing incumbent. The work is novel, significant, and technically excellent. I strongly recommend acceptance.
