

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper addresses an important gap in the literature by developing a group size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), which enables fully collapsed inference for mixed-membership modeling. The authors make several key contributions: 1) a novel derivation of the BNBP's EPPF via a group size dependent approach, 2) a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions, and 3) a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that outperforms HDP-LDA in convergence speed, mixing, and compactness of representation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF represents a significant theoretical advance. The authors successfully bridge the gap between integer-valued stochastic processes and exchangeable random partitions in mixed-membership modeling, a problem that has been largely unaddressed despite the popularity of methods like HDP. Their approach of randomizing group sizes before deriving the EPPF is clever and generalizable.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically rigorous, with careful derivations of key lemmas (1-4) and proofs provided in the appendix. The comparison with HDP-LDA is well-executed, and the experimental results consistently demonstrate advantages of the BNBP approach in convergence speed (Figures 2-3) and model compactness.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The BNBP topic model is straightforward to implement with closed-form update equations (Equation 12). The empirical results show the method converges faster and mixes better than HDP-LDA, with state-of-the-art predictive performance on standard topic modeling datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of EPPF:** The EPPF (Equation 9) is highly complex and not easily interpretable. While the authors provide a prediction rule (Equation 10) that is simpler, the theoretical foundation lacks the intuitive appeal of simpler EPPFs like the Chinese Restaurant Process. A more detailed discussion of how the BNBP's EPPF differs conceptually from existing EPPFs would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The comparison is primarily limited to topic modeling. While topic modeling is a standard test case, the paper would benefit from demonstrating the BNBP's advantages on other mixed-membership problems (e.g., multi-view clustering, hierarchical clustering) to establish broader applicability.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper mentions computational complexity but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the BNBP's complexity compares to HDP-LDA or other methods. The claim that the BNBP sampler "takes about 2.5 seconds per iteration" is useful, but without context (e.g., compared to HDP-LDA on the same hardware), it's difficult to assess significance.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate how sensitive the BNBP model is to the hyperparameter choices (e.g., γ₀, c, r). The results in Figure 3 suggest the dispersion parameters rⱼ play a critical role, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to select these parameters for new applications.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when BNBP is preferable to existing methods. The authors should explicitly address the trade-offs between BNBP's compact representation (fewer topics) and potential loss of predictive power when η is not optimally chosen.

2. Consider adding a section comparing the BNBP's theoretical properties (e.g., cluster size distribution, topic coherence) with those of HDP-LDA, rather than just predictive performance metrics.

3. Provide a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity, including how the BNBP's complexity scales with the number of documents, words, and inferred topics.

4. Include a small experiment demonstrating the BNBP's application to a non-topic modeling mixed-membership problem (e.g., clustering of gene expression data or multi-view image data) to strengthen the claim of general applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper with significant theoretical contributions to the understanding of mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate clear practical advantages over HDP-LDA.

The main limitations are the complexity of the EPPF and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation. However, these are common challenges in theoretical advances, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding broader applicability and computational analysis, this work would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, advancing the state of the art in mixed-membership modeling. The quality of the technical work and the significance of the contribution meet NeurIPS standards.
