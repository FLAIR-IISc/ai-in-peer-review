

# Peer Review: "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in nonparametric Bayesian modeling by developing a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). While previous work established EPPFs for single-set mixture modeling (e.g., Chinese restaurant process), mixed-membership modeling of grouped data has lacked a unified treatment. The authors derive the BNBP's EPPF by first randomizing group sizes to obtain an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then applying Bayes' rule to arrive at the desired EPPF. They further develop a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process.

The work builds upon key prior contributions including Pitman's EPPF framework (2002), Zhou et al.'s BNBP (2014), and the HDP (Teh et al., 2006). Unlike HDP, which uses the Chinese restaurant franchise for inference, the authors demonstrate that BNBP enables fully collapsed inference for mixed-membership models—a significant advancement.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is technically sophisticated and represents a major theoretical contribution. The authors' method of deriving the EPPF through a group-size dependent model is elegant and generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes. This approach fills a critical gap in the literature where BNBP and GNBP lacked marginal distributions for mixed-membership modeling.

2. **Methodological Clarity**: The paper provides a clear prediction rule (Equation 10) that reveals how the BNBP combines global and local topic popularity multiplicatively rather than additively (as in HDP). This distinction explains the BNBP's stronger topic shrinkage behavior, which the authors empirically verify.

3. **Empirical Rigor**: The comparative evaluation on three standard topic modeling datasets (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS) demonstrates clear advantages of the BNBP topic model. The BNBP sampler converges in <100 iterations versus HDP-LDA's >300 iterations, with better mixing properties as shown in Figure 2. The perplexity results (Figure 3) are particularly compelling, showing that BNBP achieves comparable predictive performance with fewer topics.

4. **Practical Impact**: The BNBP topic model is straightforward to implement and computationally efficient. The authors provide closed-form Gibbs update equations and demonstrate that the sampler runs in ~2.5 seconds per iteration on the NIPS corpus with ~180 topics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Conceptual Clarity**: The distinction between ECPF and EPPF, while mathematically precise, is somewhat obscured by dense notation. A more intuitive explanation of why the EPPF is group-size dependent (rather than the standard size-independent EPPF) would benefit readers.

2. **Comparative Scope**: While the paper compares with HDP-LDA, it omits comparison with other recent nonparametric topic models like the discrete infinite logistic normal distribution (Paisley et al., 2012) and the infinite gamma-Poisson feature model (Titsias, 2009). These methods also address mixed-membership modeling and would provide a more complete context.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper states that the BNBP's EPPF "is dependent on the group sizes" but doesn't fully explore the implications of this dependency. In real-world applications with highly variable group sizes, this could impact performance, yet the authors don't discuss adaptation strategies.

4. **Computational Analysis**: While the paper notes that sparse computation techniques could speed up the BNBP sampler, it lacks a detailed complexity analysis comparing BNBP to HDP-LDA. The computational advantage should be quantified more precisely.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian modeling by establishing a unified framework for deriving EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is rigorous and addresses a well-documented gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model provides a compelling alternative to HDP-LDA with demonstrable advantages in convergence speed, mixing, and predictive performance.

The work is well-positioned to advance the field, as it not only provides a new model but also develops a general method for deriving EPPFs that could be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The empirical results are convincing and support the theoretical claims.

While the mathematical exposition could be made more accessible, the paper's theoretical contributions and practical advantages are substantial. The authors have demonstrated that BNBP provides a more efficient and interpretable framework for mixed-membership modeling than existing approaches.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

**Pro arguments**: Novel EPPF derivation for mixed-membership modeling, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with faster convergence, clear empirical advantages over HDP-LDA, and a framework generalizable to other stochastic processes.

**Con arguments**: Some mathematical exposition could be improved for accessibility, and the comparison with other recent nonparametric topic models could be expanded. However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
