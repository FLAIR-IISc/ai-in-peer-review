

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper addresses an important gap in the literature on nonparametric Bayesian models for mixed-membership modeling. While the EPPF framework has been successfully applied to mixture modeling of single sets of data, the authors develop a group-size dependent EPPF for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) that governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. This fills a significant theoretical gap, as previous work on mixed-membership modeling (e.g., HDP) lacked a unified treatment of the marginal distributions governing exchangeable random partitions. The authors derive the BNBP's EPPF through an innovative approach of first randomizing group sizes and then using Bayes' rule, which they generalize to other integer-valued stochastic processes. They apply this to topic modeling, constructing a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out both topic weights and the infinite-dimensional beta process.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a significant theoretical advance. Previous work on BNBP (e.g., [10, 11, 17]) focused on infinite random count matrices but did not provide the marginal distributions governing exchangeable partitions of grouped data. The authors' method of first randomizing group sizes and then deriving the EPPF via Bayes' rule is novel and could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2. **Practical Implementation:** The prediction rule (Lemma 4) is straightforward to implement, and the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model provides clear computational advantages over HDP-LDA. The empirical results demonstrate faster convergence, better mixing, and competitive predictive performance (perplexity) with a more compact representation of the corpus.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The mathematical derivations are carefully presented with proofs provided in the appendix, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts like the effect of dispersion parameters on cluster structures.

4. **Significance:** The BNBP topic model addresses a practical need for nonparametric topic modeling with automatic topic number selection. The method provides a theoretically sound alternative to HDP-LDA with clear computational advantages, particularly for applications where model interpretability and computational efficiency are important.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of EPPF:** While the prediction rule (equation 10) is practical, the EPPF (equation 9) is quite complex, which may hinder adoption by practitioners. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to implement the EPPF in practice, especially for those without deep theoretical background in nonparametric Bayesian methods.

2. **Parameter Selection for rj:** The paper correctly identifies that inference of rj is important, but it does not provide a clear method for selecting or inferring these dispersion parameters. The authors state that "it is important to infer rj rather than setting them in a heuristic manner or fixing them," but the paper lacks a systematic approach to this inference. This is a significant practical limitation.

3. **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The paper compares the BNBP topic model only with HDP-LDA, which is a reasonable baseline. However, the evaluation is limited to perplexity on three standard corpora. The authors should compare with more recent topic modeling methods (e.g., dynamic topic models, neural topic models) and include additional evaluation metrics such as topic coherence, which is crucial for practical topic modeling applications.

4. **Theoretical Justification for Performance:** While the paper demonstrates the BNBP's better convergence and mixing, the theoretical justification for why the multiplicative control mechanism (equation 12) leads to better performance than HDP's additive mechanism (equation 14) is somewhat superficial. A deeper theoretical analysis of the relationship between the variances and means of the latent topic usage count vectors would strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling, particularly for mixed-membership problems. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model is well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear computational advantages over HDP-LDA.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper would benefit from:

1. A more detailed discussion of practical implementation of the EPPF, particularly for practitioners.
2. A clear method for inferring the dispersion parameters rj, not just stating their importance.
3. A comparison with more recent topic modeling methods beyond HDP-LDA.
4. Additional theoretical insights into why the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism leads to better performance.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The theoretical advances are substantial, and the practical implementation is straightforward. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance, and addressing them would make an already strong paper even better.
