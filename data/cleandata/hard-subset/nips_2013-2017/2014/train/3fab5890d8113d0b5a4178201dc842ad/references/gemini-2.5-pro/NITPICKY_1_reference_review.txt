### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of mixed-membership modeling from a nonparametric Bayesian perspective. Specifically, it focuses on the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), a model for which fully collapsed inference has previously been unavailable. The central contribution is the derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, where data is organized into groups, and partitions are shared across groups. The authors achieve this not by a direct derivation, but through an innovative intermediate step: they first model the group sizes as random variables to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), and then use Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes, yielding the desired EPPF.

Building on this theoretical result, the paper derives a simple predictive rule for Gibbs sampling, leading to a fully collapsed sampler for a BNBP-based topic model. This sampler analytically integrates out the infinite-dimensional beta process, topic weights, and topics. The authors present experimental results on three text corpora, comparing their model to the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), demonstrating faster convergence, better mixing, and competitive predictive performance, particularly in achieving compact topic representations.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership context is a significant and novel contribution. It provides a formal basis for a class of models that previously lacked a fully collapsed inference scheme, filling an important gap in the literature and moving towards a more unified treatment of mixed-membership models akin to what EPPFs provide for single-group mixture models. The method of using a size-dependent model (ECPF) as an intermediate step is elegant and potentially applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2.  **Practical and Efficient Inference:** The paper successfully translates the complex EPPF into a simple, intuitive, and easy-to-implement predictive rule for Gibbs sampling (Equation 10). This results in a fully collapsed sampler for the BNBP topic model, which is a valuable practical outcome.

3.  **Interesting Shrinkage Mechanism:** The paper highlights a key qualitative difference between the BNBP and the HDP. The BNBP's update rule combines global and local cluster popularities multiplicatively, contrasting with the HDP's additive mechanism. This provides a new and interesting mechanism for shrinking the number of latent components, which appears empirically effective.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments, while limited, suggest clear advantages for the proposed sampler in terms of convergence speed and mixing compared to the direct assignment sampler for the HDP. The trace plots in Figure 2 are quite convincing on this point.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novelty of the core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor formatting issues to significant technical errors, which collectively undermine its quality and readiness for publication.

1.  **Formatting and Typesetting Errors:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a top-tier conference submission.
    *   The abstract contains a formatting error: "Exchangeable ￼Random Partitions".
    *   The typesetting of mathematical expressions is frequently suboptimal. For instance, in Equations (6), (7), and (9), `γ KJ 0` should clearly be `γ_0^{K_J}`. The negative sign in `e −γ0[...]` in Equation (6) has improper spacing.
    *   Raw URLs are used in footnotes instead of proper citations.

2.  **Clarity and Exposition:** Several key concepts and derivations are presented without sufficient explanation.
    *   In Section 2.2, the "digamma distribution" is introduced as a core component of the BNBP's generative story. This is a highly non-standard distribution, and the paper provides no intuition for its properties or why it arises, relying solely on a citation. This makes the generative process difficult to understand.
    *   The presentation of the main theoretical result, the EPPF in Equation (9), is almost impenetrable. While its complexity motivates the use of the simpler predictive rule, the result itself is of limited interpretive value as presented.
    *   The derivation of the marginalized likelihood in Section 3 is rushed, compressing several non-trivial steps into a few sentences.

3.  **Technical Inaccuracies:** The paper contains critical technical errors that question the validity of the proposed sampler and the experimental evaluation.
    *   **Incorrect Gibbs Sampler Update:** The predictive probability for a new topic `k = K^{-ji}_J + 1` in Equation (12) appears to be incorrect. It is missing a factor of `1/V` (or more precisely, `η / (Vη)`) that should arise from integrating out the new topic's parameters `φ_k` from the Dirichlet-multinomial likelihood term. This is a serious flaw in one of the paper's main practical contributions.
    *   **Non-Standard Evaluation Metric:** The formula for per-word perplexity provided in Section 4 is highly unconventional. Standard perplexity calculations involve averaging the log-probabilities of test words, where probabilities are normalized per document. The formula presented appears to perform a single normalization over the entire test set and all MCMC samples, which is not standard practice and whose interpretation is unclear. This calls into question the validity of the quantitative comparisons to the HDP-LDA.

4.  **Overstated or Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   The claim of a "fully collapsed" sampler is slightly imprecise. While the infinite-dimensional measures are integrated out, key hyperparameters (`γ0`, `c`, and the vector `r`) are still sampled, unlike in the canonical fully collapsed sampler for LDA.
    *   The conclusion suggests that the derivation method can be generalized to other integer-valued processes (e.g., gamma-Poisson). This is an interesting direction, but it is presented as a statement rather than speculation, with no supporting argument or evidence provided in the paper.

### Decision

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically significant derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP.
*   The resulting collapsed sampler is practical, efficient, and demonstrates superior mixing properties to the HDP.
*   Introduces an interesting multiplicative shrinkage mechanism as an alternative to the HDP.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Contains a critical error in the Gibbs sampling update equation for new topics.
*   Uses a non-standard and questionable formula for perplexity, undermining the experimental results.
*   Suffers from numerous formatting errors and a general lack of polish.
*   Key theoretical components (e.g., the digamma distribution) are poorly explained.
*   Claims regarding the sampler and generalizability are not fully supported.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper is built on a strong, novel, and significant theoretical idea, its execution is deeply flawed. The combination of major technical errors in the proposed sampler and the evaluation metric, along with a multitude of minor clarity and formatting issues, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work has clear potential, but the manuscript requires a thorough revision to correct the errors, clarify the exposition, and adhere to the standards of a top-tier publication.
