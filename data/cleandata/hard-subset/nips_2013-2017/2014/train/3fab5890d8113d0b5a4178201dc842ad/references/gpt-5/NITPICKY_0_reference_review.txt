Summary
The paper studies mixed-membership modeling via the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The main theoretical contribution is a derivation of a group-size-dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for BNBP by (i) first randomizing group sizes and deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), and (ii) then applying Bayes’ rule to condition on observed group sizes. The authors further provide a prediction rule for simulating partitions, show an interpretation in terms of Poisson–gamma augmentation, and build a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process. Empirically, they compare the BNBP topic model to HDP-LDA on three corpora, reporting faster convergence and competitive perplexities with fewer topics.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on NRMIs and DP/CRP/EPPF theory (Ferguson, Pitman), negative binomial/gamma processes, and beta/IBP processes. It connects to “negative binomial IBP,” multi-scoop IBP, and Poisson factorization. Prior collapsed samplers for HDP-LDA rely on the Chinese restaurant franchise; here a different route is taken for BNBP via an EPPF derived from integer-valued processes and a “digamma” size distribution. This direction is novel relative to common DP/HDP constructions and recent Poisson–Kingman marginal samplers; the BNBP is not in that family.

Strengths
- Conceptual: A clear, general recipe—randomize group sizes to get an ECPF, then condition to obtain an EPPF—for integer-valued CRMs in mixed-membership modeling. This is of independent interest.
- Technical: Nontrivial derivations (ECPF, matrix prior, prediction rule) and a fully collapsed sampler for BNBP topic modeling, with sensible intuition about multiplicative vs additive shrinkage relative to HDP.
- Practical: Code is provided; the sampler appears simple to implement; empirical results suggest faster mixing and compact representations.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality/technical correctness
- c (the beta-process concentration) is not assigned a prior nor a fixed value in the main text, yet appears in the prediction rule (10) and sampler (12). How is c set or inferred? This is a crucial missing detail affecting both theory and experiments.
- Predictive probability/perplexity formula: The provided expression divides by ∑s ∑v ∑k φvk(s) θjk(s), which is not the appropriate normalization for p(v|document) (it should normalize per sample by ∑k θjk(s), since ∑v φvk(s)=1). As written, the evaluation is dimensionally inconsistent. This calls into question the reported perplexities.
- New-cluster probability in (12) includes a factor V·γ0/(c+r·)·rj. For an empty topic the term from integrating φ typically yields η/(Vη)=1/V for a single token; having a multiplicative factor V (rather than 1/V) suggests either a rescaling inconsistency or a typesetting error. The analogous HDP-LDA expression in (14) also has an unexplained factor V. Please clarify and ensure the same proportionality constants are used for existing and new topics.
- The Lévy measure in (1) omits a factor c often present in other parameterizations; later expressions depend on ψ(c+⋅)−ψ(c). The parameterization should be made precise and reconciled with standard references to avoid confusion.
- The “digamma distribution” on cluster sizes is uncommon; while cited, its use and normalization are central. A short, self-contained derivation/justification in the main text would help ensure correctness (e.g., support, normalizer ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c), and how it arises from beta–gamma–Poisson compounding).
- Sanity checks are missing: for J=1, does the prediction rule reduce to a known sample-size-dependent EPPF? A brief discussion would build trust.

Clarity and presentation
- Heavy reliance on the Appendix for critical proofs and update equations; key steps (especially for Lemmas 2–4 and the sampler) deserve more detail in the main text.
- Notation is dense and sometimes inconsistent: e.g., n·k vs n·k, n:k, overuse of dots and hyphens; “disjoint disjoint”; missing parentheses; “perdition rule” instead of prediction; a figure caption fragment appears amid Eq. (10). These hinder readability.
- Reference list is incomplete (missing years/pages; several arXiv/“to appear” without publication details). Please standardize formatting.

Originality
- The route to a group-size-dependent EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership settings appears novel and useful, as is the fully collapsed sampler that integrates the beta process. The connection to a Poisson random number of i.i.d. columns with a digamma size is neat.

Significance
- If the evaluation and sampler details are corrected, the method could impact nonparametric mixed-membership modeling beyond DP/HDP, and the general ECPF→EPPF approach may be reusable for other integer-valued CRMs (gamma-Poisson, GNBP). However, empirical evidence is limited:
  - Baselines: Only HDP-LDA (direct assignment) is used. A stronger comparison would include CRF-based collapsed HDP-LDA, Pitman–Yor HDP, and modern Poisson factorization baselines.
  - Fairness: Unclear treatment of HDP hyperparameters (α, base DP) and inference specifics; no sensitivity analyses for γ0, c, rj. Results depend heavily on η; conclusions about “state-of-the-art” seem overstated without broader baselines.
  - No error bars/significance; no wall-clock vs perplexity; scalability claims rely on one machine with unoptimized Matlab.

Pros
- Novel theoretical contribution (ECPF→EPPF for BNBP); clear predictive rule.
- Fully collapsed sampler; attractive intuition about multiplicative shrinkage.
- Promising empirical mixing behavior; compact topic usage.

Cons
- Missing/incomplete key details (estimation/setting of c; perplexity computation; new-topic constants).
- Clarity/notation and numerous minor errors/typos; incomplete references.
- Limited and potentially unfair empirical comparison; overclaiming “state-of-the-art.”

Recommendations for improvement
- Specify and infer c; include priors/posteriors and updates; report sensitivity.
- Correct and clarify the predictive probability and perplexity computation; use standard document completion or left-to-right evaluation with proper normalization.
- Re-derive and present the new-topic probabilities with consistent constants; provide a short derivation in the main text.
- Add stronger baselines and report matched-K comparisons with error bars; include CRF-collapsed HDP-LDA.
- Improve exposition (notation, typos, figure captions), and include key proofs/derivations inline.

Overall recommendation: Reject (as is). The paper has a strong and interesting core idea, but technical/evaluation omissions and presentation issues are substantial. With corrections and clearer empirical validation, it could be a valuable contribution. Confidence: high.
