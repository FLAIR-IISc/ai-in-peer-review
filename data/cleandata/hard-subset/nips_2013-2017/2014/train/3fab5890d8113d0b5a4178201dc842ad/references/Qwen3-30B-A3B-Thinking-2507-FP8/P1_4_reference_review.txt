

# Peer Review: "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling by deriving the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While existing methods like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) provide fully collapsed inference for certain models, the BNBP and similar integer-valued stochastic processes lacked a proper EPPF for mixed-membership modeling. The authors develop a novel method to derive the EPPF by first randomizing group sizes and then applying Bayes' rule. They then construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process. The paper demonstrates the BNBP topic model's superior convergence speed, mixing properties, and competitive predictive performance compared to HDP-LDA.

The work builds on foundational EPPF theory introduced by Pitman [5] and extends it to mixed-membership modeling, where multiple groups share a common set of latent features. It relates to recent work on BNBP [7, 10, 11] but fills the critical gap of providing the EPPF for mixed-membership contexts, which has been missing for BNBP and similar processes.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for BNBP is a significant theoretical advance that fills a major gap in the literature. The method of randomizing group sizes to derive the EPPF is novel and could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes like the gamma-negative binomial process.

2. **Practical Impact:** The BNBP topic model demonstrates clear advantages over HDP-LDA in terms of convergence speed and mixing properties. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is straightforward to implement and shows state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The connection between the theoretical contributions and practical applications is well-established.

4. **Experimental Validation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments on three distinct corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12), demonstrating the BNBP topic model's advantages in convergence speed, mixing, and predictive performance. The comparison with HDP-LDA is comprehensive and well-justified.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper compares with HDP-LDA, it could better contextualize the BNBP in relation to other recent mixed-membership models like the gamma-negative binomial process (GNBP) and the beta-Poisson process. The paper mentions these but doesn't provide a detailed comparison.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper states the BNBP sampler is fast but lacks a formal computational complexity analysis compared to other methods. A theoretical analysis of time complexity would strengthen the claim about computational efficiency.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper shows how dispersion parameters affect model behavior, a more systematic analysis of how different hyperparameter settings affect model performance would be valuable.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance Assessment

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The derivations are correct and carefully presented. The experimental results are comprehensive and provide strong evidence for the claimed advantages. The paper is a complete piece of work, not a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Key concepts are well-defined, and mathematical notation is consistent. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The derivation of the EPPF for BNBP is novel and fills a significant gap in the literature. While BNBP itself has been studied before, the application to mixed-membership modeling with a proper EPPF is new. The method to derive the EPPF by randomizing group sizes is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** This work is highly significant as it provides a unified framework for mixed-membership modeling using BNBP, which has advantages over existing methods in terms of convergence speed and compact representation. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a practical contribution that can be easily implemented. The work advances the state of the art in nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Fills a significant gap in the literature by deriving the EPPF for BNBP for mixed-membership modeling
- Provides a novel method for deriving EPPFs from integer-valued stochastic processes
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods (HDP-LDA) in convergence speed and predictive performance
- The BNBP topic model is straightforward to implement and has state-of-the-art performance
- The theoretical contribution is sound and well-supported

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited comparison with other recent mixed-membership models (e.g., GNBP)
- Lack of formal computational complexity analysis
- Some aspects of parameter sensitivity could be explored more deeply

## Conclusion
This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the EPPF for BNBP is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods, with faster convergence and competitive predictive performance. While there are minor weaknesses in the comparison with other models and the lack of computational complexity analysis, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The work is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with a score of 8/10 (Strong Accept). The paper represents a significant advance in nonparametric Bayesian modeling for mixed-membership problems and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
