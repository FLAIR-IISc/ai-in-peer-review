Summary
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. The authors (i) derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by randomizing group sizes and marginalizing the beta process; (ii) use Bayes’ rule to obtain a group-size-dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) and a simple prediction rule; (iii) construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process; and (iv) provide empirical comparisons to HDP-LDA on three corpora using held-out perplexity and trace plots of mixing.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the DP/CRP and EPPF literature (Pitman), normalized random measures, and recent work on BNBP, gamma–Poisson, and negative-binomial processes. Previous efforts characterized BNBP via predictive structures for infinite random count matrices (e.g., negative-binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet), but not a mixed-membership EPPF and fully collapsed inference. The paper positions itself as providing a unified path to an EPPF for integer-valued processes in mixed-membership settings, which is not covered by Poisson–Kingman frameworks.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A nontrivial derivation of the BNBP ECPF and a group-size-dependent EPPF, plus a compact prediction rule. These are interesting contributions to Bayesian nonparametrics.
- Inference: A straightforward fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model with closed-form updates is practically useful and likely of interest to practitioners working on count-based models.
- Empirical indications: On three corpora, the sampler appears to mix faster than the baseline HDP direct assignment sampler; for comparable numbers of topics, the BNBP sometimes yields lower perplexity (i.e., a more compact representation without loss in predictive quality).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Fairness of comparisons: The main baseline is HDP-LDA with a direct-assignment sampler that is only partially collapsed. Since the proposed sampler is fully collapsed, mixing advantages may reflect inference scheme differences rather than model properties. A fair comparison should include a fully collapsed HDP (CRF-based) sampler and/or modern efficient HDP implementations; runtime per effective sample size is not reported.
- Limited baselines: No comparisons to other established count/topic models (e.g., Poisson factor analysis, gamma–Poisson/negative-binomial PFMs, NMF variants, CTM). The empirical case for “state-of-the-art predictive performance” is not sufficiently substantiated by a single baseline and a single metric.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The role of c is crucial in the theory but appears fixed; no inference or sensitivity for c is provided. The impact of the rj prior and learning vs fixing rj is not ablated. The strong dependence on η is documented, but broader hyperparameter robustness is missing.
- Evaluation metrics: Only perplexity is used. No topic quality/coherence or qualitative analyses, and no posterior predictive checks for overdispersion or count structure are shown.
- Scalability: The reported speed (2.5 s/iter on NIPS12) is helpful but not compelling by today’s standards; no complexity analysis vs number of groups/topics/words, and no large-scale experiments.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy. Several key derivations (e.g., normalization of the EPPF, Lemma 2 details, the digamma distribution role) are deferred to the appendix; providing a sketch in the main text would aid verification.
- Minor issues: some typos and phrasing; the “logbeta” construction is referenced but not integrated into the main derivation.

Originality
- Deriving a group-size-dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership and a usable prediction rule is novel and interesting. However, the paper stops short of showing generality beyond BNBP (despite claiming a “general method”), and the link to broader Poisson–Kingman or NRMI-based EPPFs is largely qualitative.

Significance
- On the theory side, the result fills a gap for BNBP and may stimulate further work on EPPFs for integer-valued processes. On the practice side, without broader and fairer empirical evidence, it is hard to conclude that this substantially advances topic modeling practice over well-established baselines.

Specific questions and requests
- How is c treated in inference? If fixed, justify the choice and provide sensitivity. If inferred, include the update and diagnostics.
- Include comparisons to a fully collapsed HDP-LDA (CRF) sampler and to Poisson factor analysis/negative-binomial PFMs under comparable computational budgets. Report runtime per effective sample size.
- Provide ablations: learning rj vs fixing; effect of γ0 prior; sensitivity to c; effect of document lengths (given the sample-size dependence).
- Clarify the theoretical implications of sample-size-dependent EPPFs (lack of the addition rule/projective consistency) for predictive use across corpora with varying lengths.
- Add alternative metrics (topic coherence) and qualitative topic examples.
- Confirm anonymity in the submission; the current code link appears de-anonymizing.

Pros
- Solid and interesting theoretical contribution (ECPF/EPPF/prediction rule for BNBP).
- Simple, fully collapsed sampler with closed-form updates.
- Potentially useful modeling behavior (stronger shrinkage; compact representations).

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is too narrow and not strictly fair; missing key baselines and metrics.
- Limited sensitivity analyses; unclear treatment of important hyperparameters (c).
- Clarity could be improved; key proofs relegated to appendix.
- Scalability and practical impact not convincingly demonstrated.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is promising, but the empirical validation is not sufficiently rigorous relative to established baselines, and important methodological choices are insufficiently examined. With stronger, fair comparisons, sensitivity analyses, and clarity improvements, this work could be competitive.
