This paper introduces an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The authors aim to provide a unified treatment, analogous to the EPPF for the Dirichlet Process, enabling fully collapsed inference. They derive the EPPF, a prediction rule, and demonstrate the approach with a topic model, showcasing fast convergence and competitive performance.

While the paper tackles a significant problem – extending Bayesian nonparametrics to grouped data with fully collapsed inference – and presents some novel contributions, I find a significant number of issues, ranging from minor inconsistencies to substantial gaps in justification, that ultimately weigh against acceptance. My assessment is particularly stringent due to the claim of providing a “unified treatment” which, in my view, is not fully substantiated.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The motivation for a BNBP EPPF is clear and well-articulated. Extending the EPPF framework to mixed-membership models is a valuable goal.
* **Derivation of EPPF:** The derivation of the EPPF, while complex, is a central contribution, attempting to fill a gap in existing methods.
* **Topic Model Demonstration:** The application to a topic model provides a concrete example and shows practical feasibility.
* **Collapsed Gibbs Sampler:** The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a key advantage, potentially offering computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous notational inconsistencies. For example, 'rj' is used both as a parameter of the NB distribution *and* as the group size. This makes following the derivations exceedingly difficult.  Similarly, the definitions of B, B0, and their relation to the Beta process are initially presented without sufficient clarity and require repeated reference to equations.
* **Lack of Justification for Group Size Randomization:** The initial step of randomizing group sizes (mj) feels ad-hoc. While mathematically convenient, its statistical justification is weak. Why is randomizing group size *necessary* to derive the EPPF, and what assumptions does this introduce?  This feels like a clever trick rather than a principled statistical choice.
* **EPPF Dependence on Group Sizes:** The authors acknowledge the EPPF is dependent on group sizes, contradicting the original spirit of the EPPF for DP which aims for sample size independence. While this is noted, the implications of this dependence aren't fully explored. The benefits of this dependence aren't clearly articulated—does it enhance modeling flexibility, or is it a limitation?
* **Comparison to Existing Work is Insufficient:** The comparison to related work (e.g., [16, 17]) is cursory. The authors claim to go beyond these papers, but the nuances of those differences are not sufficiently highlighted.  The connection to the "multi-scoop" IBP [10] is mentioned, but the specific advantages of the BNBP approach aren’t clearly delineated.  The paper *states* it is different, but doesn’t *demonstrate* why these differences matter.
* **Experimental Evaluation Limitations:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating competitive performance, is not entirely convincing. The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is overstated, given the reliance on standard corpora and metrics. A more thorough ablation study comparing different aspects of the BNBP topic model would be beneficial. The impact of the parameters a0, b0, e0, and f0 is not discussed.
* **Minor Technical Issues:**  Equation (6) is extraordinarily complex and difficult to parse.  Similarly, Lemma 4 (the prediction rule) lacks sufficient context. What exactly are the variables being predicted? The motivation for the specific form of the prediction rule is also not clearly explained.  Several equations (e.g., (7)) are presented without sufficient explanation of their intermediate steps.
* **Missing Proofs:** The paper claims proofs for Lemma 1 and 2 are in the Appendix, but the Appendix is not included in the submitted version. This is a major omission.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel derivation of an EPPF for the BNBP.
* Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a mixed-membership model.
* Competitive performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Numerous notational inconsistencies and clarity issues.
* Weak justification for the initial group size randomization.
* Lack of a thorough comparison to related work.
* Overstated claims of "state-of-the-art" performance.
* Missing Appendix containing crucial proofs.
* EPPF dependence on group sizes, which detracts from the theoretical elegance.
* Overall, the claim of providing a “unified treatment” feels unsubstantiated given the dependence on group sizes and the complex derivations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the problem and the novelty of the approach, the numerous issues outlined above lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper requires substantial revisions to address the clarity, justification, and completeness concerns. A more rigorous comparison to existing methods, a more thorough experimental evaluation, and inclusion of the missing appendix are essential. The authors need to significantly improve the presentation and clarify the theoretical contributions to make this work acceptable for NIPS.  The current manuscript reads as a sketch of an idea rather than a fully realized and rigorously validated contribution.
