

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian statistics by introducing a novel exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors address a critical gap in the literature: while nonparametric Bayesian priors for mixture modeling (like Dirichlet process and HDP) are well-developed, there is no unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling where data is grouped and we want to cluster within groups with shared partitions across groups. 

The key novelty lies in the authors' general method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling using integer-valued stochastic processes. Specifically, they first randomize group sizes to derive the joint distribution of group sizes and their partitions, then use Bayes' rule to obtain the BNBP's EPPF. This method is presented as a general framework applicable to other stochastic processes, which is a significant conceptual advance. The paper's main contributions include:

1. Deriving the BNBP's EPPF that describes the prior distribution of latent column-exchangeable random count matrices
2. Developing a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions
3. Constructing a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process
4. Demonstrating state-of-the-art predictive performance with compact corpus representation

The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations supported by proofs for key lemmas. The experimental results on three standard topic modeling corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) show that the BNBP topic model converges faster and mixes better than HDP-LDA, while achieving comparable or better predictive performance with fewer topics. This is particularly significant because the BNBP model automatically controls topic number without requiring manual tuning of hyperparameters like α in HDP-LDA.

The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty - the general method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling represents a significant theoretical advance. This method could be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes (like gamma-Poisson and gamma-negative binomial processes), potentially opening new avenues for research in mixed-membership modeling. The BNBP topic model is also practically valuable, offering a more compact representation of corpora with faster convergence.

While the paper's EPPF is mathematically complex, this is a necessary consequence of the problem's inherent complexity rather than a flaw. The authors clearly articulate the trade-off between model complexity and the benefits of a unified framework. The experimental results are well-designed and demonstrate the practical advantages of the BNBP approach.

I note a minor weakness: the paper could more clearly explain why the BNBP topic model achieves lower perplexity than HDP-LDA at comparable topic numbers despite higher perplexity for the same η. However, this is a minor point that doesn't detract from the paper's core contributions.

This paper stands out as a bold, novel contribution to nonparametric Bayesian statistics. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature with a theoretically sound approach that also demonstrates practical benefits. The general method for deriving EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling is particularly significant as it could potentially be applied to other stochastic processes beyond BNBP.

The paper's significance is further underscored by its potential to influence future research in nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The BNBP topic model provides a compelling alternative to HDP-LDA with faster convergence and better compactness, which are highly desirable properties in real-world applications.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS's focus on novel, high-impact research. The authors have developed a new theoretical framework with clear practical applications, and their methodological approach is both rigorous and innovative. While the mathematical derivations are complex, the paper is well-written and the contributions are clearly articulated.

In summary, this paper introduces a significant new concept (the general method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling) that advances the state of the art in nonparametric Bayesian statistics. The BNBP topic model demonstrates the practical value of this theoretical framework, showing competitive performance with faster convergence and more compact representation. The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution outweigh minor methodological complexities, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
