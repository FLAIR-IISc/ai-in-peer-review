This paper presents a significant theoretical and methodological contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics, specifically for mixed-membership modeling. The central idea is to derive the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), providing a unified framework analogous to the role the Chinese Restaurant Process plays for Dirichlet Process mixture models. The authors achieve this through a novel and clever technique: they first treat the group sizes as random variables drawn from a Poisson process, allowing them to derive a more tractable Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF). The desired EPPF for fixed, observed group sizes is then recovered by applying Bayes' rule. This theoretical result is then leveraged to construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, which is shown empirically to outperform the popular Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) topic model in terms of convergence, mixing, and predictive power for compact models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership context. This is a non-trivial task that has eluded previous work on the BNBP. The method of first randomizing the group sizes to derive the ECPF and then conditioning to obtain the EPPF is highly innovative and may be generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes (e.g., Gamma-Poisson Process), potentially opening up a new avenue for developing collapsed samplers for a broader class of models. This work takes a meaningful step towards a unified combinatorial view of mixed-membership models, extending the foundational work of Pitman [5] from single-set clustering to the more complex grouped-data setting.

2.  **Methodological Advancement:** The derivation of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model is a major practical advantage. As the authors correctly point out, popular samplers for the HDP-LDA are often only partially collapsed (e.g., the direct assignment sampler does not integrate out the global measure). Full marginalization, as achieved here, often leads to significantly improved MCMC convergence and mixing properties. The direct comparison of the predictive update equations (12) for BNBP and (14) for HDP-LDA clearly illuminates the distinct statistical properties of the two models: the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism versus the HDP's additive one. This provides valuable intuition about their differing behaviors.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the practical utility of the proposed sampler. The trace plots in Figure 2 are particularly convincing, clearly demonstrating the faster convergence and superior mixing of the BNBP sampler compared to the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler. The perplexity results in Figure 3 support the claim that the BNBP topic model can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance, often with a more compact representation (fewer topics) than the HDP-LDA. The analysis across different corpora and hyperparameter settings is thorough.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with a clear motivation, builds the theoretical argument step-by-step from the ECPF to the EPPF and prediction rule, and then demonstrates the utility of these results in a concrete application. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the connections to related work are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Complexity and Utility of the EPPF:** While the derivation of the EPPF in Eq. (9) is a key theoretical result, its functional form is exceedingly complex, involving a sum over all possible partitions of the data in the normalizing constant. This makes the EPPF itself computationally intractable. The paper's main practical payoff is the simple and elegant prediction rule in Eq. (10). The authors could be slightly more explicit that the EPPF serves as the formal justification for the sampler, but is not intended for direct computation itself.

2.  **"Group-size dependent EPPF":** The authors introduce the concept of a "group-size dependent EPPF," citing their own prior work [12]. This deviates from the classical definition of an EPPF [5], where projectivity (consistency under changing sample sizes) is a key property. While perfectly valid, this dependency is a crucial feature that distinguishes this framework from the classic theory. A brief discussion on the implications of this sample-size dependency would be beneficial for readers familiar with the traditional EPPF literature. Does it break any desirable theoretical properties, or is it a necessary feature for the mixed-membership setting?

3.  **Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The comparison to the HDP-LDA is appropriate and well-executed. However, it focuses on the direct assignment sampler. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss or compare against the Chinese Restaurant Franchise (CRF) representation of the HDP, which is also a fully collapsed sampler (though algorithmically more complex). Furthermore, the evaluation is based on perplexity and the number of topics. An analysis of topic quality (e.g., using topic coherence measures) would provide a more complete picture of the practical differences between the topics learned by the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage and the HDP's additive shrinkage.

4.  **Positioning Relative to [16, 17]:** The paper does a good job of citing recent related work on the combinatorial structure of BNBP-like processes. However, it could more sharply delineate its contribution. The key distinction is that works like [16, 17] primarily characterize the distribution of an infinite random count matrix itself (analogous to the IBP), whereas this paper's primary focus is on deriving the conditional distribution of partitions given fixed row sums (`m_j`), which is the central problem in mixed-membership modeling of observed data. Explicitly stating this difference would further highlight the paper's unique contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to both the theory and practice of Bayesian nonparametrics. The novel derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant theoretical advance, and the resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler provides a powerful and practical tool for mixed-membership modeling. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach over the widely used HDP-LDA. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions to the text. I strongly recommend acceptance.
