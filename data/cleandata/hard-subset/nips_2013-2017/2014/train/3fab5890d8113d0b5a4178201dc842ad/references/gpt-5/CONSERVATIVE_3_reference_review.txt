Summary
This paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. The key technical contribution is to derive a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for BNBP by first introducing an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that treats group sizes as random, marginalizing the beta process, and then applying Bayes’ rule to condition on observed group sizes. From this, the authors obtain: (i) a finite-dimensional ECPF (Eq. 6), (ii) a prior over a column-exchangeable random count matrix with i.i.d. columns (via a “digamma” cluster-size distribution and Dirichlet-multinomial within-column allocation), (iii) a predictive assignment rule (Eq. 10), and (iv) a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process (Eq. 12). Experiments on three corpora compare to HDP-LDA (direct assignment), suggesting faster convergence/mixing and comparable or lower perplexity at a more compact number of topics.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of CRMs/NRMIs and mixed-membership models. While EPPFs are classical for mixture models (e.g., CRP, Pitman’s EPPFs), a general EPPF treatment for mixed-membership is less established. Prior BNBP work has focused on count modeling, Poisson factorization, and feature allocations (e.g., IBP variants, Heaukulani & Roy; Zhou et al.), but not an explicit EPPF for grouped partitions. The paper’s group-size dependent EPPF and predictive rule appear novel and extend sample-size dependent EPPFs (Zhou & Walker) to the mixed-membership case.

Strengths
- Derivation: The path from BNBP to ECPF to a group-size dependent EPPF is technically interesting and, to my knowledge, new for mixed-membership modeling.
- Predictive rule: The simple assignment rule (Eq. 10) is appealing and provides intuition about global vs local popularity via a multiplicative form.
- Collapsed sampler: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP topic modeling is a useful algorithmic contribution; the factorization and marginalization are neat and practically relevant.
- Clarity of the modeling bridge: The beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation and the Poisson-to-multinomial link are well used to connect count and mixture modeling.

Weaknesses
- Experimental validation is limited relative to the paper’s claims. The only baseline is HDP-LDA with a direct-assignment sampler; there is no comparison to the collapsed CRF sampler (Teh et al., 2006), to modern, optimized HDP implementations, or to strong Poisson factorization baselines (e.g., gamma–NBP/GNBP variants). The claim of “state-of-the-art predictive performance” is not substantiated.
- Hyperparameter treatment may bias comparisons. It is unclear whether α for HDP is inferred (common practice) or tuned; using a direct-assignment sampler with fixed α can handicap HDP. Similarly, sensitivity of BNBP to c, γ0, and priors on rj is not explored.
- Perplexity evaluation lacks rigor. Results are shown versus η and versus inferred number of topics, but without careful control for equal model capacity, runtime, or statistical significance. Only three corpora are used; no topic quality (e.g., coherence) or downstream tasks are reported.
- Scalability claims are weak. Wall-clock per iteration in unoptimized Matlab on small/medium corpora is not informative. There is no analysis of computational complexity as a function of KJ, nor memory footprint under realistic scales.
- Theoretical details are mostly deferred to the appendix. Given the centrality of Lemmas 2–4, more intuition and partial derivations in the main text would help assess correctness (e.g., conditions ensuring normalizability of the EPPF, implications of group-size dependence for exchangeability).
- Clarity/notation: The manuscript is notation-heavy with several typos (“perdition rule”), and could use tighter editing. The “digamma distribution” is nonstandard in ML; a clearer definition (support, moments) and intuition would help.

Quality
The mathematical development appears plausible and is consistent with known BNBP marginalizations and CRM calculus, but key proofs are absent from the main text. The sampler derivation is sound and standard for collapsed topic models. The experimental section does not yet meet the level of rigor expected to validate superiority over established baselines.

Clarity
Generally understandable for experts in BNP/CRMs, but dense. The paper would benefit from:
- A high-level overview of the ECPF→EPPF construction.
- Explicit normalization and support for the “digamma” PMF.
- Clear description of hyperparameter inference (α in HDP, c, γ0, rj).
- Complexity analysis of the collapsed sampler.

Originality
The EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership and the associated prediction rule appear original. The collapsed topic model instantiation is a reasonable application of the theory.

Significance
Potentially significant for BNP practitioners: providing an EPPF and collapsed sampler for BNBP could enable new mixed-membership models beyond HDP. However, the empirical section falls short of convincingly demonstrating advantages, which tempers practical impact.

Questions for the authors
1) Do you infer α for HDP-LDA? If not, please add a version with α sampled (e.g., Escobar–West) and a collapsed CRF baseline.
2) How sensitive are results to c, γ0, and priors on rj? Please include ablations and posterior summaries.
3) Can you provide a left-to-right perplexity estimate and topic coherence metrics?
4) What are the computational and memory complexities per iteration as functions of KJ, J, V?
5) Does the group-size dependent EPPF preserve exchangeability within groups and across groups conditional on m? Any implications for consistency?

Pros
- Novel EPPF/ECPF derivation for BNBP mixed-membership.
- Simple, interpretable predictive rule.
- Fully collapsed sampler; clear link to Poisson factorization.

Cons
- Insufficient empirical validation against strong, established baselines.
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and scalability.
- Dense presentation; theoretical details deferred.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theory is promising and could be impactful, but the experimental evidence and comparative analysis are not yet strong enough to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section with stronger baselines, fair hyperparameter treatment, scalability analysis, and clearer exposition would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence
High for evaluation of empirical rigor; moderate for deep technical correctness pending full proofs.
