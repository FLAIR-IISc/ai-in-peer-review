Summary
This paper develops a sample-size (group-size) dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for mixed-membership modeling under the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The key methodological step is to “Poissonize” each group’s size, derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by marginalizing the beta process in a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, then condition back on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to obtain the EPPF that governs column-exchangeable random count matrices with fixed row sums. From this, the authors derive a simple prediction rule to simulate partitions and construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that analytically marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process. Empirically, on three corpora, the BNBP sampler mixes faster than an HDP-LDA direct-assignment sampler; for a comparable number of topics, the BNBP achieves lower perplexity, while in general it tends to yield more compact representations (fewer topics) due to a multiplicative shrinkage mechanism.

Relation to prior work
- Links to CRMs/NRMIs and EPPFs (Pitman; Lijoi–Prünster) and to sigma-stable Poisson–Kingman samplers, noting BNBP is outside that family.
- Builds on BNBP/PFA work (Zhou et al.) and combinatorial analyses of BNBP/IBP variants (Heaukulani & Roy), but focuses on grouped partitions with fixed row sums—distinct from binary features and infinite count matrices.
- Contrasts with HDP and the Chinese restaurant franchise (CRF) for collapsed inference; here, full marginalization avoids CRF bookkeeping.

Technical assessment
- The augmentation Xj|Θj ~ PP(Θj), Θj|B ~ ΓP[rj, B/(1−B)], B ~ BP(c,B0) is standard and clarifies the mixed-membership connection via Poissonization.
- The ECPF (Eq. 6) features the expected exp{−γ0[ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c)]} term from integrating out unobserved atoms, and yields a Poisson number of columns with digamma-distributed sizes (Sibuya) and Dirichlet–multinomial row allocations—consistent with earlier BNBP count-matrix results.
- The sample-size dependent EPPF (Eq. 9) is complicated but conceptually sound; its primary computational role is to justify the predictive rule (Eq. 10) underpinning a marginal Gibbs sampler.
- The prediction rule has an interpretable multiplicative form: an “across-group popularity” factor for cluster k times a “within-group” factor (n−jijk + rj). However, the new-cluster probability appears to differ across places in the paper: sometimes involving ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c), elsewhere showing γ0 rj/(c+r·). Please reconcile these constants and indicate precisely what cancels under conditioning on m. A short derivation or pointer would improve confidence.
- The topic-model sampler (Eq. 12) is a clean fully collapsed rule. The V·γ0/(c+r·) rj term for a new topic likewise would benefit from a clear statement of where the digamma normalization goes after conditioning on m and integrating φ.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a general recipe to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership models built from integer-valued CRMs by randomizing group sizes and conditioning back.
- Clear probabilistic structure for BNBP count matrices: Poisson number of clusters, digamma cluster-size distribution, DirMult row allocations.
- A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that is simple to implement and mixes quickly in practice.
- Insightful comparison with HDP-LDA: multiplicative vs additive shrinkage of global/local popularity, with implications for compactness and perplexity.
- Empirical evidence on several corpora supporting faster convergence and competitive or better perplexity at a given K.

Weaknesses and questions
- Consistency of constants in prediction rule: clarify the presence/absence of ψ(c+r·)−ψ(c) in the new-cluster term for both the partition model (Eq. 10) and topic model (Eq. 12). As written, there are apparent inconsistencies.
- Experimental fairness: the HDP baseline is the direct-assignment (partially collapsed) sampler. Since your sampler is fully collapsed, some of the mixing advantage may be due to that difference. Please add a baseline using the CRF collapsed sampler (Teh 2006) or cite results showing comparable mixing for that baseline.
- Sensitivity/ablation: the paper stresses the importance of inferring rj, but shows no quantitative analysis. Please include an ablation fixing rj and show the impact on partitions/perplexity; also report posterior summaries of rj (e.g., histograms vs document length).
- Broader baselines: include Poisson factorization/NBFA baselines (e.g., Gamma–Poisson models) on perplexity to situate performance among count-model topic methods.
- The EPPF is sample-size dependent; while this is intentional and well-motivated, a brief discussion of projectivity/exchangeability properties (e.g., behavior under merging/splitting groups or adding observations) would strengthen the theoretical section.
- Clarity/notation: the text is dense and repeats some statements. A small worked example of generating NJ via Eq. 8 would aid intuition.

Quality: High, with solid probabilistic derivations and a practically useful sampler. Please resolve the normalization/new-cluster probability constants and improve experimental baselines.

Clarity: Generally good but heavy; minor typos and duplicated words (“disjoint disjoint”, “perdition rule”), and inconsistent constants should be fixed. A schematic/pseudocode for the sampler would help reproducibility.

Originality: Strong. While BNBP and IBP variants are known, deriving a group-size dependent EPPF for mixed-membership and a fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process is novel.

Significance: High. The recipe to obtain EPPFs for integer-valued CRMs could influence future mixed-membership models (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson). The sampler is practically valuable for topic modeling and other grouped count data.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel EPPF/ECPF construction for mixed-membership with integer-valued CRMs
- Fully collapsed, simple, and fast sampler; strong empirical mixing
- Clear theoretical characterization (digamma/DirMult factorization)
- Useful insights into shrinkage mechanisms vs HDP

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistencies/missing constants in prediction rules need clarification
- Baseline comparison omits fully collapsed HDP (CRF) sampler
- Limited analysis of rj inference and sensitivity
- Clarity can be improved in derivations and notation

Overall: A strong and timely contribution. Conditional on clarifying the predictive constants and strengthening the experimental comparison and ablations, I recommend acceptance.
