Summary
The paper develops a principled EPPF-style characterization for mixed-membership models driven by the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The key idea is to first construct a sample-size dependent model (randomizing group sizes) to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by marginalizing the beta process; from this, the authors obtain (i) a prior over a column-exchangeable random count matrix with fixed row sums, (ii) a group-size dependent EPPF for the BNBP, and (iii) a simple prediction rule enabling fully collapsed inference. Building on these results, the paper proposes a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the infinite-dimensional beta process, with closed-form updates. Empirically, on three corpora, the sampler converges quickly and yields competitive or better perplexities than HDP-LDA for comparable numbers of topics, often using a more compact set of topics.

Relation to prior work
- Prior EPPF/CRP results cover mixture modeling of a single dataset (DP, Pitman’s EPPF, NRMIs). Mixed-membership with shared clusters across groups is typically handled by HDP via the Chinese restaurant franchise; fully collapsed inference for BNBP/GNBP has been lacking.
- Recent work on BNBP/negative-binomial IBP and variants (e.g., “ice cream” buffet, multi-scoop IBP) addresses feature allocations and infinite random count matrices, not the fixed-group-size mixed-membership partition law.
- The paper generalizes sample-size dependent EPPFs to grouped settings and derives a tractable prediction rule, which is new in this mixed-membership count setting.
- The matrix prior interpretation (Poisson number of i.i.d. columns with digamma-distributed column sizes and Dirichlet-multinomial partitioning across groups) provides an insightful probabilistic decomposition.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: The route from ECPF (with randomized group sizes) to a group-size dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership is elegant and, to my knowledge, new. It fills a real gap in the literature by giving a collapsed representation and prediction rule for BNBP in grouped mixture modeling.
- Technical soundness: The augmentation (beta–gamma–Poisson) and marginalization steps are standard and carefully applied; the resulting finite-dimensional distributions, Poisson number of columns, digamma/DirMult structure, and the prediction rule are internally consistent and useful.
- Practical impact: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model is a substantial practical advance; it removes truncation and auxiliary indexing (no CRF bookkeeping) and mixes fast in experiments.
- Empirics: The experiments, though not exhaustive, are thoughtful: they examine convergence/mixing, effect of η, compactness vs perplexity trade-offs, and provide fair training/test splits and multiple corpora. Code availability supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines and fairness: The comparison is against the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler, which is known to mix more slowly than the CRF-based collapsed sampler. Including (or at least discussing) results vs the CRF sampler would strengthen claims about mixing and perplexity. Also clarify hyperparameter learning for HDP (α, γ) and the base DP, to ensure fair tuning across methods.
- Clarity and notation: The manuscript is dense. Some notation is heavy (e.g., repeated overloaded dots and primes), and important definitions (e.g., the “digamma distribution”) should be foregrounded with intuition and normalization checks. A concise “map” from ECPF to EPPF to prediction rule would help the reader.
- Scope and generality: The authors suggest the method could generalize to other integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP). A brief sketch or proposition showing what conditions are needed for the ECPF→EPPF route to go through would increase the broader impact.
- Evaluation breadth: Perplexity is the main metric; qualitative topic inspections or downstream tasks (e.g., document classification) could better illustrate the claimed compactness vs performance benefits. Some runtime curves vs number of topics and dataset size would also be informative.
- Positioning vs Heaukulani & Roy (combinatorial structure of BNBP) and related negative-binomial process works: The paper cites these but could more explicitly delineate differences (feature allocations vs partitions with fixed group sizes; exchangeability structures; inference implications).

Quality
Technically solid overall. The derivations appear correct and consistent with the augmented process representation, and the prediction rule is compelling. Some proofs are deferred to the appendix; a bit more intuition in the main text would improve accessibility.

Clarity
Generally clear but very dense. Improving notation hygiene and adding a concise running example would help. The empirical section is readable and practical.

Originality
High. Providing an EPPF-style law and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership with fixed group sizes, and exploiting it for a fully collapsed sampler, is a notable and timely contribution.

Significance
High. Both theoretically (a new way to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership count processes) and practically (a fast, collapsed BNBP topic model). Likely to influence subsequent work on nonparametric count processes and mixed-membership inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical framework (ECPF→EPPF) for BNBP mixed-membership
- Simple, effective prediction rule enabling fully collapsed inference
- Practical sampler with good mixing and competitive perplexity, often with fewer topics
- Clear potential for generalization to other count processes

Cons against acceptance
- Limited baseline strength (no CRF-collapsed HDP comparison)
- Heavy notation and some clarity gaps
- Evaluation focused on perplexity; limited qualitative or downstream analyses

Minor comments/typos
- Some duplicated words (“disjoint disjoint”), formatting glitches in equations, and missing years in references.
- Please define and motivate the “digamma distribution” earlier and verify support/normalization explicitly.

Recommendation: Accept
Confidence: High
