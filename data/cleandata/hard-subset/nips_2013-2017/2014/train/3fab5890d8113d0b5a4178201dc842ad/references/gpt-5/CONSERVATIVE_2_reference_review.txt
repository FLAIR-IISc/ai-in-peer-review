Summary
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. The main theoretical contribution is to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for grouped data by introducing a group-size–dependent model and then, via Bayes’ rule, an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) that governs column-exchangeable random count matrices with fixed row sums. This yields (i) a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions under the BNBP, (ii) a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, and (iii) an empirical study comparing to HDP-LDA on three corpora.

Relation to prior work
The work sits among nonparametric Bayesian mixture/mixed-membership models: Dirichlet process/CRP, NRMIs, and HDP for grouped data. It builds on BNBP/negative-binomial processes for counts and related combinatorial structures (e.g., negative binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet). Prior work has characterized BNBP as an integer-valued process and provided slice/truncated inference; Heaukulani & Roy and Zhou et al. (arXiv) have discussed combinatorial aspects/random count matrices. This paper’s novelty is specifically an ECPF/EPPF for grouped data leading to a fully collapsed sampler.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A carefully constructed route from an augmented beta–gamma–Poisson representation to a sample-size–dependent ECPF and then an EPPF for grouped data. The predictive rule (Eq. 10) is intuitive and useful.
- Collapsed inference: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP topic modeling is appealing; eliminating the beta process and weights analytically is nontrivial and can yield better mixing.
- Empirical indications: On three corpora, the sampler mixes faster than a standard HDP-LDA direct-assignment baseline; the BNBP often achieves comparable or lower perplexity with fewer topics (i.e., more compact representations).
- Clarity of the high-level story: The link from count modeling to mixed membership via Poisson augmentation is well motivated.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/soundness
- The EPPF is explicitly group-size dependent and does not satisfy the usual addition rule; while the paper cites justification (Zhou & Walker), it would be important to provide sanity checks that the proposed EPPF reduces appropriately in limiting/special cases (e.g., J=1; relation to CRP-like behavior), beyond referencing. These checks are not shown.
- Many derivations (e.g., Lemmas 2–4) are deferred to the appendix; the main text provides limited proof sketches. Given the centrality of these results, more in-text verification or cross-checks would increase confidence.
- The “digamma distribution” for column sizes is uncommon; the normalization and support conditions require c>0. The paper could better justify existence/finite-variance conditions and discuss behavior as c→0 or large r⋅.

Empirical validation
- Baselines are weak. The sole baseline is HDP-LDA with the direct-assignment sampler, which is known to mix slowly and is not fully collapsed. A fair comparison should include the collapsed Chinese Restaurant Franchise sampler for HDP-LDA (Teh et al., 2006) and/or strong modern implementations (e.g., split-merge, sparse collapsed HDP samplers), as well as competitive Poisson factorization baselines.
- Hyperparameter treatment appears asymmetric. BNBP learns rj and γ0; it is unclear how α (and γ for the top-level DP) are handled for HDP—are they fixed/tuned/learned? Without learning α, the HDP baseline may be handicapped.
- Evaluation is limited to perplexity under a single data split protocol. State-of-the-art topic model evaluation often uses left-to-right perplexity, topic coherence, and sensitivity analyses. The current study does not establish “state-of-the-art” conclusively.
- Efficiency claims are mostly qualitative (trace plots and wall clock per iteration in MATLAB). Per-token throughput and convergence diagnostics against equally optimized baselines are missing.

Clarity
- The paper is notation-dense; several definitions (e.g., Digam) and assumptions are presented tersely. Some figures/tables illustrating the generative mechanism and the effect of rj and c on cluster size distributions would aid intuition.
- References are incomplete/inconsistently formatted; several lack years/pages.

Originality
- Deriving a group-size–dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership modeling appears novel and useful. However, the connection to closely related work (Heaukulani & Roy; Zhou et al. on random count matrices and negative binomial processes) could be tightened to delineate what is fundamentally new here versus reorganization of known components (e.g., Poisson augmentation + gamma-Beta conjugacy + DirMult and Sibuya/digamma structures).

Significance
- If correct and adopted, the EPPF and prediction rule can enable collapsed samplers for other integer-valued processes. For topic modeling, the practical impact hinges on demonstrating clear performance gains over strong HDP and Poisson factorization baselines; current evidence is suggestive but not definitive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to: (i) collapsed CRF sampler for HDP-LDA; (ii) competitive Poisson factorization methods; (iii) other negative binomial process topic models (including truncated/adaptively truncated versions) with parallelization where relevant.
- Learn HDP hyperparameters (α, γ) and report sensitivity. Likewise, show sensitivity to c, γ0, and priors on rj for BNBP; include ablations isolating the multiplicative shrinkage effect.
- Use left-to-right perplexity and topic coherence metrics; report statistical significance across multiple splits.
- Provide sanity checks: reductions/special cases; posterior predictive checks; behavior as rj vary; relationship to σ-stable PK models and why BNBP lies outside that class.
- Improve clarity: expand on the digamma distribution properties; ensure complete bibliographic info; add intuitive explanations and diagrams.

Pros
- Solid theoretical development yielding a usable prediction rule.
- Fully collapsed sampler with promising mixing.
- Potentially general method applicable to other integer-valued processes.

Cons
- Empirical validation against weak baselines; claims of state-of-the-art not substantiated.
- Heavy reliance on appendix proofs; limited in-text verification and sanity checks.
- Nonstandard, sample-size–dependent EPPF without thorough discussion of implications.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper does not yet meet the empirical rigor expected at NIPS: stronger baselines, fair hyperparameter treatment, and broader evaluation are needed. With strengthened experiments and clearer positioning relative to closely related work, this could become a compelling contribution.
