This paper introduces a novel method for deriving the marginal distribution of exchangeable random partitions for mixed-membership models based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). Mixed-membership modeling, with the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) as a prominent example, aims to cluster grouped data using a shared, potentially infinite set of clusters. While the BNBP has been proposed as an alternative, it has lacked a fully collapsed inference scheme, limiting its practical application. This paper's central contribution is the derivation of the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP in this context. The authors achieve this through a clever, indirect approach: they first define a model where group sizes are random and derive the joint distribution of these sizes and their partitions (the Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function, or ECPF). By marginalizing out the group sizes and applying Bayes' rule, they arrive at the desired EPPF for fixed, observed group sizes. This theoretical result enables the derivation of a simple and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework by developing a BNBP topic model and comparing it empirically to HDP-LDA on several text corpora, showing faster convergence, better mixing, and strong predictive performance, particularly for models that favor compact representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting is a significant and novel theoretical contribution. It fills a well-known gap in the literature, providing a complete and analytically tractable Bayesian nonparametric framework for this class of models. The technique of using a size-dependent ECPF as an intermediate step is elegant and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening up new avenues for research. This work makes the BNBP a much more viable and principled alternative to the HDP.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully and build upon one another logically. The application to topic modeling is a natural and effective way to demonstrate the framework's power. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard datasets and metrics (held-out perplexity). The comparison against HDP-LDA is appropriate and insightful. The authors are commendably transparent about the model's behavior, noting that the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism leads to more compact models compared to the HDP's additive one, and they fairly discuss the performance trade-offs.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the central problem, the proposed solution, and the resulting benefits. The hierarchical model description is clear, and the progression from the general theory (ECPF, EPPF, prediction rule) to the specific application (BNBP topic model) is easy to follow. The direct comparison of the Gibbs update equations for LDA, HDP-LDA, and the proposed BNBP-LDA (Eqs. 12-14) is particularly effective at providing intuition about the differences between these models.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Choice of HDP Baseline:** The experiments compare against the direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA. While this sampler is popular and its update equation allows for a clean comparison, it is known to mix more slowly than the original Chinese Restaurant Franchise (CRF) based sampler. A comparison against the CRF-based sampler would constitute a stronger baseline for mixing and convergence, though the authors' point about cumbersome book-keeping is valid. The paper would be strengthened by either including this comparison or, at a minimum, acknowledging this distinction and justifying the choice of the direct assignment sampler more explicitly.

2.  **Intuition on Hyperparameters:** The paper provides a good visual demonstration of the effect of the dispersion parameters `r_j`. However, more intuition could be provided for the global hyperparameters `c` and `gamma_0`. From the prediction rule (Eq. 10), `gamma_0` appears to control the base rate of creating new clusters, analogous to a concentration parameter. The role of `c` is less obvious. A brief discussion on the influence of `c` and guidance on setting it (it appears to be fixed in the experiments) would be valuable for practitioners.

3.  **Utility of the EPPF Formula:** The explicit formula for the EPPF (Eq. 9) is very complex, involving a normalization constant that sums over all possible partitions. While its derivation is the key theoretical step, its practical utility lies in licensing the derivation of the simple predictive rule (Eq. 10), rather than in direct computation. This point is implicitly made but could be stated more explicitly to avoid any confusion that one might need to compute with Eq. 9 directly.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant theoretical result: the EPPF for the BNBP in mixed-membership models.
*   Develops the first fully collapsed, efficient, and easy-to-implement Gibbs sampler for this model class, which is a major practical advancement.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence that the resulting model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves excellent predictive performance compared to the standard HDP-LDA baseline.
*   The paper is a model of clarity in its writing and organization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison could be strengthened by using a more powerful HDP-LDA sampler (CRF-based) as a baseline.
*   The intuitive interpretation of some model hyperparameters could be more fully developed.

**Decision:**

This is an excellent paper that makes both a strong theoretical contribution and a valuable practical one. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and the resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant advance for Bayesian nonparametrics. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not undermine the core contributions. The work is of high quality, original, significant, and clearly presented, making it a very strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
