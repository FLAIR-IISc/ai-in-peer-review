Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. The main technical device is to temporarily randomize group sizes to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), marginalize the beta process, and then condition back on observed group sizes to obtain the EPPF and a simple prediction rule. This closes an important gap: previous BNBP-based models lacked a marginal partition law and relied on truncation or slice sampling.

The authors then exploit the EPPF to build a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, marginalizing topics, topic weights, and the beta process. The sampler induces a multiplicative shrinkage term that depends on both global cluster popularity and group-specific dispersion rj, in contrast to the additive structure in HDP-LDA (Teh et al., 2006). Empirically, the method converges faster and mixes better than HDP’s direct-assignment sampler, and typically achieves comparable or lower perplexity using fewer topics on three corpora. The work relates to DP/CRP and EPPF literature (Blackwell & MacQueen; Pitman), HDP and Chinese restaurant franchise, IBP and negative binomial process feature models, and recent BNBP/Poisson factorization work where truncation or slice schemes were used rather than an explicit EPPF.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a group-size–dependent EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP, obtained via an ECPF construction and exact marginalization of the beta process.
- Derivation illuminates the BNBP’s prior over column-exchangeable random count matrices (Poisson number of clusters, digamma-distributed cluster sizes, Dirichlet-multinomial group allocations).
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with closed-form updates; no Chinese restaurant franchise bookkeeping; empirically fast convergence and good mixing.
- Concrete instantiation to topic modeling with competitive or improved perplexity at a given number of topics; compact representations (fewer topics).
- Useful perspective contrasting multiplicative shrinkage (BNBP) vs additive (HDP), and highlighting the role of rj in controlling overdispersion.

Weaknesses
- Complexity and clarity: while the final sampler is simple to implement, the theoretical development is heavy, and some steps (e.g., conditioning arguments from ECPF to EPPF, properties of the “digamma” count distribution) will be hard for non-specialists to digest.
- Empirical gains over strong HDP baselines are not consistently large; the narrative “state-of-the-art predictive performance” would benefit from stronger evidence across metrics and datasets.
- Comparisons are to the HDP direct-assignment sampler; a comparison to a collapsed CRF-based HDP sampler and to competitive Poisson factorization baselines would better calibrate the practical advantages.
- The paper lacks an intuitive explanation for when and why multiplicative shrinkage should outperform HDP in practice; the role and learning dynamics of rj are central but underexplained.
- The device of randomizing group sizes to derive the EPPF is elegant but may raise concerns about modeling–inference mismatch; a more explicit discussion of the conditioning argument and its implications would help.

Quality
- The derivations appear technically sound, with careful augmentation (beta–gamma–Poisson) and full marginalization. The prediction rule is clear and accords with the stated EPPF.
- Experiments are competently executed, with convergence diagnostics and perplexity evaluations. Claims about mixing and compactness are supported; claims about predictive superiority are only partially supported.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some sections are dense and notation-heavy. The paper would benefit from more intuition (e.g., variance–mean analysis of counts under multiplicative vs additive shrinkage) and a higher-level roadmap of the ECPF→EPPF pathway.
- Providing a succinct pseudocode block for the collapsed sampler and a figure illustrating the generative story would improve accessibility.

Originality
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership models with a usable prediction rule and fully collapsed sampler. The ECPF-to-EPPF methodology is broadly useful.

Significance
- High for the BNP community: having an EPPF and collapsed sampler for BNBP opens the door to principled, truncation-free inference in mixed-membership count models. Practical impact will depend on dissemination and clearer guidance on when BNBP is preferable to HDP.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide an intuitive explanation (and perhaps a variance–mean calculation) showing how the multiplicative term n·k/(c+n·k+r·)·(njk+rj) leads to fewer but better-supported topics, and under what regimes it should outperform HDP?
- How sensitive are results to the priors on rj, c, and γ0? Please include a sensitivity or ablation study, especially on learning rj vs fixing it.
- How does your method compare in wall-clock time and perplexity to a collapsed CRF HDP sampler and to gamma–Poisson factorization with hierarchical gamma priors?
- Could you report topic coherence or human interpretability measures in addition to perplexity?
- To what extent can your ECPF→EPPF technique extend to GNBP or gamma–Poisson processes? Any technical obstacles?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add intuition and a short derivation sketch for the “digamma” count distribution and its role.
- Include ablations on rj and hyperparameters; discuss practical defaults.
- Compare against a collapsed HDP (CRF) implementation and a modern Poisson factorization baseline.
- Report wall-clock vs performance trade-offs and per-iteration cost.
- Provide pseudocode and release code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (BNBP EPPF and prediction rule) with clear methodological payoff (fully collapsed sampler).
- Good empirical behavior (faster mixing, compact models) and competitive perplexities.
- Potentially broad impact for mixed-membership count modeling beyond topics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy theory may limit accessibility; practical gains over strong baselines are modest in current experiments.
- Missing comparisons to the strongest HDP/Poisson factorization baselines; limited qualitative analysis and intuition.
- Some claims of “state-of-the-art” could be better substantiated.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The theoretical contribution is substantial and timely, and the method is promising; with stronger empirical comparisons and improved exposition, this could be a notable advance for BNP mixed-membership modeling.
