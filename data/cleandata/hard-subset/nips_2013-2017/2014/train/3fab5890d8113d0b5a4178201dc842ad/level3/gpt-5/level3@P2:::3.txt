Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. The authors begin by introducing a group-size–dependent exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that characterizes the joint law of group sizes and their partitions, and then obtain the desired EPPF for fixed group sizes via Bayes’ rule. They also derive a simple prediction rule that enables simulation and fully collapsed Gibbs sampling without truncating the beta process or resorting to slice sampling. As an application, they instantiate a BNBP topic model and compare it against HDP-LDA, highlighting a distinct multiplicative shrinkage mechanism that tends to favor more compact topic representations with fast mixing.

The work connects to several strands in Bayesian nonparametrics. It parallels the Chinese restaurant process/EPPF developments for Dirichlet process mixtures (Pitman; Teh et al., 2006) but extends the scope to mixed-membership count models where deriving marginal partition laws has been notably harder. Prior BNBP inference relied on finite truncation or slice sampling. Related developments on Poisson–Kingman and σ-stable processes (e.g., Lomelí et al.) do not encompass the BNBP considered here. The paper also clarifies relations to negative-binomial IBP variants and to gamma–Poisson mixed-membership count models. Overall, the contribution fills a gap: a principled marginal (truncation-free) characterization and collapsed inference scheme for BNBP in grouped count data.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations appear careful and correct. The move from the augmented beta–gamma–Poisson representation to an ECPF, then to a group-size–dependent EPPF, is well motivated. The prediction rule is intuitive and matches the stated EPPF. Proofs are delegated to the appendix but are outlined clearly.
- Empirical support: Experiments on three corpora show that the fully collapsed BNBP sampler mixes quickly and tends to achieve comparable or better perplexity than HDP-LDA when the inferred number of topics is similar, with fewer topics overall. The performance gains over HDP-LDA are modest; the more consistent benefit is compactness and convergence speed.
- Evaluation completeness: The main baseline is HDP-LDA with a direct-assignment sampler. Given the focus on inference, it would strengthen the paper to include comparisons to (i) slice-sampling–based BNBP inference and (ii) truncated/block Gibbs samplers for BNBP, to quantify mixing and wall-clock tradeoffs under different representations.

Clarity
The manuscript is well written and organized. The roles of the ECPF, EPPF, and prediction rule are clearly delineated. Notation is heavy but standard for this area. The section on the “digamma distribution” could use a brief intuition and reference for unfamiliar readers. A small schematic or pseudo-code of the collapsed sampler would improve accessibility. The discussion contrasting multiplicative vs additive shrinkage (BNBP vs HDP) is clear and helpful.

Originality
Deriving an EPPF for BNBP in the mixed-membership setting and showing how to proceed from a size-dependent ECPF to a usable EPPF/prediction rule is novel. The general recipe (randomize group sizes, derive ECPF, then condition to obtain an EPPF) is conceptually useful and likely extensible to other integer-valued processes (e.g., gamma–Poisson, gamma–negative binomial). The fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process is a substantial methodological contribution distinct from existing HDP samplers.

Significance
The contribution is important for practitioners who want truncation-free, fast-mixing inference in count-based mixed-membership models. Even if perplexity improvements over HDP-LDA are not dramatic, enabling a fully collapsed BNBP sampler with simple updates and good mixing is valuable. The compactness (fewer topics for similar perplexity) and convergence speed can matter in practice. The proposed derivation strategy may inspire similar developments for other processes.

Pros
- Clear and principled derivation of an EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP in grouped data.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the infinite beta process and demonstrates fast convergence and good mixing.
- Insightful comparison to HDP-LDA, highlighting distinct shrinkage mechanisms and practical implications.
- Solid experiments on multiple corpora; code availability is noted.

Cons
- Empirical gains in predictive performance over HDP-LDA are generally marginal; the main advantage is compactness/speed.
- Baselines are limited to HDP-LDA; missing a direct comparison to slice-based and truncated BNBP inference schemes.
- Less discussion on sensitivity to hyperparameters (c, γ0) and the posterior behavior of dispersion parameters rj.
- The size-dependent nature of the EPPF and its implications (e.g., projectivity) could be discussed more explicitly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to (a) slice sampling for BNBP (e.g., Heaukulani & Roy; Broderick et al.) and (b) truncated/block BNBP samplers, reporting convergence diagnostics and wall-clock to a target perplexity.
- Discuss uncollapsed or partially collapsed variants for parallelization and quantify mixing vs throughput tradeoffs relative to the proposed fully collapsed sampler.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for c, γ0, and priors on rj; visualize posterior dispersion parameters and their impact on partition structure.
- Include topic coherence metrics and sparsity/compactness analyses, complementing perplexity.
- Clarify the “digamma distribution” terminology and provide a short intuition; include sampler pseudo-code for reproducibility.
- Consider comparing against a more collapsed HDP (CRF) sampler to control for the known mixing benefits of collapsed inference.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong submission. The theoretical development is original and technically sound, and the resulting inference procedure is elegant and practically useful. While the empirical advantage over HDP-LDA is not overwhelming, the conceptual and algorithmic contributions justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance after addressing minor experimental clarifications and comparisons.
