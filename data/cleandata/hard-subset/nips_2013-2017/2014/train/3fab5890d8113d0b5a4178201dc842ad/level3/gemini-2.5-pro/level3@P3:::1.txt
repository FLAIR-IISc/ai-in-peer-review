### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for mixed-membership modeling based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While the BNBP has been previously proposed for such tasks, inference has been limited by the lack of a marginal distribution governing the random partitions, necessitating truncation or approximation methods. The central contribution of this work is the derivation of the group-size dependent Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP.

The authors achieve this significant theoretical result through a clever technique: they first treat the group sizes (e.g., document lengths) as random variables. This allows them to derive an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF) describing the joint distribution of group sizes and their partitions. By applying Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes, they then arrive at the desired EPPF. This EPPF enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based topic model, which marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process. The resulting model and sampler are compared empirically against the widely-used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP). The authors position their work as an important step toward a unified EPPF-based treatment of mixed-membership models, beyond the scope of the Dirichlet process.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of research. The derivation of the EPPF from the BNBP is a non-trivial and impressive theoretical achievement. The proofs provided seem correct, and the resulting prediction rule (Eq. 10) and collapsed sampler (Eq. 12) follow logically from the theory. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed model to the most relevant baseline (HDP-LDA) on standard corpora using a standard evaluation metric (perplexity). The authors are also candid about the complexity of the final EPPF expression.

However, the experimental results, while demonstrating faster convergence, do not provide compelling evidence for the model's superior predictive performance. The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" seems overstated, as Figure 3 shows that HDP-LDA often achieves lower perplexity, albeit with a larger number of topics. The BNBP model's advantage appears to be in achieving comparable perplexity with a more compact topic representation, which is a valuable but more nuanced contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for deriving an EPPF for the BNBP is clearly articulated in the introduction. Section 2 walks the reader through the novel technical steps of deriving the ECPF and then the EPPF, which is helpful. The direct comparison of the Gibbs update equations for the proposed model, LDA, and HDP-LDA in Section 3.1 is particularly effective, as it crisply highlights the structural differences between the models (i.e., the multiplicative versus additive sharing mechanisms).

The main weakness in clarity lies in the lack of an intuitive explanation for the model's behavior. The paper successfully shows *that* the BNBP leads to a multiplicative interaction between a topic's global popularity and its local, document-specific popularity. However, it does not explain *why* this modeling choice is desirable or what underlying assumptions about the data it reflects. Providing such intuition would significantly strengthen the paper and help readers understand when to prefer this model over the HDP.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. Deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in the context of mixed-membership modeling is a novel and significant contribution to the Bayesian nonparametrics literature. The technique of randomizing group sizes to derive marginals is an innovative methodological contribution that may be generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes. This work successfully expands the toolkit for mixed-membership modeling beyond the HDP and its associated Chinese restaurant franchise metaphor, making a clear contribution toward a more unified EPPF-based framework.

**Significance:** The theoretical significance of this paper is high. It provides a deeper understanding of the BNBP and establishes a formal connection between this process and the theory of exchangeable random partitions for grouped data. This is an important result for researchers in Bayesian nonparametrics and could inspire similar derivations for other stochastic processes.

The practical significance is less clear. While the sampler exhibits faster convergence and better mixing properties, the empirical improvements in predictive power over the well-established HDP are insufficient to compellingly justify the model's added conceptual and implementation complexity. For a practitioner, the motivation to switch from HDP to this new model is not strongly established by the experiments. The main practical advantage seems to be model compactness, which is valuable but might not be the primary concern in all applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a significant and novel theoretical contribution by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP for mixed-membership modeling.
2.  The methodological approach of using randomized group sizes to derive the marginal partition distribution is innovative and could be impactful.
3.  The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler demonstrates superior convergence and mixing properties compared to a standard HDP-LDA sampler.
4.  The work helps bridge the gap between EPPF-based analysis and a broader class of BNP priors for grouped data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The empirical evidence for improved predictive performance over HDP is weak and does not fully support the paper's claims.
2.  The paper lacks a clear intuitive justification for why the BNBP's multiplicative sharing mechanism should be preferred over the HDP's additive one in practice.
3.  The increased model complexity may not be justified by the marginal performance gains, limiting its practical impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you provide more intuition on the practical scenarios or data characteristics where the BNBP's multiplicative sharing mechanism would be a more appropriate prior than the HDP's additive one? For example, does it better model "bursty" topic usage or some other phenomenon?
2.  The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" appears strong given that Figure 3 shows HDP-LDA often achieving lower perplexity. Could you clarify this claim, perhaps by rephrasing it in terms of achieving a better perplexity-compactness trade-off?
3.  Given the model's complexity, the faster convergence seems to be its most compelling practical advantage. Do you see other practical benefits over HDP-LDA that would warrant its adoption?

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The derivation of the BNBP-EPPF is elegant and novel. While the practical justification could be strengthened, the theoretical value is high enough to warrant publication. I lean toward **acceptance**, with the recommendation that the authors temper their claims about predictive performance and add more discussion on the model's intuitive motivation in the final version.
