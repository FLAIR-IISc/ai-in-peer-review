This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), aiming to provide a tractable inference method for mixed-membership modeling. The authors address a key limitation of existing BNBP inference techniques – the need for truncation – by deriving an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) and a corresponding CG sampler. The work builds on the established framework of EPPF models, extending it to the mixed-membership setting and offering a potential alternative to the widely used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP).

The novelty of the paper lies in the derivation of Lemma 1 and the resulting CG sampler. Establishing the EPPF for the BNBP in the context of grouped data is a significant contribution, enabling fully collapsed inference and simplifying the computational aspects. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of nonparametric Bayesian methods, referencing relevant prior work on Dirichlet processes, normalized random measures with independent increments, and the Chinese restaurant process. They also accurately highlight the limitations of existing BNBP inference approaches and the advantages of a collapsed sampler.

However, the experimental evaluation is the major weakness of this paper. The authors compare their BNBP topic model to the HDP-LDA, but this comparison feels misdirected. The core contribution of the paper is a new *sampler* for the BNBP, not a new statistical model. Therefore, the primary evaluation should focus on comparing the performance – in terms of convergence speed, mixing, and computational efficiency – of the proposed CG sampler *to other existing BNBP sampling algorithms* (e.g., those based on truncation or slice sampling, as mentioned in the introduction). Comparing against HDP-LDA obscures the specific benefit of the new sampler, as differences in performance could be attributable to the underlying model rather than the inference method. The provided results, while demonstrating good performance of the BNBP topic model, don’t adequately validate the core contribution of a more efficient BNBP sampler. The authors acknowledge the potential for parallelization, which could further improve the sampler's performance, but this is not demonstrated experimentally.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail for a reader familiar with nonparametric Bayesian methods to understand the key ideas. The motivation for the work – simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and strong predictive performance – is clearly articulated. It's important to note that this work focuses on algorithmic development, specifically the sampler, rather than proposing a fundamentally new statistical model. This focus is reasonable, but it underscores the need for a more targeted experimental evaluation. The inclusion of Matlab code is a positive step towards reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations are well-presented and appear correct.
*   **Novelty:** The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and the corresponding CG sampler represent a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Potential Significance:** A tractable sampler for the BNBP could broaden the applicability of this flexible model.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The comparison to HDP-LDA is not the most relevant evaluation and fails to demonstrate the advantages of the new sampler over existing BNBP samplers.
*   **Lack of Comparative Sampler Benchmarking:** The paper doesn’t compare its sampler to other BNBP inference methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution in the form of a novel CG sampler for the BNBP. However, the lack of a targeted experimental evaluation significantly weakens the paper's impact. While the results demonstrate good performance in a topic modeling context, they do not convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed sampler *as a sampler*. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the experimental evaluation to include a comparison of the proposed CG sampler to other existing BNBP sampling algorithms. This comparison should focus on metrics such as convergence speed, mixing properties, and computational cost. Addressing this key weakness would significantly strengthen the paper and justify its acceptance at NIPS.
