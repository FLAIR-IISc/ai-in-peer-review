

This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabling fully collapsed inference for mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive the EPPF by first randomizing group sizes to obtain an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then applying Bayes' rule to arrive at the EPPF governing the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. The key contribution is a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP that analytically marginalizes out not only topics and topic weights but also the infinite-dimensional beta process, resulting in closed-form update equations.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a novel derivation of the BNBP's EPPF, which has been missing from the literature. This generalizes the technique developed in Zhou et al. (2014) to mixed-membership modeling. Second, it constructs a BNBP topic model that is distinct from existing nonparametric Bayesian topic models like HDP-LDA. Third, it demonstrates that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance compared to HDP-LDA.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The derivation of the EPPF (Lemma 3) and the prediction rule (Lemma 4) are theoretically sound and well-motivated. The authors carefully develop the mathematical framework, building upon the beta process literature (e.g., Thibaux & Jordan, 2007; Zhou et al., 2012). The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (Equation 12) is a significant methodological contribution, as it avoids the need for tuning parameters like the topic Dirichlet smoothing parameter α in LDA or the concentration parameter in HDP-LDA. The derivation of the prediction rule (Equation 10) is particularly elegant, showing how the probability of selecting a topic combines both global popularity and document-specific popularity in a multiplicative manner.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and clear mathematical notation. The authors effectively explain the relationship between the BNBP and other stochastic processes, and the comparison with HDP-LDA in Section 3.1 is insightful. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the impact of dispersion parameters on topic structure.

Regarding originality, this work fills a significant gap in the literature by providing the first EPPF for the BNBP. While the BNBP has been used in mixed-membership modeling (Zhou et al., 2012; Broderick et al., 2013), the lack of an explicit EPPF has prevented fully collapsed inference. The authors' approach of randomizing group sizes to derive the EPPF is novel and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

The significance of this work is demonstrated by the BNBP topic model's strong performance. The authors show that their model achieves comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA with fewer topics, which is particularly valuable for applications where compact representation is desired. The paper also provides a new perspective on how different nonparametric Bayesian models control topic sparsity.

However, there are several significant weaknesses that prevent this paper from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The most serious issue is the insufficient experimental evaluation. The authors' main motivation centers on the sampler's advantages: simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and strong predictive performance. Yet the experiments primarily compare the BNBP topic model to HDP-LDA, not to other BNBP sampling algorithms. As noted in the key points, the paper should have compared the proposed sampler to other BNBP samplers, such as those using finite truncation (Zhou et al., 2012) or slice sampling (Zhou et al., 2014), to properly validate the claimed advantages.

The paper does not provide any evidence that the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler converges faster than alternative BNBP samplers. The trace plots in Figure 2 show the BNBP model converging faster than HDP-LDA, but this comparison is irrelevant to the authors' main claim about the sampler's advantages. Without comparing to other BNBP samplers, the claim about fast convergence and good mixing remains unsubstantiated.

Furthermore, the paper focuses primarily on the sampling algorithm rather than introducing new statistical modeling. While the BNBP topic model is novel, the authors do not provide evidence that it captures meaningful structure in the data that other models miss. The predictive performance is measured via perplexity, which is standard in topic modeling, but the paper lacks analysis of the actual topic quality or interpretability.

The significance of the work is also limited by the narrow scope. While the authors claim their method could generalize to other integer-valued stochastic processes, they do not explore this direction. The paper's contribution is largely algorithmic, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims about the sampler's advantages.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the development of EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling, with a novel derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. However, the experimental evaluation is inadequate to support the claims about the sampler's advantages over alternative BNBP sampling methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by additional experiments comparing the proposed sampler to other BNBP samplers, as well as a more thorough analysis of the topic quality and interpretability.

Pro: Novel derivation of BNBP's EPPF, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with closed-form updates, theoretically sound, clear presentation of mathematical framework.

Con: Insufficient experimental validation (should compare to other BNBP samplers, not HDP-LDA), narrow scope (focuses on algorithm rather than new statistical modeling), limited evidence of practical advantages beyond perplexity comparison.
