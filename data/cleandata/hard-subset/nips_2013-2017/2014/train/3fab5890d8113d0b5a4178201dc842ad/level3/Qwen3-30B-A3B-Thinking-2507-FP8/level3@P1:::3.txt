

This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabling nonparametric mixed-membership modeling with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The core contribution is deriving the BNBP's EPPF—a gap in prior work where existing methods (e.g., HDP) rely on indirect representations like the Chinese restaurant franchise. The authors generalize the EPPF framework to handle grouped data with observed sizes, using a group-size-dependent model to derive the EPPF via Bayes' rule and a prediction rule for simulation. This leads to a novel BNBP topic model that analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process, yielding closed-form updates and state-of-the-art predictive performance with compact topic representations.

**Quality** is strong. The derivation of the EPPF (Lemma 3) and prediction rule (Lemma 4) is technically rigorous, with careful handling of the marginalization of the beta process. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (Equation 12) is well-justified, and the paper provides closed-form updates for all parameters. The empirical evaluation on three corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) demonstrates faster convergence and better mixing than HDP-LDA (Figure 2), with the BNBP achieving comparable perplexity to HDP-LDA while using fewer topics (Figure 3). The authors responsibly note that BNBP's advantage is context-dependent: it excels in compact representation but shows marginal gains in perplexity under standard η settings. The technical soundness is further validated by the derivation of the ECPF (Lemma 2) and the connection to Poisson factor models.

**Clarity** is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with a clear progression from EPPF theory to topic modeling application. The prediction rule (Equation 10) and comparison to HDP-LDA (Equation 14) are particularly accessible. The intuition for how dispersion parameters {r_j} control overdispersion (Figure 1) is effectively conveyed. Minor improvements could include a more explicit discussion of the EPPF's complexity (Equation 9) versus the practical simplicity of the prediction rule.

**Originality** is the paper's strongest aspect. The group-size-dependent EPPF framework fills a critical gap in nonparametric mixed-membership modeling, as prior work (e.g., [5, 6, 7]) lacked a unified treatment for integer-valued processes like BNBP. While the BNBP topic model is not entirely novel (extending [10, 11]), the EPPF derivation is original and generalizable to other processes (e.g., gamma-Poisson). The paper properly contextualizes this against related work, citing [12, 17] for EPPF extensions and [7, 10] for BNBP inference limitations.

**Significance** is high. The EPPF method provides a template for deriving similar functions for other integer-valued processes, advancing the theory of exchangeable partitions. The BNBP topic model offers practical benefits: it avoids tuning parameters (unlike LDA) and achieves faster convergence than HDP-LDA. While perplexity gains over HDP-LDA are marginal, the model’s compactness (fewer topics for equivalent predictive power) is valuable for real-world applications. The paper also opens avenues for future work on parallelization (e.g., comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers for scalability).

**Key weaknesses** include: (1) The empirical comparison lacks a direct benchmark against the sliced sampler [10] or truncated sampler [8] for BNBP inference, as suggested in the key points. The authors note [7] uses truncated sampling but do not contrast it with their collapsed approach in experiments. (2) The claim that BNBP "achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance" is slightly overstated; results show comparable perplexity to HDP-LDA, with the advantage being topic compactness rather than raw performance. (3) The discussion of parallelization potential (Section 4) is underdeveloped—while the collapsed sampler is efficient, the paper does not evaluate uncollapsed variants for parallelization, as recommended.

**Pro/con acceptance summary**:  
*Pro*: Original EPPF derivation fills a critical gap; technically sound; clear, practical topic model; faster convergence than HDP-LDA; generalizable method.  
*Con*: Marginal perplexity gains over HDP-LDA; missing comparisons to BNBP-specific samplers [8, 10]; overstatement of "state-of-the-art" in some contexts.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the theory and practice of nonparametric mixed-membership modeling. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically rigorous, clearly written, significantly original, and impactful for both theory (EPPF framework) and application (topic modeling). The minor weaknesses are addressable via brief revisions (e.g., adding comparisons to [8, 10] in experiments). The work is broadly relevant to NIPS audiences studying Bayesian nonparametrics and topic models, and it clearly advances the state of the art beyond prior EPPF-based methods. I recommend acceptance.
