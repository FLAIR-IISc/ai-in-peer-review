Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops the first exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed‑membership setting. The key technical idea is to randomize group sizes and derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for the joint law of group sizes and partitions, then condition back on the observed group-size vector to obtain a sample‑size dependent EPPF and a simple prediction rule. This yields a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the beta process, group‑specific gamma processes, topic parameters, and topic weights. Applied to topic modeling, the resulting BNBP topic model differs from HDP‑LDA in that the assignment probabilities combine global and local popularity multiplicatively rather than additively.

The work connects to: Dirichlet process and EPPF/CRP (Ferguson; Pitman), HDP and the Chinese restaurant franchise (Teh et al.), Poisson factorization links for mixed membership (Lo; Titsias), and recent BNBP/GNBP literature including finite/slice approximations, negative‑binomial IBP, and “ice‑cream” buffet processes. It also clarifies why the BNBP is outside the σ‑stable Poisson–Kingman family treated by recent marginal samplers.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: a clear route (ECPF → EPPF) to obtain sample‑size dependent EPPFs for integer‑valued processes in mixed‑membership modeling; likely reusable beyond BNBP.
- Clean prediction rule and collapsed sampler; the sampler is simple to implement once derived, with no truncation and no auxiliary table bookkeeping.
- Well‑articulated distinction vs. HDP‑LDA (multiplicative vs. additive shrinkage).
- Empirical evidence on three corpora that the sampler mixes quickly and tends to produce compact representations (fewer topics at comparable perplexity); public code.

Weaknesses
- Practical benefit over HDP‑LDA is not decisive: perplexity improvements are small/inconsistent across η; when η is very small, HDP can outperform; when matched by number of topics, BNBP is competitive but not clearly superior.
- The EPPF is sample‑size dependent. The paper acknowledges this but does not fully discuss implications for coherence with classical EPPF addition rules or for predictive exchangeability when group sizes change.
- The path “randomize group sizes → condition back on observed sizes” is mathematically elegant, but an intuitive explanation of why this should improve modeling/practical performance over HDP is limited.
- Complexity: While the final update is compact, the theory and implementation burden (new distributions, special‑function terms, hyperparameters c, γ0, rj) is higher than the HDP direct‑assignment sampler. Empirical results do not yet clearly justify this added complexity.
- Evaluation breadth: missing comparisons to (i) HDP collapsed CRF sampler; (ii) other Poisson factorization or GNBP/BNBP variants; (iii) wall‑clock breakdowns and memory use; (iv) ablations on learning rj vs. fixing them.

Quality
- The derivations appear sound and self‑consistent (ECPF, EPPF, prediction rule, random count matrix representation with digamma and Dirichlet‑multinomial). The collapsed sampler follows by standard marginalization steps.
- Some theoretical aspects would benefit from elaboration: normalization of the proposed EPPF; conditions guaranteeing exchangeability and consistency when conditioning on m; implications of sample‑size dependence for posterior predictive distributions.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but mathematically dense. The main message could be made more accessible:
  - Provide a high‑level diagram linking B, Θj, Xj, zji, and m.
  - Include pseudocode for the collapsed sampler and a short “how to implement” section.
  - Offer a more intuitive narrative for the multiplicative shrinkage and the role of rj.
  - Clarify notation early (ψ, Digam distribution) and collect key definitions.

Originality
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first EPPF for BNBP in the mixed‑membership setting and a novel ECPF→EPPF route that may generalize to other integer‑valued processes.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is strong for Bayesian nonparametrics: it bridges EPPFs and mixed‑membership models beyond the HDP. Practical significance is moderate: the topic‑model results show compactness and fast mixing, but predictive gains over strong HDP baselines are modest.

Questions and requests for clarification
1) Sample‑size dependence: What are the precise implications for addition rules and predictive exchangeability? Are there settings where this dependence leads to undesirable behavior as m changes?
2) Intuition: Why should the multiplicative shrinkage yield better generalization at comparable K? Can you relate this to variance‑mean control of topic usage counts and provide a simple toy example?
3) Hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to priors on c, γ0, and rj? Provide posterior summaries or sensitivity plots.
4) rj: Show an ablation with rj fixed vs. learned to support the claim that inferring rj is important.
5) Baselines: Add comparisons to the HDP collapsed CRF sampler and to GNBP/Poisson factor models with similar collapse.
6) Complexity: Report per‑iteration time, memory, and total runtime to target perplexity for BNBP vs. HDP (both direct assignment and CRF).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid EPPF for BNBP; clear methodological advance.
- Fully collapsed, truncation‑free sampler with good mixing and compact topic usage.
- Potentially general methodology applicable to other integer‑valued processes.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical improvements over HDP are limited; practical impact not yet compelling.
- Higher conceptual and implementation complexity.
- Limited discussion of consequences of sample‑size dependent EPPFs.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is substantial and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the intuitive and practical implications would make the case more compelling.

Reviewer confidence
Moderate: familiar with BNP, EPPFs, and topic models; did not verify all measure‑theoretic details in the appendix.
