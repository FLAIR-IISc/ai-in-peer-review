This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian models by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. This problem is important because while integer-valued stochastic processes like the BNBP have been proposed as alternatives to the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP), their practical use has been hampered by the lack of fully collapsed inference schemes. Previous work on BNBP inference has relied on finite truncation or slice sampling. The authors' main contribution is to derive the marginal distribution of the random partitions generated by the BNBP, which enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The derivation is achieved by first defining a sample size dependent model to find an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and then using Bayes' rule to arrive at the desired group-size dependent EPPF. As a practical demonstration, the paper develops a BNBP topic model and its corresponding collapsed Gibbs sampler, which is then empirically compared to the HDP.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a novel and valuable contribution. It fills an important gap in the literature, providing a unified EPPF-based perspective on a key model for mixed-membership analysis. This theoretical result is general and could be used to develop collapsed inference for other applications of the BNBP beyond topic modeling. The method of using a sample size dependent model as an intermediate step to derive the EPPF is also clever and potentially applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations leading from the BNBP's definition to the ECPF (Lemma 2), the EPPF (Lemma 3), and the simple predictive rule (Lemma 4) are well-explained and appear correct. The resulting Gibbs sampling update equations for the BNBP topic model are elegant and provide a clear intuition for how the model operates, particularly the multiplicative interaction between a topic's global popularity and its group-specific popularity.

3.  **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the context of existing literature on EPPFs and mixed-membership models, and walking the reader through their theoretical derivations. The comparison of the BNBP topic model's update rule (Eq. 12) with those for LDA and HDP-LDA is particularly insightful for understanding the model's distinct shrinkage properties.

4.  **Empirical Insights:** The experiments, while not demonstrating overwhelming superiority, effectively highlight the distinct characteristics of the BNBP topic model compared to HDP-LDA. The faster convergence and better mixing shown in Figure 2 are a significant practical advantage of the fully collapsed sampler. The results in Figure 3 support the paper's claim that the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism leads to more compact models (fewer topics) for a given level of perplexity, which is often a desirable property in practice.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection/Revision):**

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the paper introduces a new *inference method* for the BNBP (a fully collapsed sampler), it is only compared against a different *model* (HDP-LDA). A more direct and informative comparison would be against other existing inference schemes for the BNBP, such as the truncated Gibbs sampler [7, 10] and the slice sampler [11]. Such a comparison would directly quantify the benefits of the proposed collapsed sampler in terms of convergence speed, mixing, memory usage, and final predictive accuracy on the same underlying model.

2.  **Overstated Performance Claims:** The abstract's claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" seems slightly too strong given the presented results. The empirical findings are more nuanced: the BNBP topic model offers a different trade-off, achieving competitive perplexity with a more compact topic representation. However, Figure 3 shows that HDP-LDA can achieve lower perplexity in several settings, albeit with more topics. The main conclusion should be framed more around the model's distinct and useful characteristics rather than outright superiority.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Scalability Trade-offs:** The paper mentions that blocked Gibbs samplers (based on truncation or slicing) have the advantage of being easy to parallelize. This is a critical point that is somewhat understated. While the proposed collapsed sampler shows superior serial performance (faster convergence/mixing), its Gibbs updates are inherently sequential. A more balanced discussion is needed on the trade-offs between the statistical efficiency of the collapsed sampler and the potential computational scalability of uncollapsed or partially collapsed alternatives, which might be preferred for massive datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very good paper that presents a strong, novel, and useful theoretical result. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling is a significant contribution to the nonparametric Bayes community. The paper is well-written, and the technical content is solid. The main weaknesses are confined to the experimental section, which could be strengthened by including more direct comparisons to alternative BNBP inference methods and by tempering the claims of state-of-the-art performance. Despite these minor shortcomings in the experimental validation, the strength of the theoretical contribution is sufficient to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper is interesting, advances our understanding of nonparametric models, and provides a practical tool for researchers working with mixed-membership models.
