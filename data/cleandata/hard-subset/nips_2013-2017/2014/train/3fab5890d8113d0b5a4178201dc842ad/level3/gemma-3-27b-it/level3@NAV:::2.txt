## Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper presents a novel fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for inference in the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), specifically tailored for mixed-membership modeling. The core contribution lies in deriving an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, enabling fully collapsed sampling without truncation – a significant advancement over previous approaches that relied on approximations. The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of nonparametric Bayesian methods, referencing key papers on Dirichlet processes, normalized random measures, and related EPPF models. They highlight the challenges of applying these techniques to grouped data and position the BNBP as a viable alternative to the more commonly used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP).

The derivation of Lemma 1 and the resulting sampler are genuinely interesting and appear technically sound. The paper clearly articulates the benefits of their approach – simple implementation, potentially faster convergence, and good mixing – and attempts to demonstrate these advantages through experiments. The motivation for developing this sampler, focused on practical advantages for inference, is well-defined.

However, the primary weakness of this paper is the insufficient experimental evaluation. While the authors compare their BNBP topic model to HDP-LDA, this comparison is misdirected. As rightly pointed out in the conference guidelines, the focus of this work is on a *sampling algorithm* for the BNBP, not a novel statistical model. Therefore, the experiments should compare the performance of the proposed CG sampler *against other existing BNBP sampling algorithms* (e.g., slice sampling from [11], truncated Gibbs samplers [7]), not against an entirely different modeling approach like HDP-LDA. The lack of such a comparison severely limits the ability to assess the true contribution of the new sampler.  The discussion of results, while thorough, feels largely geared towards justifying the benefits relative to HDP-LDA, missing the crucial assessment of performance *within* the BNBP framework. 

Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge potential for parallelization, a discussion of the computational complexity compared to other BNBP samplers would be valuable. The inclusion of runtime comparisons would strengthen the claim of faster convergence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel derivation of the BNBP EPPF and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
*   Clear explanation of the theoretical advantages of the proposed sampler.
*   Well-written and technically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental evaluation – lacks comparison to other BNBP samplers.
*   The chosen experimental setup doesn't directly assess the contribution of the new sampler.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods. However, the lack of a targeted experimental evaluation significantly weakens the paper’s impact. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing the concerns regarding the experimental design. A more focused evaluation, comparing the proposed sampler to existing BNBP inference methods, would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate its practical value.
