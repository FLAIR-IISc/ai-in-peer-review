This paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) and uses it to derive a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for mixed-membership modeling, with a focus on topic models. The authors proceed via an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and a group-size–dependent EPPF, then obtain a simple prediction rule that enables a truly collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process, topic distributions, and topic weights. The approach connects to prior NIPS and related work on EPPFs (Pitman), HDP/Chinese restaurant franchise (Teh et al.), and earlier BNBP/negative binomial process inference via truncation and slice sampling ([7], [10], [11]). Lemma 1 establishing equivalence between the group-size–dependent construction and the BNBP, and the resulting sampler, appear novel and interesting.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound; the marginalizations and prediction rule are well motivated, and the sampler is clearly specified with closed-form updates. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the stated advantages (simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, strong predictive performance). The comparisons focus on HDP-LDA, which differs in prior and shrinkage behavior, rather than on competing BNBP samplers (e.g., truncated blocked Gibbs [7], slice sampling [11]). Claims of “state-of-the-art predictive performance” for the sampler require head-to-head evaluations under the same BNBP model.

Clarity: Generally clear but notation-heavy; the paper would benefit from a succinct algorithm box, complexity per sweep, and a brief discussion clarifying the implications of sample-size–dependent EPPFs versus classical addition rules.

Originality: High on the inference side. Providing a BNBP EPPF for grouped data and a fully collapsed sampler is new; the work is not about a new model class but about improved sampling.

Significance: Potentially high. A practical CG sampler for BNBP could impact count-based nonparametrics and topic modeling. Demonstrating superiority over existing BNBP inference would make the contribution compelling.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel EPPF/ECPF derivations for BNBP and elegant prediction rule (Lemma 1–4).
- Fully collapsed sampler likely to improve mixing and efficiency.
- Clear relation to and differentiation from HDP-LDA.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lacks comparisons to existing BNBP samplers ([7], [11]); current experiments do not isolate sampler benefits.
- Limited diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, wall-clock per effective sample) and ablations (role of rj, sensitivity to c, γ0).
- No synthetic recovery tests or scalability experiments.

Recommendation: Weak reject unless the camera-ready includes rigorous comparisons against BNBP samplers and stronger empirical diagnostics supporting the claimed advantages.
