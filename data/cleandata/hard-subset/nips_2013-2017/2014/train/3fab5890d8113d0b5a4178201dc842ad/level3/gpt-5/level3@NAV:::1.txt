This paper develops a Bayesian nonparametric mixed‑membership model grounded in the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). The central contribution is an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for BNBP along with a prediction rule, obtained by a clever device that randomizes group sizes to derive joint and marginal distributions, and then conditions back on observed sizes. This fills a genuine gap: while EPPFs are classical for the DP/NRMIs and the HDP relies on the Chinese Restaurant Franchise, BNBP‑based mixed‑membership models have lacked a marginal partition law. The paper also provides a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model, contrasting its multiplicative shrinkage with the HDP’s additive mechanism, and relates to prior constructions such as the negative‑binomial IBP and recent Poisson‑Kingman samplers.

Quality: The theory appears technically sound and carefully developed, with clear lemmas for the ECPF, EPPF, and prediction rule. The group‑size randomization is an insightful maneuver. However, the sampler and analysis are more complex than the HDP, and the empirical gains over HDP are modest; the paper would benefit from a tighter, quantitative complexity analysis and more diagnostic experiments (e.g., sensitivity to priors on rj, ablations isolating the multiplicative term).

Clarity: The manuscript is mathematically dense. While derivations are detailed, the paper lacks intuition for when and why BNBP’s multiplicative popularity term should outperform HDP in practice. Adding a simple running example, geometric intuition for the Digamma/DirMult structure, and a high‑level schematic of the size‑randomization trick would help. Some notation could be streamlined.

Originality: High. Deriving an EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed‑membership modeling, and showing how to get marginal laws by randomizing group sizes, is novel and likely to generalize.

Significance: Theoretical significance is strong; the BNBP–EPPF connection is a step toward unifying EPPF analysis and BNP for grouped data. Practical impact is less clear given the modest empirical gains relative to added complexity.

Pros for acceptance:
- First EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed‑membership; solid theoretical advance.
- Fully collapsed sampler; good mixing and convergence.
- Unifying perspective that may extend to GNBP/gamma–Poisson processes.

Cons against acceptance:
- Empirical advantages over HDP are limited; complexity is higher.
- Insufficient intuitive explanation for practical benefits; limited guidance on when to prefer BNBP.

Requests to authors:
- Provide an intuitive account (and synthetic experiments) showing regimes where multiplicative shrinkage helps.
- Report wall‑clock, memory, and implementation complexity vs HDP (direct‑assignment and CRF).
- Include sensitivity to rj priors and comparison to GNBP/gamma–Poisson.

Recommendation: Lean toward acceptance for its clear theoretical contribution and potential to catalyze further work; strengthen the empirical narrative and practical guidance.
