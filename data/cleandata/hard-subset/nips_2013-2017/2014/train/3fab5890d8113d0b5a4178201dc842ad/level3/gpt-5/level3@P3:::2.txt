Summary
The paper develops a group-size–dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, along with an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and a simple prediction rule. Building on these, the authors derive a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes out the beta process, group-level gamma processes, topic distributions, and topic weights. The paper positions this as filling a gap relative to prior BNBP/GNBP work that relied on truncation, slice/adaptive truncation, or partially collapsed updates. It relates to CRP/EPPF traditions for mixture models, but extends the EPPF program to mixed-membership via a count-matrix view. Comparisons are drawn to LDA and HDP-LDA samplers; empirical results suggest faster convergence and good mixing for the proposed CG sampler, with competitive perplexity using fewer topics.

Relative to prior work: (i) previous BNBP inference has typically used finite truncation or slice-based schemes [10, 11, 17]; (ii) EPPFs for NRMIs are well-developed for single-group mixture models but much less so for mixed-membership; (iii) the “ice-cream/IBP” lines characterize predictive structures for infinite count matrices, whereas this paper explicitly targets a fixed group-size partitioning EPPF and uses it to construct a CG sampler.

Strengths
- Novelty of Lemma 1 and the ensuing construction: showing the equivalence between the group-size–dependent Poisson/gamma augmentation and the original BNBP, then leveraging it to obtain an ECPF, a group-size–dependent EPPF, and a clean prediction rule. This is technically interesting and useful.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler: analytically integrates out the beta process, group gamma processes, and topic parameters; update rule is simple to implement and practically attractive.
- Clear motivation for collapsed sampling: the paper argues for simplicity, faster convergence, and better mixing, and provides qualitative support via trace plots and runtime.
- Conceptual contribution: general method for deriving an EPPF for mixed-membership using an integer-valued process; potentially reusable beyond BNBP.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient for establishing the sampler’s advantages. The core contribution is an inference algorithm for BNBP, yet the comparisons focus primarily on HDP-LDA. To validate the claimed benefits (simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, strong predictive performance), the paper should compare against other BNBP samplers: finite-truncation blocked Gibbs [10, 7], slice/adaptive truncation variants [11, 17], or related BNBP implementations in Poisson factorization. Without these, it is unclear whether the gains come from the BNBP model vs. HDP-LDA, or from the collapsed implementation vs. alternative BNBP samplers.
- Baselines for HDP are not the strongest: using only the direct-assignment sampler disadvantages HDP relative to its collapsed CRF samplers; thus claims about convergence/mixing versus HDP-LDA are not definitive.
- Limited diagnostics: the paper shows trace plots and perplexities, but lacks standard MCMC metrics (autocorrelation, effective sample size), wall-clock to a target perplexity, and ablations (e.g., partially collapsed vs fully collapsed).
- Clarity/organization: the EPPF normalization (Eq. 9) is quite dense; while the sampler avoids evaluating it, more intuition would help. Notation (n:k vs n·k, r·, ψ) could be streamlined, and the “digamma distribution” might benefit from a brief definition in the main text. A concise pseudocode would aid reproducibility.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears high. The augmentation (Lemma 1), ECPF (Lemma 2), EPPF (Lemma 3), and prediction rule (Lemma 4) are coherent and connect cleanly to the collapsed sampler. The construction is careful about group-size dependence, which is appropriate for mixed-membership.
- Claims about convergence and mixing are plausible for a CG sampler, but are only partly supported by experiments against non-BNBP baselines.

Clarity
- Well written overall, but dense. Adding a short algorithm box for the CG sampler, clarifying the role of hyperparameters (γ0, c, rj), and briefly summarizing computational complexity per iteration would help.

Originality
- High on the inference side: a fully collapsed BNBP sampler and group-size–dependent EPPF for mixed-membership are, to my knowledge, new. The modeling itself is not new (BNBP topic modeling has precedents), but that is not the focus here.

Significance
- Potentially significant, as CG samplers are widely adopted in practice. If empirically validated against other BNBP samplers, this could become a standard inference method for BNBP topic and count models. As it stands, the empirical support is suggestive but not decisive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add direct comparisons to other BNBP samplers:
  - Truncated blocked Gibbs (e.g., BNBP Poisson factorization [10], and the block sampler in [7]).
  - Slice/adaptive truncation samplers [11, 17].
  - Metrics: wall-clock to reach a target perplexity, ESS per second for K and held-out likelihood, final perplexity at matched K.
- Include an HDP-LDA collapsed CRF baseline to make HDP comparisons fair.
- Provide ablations: effect of collapsing each layer (topics only vs topics+weights vs full collapse), to isolate which integrations drive the gains.
- Add synthetic experiments to validate the prediction rule and the role of rj on dispersion (recoverability under known truth).
- Report complexity and memory usage; discuss scalability and sparse updates.

Pros
- Novel derivation (Lemma 1) and theoretically grounded sampler.
- Fully collapsed, simple update; likely faster mixing than partially collapsed alternatives.
- Potentially general method for deriving mixed-membership EPPFs for other integer-valued processes.

Cons
- Lacks head-to-head comparisons with existing BNBP samplers; current baselines don’t isolate the contribution of the proposed sampler.
- HDP-LDA baseline is not the strongest variant; convergence/mixing claims are therefore incomplete.
- Limited diagnostics and ablation studies.

Overall assessment
A technically strong paper with a compelling and novel collapsed sampler for the BNBP and a thoughtful derivation of a group-size–dependent EPPF. However, the empirical section does not yet substantiate the central claims about the sampler’s advantages because it omits comparisons to other BNBP inference schemes and stronger HDP baselines. With a more targeted experimental evaluation, this could be a solid contribution. In its current form, I view it as borderline, leaning positive on technical merit and originality but needing stronger empirical validation of the sampler itself.
