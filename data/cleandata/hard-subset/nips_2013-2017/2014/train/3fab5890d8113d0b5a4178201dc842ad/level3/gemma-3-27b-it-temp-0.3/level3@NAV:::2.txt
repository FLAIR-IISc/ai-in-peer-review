## Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), aiming to provide a more tractable inference method for mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, enabling fully collapsed sampling and presenting a novel nonparametric Bayesian topic model. The work builds upon existing literature in nonparametric Bayesian methods, particularly the Dirichlet process and its extensions, and addresses a gap in available inference techniques for the BNBP. The motivation is clear: to develop a sampler with simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and strong predictive performance.

The core contribution – Lemma 1 and the derived CG sampler – is indeed novel and interesting. The derivation of the EPPF and the subsequent development of a fully collapsed sampler are valuable additions to the toolkit for BNBP inference. The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding of the BNBP and its relationship to other stochastic processes. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as truncation or slice sampling, and offer a compelling alternative.

However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the authors demonstrate the sampler’s functionality on standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS), the comparison is almost exclusively to the HDP-LDA model. As rightly pointed out in the paper itself, the focus is on sampler development, not new statistical modeling. Therefore, a more appropriate evaluation would involve comparing the proposed CG sampler to *other* BNBP sampling algorithms (e.g., those using truncation or slice sampling, as referenced in the introduction). The lack of such a comparison makes it difficult to assess the true benefits of the proposed method *specifically as a BNBP sampler*. The reported perplexity results, while showing competitive performance, are less informative without a baseline comparison to other BNBP inference methods. 

Furthermore, the discussion of convergence and mixing, while insightful, relies on trace plots, which are somewhat subjective. More quantitative measures of convergence (e.g., R-hat statistics) would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound derivation of the BNBP EPPF and CG sampler.
*   Addresses a clear gap in the literature regarding efficient BNBP inference.
*   Demonstrates good performance on standard topic modeling datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental evaluation – lacks comparison to other BNBP samplers.
*   Reliance on subjective measures (trace plots) for assessing convergence.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. However, the limited experimental evaluation significantly weakens its impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after including a thorough comparison to existing BNBP sampling algorithms. Addressing this key weakness would substantially strengthen the paper and demonstrate the true value of the proposed CG sampler.
