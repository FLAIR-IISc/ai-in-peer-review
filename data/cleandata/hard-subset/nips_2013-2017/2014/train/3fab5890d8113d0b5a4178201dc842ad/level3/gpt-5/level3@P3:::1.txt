Summary and relation to prior work
This paper derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. The central technical idea is to randomize group sizes to obtain an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then condition on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to recover a group-size–dependent EPPF and a corresponding prediction rule. This closes a longstanding gap: while mixed-membership models like the HDP enjoy CRF-style marginal constructions, integer-valued processes such as the BNBP (and related gamma–Poisson/gamma–NB processes) lacked marginal partition laws suitable for fully collapsed inference. The paper further instantiates a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (marginalizing global beta process, per-group gamma processes, and topics), and compares against HDP-LDA across three corpora. The authors position the BNBP–EPPF link as a step toward unifying EPPF analysis and BNP research beyond single-sample mixture models, building on Pitman’s EPPF framework, IBP/BNBP-related constructions, and recent Poisson–Kingman marginal samplers.

Quality
Technical soundness: The derivation via a size-randomized ECPF is elegant and appears careful. The resulting EPPF and prediction rule are novel and technically nontrivial; proofs and augmentation arguments (beta–gamma–Poisson) are consistent with prior BNBP literature. The induced count-matrix prior with digamma and Dirichlet–multinomial components is well explained. The prediction rule is intuitive: it multiplies a cross-group popularity term by a within-group popularity/dispersion term. The topic model sampler is fully collapsed (in contrast to HDP direct assignment), and the conditional updates appear correct.

Empirics: The comparisons on JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS show faster convergence and better local mixing for BNBP. Perplexity comparisons are nuanced: HDP-LDA may achieve lower perplexity at very small η (with many topics), while BNBP often achieves comparable or lower perplexity for a similar number of topics and provides a more compact representation. However, the improvements over a well-tuned HDP appear modest, and the paper would benefit from stronger, more systematic evidence of practical gains (e.g., held-out likelihood vs number of topics/time curves, sensitivity to c, γ0, priors on rj, and ablations clarifying the role of multiplicative vs additive shrinkage).

Clarity
The paper is well organized but technically dense. The ECPF→EPPF route is carefully laid out, and the predictive rule provides welcome intuition. Still, the EPPF itself is complex, and many readers would benefit from:
- A small running toy example connecting the prediction rule to observed partitions.
- More intuitive explanation for when multiplicative shrinkage (BNBP) should outperform additive shrinkage (HDP) in practice.
- A practitioner’s guide for choosing hyperparameters (c, γ0) and interpreting rj.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first derivation of a group-size–dependent EPPF for a mixed-membership integer-valued process, and the randomize-then-condition recipe is broadly useful. The construction bridges EPPF analysis and BNP mixed-membership models beyond the HDP/CRF. The fully collapsed BNBP topic sampler that marginalizes the beta process and gamma processes is also novel.

Significance
The theoretical contribution is substantial: it provides a new tool for analyzing and designing mixed-membership BNP models, and a plausible path to analogous EPPFs for other count-valued processes (e.g., gamma–Poisson, gamma–NB). Practically, the sampler’s convergence/mixing benefits are attractive. That said, the empirical advantage over HDP-LDA is not yet decisive; the paper’s impact will likely be stronger on the theory/methodology side than on immediate practice unless clearer performance gains are demonstrated.

Strengths
- First EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership models; principled ECPF→EPPF construction by randomizing group sizes.
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free Gibbs sampler; clear connection to count-matrix priors (digamma + DirMult).
- Good mixing and faster convergence than HDP direct assignment on three corpora.
- Conceptual unification of EPPF analysis with BNP mixed-membership modeling.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical and implementation complexity are higher than HDP’s commonly used samplers; the claim of “simple implementation” is debatable.
- Empirical improvements over HDP-LDA are modest and dataset/η-dependent; benefits are clearest in compact representations rather than raw perplexity.
- Limited intuition for when BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage should systematically dominate HDP’s additive shrinkage.
- Sensitivity analyses (hyperparameters c, γ0; priors on rj; document length variability) are limited.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Provide intuition and, if possible, a simple analytical comparison explaining when multiplicative shrinkage yields better predictive performance than additive shrinkage. Are there regimes of overdispersion across groups (e.g., small rj, heavy-tailed document lengths) where BNBP is provably or empirically preferable?
- Clarify practical guidance for hyperparameters c and γ0 and priors on rj. How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Please add held-out perplexity vs runtime (and vs number of topics) curves to clarify the speed–accuracy trade-off relative to HDP-LDA and collapsed CRF-based samplers.
- Discuss the implications of the sample-size–dependent EPPF: does conditioning on observed m fully restore the desired exchangeability properties? Any caveats for downstream tasks?
- Can you reconcile your “simple implementation” claim with the higher theoretical/algorithmic complexity relative to HDP’s widely used samplers, and comment on code availability?

Pros for acceptance
- Significant and novel theoretical advance (BNBP EPPF + prediction rule) with a general recipe likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Fully collapsed sampler with strong mixing properties and compact representations.
- Clear positioning within BNP/EPPF literature and potential to unify analysis across integer-valued processes.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical gains over HDP are not consistently compelling; added complexity may not be justified for all applications.
- Limited intuition and guidance for practitioners on when to prefer BNBP over HDP.

Recommendation
Lean toward acceptance for its substantial theoretical contribution and clean sampler, with the caveat that the authors should better articulate and empirically validate the practical performance gains and provide stronger intuition for when BNBP’s design is advantageous.
