This paper develops, for the first time, an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. Starting from the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation of the BNBP, the authors first derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for random group sizes, then obtain the group-size–dependent EPPF via Bayes’ rule. They provide a simple prediction rule that yields a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler and apply it to topic modeling, yielding a nonparametric alternative to HDP-LDA. Empirically, the sampler mixes very well and converges quickly; the BNBP topic model tends to achieve comparable or lower perplexity than HDP-LDA when matched on the number of topics, while often using fewer topics due to a stronger multiplicative shrinkage mechanism.

Relation to prior work: Prior EPPFs (e.g., CRP and Pitman’s EPPF) focus on single-sample mixture modeling; collapsed HDP inference relies on the Chinese restaurant franchise to marginalize group-specific DPs. For BNBP, existing inference either truncates the beta process [Zhou & Carin; Zhou et al.] or uses slice sampling [Broderick et al.]. Recent “ice-cream/negative-binomial IBP” works marginalize B but model infinite count matrices rather than mixed-membership partitions with fixed group sizes. The Poisson–Kingman marginal samplers do not include the BNBP class. This paper fills a clear gap by deriving the marginal law governing exchangeable random partitions for grouped count data under the BNBP.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A principled route from an integer-valued process to a group-size–dependent EPPF via an ECPF is elegant and broadly useful. The derived prediction rule is simple and enables fully collapsed inference without truncation or slice variables.
- Clarity of mechanisms: The comparison to HDP-LDA highlights a distinct multiplicative shrinkage term n·k/(c+n·k+r·)×(njk+rj) versus the HDP’s additive (njk+αr̃k), giving intuition for why BNBP tends to prefer more compact topic allocations.
- Practical impact: The collapsed sampler is straightforward to implement, runs fast, and mixes well. Results on three corpora show competitive or better perplexity when the number of topics is comparable and fewer topics for similar predictive quality.
- Scope: The approach suggests a general template for deriving EPPFs for other integer-valued processes (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical comparisons: While HDP-LDA is a strong baseline, the paper should also compare against existing BNBP inference schemes: (i) slice sampling and (ii) finite truncation/blocked Gibbs (as used in prior BNBP/NBP work). This would isolate the benefit of the collapsed sampler (mixing speed, complexity, memory) for the same prior.
- Beyond perplexity: The evaluation focuses on perplexity and number of topics. Consider additional measures (e.g., topic coherence), and synthetic experiments to verify that the learned partition statistics match the EPPF’s predictions (e.g., cluster-size distributions).
- Parallelization/scalability: Collapsed samplers are harder to parallelize than blocked/sliced variants. A discussion (or small experiment) contrasting uncollapsed representations for distributed settings would be valuable, including mixing tradeoffs versus the proposed collapsed sampler.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The role of rj is central; more systematic sensitivity analyses (priors on rj, c, γ0), and an ablation illustrating how rj influences overdispersion and the number of topics, would strengthen the empirical section.
- Notational load and exposition: While generally clear, the EPPF/ECPF sections are dense. Brief intuition for the “digamma” distribution’s appearance, a compact derivation sketch in the main text (with proofs in appendix), and a cleaned-up notation table would help. Some references are missing years/pages and could be completed for reproducibility.

Quality
- The derivations appear technically sound, with careful measure-theoretic treatment via the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation. The resulting ECPF/EPPF and prediction rule are consistent and practically usable. The topic model inference is fully collapsed over topics, weights, and the beta process.

Clarity
- The manuscript is well written and organized; figures effectively illustrate mixing and shrinkage behavior. Minor suggestions above would further improve accessibility.

Originality
- High. The first EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership partitions and a general route from integer-valued processes to group-size–dependent EPPFs. The prediction rule and fully collapsed sampler are novel for this setting.

Significance
- Significant. The result removes a long-standing barrier to marginal/fully collapsed inference for BNBP-style mixed-membership models, with immediate implications for topic modeling and potential extensions to other count processes.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and fills a clear theoretical gap.
- Enables practical, truncation-free, fully collapsed inference with strong empirical mixing.
- Clear empirical characterization of differences with HDP-LDA, often achieving comparable/lower perplexity with fewer topics.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical gains over HDP-LDA are sometimes marginal when compared at fixed η; superiority is nuanced rather than uniform.
- Missing direct comparisons to existing BNBP inference (slice/truncated) and limited discussion of scalability/parallelization.
- Dense presentation of EPPF; some readers may need more intuition.

Overall recommendation
- Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution with practical impact. I suggest adding: (1) a comparison to slice and truncated BNBP samplers; (2) a brief scalability/parallelization discussion; and (3) light exposition improvements around the EPPF/prediction rule and sensitivity to rj. These are incremental and do not detract from the core contributions.
