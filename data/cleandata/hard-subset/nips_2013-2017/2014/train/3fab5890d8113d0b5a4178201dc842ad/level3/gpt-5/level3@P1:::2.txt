This paper develops the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) and a prediction rule for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, and leverages these results to construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the infinite beta process, topic parameters, and group-wise weights. The authors show that by viewing the BNBP via a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, one can derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then obtain a group-size dependent EPPF by conditioning on observed group sizes. These results yield a simple prediction rule enabling truncation-free, collapsed inference. Instantiated as a topic model, the sampler exhibits fast convergence and good mixing on three corpora, and often attains comparable or better perplexity than HDP-LDA with a more compact topic set.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on classic EPPF ideas (Pitman) and NRMI-based mixture modeling, but extends them to mixed-membership with integer-valued processes. Prior BNBP work relied on truncation [Zhou & Carin; Zhou et al.] or slice/adaptive truncation [Broderick et al.; Zhou et al.], and predictive constructions like the negative-binomial IBP/“ice cream” buffet. In contrast, this paper provides the missing marginal law that governs exchangeable partitions across groups and a fully collapsed sampler—conceptually paralleling how the Chinese restaurant process/CRF enable collapsed inference for the DP/HDP. The topic-model instantiation invites comparison with HDP-LDA (Teh et al.) and PFA-style count factorization.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A clear, general recipe to obtain an EPPF for mixed-membership models from an integer-valued process by randomizing group sizes, deriving an ECPF, and conditioning. This fills a notable gap for the BNBP and is potentially reusable for other count processes (e.g., GNBP).
- Novel derivations: Lemma 1’s equivalence via beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, the closed-form ECPF/EPPF, and the simple prediction rule are technically elegant and useful. The characterization of the column-exchangeable random count matrix (Poisson number of columns, digamma column sizes, Dirichlet-multinomial row splits) is insightful.
- Inference contribution: A truly fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP topic modeling (marginalizing B, topics, and weights), with simple updates and no truncation or slice variables. The multiplicative shrinkage mechanism differs meaningfully from HDP’s additive structure.
- Empirical indications: Convergence traces and perplexity curves suggest faster mixing than HDP’s direct-assignment sampler and competitive or superior predictive performance at matched topic counts, with compacter representations.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation misaligned with the stated motivation. The paper’s main contribution is an inference method for BNBP; however, the experiments primarily compare to HDP-LDA. The most relevant baselines—existing BNBP samplers (truncated/blocked Gibbs, slice/adaptive truncation)—are not empirically compared. The text asserts advantages in speed, complexity, and memory, but quantitative evidence is missing.
- Limited diagnostics. Claims of “fast convergence, good mixing” would be more convincing with time-to-target plots, effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, and wall-clock comparisons against prior BNBP samplers.
- Scope of datasets and sensitivity analyses. While three corpora and multiple η settings are considered, there is little exploration of sensitivity to rj, c, γ0 priors, or to corpus size, and limited ablations on collapsing choices.
- Clarity issues. The “digamma distribution” is nonstandard and might benefit from more intuition. Some notation is dense; brief proof sketches for key lemmas (beyond the appendix) and pseudo-code for the sampler would aid reproducibility.

Quality
- The mathematical development appears sound and well-motivated, with appropriate links to the literature. The derivations are careful, and the resulting sampler is consistent with the constructed EPPF/prediction rule.
- The experimental methodology for perplexity is standard, but the absence of direct BNBP-baseline comparisons weakens the empirical case for the proposed sampler’s advantages.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, but heavy. Minor suggestions: define and motivate the “digamma” PMF in the main text, provide a compact algorithm box for the collapsed sampler, and unify notation for counts and hyperparameters.

Originality
- High on the inference/theory side: the mixed-membership EPPF for BNBP and corresponding prediction rule are, to my knowledge, new. The fully collapsed BNBP sampler is also novel. The topic-model application is expected, but the shrinkage mechanism is distinct from HDP.

Significance
- Significant for Bayesian nonparametrics and count-based mixed-membership models: enables truncation-free, collapsed inference for BNBP and likely inspires analogous developments for other integer-valued processes. For topic modeling, the impact depends on demonstrating clear practical gains over existing BNBP samplers.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add empirical comparisons to (i) truncated/blocked BNBP samplers (e.g., [7], [10]), (ii) slice/adaptive truncation samplers (e.g., [11], [17]). Report wall-clock to a fixed perplexity, ESS per second, number of active topics, and memory footprint.
- Provide mixing diagnostics and sensitivity analyses for rj, c, γ0; include an ablation showing the effect of collapsing B and Θ separately.
- Consider comparison to CRF-collapsed HDP samplers for fairness, not only to direct-assignment HDP.
- Include pseudo-code and a brief main-text proof sketch of the key lemmas; add intuition for the prediction rule.
- Discuss the implications of sample-size dependent EPPFs and when this dependence is desirable.

Arguments for acceptance
- New, general EPPF construction for mixed-membership BNBP with a clean prediction rule.
- First fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP; simple, elegant, and practically appealing.
- Evidence of faster convergence and compact representations; potential to influence inference for other count processes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation does not directly validate the central claim (superior BNBP inference) against prior BNBP samplers.
- Lack of quantitative mixing/efficiency diagnostics and sensitivity studies.
- Some clarity and presentation gaps for broader accessibility.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical and algorithmic contribution whose empirical section needs to be better aligned with its stated goals. With the suggested comparisons and diagnostics, it would be a clear accept; in its current form, I view it as borderline, leaning positive on the strength of the inference contribution.
