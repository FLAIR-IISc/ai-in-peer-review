**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. Previous work on the BNBP for such tasks has relied on approximate inference methods like finite truncation or slice sampling, as a mechanism for collapsed inference (akin to the Chinese restaurant franchise for the HDP) was not available. The authors overcome this challenge through a novel technical approach: they first treat the group sizes as random variables to derive a joint distribution over partitions and group sizes, which they term an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF). From this, they use Bayes' rule to derive the desired EPPF for fixed, observed group sizes.

This theoretical development enables the construction of a new nonparametric topic model based on the BNBP, for which the authors derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. This sampler analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process, providing an elegant and efficient inference algorithm. The paper positions this work as a step toward a unified EPPF-based treatment for mixed-membership models, extending beyond the single-set clustering problems where EPPFs are more commonly used. Experimental results on several text corpora compare the proposed BNBP topic model to the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP), showing faster convergence, good mixing, and competitive predictive performance, particularly when a compact model representation is desired.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The derivation of the EPPF by first randomizing the group sizes is a clever and powerful technique that may be applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a complete and valuable piece of work. The claims about faster convergence and mixing appear well-supported by the trace plots in Figure 2. The authors are also reasonably transparent about the performance trade-offs with HDP in their experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though the theoretical sections are necessarily dense. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within the broader literature on EPPFs and mixed-membership models. The derivation of the prediction rule (Eq. 10) and its final form in the Gibbs sampler (Eq. 12) are clear and provide insight into the model's mechanism. However, the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the practical implications of its core mechanism. While the multiplicative nature of the update rule is contrasted with the HDP's additive rule, the paper does not fully articulate *why* or in what scenarios this multiplicative interaction is preferable for modeling real-world data.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The primary contribution—the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—is novel and solves an open problem. This provides a new, principled tool for the Bayesian nonparametric toolbox, distinct from the well-known HDP. While the BNBP itself is not new, this new formalization and the resulting fully collapsed sampler represent a significant advance over prior work that used truncation-based approximations.

**Significance:** The theoretical significance is high. This work helps bridge the gap between EPPF theory and a broader class of stochastic processes used for mixed-membership modeling. The practical significance is promising but less clear-cut. The development of a fully collapsed sampler that converges faster than the HDP's direct assignment sampler is a valuable practical contribution. However, the empirical results suggest that the performance benefits in terms of perplexity are nuanced. The model seems to excel at finding compact representations, but it is not shown to consistently outperform a well-tuned HDP that is allowed to use more topics. The added theoretical complexity of the BNBP-EPPF may be a barrier to adoption if the practical gains over the more established HDP are not more substantial or better characterized.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership context, which was an open problem.
*   **Pro:** The development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP is a solid contribution that enables efficient, truncation-free inference.
*   **Pro:** The proposed inference algorithm demonstrates clear empirical advantages in terms of convergence speed and mixing over the widely used HDP direct assignment sampler.
*   **Pro:** The work provides a new perspective on mixed-membership modeling, contrasting the BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage mechanism with the HDP's additive one.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The paper lacks a strong intuitive justification for why the BNBP model should be preferred over the HDP in practice. The connection between the model's mathematical properties and expected improvements on real-world tasks could be strengthened.
*   **Con:** The empirical improvements in predictive performance (perplexity) over HDP are not decisive enough to fully justify the model's considerable theoretical complexity. The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance seems conditional on desiring model compactness.

**Conclusion and Questions for Authors**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is elegant and the resulting fully collapsed sampler is a valuable addition to the literature. My main reservation is that the practical motivation and benefits are not as clearly articulated as the theoretical advances. Nevertheless, the theoretical novelty and the demonstrated improvements in sampler efficiency warrant acceptance. I am leaning toward accepting this paper.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following in their rebuttal:
1.  Could you provide more intuition on the practical implications of the multiplicative update rule in Eq. 12 compared to the additive one in HDP-LDA? For example, how does this affect the discovery of rare vs. common topics, or the topic proportions within documents?
2.  The experiments suggest the main advantage of the BNBP topic model is achieving strong performance with a more compact representation (fewer topics). Are there scenarios where you would expect the BNBP to achieve superior predictive performance over HDP-LDA even when the number of topics is not constrained? If so, what are the data characteristics that would favor the BNBP?
