

# Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on nonparametric Bayesian modeling by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabling fully collapsed inference for mixed-membership modeling. The authors extend the theoretical framework of EPPFs, which has been successfully applied to single-set mixture modeling (e.g., Chinese restaurant process), to the more challenging setting of grouped data where each group has an exchangeable set of data points.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF, which describes how the BNBP clusters data points across multiple groups. This is achieved by first developing an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for a group-size dependent model, then using Bayes' rule to derive the EPPF that describes the prior distribution of a latent column-exchangeable random count matrix. The authors also derive a simple prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions governed by the BNBP's EPPF.

The practical application of this theory is demonstrated through a novel BNBP topic model. The authors develop a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out not only the topics and topic weights but also the infinite-dimensional beta process. This results in a topic model with simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance.

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The theoretical derivations are clear and well-supported, with appropriate connections to prior work on EPPFs and mixed-membership modeling. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature regarding the lack of marginal distributions for integer-valued stochastic processes in mixed-membership modeling, and their approach to filling this gap is novel and well-executed.

In terms of significance, the paper's main contribution—deriving the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—is broadly useful and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The BNBP topic model provides a distinct alternative to existing approaches like HDP-LDA, with interesting theoretical differences in how the models control topic numbers (multiplicative vs. additive shrinkage mechanisms). However, the empirical results show only marginal performance gains over HDP-LDA, with the BNBP model typically representing the corpus with fewer topics but sometimes higher perplexity at small topic Dirichlet smoothing parameters (η).

The paper could be strengthened by comparing the collapsed sampler to the sliced sampler [10] and truncated sampler [8] for BNBP inference, as suggested in the key points. Additionally, the authors should evaluate uncollapsed representations for potential parallelization advantages and examine the mixing tradeoffs versus the collapsed sampler.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical contribution—deriving the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—which fills an important gap in the literature. The paper also provides a clear, practical application through the BNBP topic model, which demonstrates the utility of the theoretical framework.

The main weakness is that the empirical performance gains over HDP-LDA are marginal, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explore why the BNBP model might be preferable in certain applications beyond its compact representation. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could better articulate the specific scenarios where the BNBP's distinct characteristics would be advantageous.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. In clarity, it's well-organized and readable, though some of the more complex derivations could benefit from additional explanation for non-specialists. The originality is strong, as the paper addresses a gap in the literature that previous NIPS papers have not filled. The significance is moderate—while the BNBP topic model provides a useful alternative to HDP-LDA, the paper doesn't demonstrate a clear, substantial improvement in predictive performance that would justify widespread adoption.

The paper meets NIPS publication threshold with only minor experimental-section comments. The theoretical contribution is significant, the empirical evaluation is reasonable, and the writing is clear. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the literature on nonparametric Bayesian modeling by developing the first EPPF for the BNBP and demonstrating its application to topic modeling.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature, provides a clear and well-supported derivation of the BNBP's EPPF, and demonstrates a practical application through a novel topic model with good empirical performance.

Arguments against acceptance: The empirical performance gains over HDP-LDA are marginal, and the paper could have better articulated the specific advantages of the BNBP model in practical applications.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling, with a strong theoretical foundation and a practical application. It meets all NIPS publication criteria and should be accepted.
