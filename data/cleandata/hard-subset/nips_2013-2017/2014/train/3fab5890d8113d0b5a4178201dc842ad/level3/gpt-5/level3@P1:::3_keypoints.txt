- Main contribution: First derivation of an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting via a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation.
- Method: Derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for random group sizes, use Bayes’ rule to get a group-size–dependent EPPF, and provide a simple prediction rule enabling a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
- Application: Applied to topic modeling as a nonparametric alternative to HDP-LDA; sampler mixes well, converges quickly, and often uses fewer topics due to multiplicative shrinkage.
- Empirical results: Achieves comparable or lower perplexity than HDP-LDA when matched on number of topics and often attains similar predictive quality with fewer topics.
- Relation to prior work: Fills a gap absent from prior EPPFs/Poisson–Kingman samplers and complements existing BNBP work that used truncation or slice sampling; previous marginalizations targeted count matrices, not mixed-membership partitions.
- Technical strength: Principled, elegant route from integer-valued processes to group-size–dependent EPPFs; enables fully collapsed inference without truncation or slice variables.
- Mechanistic clarity: Clear comparison to HDP-LDA illustrating a multiplicative shrinkage mechanism that explains preference for more compact topic allocations.
- Practical impact: Collapsed sampler is straightforward to implement, runs fast, mixes well, and is practically usable for topic modeling.
- Scope/Generality: Approach suggests a template for deriving EPPFs for other integer-valued processes (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson).
- Missing empirical baselines: Should compare directly against existing BNBP inference schemes (slice sampling and finite truncation/blocked Gibbs) to isolate benefits of the collapsed sampler.
- Evaluation metrics: Over-reliance on perplexity and topic count; should include topic coherence and synthetic experiments to validate that learned partition statistics match EPPF predictions.
- Scalability/parallelization concern: Collapsed samplers are harder to parallelize; need discussion or small experiments contrasting uncollapsed/blocked/sliced representations for distributed settings.
- Sensitivity analyses needed: More systematic study of hyperparameters (rj, c, γ0), priors, and an ablation showing how rj affects overdispersion and topic counts is warranted.
- Exposition issues: EPPF/ECPF sections are dense; add intuition for the “digamma” distribution, a compact derivation sketch in main text, a notation table, and complete missing reference details.
- Technical quality: Derivations appear technically sound with careful measure-theoretic treatment and consistent ECPF/EPPF and prediction rule; inference is fully collapsed over topics, weights, and the beta process.
- Clarity: Manuscript is generally well written and organized; figures effectively illustrate mixing and shrinkage, though minor accessibility improvements are suggested.
- Originality: High — first EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership partitions and a novel fully collapsed prediction rule for this setting.
- Significance: Significant — removes a barrier to marginal/fully collapsed inference for BNBP-style models with immediate implications for topic modeling and extensions.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel theoretical result, enables truncation-free fully collapsed inference with strong empirical mixing, and provides clear empirical contrasts with HDP-LDA.
- Cons for acceptance: Empirical superiority is nuanced rather than uniformly strong, lacks direct comparisons to other BNBP samplers, and has a dense presentation that may hinder some readers.
- Recommendation: Accept, with requested additions—(1) comparisons to slice/truncated BNBP samplers, (2) a brief scalability/parallelization discussion or experiment, and (3) light exposition and sensitivity-analysis improvements.
