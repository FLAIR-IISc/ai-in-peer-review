Summary and relation to prior work
This paper derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership/count-mixture setting. The key trick is to first construct a group-size dependent model and derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for random group sizes and partitions, then marginalize and apply Bayes’ rule to obtain the desired EPPF over partitions with fixed, observed group sizes. The authors further derive a simple prediction rule that enables a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. As an illustration, they build a BNBP topic model whose sampler marginalizes the infinite beta process, topic weights, and topics.

This contribution sits alongside a long line of work on marginal samplers based on EPPFs for mixture models (e.g., CRP/NRMIs), but extends that paradigm to mixed-membership models where group-structured data and shared clusters complicate marginalization. It complements HDP’s Chinese Restaurant Franchise construction by providing, to my knowledge, the first explicit EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling. It also clarifies connections to gamma–Poisson negative-binomial constructions and related NB-IBP style models, while not falling within the standard σ-stable Poisson–Kingman class. The topic-model application highlights a multiplicative shrinkage mechanism distinct from the additive mechanism in HDP-LDA.

Strengths
- Clear, technically careful derivations: ECPF, EPPF, and prediction rule are well motivated and consistent with the augmented beta–gamma–Poisson view.
- Primary contribution is novel and broadly useful: an EPPF-based, truncation-free route to collapsed inference for BNBP mixed-membership models.
- Collapsed Gibbs sampler is simple to implement, mixes quickly in practice, and provides an instructive contrast to HDP-LDA (multiplicative vs additive popularity/shrinkage).
- Empirical study is competent: three corpora, perplexity-based evaluation, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to η. Results suggest distinct operating characteristics from HDP-LDA and comparable predictive performance with a more compact representation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical gains over HDP-LDA are generally marginal; the main conclusion is “different behaviors, not clear superiority.” This is fine, but the paper could better characterize when BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage is advantageous (e.g., document length dispersion, topic burstiness).
- Comparisons are limited to HDP-LDA’s direct-assignment sampler. Since the contribution is an inference scheme, please compare the collapsed sampler to alternative BNBP inference methods:
  - A sliced sampler [10].
  - A truncated/block sampler [8].
  Report convergence (ESS/autocorrelation), wall-clock to target perplexity, memory, and final perplexity.
- Parallelization: collapsed samplers can be hard to scale. Please evaluate uncollapsed/partially collapsed representations (e.g., with explicit Θ or B) that admit data-parallel updates, and discuss mixing tradeoffs versus the fully collapsed sampler.
- Additional diagnostics: include effective sample sizes for topic counts and number of topics, acceptance of new-topic proposals, and sensitivity to c, γ0, and inferred dispersion rj (these parameters are central to the model’s behavior).
- Broaden evaluation beyond perplexity: topic coherence or intruder tasks; qualitative topics; robustness across different train/test splits and smaller η regimes; runtime per iteration vs KJ.
- Clarity nits: briefly restate the implications of the sample-size dependence of the EPPF (lack of addition rule) and how this affects using the prediction rule in practice. Tighten the discussion of connections to negative-binomial IBP/“ice-cream” processes and how those differ in focus (feature allocation vs group-partition EPPF).

Quality
- Technically sound, with coherent derivations and a well-specified collapsed sampler. Claims about mixing and speed are supported by traces; predictive performance is fairly compared. Please strengthen by adding the sliced/truncated BNBP baselines and more rigorous MCMC diagnostics.

Clarity
- Well written and organized. The ECPF→EPPF flow, augmented representation, and topic-model sampler are clearly presented. Minor suggestions above would further improve readability and practical guidance.

Originality
- High. Deriving a group-size dependent EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling is new and nontrivial, and the general recipe may transfer to other integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP).

Significance
- Moderate to high. An EPPF-based collapsed sampler for BNBP removes truncation/slicing overhead and can influence a range of count-mixture applications. Empirically, performance is comparable to HDP-LDA with different inductive biases; clearer guidance on when to prefer BNBP would increase impact.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to priors on rj, c, and γ0? Do you observe multimodality in rj that affects topic counts?
- Can you provide a wall-clock comparison vs HDP-LDA CRF-style collapsed sampler (not only direct-assignment)?
- Does the prediction rule admit sparse sampling tricks akin to alias tables or F+ algorithms to reduce dependence on KJ?

Pros
- Novel, broadly useful theory (EPPF/prediction rule) for BNBP mixed-membership models.
- Practical, fully collapsed sampler with good mixing characteristics.
- Clear exposition; code availability.

Cons
- Limited baseline comparisons for BNBP inference (no sliced/truncated baselines).
- Empirical advantages over HDP-LDA are marginal; guidance on applicability is limited.
- No parallelization study.

Recommendation
- Weak Accept. Interesting and technically solid work that meets the bar, with minor experimental-section additions (sliced/truncated comparisons, diagnostics) that would strengthen the paper.
