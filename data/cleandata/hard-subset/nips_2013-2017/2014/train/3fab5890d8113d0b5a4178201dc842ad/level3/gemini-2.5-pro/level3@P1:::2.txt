This paper presents a novel inference method for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) applied to mixed-membership modeling. The primary contribution is the derivation of an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP. Previous inference methods for the BNBP, such as those in [10] and [11], have relied on finite truncation or slice sampling, as the marginal distribution governing the random partitions was unknown. By first deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for a model with random group sizes, the authors cleverly use Bayes' rule to arrive at the EPPF for fixed, observed group sizes. This EPPF enables the construction of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based models, which marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process. The authors demonstrate this sampler on a BNBP topic model, providing closed-form update equations and comparing its performance to the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) topic model.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is the paper's core strength. This is a significant and non-trivial theoretical result that advances the understanding and usability of the BNBP. The method of first defining a model with random group sizes to derive the ECPF, and then conditioning on the observed group sizes to obtain the EPPF, is an elegant and potentially generalizable technique. This provides a key missing piece for the BNBP, allowing it to be placed within the same theoretical framework as other nonparametric models for which collapsed samplers are known.

2.  **A Complete and Usable Algorithm:** The paper successfully translates the theoretical derivation into a practical, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model. The prediction rule (Eq. 10) and the final Gibbs update equation (Eq. 12) are presented clearly and appear straightforward to implement. This makes the contribution immediately useful to practitioners and researchers working with mixed-membership models.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and walking the reader through the steps of their derivation from the ECPF to the EPPF and the final sampler. The distinction between their approach and prior work on the BNBP and HDP is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Insufficient and Mismatched Experimental Evaluation:** This is the major weakness of the paper. The authors motivate their work by claiming the proposed sampler offers "simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance." However, the experiments do not provide the necessary evidence to substantiate these claims in the most relevant context.

    The paper's contribution is a new **sampling algorithm** for the **BNBP model**. Therefore, the most direct and crucial comparison would be against other existing samplers for the BNBP, such as the finite truncation sampler [7, 10] or the slice sampler [11]. Such a comparison would isolate the benefits of the proposed fully collapsed sampler in terms of convergence speed, mixing, and computational efficiency for the *same underlying model*.

    Instead, the authors compare their BNBP sampler against a sampler for the HDP-LDA. This is a comparison between two different **models** (BNBP vs. HDP) as well as two different **samplers**. The observed differences in convergence, mixing, and perplexity are therefore confounded; it is impossible to disentangle whether the effects are due to the superiority of the proposed sampler or fundamental differences in the posterior landscapes of the two models. For example, is the faster convergence shown in Figure 2 due to the efficiency of the collapsed sampler or simply because the BNBP posterior is easier to explore than the HDP posterior? The current experiments cannot answer this question. Without a direct comparison to other BNBP inference schemes, the central claims about the sampler's advantages remain empirically unproven.

**Evaluation based on NIPS criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical derivations appear to be technically sound and represent a complete piece of theoretical work. However, the paper's overall quality is diminished by the weak experimental section, which fails to adequately support the claims made about the proposed sampler.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The mathematical developments are easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The derivation of the EPPF and the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP is highly original. It is a novel contribution that clearly differs from previous work on this model.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance is high. A fast, efficient, and theoretically-grounded inference algorithm for the BNBP would be a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian machine learning. However, the significance is currently undermined by the lack of a proper empirical demonstration of its advantages over existing BNBP samplers.

In summary, the paper presents a strong and elegant theoretical contribution but fails to validate it with the appropriate experiments. The comparison with HDP-LDA is interesting as a model comparison, but it does not serve to evaluate the central claims about the proposed sampling algorithm. To make a compelling case for acceptance, the authors should provide a direct experimental comparison of their fully collapsed Gibbs sampler against at least one other existing inference method for the BNBP.
