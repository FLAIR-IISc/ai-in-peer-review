This paper introduces a novel method for performing inference in models based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). The primary contribution is the derivation of a group-size dependent Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP. This theoretical result enables the development of the first fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership models, which analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process. Previously, inference for such models relied on approximations like finite truncation or slice sampling. The authors demonstrate their method by constructing a BNBP topic model and presenting an efficient CG sampler for it. The work positions itself as a contribution to inference methodology, aiming to provide a sampler that is simple to implement, converges quickly, mixes well, and achieves strong predictive performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP (Lemmas 1-3) and the resulting prediction rule (Lemma 4) is a significant and elegant theoretical contribution. This is a non-trivial result that advances the understanding of the combinatorial structure of the BNBP and provides a new avenue for exact inference. It successfully generalizes prior work on EPPFs for single sets of data to the more complex mixed-membership setting.

2.  **Algorithm Development:** The paper successfully translates the theoretical EPPF into a practical and simple-to-implement fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The update equations are intuitive and highlight the distinct multiplicative mechanism by which the BNBP combines global and local cluster popularities, contrasting nicely with the additive mechanism of the HDP. This provides a valuable new tool for practitioners and researchers using BNBP models.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do a good job of explaining the connections between the beta process, negative binomial process, and the augmented beta-gamma-Poisson representation, which helps in understanding the derivation of the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and the subsequent EPPF.

**Weaknesses**

The main weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is insufficient to support the central claims about the proposed sampler.

1.  **Mismatched Motivation and Evaluation:** The paper's motivation is centered on the advantages of the proposed *sampler*: "simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance." However, the experiments do not directly validate these claims. Instead of evaluating the sampler, the experiments compare two different statistical models: the BNBP topic model and the HDP-LDA. While this comparison is interesting, it confounds the properties of the models with the properties of their respective samplers. The observed differences in perplexity and the number of topics could be due to the different inductive biases of the BNBP and HDP, rather than the efficiency of the proposed inference algorithm.

2.  **Inappropriate Baseline for Sampler Comparison:** As the paper's primary contribution is a new inference algorithm for the BNBP, the most critical experimental comparison is missing: a direct comparison of the proposed fully collapsed sampler against other existing inference methods for the *same* BNBP model. Relevant baselines would include the truncated block Gibbs sampler (as in Zhou et al., 2012 [7, 10]) and the slice sampler (Broderick et al., 2013 [11]). Such a comparison would directly test the claims of faster convergence and better mixing. Without it, the advantages of the new sampler over existing BNBP inference techniques remain unsubstantiated.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The authors claim in the discussion that their "collapsed Gibbs sampler clearly outperforms the blocked Gibbs sampler of [7] in terms of convergence speed, computational complexity and memory requirement." This is a key claim that should be the focus of the experimental section, yet no empirical evidence is provided to support it. The trace plots in Figure 2 show good mixing for the BNBP sampler, but this is only shown in comparison to an HDP sampler, not another BNBP sampler.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

The theoretical contribution of this paper is strong. To become a top-tier conference paper, the experimental validation needs to be significantly strengthened to align with the paper's stated contributions.

*   The authors should perform a direct empirical comparison of their fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with other BNBP samplers (e.g., truncation-based or slice samplers).
*   This comparison should be conducted on the same BNBP topic model and datasets. Key metrics to report would include:
    *   **Convergence Speed:** Plot the held-out log-likelihood or perplexity against wall-clock time and iteration number for all compared samplers. This would directly test the "fast convergence" claim.
    *   **Mixing:** Provide autocorrelation plots for key model parameters (like the number of topics or hyperparameters) to empirically demonstrate the "good mixing" claim.
    *   **Predictive Performance:** Compare the final perplexity achieved by each sampler to ensure that the collapsed sampler reaches a comparable or better posterior mode.
*   The comparison with HDP-LDA is valuable but should be framed as a secondary analysis that explores the modeling differences between BNBP and HDP, once the efficiency of the proposed sampler has been established.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a strong and novel theoretical contribution by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP and developing the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. However, the experimental evaluation is critically flawed as it fails to compare the proposed sampler against the appropriate baselines (i.e., other BNBP samplers). This omission undermines the central claims about the sampler's superior performance in terms of convergence and mixing. While the theoretical work is of high quality, the lack of proper empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed algorithm. I would be enthusiastic about this paper if the experimental section were revised to directly support its claims.
