- Main contribution: derivation of the EPPF for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out the beta process without truncation or slice sampling.  
- Method: introduce an auxiliary model with random group sizes to obtain an ECPF, then use Bayes’ rule to condition on fixed observed group sizes and derive the EPPF.  
- Implementation: develop a BNBP topic model with simple closed-form Gibbs update equations.  
- Claimed advantages: simpler implementation, faster convergence, better mixing, and improved predictive performance relative to alternatives like HDP-LDA.  
- Strength (theory): the EPPF derivation is novel, elegant, and potentially generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes.  
- Strength (inference): enables a truly collapsed sampler for the BNBP, improving on prior truncation- or slice-based inference.  
- Strength (presentation): paper is well-written, clearly structured, and explains connections to LDA and HDP-LDA.  
- Potential significance: if empirical claims hold, the sampler would make BNBP a practical alternative to HDP for mixed-membership modeling.  
- Main weakness: experimental evaluation does not adequately support claims about the sampler’s advantages.  
- Experimental design flaw: comparisons are mainly to HDP-LDA, conflating model differences (BNBP vs HDP) with inference-algorithm differences.  
- Missing baselines: no direct comparison to existing BNBP inference methods (truncated block Gibbs by Zhou et al. 2012, slice sampler by Broderick et al. 2013).  
- Missing metrics: should report wall-clock time to convergence, trace plots, sample autocorrelations, and final predictive perplexity for fair algorithmic comparison.  
- Unsupported claims: “fast convergence,” “good mixing,” and “state-of-the-art predictive performance” are not substantiated by controlled comparisons to other BNBP samplers or modern topic models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, sound theoretical contribution; important algorithmic development; clear writing and communication.  
- Arguments against acceptance: critically flawed empirical validation and confounded comparisons prevent verification of the paper’s central algorithmic claims.  
- Conclusion: theoretically strong and promising, but empirical validation is insufficient—acceptance contingent on revised experiments directly comparing the proposed sampler to other BNBP inference schemes.
