Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, addressing a gap where prior work had to rely on truncation or slice methods because the marginal law governing partitions of grouped data was unknown. The authors proceed by (i) introducing a sample-size dependent model that yields an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for joint group sizes and partitions; (ii) marginalizing to obtain a prior on a column-exchangeable random count matrix; and (iii) using Bayes’ rule to derive the group-size dependent EPPF and a simple prediction rule. They then instantiate these results in a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, document-specific topic weights, and the beta process, and they compare to LDA and HDP-LDA.

The work builds on and relates to the DP/HDP literature and EPPFs (e.g., CRP and Pitman’s framework [4,5,6]), and to recent BNBP/GNBP and count-mixture models [7,10,11]. It also connects to “negative binomial IBP” and related buffet-process constructions [16,17], clarifying how the BNBP induces partitions over grouped data with observed group sizes—distinct from infinite random count-matrix settings. The topic-model application highlights a multiplicative shrinkage mechanism differing from the HDP’s additive one, with comparisons in perplexity and mixing behavior.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A principled derivation of an ECPF and a group-size dependent EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership modeling, plus a clear prediction rule. This fills a notable theoretical gap and unifies the integer-valued process view with partition-based inference.
- Algorithmic novelty: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP topic modeling that marginalizes the beta process, topic distributions, and local weights. The update is simple to implement and computationally attractive.
- Conceptual clarity: The augmented beta–gamma–Poisson view and the multiplicative global–local popularity term provide useful insight and a distinct alternative to HDP-LDA.
- Practical promise: Empirically good mixing and faster convergence than the HDP direct-assignment sampler, with competitive or better perplexity for comparable numbers of topics and a more compact representation.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is not aligned with the paper’s main algorithmic contribution. The primary comparisons focus on HDP-LDA and LDA, but the paper does not provide head-to-head comparisons against existing BNBP samplers (finite truncation/block Gibbs [7,10], slice/adaptive truncation [11], or related NB-process samplers).
- Limited diagnostics. Beyond trace plots, there is no report of effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, ESS/sec, or wall-clock time to a target accuracy—metrics that are essential when the main claim is “fast convergence” and “good mixing.”
- Missing ablations. The benefits of full collapsing (vs a partially collapsed or truncated BNBP) are not quantified; the impact of inferring rj versus fixing them is only qualitatively discussed.
- Clarity/organization: Some definitions (e.g., the “digamma distribution”) and the implications of sample-size dependent EPPFs could be explained earlier and more intuitively. The relationship to [16,17] and why those predictive structures do not yield an EPPF for grouped data with fixed sizes could be made more explicit.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The derivations and constructions appear technically sound, with proofs sketched and relegated to the appendix. The sampler is well specified. The empirical section demonstrates feasibility and competitive perplexity, but the lack of comparisons to other BNBP samplers and absence of rigorous mixing diagnostics undercut the strength of the empirical claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written, with clear notation and motivation. That said, readers would benefit from: (i) a more intuitive discussion of sample-size dependent EPPFs and exchangeability; (ii) a brief standalone description of the “digamma distribution”; (iii) a concise complexity analysis per iteration and memory costs; and (iv) more guidance on hyperparameter settings and sensitivity.
- Originality: High on the theoretical side (ECPF/EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership; prediction rule; fully collapsed sampler). The topic-model instantiation is natural rather than novel as a model, but the inference scheme is new and insightful.
- Significance: Potentially significant for Bayesian nonparametrics and mixed-membership modeling. The general technique to derive EPPFs for integer-valued processes could influence future work (e.g., GNBP). However, the empirical significance is currently limited by insufficient comparisons to competing BNBP inference methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add direct comparisons to other BNBP samplers:
  - Finite-truncation/block Gibbs [7,10] and adaptive truncation/slice sampling [11].
  - Report perplexity, runtime, ESS and ESS/sec for key latent quantities (topic counts, assignments, rj), and wall-clock time to reach a target perplexity.
  - Compare the inferred number of topics and memory/compute use.
- Include synthetic experiments with known ground truth to evaluate partition recovery (e.g., NMI/ARI) under varying rj, γ0, c, and group sizes; test robustness to overdispersion.
- Provide ablations: (i) fully collapsed vs partially collapsed/truncated BNBP; (ii) fixing versus inferring rj; (iii) sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization.
- Expand clarity:
  - Brief tutorial on sample-size dependent EPPFs and their implications for modeling and prediction.
  - Define the digamma distribution and give intuition for its role.
  - Complexity analysis in terms of J, total tokens, and active topics KJ; discuss sparse updates.
- Ensure references are complete and formatted uniformly.

Arguments for acceptance
- Fills an acknowledged theoretical gap by providing an EPPF/prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Fully collapsed sampler is elegant, simple, and likely useful beyond topic modeling.
- Empirical evidence suggests faster convergence and good mixing relative to HDP direct assignment, with compact representations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation does not test the central claim against the most relevant baselines (other BNBP samplers).
- Lacks rigorous mixing diagnostics and scalability analyses.
- Some clarity gaps around sample-size dependent EPPFs and distributions used.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper has substantial theoretical and algorithmic contributions and is very promising, but the experimental section does not adequately validate the claimed advantages against the most relevant alternatives. Strengthening the empirical evaluation as suggested would likely tip this to an easy accept.
