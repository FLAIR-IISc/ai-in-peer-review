

This paper makes a significant contribution by deriving the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabling a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for mixed-membership modeling. The authors generalize the EPPF framework to handle grouped data with fixed group sizes, which is a notable extension beyond previous work on single-set mixture models. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and prediction rule provides a theoretically sound foundation for understanding how this integer-valued stochastic process governs exchangeable random partitions across multiple groups. The BNBP topic model they construct demonstrates the practical utility of this theoretical advance, with a straightforward implementation that converges faster and mixes better than HDP-LDA in their experiments.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful proofs of key lemmas, and the manuscript is well-organized with clear explanations of the novel concepts. The authors effectively relate their work to related processes like the HDP and negative binomial IBP, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field. The originality is substantial, as the group-size-dependent EPPF for mixed-membership modeling represents a new theoretical framework not previously developed for integer-valued stochastic processes.

However, the empirical evaluation has limitations. While the BNBP topic model shows promising convergence properties, the performance gains over HDP-LDA are marginal, and the paper doesn't clearly establish a clear advantage in predictive performance under standard evaluation metrics. The authors should compare their collapsed sampler against alternative inference methods like the sliced sampler [10] and truncated sampler [8] for BNBP, as these would provide more comprehensive insights into the sampler's efficiency. Additionally, exploring uncollapsed representations for potential parallelization advantages would strengthen the empirical section.

The significance of this work is high, as the derived EPPF provides a unified theoretical framework for mixed-membership modeling with integer-valued processes, which could inspire future work on other stochastic processes. While the BNBP topic model may not outperform HDP-LDA in all settings, it offers a distinct modeling perspective with practical advantages in implementation and convergence speed.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The minor suggestions for additional experimental comparisons would further strengthen the work, but the paper already meets the publication threshold for NIPS with its clear exposition, technical soundness, and novel theoretical framework. I recommend acceptance.
