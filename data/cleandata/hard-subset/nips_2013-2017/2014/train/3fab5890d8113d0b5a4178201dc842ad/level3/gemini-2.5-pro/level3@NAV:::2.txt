This paper introduces a significant theoretical development for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), a non-parametric prior used for mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive a novel, group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP. This key result enables the development of the first fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for this model class, providing an alternative to previous inference methods that relied on finite truncation or slice sampling. The work is well-situated in the literature, building upon prior work on the BNBP (e.g., Zhou et al., 2012; Broderick et al., 2013) and drawing connections to the broader framework of EPPFs and other mixed-membership models like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP).

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical originality and quality. The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF and the resulting prediction rule (Lemma 1-4) is a novel and valuable contribution. This allows for the construction of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, which is a desirable achievement, as such samplers often exhibit superior mixing properties and faster convergence compared to partially collapsed or uncollapsed versions. The paper is clearly written, and the mathematical derivations are presented logically, making the work accessible and reproducible for experts in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weakness of the paper is a significant disconnect between its core contribution and its experimental evaluation. The authors' motivation for developing the new sampler centers on its purported advantages in implementation, convergence speed, and mixing. However, the experiments do not validate these claims against the appropriate baselines. The paper's contribution is algorithmic—a new sampler for the BNBP—not a new statistical model. Therefore, the empirical evaluation should demonstrate the superiority of this new sampler over existing inference algorithms for the *same model*.

Instead, the experiments compare the proposed BNBP-CG sampler against a sampler for a different model, the HDP-LDA. This comparison confounds the performance of the model with the performance of the inference algorithm, making it impossible to conclude anything about the specific advantages of the proposed sampler itself. To substantiate the claims, the authors should have compared their CG sampler against existing BNBP samplers, such as the truncated blocked Gibbs sampler or slice sampler. Without this direct comparison, the claims about faster convergence and better mixing remain unsupported by empirical evidence, which substantially weakens the paper's impact. While the theoretical work is strong, its practical significance is not adequately demonstrated.
