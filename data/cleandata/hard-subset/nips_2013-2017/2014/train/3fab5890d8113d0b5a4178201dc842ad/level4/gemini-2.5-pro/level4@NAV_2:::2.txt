This paper addresses an important limitation in the application of the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. Current inference methods for the BNBP, such as those based on finite truncation or slice sampling, are necessitated by the absence of a known marginal probability distribution that governs the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. The central contribution of this work is the derivation of this marginal distribution, which the authors formulate as a group-size dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF). This theoretical development enables the construction of the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, which analytically marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process. The authors apply this new inference scheme to a BNBP topic model, demonstrating its properties and comparing its performance against the widely-used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) topic model.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Theoretical Quality:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a novel and significant theoretical contribution. By first deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for random group sizes and then using Bayes' rule, the authors provide a principled method to obtain the desired EPPF for fixed group sizes. This closes an important theoretical gap and provides a more elegant framework for inference in BNBP-based models.
*   **Algorithmic Contribution:** The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a direct and valuable outcome of the theoretical work. The prediction rule presented in Lemma 4 is simple and intuitive, combining a topic's global popularity with its group-specific popularity in a multiplicative fashion, which is distinct from the additive nature of the HDP.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively motivates the problem, details the theoretical derivations, and explains the resulting topic model and its inference.

**Weaknesses:**

My primary concern relates to the experimental evaluation and its alignment with the paper's stated motivations. The paper presents the development of the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler as a key contribution, motivated by its purported advantages in "simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance."

However, the experiments primarily compare the BNBP topic model with the HDP-LDA topic model. This experimental design evaluates the characteristics of the BNBP *model* against the HDP *model*, rather than the proposed *sampler* against alternative samplers for the *same model*. To truly substantiate the claims about the sampler's superior convergence and mixing, a direct comparison against other inference algorithms for the BNBP is required. The introduction mentions existing methods, including finite truncation [10, 7] and slice sampling [11]. A comparative study demonstrating the proposed sampler's improved convergence speed, mixing properties, and computational efficiency over these alternatives would provide much stronger evidence for its practical advantages and would be more consistent with the paper's focus on the sampling algorithm. While the comparison to HDP-LDA is interesting for model characterization, it does not validate the specific claims made about the new inference algorithm itself.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a strong, novel, and valuable theoretical result for the Bayesian nonparametrics community.
*   The paper introduces the first fully collapsed, truncation-free Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, which is a significant algorithmic contribution.
*   The work has the potential for high impact, making the BNBP a more practical and accessible tool for mixed-membership modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is mismatched with the central claims of the paper. Claims about the sampler's fast convergence and good mixing are not sufficiently supported, as there is no comparison to existing samplers for the same BNBP model.
