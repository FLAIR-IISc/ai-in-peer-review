

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), with a focus on deriving its exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF). The authors address a key gap in the literature by developing a marginal distribution for the BNBP that governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, which was previously unavailable. This work builds upon foundational contributions to exchangeable partitions (Pitman, 2006) and mixed-membership modeling (HDP, 2006), but extends them by introducing a method to derive EPPFs for integer-valued stochastic processes through randomization of group sizes—a technique not previously applied to BNBP. The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions, including the derivation of an EPPF for the BNBP, a prediction rule for generating exchangeable random partitions, and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a novel topic model that analytically marginalizes out both the topic weights and the infinite-dimensional beta process.

The strengths of this work are substantial. The theoretical contribution is significant, as the authors successfully derive an EPPF for BNBP, which has been missing from the literature despite previous applications of BNBP to mixed-membership modeling. The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous mathematical derivations, with clear connections to prior work on exchangeable partitions (e.g., Pitman's EPPF model) and the broader context of normalized random measures with independent increments (NRMIs). The clarity of the paper is good, with well-organized presentation of the mathematical developments and the connection to practical topic modeling applications. The originality is high, as the method to derive EPPFs via randomization of group sizes represents a novel technique that could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The significance of the work is evident in the state-of-the-art predictive performance achieved by the BNBP topic model, which outperforms HDP-LDA in terms of convergence speed, mixing properties, and compact representation of the corpus.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The practical utility of the proposed framework remains somewhat unclear. While the BNBP topic model demonstrates superior convergence and mixing properties compared to HDP-LDA, the inference algorithm is more complex both theoretically and computationally. The paper does not sufficiently justify why this increased complexity is warranted in practical applications, as the improvement in predictive performance (measured by perplexity) appears marginal when the number of topics is comparable. The authors fail to provide an intuitive explanation for why the BNBP framework would outperform HDP in real-world applications, which is a critical gap for practitioners who must weigh computational cost against performance gains. Additionally, the paper does not adequately address how the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism for topic shrinkage translates to practical advantages over the HDP's additive mechanism.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution that fills a significant gap in the literature by deriving the BNBP's EPPF; novel method for deriving EPPFs via randomization of group sizes; fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with fast convergence and good mixing properties; state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact corpus representation.

Arguments against acceptance: Increased algorithmic complexity without compelling evidence of practical benefits; marginal improvement in predictive performance over HDP-LDA; lack of intuitive explanation for why the BNBP framework would outperform HDP in practical applications; insufficient justification for the computational overhead.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and provides a well-motivated framework for mixed-membership modeling. While the practical benefits require further demonstration, the theoretical novelty and technical soundness of the work justify its acceptance, particularly given the growing interest in unified approaches to exchangeable partitions and mixed-membership modeling at NIPS.
