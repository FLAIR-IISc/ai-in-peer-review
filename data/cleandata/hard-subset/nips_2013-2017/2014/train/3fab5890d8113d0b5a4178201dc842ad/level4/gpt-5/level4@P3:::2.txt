Summary
This paper develops the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership models, where data are grouped and each group’s observations are partitioned into a shared, potentially unbounded set of clusters. The authors proceed in two stages: (i) they introduce a group-size–dependent model that treats group sizes as random and derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by marginalizing the beta process; (ii) they then condition on fixed group sizes to obtain the BNBP’s EPPF and a simple prediction rule. Building on these results, they propose a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the beta process, topic parameters, and group-specific weights, yielding a nonparametric Bayesian topic model distinct from HDP-LDA. Empirically, they compare the proposed BNBP sampler with HDP-LDA on several corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS), reporting faster convergence, good mixing, and competitive or better predictive perplexity at comparable topic counts.

The paper situates the contribution relative to (i) classical EPPF/CRP developments for mixture models (Pitman; Blackwell–MacQueen), (ii) mixed-membership priors such as the HDP (Teh et al.), and (iii) BNBP- and NBP-related work using truncation or slice sampling for inference (Zhou et al.; Heaukulani & Roy; Zhou et al., arXiv), as well as Indian buffet process–style feature models. The key novelty is an explicit, tractable EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, enabling a truly collapsed sampler without truncations.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Derivation of the ECPF and the group-size–dependent EPPF for the BNBP using a beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation and Poissonization. The prediction rule is simple and intuitive, decomposing global vs. local popularity.
- Inference: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that integrates out the beta process and topic parameters, yielding fast mixing and reduced bookkeeping compared to the HDP’s Chinese restaurant franchise.
- Conceptual clarity: The paper clarifies how integer-valued processes (BNBP) induce exchangeable partitions in grouped data, bridging count modeling and mixed-membership clustering.
- Practical relevance: The topic modeling instantiation is well motivated; the sampler appears computationally efficient and easy to implement; experiments indicate strong mixing and competitive perplexity with a compact topic representation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental scope: The central claim is about the benefits of a fully collapsed sampler for the BNBP. However, the empirical comparison is primarily to HDP-LDA rather than to competing inference schemes for the BNBP itself (e.g., finite or adaptive truncation with blocked Gibbs; slice sampling). To substantiate the algorithmic advantage, please compare against:
  - Truncated BNBP/GNBP samplers (e.g., Zhou & Carin, PAMI; Zhou et al., AISTATS).
  - Slice samplers or other non-truncation methods for BNBP/GNBP (e.g., Heaukulani & Roy; adaptive truncation).
  Report wall-clock time, iterations to convergence, effective sample size, and perplexity vs. time.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Provide ablations isolating the impact of full collapse (e.g., partially collapsed vs. fully collapsed) and sensitivity analyses for c, γ0, and priors on rj. The paper argues rj critically controls overdispersion; demonstrate posterior robustness and the effect on predictive performance.
- Evaluation metrics: Perplexity is appropriate, but additional diagnostics (topic coherence, sparsity/compactness trade-offs, and calibration of held-out likelihoods) would strengthen claims. Include perplexity vs. number of topics and perplexity vs. runtime plots for all methods.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - The EPPF is sample-size dependent by design; briefly discuss practical implications and when this is desirable. Consider adding an intuitive description before the formal expression.
  - Define the “digamma distribution” more clearly and justify its use; a short intuitive explanation would aid readers unfamiliar with this construction.
  - Streamline notation early (e.g., consistent use of dots for sums, indexing conventions) and relocate less critical identities to the appendix while keeping a roadmap in the main text.

Quality
- The mathematical development appears sound, with careful augmentation and marginalization. The prediction rule aligns with the EPPF structure and supports a clean collapsed sampler. However, the empirical validation does not yet directly test the main algorithmic claim against alternative BNBP inference methods; this limits the strength of the conclusions about “fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance” as properties of the sampler rather than of the model class.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but dense. Providing brief intuitions around the ECPF/EPPF and the role of rj would help a broader NIPS audience. Minor notation and exposition refinements as noted above would improve readability.

Originality
- High on the inference/theory side: to my knowledge, this is the first explicit EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling, and the resulting fully collapsed sampler is new. The topic modeling application is a natural but standard testbed.

Significance
- The result is likely to be useful: a principled, truncation-free, collapsed sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership models fills a gap and could stimulate further work on integer-valued processes beyond the Dirichlet family. The general method (via ECPF and conditioning on group sizes) may extend to other processes (e.g., gamma–Poisson, gamma–negative binomial), increasing impact. The empirical section will better support these claims once comparisons to alternative BNBP inference schemes are added.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid derivation of an EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP in mixed-membership settings.
- Fully collapsed sampler that is simple and potentially widely useful.
- Clear connection between count processes and exchangeable partitions; practical topic modeling results.

Cons for acceptance
- Experiments do not compare to other BNBP inference algorithms; current comparisons mostly test models rather than samplers.
- Limited sensitivity and ablation analyses; limited evaluation metrics.
- Dense presentation in places; certain definitions could be clearer for a general NIPS audience.

Overall assessment
A strong theoretical and algorithmic contribution that addresses an important gap and is relevant to the NIPS community. With additional empirical comparisons to existing BNBP inference methods and modest clarity improvements, this paper would make a compelling case for acceptance.
