

This paper introduces a novel exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling, where data is grouped into exchangeable subsets with potentially unbounded partitions shared across groups. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while EPPFs have been extensively studied for single-set data (e.g., Chinese restaurant process), no unified treatment existed for mixed-membership modeling. They develop a method to derive the BNBP's EPPF by first randomizing group sizes, deriving the joint distribution of group sizes and partitions, and then using Bayes' rule to obtain the EPPF governing the prior distribution of a latent column-exchangeable random count matrix. This approach generalizes prior work on EPPFs [5, 12] and provides a foundation for fully collapsed inference in BNBP-based models.

The paper's primary technical contribution is the rigorous derivation of the BNBP's EPPF, which enables a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model. The authors demonstrate that their method avoids the truncation requirements of prior BNBP inference approaches [7, 10, 11] and provides closed-form update equations that are straightforward to implement. The theoretical development is sound, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the beta process and negative binomial process, and the paper correctly situates its contribution within the broader context of nonparametric Bayesian models [1, 2, 6, 7].

The BNBP topic model serves as a compelling application of their theoretical development. The model is distinct from existing approaches like HDP-LDA [6, 22] in its multiplicative mechanism for combining topic popularity across groups (n−ji·k/(c+n−ji·k+r·) · (n−jijk + rj) versus HDP's additive mechanism (n−jijk + αr̃k)). The authors provide a thorough comparison of the sampling mechanisms, highlighting how the BNBP's collapsed sampler eliminates the need for tuning parameters like α and K, while achieving faster convergence and better mixing than HDP-LDA (as demonstrated in Figure 2).

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured theoretical development and intuitive explanations of complex concepts. The presentation of the prediction rule (Equation 10) and the simulation results in Figure 1 effectively illustrate how the BNBP's dispersion parameters control overdispersion in the data. The experimental section is comprehensive, with evaluations on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) and careful attention to the relationship between topic count, Dirichlet smoothing parameter η, and perplexity (Figure 3).

However, the significance of the work could be strengthened by more clearly articulating how the BNBP's EPPF derivation method generalizes beyond the specific process studied. While the authors mention the potential to apply this approach to other integer-valued stochastic processes (gamma-Poisson, gamma-negative binomial), they do not demonstrate this generalization with a concrete example. The experimental comparison is also limited to HDP-LDA, without contrasting against the sliced sampler [10] or truncated samplers [8] that have been previously used for BNBP inference. Such comparisons would better highlight the advantages of their collapsed sampler, particularly regarding parallelization potential (a key practical consideration for large-scale applications).

The paper is technically strong, with well-supported claims and careful attention to theoretical details. The derivation of the EPPF is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model provides a practical demonstration of the theoretical advances, though the performance improvements over HDP-LDA are marginal in some settings (as shown in Figure 3), suggesting the primary contribution is methodological rather than empirical.

Strengths:
- Novel derivation of BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling
- Rigorous theoretical development with careful mathematical treatment
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with closed-form updates
- Clear comparison with HDP-LDA and analysis of sampling mechanisms
- Strong empirical validation across multiple corpora

Weaknesses:
- Limited experimental comparison with alternative BNBP inference methods
- Insufficient demonstration of how the general EPPF derivation method applies to other processes
- Marginal practical improvements over HDP-LDA in some settings

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by developing the first EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using the BNBP. The method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership models is generalizable and could be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes, making it a valuable tool for the field. The BNBP topic model is well-motivated and demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical development.

Con-acceptance arguments: The empirical results, while solid, do not strongly demonstrate the practical advantages of the BNBP over existing approaches. The comparison with HDP-LDA is standard, but the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with alternative BNBP inference methods to better showcase the advantages of the collapsed sampler.

Overall, this is an important theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the practical advantages over existing methods are somewhat limited, the theoretical development and methodological contribution are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The authors should consider expanding the discussion of their general EPPF derivation method to better highlight its broader applicability.
