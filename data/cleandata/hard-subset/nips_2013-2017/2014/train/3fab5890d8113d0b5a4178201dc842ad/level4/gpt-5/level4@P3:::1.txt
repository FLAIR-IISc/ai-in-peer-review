Summary
The paper develops a principled, truncation-free treatment of the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) for mixed-membership modeling. The core technical contribution is an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP that governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. Because directly deriving an EPPF is difficult, the authors first introduce an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by randomizing group sizes, then apply Bayes’ rule to obtain the group-size–dependent EPPF and an associated prediction rule. Building on this, they construct a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler and instantiate a nonparametric topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding simple updates and good empirical behavior.

Relation to prior work
- For single-population mixture modeling, marginalizations of normalized random measures (e.g., Dirichlet process) yield EPPFs such as the Chinese restaurant process (Blackwell & MacQueen; Pitman), enabling fully collapsed inference.
- For mixed-membership models (grouped data), the HDP achieves collapsed inference via the Chinese restaurant franchise, but analogous EPPFs for integer-valued processes like the BNBP or gamma–Poisson/GNBP had been unavailable. Prior inference used truncation or slice sampling.
- The paper extends the line of work on sample-size dependent EPPFs (e.g., Zhou & Walker) and distinguishes the BNBP from σ-stable Poisson–Kingman mixtures (Lomelí et al.), for which existing marginal samplers do not apply. It also clarifies connections to IBP variants for count matrices (e.g., “multi-scoop,” “ice-cream,” and negative-binomial IBPs).
- The proposed BNBP topic model differs from LDA and HDP-LDA in its multiplicative shrinkage mechanism combining global and local popularity, versus the additive form in HDP.

Strengths
- Original theory: A clear, general path from an integer-valued stochastic process to an ECPF and then to a group-size–dependent EPPF for mixed-membership modeling. To my knowledge, this is the first such EPPF for the BNBP.
- Useful prediction rule: Despite the complex EPPF, the authors derive a simple and intuitive prediction rule that decouples global and local popularity and highlights the role of the dispersion parameters rj.
- Fully collapsed sampling: The topic model’s sampler marginalizes the infinite beta process as well as topics and weights, leading to fast convergence and good mixing in practice.
- Empirical evidence: On three corpora, the method achieves competitive or improved perplexity with fewer topics than HDP-LDA under moderate Dirichlet smoothing, and the chains mix substantially faster.
- Practical insight: The analysis and simulations illuminate how rj controls overdispersion and, thereby, the effective number and sharing of topics.

Weaknesses
- Practical advantage vs HDP-LDA is not unequivocal. When η is very small, HDP-LDA can achieve lower perplexity (with many topics), and the paper’s argument for BNBP’s multiplicative shrinkage as a practical advantage could use stronger, more intuitive justification and broader empirical support.
- Evaluation scope: Comparisons are restricted to HDP-LDA. Including other BNBP/GNBP/Poisson factor baselines or recent scalable HDP variants would better position the contribution.
- Clarity and accessibility: Some notation and derivations (ECPF → EPPF, digamma distribution, dependence on group sizes) are dense. Additional intuition, small running examples, or diagrams would help non-experts.
- Complexity and scalability: Although the sampler is fully collapsed and fast in Matlab for moderate K, it would help to quantify per-iteration cost relative to HDP-LDA across a range of topic counts and discuss memory/sparsity optimizations and parallelization.
- Sensitivity analyses: Results would benefit from a systematic study of sensitivity to γ0, c, and priors on rj, and an ablation showing the impact of inferring rj vs fixing it.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and carefully executed, with appropriate references and proofs indicated. The empirical methodology (held-out perplexity, multiple η settings, multiple splits) follows common practice, though additional baselines and runtime/token metrics would strengthen the conclusions. Claims about faster convergence and compactness are well illustrated; claims about predictive superiority should be framed more conditionally.

Clarity
Overall well written but mathematically dense. The paper would benefit from:
- A concise high-level roadmap for the ECPF→EPPF derivation and a small worked example of the prediction rule.
- An intuitive comparison of multiplicative vs additive shrinkage (perhaps via a variance–mean analysis of topic-usage counts).
- Streamlined notation and a brief table of symbols.

Originality
The EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting are novel, as is the fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process. The approach of randomizing group sizes to obtain tractable marginal laws is innovative and likely applicable beyond this specific model.

Significance
The theoretical contribution closes a gap between integer-valued processes and EPPF-based inference for grouped data, offering a unified view that can inspire new models and algorithms. Practically, the topic model is competitive and often more compact, which may matter for interpretability and storage. Adoption will hinge on clarity, scalability, and broader comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an intuitive explanation (and perhaps a small synthetic experiment) illustrating when and why multiplicative shrinkage yields better predictive–complexity trade-offs than additive shrinkage.
- Include additional baselines (e.g., Poisson factor analysis, gamma–NBP, truncated/block BNBP) and report runtime per token and memory use vs K.
- Add a sensitivity analysis for γ0, c, and priors on rj, and an ablation that fixes vs infers rj.
- Clarify the scope of the group-size dependence of the EPPF and discuss generalization to other integer-valued processes.
- Improve presentation: a symbol table, a schematic of the generative process, and a short example walk-through of the collapsed updates.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (EPPF, prediction rule) with solid connections to BNP literature.
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free sampler with good empirical mixing and compact representations.
- Clear potential for broader impact on mixed-membership modeling beyond topic models.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical advantage over HDP-LDA not uniformly demonstrated; empirical comparisons could be broader.
- Some clarity issues may hinder accessibility to a general NIPS audience.
- Limited discussion of scalability and sensitivity.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers an important theoretical advance with a practical algorithm and encouraging results. With improved exposition and a slightly stronger empirical section, it would make a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
