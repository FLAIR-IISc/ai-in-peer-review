This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) topic model. The primary contribution is an elegant sampling algorithm derived from a novel theoretical result, presented as Lemma 1.

**Strengths**

The theoretical contribution of this work is commendable. Lemma 1 is novel, interesting, and provides the necessary foundation for an elegant collapsed Gibbs sampling scheme for the BNBP. The derivation of the sampler appears sound and represents a significant technical step forward for inference in BNBP models. Developing a fully collapsed sampler is a non-trivial accomplishment that could be of great benefit to practitioners using this model.

**Weaknesses**

My primary concern lies with the experimental validation, which in its current form, does not adequately support the paper's claims. The authors' stated motivation is to construct a CG sampler for the BNBP that offers advantages such as "simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance." This framing establishes the work as a contribution to sampling methodology, not statistical modeling.

Consequently, the experiments should be designed to isolate and demonstrate these specific algorithmic advantages. However, the current experiments compare the proposed sampler for the BNBP against an implementation of HDP-LDA. This comparison is problematic because it conflates the performance of the inference algorithm with the performance of the statistical model itself. Any observed differences in predictive performance could be attributed to the inherent modeling assumptions of BNBP versus HDP-LDA, rather than the efficiency or effectiveness of the proposed sampler versus other samplers.

To substantiate the claims about the sampler's superiority, a more direct and appropriate experimental comparison is required. The authors should compare their proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler against other existing inference algorithms for the *same underlying BNBP model*. Potential baselines could include:

1.  An uncollapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP.
2.  Variational inference methods for the BNBP, if available.
3.  Other MCMC techniques that have been previously applied to this model.

Such a direct comparison would allow for a clear evaluation of convergence speed (e.g., by tracking perplexity or held-out likelihood over iterations/time), mixing properties, and overall computational efficiency. This would provide the necessary evidence to support the central claims of the paper. Without this baseline, it is impossible to conclude that the proposed sampler is indeed faster, mixes better, or is otherwise superior to alternative inference strategies for this model.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical result that leads to a new collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP. However, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the claims regarding the sampler's performance advantages. The comparison to HDP-LDA does not provide a meaningful benchmark for an algorithmic contribution. A major revision is needed to include experimental results that directly compare the proposed method against other inference techniques for the BNBP model.
