

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for mixed-membership analysis of grouped data, centered on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While the BNBP has been explored in prior work, this contribution distinguishes itself through two key theoretical advances. First, the authors derive an Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP and establish explicit prediction rules. This connection bridges a significant gap: although EPPF analysis has been foundational in Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) statistics since Pitman’s work, its practical integration into BNP model design remains underdeveloped. By formalizing this link, the paper provides a crucial step toward unifying theoretical EPPF frameworks with applied BNP research—a direction with substantial potential for future work. Second, the model innovatively treats group sizes as random variables rather than fixed constants, enabling the derivation of tractable marginal distributions. This approach elegantly resolves a common limitation in existing grouped-data models and enhances the model’s flexibility.

The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed. The derivation of the EPPF and associated prediction rules is rigorous, and the randomization of group sizes offers a principled solution to a persistent modeling challenge. These advances represent meaningful progress in the theoretical landscape of BNP methods for mixed-membership problems.

However, the paper’s practical utility requires stronger justification. The proposed inference algorithm is notably more complex than established methods like the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), both in theoretical formulation and computational implementation. Crucially, the empirical results demonstrate only marginal improvements over HDP in the reported experiments. The authors do not provide a clear, intuitive explanation for why the BNBP framework should outperform HDP in real-world scenarios—beyond the stated theoretical advantages. Without evidence of tangible gains in predictive performance, scalability, or robustness, the increased complexity is difficult to justify. For instance, the paper does not address whether the model’s benefits manifest in larger-scale datasets, different data modalities, or scenarios where group size variability is a critical factor. A more detailed comparison of computational overhead (e.g., runtime, memory) versus performance gains would be essential to support the practical relevance claim.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by explicitly addressing *why* the BNBP’s theoretical properties translate to practical advantages. The authors should clarify: (1) under what specific conditions the BNBP outperforms HDP (beyond the limited experiments shown); (2) how the random group size mechanism directly improves performance in applications; and (3) whether the marginal distributions derived offer concrete benefits for inference efficiency or interpretability. Without these clarifications, the practical value of the model remains speculative.

That said, the theoretical novelty is compelling. The EPPF derivation alone constitutes a valuable contribution to the BNP literature, offering a new lens for analyzing grouped-data models. The paper successfully demonstrates how theoretical insights can inform model design—a direction that deserves more attention in the field. While the practical case is not yet fully convincing, the theoretical groundwork is robust and potentially influential.

In summary, this work makes an important theoretical contribution by formalizing the BNBP’s EPPF and leveraging random group sizes to enable new analytical insights. The practical implications, however, require clearer justification. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to strengthen the discussion of practical utility, including a more explicit rationale for the BNBP’s advantages over HDP in applied settings. The authors should provide a concise, evidence-based argument explaining when and why their method would be preferable to existing approaches. If these points are addressed, the paper will be a strong addition to the literature.
