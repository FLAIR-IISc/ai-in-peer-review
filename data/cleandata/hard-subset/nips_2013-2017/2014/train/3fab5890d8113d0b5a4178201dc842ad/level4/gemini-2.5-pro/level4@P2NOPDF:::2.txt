This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). The core of this contribution is Lemma 1, which provides the necessary conditional distributions to integrate out the random measures, thereby enabling the collapsed sampling scheme.

The theoretical contribution of this work is novel and interesting. The derivation of Lemma 1 and the subsequent sampler appears sound and represents a valuable addition to the inference toolkit for BNBP models. Developing a collapsed sampler is a well-motivated goal, as such algorithms can offer significant advantages in terms of convergence speed and mixing properties over uncollapsed alternatives.

My primary concern, however, lies with the experimental validation of the proposed method. The paper's central motivation is to introduce a new sampling algorithm that is claimed to have benefits such as simpler implementation, faster convergence, better mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance. This framing establishes the contribution as primarily algorithmic rather than a new statistical model.

Given this focus, the experimental evaluation should be designed to substantiate these algorithmic claims. A convincing evaluation would compare the proposed CG sampler against other existing inference methods for the *same* underlying model—the BNBP. Such baselines could include uncollapsed Gibbs samplers, slice samplers, or variational inference approaches developed for the BNBP. This direct comparison would allow for a clear assessment of the proposed sampler's relative performance in terms of convergence rate (e.g., wall-clock time to reach a certain perplexity), mixing efficiency (e.g., analyzing trace plots or autocorrelation), and final predictive accuracy.

The current experiments, which compare the BNBP (using the proposed sampler) against the HDP-LDA, do not provide this crucial evidence. This comparison conflates the performance of the model (BNBP vs. HDP-LDA) with the performance of the inference algorithm. While the results may show that the BNBP is a competitive model for the chosen tasks, they fail to demonstrate that the proposed CG sampler is superior to, or even competitive with, other ways of performing inference within the BNBP framework.

In summary, while the paper provides a strong theoretical contribution with a novel collapsed sampler for the BNBP, the empirical results are insufficient to support the claims made about the sampler's practical advantages. To strengthen the paper, the authors must include experimental comparisons against other inference algorithms for the BNBP. Without this evidence, the central claims about the sampler's superior performance remain unsubstantiated.
