

This paper presents a novel derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling. The authors develop a rigorous framework for deriving the BNBP's EPPF by first constructing a group-size dependent model, then applying Bayes' rule to obtain the EPPF that governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. This approach fills a significant gap in the literature, as prior work on integer-valued stochastic processes (including the BNBP) lacked a marginal distribution describing exchangeable random partitions across groups. The authors further demonstrate the utility of their EPPF by constructing a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out both the topic weights and the infinite-dimensional beta process.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling, which is a substantial theoretical advance over existing work. The authors generalize techniques from [12, 17] to develop a method that could potentially be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes for mixed-membership modeling. Second, they derive a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions governed by the BNBP's EPPF. Third, they construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that delivers state-of-the-art predictive performance with a compact representation of the corpus.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The derivation of the EPPF is mathematically rigorous, with detailed proofs provided in the appendix. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of previous approaches (e.g., the need for truncation in BNBP inference [10, 11]), and the authors demonstrate how their method overcomes these limitations. The comparison with HDP-LDA in Section 3.1 is particularly insightful, highlighting how the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism for topic shrinkage differs fundamentally from HDP-LDA's additive mechanism. The experimental results in Section 4 provide compelling evidence of the BNBP topic model's advantages: the sampler converges significantly faster (in under 100 iterations versus over 300 for HDP-LDA) and mixes better, as demonstrated through trace plots. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of the trade-offs between topic count and perplexity, showing that the BNBP model achieves comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA with fewer topics.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some technical details could be presented more accessibly. The derivation of the EPPF is mathematically dense, and while the appendix provides necessary proofs, a more intuitive explanation of the key insights might help readers grasp the significance of the result. The presentation of the prediction rule (equation 10) and its interpretation is particularly clear, making the theoretical contribution more accessible. The experimental methodology is well-described, with appropriate attention to hyperparameter selection and evaluation metrics.

The paper demonstrates strong originality. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF represents a novel contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling, addressing a gap that has persisted since the development of EPPFs for single-set mixture modeling [5]. The authors explicitly acknowledge related work (e.g., [16, 17] on the negative binomial IBP), but clearly differentiate their approach by focusing on the mixed-membership setting with observed group sizes rather than random group sizes. The paper's method for deriving EPPFs could potentially be extended to other integer-valued stochastic processes, opening up new research directions.

The significance of the work is substantial. The BNBP topic model provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing nonparametric topic models that offers practical advantages in terms of convergence speed, mixing properties, and model compactness. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the BNBP model achieves comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA while using fewer topics, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. The method for deriving EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling could prove useful for other researchers working with integer-valued stochastic processes, potentially leading to new models for a range of applications beyond topic modeling.

The paper could be strengthened by addressing a few points. First, while the comparison with HDP-LDA is valuable, the authors could more thoroughly contrast their method with the sliced sampler of [10] and the truncated sampler of [8] for BNBP inference, as this would better establish the advantages of their collapsed approach. Second, the discussion of parallelization potential (mentioned in Section 4) could be expanded to include concrete suggestions for how the collapsed sampler might be parallelized, given that uncollapsed representations often have natural advantages for parallelization. Finally, while the authors note that the BNBP's dispersion parameters play a critical role in controlling overdispersion, they could provide more guidance on how to infer these parameters in practice.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and well-executed, and the resulting topic model demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper addresses all four key criteria for evaluation: it is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and compelling experimental results (quality); it is clearly written with sufficient detail for reproduction (clarity); it presents a genuine advance beyond prior work on EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling (originality); and it offers a useful new tool for practitioners with demonstrated advantages in efficiency and predictive performance (significance). The paper meets the high standards of NIPS and should be accepted.
