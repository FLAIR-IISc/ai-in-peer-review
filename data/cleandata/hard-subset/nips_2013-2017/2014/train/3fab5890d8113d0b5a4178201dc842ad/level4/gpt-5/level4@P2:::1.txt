Summary
This paper develops a principled connection between the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) and exchangeable random partitions for mixed-membership modeling. The main theoretical contribution is a group-size–dependent exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, obtained by first randomizing group sizes to derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), then using Bayes’ rule to obtain the EPPF for fixed group sizes. The authors further derive a simple prediction rule that enables Gibbs sampling without truncation. Building on these results, they propose a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based topic model that analytically marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the infinite beta process. Empirically, they compare against HDP-LDA on three corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12), reporting faster convergence/mixing and competitive or lower perplexity at comparable topic counts, with a more compact representation.

Relation to prior work
- The work situates itself within Bayesian nonparametrics beyond the Dirichlet process and NRMIs, and leverages Pitman’s EPPF framework. For mixed-membership modeling across groups, HDP’s Chinese restaurant franchise has been the dominant marginal approach; in contrast, previous BNBP inference required truncation or slice-based samplers and did not have an explicit marginal partition law for grouped data.
- The paper connects to recent count-based constructions (e.g., gamma–Poisson, IBP variants) but focuses on finite observed group sizes and constructs a sample-size–dependent EPPF for mixed membership—something not covered by existing σ-stable Poisson–Kingman or IBP-based results.

Strengths
- Originality: Provides the first EPPF (and associated prediction rule) for BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, using a clean ECPF→EPPF derivation. This is a notable conceptual advance that unifies EPPF analysis with integer-valued processes.
- Technical quality: Derivations appear careful and complete; the count-matrix representation with digamma and Dirichlet–multinomial components is elegant and yields a truncation-free, fully collapsed sampler.
- Significance: The method enables practical inference for BNBP mixed-membership models and could generalize to other integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP), potentially broadening the toolbox for grouped count data.
- Empirical observations: The collapsed sampler mixes quickly and, at similar topic counts, often matches or improves HDP-LDA perplexity while using fewer topics.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical utility vs complexity: Although the sampler is “fully collapsed,” the framework and update rules are more intricate than HDP’s familiar CRF-based samplers. The paper would benefit from:
  - Wall-clock comparisons to reach a target perplexity (not just per-iteration times), and memory usage.
  - A clearer discussion of when the multiplicative shrinkage (n·k/(c+n·k+r·) × (n_jk + r_j)) is expected to yield practical gains over HDP’s additive mechanism.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: The main comparison is to HDP-LDA (direct assignment, partially collapsed). It would strengthen the case to include:
  - Collapsed CRF-based HDP samplers (where feasible), Pitman–Yor HDP topic models, and count-based baselines (e.g., gamma–Poisson/PFA, GNBP).
  - A comparison to previous truncated/blocked BNBP/GNBP samplers to quantify the benefit of full collapse.
- Sensitivity and interpretability:
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for c, γ0, and hyperpriors on r_j; show effects on the expected number of topics and perplexity.
  - Include an ablation on learning r_j vs fixing r_j to illustrate its impact (the paper argues r_j is crucial).
  - Offer more intuition for the sample-size–dependent EPPF: practical implications, advantages/limitations, and how users should set/interpret c and γ0.
- Clarity:
  - Streamline notation (e.g., define ψ on first use, avoid switching symbols), and add a brief intuitive explanation of the digamma distribution’s role.
  - Provide a small worked example illustrating the prediction rule and how multiplicative shrinkage operates across documents.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: Strong theoretical development with complete derivations and a workable sampler; empirical evidence is positive but could be broader and more conclusive with additional baselines and runtime-to-accuracy metrics.
- Clarity: Generally well written but dense; more intuition, notation cleanup, and guidance for practitioners would help.
- Originality: High; the ECPF→EPPF construction for BNBP in mixed membership and the fully collapsed sampler are novel.
- Significance: Promising. The approach can influence future work on count-based nonparametrics and practical modeling of grouped data; immediate impact in topic modeling appears moderate but meaningful.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and rigorous EPPF for BNBP in mixed-membership modeling.
- Truncation-free, fully collapsed sampler with good mixing.
- Clear conceptual advance linking EPPFs and integer-valued processes.
- Competitive predictive performance with more compact topic representations.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical advantages over HDP remain somewhat incremental in reported experiments.
- Limited baseline coverage; fairness of comparison could be discussed more deeply.
- Heavier theoretical apparatus may limit immediate adoption without additional guidance.

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial and of clear interest to the NIPS community. With a stronger empirical section (runtime-to-accuracy, sensitivity, and broader baselines) and more intuition for practitioners, this work could have significant impact.
