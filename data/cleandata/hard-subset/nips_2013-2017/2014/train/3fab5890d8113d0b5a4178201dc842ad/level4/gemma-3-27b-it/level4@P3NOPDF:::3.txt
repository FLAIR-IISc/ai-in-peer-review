## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper presents a derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta negative binomial process (BNBP) and demonstrates its application through a collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model. 

The paper is well-written and technically sound, presenting a reasonably clear exposition of a challenging topic. The core contribution – the derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF within the context of mixed-membership modeling, achieved through a “count-mixture” framework – is both theoretically interesting and practically valuable. This result provides a potentially useful toolkit for deriving EPPFs for other related models, expanding the methodological options available to researchers.

The authors effectively demonstrate the utility of the derived EPPF and associated prediction rule through its application to the BNBP topic model. However, the empirical evaluation reveals only marginal improvements compared to the established hierarchical Dirichlet process latent Dirichlet allocation (HDP-LDA) model. The authors accurately acknowledge that the primary finding is the distinct characteristics of the BNBP topic model and HDP-LDA, rather than a clear performance advantage for the BNBP approach.

To further strengthen the paper’s contribution, I suggest a more targeted comparison of the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler against alternative inference methods specifically designed for the BNBP topic model. In particular, contrasting performance against the sliced sampler [10] and the truncated sampler [8] would be highly informative.  These alternative samplers leverage uncollapsed representations, which offer inherent advantages in terms of parallelization. An investigation of the mixing properties and computational tradeoffs between these uncollapsed samplers and the proposed collapsed sampler would significantly enhance the paper's impact. Specifically, understanding the performance characteristics of the collapsed sampler in relation to these alternatives is crucial for determining its practical utility.

The experimental section is generally well-executed, but the discussion could benefit from a more detailed analysis of *why* the observed differences between BNBP and HDP-LDA emerge.  Exploring the characteristics of the topics learned by each model, for example, could offer valuable insights.

Overall, this is an interesting and valuable contribution that clearly exceeds the publication threshold. The derivation of the EPPF is a significant step forward, and the application to the BNBP topic model provides a concrete illustration of its utility. Addressing the suggestions regarding a more comprehensive comparison to alternative BNBP inference methods would further solidify the paper’s impact.
