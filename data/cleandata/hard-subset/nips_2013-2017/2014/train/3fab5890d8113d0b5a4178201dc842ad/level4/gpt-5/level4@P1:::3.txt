Summary
The paper derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, using a “count-mixture” construction to handle grouped data. It further develops a prediction rule and a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler based on this EPPF, and applies the methodology to topic modeling. The work relates to prior nonparametric Bayesian models for mixture and mixed-membership modeling, including the Dirichlet process, NRMIs, and the HDP (with the Chinese restaurant franchise), as well as recent BNBP and gamma–negative binomial process formulations whose marginal partition laws were previously unavailable. The resulting BNBP topic model is positioned as distinct from HDP-LDA, with collapsed inference that is simple to implement and shows good convergence and mixing.

Strengths
- Clear and technically solid. The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for grouped data is well motivated and carefully executed.
- Conceptual contribution. The route to an EPPF via count-mixture modeling is interesting in its own right and may provide tools for deriving EPPFs for related integer-valued processes.
- Inference contribution. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is straightforward, truncation-free, and exhibits fast convergence and good mixing in the reported experiments.
- Empirical characterization. The comparison to HDP-LDA highlights distinct shrinkage mechanisms (multiplicative for BNBP versus additive for HDP) and shows that BNBP can achieve comparable predictive performance with a more compact representation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical gains over HDP-LDA are only marginal. The most defensible conclusion from the experiments is that BNBP and HDP-LDA have different inductive biases and shrinkage behaviors, rather than a clear superiority of one over the other.
- Missing comparisons to alternative BNBP inference schemes. A more informative study would contrast the collapsed sampler with the slice sampler of [10] and the truncated sampler of [8] for inference within the same BNBP topic model. Such comparisons would clarify mixing behavior and runtime trade-offs.
- Parallelization. Uncollapsed (slice/truncated) representations naturally facilitate parallel implementations. It would be useful to explore the parallelization/mixing trade-offs relative to the collapsed sampler presented here.
- Only minor comments on the experimental section beyond the above: the current experiments reasonably illustrate the method, but a more comprehensive comparison to alternative BNBP inference strategies would strengthen the empirical claims.

Quality
Technically sound, with the main derivations and samplers well supported. The methodology is internally consistent and carefully described.

Clarity
Well written and reasonably clear. The organization is effective, and the key distinctions from HDP-LDA are articulated.

Originality
The EPPF for BNBP in the mixed-membership setting, derived via count-mixture modeling, is novel and useful. The collapsed inference scheme that marginalizes the beta process alongside topics and weights is also a meaningful contribution.

Significance
The contributions are valuable for the development of nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership models. Even if empirical gains over HDP-LDA are modest, the derivation and inference tools expand the methodological toolkit and should be useful to researchers working with count-based processes.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-executed paper that meets the publication bar. While the application to topic modeling shows only marginal improvements over HDP-LDA, the theoretical and algorithmic contributions—deriving the BNBP EPPF and a fully collapsed sampler—are worthwhile and likely to influence future work. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that broader empirical comparisons (e.g., to slice and truncation-based BNBP samplers) would further strengthen the paper.
