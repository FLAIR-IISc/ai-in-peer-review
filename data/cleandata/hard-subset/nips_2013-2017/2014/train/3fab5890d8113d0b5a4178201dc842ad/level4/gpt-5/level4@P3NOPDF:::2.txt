This paper proposes a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabled by a key marginalization result (Lemma 1). The derivation is elegant, and both the lemma and the resulting sampler appear novel and potentially impactful. The approach promises simple implementation, faster convergence, improved mixing, and competitive predictive performance, and the manuscript is generally well written.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: Lemma 1 is interesting and, as presented, seems correct and broadly useful for BNBP-based models. The fully collapsed construction is clean and could simplify practical inference.
- Potential impact: A robust collapsed sampler for the BNBP would be valuable, as BNBP models are increasingly used for count data and topic modeling. If the claimed gains in mixing and runtime hold, this could become a default inference method for such models.
- Clarity of derivation: The sampler is derived clearly, and the intuition around marginalization is helpful.

Primary concern: experimental evaluation and baselines.
- The motivation centers on an improved sampling algorithm, not a new statistical model. To substantiate claims about simplicity, convergence speed, mixing, and predictive quality attributable to the sampler, the experiments must compare the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler against other inference schemes for the same BNBP model.
- The current comparison to HDP-LDA is not sufficient. HDP-LDA is a different model family; differences in predictive performance may reflect model differences rather than the quality of the sampler. Such a comparison can be informative as a secondary result, but it does not isolate the effect of the proposed inference method.

What is needed to support the main claims:
- Baselines within BNBP: At minimum, include (i) an uncollapsed/standard Gibbs sampler for BNBP, and (ii) a partially collapsed or blocked Gibbs variant. If available in the literature, also consider slice sampling or other MCMC implementations for BNBP, and, where feasible, a variational baseline. If prior BNBP samplers are not available as code, a straightforward uncollapsed implementation provides a meaningful baseline.
- Metrics that reflect sampler quality: Report wall-clock time to reach a target held-out likelihood or perplexity, effective sample size per second (or integrated autocorrelation time) for key latent variables and hyperparameters, and mixing diagnostics (e.g., R-hat across multiple chains). Include trace plots or summaries to demonstrate convergence behavior.
- Fair, controlled comparisons: Hold the model, data preprocessing, hyperparameters, initialization strategy, and computational budget constant across samplers. Report the number of iterations, burn-in, thinning, dataset sizes, and computing hardware. Use the same train/validation/test splits and the same held-out evaluation method across all samplers.
- Ablations: Quantify the contribution of full collapsing by comparing against a semi-collapsed version and against the uncollapsed baseline. This would directly validate the benefit of the proposed marginalizations.
- Scalability and robustness: Show results across small to large corpora, including sensitivity to hyperparameters and to rare-word frequency. Provide per-iteration computational and memory complexity, and empirical scaling with vocabulary size and token count.

Additional comments and suggestions:
- If claiming “state-of-the-art predictive performance,” the evidence should include comparisons to the best-performing inference methods for the BNBP model class, not only to different models (e.g., HDP-LDA). It is fine to keep HDP-LDA as a reference point, but the core claims should be grounded in within-model comparisons.
- Please make the assumptions of Lemma 1 explicit, including any conjugacy or exchangeability conditions, and state clearly the range of BNBP-based likelihoods for which the result applies. If non-conjugate extensions are possible, clarify how the sampler would adapt (e.g., via auxiliary variables or Metropolis steps).
- Provide pseudocode for the full algorithm and complexity analysis to aid reproducibility. If possible, release code and scripts used to produce the figures and tables.
- Minor clarity points: ensure notation is consistent across the main text and appendix; define all symbols upon first use; specify how predictive likelihood is estimated (e.g., document completion vs. left-to-right perplexity) to avoid evaluation biases.

Overall evaluation:
- Technical novelty: strong.
- Potential impact: high, contingent on stronger empirical validation.
- Experimental support: currently insufficient to isolate and substantiate the claimed advantages of the sampler.

Recommendation: I am positive about the core idea and the quality of the derivation, but I view the paper as incomplete without direct, controlled comparisons to alternative BNBP samplers and without sampler-focused diagnostics. I would lean toward acceptance if the authors add within-BNBP baselines, provide mixing and efficiency metrics, and include ablations demonstrating the benefits of full collapsing; otherwise, this remains borderline due to the experimental gap.
