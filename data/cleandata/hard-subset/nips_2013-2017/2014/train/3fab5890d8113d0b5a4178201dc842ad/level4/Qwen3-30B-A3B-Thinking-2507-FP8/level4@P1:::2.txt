

This paper proposes a novel exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) and develops a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership modeling. The authors derive the EPPF by first randomizing group sizes and then using Bayes' rule to arrive at the marginal distribution, which enables a fully collapsed inference procedure. This leads to a nonparametric Bayesian topic model that is claimed to have simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing properties, and state-of-the-art predictive performance.

The paper makes several valuable contributions. First, it provides a rigorous derivation of the EPPF for BNBP, which fills an important gap in the literature. Previous work on mixed-membership modeling (such as the hierarchical Dirichlet process) lacks a unified treatment for integer-valued stochastic processes like BNBP. Second, the authors derive a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions governed by the BNBP's EPPF. Third, they construct a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out not only topics and topic weights but also the infinite-dimensional beta process, resulting in closed-form update equations.

The technical contributions are sound and well-motivated. Lemma 1, which establishes the equivalence between the BNBP and the augmented group-size dependent model, is particularly important and well-executed. The derivation of the EPPF (Lemma 3) and prediction rule (Lemma 4) are novel and address a significant gap in the literature on mixed-membership modeling. The paper also provides a thorough comparison with existing topic models, particularly HDP-LDA, highlighting how the BNBP model's multiplicative mechanism for topic shrinkage differs from HDP-LDA's additive approach.

However, I have concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. The paper's primary contribution is the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP, which is motivated by its claimed advantages: simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance. Consequently, the evaluation should focus on comparing the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler with other sampling algorithms specifically designed for BNBP, rather than comparing it with HDP-LDA, which is a fundamentally different model.

The current experimental section compares the BNBP topic model with HDP-LDA, but does not include comparisons with other BNBP sampling methods. For instance, the paper references [10, 11] which use finite truncation and slice sampling for BNBP inference, but these approaches are not evaluated against the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed advantages of the proposed sampler (fast convergence, good mixing) are truly attributable to the new method rather than other factors.

The paper states that "the straightforward to implement BNBP topic model sampling algorithm converges fast, mixes well, and produces state-of-the-art predictive performance with a compact representation of the corpus," but the experimental results do not provide sufficient evidence to support these claims. The results show that the BNBP model achieves comparable perplexity to HDP-LDA when using a moderate number of topics, but this doesn't demonstrate that the collapsed Gibbs sampler specifically converges faster or mixes better than alternative BNBP samplers.

I recommend adding experiments that compare the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler with other BNBP sampling approaches (e.g., finite truncation as in [10], slice sampling as in [11], or blocked Gibbs sampling as in [7]). This would allow the authors to properly evaluate their key claim about the sampler's convergence speed and mixing properties. Without such comparisons, the experimental evaluation does not fully support the paper's main technical contribution.

The paper is well-written and makes an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of BNBP for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the EPPF is novel and fills a significant gap in the literature. The BNBP topic model is also well-motivated and provides an alternative to existing nonparametric topic models. However, the experimental evaluation should be strengthened by comparing with other BNBP sampling algorithms to properly validate the claimed advantages of the proposed sampler.

In summary, the paper has strong theoretical contributions but lacks a proper evaluation of the claimed advantages of the proposed sampling algorithm. The authors should compare their fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with other BNBP sampling methods to validate their claims about convergence speed, mixing properties, and predictive performance. With this additional evaluation, the paper would make a more compelling contribution to the field.
