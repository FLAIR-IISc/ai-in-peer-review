

This paper introduces the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta negative binomial process (BNBP) and leverages it to derive a collapsed Gibbs sampling procedure for BNBP-based topic models. The work is well-written, technically solid, and presents a novel derivation of the BNBP's EPPF through count-mixture modeling—a contribution that is both theoretically interesting and provides a reusable framework for deriving EPPFs in related processes.  

The primary technical contribution is the EPPF derivation, which effectively enables mixed-membership modeling under the BNBP. This advance is valuable for extending the class of exchangeable nonparametric priors and offers a systematic approach for future work. The BNBP topic model serves as a reasonable application, though the empirical comparison to HDP-LDA reveals only marginal performance gains. The only robust conclusion supported by the results is that the BNBP topic model and HDP-LDA exhibit distinct modeling properties, rather than one clearly outperforming the other.  

To strengthen the paper, a more compelling experimental comparison would directly evaluate the proposed collapsed sampler against alternative inference methods for BNBP, such as the sliced sampler (reference [10]) and truncated sampler (reference [8]). The uncollapsed variants of BNBP inference offer inherent advantages for parallelization, and a thorough analysis of mixing efficiency and scalability tradeoffs between these approaches would significantly enhance the paper's impact. Such a comparison would clarify whether the collapsed sampler's computational benefits justify its marginal empirical gains relative to established alternatives.  

Minor suggestions for the experimental section include: (1) explicitly reporting runtime comparisons across samplers to contextualize the marginal performance differences; (2) clarifying the hyperparameter selection procedure for fair model comparison; and (3) adding a discussion of the BNBP's flexibility in handling varying document lengths, which could highlight its practical advantages over HDP-LDA.  

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a meaningful theoretical contribution. While the empirical validation could be strengthened through the suggested comparisons, the work meets the standards for publication at NIPS. The derivation of the BNBP EPPF is sufficiently novel and useful to warrant attention from the community, and the methodological insights are broadly applicable to other exchangeable processes. The paper is recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing the experimental analysis.
