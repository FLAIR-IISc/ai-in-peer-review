

This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling, addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous EPPF models were limited to single-set data rather than grouped data. The authors derive the BNBP's EPPF through a group-size dependent approach, which enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model. This work represents an important theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling, extending the framework established by [5, 12] to handle grouped data with random partition sizes.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous derivation of the EPPF and its corresponding prediction rule. The authors successfully establish the connection between the BNBP and exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, providing closed-form Gibbs sampling update equations that analytically marginalize out both the topics and the infinite-dimensional beta process. This fully collapsed inference approach offers clear advantages over existing methods in terms of implementation simplicity and convergence speed. The theoretical development is well-supported by detailed proofs in the appendix, demonstrating the authors' careful attention to mathematical rigor.

Regarding clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-organized and written. The authors provide clear motivation for their approach by carefully contrasting their work with previous methods like the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) [6] and the negative binomial Indian buffet process [16]. The presentation of the EPPF derivation is logical and accessible, with helpful intermediate lemmas and intuitive explanations of the sampling procedure. The experimental section is well-structured, with clear descriptions of the datasets, evaluation metrics, and implementation details.

The work exhibits significant originality in its theoretical contribution. While previous research has explored integer-valued stochastic processes for mixed-membership modeling [7, 10, 11], none have derived the marginal distribution governing exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. The authors' method of deriving the EPPF via a group-size dependent model represents a novel approach that could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes. The BNBP topic model they develop is distinct from existing nonparametric Bayesian topic models, particularly in its multiplicative shrinkage mechanism that differs from the additive mechanism of HDP-LDA [6].

In terms of significance, the paper advances the state of the art in nonparametric Bayesian modeling for mixed-membership problems. The BNBP topic model demonstrates state-of-the-art predictive performance with a compact representation of the corpus, which is particularly valuable for large-scale text analysis. The experimental results on three standard corpora (JACM, PsyReview, and NIPS12) provide compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness, with the BNBP topic model achieving comparable or better predictive performance than HDP-LDA while using fewer topics.

The main weakness of the paper lies in the experimental comparison. While the authors demonstrate that the BNBP topic model converges faster and mixes better than HDP-LDA, the performance improvements are marginal in terms of perplexity, particularly when the topic Dirichlet smoothing parameter η is set to moderate values. A more compelling comparison would have directly contrasted the collapsed sampler against the sliced sampler [10] and truncated sampler [8] for inference in the BNBP topic model, as these represent the primary alternatives for BNBP inference. The paper would benefit from a more thorough exploration of the tradeoffs between the collapsed sampler developed here and the uncollapsed representations that naturally support parallelization.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound and practically useful framework for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is particularly significant, as it fills an important theoretical gap that has previously hindered the application of BNBP to mixed-membership problems. The resulting topic model is both theoretically interesting and practically useful, offering a simpler implementation with faster convergence than existing methods.

The authors have made a clear and important contribution to nonparametric Bayesian statistics that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners working with mixed-membership models. While the experimental comparison with HDP-LDA could be strengthened, the paper's theoretical advances and practical implementation make it a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. The work represents a meaningful step forward in the development of nonparametric Bayesian methods for modeling grouped data with exchangeable partitions.
