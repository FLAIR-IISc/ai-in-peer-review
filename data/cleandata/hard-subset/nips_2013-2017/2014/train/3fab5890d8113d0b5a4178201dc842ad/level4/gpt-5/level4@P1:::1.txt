This paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data based on the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP). While prior work has used the BNBP, this paper makes two notable contributions. First, it derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP and the corresponding prediction rules. The relationship between Bayesian nonparametrics and EPPFs, rooted in Pitman’s work, has been studied extensively in statistics but is less exploited in practical BNP modeling. The connection established here between the BNBP and an EPPF strikes me as an important step toward unifying EPPF analysis and BNP modeling, and could stimulate further research along this direction. Second, the idea of randomizing group sizes—typically treated as fixed—enables the derivation of marginal distributions that were previously inaccessible. This is an interesting modeling device with clear analytical benefits.

Overall, I view the paper’s contributions as primarily theoretical. The authors provide new foundations for understanding and using the BNBP in mixed-membership settings. However, I am less convinced about the practical utility. The proposed inference algorithm appears more complex than that of the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP), both conceptually and computationally. The empirical improvements shown are not, in my view, strong enough to justify this added complexity in practice. Moreover, the paper does not provide an intuitive explanation or justification for why the proposed framework should outperform the HDP in real applications.

Strengths:
- Important theoretical advance: an EPPF for the BNBP with associated prediction rules.
- A clever and useful device—randomizing group sizes—to obtain marginal distributions.
- Potential to unify EPPF-based analysis with BNP modeling and spur further work.

Weaknesses:
- Practical value is unclear relative to the HDP, given the increased algorithmic complexity.
- Empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate that the additional complexity is warranted.
- Lack of an intuitive explanation for when and why the method should work better than the HDP.

Requests for clarification:
- Please explain, at a high level, why the BNBP’s EPPF and its prediction rule should yield better practical performance than the HDP in realistic settings.
- Clarify the computational trade-offs relative to standard HDP inference, both in theory and in wall-clock terms, and relate these to the observed empirical gains.

Conclusion: The paper has clear theoretical value, but the practical benefits remain uncertain. I would like to see a stronger, more intuitive justification for practical improvements over the HDP. Overall, I still lean toward acceptance.
