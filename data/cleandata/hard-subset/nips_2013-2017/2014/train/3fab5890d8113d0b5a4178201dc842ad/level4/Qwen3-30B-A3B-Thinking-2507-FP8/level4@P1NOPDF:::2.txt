

This paper proposes a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the Bayesian nonparametric nested Chinese restaurant process (BNBP). Lemma 1 and the derived sampling procedure are novel and well-motivated. However, the experimental evaluation requires significant revision.

The authors' motivation for developing this collapsed Gibbs sampler centers on its claimed advantages: simplicity of implementation, fast convergence, strong mixing properties, and state-of-the-art predictive performance. Crucially, this work focuses on algorithmic development rather than statistical modeling, meaning the primary contribution is the sampling method itself. Consequently, the evaluation must compare the proposed sampler against *other existing sampling algorithms specifically designed for the BNBP*, not against HDP-LDA.

The current experiments compare the proposed method to HDP-LDA, which is inappropriate. HDP-LDA is a distinct hierarchical topic model built upon the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP), not a sampler for the BNBP. Comparing a BNBP sampler to an HDP-LDA model (which uses a different inference approach, typically collapsed Gibbs for the HDP) does not assess the core claim about the BNBP sampler's performance relative to alternative BNBP inference techniques. This comparison fails to address the paper's central algorithmic contribution.

To validate the claimed advantages, the authors must provide experiments that directly compare the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler to other established sampling methods for the BNBP. This includes reporting results on standard BNBP benchmarks using metrics like predictive perplexity or effective sample size, alongside computational efficiency measures. Without this, the experimental section cannot support the paper's central claim about the sampler's superiority within the BNBP framework. The current evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the proposed method's benefits over existing BNBP-specific samplers, undermining the contribution's significance. The missing comparative results are essential for a fair assessment of the algorithmic contribution.
