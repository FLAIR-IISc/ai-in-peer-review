Summary
The paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, addressing a gap where existing BNBP-based models lacked a marginal partition law and thus relied on truncation or slice sampling. The authors proceed via an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that jointly models random group sizes and partitions, then condition on observed group sizes to obtain the EPPF and a simple prediction rule. Building on this theory, they derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (marginalizing topics, topic weights, and the infinite beta process) and instantiate a nonparametric topic model. Empirically, they report faster convergence, better mixing, and competitive predictive perplexity relative to HDP-LDA. This work is related to Dirichlet processes and the broader class of normalized random measures, prior EPPF developments (e.g., Pitman; sample-size dependent EPPFs), and BNBP/negative-binomial process literature using truncation or slice sampling.

Strengths
- Novelty: Lemma 1 and the subsequent ECPF/EPPF derivation for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling are new and interesting. The prediction rule is simple and intuitive.
- Algorithmic contribution: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP is presented with closed-form updates, conceptually straightforward implementation, and clear potential for improved mixing.
- Clarity of the modeling bridge: The augmented beta–gamma–Poisson representation cleanly connects count modeling and partition modeling, and the relationship to Poisson factorization is well articulated.
- Potential impact: The approach could generalize to other integer-valued processes and provides a useful nonparametric alternative to HDP-based topic modeling.

Primary Concern: Experimental Evaluation
The paper’s stated motivation emphasizes the sampling algorithm—simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and strong predictive performance—rather than proposing a new statistical model per se. Accordingly, the most relevant empirical evaluation should compare alternative inference schemes for the BNBP itself. The current experiments primarily compare the proposed BNBP sampler against HDP-LDA. While that comparison is informative, it does not directly establish the advantages of the new sampler over existing BNBP inference approaches (e.g., finite truncation/block Gibbs, slice sampling).

To substantiate the claims, the paper should include comparisons with other BNBP samplers and closely related negative-binomial process samplers from prior work, such as:
- Finite/truncated block Gibbs samplers for BNBP or related models (e.g., Zhou & Carin 2015; Zhou et al. 2012).
- Slice sampling approaches for BNBP (e.g., Broderick et al.; adaptive truncation).
- Related BNBP/IBP-style constructions (negative-binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet process).

The comparison should cover:
- Convergence speed and wall-clock time to reach a target perplexity.
- Mixing diagnostics (e.g., effective sample size per unit time), acceptance/creation rates for new clusters, and number of active topics over iterations.
- Scalability with corpus size and vocabulary size.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., η, c, γ0, priors on rj), as well as initialization.
- Memory usage and computational complexity per iteration.

Additional Comments by Evaluation Criterion
- Quality: Theoretical development appears technically sound, with proofs delegated to the appendix. However, the empirical evidence is not yet aligned with the paper’s core algorithmic claims because it omits baselines using other BNBP samplers.
- Clarity: The exposition of the ECPF/EPPF and the prediction rule is clear overall. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of KJ, explicit early definition of the “digamma distribution”), and a brief pseudo-code summary of the collapsed sampler would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The derivation of a group-size-dependent EPPF for the BNBP and a fully collapsed sampler is original and advances inference for mixed-membership models beyond truncation or slice methods.
- Significance: If the empirical advantages over existing BNBP samplers are demonstrated, the contribution would be significant for scalable nonparametric inference in topic modeling and other grouped count applications.

Pros
- New EPPF/ECPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with simple closed-form updates.
- Clear connection to Poisson factorization and practical topic modeling.
- Promising empirical behavior versus HDP-LDA.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to other BNBP inference algorithms; current experiments do not isolate the benefits of the proposed sampler over established BNBP samplers.
- Limited diagnostic analysis of convergence/mixing beyond qualitative trace plots.
- Sensitivity analyses (e.g., hyperparameters, priors on rj) could be more systematic.

Recommendations
- Add experimental comparisons to truncated/block Gibbs and slice sampling BNBP baselines, with comprehensive efficiency and mixing diagnostics.
- Report wall-clock time to reach comparable perplexity, effective sample sizes, and memory footprint.
- Include sensitivity analyses for η, c, γ0, and priors on rj, and discuss robustness.
- Provide pseudo-code and release code to facilitate replication.

Conclusion
The theoretical contribution and the collapsed sampler are novel and appealing. However, because the paper’s emphasis is on the sampling algorithm, a fair assessment requires direct comparison with other BNBP sampling strategies. Providing those experimental results would substantially strengthen the paper and align the evaluation with its core claims.
