This paper addresses a key gap in the literature on mixed-membership modeling with the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While the BNBP has been previously proposed for such tasks, inference has been limited by the lack of a marginal probability distribution governing the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data, necessitating the use of truncation or slice sampling. The main contribution of this work is the derivation of this distribution, which the authors term the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP. The derivation is novel, proceeding by first randomizing the group sizes to derive an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF) and then using Bayes' rule to obtain the desired group-size dependent EPPF.

Based on this theoretical result, the authors derive a simple predictive rule and develop a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a new nonparametric topic model based on the BNBP. They present experimental results on several text corpora, comparing their model's convergence, mixing, and predictive performance against the well-known Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) topic model.

**Strengths:**
*   The primary theoretical result of the paper—the derivation of the BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—is a significant and elegant contribution. It provides a deeper understanding of the clustering properties of the BNBP.
*   The method used to arrive at the EPPF is interesting in its own right and could potentially serve as a template for deriving similar partition probability functions for other integer-valued stochastic processes, such as the gamma-Poisson process.
*   The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model is a practical outcome of the theoretical work. The update equations are simple and intuitive, combining global and local topic popularities in a distinctive multiplicative manner, which contrasts with the additive nature of the HDP.
*   The paper is well-written, technically sound, and the arguments are presented clearly. The connection to prior work is well-established.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**
*   While the BNBP topic model is a reasonable application to illustrate the utility of the derived EPPF, the experimental comparison with HDP-LDA is not fully convincing. The results show that the BNBP model has faster convergence and better mixing, but the improvements in predictive perplexity are marginal. The main conclusion from the experiments seems to be that the BNBP and HDP models have distinct characteristics and shrinkage properties, rather than one being demonstrably superior.
*   A more direct and arguably more insightful experimental comparison would be against other existing inference schemes for the BNBP topic model itself, such as the truncated sampler used in [7, 10] or the slice sampler in [11]. This would directly quantify the practical benefits (e.g., in convergence speed, mixing, and accuracy) of the proposed fully collapsed sampler over uncollapsed or approximate methods for the exact same model.
*   Such a comparison would be particularly valuable given that uncollapsed representations often have advantages in terms of parallelization. A discussion of the trade-offs in this regard would strengthen the paper's claims about the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a novel result that advances our understanding of this class of models. While the experimental section could be strengthened by including comparisons to other BNBP inference methods, the paper's theoretical merits are clear. The work is interesting, technically sound, and makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics. I recommend acceptance.
