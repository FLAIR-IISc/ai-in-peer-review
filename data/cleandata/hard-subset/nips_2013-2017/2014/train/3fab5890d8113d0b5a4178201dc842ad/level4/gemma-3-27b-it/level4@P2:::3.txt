This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) and leverages it to construct a collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model. 

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and generally clear. Its primary contribution – the derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for mixed-membership modeling, achieved through a novel approach involving “count-mixture” models – is both inherently interesting and offers potentially valuable tools for deriving EPPFs for related models. This work builds on the established framework of exchangeable partition probability functions, extending it to a challenging setting beyond single datasets. The authors successfully address the difficulty of defining a marginal distribution for the BNBP, a key limitation of previous approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous and well-justified. The use of the EPPF to enable fully collapsed Gibbs sampling is a significant technical achievement.
*   **Novelty:** The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a novel contribution and fills a gap in the existing literature. The approach of deriving it through a group size dependent model is particularly clever.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand the key ideas and technical details.
*   **Potential Impact:** The derived EPPF provides a foundation for more efficient inference in BNBP-based models and could facilitate the development of new models based on other integer-valued stochastic processes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Marginal Improvements:** The empirical evaluation suggests that the BNBP topic model offers only marginal improvements over the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP)-LDA. While the authors acknowledge this, a stronger demonstration of benefit would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to other BNBP inference methods (e.g., slice sampling [10] or truncated sampling [8]) would be beneficial. Evaluating the collapsed sampler against these alternatives would provide a more comprehensive understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** While the authors briefly mention parallelization, a more detailed discussion of scalability and computational considerations, especially in comparison to uncollapsed methods, would be valuable. The trade-offs between collapsed and uncollapsed inference are important for practitioners.
*   **Experimental Depth:** The experimental section is relatively limited. While the comparisons to HDP-LDA are useful, exploring more diverse datasets and hyperparameter settings would increase confidence in the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution in deriving the BNBP’s EPPF.
*   The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler enables efficient inference, addressing a key limitation of previous BNBP implementations.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
*   The work contributes to the broader field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling and has the potential to inspire further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical improvements over existing methods (HDP-LDA) are modest.
*   The lack of a thorough comparison to other BNBP inference techniques.
*   The limited discussion of scalability and computational complexity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. While the empirical improvements are not dramatic, the theoretical contributions and the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler are significant. The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and presents its results in a clear and concise manner. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest expanding the experimental section to include comparisons to other BNBP inference techniques and providing a more detailed discussion of scalability and computational complexity.
