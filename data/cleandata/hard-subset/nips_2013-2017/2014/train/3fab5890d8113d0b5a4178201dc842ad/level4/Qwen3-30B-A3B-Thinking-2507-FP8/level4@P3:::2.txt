

This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) and develops a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership modeling. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: previous BNBP inference methods required truncating the number of atoms, while the proposed EPPF enables direct inference without truncation. The paper develops a novel nonparametric Bayesian topic model based on BNBP, demonstrating simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and competitive predictive performance.

The theoretical contribution is strong. The authors derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) and subsequently the EPPF for BNBP by first randomizing group sizes and then applying Bayes' rule. This approach generalizes the techniques from Zhou et al. (2014) and extends the work of [12] to mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the prediction rule (Lemma 4) is particularly valuable for implementing the model. The paper provides clear theoretical justification for the BNBP's unique properties, especially the multiplicative control mechanism for topic shrinkage compared to the additive mechanism in HDP-LDA (equation 14 vs. 12).

The topic modeling application is well-motivated as a special case of mixed-membership modeling. The authors clearly articulate how the BNBP's properties translate to a topic model with intuitive interpretation. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler (equation 12) is indeed simple to implement and avoids the need for tuning parameters like the number of topics or the topic proportion Dirichlet smoothing parameter α required in LDA.

However, I have significant concerns about the experimental evaluation. The paper's primary claim is that the BNBP topic model offers "simple implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance." To validate these claims, the authors should compare the proposed fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with other BNBP inference methods (e.g., the finite truncation approach in [10] or slice sampling in [11]), not with HDP-LDA. The HDP-LDA comparison is misleading because it compares two fundamentally different models rather than evaluating the proposed BNBP inference method against alternative inference approaches for BNBP. 

While Figure 3 shows that the BNBP model achieves comparable perplexity to HDP-LDA with fewer topics, this comparison doesn't address whether the proposed inference method is superior to existing BNBP inference methods. The paper should include experiments comparing the proposed fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with alternative BNBP inference techniques to demonstrate the claimed advantages in convergence speed and mixing. The current experimental setup makes it impossible to determine whether the improvements are due to the BNBP model itself or the proposed inference method.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by this experimental weakness. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the claimed "state-of-the-art predictive performance" requires more convincing evidence. The comparison with HDP-LDA is interesting from a modeling perspective but does not support the paper's primary claim about the inference algorithm. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a direct comparison of inference methods for the same BNBP model.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear mathematical derivations and well-structured exposition. The paper could be improved by providing more intuition about why the BNBP model is particularly well-suited for mixed-membership modeling compared to other integer-valued processes like the gamma-Poisson process. The connection to previous work on the negative binomial Indian buffet process [16] and "ice cream" buffet process [17] could also be more thoroughly discussed.

In terms of originality, the development of the EPPF for BNBP is novel. While the paper acknowledges related work on EPPFs for single-group data, the extension to mixed-membership modeling with exchangeable partitions across groups is a significant contribution. The derivation of the EPPF via a group-size-dependent model is an important technical advance that could be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian inference for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is sound and fills an important gap. However, the experimental evaluation undermines the claims about the algorithm's advantages. To strengthen the paper, the authors should replace the HDP-LDA comparison with a comparison against other BNBP inference methods, particularly the finite truncation approach in [10] and slice sampling in [11].

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical contribution: derivation of BNBP's EPPF for mixed-membership modeling
- Clear and well-structured mathematical development
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with simple implementation
- Good theoretical justification for the model's properties

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation miscompares with HDP-LDA instead of other BNBP inference methods
- Cannot verify claims about convergence speed and predictive performance without proper comparison
- Limited discussion of why BNBP is particularly well-suited for mixed-membership modeling

I recommend rejection due to the fundamental flaw in the experimental evaluation. The paper's core claim about the inference algorithm's advantages cannot be validated with the current experimental setup. To merit acceptance, the authors would need to conduct a fair comparison of the proposed inference method against alternative BNBP inference approaches.
