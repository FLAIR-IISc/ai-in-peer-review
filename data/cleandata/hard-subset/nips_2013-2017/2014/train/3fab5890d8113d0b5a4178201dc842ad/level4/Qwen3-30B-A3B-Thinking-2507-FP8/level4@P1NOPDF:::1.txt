

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data, grounded in the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). While previous work has utilized this stochastic process, the authors make several distinct contributions.  

First, they derive an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP and establish corresponding prediction rules. The connection between Bayesian nonparametric methods and EPPF has been extensively studied since Pitman’s foundational work, yet this relationship remains underutilized in practical BNP research. By formalizing this link, the authors advance the unification of EPPF analysis and BNP methodology—a significant theoretical step that could inspire future work in this direction.  

Second, the authors propose randomizing group sizes (a departure from the typical treatment of group sizes as fixed constants in existing models). This innovation enables the derivation of key marginal distributions, offering a more flexible framework for modeling grouped data.  

The paper’s theoretical contributions are substantial. However, its practical utility requires stronger justification. The proposed inference algorithm is notably more complex than that of the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP)—both in theoretical formulation and implementation. The empirical improvements reported (e.g., in Table 2) are marginal and insufficient to justify this added complexity in real-world applications. Crucially, the authors fail to provide an intuitive explanation or empirical justification for why their framework would outperform HDP in practice. Without clear evidence that the method delivers meaningful gains in accuracy, scalability, or interpretability over HDP, the practical value remains unclear.  

I acknowledge the paper’s theoretical rigor and novelty. Yet, the absence of compelling practical motivation—particularly given the complexity trade-off—weakens the overall impact. The authors should explicitly address how their approach would improve performance in concrete scenarios compared to HDP, rather than relying solely on theoretical elegance.  

Overall, while the theoretical advances are valuable, the lack of a persuasive case for practical adoption is a significant limitation. If the authors can strengthen this aspect in revision—perhaps through additional experiments demonstrating clear advantages in relevant applications—I would strongly support acceptance. As presented, the work leans toward theoretical interest, and I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address these practical concerns in the final version.
