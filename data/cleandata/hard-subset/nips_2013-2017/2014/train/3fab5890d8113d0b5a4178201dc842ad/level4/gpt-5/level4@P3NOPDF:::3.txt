Summary
The paper derives the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the context of mixed-membership modeling via a count-mixture construction, and uses this result to develop a collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model. The EPPF-based prediction rule is then used to perform inference. The work is clearly presented and technically careful.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation with sound derivations. The theoretical development appears correct and is easy to follow.
- The main contribution—the derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for mixed-membership modeling—is interesting and valuable in its own right. It provides a principled foundation for collapsed inference in BNBP models and a template that could facilitate the derivation of EPPFs for related processes.
- The connection through the count-mixture perspective is insightful and helps unify viewpoints across BNP models, potentially aiding future methodological development.

Comments and Suggestions
- Empirical results: The BNBP topic model is a reasonable illustration of the utility of the derived EPPF and prediction rule. However, the improvements over HDP-LDA appear marginal. The experiments primarily show that the BNBP topic model and HDP-LDA embody different inductive biases rather than demonstrating a clear performance advantage. It would strengthen the empirical section to more explicitly discuss scenarios where the BNBP is expected to excel (e.g., modeling over-dispersion or burstiness in count data) and to provide evidence aligned with those hypotheses.
- Comparison of inference schemes: A more informative experiment would directly compare the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler with alternative samplers for the same BNBP topic model, specifically the sliced sampler of [10] and the truncated sampler of [8]. Such a comparison would clarify the practical trade-offs among:
  - Mixing behavior and sample efficiency (e.g., effective sample sizes, autocorrelation).
  - Convergence speed and wall-clock time.
  - Computational complexity per iteration and scalability.
  - Parallelization: uncollapsed representations often parallelize more naturally; contrasting their scalability and throughput with the collapsed sampler would be valuable.
- Minor experimental details that would improve clarity and reproducibility:
  - Report per-iteration runtime, total runtime to a fixed accuracy, and convergence diagnostics.
  - Provide details on datasets, preprocessing, and hyperparameters; clarify whether priors and initialization are matched across methods.
  - Include sensitivity analyses to key hyperparameters, if feasible.

Overall Assessment
This is an interesting and technically solid paper. The EPPF derivation for the BNBP and its use in constructing a collapsed sampler constitute a meaningful contribution to Bayesian nonparametrics and mixed-membership modeling. While the empirical improvements over HDP-LDA are modest, the work is methodologically valuable and provides tools likely to be useful for related models. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion to strengthen the empirical section by comparing against sliced and truncated samplers for the BNBP topic model and discussing the associated mixing and scalability trade-offs.
