

This paper introduces the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) as a novel integer-valued stochastic process for mixed-membership modeling, with a particular focus on developing an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) that governs its random partitioning of grouped data. The authors derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, which they apply to construct a nonparametric Bayesian topic model. The key contributions include: (1) a theoretically rigorous derivation of the BNBP's EPPF through a group-size-dependent model, (2) a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions, and (3) a novel topic model that achieves fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance.

The paper builds upon the well-established framework of exchangeable partition probability functions (EPPFs) used in nonparametric Bayesian mixture modeling. Previous work, such as the Chinese restaurant process and hierarchical Dirichlet processes (HDPs), has primarily focused on single-set data clustering. The authors correctly identify that while the HDP has been successfully applied to mixed-membership modeling, the BNBP and related integer-valued stochastic processes lack a proper EPPF that describes their marginal distributions. This gap represents a significant theoretical limitation, and the authors make a valuable contribution by developing the BNBP's EPPF through a novel approach that first randomizes group sizes and then derives the marginal distribution using Bayes' rule.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality. The derivations in Lemmas 1-4 are mathematically rigorous, and the authors provide clear explanations of how the BNBP's EPPF differs from previous approaches. The paper also shows good clarity in its presentation of the theoretical framework, though some sections (particularly in the appendix) could benefit from more detailed exposition. The topic model construction is logically developed, and the comparison with HDP-LDA in Section 3.1 thoughtfully highlights the distinct multiplicative shrinkage mechanism of the BNBP compared to the additive mechanism of HDP-LDA.

The originality of this work is substantial. The authors successfully extend the EPPF framework to mixed-membership modeling using the BNBP, which has not been previously accomplished for this integer-valued stochastic process. The paper provides a general method to arrive at an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using integer-valued stochastic processes, which could potentially be applied to other processes like the gamma-Poisson or gamma-negative binomial processes. This methodological contribution represents a significant advancement in the theoretical understanding of nonparametric Bayesian models for grouped data.

The significance of this work is evident in its practical applications. The BNBP topic model demonstrates state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact representation of the corpus compared to HDP-LDA. The empirical results show that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves comparable or better perplexity with fewer topics. These advantages are particularly valuable in real-world applications where computational efficiency and model interpretability are critical.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed. The most significant issue concerns the experimental evaluation. The authors claim their method achieves "state-of-the-art predictive performance" but compare against HDP-LDA rather than other BNBP sampling approaches. This is problematic because the paper's primary contribution is the sampling algorithm for BNBP, not the topic model itself. The authors should have compared their fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with existing BNBP inference methods (such as those using finite truncation or slice sampling as mentioned in the paper's references). Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed sampler truly offers advantages over existing approaches for the BNBP. The paper cites two recent papers [16, 17] that marginalize out the beta process from the negative binomial process, yet fails to compare with these methods.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity. While the authors mention that the BNBP sampler takes "about 2.5 seconds per iteration on the NIPS12 corpus," they don't provide a thorough comparison of computational efficiency with other BNBP samplers. The claim that the BNBP topic model is "straightforward to implement" could be strengthened with more specific implementation details or comparisons to other topic models.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion about the relationship between the BNBP and other integer-valued stochastic processes mentioned in the introduction. While the authors acknowledge the existence of the gamma-Poisson process and gamma-negative binomial process, they don't clearly articulate why the BNBP is preferable or how the proposed method could be generalized to these other processes.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically sound and original contribution to the development of EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and valuable, and the resulting topic model demonstrates practical advantages over HDP-LDA in terms of convergence speed and model compactness. However, the experimental evaluation is inadequate because it fails to compare the proposed sampler with existing BNBP inference methods. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a comprehensive comparison of their fully collapsed Gibbs sampler with other BNBP inference approaches, and they should clarify the relationship between BNBP and other integer-valued stochastic processes. With these improvements, this work would make a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling.
