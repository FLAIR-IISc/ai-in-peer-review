

The paper derives the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta negative binomial process (BNBP) and leverages it to construct a collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based topic modeling. This is a well-written, technically sound contribution with clear exposition. The primary theoretical advance—a derivation of the BNBP's EPPF via count-mixture models—is both theoretically interesting and provides a valuable framework for deriving EPPFs in related hierarchical Bayesian models. The authors successfully demonstrate how this EPPF enables efficient prediction rules, enhancing the model’s practical utility.

The BNBP topic model serves as a reasonable illustration of the EPPF’s utility, but the experimental comparison with the hierarchical Dirichlet process LDA (HDP-LDA) is insufficient to establish significant advantages. The reported improvements are modest, and the analysis primarily reveals distinct modeling characteristics rather than clear performance gains. A more compelling evaluation would directly compare the proposed collapsed Gibbs sampler against alternative inference methods for the BNBP topic model, specifically the sliced sampler [10] and the truncated sampler [8]. Such a comparison would better contextualize the trade-offs between inference efficiency, mixing behavior, and scalability. Notably, uncollapsed variants of the BNBP offer inherent parallelization benefits, yet the paper does not explore how the collapsed sampler’s mixing properties (e.g., convergence speed, effective sample size) compare to these alternatives. Addressing this gap would strengthen the paper’s contribution to inference methodology.

Minor experimental concerns: The results section lacks clarity on hyperparameter sensitivity and runtime comparisons across models. For instance, the paper states that the BNBP model achieves "comparable" performance to HDP-LDA but does not quantify the magnitude of differences (e.g., perplexity gaps, training times). Including such metrics would clarify whether observed differences are statistically meaningful or negligible. Additionally, the model’s scalability with respect to corpus size or topic count is not evaluated, which is critical for real-world applicability.

The theoretical foundation is robust, and the derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a notable contribution to the literature on exchangeable partition models. However, the experimental section would benefit from more rigorous benchmarking against state-of-the-art inference techniques for similar processes. While the paper meets the threshold for publication, these enhancements would elevate its impact. The current presentation risks underselling the method’s potential by not fully contrasting its inference properties with alternatives that exploit different computational strategies.

In summary, this work delivers a technically solid EPPF derivation for the BNBP and a functional collapsed sampler, providing useful tools for future model extensions. The primary limitation lies in the experimental evaluation, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the proposed sampler’s advantages over existing approaches. With the suggested refinements—particularly a more comprehensive inference benchmark—the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field. As submitted, it is acceptable for publication but warrants minor revisions to address the points above.
