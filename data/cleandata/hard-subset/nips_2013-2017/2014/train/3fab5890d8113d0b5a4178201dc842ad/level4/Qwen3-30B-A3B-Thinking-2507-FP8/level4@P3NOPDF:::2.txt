

This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the Beta-Negative-Binomial-Poisson (BNBP) process, with Lemma 1 and the derived sampling procedure presenting novel and interesting theoretical contributions. The motivation—emphasizing simplicity, fast convergence, efficient mixing, and strong predictive performance—is well-aligned with the goal of advancing sampling methodology for the BNBP. However, the experimental evaluation critically undermines the paper’s central claims due to an inappropriate baseline comparison.  

The core weakness lies in the choice of baselines. The authors compare their method exclusively against HDP-LDA, a topic modeling framework based on the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process, rather than against established sampling algorithms designed for the BNBP itself. This is methodologically unsound: the paper’s focus is strictly on *sampling algorithms* for the BNBP, not on statistical modeling or comparative analysis of different probabilistic models. HDP-LDA and BNBP are fundamentally distinct processes (HDP-LDA models topic hierarchies, while BNBP models cluster structures with power-law properties), making their comparison irrelevant to the paper’s stated objective. The claimed advantages—such as "fast convergence" and "good mixing"—cannot be validated without direct empirical comparison to other BNBP samplers (e.g., standard Gibbs, variational inference, or alternative collapsed schemes).  

The absence of such comparisons renders the experimental results inconclusive. For instance, the paper asserts "state-of-the-art predictive performance," yet no baseline BNBP samplers are evaluated on standard metrics (e.g., perplexity, clustering accuracy, or convergence speed). Without this, the paper fails to demonstrate whether the proposed sampler *actually* outperforms existing methods for the BNBP. The reader is left to question whether the observed results stem from the sampler’s design or from the inherent properties of the BNBP model itself.  

To address this, the authors must:  
1. **Replace HDP-LDA with relevant baselines**: Include comparisons against standard Gibbs sampling for BNBP, variational methods, or other collapsed samplers (e.g., as in Blei & Jordan, 2004, or subsequent BNBP works).  
2. **Report necessary metrics**: Quantify convergence speed (e.g., effective sample size, autocorrelation), mixing quality (e.g., trace plots, Geweke diagnostics), and predictive performance (e.g., held-out likelihood) under identical experimental conditions.  
3. **Clarify the scope**: Explicitly state that the evaluation is confined to BNBP sampling algorithms, avoiding any model-centric comparisons.  

The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the experimental section must be restructured to validate the *methodological* claims. Without this, the paper’s core assertion—that the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler offers superior performance for BNBP—lacks empirical support. The current evaluation risks misleading readers into conflating model choice (BNBP vs. HDP-LDA) with algorithmic innovation.  

In summary, while Lemma 1 and the sampler design are novel, the experimental flaws significantly weaken the paper’s contribution. The work would be substantially strengthened by a rigorous comparison against existing BNBP sampling approaches. As currently presented, the results fail to justify the methodological claims, and the paper’s potential is not fully realized. I recommend rejection unless these critical experiments are added in revision.
