This paper addresses a significant challenge in the application of the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP) to mixed-membership modeling. The core problem is that, until now, the marginal probability distribution of the BNBP that governs the exchangeable random partitions of grouped data was unknown. This has prevented the development of fully collapsed inference schemes, forcing previous work to rely on less efficient methods like finite truncation or slice sampling. The main contribution of this paper is the derivation of this missing piece: the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP. To achieve this, the authors introduce a novel methodological step, first deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by treating group sizes as random, and then using Bayes' rule to condition on the observed group sizes to arrive at the desired EPPF.

This theoretical breakthrough enables the paper's second main contribution: the first fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP-based model. The authors demonstrate the utility of this sampler by constructing a BNBP topic model and deriving simple, closed-form update equations. The paper positions this work as an alternative to the popular Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), noting the distinct multiplicative mechanism for sharing statistical strength across groups, in contrast to the HDP's additive one.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations in Section 2, culminating in the EPPF (Lemma 3) and the simple predictive rule (Lemma 4), appear to be sound and are the central strength of the work. This is a complete piece of research that presents a novel theoretical result and follows through with its practical application in a new topic model and inference algorithm. The claims regarding the properties of the sampler are supported by empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem and clearly explaining the distinction between the ECPF and the EPPF, which is a crucial step in their derivation. The hierarchical model construction is clearly laid out, and the resulting Gibbs sampling update equations are presented in a way that makes them easy to understand and compare to related samplers like those for LDA and HDP-LDA. The inclusion of Figure 1, which visualizes the partitions generated under different parameter settings, provides valuable intuition about the model's behavior.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a novel theoretical contribution that fills a known gap in the literature on nonparametric Bayesian processes. While the BNBP itself is not new, developing a method for fully collapsed inference is a significant advance. The resulting BNBP topic model and its associated sampler are distinct from existing models and provide a new tool for practitioners. The connection made to the digamma and Dirichlet-multinomial distributions in the prior construction of the random count matrix is also insightful.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian nonparametrics and machine learning. The theoretical framework for deriving an EPPF from an integer-valued stochastic process could potentially be generalized to other processes, as the authors suggest in their conclusion. From a practical standpoint, the development of a simple and efficient fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP makes it a much more viable and attractive alternative to the HDP for mixed-membership modeling. The experimental results, which suggest the BNBP can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance with more compact models, further underscore its potential impact.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant and fundamental contribution to the understanding of this stochastic process. It solves a long-standing problem that has hindered the development of efficient inference.
2.  **New and Efficient Inference Algorithm:** The paper successfully translates the theoretical result into a practical, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The sampler is simple to implement, and the empirical evidence provided in Figure 2 strongly supports the claims of fast convergence and good mixing properties compared to the widely used HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler.
3.  **Insightful Model Comparison:** The comparison between the multiplicative update rule of the BNBP sampler and the additive rule of the HDP-LDA sampler provides a clear intuition for why the two models behave differently, particularly in how they control model complexity.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons) / Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Missing Experimental Baselines:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The paper's motivation is to introduce a superior *inference algorithm* for the BNBP, highlighting its advantages in convergence and mixing. However, the experiments exclusively compare the BNBP (using the proposed sampler) against the HDP-LDA. While this is a valuable comparison of two different *models*, it does not directly validate the claims about the superiority of the *sampler*.

    To make a more compelling case for the proposed algorithm, a direct comparison against existing inference methods for the *same* BNBP model is necessary. The authors should include results from the finite truncation Gibbs sampler (e.g., Zhou et al., 2012) and/or the slice sampler (Broderick et al., 2013) for the BNBP. This would isolate the benefits of the fully collapsed sampler (e.g., convergence speed, mixing, perplexity, computational time per iteration) from the inherent properties of the BNBP model itself. While the paper states that the collapsed sampler outperforms the blocked sampler of [7], providing empirical evidence for this would substantially strengthen the paper's claims.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution that has immediate practical implications. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is elegant and important. The resulting collapsed Gibbs sampler is a valuable new tool. The primary weakness is the lack of a direct comparison to other BNBP samplers, which would have provided definitive proof of the new algorithm's superiority. However, the theoretical novelty is substantial enough on its own to warrant publication. The comparison with HDP-LDA is still informative, and the results on convergence and mixing are convincing. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to consider adding the suggested experimental comparisons for the final version to make the paper's contributions even more impactful and irrefutable.
