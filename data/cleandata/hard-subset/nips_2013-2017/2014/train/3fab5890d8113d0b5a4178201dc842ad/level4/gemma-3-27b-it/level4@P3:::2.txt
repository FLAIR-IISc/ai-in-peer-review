This paper introduces a novel and valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling: a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP). The authors address a key limitation of existing BNBP inference methods – the need for truncation – by deriving an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) that enables fully collapsed sampling. The core idea of deriving the EPPF through a group size dependent model (Lemma 1) is particularly insightful. The resulting sampler offers potential advantages in terms of simplicity, speed, and mixing, as highlighted by the authors.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in nonparametric Bayesian methods, specifically referencing the Dirichlet process [1], normalized random measures with independent increments (NRMIs) [2, 3], the Chinese restaurant process [4, 5], and the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) [5]. It correctly identifies the challenges in extending EPPF models to mixed-membership modeling, noting the limitations of previous approaches like the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) [6] and existing BNBP inference methods [7, 10, 11]. The authors appropriately situate their work within this context and demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EPPF and the subsequent Gibbs sampler appear technically sound, with clear mathematical formulations and logical steps. The lemmas provide a solid theoretical basis for the proposed approach.
* **Novelty:** The development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP, leveraging the derived EPPF, is a significant and original contribution. This addresses a practical limitation in applying the BNBP and opens up possibilities for more efficient inference.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The mathematical notation is reasonably clear, and the lemmas are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Potential Significance:** A fully collapsed sampler for the BNBP has the potential to significantly advance the use of this model in various applications, including topic modeling and mixed-membership modeling. The demonstrated fast convergence and good mixing properties are particularly encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental evaluation. While the authors demonstrate the performance of their sampler on standard topic modeling datasets (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12), the comparison is primarily with the HDP-LDA [6, 23]. As the reviewer correctly points out, the stated motivation is to improve the *sampling algorithm* for the BNBP, not to compete with alternative models like HDP-LDA. A more compelling evaluation would include a comparison of the proposed Gibbs sampler with *other* sampling algorithms for the BNBP, such as those based on finite truncation [7] or slice sampling [11]. Demonstrating performance gains specifically *within* the BNBP framework would strengthen the paper’s claims.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the authors mention the potential for parallelization, the paper lacks a more detailed discussion of practical considerations for implementing and scaling the proposed sampler. This could include computational complexity analysis or guidance on parameter tuning.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" is somewhat overstated, given the limited scope of the comparison. While the results are competitive with HDP-LDA, a more cautious phrasing would be appropriate.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian inference.
* The derived EPPF and Gibbs sampler address a significant practical limitation of existing BNBP methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.
* The experimental results, while not definitive, are promising and suggest the potential for improved performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is not focused on comparing the proposed sampler with other BNBP samplers.
* The paper lacks a thorough discussion of practical considerations for implementation and scalability.
* Some of the claims regarding predictive performance are overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The technical development of the EPPF and fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP is impressive. However, the experimental evaluation needs strengthening to truly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. **Add a comparison to other BNBP sampling algorithms:** Include results comparing the proposed Gibbs sampler to methods based on finite truncation or slice sampling.
2. **Moderate the performance claims:** Rephrase the claim of “state-of-the-art performance” to reflect the limited scope of the comparison.
3. **Expand the discussion of practical considerations:** Provide additional insights into the computational complexity and parameter tuning of the sampler.
