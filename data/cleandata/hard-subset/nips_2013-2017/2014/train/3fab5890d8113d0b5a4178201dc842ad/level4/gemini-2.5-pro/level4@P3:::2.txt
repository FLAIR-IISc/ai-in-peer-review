### Review of "Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling"

#### Summary of the Paper

This paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling, specifically for mixed-membership models. The central contribution is the derivation of the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). This was a previously open problem, and its solution enables, for the first time, the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based models.

The authors achieve this by first defining a group-size dependent model to derive an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF), which describes the joint distribution of random group sizes and their partitions. Using this intermediate result and Bayes' rule, they derive the target EPPF for fixed, observed group sizes. This EPPF is then used to construct a simple and efficient prediction rule. As a practical application, the authors develop a BNBP topic model and implement a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional beta process, topic proportions, and topics. The paper claims this new sampler offers simple implementation, faster convergence, better mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance compared to existing methods, particularly the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP). Experimental results on three text corpora are presented to support these claims.

#### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in the mixed-membership setting is a novel and important theoretical contribution. It fills a notable gap in the literature, as previous inference schemes for the BNBP relied on finite truncation [10] or slice sampling [11]. This work provides a more complete and elegant characterization of the BNBP's combinatorial structure, advancing the unified treatment of mixed-membership models in a way analogous to what EPPFs have done for standard mixture models [5].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The mathematical derivations, particularly in Lemmas 1-4, appear to be sound and are presented with clarity. The method of using a size-dependent process to derive the ECPF and then conditioning to obtain the EPPF is clever and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes, as the authors suggest.

3.  **Clarity and Practical Utility:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. A key outcome of the theoretical work is the simple and intuitive prediction rule (Eq. 10). This rule makes the implementation of the sampler straightforward, a significant advantage for practitioners. The resulting Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model (Eq. 12) is fully collapsed, which is known to lead to better mixing and faster convergence in many Bayesian models.

4.  **Demonstrated Model Properties:** The empirical comparison with the HDP-LDA provides valuable insights into the distinct shrinkage properties of the BNBP model. The multiplicative interaction between global and local topic popularity in the BNBP sampler, as opposed to the HDP's additive mechanism, is an interesting structural difference that appears to encourage more compact topic representations. The results suggest the BNBP topic model is a compelling alternative to the HDP, especially when a smaller number of topics is desired without sacrificing predictive power.

#### Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Mismatch between Claims and Experimental Validation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental design, which does not fully support the central claims about the proposed *sampling algorithm*. The abstract and introduction motivate the work by highlighting the advantages of the new *sampler* (fast convergence, good mixing). However, the experiments exclusively compare the BNBP model (inferred with the new sampler) against a different model, the HDP-LDA (inferred with its own sampler).

    *   This comparison confounds the effects of the model (BNBP vs. HDP) and the inference algorithm (fully collapsed vs. direct assignment/partially collapsed).
    *   To convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed **sampler**, it should be benchmarked against other inference schemes for the **same BNBP model**. This would involve comparing the proposed fully collapsed sampler against the truncated block Gibbs sampler [7, 10] or an adaptive slice sampler [11] for the BNBP.
    *   Such a comparison should evaluate metrics directly related to sampler performance, such as wall-clock time to convergence, log-likelihood trace plots over time, and autocorrelation plots for key variables. While the trace plots in Figure 2 are suggestive, they compare two different models and samplers simultaneously. A controlled experiment is needed to isolate the contribution of the sampler itself.

2.  **Strength of "State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" may be slightly overstated. As shown in Figure 3, the HDP-LDA can achieve lower perplexity than the BNBP topic model, albeit by using a significantly larger number of topics. The strength of the BNBP appears to be in achieving competitive perplexity with a much more compact model. The claim could be nuanced to more accurately reflect this, for example, by stating it achieves state-of-the-art performance for a given model complexity or number of topics.

#### Overall Evaluation

This paper presents a strong and valuable contribution. The theoretical development of the EPPF for the BNBP is elegant and closes an important gap in the nonparametric Bayesian literature. The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant practical achievement that makes the BNBP model more accessible and efficient.

The main reservation is the experimental section, which compares models rather than the samplers, thus failing to provide direct evidence for the claims about the sampler's superior convergence and mixing properties relative to existing BNBP inference methods.

Despite this weakness, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The derivation of the EPPF and the resulting sampler is a solid piece of work that will likely be influential and built upon by other researchers. The authors are strongly encouraged to revise the experimental section to include a direct comparison with other BNBP samplers to make the paper's claims about the algorithm's performance more robust.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The theoretical contributions are excellent, though the experimental validation of the sampler's performance could be strengthened considerably.
