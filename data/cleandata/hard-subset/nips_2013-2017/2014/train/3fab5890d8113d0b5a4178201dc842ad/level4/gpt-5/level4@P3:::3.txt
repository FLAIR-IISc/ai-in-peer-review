Summary and relation to prior work
The paper derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, where data arrive as groups and clusters are shared across groups. The key technical move is to introduce an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) by “Poissonizing” group sizes, marginalize the beta process to obtain a finite-dimensional distribution over a random count matrix, and then recover the EPPF via Bayes’ rule conditioned on observed group sizes. The authors further provide a simple prediction rule for collapsed Gibbs sampling and instantiate a BNBP topic model with fully collapsed updates (marginalizing topics, topic weights, and the beta process), comparing against HDP-LDA.

The work connects to the classical EPPF/CRP literature (Pitman; Blackwell–MacQueen; NRMI marginalizations), extends sample-size-dependent partition models (Zhou–Walker) to grouped data, and complements recent work on negative-binomial/IBP-style processes for infinite random count matrices (Heaukulani–Roy; Zhou et al.) and σ-stable Poisson–Kingman samplers (Lomelí–Favaro–Teh), which do not directly cover BNBP mixed-membership EPPFs. For topic modeling, it stands alongside HDP-LDA (Teh et al.) and Poisson factorization models, but provides a distinct multiplicative shrinkage mechanism that couples global and local popularity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The augmentation from BNBP to beta–gamma–Poisson is standard and correct. The factorization obtained by pairing categorical assignments with the Poisson total count is clean and enables analytic marginalization. The resulting ECPF and the induced random count matrix prior (with Digam/DirMult structure) are plausible and internally consistent. The prediction rule is simple and agrees with the generative story. Proofs are deferred but the steps are well motivated.
- Scope and completeness: The paper delivers a complete pipeline: law of partitions (ECPF→EPPF), a prediction rule, and an instantiated topic model with fully collapsed sampling and closed-form updates. Experimental comparisons focus on HDP-LDA; comparisons to alternative BNBP inference schemes (slice or truncation) are discussed but not executed.
- Empirics: Perplexity experiments on three corpora and mixing diagnostics show fast convergence and good local mixing for the collapsed sampler. The improvements over HDP-LDA are modest overall; the main empirical takeaway is that BNBP attains similar or better perplexity with fewer topics for moderate η, consistent with its stronger multiplicative shrinkage.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The probabilistic constructions are clearly presented, and the intuition behind the prediction rule is helpful. Some suggestions:
- Provide a brief intuition for the “digamma distribution” and its role; it is less standard than DirMult.
- Reduce notational overload where possible and collect key symbols in a table (e.g., KJ, n·k, r·).
- Include pseudo-code for the collapsed sampler and summarize computational complexity per iteration.
- A light proofreading pass would help (e.g., minor typos and duplicated words).

Originality
The main contribution—deriving a group-size-dependent EPPF for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling via an ECPF route—is novel and, to my knowledge, not available for BNBP in prior work. The prediction rule and fully collapsed BNBP topic model follow naturally but are also practically valuable. The general methodology for obtaining EPPFs for integer-valued processes appears portable to other processes (e.g., GNBP, gamma–Poisson), which increases novelty.

Significance
Conceptually, the work fills a gap: an explicit marginal partition law for BNBP in grouped settings, enabling truncation-free collapsed inference. Practically, the collapsed sampler is simple, fast, and mixes well, and the topic model is competitive with HDP-LDA while using fewer topics under common settings. The approach should interest researchers working on Bayesian nonparametrics, count processes, and scalable topic models, and may catalyze analogous results for related processes.

Strengths
- Clear, principled derivation of an EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Simple prediction rule enabling a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
- Distinct multiplicative shrinkage mechanism with intuitive interpretation.
- Solid empirical evidence of fast convergence and compact representations.
- Methodology likely transferable to other count processes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope: Comparisons are limited to HDP-LDA. It would strengthen the paper to compare the proposed collapsed sampler against BNBP slice sampling (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS 2012) and truncated/block samplers, assessing mixing, wall-clock time, memory, and parallelizability trade-offs.
- Sensitivity analyses: Report the impact of hyperparameters (c, γ0, priors on rj) and ablate the effect of inferring rj versus fixing them.
- Evaluation breadth: Consider additional baselines (e.g., gamma–Poisson factorization) and report runtime scaling with vocabulary size, documents, and tokens. Include topics-per-document sparsity and qualitative topic coherence to complement perplexity.
- Theory: Since the EPPF is sample-size dependent, a brief discussion of asymptotic behavior or when this dependence is beneficial would be useful.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether the addition rule is violated or simply not required, and what that implies for exchangeability across varying sample sizes.
- Unify notation and spacing for KJ and superscripts “−ji.”
- Check minor typos (e.g., “perdition” → “prediction,” duplicated words).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid derivation of a useful EPPF with clear practical payoff.
- Truncation-free, fully collapsed inference with good empirical properties.
- Methodological contribution likely to generalize to other integer-valued processes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical comparisons are somewhat narrow; state-of-the-art claims would benefit from broader baselines and direct comparisons to alternative BNBP inference schemes.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameter sensitivity and scaling could be expanded.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper with a substantive theoretical contribution and a practical algorithm that is easy to implement and effective. I recommend acceptance, with the above suggestions aimed at strengthening the empirical section and clarifying minor points.
