This paper proposes a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). The derivation of the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) and the resulting sampler are novel and interesting contributions to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the existing literature on Dirichlet processes, normalized random measures with independent increments, and mixed-membership modeling. The paper effectively bridges a gap in the existing approaches by providing a unified treatment for mixed-membership modeling, analogous to the EPPF model for single datasets.

However, a primary concern arises regarding the experimental evaluation. The motivation for developing the CG sampler stems from its potential for simplified implementation, fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance. Given this focus on the sampling algorithm itself, the primary comparison should be with *other* sampling algorithms specifically designed for the BNBP, rather than with the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process Latent Dirichlet Allocation (HDP-LDA). While the comparison with HDP-LDA provides some context, it doesn’t directly address the core claim of improving BNBP sampling. The experimental section would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison against existing BNBP inference methods, such as the finite truncation methods of [10, 7] or the slice sampling approach of [11].  Providing results for alternative samplers, even if implemented in a less optimized fashion, would allow for a more direct assessment of the benefits of the proposed fully collapsed sampler.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF and the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler represent a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous and well-supported. The connection to existing theory on exchangeable random partitions and NRMI’s is clearly established.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:**  Despite the complexity of the topic, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The step-by-step derivation of the EPPF and the sampler is relatively easy to follow.
*   **Potential for Efficiency:** A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, if well-implemented, can offer substantial computational advantages over alternative inference methods.
*   **Unified Framework:** The paper advances a unified framework for mixed-membership modeling, addressing a recognized limitation in the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Inappropriate Comparison:** The primary comparison with HDP-LDA doesn’t directly assess the performance gains of the proposed sampler *relative to other BNBP samplers*.
*   **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The absence of experimental results for alternative BNBP sampling algorithms (e.g., finite truncation, slice sampling) limits the assessment of the proposed method's advantages.
*   **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation relies solely on perplexity, which, while useful, doesn’t provide a comprehensive assessment of the model’s performance.  Consider including other metrics, such as topic coherence or qualitative analysis of the learned topics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling.
*   The derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF is a significant advancement, providing a more complete understanding of the model's properties.
*   The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler has the potential to improve the efficiency and scalability of BNBP inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully validate the claimed benefits of the proposed sampler.
*   The lack of comparison with alternative BNBP sampling algorithms weakens the justification for the method's novelty.
*   The reliance solely on perplexity as an evaluation metric may not provide a complete picture of the model’s performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The theoretical derivations are sound, and the proposed sampler has the potential to be a useful tool for researchers. However, the experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened by including a comparison with alternative BNBP sampling algorithms.  If the authors can address this concern, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.  I recommend a borderline accept, contingent on the authors adding a comparison with existing BNBP samplers in the revised manuscript.
