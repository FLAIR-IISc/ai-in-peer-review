Summary
The paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting and uses it to derive a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The authors proceed by first introducing an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for jointly modeling random group sizes and their partitions, then marginalize to obtain a group-size dependent EPPF. They also derive a simple prediction rule and apply the framework to topic modeling, yielding a nonparametric BNBP topic model whose sampler marginalizes the topics, topic weights, and the infinite beta process. Empirically, the method is compared primarily against HDP-LDA and shows fast mixing and competitive or better perplexity when the number of inferred topics is comparable.

Relative to prior work, the paper connects to EPPF-based treatments of mixture models (e.g., Pitman), extends sample-size–dependent EPPFs to the mixed-membership case, and complements the HDP’s Chinese restaurant franchise by providing an EPPF for the BNBP. It also relates to previous BNBP/GNBP and Poisson-gamma constructions for counts and factorization [7,10,11,16,17], but differs in providing a truncation-free, fully collapsed sampler and an explicit prediction rule for grouped partitions.

Strengths
- Novelty: Deriving an EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership modeling is original and useful. The construction via an ECPF and Bayes’ rule is elegant.
- Algorithmic contribution: The fully collapsed sampler is simple to implement, avoids truncation, and appears to mix rapidly.
- Clarity of the modeling link: The beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation cleanly motivates the mixed-membership interpretation and connects count modeling and mixture modeling.
- Practical impact: A collapsed BNBP topic model is of interest to practitioners seeking nonparametric models with compact representations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental scope: The central claim is an improved sampling algorithm (simplicity, speed, mixing, predictive performance). However, experiments focus mainly on comparisons with HDP-LDA. To substantiate the algorithmic contribution, comparisons against other BNBP inference methods are necessary, e.g., finite-truncation/blocked Gibbs samplers [7,10] and slice-based samplers [11,16,17]. Report wall-clock time to a target perplexity, effective sample size, autocorrelation, and memory use.
- Ablations: Since rj strongly controls dispersion, please include ablations comparing fixed vs inferred rj, and sensitivity analyses over priors for rj, γ0, and c. It would also help to report whether c is inferred or fixed and assess sensitivity to c.
- Metrics and fairness: Beyond perplexity, consider held-out log-likelihood, document completion, or predictive likelihood on held-out words at fixed computational budgets. When comparing to HDP-LDA, equalize either (i) the number of topics or (ii) compute to a fixed time budget; include convergence diagnostics.
- Complexity: Provide per-iteration complexity in terms of nnz counts and KJ, and wall-clock runtimes for all methods. Include pseudocode for the collapsed sampler.
- Clarity: The presentation is dense. Suggestions:
  • More intuition for the group-size dependent EPPF and its implications relative to Pitman’s addition rule.
  • Move the prediction rule and its intuition into the main text earlier; include a small, worked example.
  • Clearly distinguish ECPF vs EPPF and summarize the key steps of the derivation in the main text (with proofs in the appendix).
  • Unify notation early (e.g., n·k, n:k, m, KJ) and reduce repetition.
- Claims: “State-of-the-art predictive performance” should be backed by broader baselines (e.g., HDP variants, DILN, Poisson factorization baselines) or be softened.

Quality
- Technical correctness appears sound: the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation, marginalization steps, and resulting digamma/Dirichlet-multinomial structure are consistent with prior literature. Since many proofs are in the appendix, pointers to key lemmas and assumptions (e.g., conditions under which the EPPF holds and its sample-size dependence) would help.
- The derivation of the prediction rule is a valuable contribution that enables straightforward Gibbs sampling.

Clarity
- Generally well organized but heavy on notation. Providing a concise roadmap and a notation table would improve readability. Minor typographical issues exist (e.g., duplicated words, spacing).

Originality
- High. An EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling and a fully collapsed, truncation-free sampler are, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
- Potentially high. If the proposed sampler materially improves mixing and reduces implementation complexity relative to existing BNBP samplers, it will be useful in topic modeling and other grouped count applications. Demonstrating superiority over other BNBP samplers experimentally is essential to realize this significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP in mixed-membership settings.
- Fully collapsed, simple sampler with promising empirical behavior.
- Clear link between count modeling and mixed-membership modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks direct comparisons to existing BNBP inference algorithms (truncation-based and slice-based).
- Limited evaluation metrics and ablations; no runtime/ESS comparisons.
- Some clarity issues and overstrong empirical claims without broader baselines.

Recommendation
Borderline reject/weak reject in current form. The theoretical contribution is strong and likely publishable, but the empirical evaluation should include head-to-head comparisons with other BNBP samplers and more thorough diagnostics. Strengthening the experiments and clarifying the EPPF/ECPF exposition would make this a solid acceptance.
