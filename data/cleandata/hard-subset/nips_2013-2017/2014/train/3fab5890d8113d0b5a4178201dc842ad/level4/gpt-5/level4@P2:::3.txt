Summary
This paper derives an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting. The authors proceed by first formulating an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for randomized group sizes, then using Bayes’ rule to obtain the EPPF that directly governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data with fixed sizes. They further provide a prediction rule, show how the EPPF induces a prior on a column-exchangeable count matrix, and develop a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the beta process, topic parameters, and topic weights. Applied to topic modeling, the resulting BNBP topic model is distinct from HDP-LDA in its multiplicative coupling of global and document-specific popularity and is shown to mix rapidly and achieve competitive held-out perplexities with a relatively compact set of topics.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates the contribution within the literature on nonparametric mixture and mixed-membership models: Dirichlet process and EPPFs (e.g., CRP/CRF), NRMIs, and integer-valued processes (gamma–Poisson, BNBP, GNBP). Prior BNBP inference has typically required truncation or slice sampling. Unlike those, the present work provides a truncation-free, fully collapsed sampler enabled by a new EPPF for grouped data.

Strengths
- Clear, principled derivation of a group-size–dependent EPPF for the BNBP, filling a gap for mixed-membership modeling with integer-valued processes.
- The general methodology (via ECPF and Bayes’ rule) is potentially applicable beyond the BNBP.
- The prediction rule is simple and informative, highlighting the role of dispersion parameters.
- The collapsed sampler is straightforward to implement and empirically exhibits fast convergence and good mixing.
- Topic modeling experiments indicate competitive or better perplexity for a given number of topics, with a compact representation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical gains over HDP-LDA are generally modest; the main empirical takeaway is that the two models impose different shrinkage mechanisms and thus exhibit distinct behavior.
- The experimental comparison would be stronger by including uncollapsed BNBP samplers (slice-based and finite/truncated variants from prior work), to quantify mixing speed, computational cost, and accuracy trade-offs against the collapsed sampler.
- Discussion of scalability and parallelization is limited. Since uncollapsed samplers often parallelize more naturally, a comparison of wall-clock performance under practical parallel settings would be valuable.
- Sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (e.g., η, dispersion rj, c, γ0) could be expanded, as the results show notable dependence on η.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and careful. The derivations and augmentation strategies are well motivated, and the sampler is correctly constructed. Claims are supported by analyses and experiments.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The exposition around the ECPF→EPPF transition and the role of the digamma distribution is clear; minor streamlining of notation might aid readability for non-specialists.
- Originality: High. Providing an EPPF for the BNBP in mixed-membership settings and the associated prediction rule is new and useful. The collapsed sampler that integrates out the beta process and topic variables is also novel in this context.
- Significance: Moderate to high. The theory enables truncation-free, collapsed inference for a popular count-based nonparametric prior and suggests a path for analogous developments with other integer-valued processes. Empirical benefits are situational but convincing when compact models are preferred.

Pros
- New EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Fully collapsed Gibbs sampler; simple implementation; fast mixing.
- Clear connection to and differentiation from HDP-LDA.
- Competitive predictive performance with fewer topics.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparison to alternative BNBP inference schemes (slice/truncation).
- Modest average improvements over HDP-LDA; benefits are primarily in compactness and mixing speed.
- Limited discussion of parallelization and large-scale deployment.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid and interesting contribution that meets the publication threshold. The theoretical development is substantive, and the algorithmic implications are practical. I recommend acceptance, with a suggestion to add comparisons to slice and truncated BNBP samplers and to discuss parallelization/mixing trade-offs more explicitly.
