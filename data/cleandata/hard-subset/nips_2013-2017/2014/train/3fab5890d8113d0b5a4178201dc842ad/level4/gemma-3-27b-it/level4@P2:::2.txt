This paper introduces a fully collapsed Gibbs (CG) sampler for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), addressing a key challenge in applying this model—efficient inference. The authors derive an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, enabling the development of this CG sampler. This work is motivated by the difficulty of inference in the BNBP due to the lack of a readily available marginal distribution, which necessitates truncation or more complex sampling methods. The proposed sampler offers a compelling solution through full analytical marginalization. Lemma 1 and the resulting sampler represent a novel and valuable contribution to the field.

The paper effectively builds upon prior work in nonparametric Bayesian methods, particularly those related to Dirichlet processes, normalized random measure with independent increments (NRMIs), and exchangeable partition probability functions (EPPFs). It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches for the BNBP, such as the truncation methods used in [10, 7] and the slice sampling of [11]. The authors also acknowledge related work on infinite random count matrices, such as the negative binomial Indian buffet process [16] and “ice cream” buffet process [17], but emphasize the unique contribution of their EPPF-based approach and the derivation of a fully collapsed sampler.

The strengths of this work lie in its technical soundness and the clarity of its derivations. The mathematical development of the EPPF and the subsequent derivation of the CG sampler appear rigorous and well-presented. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying theory and effectively connect their work to existing literature. The inclusion of closed-form update equations for the model parameters is a significant practical benefit.

However, a key weakness is the choice of experimental comparison. While the results demonstrate the performance of the BNBP topic model, the primary motivation of the paper centers on the *sampling algorithm* itself. Therefore, a more appropriate evaluation would involve a direct comparison of the proposed CG sampler with other existing sampling algorithms *for the BNBP*, rather than with the HDP-LDA. This comparison would more directly demonstrate the benefits of the authors' contribution – namely, its speed of convergence, mixing properties, and computational efficiency. The current comparison to HDP-LDA, while insightful, feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution. The claim of “state-of-the-art predictive performance” is weakened by the lack of a direct algorithmic comparison within the BNBP framework. The experimental section would be significantly strengthened by including results from, for example, truncated Gibbs sampling or slice sampling applied to the BNBP.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP and the resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler are significant contributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations are rigorous and well-explained.
*   **Practical Value:** The CG sampler offers a computationally efficient and easily implementable solution for BNBP inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation focuses on comparing to HDP-LDA rather than directly evaluating the performance of the proposed sampler against other BNBP samplers.
*   **Missing Benchmarks:** The absence of a comparison with existing BNBP sampling methods leaves the claim of “state-of-the-art” performance unsubstantiated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP and the subsequent development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler are significant accomplishments. However, the lack of a direct comparison with other sampling algorithms for the BNBP weakens the evaluation of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider including a more focused experimental evaluation that compares the performance of their CG sampler with other BNBP sampling methods, providing a more compelling demonstration of its advantages. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and increase its impact.
