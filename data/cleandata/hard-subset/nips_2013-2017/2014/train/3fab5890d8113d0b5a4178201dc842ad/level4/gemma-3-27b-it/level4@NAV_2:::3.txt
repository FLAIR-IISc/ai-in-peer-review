The paper presents the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta negative binomial process (BNBP) and utilizes it to develop a collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model.

This is a well-written, reasonably clear, and technically sound paper. The primary result – a derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF for mixed-membership modeling, achieved through a novel approach leveraging “count-mixture” models – is both interesting in its own right and provides potentially useful tools for deriving EPPFs for related models. This work builds upon the established framework of exchangeable partition probability functions [5] and extends it to the context of the BNBP, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches [10, 11] which relied on truncation or slice sampling for inference. The derivation of the EPPF is a significant contribution, offering a principled way to model exchangeable random partitions in grouped data. 

The paper effectively connects the BNBP to related work in nonparametric Bayesian methods, including the Dirichlet process [1] and normalized random measures with independent increments [2, 3]. The authors clearly position their contribution within the broader landscape of mixed-membership modeling [6, 7], and appropriately contrast their approach with existing methods like the hierarchical Dirichlet process. The authors also correctly identify the recent related work on the negative binomial Indian buffet process [16] and “ice cream” buffet process [17], appropriately differentiating their contribution.

Strengths of the paper include the clear derivation of the EPPF, the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler enabling efficient inference, and the demonstration of the method through a topic modeling application. The Gibbs sampler’s analytical tractability is a significant advantage. The comparisons to the HDP-LDA are helpful, even if the performance differences are subtle.

Weaknesses primarily lie in the empirical evaluation. While the results demonstrate competitive performance, the improvements over the HDP-LDA are marginal, and the conclusion regarding distinct characteristics could be strengthened with more detailed analysis. A more insightful comparison would involve contrasting the collapsed sampler presented here with alternative inference techniques for the BNBP, such as the sliced sampler [10] and truncated sampler [8]. Exploring the trade-offs between collapsed sampling and these alternative approaches would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the advantages of uncollapsed representations for parallelization [24, 27, 28], a discussion of potential parallelization strategies for the collapsed sampler would be valuable. The choice of hyperparameters (a0, b0, e0, f0) could benefit from further justification or sensitivity analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel derivation of the BNBP’s EPPF, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   Development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, enabling efficient inference without truncation.
*   Clear and well-motivated application to topic modeling, demonstrating the utility of the proposed method.
*   Rigorous mathematical derivations and connections to related nonparametric Bayesian techniques.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Empirical improvements over existing methods (e.g., HDP-LDA) are marginal.
*   Limited comparison to alternative inference methods for the BNBP (e.g., slice sampling, truncated sampling).
*   Potential for further discussion regarding parallelization strategies.

Overall, this is an interesting and valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. While the empirical results do not represent a dramatic advance, the theoretical contributions are significant, and the paper meets the publication threshold for a conference like NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding the experimental evaluation and potential for parallelization.
