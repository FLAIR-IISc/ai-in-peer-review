This paper addresses the problem of mixed-membership modeling using the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), an integer-valued stochastic process. The primary contribution is the derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP in this setting. Prior work on BNBP for mixed-membership modeling, such as that by Zhou et al. (2012), relied on finite truncation for inference because the marginal distribution governing the random partitions was unknown. This paper fills that gap, enabling the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that does not require truncation.

The authors' approach is to first define a sample-size-dependent model where the number of data points in each group is random. For this model, they derive an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF), which describes the joint distribution of the random group sizes and their partitions. Using the ECPF and Bayes' rule, they then derive the EPPF for the case where group sizes are fixed and observed. While the resulting EPPF is complex, the authors derive a simple and elegant predictive rule from it. As a practical application, they construct a BNBP topic model and provide a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. They compare this model empirically to the hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP) topic model, a standard approach for this problem.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations that lead from the BNBP definition to the ECPF and finally to the EPPF and the predictive rule appear correct and are well-motivated. The augmentation of the BNBP as a beta-gamma-Poisson process provides a clear path for the derivation. The connection between the prior on the random count matrix and the digamma and Dirichlet-multinomial distributions (Equation 8) is insightful and builds upon recent related work. The derivation of the fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for the BNBP topic model is a direct and valuable result of the theoretical development.

The experimental evaluation supports the claims about the sampler's properties. The trace plots in Figure 2 provide clear evidence of faster convergence and better mixing compared to the direct assignment sampler for HDP-LDA. The perplexity results in Figure 3 are presented honestly, showing that while HDP-LDA can sometimes achieve lower perplexity (by using more topics), the BNBP model offers a more compact representation and can achieve lower perplexity than HDP-LDA when the number of inferred topics is comparable. This demonstrates a careful evaluation of the model's strengths.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly situates the work within the context of nonparametric Bayesian models, EPPFs, and mixed-membership modeling, explicitly stating the limitations of prior work that this paper resolves. The progression of ideas in Section 2—from the BNBP definition to the ECPF, the random count matrix prior, the EPPF, and the predictive rule—is logical. Section 3.1, which directly compares the update equations of the BNBP topic model with those of LDA and HDP-LDA, is particularly effective at highlighting the unique multiplicative shrinkage mechanism of the proposed model. The experimental setup and results are described clearly, enabling the reader to understand the evaluation process and its outcomes.

One area where clarity could be slightly improved is in the interpretation of the EPPF itself (Equation 9). The expression is formidable, and while the authors correctly state that the simple predictive rule (Equation 10) is what matters for implementation, a brief intuitive explanation of the terms in the EPPF could have been beneficial.

**Originality**
The work is highly original. The central contribution—the derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP for mixed-membership models—is a novel theoretical result that solves a known open problem. The method used to achieve this, by first considering a model with random group sizes (ECPF) and then conditioning on the observed sizes, is a clever and potentially generalizable technique for deriving EPPFs for other integer-valued stochastic processes.

While the BNBP itself is not new, this paper is the first to provide a framework for fully collapsed, truncation-free inference in the mixed-membership setting. This distinguishes it significantly from prior BNBP literature that used truncation or slice sampling. The resulting BNBP topic model is a novel nonparametric alternative to HDP-LDA with a fundamentally different mechanism for sharing statistical strength across groups (multiplicative vs. additive), which is a valuable contribution to the topic modeling literature.

**Significance**
The results of this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian nonparametrics and machine learning.
1.  **Theoretical Advance:** It provides a unified treatment for the BNBP in the context of mixed-membership modeling, analogous to what the Chinese restaurant process provides for the Dirichlet process. This enhances our understanding of the combinatorial structures underlying these models.
2.  **Practical Algorithm:** The paper delivers a practical and easy-to-implement fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The demonstrated advantages in convergence speed and mixing over the HDP-LDA sampler are compelling practical benefits.
3.  **New Modeling Tool:** The BNBP topic model offers a new tool for document analysis and other mixed-membership tasks. Its tendency to produce more compact representations while maintaining competitive predictive power makes it an attractive alternative to HDP-LDA, particularly in applications where model interpretability and parsimony are important. The distinct shrinkage properties may be better suited for certain data types.

The work opens up avenues for future research, including applying the derivation technique to other processes like the gamma-Poisson process, and exploring the properties of the multiplicative shrinkage mechanism further.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting.
*   Resolves a key limitation of previous work on the BNBP, enabling a practical, fully collapsed Gibbs sampler.
*   The proposed BNBP topic model is a compelling and well-motivated alternative to the widely used HDP-LDA.
*   Empirical results demonstrate clear advantages in terms of convergence speed, mixing, and the ability to find compact yet powerful representations.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The claim of "state-of-the-art" predictive performance could be interpreted as overly strong, as the HDP-LDA achieves lower perplexity in some settings (albeit with more topics). A more nuanced discussion of the perplexity-vs-model-complexity trade-off could strengthen this point.
*   The experimental comparison is limited to one type of sampler for HDP-LDA.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and practical significance, far outweigh the minor weaknesses in the experimental section. This is a complete and high-quality piece of work that represents a clear advance in the field.
