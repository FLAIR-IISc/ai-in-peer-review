

This paper introduces a novel exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) to address mixed-membership modeling, extending the EPPF framework beyond single-set mixture modeling. The authors develop a group-size dependent EPPF that explicitly describes how BNBP clusters data points across groups, which is a significant gap in the literature since prior work on integer-valued stochastic processes (e.g., HDP, BNBP) lacked this marginal distribution. The paper builds on Pitman's EPPF framework [5] and Zhou et al.'s BNBP work [10,11], but uniquely generalizes to grouped data with a method that randomizes group sizes before deriving the EPPF.

The strengths are substantial. The paper demonstrates high technical quality with rigorous theoretical derivations of the EPPF, prediction rule, and fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The BNBP topic model shows clear advantages: it converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance with a more compact topic representation compared to HDP-LDA. The clarity is excellent - the mathematical framework is well-structured, with sufficient detail for reproduction. The originality is strong: the group-size dependent EPPF derivation method is novel, and the BNBP topic model represents a distinct approach from existing nonparametric topic models. The significance is high as it fills a critical gap in mixed-membership modeling theory and provides practical benefits for applications like topic modeling.

Minor weaknesses include limited discussion of computational complexity comparisons with other methods beyond empirical timing, and the paper could better contextualize how the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism compares to other nonparametric priors beyond HDP. However, these are minor given the paper's strong contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of mixed-membership modeling while delivering practical benefits. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (quality), well-written (clarity), offers a novel EPPF derivation method (originality), and provides significant improvements for topic modeling applications (significance). The work is clearly within scope for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling with broad applicability. I strongly recommend acceptance.
