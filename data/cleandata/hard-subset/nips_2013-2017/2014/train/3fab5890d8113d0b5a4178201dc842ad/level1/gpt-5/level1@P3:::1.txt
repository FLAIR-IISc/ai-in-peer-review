Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in the mixed-membership setting, where data are grouped and within-group items are exchangeable. The key idea is to start from the beta–gamma–Poisson augmentation of the BNBP, treat per-group sizes as random to obtain a joint distribution over group sizes and partitions (an exchangeable cluster probability function, ECPF), marginalize the beta process, then condition back on observed group sizes via Bayes’ rule to obtain a group-size-dependent EPPF over a latent column-exchangeable count matrix. From this, the authors derive a simple prediction rule enabling fully collapsed Gibbs sampling without truncating the beta process. They instantiate these ideas in a nonparametric topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, and compare against LDA and HDP-LDA.

This work is positioned against: (i) classic EPPFs for mixture modeling of a single sample (e.g., Pitman’s framework, CRP), (ii) mixed-membership nonparametric priors such as the HDP (Teh et al., 2006), and (iii) BNBP/GNBP/gamma–Poisson processes for counts (e.g., Zhou & Carin; Titsias). Related efforts that marginalize the beta process in count-feature settings (negative-binomial IBP, “ice cream” buffet) consider feature allocations over infinite random count matrices; here the focus is partitions of tokens within each group and a grouped-data EPPF. The approach also connects to sample-size-dependent species models (Zhou & Walker) and Poisson–Kingman families (Lomelí et al.), but BNBP is outside the latter class.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A clear, principled route to a group-size-dependent EPPF for the BNBP, via ECPF and count-matrix marginalization, including finite-dimensional distributions and a compact prediction rule. This fills an acknowledged gap for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Inference: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that integrates out topics, topic weights, and the beta process—unlike HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler which is only partially collapsed. The multiplicative popularity term provides an interesting contrast to the HDP’s additive shrinkage.
- Empirical: Experiments on three corpora demonstrate faster convergence and better mixing than HDP-LDA, and favorable perplexity when matched on effective number of topics, suggesting more compact representations for comparable predictive power.
- Clarity of probabilistic structure: The augmentation and its link from count modeling (Poisson factorization) to mixture modeling is well explained and insightful.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Sample-size dependence and exchangeability: The EPPF explicitly depends on group sizes. While the authors cite and generalize sample-size-dependent EPPFs, more discussion is needed on projectivity and predictive coherence when conditioning on different subsets/sizes, and on the practical implications for exchangeability assumptions in grouped data.
- Scope of comparisons: The empirical comparison is limited to HDP-LDA’s direct assignment sampler. It would be stronger to include the collapsed Chinese Restaurant Franchise sampler for HDP (and/or modern efficient implementations), and to compare with slice/adaptive truncation BNBP/GNBP samplers, and Poisson factorization baselines, to substantiate claims about mixing and computational advantages.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: The concentration c for the beta process appears fixed (no prior/updates shown), and sensitivity to c and γ0 is not explored. Since these govern the number/size of clusters via ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c), empirical robustness to these choices should be discussed. The study also varies η widely; some choices (e.g., 0.001) induce very large topic counts and may favor certain models; it would help to present results under more standard priors and report the inferred K alongside perplexity systematically.
- Perplexity computation: The predictive probability expression normalizes by a sum over s, v, k; because the BNBP uses unnormalized Poisson weights, the normalization needs to be per document and per sample. Please clarify exactly how probabilities are computed (e.g., document-wise normalization per MCMC sample) to ensure a fair comparison to HDP-LDA.
- Reproducibility and details: Pseudocode for the collapsed sampler and explicit update equations (beyond the assignment rule) would aid reproduction. Likewise, quantitative tables (means and variances of perplexities, K, runtimes) would make the empirical claims more transparent than figures alone.
- Theoretical insight: The prediction rule is elegant; however, more intuition about the “digamma” column-size distribution and its role relative to NB/DirMult components would help readers, as would a brief note on when the multiplicative shrinkage is expected to outperform additive HDP shrinkage.

Quality:
The derivations are sound and connect cleanly to known augmentations; proofs are deferred but the main steps are transparent. The experimental methodology is standard for topic modeling, though additional baselines and reporting would strengthen the case. Claims about “state-of-the-art” should be tempered or substantiated with broader comparisons.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but largely well organized. The ECPF→EPPF pathway is clear. Notation is heavy; a small notational table and a concise algorithm box would improve readability. Clarify the role/setting/inference of c, and the normalization in predictive likelihoods.

Originality:
High. A group-size-dependent EPPF for BNBP mixed-membership and a fully collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process for token-level partitions are, to my knowledge, novel.

Significance:
High. The approach provides a new tool for mixed-membership models beyond HDP, with potential impact in topic modeling and other grouped-count applications. The general recipe (integer-valued process → ECPF → EPPF) could inspire analogous developments for gamma–Poisson and GNBP.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid development of an EPPF for the BNBP in grouped settings.
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free inference with clear practical benefits.
- Empirical evidence of faster mixing and competitive or better perplexity at comparable model sizes.
- Generalizable methodology with potential broader impact.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical baselines (no collapsed HDP comparisons; no slice/blocked BNBP/GNBP baselines).
- Missing sensitivity analysis and unclear treatment of key hyperparameters (c).
- Some aspects of sample-size dependence and predictive normalization need clearer exposition.

Suggestions:
- Add a collapsed HDP-LDA (CRF) baseline and at least one Poisson factorization baseline.
- Report quantitative summaries (perplexity, K, runtime) with variance across splits.
- Include sensitivity to c and γ0, and discuss priors/updates for c.
- Provide pseudocode and a brief algorithmic complexity discussion.
- Expand the discussion of exchangeability/projectivity under group-size dependence.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution; with the suggested clarifications and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be well suited for NIPS.
