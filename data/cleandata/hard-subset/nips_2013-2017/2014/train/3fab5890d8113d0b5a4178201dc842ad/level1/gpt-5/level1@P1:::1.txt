Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops the marginal law governing exchangeable partitions induced by the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) in mixed-membership settings. Whereas the Dirichlet process (DP) admits a marginal EPPF (Chinese Restaurant Process) enabling fully collapsed inference in mixture modeling, analogous partition laws for integer-valued processes like the BNBP have been lacking for grouped (mixed-membership) data. The authors proceed by (i) introducing a group-size dependent construction that randomizes each group size mj via a Poisson-gamma augmentation of the BNBP, (ii) deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that jointly models group sizes and partitions (Eq. 6), (iii) obtaining an EPPF for fixed group sizes by conditioning via Bayes’ rule (Eq. 9), and (iv) deriving a simple prediction rule (Eq. 10) that makes simulation and collapsed Gibbs sampling straightforward.

They show that the induced latent count matrix has i.i.d. columns with column counts drawn from a “digamma” distribution and group allocations drawn from a Dirichlet-multinomial (Eq. 8), implying KJ ∼ Pois{γ0[ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c)]}. Building on this, they propose a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a nonparametric BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the beta process, yielding a concise update (Eq. 12). They compare to LDA and to HDP-LDA (direct-assignment) and report faster mixing/convergence and competitive or better perplexity at comparable numbers of topics on three corpora.

This contribution connects to prior NIPS work on DP/HDP and EPPFs (Blackwell–MacQueen; Pitman; Teh et al.), on integer-valued processes and Poisson factorization (Lo; Titsias; Zhou et al.), and on IBP-like constructions for BNBP-related feature models (Griffiths & Ghahramani; Heaukulani & Roy). Distinct from “negative-binomial IBP”/“ice-cream buffet” work (feature allocations over infinite random count matrices), this paper targets partitions of grouped data with fixed group sizes and delivers an EPPF/prediction rule enabling fully collapsed inference.

Quality
- Theory: The augmentation from BNBP to a beta–gamma–Poisson hierarchy is standard and carefully used to derive a factorized joint (Eq. 4) and the ECPF (Eq. 6). The matrix prior factorization (Eq. 8) and Poisson law for KJ appear correct and insightful. The EPPF (Eq. 9) is sample-size dependent (explicitly acknowledged and related to Zhou & Walker, 2014), and the prediction rule (Eq. 10) is simple and interpretable. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; the main steps are plausible and align with known conjugacies.
- Inference: The topic-model sampler (Eq. 12) is fully collapsed, a practical advantage over HDP direct-assignment samplers (which do not marginalize α and often not G). Empirically, the chains mix faster and converge quickly.
- Experiments: Three corpora, multiple η settings, standard perplexity evaluation with multiple posterior samples and 5 splits. Comparisons focus on HDP-LDA (direct assignment). Results support claims on convergence and competitive perplexity with fewer topics.
- Minor concerns: Sensitivity to c and γ0 is not explored (γ0 is inferred; c appears fixed). Baselines omit fully collapsed HDP-CRF samplers. Evaluation is confined to text.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The key idea—randomize group sizes to derive an ECPF, then condition back to an EPPF—is clear and useful.
- Suggestions:
  - Clarify terminology around EPPF: traditionally sample-size independent; here it is explicitly sample-size dependent. Make the scope and implications (e.g., addition rule) prominent earlier and connect to [Pitman, 2006] and [Zhou & Walker, 2014] with a short discussion on modeling consequences.
  - Define the “digamma distribution” more carefully (name can be confused with the digamma function); a brief intuition for Eq. (8) would help readers.
  - Provide a concise pseudocode block for the collapsed sampler for reproducibility.
  - Fix minor typos (“perdition” → “prediction”), unify notation (KJ vs K), and streamline the explanation around column exchangeability.

Originality
- Deriving an ECPF/EPPF and prediction rule for the BNBP in mixed-membership settings is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The method of going via a group-size dependent model to a conditioned EPPF is a valuable general recipe potentially applicable to other count processes (gamma–Poisson, GNBP).
- The fully collapsed sampler that also marginalizes the beta process is novel and practically important.

Significance
- Theoretical: Provides a missing marginal characterization bridging integer-valued processes and mixed-membership EPPFs; likely to influence future work on nonparametric count/mixed-membership models.
- Practical: The sampler is simple, converges quickly, and yields compact topic representations with competitive perplexity. The work could spur extensions to other modalities (e.g., networks, counts with covariates).
- Limitations: Empirical scope is limited to topic modeling; broader tasks or additional baselines (e.g., HDP-CRF, Pitman–Yor variants) would strengthen the case.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please discuss sensitivity to c and hyperpriors on γ0 and rj; consider placing a prior on c and sampling it.
- Compare run-time and mixing against a collapsed HDP-CRF sampler (even if bookkeeping is heavier) to isolate gains due to full marginalization vs model differences.
- Explore inferring η (or a hierarchical prior) to avoid overfitting at very small η and to make comparisons more balanced.
- Empirically validate the Poisson law for KJ by comparing observed KJ to γ0[ψ(c + r·) − ψ(c)] across runs.
- Clarify exchangeability structure: within-group exchangeability of data; column exchangeability across clusters; behavior under adding/removing groups.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid derivation of ECPF/EPPF and a usable prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling.
- Fully collapsed sampler that is simple, fast, and mixes well.
- Clear empirical benefits in convergence and compactness; competitive predictive performance.
- General methodology likely extensible to other integer-valued processes.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation limited in scope and baselines (no collapsed HDP-CRF or PYP comparisons; only text).
- Sensitivity to some hyperparameters (notably c) not investigated; η fixed rather than inferred.
- Some notation/terminology could be clarified; proofs in appendix limit accessibility.

Recommendation
Accept. Strong theoretical contribution filling a known gap, with a practical sampler and solid empirical evidence. My confidence is high.
