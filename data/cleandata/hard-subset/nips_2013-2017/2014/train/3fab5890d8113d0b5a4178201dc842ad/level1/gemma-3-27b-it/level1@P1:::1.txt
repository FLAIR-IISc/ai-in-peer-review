This paper introduces an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), enabling fully collapsed Gibbs sampling for a novel nonparametric Bayesian topic model. The motivation stems from the lack of marginal distributions for the BNBP, hindering its use in mixed-membership modeling where data is grouped. The authors address this by deriving an EPPF, providing a prediction rule, and demonstrating the efficacy of the resulting topic model through experiments.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of nonparametric Bayesian methods, referencing key papers on Dirichlet processes [1, 3], exchangeable partition probability functions [5], and the hierarchical Dirichlet process [6]. The authors correctly identify the gap in existing literature regarding the marginal distributions of integer-valued stochastic processes like the BNBP [10, 11] needed for fully collapsed inference.  The connection to related work such as the negative binomial Indian buffet process [16] and “ice cream” buffet process [17] is appropriately acknowledged.  The paper carefully positions itself within the broader context of mixture modeling and mixed-membership modeling, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant contribution. It addresses a key limitation of the BNBP and opens the door for more efficient and scalable inference. The proposed method for deriving the EPPF via a group size dependent model appears general and potentially applicable to other integer-valued stochastic processes.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical development of the EPPF, with clear definitions and derivations. The Gibbs sampling algorithm is well-defined, and the accompanying equations are complete and appear correct. The Lemma proofs, though deferred to the Appendix, are crucial for establishing the validity of the approach.
* **Completeness:** The paper is relatively complete. It defines the problem, proposes a solution, develops the necessary theoretical framework, and provides empirical validation. The inclusion of closed-form update equations and convergence analysis strengthens the practical utility of the work.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments on standard topic modeling datasets (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS) demonstrate the competitive performance of the proposed BNBP topic model, particularly in terms of perplexity and convergence speed.  The comparison to HDP-LDA [6, 23] is relevant and informative.  The observations about the different shrinkage mechanisms are insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections are quite dense with mathematical notation. The introduction effectively motivates the work and provides context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Density:** While the technical rigor is a strength, the extensive mathematical notation can be a barrier to entry for readers unfamiliar with the field. More intuitive explanations of key concepts would be beneficial.  For example, the interplay between the ECPF and EPPF could be explained with simpler language.
* **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameters:** While the authors mention setting hyperparameters (a0, b0, e0, f0), the rationale behind these choices isn’t fully explained.  A sensitivity analysis of the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings would be valuable.  The effect of the η parameter is discussed, but a more thorough investigation would be ideal.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** The paper notes that the Gibbs sampler is comparable in complexity to HDP-LDA, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (e.g., scaling with corpus size and vocabulary size) would be helpful. It would be beneficial to compare the runtime profiles of the two samplers.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation focuses solely on topic modeling. While this is a valid application, demonstrating the applicability of the EPPF to other mixed-membership modeling problems (e.g., community detection, image segmentation) would further strengthen the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a significant problem in nonparametric Bayesian modeling.
* The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a substantial contribution to the field.
* The experimental results demonstrate the competitive performance of the proposed model.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, despite its technical density.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical density of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The discussion of hyperparameters and computational cost could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP is a significant achievement, and the resulting topic model performs well in experiments. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, they are outweighed by its strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and scope of the experiments and hyperparameter discussion. Specifically, I suggest adding more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts, performing a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters, and considering experiments on additional mixed-membership modeling tasks.
