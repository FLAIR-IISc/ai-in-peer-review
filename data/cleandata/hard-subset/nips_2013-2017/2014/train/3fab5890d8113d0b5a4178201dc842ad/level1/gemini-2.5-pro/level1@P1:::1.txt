This paper addresses the problem of inference in mixed-membership models based on the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP). The primary contribution is the derivation of the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the BNBP, which describes the marginal distribution over partitions of grouped data after integrating out the infinite-dimensional beta process. This result enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler, avoiding the need for truncation or other approximations common in previous work on BNBP-based models. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework by constructing a BNBP topic model and comparing it empirically to the Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), a state-of-the-art approach for nonparametric topic modeling.

The work is well-situated within the context of nonparametric Bayesian methods. It builds on the foundational concept of EPPFs, as established by Pitman and others, which provide a powerful tool for deriving collapsed inference schemes for models based on processes like the Dirichlet Process. The paper's focus on mixed-membership modeling places it in direct conversation with the HDP [Teh et al., JASA 2006], which has been a dominant model in this space. While the BNBP has been previously proposed for count and mixture modeling [Zhou et al., PAMI 2012; AISTATS 2012], inference has been a key challenge. This paper makes a significant leap forward by providing the missing theoretical machinery for a fully collapsed sampler, moving beyond the truncated inference of prior work.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership context is a novel and significant theoretical contribution. It solves a known open problem and provides a new, principled tool for Bayesian nonparametrics. The technical approach—first deriving an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF) for a model with random group sizes, then using Bayes' rule to obtain the EPPF for fixed group sizes—is clever and potentially generalizable to other integer-valued stochastic processes. This work provides a more unified view of mixed-membership modeling, analogous to what the EPPF framework provides for simple mixture modeling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The derivation proceeds logically through a series of lemmas, culminating in a simple and elegant predictive distribution (Equation 10) for Gibbs sampling. The resulting BNBP topic model sampler (Equation 12) is fully collapsed, marginalizing out all infinite-dimensional and continuous random variables. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard datasets, appropriate metrics (held-out perplexity), and comparison against the most relevant and powerful baseline (HDP-LDA). The analysis of MCMC convergence and mixing (Figure 2) is particularly compelling and provides clear evidence for the practical advantages of the proposed sampler.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the problem, the limitations of prior work, and the nature of its contributions. The direct comparison of the Gibbs update equations for BNBP, LDA, and HDP (Equations 12-14) is highly effective at highlighting the core difference in how these models operate. The distinction between the BNBP's multiplicative sharing mechanism and the HDP's additive one is explained clearly and provides valuable intuition for the empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of Derivation:** While the overall logic is clear, the derivation of the EPPF (Lemma 3) from the ECPF (Lemma 2) in Section 2.2 is quite condensed. The EPPF is presented as the ECPF divided by a complex normalization constant (the marginal probability of the count vector **m**). While the resulting prediction rule (Lemma 4) is simple, a bit more exposition in the main text on how this normalization constant is handled or cancels out during the derivation of the predictive probability would enhance the paper's self-containedness and clarity for readers trying to follow the technical details.

2.  **Discussion of Model Properties:** The experiments convincingly show that the BNBP topic model favors more compact representations (fewer topics) than the HDP for a given hyperparameter setting. The paper attributes this to the multiplicative shrinkage mechanism. This analysis could be strengthened by a deeper qualitative discussion on the implications. For what kinds of corpora or data structures would this stronger shrinkage be particularly beneficial or potentially detrimental? A more nuanced discussion of the modeling biases inherent in the multiplicative vs. additive structures would add significant value.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The paper states that the computational complexity is "comparable" to the HDP-LDA direct assignment sampler. While this is plausible, a plot of runtime per iteration versus the number of inferred topics for both models would provide more concrete evidence to support this claim.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a significant theoretical breakthrough by deriving the EPPF for the BNBP, enabling the first fully collapsed sampler for this class of models.
*   **Pro:** The resulting inference algorithm is simple to implement, converges remarkably fast, and mixes well compared to the widely used HDP sampler.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are strong, demonstrating that the BNBP topic model can achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance, often with a more compact latent representation.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very well-written and provides clear intuition about the differences between the BNBP and HDP.
*   **Con:** Some of the key theoretical steps in the main text are brief, relying heavily on the appendix.
*   **Con:** The discussion on the practical implications of the different model shrinkage properties could be more extensive.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and its applications. The combination of a novel theoretical result with a high-performing, practical algorithm is precisely what a top-tier conference like NIPS looks for. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core value of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
