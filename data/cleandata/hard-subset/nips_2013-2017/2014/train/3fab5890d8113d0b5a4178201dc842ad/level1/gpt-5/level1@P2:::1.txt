Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the beta–negative binomial process (BNBP) as a prior for mixed-membership modeling of grouped data. Prior work (Dirichlet process, NRMIs, and EPPFs such as the CRP) yielded collapsed inference for mixture models of a single set, and the HDP introduced the Chinese restaurant franchise to handle grouped data. For integer-valued stochastic processes like the gamma–Poisson, BNBP, and gamma–negative binomial processes, previous inference typically required truncation or slice sampling because marginal laws governing exchangeable random partitions of grouped data were unknown. This paper fills that gap for the BNBP by (i) deriving an exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) that jointly describes random group sizes and their partitions, (ii) integrating out the beta process to obtain a prior on a latent column-exchangeable random count matrix with a Poisson number of i.i.d. columns whose sizes follow a “digamma” distribution and within-column allocations follow a Dirichlet–multinomial, and (iii) using Bayes’ rule to get a group-size dependent EPPF and a simple prediction rule. Building on this, the authors develop a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for a BNBP topic model that marginalizes topics, topic weights, and the infinite beta process. Empirically, on three corpora, the sampler mixes quickly and achieves competitive or better perplexity than HDP-LDA for comparable numbers of topics, often using fewer topics.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: The derivation of an EPPF/prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling is novel and addresses a recognized limitation of count-process priors. The ECPF→EPPF path via group-size randomization is a neat, generalizable technique.
- Inference: A fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes the beta process is practically valuable; the prediction rule is simple and interpretable. No truncation needed.
- Conceptual clarity: The paper cleanly links the BNBP’s augmented beta–gamma–Poisson representation to mixed-membership structure and elucidates the multiplicative global/local popularity term, contrasting it to HDP’s additive mechanism.
- Empirical evidence: Faster mixing and convergence than a direct-assignment HDP-LDA baseline; competitive perplexity with more compact topic usage. Computational cost per iteration reasonable.
- Potential impact: Provides a general recipe likely extensible to other integer-valued processes.

Weaknesses
- Scope of comparisons: Experiments focus on HDP-LDA (direct assignment). A fully collapsed CRF sampler baseline, or comparisons to related count-process models (e.g., gamma–Poisson/gamma–negative binomial) would strengthen claims.
- Evaluation breadth: Perplexity is the sole metric; no qualitative topic analysis, wall-clock convergence comparisons, or memory/scalability results beyond iteration timing.
- Sensitivity: Results hinge strongly on η; limited discussion of sensitivity to c, γ0, and priors on rj. The practical guidance for setting/learning these is brief.
- Theoretical framing: The EPPF is group-size dependent (sample-size dependent), which deviates from classic EPPFs; while acknowledged, the implications for exchangeability and for posterior predictive across changing group sizes could be explained more thoroughly.
- Clarity and notation: The manuscript is dense. Some definitions (e.g., “digamma distribution”) are nonstandard and could use more intuition and properties. A running toy example would aid understanding.

Assessment by review criteria
Quality: The technical development appears sound. The chain from ECPF to matrix prior to EPPF and prediction rule is coherent, and the fully collapsed sampler is correctly derived by marginalizing topics, weights, and the beta process. Proof details are deferred to the appendix; from what is presented, the arguments are plausible. Empirical methodology follows common topic-model evaluation practice; however, additional baselines and ablations would improve robustness.

Clarity: Generally well written but heavy. The key ideas are present and well motivated, yet notation is dense and some steps (e.g., normalization in the EPPF denominator, construction and properties of the “digamma” PMF) could be explained more intuitively. Suggestions:
- Add an algorithm box for the collapsed sampler with all updates.
- Include a small synthetic example to illustrate the prediction rule and the effect of rj.
- Clarify the role of sample-size dependence in the EPPF and its implications for prediction in new groups.

Originality: High. To my knowledge, an EPPF and prediction rule for BNBP mixed-membership modeling had not been available, and the derivation technique is new relative to HDP/CRF treatments. The collapsed sampler that marginalizes the beta process for topics is also a useful contribution.

Significance: High. This work enables truncation-free, collapsed inference for a prominent count-based nonparametric prior in mixed-membership settings, with practical impact in topic modeling and potentially other grouped count problems. The modeling distinction (multiplicative shrinkage) is meaningful and could inspire further methods and theory.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide results with a collapsed HDP-LDA (CRF) sampler to control for inference differences.
- Report wall-clock to reach a target perplexity and effective sample size diagnostics to substantiate mixing claims.
- Analyze sensitivity to c, γ0, and the Gamma prior on rj; show posterior of rj and its effect on topic usage dispersion.
- Clarify the “digamma distribution”: discuss tail behavior, moments, and provide sampling details.
- Discuss extensibility of the ECPF→EPPF method to gamma–Poisson and GNBP; what obstacles arise?

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid derivation of BNBP EPPF/prediction rule for mixed-membership.
- Fully collapsed, truncation-free sampler with clear practical benefits.
- Empirical evidence of good mixing and competitive or better predictive performance with compact topic allocations.
- Likely to stimulate further work on integer-valued processes in mixed-membership modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental baselines and metrics; absence of a collapsed HDP comparison.
- Heavy notation and some nonstandard distributions may hinder accessibility.
- Reliance on sample-size dependent EPPF may raise conceptual questions not fully addressed.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper makes a substantive theoretical and algorithmic contribution with clear practical implications. Strengthening the experimental comparison and adding clarifications would make it even more compelling.
