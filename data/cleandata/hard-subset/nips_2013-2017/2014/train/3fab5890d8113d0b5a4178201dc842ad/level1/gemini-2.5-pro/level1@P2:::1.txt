### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian methods for mixed-membership modeling. The central focus is on the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), an integer-valued stochastic process that can be used as an alternative to the more conventional hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP). The main problem addressed is that prior work on BNBP-based models lacked a "fully collapsed" inference scheme, relying instead on truncation or slice sampling, which can be inefficient or approximate.

The key contribution is the derivation of the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP in the context of grouped data. This EPPF explicitly describes the prior distribution over partitions of data within multiple groups, where partitions (clusters) are shared across groups. The authors achieve this through a novel and elegant technique: they first define a model where group sizes are random variables and derive the joint distribution of these sizes and their partitions (termed the exchangeable cluster probability function, or ECPF). By conditioning on the observed, fixed group sizes via Bayes' rule, they then arrive at the desired EPPF.

This theoretical result enables the development of a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP-based mixed-membership models. The authors demonstrate this by constructing a BNBP topic model. The resulting sampler is simple to implement, analytically marginalizing out the infinite-dimensional beta process. The paper provides an insightful comparison of the derived sampling equations to those of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) and the HDP, highlighting a key difference: the BNBP employs a multiplicative interaction between a topic's global popularity and its local popularity within a document, whereas the HDP uses an additive one. Experimental results on three text corpora show that the BNBP sampler converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive perplexity, often with a more compact topic representation than the HDP.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations, while dense and largely relegated to the appendix, appear sound and lead to a coherent and elegant result in the form of the predictive probability rule (Equation 10). The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical development and the empirical evaluation. The experimental methodology is standard for evaluating topic models, using held-out perplexity on several benchmark datasets. The authors are careful to evaluate performance across a range of hyperparameters, providing a nuanced view of the model's behavior. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a theoretical result, a practical algorithm derived from it, and a thorough empirical validation against the primary state-of-the-art competitor (HDP-LDA).

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem by situating the BNBP in the context of existing nonparametric priors like the Dirichlet Process and HDP. The step-by-step progression from the BNBP definition to the ECPF, EPPF, and the final Gibbs sampler is logical. A particular strength is the direct comparison of the conditional probability equations (12, 13, 14), which makes the distinction between the BNBP, LDA, and HDP models crystal clear.

One minor point for improvement would be to provide more intuition in the main text for the form of the ECPF (Lemma 2). While the proof is in the appendix, a brief conceptual explanation of where the various Gamma functions and other terms arise from could help the reader build a stronger intuition without having to dive into the full derivation immediately. However, given the space constraints of a conference paper, the current presentation is understandable and effective.

### Originality

The paper is highly original. The primary contribution—the derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP for mixed-membership modeling—is novel and fills a significant gap in the literature. While the BNBP itself was not new, understanding its marginal partition structure in this setting is a fundamental advance. The method used for the derivation (via the ECPF for random group sizes) is also creative and may be applicable to other integer-valued processes.

The resulting BNBP topic model is a novel contribution that is clearly distinguished from previous work. It provides a new nonparametric alternative to HDP-LDA with a distinct shrinkage mechanism. The work adequately references related literature, including prior work on the BNBP, EPPFs, and HDP.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant.
1.  **Theoretical Significance:** It provides a deeper understanding of the combinatorial properties of the BNBP, placing it on a more solid theoretical foundation comparable to that of the Dirichlet process and its Chinese Restaurant Process representation. This opens the door for further theoretical analysis of this class of models.
2.  **Practical Significance:** It delivers a simple, efficient, and high-performing algorithm for a very common task (topic modeling/mixed-membership modeling). The fully collapsed sampler is a substantial improvement over previous inference methods for the BNBP and offers clear advantages in convergence and mixing over commonly used HDP samplers. Researchers and practitioners are likely to use this model and sampler as a strong alternative to the HDP, particularly when faster convergence and more compact models are desired. The work demonstrably advances the state of the art.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant theoretical result: the EPPF for the BNBP in a mixed-membership setting.
*   The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is elegant, simple to implement, and practically useful.
*   Empirical results are strong, showing clear advantages over the standard HDP-LDA in terms of convergence speed, mixing, and the ability to find compact, high-quality models.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides valuable insights by comparing the BNBP's mechanism to that of the HDP.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation, while solid, is confined to topic modeling and perplexity. An exploration of the model on other types of grouped data or with other evaluation metrics (e.g., topic coherence) could have broadened the paper's impact.
*   The sensitivity to certain hyperparameters of the beta process, such as the concentration parameter `c`, is not fully explored in the experiments.

The arguments for acceptance overwhelmingly outweigh the minor arguments against. The paper makes a fundamental contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics with clear practical benefits. It is an excellent fit for NIPS.
