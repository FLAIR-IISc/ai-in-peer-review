

# Peer Review: Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theory of nonparametric Bayesian modeling by deriving an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP), which governs exchangeable random partitions of grouped data. Previous work on mixed-membership modeling (e.g., HDP [6]) has struggled with integer-valued stochastic processes like BNBP that cannot be directly marginalized like the Dirichlet process. The authors address this gap by first developing a group size-dependent model, then using Bayes' rule to obtain the EPPF for observed group sizes, which had not been previously established for BNBP.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial. Lemma 2 provides the exchangeable cluster probability function (ECPF) for BNBP, while Lemma 3 derives the EPPF (equation 9) and Lemma 4 establishes a simple prediction rule (equation 10). This method to derive EPPFs for mixed-membership modeling via group size-dependent models is novel and generalizable. The BNBP topic model (Section 3) is a natural application that demonstrates the utility of this theoretical advance, with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that marginalizes out the beta process, topics, and topic weights.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous derivations supported by proofs in the appendix. The theoretical development is complete and carefully addresses the limitations of previous approaches. The empirical evaluation on three standard topic modeling corpora (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) provides strong evidence of the BNBP topic model's advantages: faster convergence (Figure 2), better mixing, and competitive predictive performance (Figure 3). The authors appropriately acknowledge that the BNBP's multiplicative shrinkage mechanism (equation 12) leads to fewer topics than HDP-LDA, which can be advantageous for compact representation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical development is presented logically, with clear explanations of the theoretical contributions. The connection between the BNBP's properties and its practical application to topic modeling is particularly well-explained. The presentation of the prediction rule (equation 10) and its intuitive interpretation is especially effective.

**Originality:** This work makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. While previous papers [10, 11, 16, 17] explored the BNBP for infinite random count matrices, none derived the EPPF for mixed-membership modeling. The authors explicitly differentiate their work from related NIPS papers, including their own [7, 10, 11], and establish a clear theoretical advance. The method to derive EPPFs via group size-dependent models is novel and could be applied to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in the literature on nonparametric Bayesian mixed-membership modeling. The BNBP topic model provides a practical alternative to HDP-LDA with demonstrable advantages in convergence speed and model compactness. The empirical results (Figure 3) show that the BNBP model achieves similar or better predictive performance with fewer topics, which is particularly valuable for practical applications. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is straightforward to implement and outperforms existing methods in terms of computational efficiency.

**Strengths:** The theoretical contribution of deriving the BNBP's EPPF is substantial and fills a critical gap in the literature. The connection to mixed-membership modeling is well-motivated and clearly explained. The empirical results are convincing and address a practical concern (convergence speed) that is often overlooked in theoretical papers. The paper's clarity and mathematical precision are exceptional.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper demonstrates clear advantages over HDP-LDA, it would be strengthened by comparing with more recent topic models from the NIPS 2013-2014 proceedings (e.g., [26, 27, 28]). The claim of "state-of-the-art predictive performance" is somewhat vague without direct comparisons to the most recent topic modeling approaches. The paper could also elaborate on when the BNBP would be preferred over other nonparametric models (e.g., the hierarchical gamma process [26]).

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is a significant advance that fills a gap in the literature, and the resulting topic model demonstrates clear advantages in implementation and convergence properties. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to include more recent baselines, the core theoretical contribution is substantial and well-supported.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:** The paper solves a fundamental problem in mixed-membership modeling by deriving the EPPF for BNBP, a theoretically important advance that enables fully collapsed inference. The resulting topic model is practical, easy to implement, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods in convergence and model compactness.

**Con Acceptance Arguments:** The empirical comparison is limited to HDP-LDA, and the paper could benefit from more direct comparisons to the most recent topic modeling approaches from the NIPS 2013-2014 proceedings.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian modeling. It advances the theoretical understanding of mixed-membership modeling and provides a practical algorithm with demonstrable advantages. I recommend acceptance.
