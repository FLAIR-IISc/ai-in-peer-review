

# Peer Review: Beta-Negative Binomial Process and Exchangeable Random Partitions for Mixed-Membership Modeling

This paper introduces the beta-negative binomial process (BNBP) as a novel integer-valued stochastic process for mixed-membership modeling, where data is grouped and each group consists of exchangeable data points. The authors develop an exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF) for the BNBP, which had not been previously developed for mixed-membership modeling. This is a significant gap in the literature, as previous work on mixed-membership modeling (like the hierarchical Dirichlet process - HDP) lacked a fully collapsed inference mechanism for BNBP. The paper then derives a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler for BNBP and applies it to topic modeling, creating a novel nonparametric Bayesian topic model. The authors relate their work to previous NIPS contributions on exchangeable partition probability functions (EPPFs) [5, 12, 17], showing how their approach generalizes these methods to mixed-membership settings.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it develops a method to derive an EPPF for mixed-membership modeling using a group-size dependent model, which is a novel technique with potential applicability to other stochastic processes. Second, it provides a prediction rule for simulating exchangeable random partitions governed by the BNBP's EPPF. Third, it constructs a BNBP topic model with a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler that analytically marginalizes out both the topics and the infinite-dimensional beta process, providing closed-form update equations. The experimental results on three standard topic modeling datasets (JACM, PsyReview, NIPS12) demonstrate that the BNBP topic model converges faster, mixes better, and achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance compared to HDP-LDA.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The authors carefully develop the ECPF (exchangeable cluster probability function) before deriving the EPPF, providing a clear path from the BNBP to its exchangeable partition properties. The theoretical contributions are well-supported by experimental results showing the BNBP topic model's advantages in convergence speed and predictive performance. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism leading to fewer topics than HDP-LDA, and discuss how this affects the model's behavior.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing different exchangeable partitions under varying dispersion parameters) effectively illustrate key concepts. The comparison with existing methods (LDA, HDP-LDA) is thorough, with detailed analysis of the differences in their prediction rules. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including closed-form Gibbs sampling update equations.

The work is highly original. While previous papers have studied the BNBP [7, 10, 11, 16, 17], none have derived its EPPF for mixed-membership modeling or developed a fully collapsed inference algorithm. The paper fills a clear gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that "there is hardly a unified treatment to mixed-membership modeling." The technique to derive the EPPF via a group-size dependent model is innovative and could potentially be generalized to other integer-valued stochastic processes.

The significance of this work is substantial. Mixed-membership modeling is an important problem in machine learning, and the BNBP topic model offers a practical solution with clear advantages over existing approaches. The model's fast convergence, good mixing, and state-of-the-art predictive performance with a compact representation of the corpus make it valuable for practitioners. The paper also advances the theoretical understanding of exchangeable random partitions in mixed-membership settings, which is a fundamental problem in nonparametric Bayesian statistics.

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas for improvement. The EPPF itself is quite complex, and the paper could provide more intuition about why this specific form is necessary for BNBP. The comparison with HDP-LDA is thorough, but the paper could better explain how the BNBP's multiplicative control mechanism leads to better practical performance. Additionally, while the paper mentions potential generalizations to other stochastic processes, it would be valuable to see a brief discussion of how the proposed method might apply to the gamma-negative binomial process (GNBP) or other related models.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to nonparametric Bayesian statistics and provides a practical, high-performing algorithm for mixed-membership modeling. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a major practical contribution, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the model's advantages. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in a meaningful way. I recommend acceptance.

Strengths: Novel theoretical contribution (EPPF for BNBP), practical fully collapsed inference, strong experimental validation, clear writing, well-motivated problem.

Weaknesses: EPPF complexity, limited discussion of generalization to other processes, some aspects of the comparison with HDP-LDA could be more thorough.

Arguments for acceptance: Fills a significant gap in mixed-membership modeling literature, provides a novel and practical algorithm with strong empirical results, makes important theoretical contributions, well-written and well-supported.

Arguments against acceptance: Minimal - the paper is strong in all aspects and makes a clear contribution to the field.
