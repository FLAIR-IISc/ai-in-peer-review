This paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of nonparametric Bayesian models for mixed-membership data. The authors derive the Exchangeable Partition Probability Function (EPPF) for the Beta-Negative Binomial Process (BNBP), which describes the marginal distribution over partitions of grouped data. This has been a notable gap in the literature, as previous inference methods for BNBP models, such as those by Zhou et al. (2012) and Broderick et al. (2013), required finite truncation or slice sampling. The key innovation is a novel two-step derivation that first defines an Exchangeable Cluster Probability Function (ECPF) under random group sizes and then uses Bayes' rule to obtain the desired EPPF for fixed group sizes. This framework is then applied to develop a BNBP topic model, for which a fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is derived. This work stands in contrast to the widely used Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP), whose collapsed sampler relies on the more complex Chinese restaurant franchise representation.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The paper's quality and originality are exceptionally high. The derivation of the BNBP's EPPF is a novel and important theoretical result that allows for exact, truncation-free inference. The clarity of the paper is commendable; it carefully builds the theoretical argument and provides an intuitive interpretation of the resulting prediction rule, effectively contrasting it with those of LDA and HDP-LDA. The most significant contribution is the resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler. The experiments on three standard text corpora provide compelling evidence for its practical advantages, demonstrating markedly faster convergence and better mixing compared to the HDP's direct assignment sampler. Furthermore, the results show that the BNBP topic model can achieve competitive, and often superior, predictive perplexity with a more compact representation of the corpus, a valuable feature for practical applications.

A minor weakness is that the EPPF itself (Eq. 9) is mathematically complex, although this is mitigated by the simplicity of the final prediction rule (Eq. 10) used for inference. Additionally, while the authors suggest their derivation method may be generalizable to other integer-valued processes, this is presented as future work without a detailed discussion of potential challenges.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Presents a novel and theoretically sound derivation of the EPPF for the BNBP, filling a known gap in the field.
*   **Pro:** The resulting fully collapsed Gibbs sampler is a significant practical contribution, demonstrating clear advantages in convergence, mixing, and implementation simplicity over the HDP.
*   **Pro:** The work is technically strong, clearly written, and backed by a thorough empirical evaluation that supports its claims of state-of-the-art performance.

This is a complete and impactful piece of research that advances both the theory and practice of nonparametric topic modeling. It should be accepted.
