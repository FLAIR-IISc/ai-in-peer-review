Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent rate networks of excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) neurons that store and retrieve multiple analog (graded) memories as stable attractors while respecting key biological constraints: Dale’s law, low firing rates without saturating nonlinearities, and balanced excitation/inhibition. The approach directly optimizes synaptic weights so that prescribed excitatory patterns become fixed points (by minimizing the velocity dv/dt at those states) and locally stable (by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA) across all stored memories. Inhibitory neuronal activities at each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables during optimization. The trained networks exhibit balanced E/I inputs, robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, emergence of sparse effective connectivity, and a reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

This contributes to the long-standing literature on associative memory networks initiated by Hopfield and successors, addressing known limitations: symmetric weights and binary patterns [1,2], difficulty realizing high analog capacity with dynamical stability [3–6], and lack of explicit inhibitory populations or reliance on saturation in LIF models [7–9]. Compared to spatially structured “bump” attractors [10,11], the method stores arbitrary analog patterns without special topology. The use of SSA from robust control [13,14] to enforce stability across multiple attractors is novel in this context. The work is related to stabilized supralinear networks [15] and balanced memory hypotheses [8], but provides an optimization-based constructive method.

Quality
The technical idea—jointly optimizing for near-zero dv/dt and negative SSA at each target pattern under Dale’s law—is sound and well-motivated. The SSA is a differentiable proxy for the spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based optimization under nonconvex constraints; this is an elegant import from control theory. The empirical results are convincing: recall from noisy cues approaches a nearest-neighbor “ideal observer”; E/I balancing is demonstrated per neuron across attractors; trial-to-trial variability quenches upon stimulus onset, matching cortical observations [21]; and many weak synapses can be pruned with limited loss.

There are, however, some gaps that, if addressed, would strengthen the paper:
- Fixed points vs. slow points: Because dv/dt at target patterns is small but nonzero, stability is assessed via the Jacobian at a state that is not exactly a fixed point. The authors argue nearby fixed points exist. It would be helpful to explicitly verify: (i) the norm of the displacement between each target and the actual fixed point; (ii) the basin size (e.g., via empirical convergence radii); and (iii) that the Jacobian at the realized fixed point has negative spectral abscissa.
- Role of optimized inhibitory steady states: During training, inhibitory activities vinhμ are free per memory. Please clarify that the learned weights induce dynamics that actually drive inhibitory neurons to those optimized values when the excitatory pattern is present, and whether uniqueness holds. A post hoc check showing that the steady-state inhibitory rates reached by dynamics match the optimized vinhμ would address concerns about consistency.
- Capacity and scaling: The work shows m = 30 memories in n = 150 neurons (about 0.2 per neuron; excitatory subpopulation is smaller). A systematic study of capacity vs. network size, fraction of inhibitory neurons, pattern statistics, and regularization would be valuable. Even empirical scaling curves (with confidence intervals) would contextualize significance relative to classic results [3–6].
- Sparsity and structural realism: The network is initially dense; sparsity emerges as many small weights, but actual connectivity remains dense unless thresholded. A comparison where a fixed connection probability is enforced (e.g., 10–20% per anatomical data) would test feasibility under stronger structural constraints.
- Robustness analyses: Beyond clipping weak weights, it would be useful to quantify robustness to synaptic perturbations (e.g., multiplicative noise), parameter heterogeneity (τi, external inputs), and choice of SSA smoothing ε. A sensitivity sweep would inform generality.
- Baselines: In addition to the “ideal observer,” a comparison to classical graded-attractor constructions (e.g., pseudo-inverse learning without E/I separation), or to balanced-memory models with global inhibition [8,9], even if capacity is lower, would help isolate the added value of the SSA-based optimization.
- Spiking instantiation: The authors argue the rectified power-law gain supports transfer to spiking networks; a brief demonstration (e.g., LIF network with learned weights) would significantly elevate impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a well-structured objective and optimization description. A few suggestions:
- Provide the explicit definition/algorithm for SSA and its gradient (or a clear pointer to an appendix), along with computational complexity.
- Summarize Table 1 hyperparameters in the main text and describe how they were chosen; report sensitivity.
- Report exact network composition (nE, nI) and time constants; specify whether τi are homogeneous.
- When defining E and I inputs, make explicit any rectification used in bWc+ and how this relates to Dale’s law.

Originality
The combination of: (i) enforcing multiple analog attractors in an E/I network under Dale’s law; (ii) using SSA to shape local stability; and (iii) showing balanced regimes across attractors and variability quenching, is novel and nontrivial. This is more than a recombination of known techniques.

Significance
If scalable, this framework provides a practical tool to construct biologically plausible analog memory networks and a bridge between robust control and theoretical neuroscience. The explanatory link to trial-to-trial variability reduction is compelling. The main limitation to significance is the lack of capacity/scaling characterization and absence of a spiking demonstration; addressing these would broaden impact.

Pros
- Elegant, principled optimization framework using SSA.
- Satisfies key biological constraints (Dale’s law, balanced E/I, low rates).
- Robust recall and variability reduction aligned with cortical data.
- Emergent sparsity and robustness to pruning.
- Clear potential to inform future work on learning rules (e.g., inhibitory plasticity [27]).

Cons
- No systematic capacity vs. size analysis; results on relatively small networks.
- Fixed points not enforced exactly; limited analysis of basins and realized equilibria.
- Reliance on per-memory inhibitory targets during training needs stronger validation.
- Dense initial connectivity; limited tests under enforced sparse wiring.
- No spiking-network validation; limited robustness/sensitivity analyses.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify distance from targets to realized fixed points and empirical basin sizes.
- Verify that inhibitory steady states reached dynamically match optimized vinhμ.
- Provide capacity scaling experiments and sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF.
- Evaluate under enforced sparse connectivity and heterogeneous τi.
- Add a spiking instantiation (even small-scale) to corroborate transferability.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful and technically solid contribution that advances the construction of analog attractor networks under realistic biological constraints. With additional analyses on capacity, fixed-point realization, and robustness (or a spiking proof-of-concept), this would be a strong NIPS paper. As it stands, it is promising and of broad interest to computational neuroscience and control-minded machine learning researchers.
