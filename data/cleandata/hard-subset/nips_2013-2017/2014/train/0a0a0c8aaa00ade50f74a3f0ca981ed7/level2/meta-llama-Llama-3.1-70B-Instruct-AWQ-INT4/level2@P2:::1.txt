This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. The authors propose a method to directly optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. The resulting networks operate in the balanced regime, are robust to corruptions of the memory cue as well as to ongoing noise, and incidentally explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset that is ubiquitously observed in sensory and motor cortices.

Strengths:
- The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of theoretical neuroscience by providing a novel framework for building attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.
- The authors use a control-theoretic approach to optimize network parameters, which is a unique and innovative method in this field.
- The results show that the optimized networks can store and recall analog patterns in a robust and stable manner, which is a significant improvement over previous models.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the network's performance, including its robustness to noise and its ability to reduce trial-to-trial variability.

Weaknesses:
- The paper assumes a rate-based formulation of the circuit dynamics, which may not be directly applicable to spiking memory networks.
- The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how local synaptic learning rules can achieve the stabilization objective, which is an important question for future research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with previous models and a clearer explanation of how the proposed framework differs from existing approaches.
