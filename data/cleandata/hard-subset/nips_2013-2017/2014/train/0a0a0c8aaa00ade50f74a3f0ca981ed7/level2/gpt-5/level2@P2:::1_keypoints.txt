- Proposes a control-theoretic method to train rate-based E/I recurrent networks that embed multiple analog memories as stable fixed points while obeying Dale’s law.  
- Core objective: minimize dv/dt at target patterns and minimize Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian, with softplus weight reparameterization to enforce sign constraints.  
- Treats inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ as per-memory auxiliary variables optimized to aid stability.  
- Resulting networks show emergent E/I balance, low non-saturating firing rates, robustness to corrupted cues and noise, and reduced across-trial variability at stimulus onset.  
- Strength: novel use of SSA/robust-control tools to jointly target fixed-point accuracy and local stability under Dale’s law.  
- Strength: demonstrates graded (analog) memories with realistic low-rate activity without relying on saturation or global inhibition.  
- Strength: quantitative robustness tests including comparison to an “ideal observer” diagnostic.  
- Strength: emergent sparsity of synaptic strengths and analysis showing many weak weights are dispensable.  
- Strength: links attractor dynamics to stimulus-onset variability reduction for graded patterns.  
- Weakness: unclear scalability and computational cost; experiments limited to n=150, m=30 with no scaling analysis or capacity bounds.  
- Weakness: optimizing n_I × m inhibitory steady-state variables inflates degrees of freedom; contribution of this flexibility to performance is not quantified.  
- Weakness: fixed-point accuracy at targets is not exact (dv/dt ≠ 0); no explicit post-training verification of true fixed points, their distances to targets, or basins of attraction.  
- Weakness: biological plausibility of connectivity is partially supported; emergent sparsity arises from dense parameterization + L2 rather than fixed sparse masks or distance-dependent wiring.  
- Weakness: explanation for trial-to-trial variability reduction is suggestive but lacks mechanistic analysis of transient dynamics, timescales, and stimulus-strength modulation.  
- Weakness: no spiking-network implementation shown; transfer to LIF networks would strengthen biological relevance.  
- Quality: technically sound and well-motivated use of SSA, but needs more rigorous validation of fixed points, stability margins, and capacity scaling.  
- Clarity: generally clear and organized, but missing details on computational complexity, SSA gradient computation, sensitivity to hyperparameters, optimization steps, and convergence criteria.  
- Originality: novel application of robust control/SSA to stabilize analog attractors under E/I constraints; per-memory inhibitory optimization is an interesting modeling choice.  
- Significance: conceptually valuable and timely if scalable; current small-scale demonstrations and dense optimization limit immediate applicability.  
- Key suggestions: provide scaling results, quantify v_inh^μ contribution, locate true fixed points and basins post-training, test fixed sparse connectivity masks, report stability margins, and demonstrate spiking transfer.  
- Minor issues: inconsistent reference formatting, unclear noise units, and unmotivated success threshold choice (0.001) needing interpretation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled and novel method producing biologically plausible analog attractors with E/I balance and robustness.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scale, reliance on per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom, incomplete fixed-point/stability validation, and no spiking demonstration.  
- Overall assessment: strong, original methodological contribution with promising demonstrations at modest scale; acceptance recommended contingent on addressing main concerns about scalability, fixed-point verification, and biological plausibility.
