- Proposes a control-theoretic framework for attractor networks that enforces Dale’s law, sparse/recurrent connectivity, and graded neuronal activity.  
- Directly optimizes network parameters so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points.  
- Networks operate in a balanced excitation–inhibition regime and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Demonstrates robustness to corrupted memory cues and ongoing noise.  
- Addresses physiological constraints missing from prior models (Dale’s law, sparsity).  
- Enables storage and retrieval of analog (continuous) memory patterns, not just binary.  
- Shows biologically relevant robustness to noise and cue corruption.  
- Produces dynamics consistent with experimentally observed balance of excitation and inhibition.  
- Optimization algorithm may be computationally expensive, limiting scalability to larger networks.  
- Uses a rate-based formulation, which may not capture full spiking-network complexity.  
- The training/optimization method is likely not biologically plausible (requires global optimization).  
- Presents a novel and significant contribution to theoretical neuroscience.  
- Advances understanding of memory mechanisms and the role of balanced network operation.  
- Has implications for developing more realistic neural-network models for cognitive processes.  
- Paper is clearly written with a good introduction, well-organized sections, and helpful figures.  
- Minor issues: a few typos, could cite more related work, and should discuss future directions and applications.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
