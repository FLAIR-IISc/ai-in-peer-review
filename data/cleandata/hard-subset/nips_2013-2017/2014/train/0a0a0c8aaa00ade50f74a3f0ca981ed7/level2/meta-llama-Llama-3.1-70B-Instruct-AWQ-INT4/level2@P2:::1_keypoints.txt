- Novel control-theoretic framework to build attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.  
- Method to directly optimize excitatory/inhibitory networks so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points.  
- Resulting networks operate in the balanced regime and are robust to corrupted cues and ongoing noise.  
- Model explains reduction of trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset observed in cortex.  
- Control-theoretic optimization is an innovative contribution to theoretical neuroscience.  
- Optimized networks can store and recall analog patterns more robustly than prior models.  
- Thorough analysis of robustness, stability, and variability reduction strengthens the claims.  
- Assumes a rate-based formulation, limiting direct applicability to spiking memory networks.  
- Lacks a clear account of how local synaptic learning rules could achieve the stabilization objective.  
- Comparison to previous models is insufficient; differences from existing approaches need clarification.  
- Paper is technically sound with clear methods and well-supported claims.  
- Writing and organization are good, with sufficient information for expert reproduction.  
- Some sections (control-theoretic approach, optimization procedure) need clearer explanations.  
- Framework is original and potentially significant for understanding neural memory substrates.  
- Potential to advance the field, but broader applicability and biological plausibility remain open questions.
