- Proposes a novel optimization-based framework to design recurrent networks that store analog activity patterns as stable attractors while respecting biological constraints (Dale’s law, separate E/I populations).

- Frames memory storage as minimizing pattern velocity (||dv/dt||) and a stability measure simultaneously for each target pattern.

- Introduces the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa to enable gradient-based stability optimization.

- Optimizes synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activities per pattern to construct functional memory networks.

- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues and emergent biological features: dynamic E/I balance, sparse connectivity, and variability quenching.

- Technically sound and well-supported by simulation results; represents a significant advance in biologically realistic attractor modeling.

- Highly original use of modern robust control theory (SSA) to stabilize multiple analog patterns in neural networks.

- Clever modeling choice to treat inhibitory activities as optimizable free parameters for stability.

- Exceptionally clear writing, well-motivated problem statement, and informative figures.

- Main weakness: learning algorithm is a global optimization requiring full network/Jacobian knowledge and is not biologically plausible as a synaptic rule.

- Scalability concerns: experiments run on 150 neurons; unclear feasibility for networks with thousands of neurons due to SSA computation cost.

- Nature of fixed points is uncertain: residual nonzero velocity at targets suggests nearby, but not exact, fixed points—needs direct demonstration of final attractors.

- Storage capacity analysis is limited: shows 30 patterns for 100 excitatory neurons (α=0.3) but lacks systematic scaling with network size or pattern statistics.

- Recommendation: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; paper provides a valuable normative benchmark and merits acceptance.
