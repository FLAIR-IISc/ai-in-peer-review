- Core contribution: Uses a control-theoretic Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to build biologically plausible attractor networks that store analog patterns while respecting Dale’s law, graded firing, and balanced E–I dynamics.  
- Addresses gap: Resolves limitations of prior models that violated Dale’s law, enforced binary states, or relied on saturating transfer functions or restrictive bump architectures.  
- Method novelty: Directly optimizes synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron dynamics to enforce stability around arbitrary analog fixed points.  
- Strength — stability: SSA framework achieves stable fixed points for graded patterns without artificial saturation (Fig. 2).  
- Strength — balance & variability: Networks operate in a balanced E–I regime and explain reduction in trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus (Fig. 5, 6b).  
- Strength — sparsity: Synaptic weight sparseness emerges naturally from stability optimization rather than from L2 regularization (Fig. 4).  
- Strength — robustness: Demonstrates noise-robust recall with performance comparable to an ideal observer (Fig. 3).  
- Strength — generality: Approach can generalize to spiking networks by matching gain functions (Section 4).  
- Weakness — hyperparameters: Choices for ηs and ηF are heuristic; paper needs a brief sensitivity analysis to justify robustness.  
- Weakness — ideal observer baseline: Superior baseline performance needs clarification—log-normal mean aligns with baseline rate, making that state more likely.  
- Weakness — scalability: Lacks discussion of how storage capacity scales with network size or pattern complexity (e.g., log-normal variance).  
- Overall assessment: Clear, significant contribution unifying physiological constraints, yielding testable predictions, and advancing understanding of cortical memory mechanisms.  
- Recommendation: Accept; suggested revisions are minor and easily addressed.
