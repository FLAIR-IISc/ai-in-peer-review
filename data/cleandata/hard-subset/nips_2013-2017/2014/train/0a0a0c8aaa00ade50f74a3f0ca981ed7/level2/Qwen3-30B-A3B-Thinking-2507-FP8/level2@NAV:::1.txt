

This paper presents a significant advance in modeling cortical memory networks by addressing long-standing physiological constraints. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes E-I networks to stabilize arbitrary analog memory patterns as fixed points while satisfying Dale's law, graded activity, and balanced excitation-inhibition dynamics—addressing key limitations in prior work (e.g., binary patterns in Hopfield networks, saturation issues in LIF models). Crucially, they leverage the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization, enabling networks to store 30 analog patterns (capacity 0.2) with noise-robust recall, sparse connectivity, and a balanced E/I regime that explains reduced trial-to-trial variability in sensory cortices (Fig. 6b).

The work is exceptionally well-supported: experiments demonstrate successful recall under noisy cues (Fig. 3), quantified sparseness of synaptic weights (Fig. 4), and precise E/I balancing across neurons (Fig. 5). The comparison to an ideal observer (Fig. 3b) provides strong evidence for the model’s efficacy. Methodologically, the optimization procedure (Eq. 5) is clearly articulated, and the use of log-normal firing rate distributions aligns with in vivo observations (Fig. 1a, Ref. 18). The discussion of limitations—particularly the rate-based model’s applicability to spiking networks and the need for local learning rules (e.g., inhibitory plasticity)—is thoughtful and appropriate for a conference paper.

Strengths include the rigorous integration of physiological constraints, the novel SSA-based stability optimization, and the direct link to experimental phenomena (reduced variability). The paper’s clarity and completeness (including parameter details in Table 1) meet high standards for NIPS. Minor weaknesses: the non-zero \|dv/dt\| at fixed points (Fig. 2) warrants more discussion of practical stability margins, and the modest storage capacity (0.2) could be contextualized against theoretical limits for analog patterns (e.g., Ref. 3,4). However, these do not undermine the core contribution.

The work is highly original in unifying physiological constraints with analog memory storage, significantly advancing the field beyond binary or saturated models. It offers a reproducible framework for future studies of cortical memory, with clear practical utility for neuroscientists. The authors’ acknowledgment of limitations and future directions (local learning rules) further strengthens the contribution. This paper is a compelling, well-executed step toward biologically plausible memory networks and merits acceptance at NIPS.
