## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of building biologically plausible attractor networks for memory storage and recall. The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework to optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns, satisfying key physiological constraints like Dale’s law and avoiding saturation. They demonstrate that these networks operate in a balanced regime, are robust to noise, and can explain observed reductions in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

The core strength of this work lies in its comprehensive approach to addressing limitations of previous models. Unlike earlier approaches, the authors explicitly incorporate both excitatory and inhibitory neurons, avoid binary representations, and prevent saturation through a balanced E/I dynamic. The use of robust control theory (specifically, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to ensure stability is a significant contribution, offering a more principled approach than ad-hoc methods. The demonstration of noise robustness and the explanation of variability reduction are compelling results, linking the model to observed neurophysiological phenomena.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the methods section is detailed, the specific parameter choices (e.g., ηs, ηF in the cost function) feel somewhat arbitrary and lack detailed justification. A more thorough ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial. Additionally, the reliance on a rate-based model, while simplifying analysis, limits the direct applicability to spiking neural networks. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of how the findings translate to spiking systems would strengthen the work. The claim of explaining the reduction of trial-to-trial variability is interesting, but the connection feels somewhat superficial and could benefit from more rigorous comparison to existing models of variability reduction.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to building biologically plausible memory networks.
*   Addresses key limitations of previous models.
*   Demonstrates robustness to noise and explains observed neurophysiological phenomena.
*   Strong theoretical foundation using control theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Parameter choices in the optimization process lack detailed justification.
*   Reliance on a rate-based model limits direct applicability to spiking networks.
*   Connection between the model and variability reduction could be strengthened.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in our understanding of the neural substrate of memory. While some aspects could be further refined, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and compelling results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding parameter justification and the discussion of spiking network implications. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the connection between their model and observed neural variability.
