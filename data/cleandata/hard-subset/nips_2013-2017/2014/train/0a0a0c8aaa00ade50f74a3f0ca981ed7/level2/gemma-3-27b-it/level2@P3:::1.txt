This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that satisfy several physiological constraints, including Dale’s law, graded activity representation, and operating in a balanced regime. The core idea is to directly optimize network parameters – synaptic connectivity and inhibitory neuron firing rates – to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points of the network dynamics, leveraging the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The work presents a comprehensive set of experiments demonstrating successful memory storage and recall, robustness to noise, and the emergence of biologically plausible network properties like balanced excitation and inhibition, and trial-to-trial variability reduction following stimulus onset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a long-standing challenge in understanding the neural substrate of memory. Existing models often fall short in incorporating key physiological constraints in a unified way, and this work makes a significant step towards bridging that gap. The use of control theory, particularly the SSA, is a novel and effective approach to guarantee stability in the presence of these constraints. The thorough experimental validation, including tests of robustness to noise and comparisons with an “ideal observer”, strengthens the claims. The observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability is a particularly compelling result, linking the model to empirical observations in cortical circuits.

However, there are areas where the work could be improved. While the paper extensively validates the proposed framework, the computational cost of the optimization process is not discussed. Given the complexity of the optimization problem (optimizing n(n-1) + nIm parameters), scalability to larger networks remains a concern.  A discussion of the computational limitations and potential strategies for improving efficiency (e.g., sparsity-promoting regularization, more efficient SSA computation) would be valuable.  

The choice of the threshold-quadratic gain function (Eq. 1) is somewhat arbitrary.  While justified by experimental observations, a brief exploration of alternative gain functions and their impact on network performance might be informative.  Additionally, the parameters ηs and ηF in the cost function (Eq. 5) are set “heuristically”. A more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters, or a principled method for their selection, would enhance the robustness of the conclusions.

The analysis of the synaptic weights (Fig. 4) reveals sparseness, but the explanation that it “must be a genuine consequence” feels somewhat assertive. Further investigation into the relationship between the optimization process, the SSA criterion, and the emergence of sparsity would be insightful.  It would be helpful to show how the network performance changes as the level of sparsification is increased beyond the naturally occurring level.

Finally, the paper focuses on a specific network architecture (fully connected within excitatory and inhibitory populations).  While this simplifies the optimization problem, it’s unclear how the results generalize to more biologically realistic network topologies, such as those with sparse and structured connectivity.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to building attractor networks with physiological constraints.
*   Strong experimental validation demonstrating successful memory storage and recall, robustness, and emergence of balanced activity.
*   Compelling link to empirical observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability.
*   Addresses a significant open problem in computational neuroscience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of the optimization process is not discussed.
*   Heuristic parameter settings in the cost function.
*   Limited exploration of alternative gain functions and network topologies.
*   Explanation for the emergence of sparseness is somewhat speculative.



**Overall assessment:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field.  The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses.  Addressing the concerns regarding computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and generalizability would further strengthen the work.  A rejection at this stage would be unwarranted.  Minor revisions are recommended.
