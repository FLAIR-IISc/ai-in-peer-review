This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that satisfy several physiological constraints, including Dale’s law, graded activity representations, low firing rates, and a balanced excitation-inhibition (E/I) regime. The authors directly optimize network parameters to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points, utilizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The results demonstrate robust memory recall, resilience to noise, and an intriguing explanation for the observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in attractor network theory (e.g., Hopfield [1], Treves [3, 4], Latham [7]) that often struggles to simultaneously meet realistic biological constraints. Previous efforts have either relaxed Dale’s law, restricted representations to binary formats, or relied on saturating neuron dynamics. This work addresses these limitations directly, offering a principled approach to designing networks that exhibit more biologically plausible behavior. The connection to robust control theory, specifically the use of SSA, is a key contribution and allows for effective stability optimization in a high-dimensional parameter space.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses key limitations of previous models:** The authors successfully integrate several critical biological constraints that are often ignored or simplified in existing attractor network models.
* **Novel control-theoretic approach:**  Framing memory storage as a control problem and using the SSA for optimization is a significant methodological advancement.  The approach is well-motivated and appears to overcome issues with direct stability analysis.
* **Robustness to noise:** The demonstrated robustness of recall to noisy cues is an important property, aligning with experimental observations of associative memory.
* **Explanation of trial-to-trial variability reduction:** The finding that the network dynamics can explain the reduction in neuronal variability following stimulus onset is particularly compelling and connects the model to empirical findings [21]. This offers a mechanistic explanation for a ubiquitous cortical phenomenon.
* **Comprehensive analysis:** The paper includes detailed analysis of synaptic weight distributions, the balance of E/I inputs, and the impact of synaptic pruning, providing a thorough understanding of the network's behavior.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational cost:** The optimization process appears computationally intensive. While not a fatal flaw, scaling this approach to larger, more realistic networks could be challenging. The authors could briefly discuss this limitation and potential avenues for mitigation (e.g., more efficient optimization algorithms, dimensionality reduction).
* **Parameter tuning:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) seems somewhat heuristic.  A more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Justification of the gain function:** While the authors provide a rationale for the choice of the threshold-quadratic gain function [15], a more detailed discussion of alternative gain functions and their potential impact on the results would be beneficial.  Could a different gain function simplify the optimization or lead to different network properties?
* **Limited exploration of spiking dynamics:** The authors acknowledge the need to translate the rate-based results to spiking networks, but a more concrete discussion of how this could be achieved (beyond simply matching gain functions) would be valuable. For instance, how would spike timing correlations be affected, and what role might they play in memory recall?
* **Lack of comparison to other recent models:**  Recent work has explored related concepts, such as the role of structured synaptic connectivity in memory [e.g., similar ideas in ref 20, but with different goals]. While the authors cite relevant work, a more direct comparison to these newer models would help to highlight the unique contributions of this paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to building biologically plausible attractor networks.
* The results address a significant open problem in computational neuroscience and offer a potential explanation for several empirical observations.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.
* The use of robust control theory is a significant methodological advancement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the optimization process could limit the scalability of the approach.
* The parameter tuning seems somewhat heuristic and could benefit from more systematic investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors have successfully addressed several key limitations of previous attractor network models and have developed a principled approach to building more biologically realistic memory networks. The connection to robust control theory is particularly innovative. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding parameter sensitivity and the connection to spiking dynamics.
