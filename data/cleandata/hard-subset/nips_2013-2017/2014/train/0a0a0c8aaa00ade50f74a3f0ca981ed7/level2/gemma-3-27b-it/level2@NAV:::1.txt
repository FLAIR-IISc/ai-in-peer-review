## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper addresses the longstanding problem of biologically plausible memory networks, proposing a novel control-theoretic framework to construct attractor networks satisfying key physiological constraints. The authors aim to overcome limitations of previous models – such as violating Dale’s Law, binary representations, and saturating activity – by directly optimizing network parameters for stable fixed points representing analog memory patterns. The core contribution lies in applying robust control theory (specifically, Smoothed Spectral Abscissa minimization) to achieve this stability within a balanced E-I network. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the shortcomings of existing memory models. The proposed approach is technically sound, framing memory storage as an optimization problem and leveraging established control-theoretic techniques. The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog patterns, robustness to noise, and a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition, aligning with observed cortical behavior. Notably, the resulting networks exhibit a reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, a ubiquitous phenomenon in sensory cortices. The inclusion of sparsity in synaptic weights and analysis of its necessity adds depth to the study.

However, the paper would benefit from further elaboration on several points. While the methods section details the optimization process, the specific choices of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) feel somewhat arbitrary and could be better justified. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of these parameters on performance would be valuable.  The connection to existing spiking neural network models could be strengthened; while acknowledged, a deeper discussion of how the rate-based results translate to spiking dynamics would be beneficial. Further, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a discussion of potential scalability issues to larger, more complex networks would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of control theory to memory network design.
*   Addresses key physiological constraints absent in previous models.
*   Demonstrates robust memory recall and biologically plausible network dynamics.
*   Successfully reproduces a known cortical phenomenon (variability reduction).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Parameter choices in the optimization process lack strong justification.
*   Limited discussion of scalability to larger networks.
*   Connection to spiking neural networks could be more thoroughly explored.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper presenting a significant advance in the field of memory network modeling. The approach is innovative, the results are promising, and the work is well-written and motivated. Despite the minor weaknesses, the potential impact of this work justifies acceptance with a request for minor revisions addressing the points raised above. I encourage the authors to resubmit after addressing these concerns.
