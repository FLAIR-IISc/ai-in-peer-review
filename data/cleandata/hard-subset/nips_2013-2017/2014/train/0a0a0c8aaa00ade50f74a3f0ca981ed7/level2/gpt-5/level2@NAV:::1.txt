Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct rate-based attractor networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that store and retrieve multiple analog memories while respecting key physiological constraints: Dale’s law, low firing rates, and E/I balance. The authors jointly optimize synaptic weights and memory-specific inhibitory steady states so that prescribed excitatory patterns are fixed points and locally stable. To handle stability, they minimize the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa from robust control. Simulations demonstrate robust autoassociative recall comparable to an ideal observer under substantial cue noise, operation in a dynamically balanced regime, emergent sparsity in effective synapses, and stimulus-induced variability quenching consistent with cortical data. This work builds on classical Hopfield-style associative memory (largely binary, symmetric, or with instantaneous/global inhibition), graded-response analyses (Treves, Rolls), balanced network theory and variability quenching, and prior use of SSA in control and computational neuroscience, but uniquely combines these elements to achieve analog memories under Dale-constrained recurrent dynamics.

Strengths:
- Quality: Methodologically careful with explicit fixed-point and stability criteria; SSA minimization is well-motivated and numerically tractable. Multiple validation axes (recall vs noise, ideal-observer comparison, ongoing noise robustness, E/I balance, variability quenching).
- Originality: Novel application of robust stability optimization to embed analog attractors in an E/I network respecting Dale’s law; moves beyond binary/global-inhibition designs.
- Significance: A promising template for constructing physiologically grounded associative memories; the link to variability quenching adds explanatory value for systems neuroscience.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with clear equations and a coherent cost function.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Capacity and scaling: Demonstrated capacity (~0.2 for n=150) is modest; no systematic scaling laws, basin-size quantification, or comparisons to theoretical bounds.
- Biological/algorithmic plausibility: Learning relies on nonlocal gradient-based optimization over n(n−1)+nI m parameters; the role of optimizing inhibitory setpoints per memory raises questions about implementability by local plasticity.
- Reproducibility: Limited details on hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., SSA ε), convergence behavior, and computational cost; code availability is not stated.
- Generality: No spiking-network validation with the learned connectivity; robustness to parameter perturbations and structural sparsity constraints remain unexplored.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide capacity and basin-size scaling with n, m, and sparsity; compare against graded Hopfield-like baselines obeying Dale’s law.
- Validate transfer of the learned connectivity to LIF networks matching the chosen gain function and quantify recall/variability quenching.
- Analyze sensitivity to ε, initialization, and regularization; report compute/time.
- Add structural sparsity constraints or L1 penalties and compare to emergent sparsity.
- Explore local/plausible learning rules (e.g., inhibitory plasticity) approximating the SSA objective.
- Release code and include a reproducible protocol.

Arguments:
Pros: principled framework; respects physiology; robust demonstrations; bridges control and neuroscience; explanatory link to variability quenching.
Cons: limited capacity/scaling analysis; nonlocal training; missing spiking validation; reproducibility details incomplete.

Overall, a promising and novel contribution; with added scaling/robustness evidence and spiking validation, it would be strong for NeurIPS.
