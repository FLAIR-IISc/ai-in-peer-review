- Introduces a control-theoretic optimization to build E/I recurrent rate networks that store/retrieve multiple analog memories while respecting Dale’s law, low firing rates, and E/I balance.
- Directly optimizes synapses to make prescribed excitatory patterns near-fixed points (minimize dv/dt) and locally stable (minimize smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA) with inhibitory activities as auxiliary variables.
- Novel use of SSA from robust control as a differentiable proxy for spectral abscissa enabling gradient-based stability shaping across many attractors.
- Empirical results show robust recall from noisy cues, per-neuron E/I balance across attractors, variability quenching after stimulus onset, emergent sparse effective connectivity, and pruning tolerance.
- Technical approach is sound and well-motivated; SSA import is elegant and optimization results are convincing.
- Concern: target states have small but nonzero dv/dt — need explicit verification of actual fixed points (distance from targets, basin sizes, Jacobian at realized equilibria).
- Concern: clarify that learned weights drive inhibitory neurons to the optimized per-memory v_inh values in dynamics and whether those inhibitory steady states are unique.
- Capacity/scaling is under-explored: only m=30 in n=150 shown; needs systematic scaling curves vs. network size, inhibitory fraction, pattern statistics, and regularization.
- Structural realism/sparsity: networks are initialized dense and only weak weights emerge; test feasibility with enforced biologically plausible connection probabilities (e.g., 10–20%).
- Robustness analyses are limited: quantify sensitivity to synaptic perturbations (multiplicative noise), parameter heterogeneity (τi, inputs), and SSA smoothing ε.
- Baselines: compare to classical graded-attractor constructions (e.g., pseudo-inverse without E/I separation) and balanced-memory models to isolate SSA benefits.
- Spiking instantiation is missing; a small LIF demonstration with learned weights would greatly strengthen claims about transferability to spiking networks.
- Clarity suggestions: provide explicit SSA definition/gradient and complexity, summarize hyperparameters and sensitivity, state network composition (nE, nI) and τi homogeneity, and clarify rectification/Dale’s-law implementation.
- Originality: combination of enforcing multiple analog attractors in E/I networks under Dale’s law with SSA-based stability shaping is novel and nontrivial.
- Significance: framework could be a practical tool linking robust control and neuroscience if scalable and transferable to spiking networks; current lack of scaling/spiking demos limits impact.
- Pros: principled SSA-based optimization; satisfies key biological constraints; robust recall and variability reduction; emergent sparsity and pruning robustness; potential to inform learning-rule studies.
- Cons: no systematic capacity analysis; fixed points not enforced exactly; reliance on per-memory inhibitory targets needs validation; initial dense wiring; no spiking validation; limited robustness sweeps.
- Key suggestions: measure target-to-fixed-point distances and basins; verify inhibitory steady states reached dynamically; run capacity scaling and sensitivity sweeps (ε, learning rates); test enforced sparse connectivity and heterogeneous τi; add small-scale spiking proof-of-concept.
- Overall assessment: technically solid and promising contribution that advances biologically plausible analog attractor construction, but needs additional analyses on fixed-point realization, capacity, robustness, and a spiking demo to maximize impact.
