- Presents a control-theoretic framework that optimizes synapses to store multiple analog memories as stable attractors while respecting Dale’s law, low graded firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Encourages fixed points by minimizing dv/dt at target states and enforces local stability by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian.  
- Treats inhibitory steady-state activities as per-memory auxiliary variables, allowing shared inhibitory circuitry to adopt different setpoints across memories.  
- Resulting networks show robust autoassociative recall from noisy cues, resilience to ongoing noise, tight E/I balance, sparse effective weight distributions, and variability quenching.  
- Extends classical attractor models by supporting graded patterns without violating Dale’s law or relying on output saturation.  
- Introduces SSA from robust control as a practical smooth surrogate for spectral abscissa in stability optimization.  
- Positions the method relative to optimization-based RNN shaping approaches but would benefit from a more focused comparison to prior work.  
- Conceptual advance: unifies analog memory storage, Dale’s law, and balanced dynamics in a principled optimization framework.  
- Technical rigor: clear fixed-point and stability objectives, practical SSA gradients, and Dale’s-law reparameterization to enforce sign constraints.  
- Biological relevance: emergent balanced E/I currents, low graded rates, log-normal rate distributions, and stimulus-linked variability quenching.  
- Empirical strengths: robustness to cue and ongoing noise, near “ideal observer” recall performance, functional sparsity, and resilience to pruning small weights.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: dynamics, objectives, and parameter choices are well-specified and illustrated with interpretable analyses.  
- Capacity and scaling are underexplored: results mainly for n=150, m=30 (capacity ~0.2); need systematic scaling curves and variance across runs.  
- Basins of attraction not quantified: should report basin sizes, critical noise thresholds per memory, and structured-cue failure rates (e.g., partial clamping).  
- Inhibitory auxiliary variables require justification: clarify that per-memory inhibitory setpoints are not equivalent to memory-specific parameters and assess sensitivity to constraints.  
- Structural sparsity is lacking: network is dense by design; explicit sparsity penalties (e.g., L1) and performance under enforced sparsity should be examined.  
- Generalization and robustness need broader sensitivity analyses to hyperparameters (ε in SSA, time constants, gains, external inputs) and optimization settings.  
- No spiking instantiation: a small LIF demonstration with matched gain would substantively increase biological significance.  
- Related-work discussion should explicitly compare to other optimization-based methods for stabilizing RNN fixed points or trajectories.  
- Methodology is sound and thoughtfully executed; SSA and dual-objective formulation are appropriate and effective.  
- Paper is clear and well-organized; adding pseudocode and explicit optimization convergence criteria would aid reproducibility.  
- Originality is high: novel application of robust control (SSA) to build balanced E/I attractor networks under Dale’s law.  
- Significance is moderate-to-high but contingent on demonstrated scalability and a spiking demonstration.  
- Essential suggested improvements: capacity scaling, basin quantification, and clarification or mild regularization of inhibitory setpoints.  
- Important optional improvements: spiking transfer demonstration, explicit structural sparsity experiments, sensitivity analyses, and extended related-work comparisons.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem with a novel principled method, strong empirical demonstrations, and clear execution.  
- Rejection arguments: limited evidence on scalability/capacity, no spiking instantiation, and lack of enforced structural sparsity may limit applicability.
