- Proposes a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes synaptic weights and inhibitory rates to create attractor networks meeting physiological constraints.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a robust stability criterion to ensure desired activity patterns are stable fixed points.  
- Demonstrates networks with robust recall, reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset, and balanced excitation/inhibition.  
- Integrates Dale’s law, graded activity representations, and balanced E/I into a functioning attractor model, addressing prior model limitations.  
- Novel application of robust control theory (SSA) to stability optimization in memory network modeling.  
- Emergent reduction in trial-to-trial variability links the model to experimental cortical observations.  
- Mathematical formulation and optimization procedure are clearly presented.  
- Model remains rate-based with a simplified gain function, limiting biological realism and direct translation to spiking networks.  
- Heuristic parameter choices (e.g., ηs, ηF) lack full justification and require more systematic exploration.  
- Storage capacity and scaling with network size are not thoroughly investigated (demonstrated only for ~30 memories).  
- Clipping of weak synapses has little effect, raising questions about redundancy or suboptimal weight optimization.  
- Lacks detailed, quantitative comparisons of capacity and robustness to other state-of-the-art memory models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel robust-control approach, physiological plausibility, and reproduction of cortical features.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited biological realism, insufficient parameter justification, and unclear capacity scaling.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution that outweighs weaknesses; recommendation for acceptance with suggestions for further work on realism, parameter sensitivity, and capacity.
