The paper "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons" presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. The authors propose a general framework that overcomes previous limitations in models of memory circuits, including the separation of neurons into distinct classes of excitatory and inhibitory cells, the presence of recurrent and sparse synaptic connections, and the possibility for each neuron to sustain graded levels of activity in different memories.

The authors' approach is based on directly optimizing network parameters, including synaptic connectivity, to satisfy two conditions: that the desired activity states be fixed points of the dynamics, and that the dynamics be stable around those fixed points. They use a Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to perform robust stability optimization, which is a differentiable relaxation of the spectral abscissa. The authors demonstrate that their approach can store multiple analog memories as stable fixed-points of their dynamics, and that these memories are stable in the face of ongoing noise and corruption of the recall cues.
