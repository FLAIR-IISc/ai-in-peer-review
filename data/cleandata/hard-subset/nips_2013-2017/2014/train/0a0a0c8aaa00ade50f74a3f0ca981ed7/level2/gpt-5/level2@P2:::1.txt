Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based recurrent networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations that embed multiple analog memories as stable fixed points while obeying Dale’s law. The core idea is to jointly enforce (i) a fixed-point condition by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at each target activity pattern and (ii) local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at those patterns, under a softplus reparameterization of weights to enforce signs. Inhibitory steady-state activities are treated as auxiliary variables optimized per memory to facilitate stability. The resulting networks exhibit E/I balance, low, non-saturating firing rates, robustness to corrupted cues and ongoing noise, and a reduction in across-trial variability at stimulus onset. The work positions itself relative to classic Hopfield models (binary codes, symmetric weights), graded memory theories (Treves & Rolls), balanced EI networks, and recent E/I-constrained models that rely on saturation or global inhibition.

Strengths:
- A principled optimization framework that directly targets both fixed-point accuracy and local stability using SSA, which is novel in the context of memory networks with Dale’s law.
- Demonstrated analog (non-binary) memories with realistic, low-rate activity and emergent E/I balance without relying on saturation.
- Robust recall from noisy cues and under ongoing noise; quantitative comparison to an “ideal observer” is a useful diagnostic.
- Emergent sparsity of synaptic strengths and an analysis showing that many weak weights are dispensable for performance.
- Clear link to the ubiquitous reduction of trial-to-trial variability at stimulus onset, extending prior attractor-based explanations to graded patterns.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Scalability and computational cost are unclear. SSA optimization per memory requires Jacobian spectra and gradients; experiments are limited to n=150, m=30 (capacity ~0.2). No scaling analysis in n or m, nor comparison to capacity bounds for graded codes under E/I constraints.
- The inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ are optimized per memory (n_I m additional free variables). While biologically interpretable (inhibitory responses differ across memories), this inflates degrees of freedom during training. It would be helpful to quantify how much this flexibility contributes to capacity and stability, and whether similar performance can be achieved if v_inh are not optimized per memory.
- Fixed-point accuracy is not exact at the optimized targets (dv/dt ≠ 0), with the claim that a nearby true fixed point exists. The paper would be stronger with explicit verification by locating the actual fixed points post-training and reporting their SA/SSA and distances to the targets, as well as basins of attraction/margins.
- Biological plausibility of connectivity: emergent sparsity is encouraging but relies on a dense parameterization plus L2 regularization. The claim that “structural sparseness” is respected is only partially supported; true sparse architectures (fixed connectivity mask) and distance-dependent constraints are not evaluated.
- The explanation of trial-to-trial variability reduction is suggestive rather than explanatory; the mechanism is consistent with the phenomenon, but the claim that it “explains” the effect may overreach without analysis of transient dynamics, timescales, and modulation by stimulus strength.
- No spiking implementation is shown; although the gain function choice is justified, demonstrating that the learned rate connectivity transfers to LIF networks would greatly increase impact.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall; the use of SSA for robust local stability is appropriate and well motivated. More rigorous validation around exact fixed points, stability margins, and capacity scaling would improve confidence.
- Experiments convincingly show recall and robustness for the tested scale. The ideal-observer comparison is helpful.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well organized. The optimization objective and parameterization are well explained.
- Missing details: computational complexity; gradient computation for SSA; sensitivity to ε, η_s, η_F; number of optimization steps; convergence criteria.
- Some references are incomplete; polishing would help.

Originality:
- Using robust control tools (SSA) to stabilize analog attractors in E/I-constrained networks is novel and differentiates this work from prior autoassociative and balanced-network models.
- The auxiliary optimization of inhibitory steady-state activities per memory is an interesting modeling choice.

Significance:
- If scalable, this approach could provide a practical route to designing biologically plausible analog attractor networks. The link to balanced dynamics and variability reduction is timely and relevant.
- Current empirical scope (small networks) and the reliance on dense optimization limit immediate applicability, but the conceptual contribution is valuable.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
1. Provide scaling results: how do computation time, convergence, and recall performance vary with n and m? Any indications of asymptotic capacity under E/I constraints?
2. Quantify the contribution of optimizing v_inh^μ: compare to a version where only W is optimized (v_inh determined by dynamics), and/or regularize variability of v_inh across memories.
3. Locate the true fixed points post-training and report SA/SSA and distances to targets; estimate basins (e.g., maximal σ for successful recall).
4. Explore training with a fixed sparse connectivity mask and local wiring constraints; compare to emergent sparsity via L2.
5. Report stability margins (e.g., most positive real part of eigenvalues) across memories; relate margins to robustness and variability reduction.
6. Show transfer to spiking neurons using the trained W and matched f–I curves.
7. Clarify sensitivity to the SSA ε parameter and hyperparameters η_s, η_F.

Minor issues:
- Reference formatting is inconsistent/missing years and page numbers in several cases.
- Define units for noise explicitly; clarify the success threshold choice (0.001) and its interpretation in Hz for multi-neuron deviations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled method bringing robust control into biologically constrained memory networks.
- Demonstrates analog attractors with balanced E/I and realistic rates; robust to noise; connects to variability reduction.
- Clear step beyond binary/saturating or globally inhibited models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scale and unclear scalability/capacity; reliance on per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom.
- Incomplete validation of exact fixed points and stability margins; no spiking demonstration.
- Structural sparsity and biological wiring constraints not thoroughly addressed.

Overall assessment:
A strong and original methodological contribution with compelling demonstrations at modest scale. With additional validation on scaling, fixed-point verification, and either structural sparsity or spiking transfer, this could have high impact. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the main concerns.
