Summary
The paper presents a control-theoretic framework for constructing recurrent rate-based networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that store multiple analog memories as stable attractors while respecting key physiological constraints: Dale’s law, low and graded firing rates, and a dynamically balanced regime. The approach directly optimizes synaptic parameters so that a set of desired analog activity patterns become fixed points and are locally stable. Fixed points are encouraged by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at the target states, and stability is enforced by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target. Inhibitory steady-state activities at each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables during optimization, allowing the same inhibitory circuitry to assume different setpoints across memories. The resulting networks exhibit robust autoassociative recall from noisy cues, resilience to ongoing noise, tight E/I balance across neurons and attractors, sparsity in the effective synaptic weight distribution, and a reduction of across-trial variability following a stimulus-like cue, paralleling cortical observations.

Relation to prior work
The work extends classical attractor models (e.g., Hopfield) by supporting graded patterns without violating Dale’s law and without relying on output saturation to stabilize high-rate states. It addresses limitations in models with global inhibition and effectively binary memories (e.g., [8,9]) and departs from earlier graded-memory approaches that either required unrealistic assumptions or were difficult to realize dynamically [3–6]. The use of SSA from robust control [13,14] for stability optimization is an important methodological contribution in this context. The framework relates to broader efforts that shape recurrent dynamics via optimization (e.g., control-theoretic stabilization and RNN training), though a focused comparison to prior optimization-based RNN methods would strengthen the positioning.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A principled optimization framework that unifies analog memory storage, Dale’s law, and balanced E/I dynamics without saturating transfer functions.
- Technical rigor: Clear formulation of fixed point and stability objectives; appropriate use of SSA as a smooth surrogate to SA with practical gradients; inclusion of Dale’s law through a reparameterization that guarantees sign constraints.
- Biological relevance: Emergence of balanced E/I currents, low and graded rates, log-normal rate distributions, and variability quenching following stimulus onset (cf. [21]).
- Empirical demonstrations: Robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise; recall performance approaching an “ideal observer” bound; effective sparsity of learned weights and resilience to removal of many small-amplitude synapses.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Dynamics and objectives are well-specified; parameter choices are documented; behavior is illustrated with interpretable analyses.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Capacity and scaling: Results are shown primarily for n=150 with m=30 (capacity ~0.2). The paper would benefit from systematic capacity curves versus network size, ratio nI/nE, and number of patterns, including variance across runs. How does SSA-SA gap and optimization difficulty scale with n and m?
- Basins of attraction: Stability is evaluated locally via SSA/SA and empirically through recall success under random cue noise. Quantitative analysis of basin sizes (e.g., critical noise levels per memory, distributions across memories) would strengthen the claims, as would reporting success rates for cues corrupted in structured ways (e.g., partial clamping).
- Inhibitory “auxiliary” variables: Inhibitory steady states are optimized per memory without additional constraints. This is reasonable (a single inhibitory population can assume different rates in different attractors), but a brief justification that this does not amount to memory-specific parameterization would be helpful. For instance, are inhibitory rates approximately consistent across nearby attractors, and how sensitive is stability to constraining inhibitory setpoints?
- Structural sparsity: The network is dense by construction; functional sparsity arises post-optimization. Since cortical circuits are structurally sparse, an L1 penalty or explicit sparsity constraint would be a natural extension. Reporting performance under explicit sparsification (beyond threshold clipping) would increase biological plausibility and practical relevance.
- Generalization and robustness: Sensitivity analyses with respect to model and optimization hyperparameters (e.g., ε in SSA, time constants, gain exponent, external inputs, parameter perturbations) would help assess robustness. The choice ε=0.01·150/n is justified heuristically; exploring its impact on outcomes would be informative.
- Spiking instantiation: The paper argues that the rectified power-law gain supports transfer to spiking models. A small demonstration (e.g., LIF with matched gain) showing that trained rate connectivity yields similar attractors would substantially elevate significance.
- Related work: A more explicit comparison to optimization-based methods for shaping recurrent dynamics (e.g., approaches that stabilize target fixed points or trajectories) would situate the contribution more clearly.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and thoughtfully executed. The dual-objective formulation and use of SSA are appropriate. Empirical results support the main claims, though more extensive scaling, basin, and robustness analyses would consolidate the evidence base.

Clarity
The paper is clear, well-organized, and largely self-contained. A brief algorithmic summary (pseudocode) and explicit reporting of optimization convergence criteria would further aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. Applying robust control tools (SSA) to construct balanced E/I attractor networks with graded patterns under Dale’s law is novel and addresses long-standing gaps in the memory literature.

Significance
Moderate to high. The framework offers a principled way to engineer physiologically plausible attractor networks and provides insight into balancing and variability quenching. Its impact will be amplified by scalability analyses and a spiking demonstration.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide capacity scaling with N and nI/nE, and variance across seeds; quantify basin sizes and failure modes.
- Clarify the role and constraints of inhibitory auxiliary variables, possibly adding a variant with mild regularization tying inhibitory rates across memories.

Important but optional:
- Demonstrate transfer of learned connectivity to a spiking network with matched gain.
- Explore explicit structural sparsity (L1 penalty) and report performance–sparsity trade-offs.
- Add sensitivity analyses for ε and other key hyperparameters; report statistics over multiple optimization runs.
- Expand related-work discussion to include optimization-based RNN stabilization approaches.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a novel and principled method that respects key biological constraints.
- Strong empirical demonstrations: balanced regime, robustness, and variability quenching.
- Clear, well-executed, and broadly relevant to computational neuroscience and control-theoretic approaches to neural computation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evidence on scalability and capacity; basins and robustness not characterized comprehensively.
- No spiking instantiation; biological plausibility remains partially inferential.
- Structural sparsity not enforced; dense architectures may limit applicability.
