This is a clearly-written and novel submission that will be of significant interest to the neural computation community. The authors provide a well-contextualized review of prior work on training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting both the contributions and limitations of existing approaches. The network architecture and training procedure are presented with commendable clarity, and the figures are well-chosen to effectively illustrate the key results. Demonstrating the network’s performance limitations under noisy conditions underscores the robustness of the learned solution.

The paper presents a rate-based auto-associative memory model incorporating biological constraints often overlooked in previous work, specifically adherence to Dale’s Law – the principle of strictly excitatory or inhibitory neuronal projections – and the capacity to encode memories without relying on saturating or binary unit responses. Memories are encoded through synaptic modifications guided by a gradient descent procedure, constrained by a recently developed method ensuring the stability of the linearized dynamics around the network’s fixed points. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training approach through simulations, showing that the resulting fixed points exhibit stable, albeit slow, network dynamics. Notably, the trained network displays characteristics observed in cortical networks: a distribution of synaptic weights centered around zero with extended tails, and a balanced distribution of excitatory and inhibitory inputs to individual neurons. The authors argue that these features contribute to the network’s robust auto-associative performance, even when perturbed from a fixed point and subjected to stochastic noise.

My primary recommendation concerns clarifying the rationale behind the chosen training procedure in relation to the stated objectives. While the authors acknowledge previous methods for enforcing Dale’s Law and achieving graded memory representations, the latter is often more challenging to implement. The current procedure successfully achieves the desired goal of an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but the text doesn’t explicitly explain *why* this particular method was selected over alternatives. Was this method chosen because it was believed to be uniquely well-suited for this task? Would other training procedures likely yield comparable results? Exploring the specific advantages of this approach – what makes it particularly effective at achieving these objectives – would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, while perhaps exceeding the scope of this work, a comparison of the trained network’s performance against existing models in terms of network capacity (i.e., the number of memories encoded as a function of network size) would be highly valuable to the community. Given the extensive history of auto-associative network training, understanding how this model compares to established benchmarks is crucial. 

In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that leverages a recent method for stabilizing fixed points in dynamical systems, resulting in a network conforming to several key biological constraints, including the ability to encode memories using non-saturating and non-binary units. This is a significant contribution, and addressing the point regarding the justification for the chosen training procedure would further enhance the impact of this work.
