

This paper makes a significant contribution to the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks by introducing a biologically plausible framework that addresses key limitations in prior work. The authors correctly identify that previous approaches often neglected biological constraints—specifically, Dale's law, the use of binary representations in rate-based models, and non-sparse memory encodings. While individual aspects of these issues have been addressed separately, the authors present a unified strategy using a novel control-theoretic approach grounded in spectral bounding techniques. Their core innovation is a cost function balancing three critical terms: (1) ensuring fixed-point stability for target memories, (2) enhancing fixed-point stability, and (3) promoting small synaptic weights to prevent neuron saturation. Crucially, they enforce Dale's law directly through weight parameterization, naturally balancing excitation and inhibition without requiring explicit constraints. Remarkably, while not enforcing single-neuron saturation, it emerges organically from the optimization. The framework further leverages inhibitory neurons as optimization variables rather than fixed components, using only excitatory neurons for memory storage—a design consistent with biological observations. Numerical validation demonstrates robust noise tolerance and successfully explains experimental findings on reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset, attributed to convergence toward the nearest attractor from initial conditions.  

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, effectively bridging classical neural computation with modern control theory. It presents a compelling case for a biologically grounded associative memory mechanism, though it acknowledges the absence of a biologically plausible learning rule at this stage, noting promising directions for future work. The strategic use of inhibitory neurons as tunable resources is particularly insightful and central to the model’s success.  

**Minor concerns requiring clarification:**  
1. **Parameter sensitivity (Eq. 3):** The choice of γ = 0.04 appears arbitrary. The authors should quantify how results vary with γ (e.g., via sensitivity analysis) to confirm robustness.  
2. **Memory state distribution (Line 115):** The log-normal distribution for memory states lacks justification. Please clarify why this distribution is biologically or computationally motivated.  
3. **Fixed-point accuracy (Eq. 4):** The cost function may yield solutions deviating from exact fixed points. Quantify the typical deviation magnitude and discuss its impact on memory retrieval performance.  
4. **Clarity of statements (Lines 254–256):** The interpretation of the results here is unclear. Please rephrase to explicitly link the mathematical findings to the biological or computational implications.  
5. **Capacity analysis:** The paper omits discussion of memory capacity—the number of patterns the network can store reliably. A preliminary estimate or scaling analysis would strengthen the contribution.  
6. **Computational role of Dale’s law:** While the paper enforces Dale’s law for biological plausibility, it does not address whether this constraint *enhances* computational efficiency under specific constraints (e.g., energy, noise tolerance). A brief discussion of this distinction would be valuable, acknowledging that Dale’s law may stem from biophysical constraints rather than computational optimization.  

**Overall assessment:** This is a strong, well-executed paper that revitalizes associative memory research through a biologically motivated, control-theoretic lens. The framework elegantly resolves longstanding issues in the field, and the results are both theoretically sound and experimentally relevant. The writing is clear and concise, making the technical contributions accessible. The minor concerns are tractable and do not undermine the paper’s core contributions. The work represents an important step toward biologically plausible neural network models and merits publication at NIPS.
