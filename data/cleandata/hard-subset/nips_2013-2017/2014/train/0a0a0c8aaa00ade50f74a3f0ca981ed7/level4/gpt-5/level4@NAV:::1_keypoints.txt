- Proposal: An auto-associative memory model in a rate-based network that enforces biologically plausible constraints (notably strict Dale’s Law) and supports graded, non-saturating memory representations.  
- Training method: Synaptic updates derived from gradient descent combined with a recent technique that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Dynamics/results: Training produces attractor states with slow trajectories that lie near (but not exactly at) fixed points, and these states are stable.  
- Emergent network properties: Learned synaptic weights are centered near zero with heavy tails, and each unit shows approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance—consistent with cortical data.  
- Robustness: The model supports robust auto-associative retrieval under perturbations and stochastic input noise.  
- Presentation strengths: Paper is well-written and original, gives a clear, succinct overview of prior work, and presents architecture, training pipeline, and figures effectively.  
- Request for clarification: Authors should more clearly explain why the chosen training method was selected and how it specifically aligns with the goal of achieving graded (non-binary) representations.  
- Alternatives/intuitions: Reviewer asks whether other training strategies could produce similar results and requests intuition for why this particular approach is especially effective.  
- Capacity comparison: Suggestion to compare storage capacity (number of memories vs. network size) to established auto-associative memory models, if feasible.  
- Reviewer summary: Leveraging a recent stability-guaranteeing technique, the work constructs a biologically constrained auto-associative network that enables graded memory representations.
