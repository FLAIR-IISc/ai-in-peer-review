This paper presents a novel framework for designing rate-based attractor networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that can store and retrieve multiple analog memories. The authors' central contribution is to formulate the storage of graded activity patterns as a control-theoretic optimization problem. This approach is designed to overcome significant limitations of previous memory network models, such as the violation of Dale's law, a reliance on binary memory representations, or the requirement for neurons to operate at saturating firing rates. By minimizing a cost function that enforces both the fixed-point condition for each memory and the dynamical stability around it (using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa), the authors successfully construct networks that respect these biological constraints. The resulting optimized networks are shown to perform robust recall from noisy cues, operate in a tight balance of excitation and inhibition, and develop sparse synaptic weight distributions that are consistent with experimental findings in the cortex.

The main arguments in favor of accepting this paper are its originality, technical quality, and significance.

*   **Quality and Significance:** The work is technically sound and presents a compelling proof of concept. The methodology for directly optimizing network stability while satisfying physiological constraints is a significant advance for the field. By demonstrating that key features of cortical circuits—such as balanced E/I inputs and sparse connectivity—can emerge as a consequence of stabilizing graded memory patterns, the paper provides new insights into the functional organization of memory systems. The model's ability to reproduce the phenomenon of stimulus-induced variability quenching further strengthens its connection to real neural dynamics.

*   **Originality:** The application of robust control theory, and specifically the minimization of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, to the problem of memory storage in a biologically constrained E-I network is highly original. This provides a principled and powerful alternative to traditional approaches for building attractor networks, moving beyond the limitations of earlier models and opening new avenues for research.

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is described with sufficient detail for the reader to understand the approach, and the results are presented in a clear and intuitive manner.

The paper is very strong, and its weaknesses are minor and could be addressed with small revisions.

*   The optimization framework, while powerful, is based on a global, offline algorithm. As the authors acknowledge in the discussion, a critical next step is to understand how such an optimized connectivity structure could be learned through local, biologically plausible plasticity rules. Expanding this point slightly, perhaps by speculating on candidate mechanisms or key challenges, would further increase the paper's impact.

*   The results are demonstrated for a specific network size and memory load. While the core findings are convincing, a brief discussion on the scalability of the method—for instance, how storage capacity might scale with network size compared to classical models—would provide a more complete picture.
