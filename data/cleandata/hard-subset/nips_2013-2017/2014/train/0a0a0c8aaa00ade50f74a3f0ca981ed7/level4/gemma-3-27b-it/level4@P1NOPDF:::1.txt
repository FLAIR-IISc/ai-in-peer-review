Summary: The authors present a rate-based neural network model of auto-associative memory, designed with a focus on biological plausibility. Previous models of auto-associative memory have often lacked key features of real biological networks, such as adherence to Dale’s Law – the principle that neurons exert either strictly excitatory or inhibitory effects – and the ability to encode memories without relying on units that operate at saturation or in a binary fashion. Memories are encoded through synaptic modifications based on gradient descent, constrained using a recently published method to ensure stability of the dynamics around the system's fixed points. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure through simulations, showing that the resulting fixed points exhibit slow, stable network dynamics, as intended. They highlight two features of the trained network that align with experimental observations of cortical networks: a distribution of synaptic weights centered around zero with long tails, and approximately balanced excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input to individual network units. The authors argue that these features are crucial for the network’s robust auto-associative memory performance when perturbed from a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic noise.

Review: This is a clearly written and novel submission that will be of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a strong review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting the contributions and limitations of prior work. The network architecture and training procedure are both presented clearly, and the figures are well-chosen to quickly convey the main results. The authors also appropriately illustrate the network's performance limitations when noise is introduced, which underscores the robustness of the solution found by their training procedure.

One recommendation would be to further clarify the rationale behind the chosen training procedure in relation to the stated objectives. While the authors acknowledge other methods for enforcing Dale’s Law or achieving graded network responses – the latter often proving more challenging – the text doesn’t explain *why* this particular method was selected. The procedure successfully builds an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but it's unclear whether the authors believe this method is particularly well-suited for this task compared to alternatives. Could another training procedure achieve similar results? What specific aspects of this method contribute to its effectiveness?

Additionally (although perhaps beyond the scope of this paper), given the extensive history of training networks for auto-associative tasks, a comparison of this network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity – the number of memories encoded as a function of network size – would be valuable to the community. 

In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks by applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system. Notably, the model allows network units to encode memories without saturating or exhibiting binary responses.
