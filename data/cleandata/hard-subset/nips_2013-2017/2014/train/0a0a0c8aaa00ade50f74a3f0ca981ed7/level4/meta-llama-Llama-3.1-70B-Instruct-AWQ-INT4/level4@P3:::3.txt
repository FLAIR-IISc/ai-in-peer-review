The paper revisits the problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and valuable insights into an old problem. The authors astutely observe that previous work in the field often disregarded essential biological aspects, such as Dale's law, utilized binary memory representations in rate-based models, and employed non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a strategy that allows them to tackle all these concerns simultaneously.

The authors employ a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that address these issues. Specifically, they propose a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of the fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights, preventing saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network where excitations and inhibition are naturally balanced.

Interestingly, while the authors do not explicitly enforce saturation in single neuron firing, this emerges naturally through the solution of the optimization problem. Notably, the authors only utilize the excitatory neurons to implement the memory, which is consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically send output to other regions. They view the inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized over, which is an innovative and beneficial approach.

The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's robustness to noise. In addition to showcasing the network's good associative memory properties, the authors also explain experimental findings that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset. This phenomenon is attributed to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. The authors demonstrate how a biologically plausible associative memory may function, although they do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point. They hint at recent proposals that may lead to such rules, leaving room for future research.

Some minor issues and suggestions for improvement:

1. The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although it is expected to be small.

2. A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. The authors should provide a clear motivation for this choice.

3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. The severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.

4. The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification.

5. The authors do not mention capacity issues, which are an essential computational aspect. It would be beneficial to make some preliminary statement about this topic.

6. The authors could discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether it enhances computational power under specific constraints. This may not be the case, and it may be a result of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints, but it would be interesting to relate to this.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation, and addressing these minor issues will further enhance its quality and impact.
