This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective and adds substantial insight. The authors argue that much prior work either violated Dale’s law, relied on binary representations in rate-based models, or failed to achieve sparse, biologically realistic connectivity. While each of these issues has been addressed individually, the paper presents a unified control-theoretic framework that simultaneously enforces these constraints.

Concretely, the authors formulate memory storage as the joint satisfaction of two conditions: target patterns must be fixed points of the dynamics and locally stable. They construct an objective function with three terms optimized by gradient descent: (1) a fixed-point term minimizing the instantaneous velocity at the target states; (2) a stability term based on the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to ensure local stability; and (3) a weight-regularization term to prevent excessively large synapses. Dale’s principle is enforced by a log-exp reparameterization of synaptic weights, and inhibitory neurons are treated as auxiliary variables whose steady-state rates are optimized, whereas memories are represented in excitatory neurons (consistent with long-range output primarily arising from E cells).

The resulting networks store analog (graded) memory patterns, operate with balanced excitation and inhibition, and exhibit robust autoassociative recall in the presence of both corrupted cues and ongoing noise. Notably, even without imposing an explicit saturation nonlinearity, firing rates remain in a realistic range due to dynamically matched inhibition. The model also reproduces the widespread reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, explained here as convergence toward the appropriate attractor from diverse initial conditions.

Quality
- The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Using SSA for robust stability optimization is appropriate and addresses the non-smoothness of the spectral abscissa. The experimental validation is convincing: simulations demonstrate successful recall, robustness to input corruption and noise, emergent E/I balance, and sparse weight structure. The observation that weak synapses can be pruned with minimal performance loss (except for one attractor) is informative.
- One methodological point deserves quantification: because the fixed-point term may not reach zero, the learned memories can be “slow points.” The paper should quantify the residual drift and its effect on recall (e.g., distances to nearest true fixed points and time-to-drift under noise-free dynamics).

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally clear and well organized. The modeling choices, constraints, and optimization strategy are explained coherently.
- Please clarify lines 254–256, which were hard to interpret as written.
- The log-normal choice for target firing-rate distributions (line 115) should be motivated. While log-normal-like skew and heavy tails have empirical support, a brief justification and perhaps sensitivity analysis would help.
- The choice γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3): a short sensitivity analysis or qualitative discussion would reassure readers that results are not finely tuned to this parameter.

Originality
- The unified, control-theoretic synthesis of E–I networks that embed analog attractors while obeying Dale’s law and achieving balanced dynamics is novel. Treating inhibitory steady-state activities as optimization variables is also an original and fruitful design choice. This goes beyond earlier approaches that relied on binary memories, symmetry constraints, global inhibition, or saturation-based stabilization.

Significance
- The framework offers a principled way to construct biologically plausible attractor networks with robust recall, balanced activity, and realistic firing regimes. It provides a compelling account of stimulus-induced variability quenching and suggests a path toward spiking implementations, given the close match between the rectified power-law gain used here and cortical f–I curves. The work could influence both theoretical studies of memory and the design of neuromorphic systems.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unified solution to multiple longstanding biological constraints (Dale’s law, graded patterns, balanced E/I, low-rate operation).
- Technically solid use of robust control (SSA) to guarantee local stability, with effective numerical implementation.
- Strong empirical validation: robust recall under cue corruption and ongoing noise; emergent sparsity; E/I balance across attractors; variability reduction consistent with cortex.
- Conceptual clarity in using inhibitory neurons as auxiliary variables and excitatory neurons as the memory substrate.

Cons and points to address
- Fixed-point accuracy: quantify how close target states are to true fixed points and how this affects recall robustness.
- Parameter sensitivity: discuss sensitivity to γ and, if feasible, to other key hyperparameters (e.g., SSA smoothing).
- Capacity: provide at least preliminary capacity estimates or scaling behaviors (e.g., memories per neuron) under this framework.
- Dale’s law: discuss whether (and how) enforcing Dale’s law changes computational power or robustness under specific constraints, or whether it primarily reflects biological constraints without conferring computational benefits.
- Clarify lines 254–256.
- Motivate the log-normal distribution for memory patterns and, if possible, report whether results depend strongly on this choice.

Minor comments
- Please correct “attractor closets” to “attractor closest” (in the discussion).
- Consider briefly relating the emergent sparsity to the weight regularization used (L2), and why sparsity arises despite the lack of an explicit L1 penalty.

Overall assessment
A clear, well-executed paper that tackles a classic problem with modern control-theoretic tools, yielding a biologically plausible framework for analog associative memories. While a biologically plausible learning rule is not provided, the paper points to promising directions (e.g., inhibitory plasticity). I found the use of inhibitory neurons as resources particularly compelling. This is a strong and impactful contribution.
