This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, adding substantial new insight. The authors note that much prior work neglected biological constraints such as Dale’s law, relied on binary memory representations in rate-based models, and employed non-sparse memory codes. Although these issues have been considered individually elsewhere, the authors present a unified strategy to address them simultaneously. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding to construct networks that satisfy these constraints. In this framework, they optimize a cost function with three terms via gradient descent: the first enforces that the target patterns are fixed points, the second promotes stability of those fixed points, and the third penalizes large weights to prevent saturation. By encoding Dale’s principle directly in the weight parameterization, the model overcomes the aforementioned limitations and yields a network with naturally balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, although single-neuron saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges from the optimization. Importantly, only excitatory neurons are used to implement the memory (consistent with excitatory cells providing long-range outputs), while inhibitory neurons are treated as variables to be optimized. The authors validate the approach with numerical simulations and demonstrate robustness to noise. Beyond strong associative memory performance, the model also accounts for experimental findings of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset (a property likely common to associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing), explained by convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper. It tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools and demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory may function. While a biologically plausible learning mechanism is not yet proposed, the authors point to recent developments that may lead to such rules. The use of inhibitory neurons as resources is interesting and beneficial. Overall, the paper makes a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues:
- The authors set gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (3). Please discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice (I expect it to be small).
- A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide motivation for this choice.
- The cost function (4) may produce solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. How severe is this deviation? This should be quantified and discussed.
- Lines 254–256 are unclear; please clarify.
- Capacity is not discussed. Even a preliminary statement about capacity would be valuable.
- Please discuss potential computational benefits of Dale’s law. Can this constraint enhance computational power under specific conditions, or is it primarily a consequence of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints? It would be helpful to relate to this question.
