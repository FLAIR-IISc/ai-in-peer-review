This is a well-written and novel contribution that will be of significant interest to the field. The authors provide a helpful overview of previous work on training neural networks for auto-associative memory, clearly outlining the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches. Both the network architecture and training methodology are presented lucidly, and the figures effectively communicate the key findings. The authors appropriately acknowledge the network's performance limitations under noisy conditions, underscoring the robustness of the developed training procedure.

I recommend the authors further clarify the rationale behind their choice of training procedure in relation to the stated goals. While other methods exist for enforcing Dale’s Law and achieving graded response dynamics, the latter has historically proven more challenging. The current procedure successfully achieves a non-saturating auto-associative network, but the text does not explain *why* this particular method was selected, or if it offers advantages over alternatives. Exploring whether this method is uniquely suited for this task, and why, would strengthen the manuscript.

Additionally, while potentially exceeding the scope of this work, a comparison of the network's capacity – the number of memories encoded as a function of network size – against existing models would be highly valuable to the community, given the extensive literature on auto-associative memory training. In summary, the authors present a new model of auto-associative memory that incorporates biological constraints, specifically leveraging a recent method for stabilizing fixed points to create a network capable of encoding memories with graded, non-saturating responses.
