This manuscript presents a novel approach to designing rate-based neuronal networks capable of storing multiple stable activity patterns, effectively serving as memories. The authors aim to address limitations of existing attractor network models, specifically those violating Dale's law or exhibiting saturated activity levels within memory states. Their method involves a gradient descent optimization of a cost function that minimizes deviations from desired attractor states, regularizes network weights via the Frobenius norm, and maximizes attractor stability. Key findings include a learned weight distribution resembling those observed experimentally, and a balanced excitation-inhibition dynamic within the attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written and clearly explains the model, training procedure, and results. The integration of biological constraints with the resulting network properties makes this a compelling contribution to the field of attractor neural networks. The authors successfully train a rate-based network to support graded, non-saturating attractors by optimizing both synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity. Imposing biologically plausible constraints on synapses and neurons leads to a network-level balance of excitation and inhibition.

This work builds on a rich history of research into attractor networks. The original Hopfield network [1] established the foundational principles, but required symmetrical connectivity violating Dale’s law and often resulted in binary memory representations [2]. Subsequent work attempted to address these limitations, exploring non-binary representations [3, 4] and incorporating inhibitory neurons [7, 8, 9], yet often faced challenges related to capacity [5, 6] or relied on saturating neuron dynamics. More recent models have explored spatially structured networks [10, 11] or spike-timing based approaches [12], but often at the cost of generality. This manuscript distinguishes itself by offering a unified control-theoretic framework that overcomes many of these constraints simultaneously. 

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses key limitations:** The paper directly tackles significant shortcomings in previous attractor network models, such as the violation of Dale’s law and the tendency towards saturated activity.
* **Biologically plausible constraints:** The incorporation of biological constraints (Dale’s law, realistic firing rates) enhances the relevance and potential applicability of the model.
* **Novel control-theoretic framework:** The use of robust control theory (specifically the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) provides a principled and effective method for optimizing network stability.
* **Balanced E/I dynamics:** The resulting networks exhibit a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition, a crucial feature of cortical circuits.
* **Clear presentation:** The manuscript is well-written and clearly explains the model, methods, and results.
* **Robustness and Noise:** The demonstration of robustness to noisy recall cues and ongoing noise adds to the biological plausibility and practical value of the model.
* **Reduction of Trial-to-Trial Variability:** The demonstration that the network reduces trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is a compelling result, aligning with experimental observations [21].

**Weaknesses:**

* **Rate-based model:** The use of a rate-based model abstracts away the complexities of spiking dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest potential extensions, it remains a limitation. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how the results might translate to spiking networks.
* **Parameter tuning:** The heuristic selection of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) in the cost function could be perceived as somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of these parameters might strengthen the results.
* **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly mentioned, optimizing large networks with this method could be computationally expensive.
* **Limited Exploration of Learning Rules:** The paper focuses on *supervised* learning of the network weights. Exploring biologically plausible unsupervised learning rules that could achieve similar results would be a valuable extension.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in computational neuroscience – understanding the neural basis of memory – with a novel and principled approach.
* The results are technically sound and supported by both theoretical analysis and computational experiments.
* The manuscript is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts involved.
* The work has the potential to advance our understanding of cortical circuits and inspire new research in the field.
* The successful demonstration of robustness and biologically realistic dynamics strengthens the significance of the findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The rate-based model is a simplification of biological reality, and the connection to spiking networks is not fully explored.
* The heuristic parameter tuning introduces a degree of arbitrariness.
* While the results are promising, the model has not been tested on a wide range of tasks or compared directly to alternative approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of attractor neural networks by developing a novel and biologically plausible model that overcomes several limitations of previous approaches. Despite the limitations mentioned above, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, with a minor revision request to briefly discuss potential implications and translation to spiking networks, and potentially to explore the sensitivity of the results to the heuristic parameter choices.
