- Proposes a biologically plausible rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces Dale's Law.  
- Demonstrates memories can be encoded without relying on saturating or binary unit responses.  
- Training uses gradient descent constrained by a recent method that guarantees stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Trained fixed points show slow dynamics (near but not exact fixed points) while preserving intended stability.  
- The trained network exhibits synaptic weight distributions centered near zero with long tails.  
- Individual units show a balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs, matching cortical observations.  
- These synaptic and balance features are identified as key to robust memory performance under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Manuscript is well-written, innovative, and likely of significant interest to the field.  
- Provides a thorough review of prior work and clearly explains architecture, training method, and experiments; figures effectively convey results.  
- Evaluates robustness to noise, showing the proposed training procedure maintains performance under noisy conditions.  
- Suggests improving the explanation of why the chosen training method was selected and whether alternatives could achieve similar outcomes.  
- Recommends, if possible, comparing memory capacity (number of memories vs. network size) to prior models as a valuable extension.
