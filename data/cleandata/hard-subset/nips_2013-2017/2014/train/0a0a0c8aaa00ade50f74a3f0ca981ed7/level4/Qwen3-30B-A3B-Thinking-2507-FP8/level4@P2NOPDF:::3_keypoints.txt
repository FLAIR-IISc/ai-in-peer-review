- Revisits associative memory with a biologically grounded, control-theoretic approach that addresses longstanding limitations.  
- Identifies three key shortcomings in prior models: violation of biological constraints (e.g., Dale’s law), binary representations in rate models, and non-sparse memory codes.  
- Proposes a unified strategy using spectral-bounding control methods to address these issues simultaneously.  
- Introduces a cost function optimized by gradient descent balancing (1) fixed-point stability for target memories, (2) enhanced fixed-point stability, and (3) small synaptic weights to avoid saturation.  
- Enforces Dale’s law directly via weight parameterization, producing natural excitation–inhibition balance without extra constraints.  
- Uses excitatory neurons to store memories and treats inhibitory neurons as tunable optimization variables, aligning with biological projection patterns.  
- Optimization yields non-saturating single-neuron firing rates and the network shows robustness to noise in simulations.  
- Explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset as convergence toward attractors, noting this may be a general associative-memory effect.  
- Effectively bridges control theory and neuroscience, offering a biologically plausible mechanism for associative memory despite lacking a biologically feasible learning rule.  
- Insight: treating inhibitory neurons as tunable resources is particularly beneficial for balanced dynamics and network performance.  
- Minor concern 1: No sensitivity analysis for γ=0.04 in Eq. (3); authors should discuss dependence on this parameter.  
- Minor concern 2: The choice of a log-normal distribution for memory states needs biological or computational justification.  
- Minor concern 3: The cost function may produce approximate rather than exact fixed points—quantify deviations and implications for memory fidelity.  
- Minor concern 4: Lines 254–256 are unclear and require clarification.  
- Minor concern 5: The paper does not address capacity (number of storable memories); a preliminary discussion is needed.  
- Minor concern 6: The computational benefits of enforcing Dale’s law should be discussed—whether and when it enhances computational power versus being purely biophysical.
