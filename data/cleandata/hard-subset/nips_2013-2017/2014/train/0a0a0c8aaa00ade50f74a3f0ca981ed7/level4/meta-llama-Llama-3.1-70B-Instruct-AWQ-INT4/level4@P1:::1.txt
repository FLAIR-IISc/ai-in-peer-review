Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network subject to a battery of biologically plausible constraints. Previous models of auto-associative memory have failed to include several key features of real biological networks, namely adherence to Dale's Law, which states that neurons have a strictly excitatory or inhibitory effect on their projections, and the observation that networks can encode memories without relying on units that simply respond at their saturation rate or respond in a binary manner. Memories are encoded in the network via synaptic modifications based on a gradient descent procedure, constrained using a recently published method for ensuring that the linearization of the dynamics around a dynamical system's fixed point is stable.

Review: This is a clearly-written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors nicely review the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are both presented in a clear way, and the figures are well-chosen to quickly illustrate the main results of the submission. The authors also effectively demonstrate the network's performance limitations when network noise is included, which highlights the robust solution the training procedure finds.

One recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors noted, other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, with the latter condition seemingly more difficult to enforce. The chosen training procedure achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but nothing in the text indicates that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly well-suited to this task. Perhaps another network training procedure would achieve the same result? Can anything be said as to why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?

Additionally, a comparison of how the trained network performs against previous models in terms of network capacity (number of memories as a function of network units) would surely be of interest to the community. This could provide valuable context for understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

Overall, the submission is well-written and effectively conveys the main contributions of the work. The authors have made a significant effort to ensure that the network architecture and training procedure are presented in a clear and concise manner. With some minor revisions to address the above points, this submission has the potential to make a significant impact on the field.

Strengths:

* Clearly-written and well-organized submission
* Effective review of the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks
* Novel and biologically plausible model of auto-associative memory
* Strong demonstration of the network's performance limitations when network noise is included

Weaknesses:

* Lack of clarity regarding the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives
* Limited comparison to previous models in terms of network capacity

Recommendations for improvement:

* Clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives
* Consider adding a comparison to previous models in terms of network capacity
* Provide additional context for understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed model
