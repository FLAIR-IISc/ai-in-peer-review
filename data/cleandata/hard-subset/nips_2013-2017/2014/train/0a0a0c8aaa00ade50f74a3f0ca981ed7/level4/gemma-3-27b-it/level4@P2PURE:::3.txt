This paper revisits the problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and significant new insights. The authors identify key limitations in prior work, including the neglect of biological constraints like Dale’s law, the use of binary representations within rate-based models, and non-sparse memory representations. While these issues have been addressed individually in previous studies, this work presents a unified strategy leveraging a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding techniques to overcome all of them simultaneously. Specifically, they introduce a cost function, optimized via gradient descent, comprising three terms: one promoting fixed-point memories, another ensuring their stability, and a third encouraging small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle through the weight parameterization, the authors achieve a network with naturally balanced excitation and inhibition, addressing the aforementioned shortcomings. Notably, saturation emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization process, rather than being explicitly enforced at the single neuron level. The authors intelligently utilize excitatory neurons to implement the memory, consistent with their typical role in transmitting output, while treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. The efficacy of their approach is demonstrated through numerical simulations showcasing robust associative memory properties and noise tolerance. Furthermore, they successfully explain experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset – a phenomenon linked to convergence towards the nearest attractor, common to associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity. 

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation with innovative control-theoretic tools, demonstrating a potential mechanism for biologically plausible associative memory. While a biologically plausible learning rule is not proposed, the authors acknowledge recent work that could inform future developments in this area. The authors’ approach of treating inhibitory neurons as a resource is particularly insightful and beneficial. Overall, the paper represents a clear, concise, and important contribution to the field of neural computation.

A few minor issues remain:

The authors employ \gamma=0.04 in equation (3). Discussing the sensitivity of the results to this parameter choice would be valuable, though a limited impact is anticipated. 

The justification for the log-normal distribution proposed for memory states on line 115 would benefit from further motivation. 

The cost function (4) may yield solutions that deviate from perfect fixed-point memories. Quantifying and discussing the extent of this violation would strengthen the analysis.

Lines 254-256 require clarification as the statements presented are not immediately clear.

The paper does not address the issue of memory capacity. Including even preliminary observations regarding this crucial computational aspect would be beneficial. 

Finally, discussing the potential computational advantages conferred by Dale’s law – for instance, whether it enhances computational power under specific constraints – would be interesting. While Dale’s law may simply reflect evolutionary or biophysical constraints, relating the work to this broader context would be valuable.
