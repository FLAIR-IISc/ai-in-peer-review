- Presents a control-theoretic framework to build rate-based attractor networks that store multiple graded (analog) memory patterns.  
- Uses a cost-minimization approach and gradient descent to ensure desired patterns are fixed points and dynamically stable.  
- Leverages Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization to provide a differentiable upper bound on the largest real part of the Jacobian eigenvalues for stability.  
- Trained networks successfully store and recall dozens of analog patterns and are robust to cue and ongoing noise.  
- Optimization yields emergent biological features: sparse synaptic weight distribution and tight per-neuron E/I balance.  
- Model reproduces stimulus-induced variability quenching seen experimentally.  
- Technically sound work with simulation results that strongly support the claims.  
- Methodology and SSA application are clearly explained with sufficient implementation detail.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally well-written, organized, and supported by clear figures.  
- Novel synthesis of robust control theory and computational neuroscience, departing from traditional Hebbian approaches.  
- Significant contribution: demonstrates a biologically constrained functional memory network and suggests a rationale for cortical E/I balance and sparsity.  
- Strength: unifies multiple biological constraints (Dale’s law, graded memories, low non-saturating firing rates) in one model.  
- Strength: robust autoassociative recall performance comparable to an ideal observer.  
- Weakness: learning uses a global, offline optimizer (L-BFGS), which is biologically implausible and needs discussion of local-rule approximations.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of scalability and capacity (results shown for n=150, m=30); computational scaling and m/n behavior are unclear.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to cost-function hyperparameters (ηs, ηF) is only heuristic and would benefit from analysis.  
- Overall assessment: excellent, novel, and impactful work with strengths far outweighing weaknesses; strongly recommended for acceptance.
