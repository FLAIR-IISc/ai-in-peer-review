- Revisits associative memory with a fresh perspective and significant new insights.  
- Identifies overlooked biological constraints in prior work: Dale’s law, binary rate-based memories, and non-sparse representations, and addresses them jointly.  
- Leverages a recent control-theoretic approach using spectral bounding techniques to construct networks.  
- Introduces a three-term cost function optimized by gradient descent to (1) encourage desired fixed points, (2) promote their stability, and (3) keep weights small to avoid saturation.  
- Enforces Dale’s principle via weight parameterization, yielding naturally balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Does not explicitly enforce neuron saturation; saturation emerges from optimization.  
- Implements memories using only excitatory neurons while treating inhibitory neurons as tunable variables.  
- Validates the approach with numerical simulations and shows robustness to noise.  
- Explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset as convergence to the nearest attractor.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and presents an important contribution; use of inhibitory neurons as tunable resources is particularly interesting.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule yet, but cites recent proposals that could lead to one.
