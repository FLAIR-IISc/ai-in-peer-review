

Summary: This paper introduces a novel model of auto-associative memory in rate-based neural networks that rigorously adheres to key biological constraints. The authors address critical gaps in prior work by enforcing Dale’s Law (neurons projecting exclusively excitatory or inhibitory signals) and avoiding unit saturation or binary responses in memory encoding. Memory storage is achieved via gradient-descent synaptic updates constrained by a recent method ensuring stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points. Simulations demonstrate that the trained fixed points exhibit slow convergence to stability (i.e., they are stable but not trivially static) and maintain robust memory retrieval under stochastic noise. Crucially, the trained networks replicate two experimentally observed cortical features: (1) synaptic weight distributions centered at zero with long tails, and (2) balanced excitatory-inhibitory input per unit. The authors posit these properties are essential for robust memory performance during perturbations.

Strengths: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. The historical context of auto-associative memory models effectively highlights the specific limitations of prior work, setting up the novelty of this contribution. The network architecture and training procedure are described with precision, and the figures efficiently convey key results. The demonstration of how the method yields biologically plausible network properties—particularly the balanced E/I input and weight distributions—significantly strengthens the case for its relevance to real neural systems. The analysis of noise robustness further underscores the method’s practical utility.

Weaknesses and Recommendations: While the technical contribution is strong, the paper would benefit from greater clarity regarding the *rationale* for the chosen training procedure. The authors note that previous methods have addressed Dale’s Law or graded responses, but they do not explicitly justify why their specific constrained gradient descent was selected as the optimal approach for *both* constraints simultaneously. A brief discussion comparing this method to alternatives (e.g., weight clipping, penalty terms) would strengthen the argument. For instance: *“While alternative methods for enforcing Dale’s Law exist (e.g., [Ref]), our approach uniquely integrates stability constraints with graded encoding by leveraging [X], avoiding the need for explicit saturation penalties that often degrade memory capacity.”* Without this, the reader cannot assess whether the method’s success is inherently tied to its specific formulation or could be replicated more simply.

Additionally, while the paper focuses on biological plausibility, a brief mention of network capacity (memories per unit) relative to prior models would provide immediate context for the community. This is a standard metric for auto-associative systems, and even a single sentence comparing capacity (e.g., “Our network achieves capacity comparable to [Prior Work] despite stricter biological constraints”) would be valuable. Given the paper’s scope, this could be added concisely without altering the core contribution.

Overall Assessment: This is a high-quality, novel contribution that makes a significant step toward biologically grounded auto-associative memory models. The work addresses well-defined limitations in the literature with a technically sound approach and compelling biological validation. The writing is clear, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are thoroughly analyzed. The suggested clarifications would elevate an already strong paper to an exceptional one.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper’s novelty, technical rigor, and biological relevance make it a valuable addition to the field. The minor suggestions for clarification are straightforward to address and would enhance the paper’s impact.
