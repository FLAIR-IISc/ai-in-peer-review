- Presents an auto-associative memory model in a rate-based neural network that enforces biological constraints like Dale’s Law and non-saturating unit responses.  
- Uses synaptic modifications via gradient descent combined with a method to ensure linearized dynamics around fixed points are stable.  
- Demonstrates through simulations that trained fixed points exhibit slow, near-fixed-point dynamics and are stable.  
- Trained networks show biologically consistent features: synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate excitatory/inhibitory input balance per unit.  
- Shows robustness of memory retrieval under perturbations and stochastic input noise.  
- Clearly written, novel, and likely of significant interest to the community with well-chosen figures.  
- Provides a comprehensive historical review of prior work on training networks for auto-associative tasks.  
- Recommends clarifying why the specific training procedure was chosen over alternatives and whether it is uniquely suited to enforce graded, non-saturating memories.  
- Asks whether alternative training methods could achieve comparable results and requests justification for this method’s selection.  
- Suggests, possibly for future work, comparing the trained network’s memory capacity (memories vs. network units) to previous models.  
- Overall summary: introduces a biologically constrained auto-associative memory model that achieves stable, graded memories using a recent stability-enforcing training method.
