

The paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a novel perspective, offering significant new insights. The authors identify that prior work often neglected biological constraints—specifically, Dale's law, binary memory representations in rate-based models, and non-sparse memory representations. While individual aspects of these limitations have been addressed separately, the authors develop a unified strategy to resolve all three. They employ a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that simultaneously satisfy these constraints. The approach introduces a cost function with three balanced terms optimized via gradient descent: the first term ensures the fixed-point nature of memories, the second enforces fixed-point stability, and the third promotes small weights to prevent neuron saturation. By directly embedding Dale's law into the weight parameterization, the authors address all prior shortcomings, yielding a network where excitation and inhibition are naturally balanced. Notably, while saturation at the single-neuron level is not explicitly enforced, it emerges naturally from the optimization. The authors utilize only excitatory neurons for memory implementation (consistent with excitatory neurons typically projecting output to other regions), treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. Numerical simulations validate the network’s noise robustness and associative memory properties, and the model also explains experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset—attributed to convergence toward the nearest attractor from the initial state (a feature common to associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing).  

Overall, this is a clear, well-written paper that tackles a foundational problem in neural computation using innovative control-theoretic methods, demonstrating a biologically plausible associative memory mechanism. While the authors do not yet propose a biologically plausible learning rule, they reference recent work that may enable such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources is particularly insightful and beneficial. The contribution is clear, concise, and significant for the field.  

Minor concerns:  
1. The choice of $\gamma=0.04$ in Eq. (3) requires sensitivity analysis, though small effects are anticipated.  
2. The log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) needs justification.  
3. The cost function (Eq. 4) may produce solutions violating exact fixed-point memory requirements; quantify and discuss the severity.  
4. Claims in lines 254–256 are unclear and require clarification.  
5. Capacity limitations—critical for computational relevance—should be addressed, even preliminarily.  
6. The computational benefits of Dale’s law warrant discussion: does it enhance computational power under specific constraints, or is it a consequence of evolutionary/biophysical constraints?
