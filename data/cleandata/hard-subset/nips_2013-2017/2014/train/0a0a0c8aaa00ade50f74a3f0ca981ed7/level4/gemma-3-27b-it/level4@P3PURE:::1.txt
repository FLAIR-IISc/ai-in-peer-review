Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory implemented in a rate-based neural network, incorporating several biologically plausible constraints. Previous models of auto-associative memory have often lacked key features of real biological networks, such as adherence to Dale’s Law – the principle that neurons exert strictly excitatory or inhibitory effects – and the ability to encode memories without relying on units that respond at saturation or in a binary manner. Memories are encoded via synaptic modifications based on a gradient descent procedure, constrained using a recently published method for ensuring the stability of the linearization of the dynamics around a dynamical system’s fixed point. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure through simulations, showing that the resulting fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics (i.e., they are close to, but not exactly at, fixed points) and are stable, as desired. They note two features of their trained network that align with experimentally observed cortical network characteristics: a distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails, and a balance of excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input to the average network unit. The authors identify these features as key components contributing to the network’s success in performing robust auto-associative memory when perturbed from a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clearly written and novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a helpful review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting the contributions and shortcomings of prior work. Both the network architecture and the training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures are well-chosen to quickly illustrate the main results. The authors demonstrate the network’s performance limitations when noise is present, which underscores the robustness of the solution found by their training procedure.

One recommendation would be to clarify the rationale behind the chosen training procedure in relation to the stated objectives. While the authors acknowledge that other methods exist for enforcing Dale’s Law or achieving graded network memories – the latter being particularly challenging – the text does not explain why this specific method was selected. The procedure successfully achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but it is unclear whether this method was chosen because it was believed to be particularly well-suited for this task. Could other network training procedures achieve comparable results? Further insight into *why* this method is effective would be valuable.

Also, while perhaps beyond the scope of this paper, given the extensive history of training networks for auto-associative tasks, a comparison of the trained network’s performance against previous models in terms of network capacity (the number of memories as a function of network units) would be of significant interest to the community. Applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the condition that network units are capable of encoding memories without saturating or binary responses.
