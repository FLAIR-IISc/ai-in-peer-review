**Summary**

The authors present a rate-based neural network model for auto-associative memory that incorporates several biologically plausible constraints. Unlike previous models, this network adheres to Dale's Law (neurons are strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and, crucially, encodes memories using graded, non-saturating responses rather than binary or fully saturated activity. Memories are encoded by modifying synaptic weights via a gradient descent procedure. This procedure incorporates a recent method that constrains the learning to ensure the stability of the fixed points in the system's dynamics. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through simulations, showing that the trained network produces stable, slow dynamics around the target memory states.

Notably, the trained network develops two features consistent with experimental observations in the cortex: a heavy-tailed distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero, and an approximate balance between total excitatory and inhibitory input to each neuron. The authors identify this E/I balance and weight distribution as key factors contributing to the network's success in robustly retrieving memories, even when perturbed or subjected to stochastic noise.

**Review**

This is a clearly-written and novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors effectively review the history of auto-associative networks, highlighting the key contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures are well-chosen to illustrate the paper's main results. The authors also demonstrate the network's performance limitations in the presence of noise, which serves to highlight the robustness of the solution found by their training procedure.

One recommendation for improvement is to better clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors note, other methods have been used to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded responses, with the latter being a particularly challenging goal. While the proposed training procedure successfully builds a network with non-saturating units, the text does not explain why this specific method was chosen over others, or what makes it particularly well-suited for this task. Could another training procedure achieve the same result? Further insight into why this particular method is so effective at producing graded responses would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, while perhaps beyond the scope of the current work, a comparison of this network's performance to previous models—particularly in terms of memory capacity (number of stored memories as a function of network size)—would be a valuable contribution and of significant interest to the community.
