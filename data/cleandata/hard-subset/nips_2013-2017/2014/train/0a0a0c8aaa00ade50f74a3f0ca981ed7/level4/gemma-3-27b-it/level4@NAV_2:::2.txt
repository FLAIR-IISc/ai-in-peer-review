In this manuscript, the authors develop a rate-based neuronal network designed to store a set of stable activity patterns, representing memories. The aim is to address limitations in existing attractor models, specifically the violation of Dale’s law and the saturation of neuronal activity within memory states. The network is trained using gradient descent on a cost function that minimizes deviations from desired attractor states and the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, while maximizing attractor stability. Key results include a weight distribution consistent with experimental observations and a balance between excitation and inhibition in the attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written and clearly presented. The model and training procedure are readily understandable, and the presentation of results is effective. The combination of biologically plausible constraints incorporated into the model and the resulting network characteristics represent a compelling contribution to our understanding of attractor neural networks. Specifically, the authors successfully train a rate-based network to support graded, non-saturating attractors by optimizing both synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activities. Imposing constraints for biological realism results in a network-level balance of excitation and inhibition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses key limitations of existing models:** The work directly tackles well-known issues in attractor network theory – namely, the incompatibility with Dale’s law and the tendency towards saturated activity states – offering a significant improvement over previous approaches [1, 2].
*   **Biologically plausible constraints:** The inclusion of Dale’s law and realistic firing rate dynamics (threshold-quadratic gain function) enhances the biological relevance of the model [15, 16].
*   **Novel optimization framework:** The use of robust control theory, specifically the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13, 14], provides a principled method for ensuring stability while optimizing network parameters.
*   **Emergent network properties:** The resulting networks exhibit important emergent properties, including sparse connectivity [19, 20], balanced excitation and inhibition [8, 9], and robustness to noise. The reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is particularly intriguing, aligning with observed cortical phenomena [21].
*   **Clear presentation:** The manuscript is well-organized, clearly written, and the figures are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Rate-based simplification:** The use of a rate-based model, while simplifying analysis, abstracts away important details of spiking dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest a path towards translating the results to spiking networks, the limitations remain.
*   **Parameter tuning:** The choice of parameters for the optimization process (ηs, ηF, ε) appears somewhat heuristic. Further investigation into the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Limited exploration of network capacity:** While the results demonstrate successful storage of a moderate number of memories (m=30, n=150), a more systematic investigation of the network’s capacity and its dependence on network size and connectivity patterns would strengthen the findings.
*   **SSA parameter:** The scaling rule applied to the smoothness parameter ε of the SSA (ε = 0.01 · 150/n) is not rigorously justified and could potentially influence the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in computational neuroscience – the neural basis of memory – and proposes a novel and theoretically sound solution.
*   The work demonstrates a significant advance over previous models by overcoming key limitations and incorporating biologically realistic constraints.
*   The results are well-supported by simulations and analysis, and the manuscript is clearly written and well-organized.
*   The emergent network properties and the connection to experimental observations (e.g., balanced excitation/inhibition, variability reduction) suggest that the model captures important aspects of cortical function.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a rate-based model limits the biological realism and generalizability of the findings.
*   The heuristic nature of parameter tuning and the limited exploration of network capacity raise some concerns about the robustness and scalability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. The strengths of the work – the novel approach, biologically plausible constraints, and emergent network properties – outweigh the weaknesses. While further investigation into the limitations mentioned above would be valuable, the current manuscript presents a compelling and well-supported study. I recommend acceptance.
