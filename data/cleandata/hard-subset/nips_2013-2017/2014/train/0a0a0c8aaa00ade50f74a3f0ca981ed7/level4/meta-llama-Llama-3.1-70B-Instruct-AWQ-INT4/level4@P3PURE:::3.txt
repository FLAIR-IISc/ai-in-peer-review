The paper offers a fresh perspective on the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, providing significant new insights into an old problem. The authors observe that previous work in this field often overlooked important biological aspects, such as Dale's law, used binary memory representations in rate-based models, and employed non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been individually addressed in other studies, the authors develop a comprehensive strategy to tackle all these concerns simultaneously. Specifically, they utilize a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that address these issues. This approach involves a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of the fixed points, and the third term promotes small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network where excitations and inhibitions are naturally balanced. Notably, although they do not explicitly enforce saturation in single-neuron firing, this property emerges naturally through the solution of the optimization problem. Importantly, the authors use only excitatory neurons to implement the memory, which is consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically send output to other regions, and view inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's robustness to noise. In addition to showcasing the network's good associative memory properties, the authors also provide an explanation for experimental findings that trial-to-trial variability decreases following stimulus onset, which they attribute to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools, demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function. Although the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point, they hint at recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is an interesting and beneficial aspect of the paper. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues require attention:

1. The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although it is expected to be small.

2. A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. The authors should provide motivation for this choice.

3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. The severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.

4. The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification.

5. The authors do not mention capacity issues, which are an essential computational aspect. It would be nice to include some preliminary statement about this topic.

6. It would be beneficial if the authors could discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether it can be shown to enhance computational power under specific constraints. Although this may not be the case, and the law may be a result of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints, relating to this aspect would be valuable.

Overall, the paper is a clear and well-written contribution to neural computation, tackling a classic problem using novel control-theoretic tools and demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function.
