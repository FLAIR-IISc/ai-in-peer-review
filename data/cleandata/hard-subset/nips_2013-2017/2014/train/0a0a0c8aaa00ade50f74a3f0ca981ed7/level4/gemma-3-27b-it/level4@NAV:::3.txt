This paper offers a fresh and insightful contribution to the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks. The authors compellingly argue that prior work often overlooks crucial biological constraints – specifically Dale’s law, the use of binary representations within rate-based models, and non-sparse memory representations – and, uniquely, present a unified framework to address all these limitations. Their approach leverages a recent control-theoretic framework, employing spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that adhere to these biological principles. 

The core of their method lies in a carefully designed cost function, balanced across three key terms: fixed-point memory formation, fixed-point stability, and weight regularization to prevent saturation. By directly incorporating Dale’s principle into the weight parameterization, they achieve a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. Notably, saturation emerges as a consequence of the optimization process rather than being explicitly imposed at the single neuron level. The authors intelligently utilize excitatory neurons for memory implementation, aligning with known neurobiological outputs, while treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated through robust numerical simulations, exhibiting resilience to noise. Beyond the core associative memory functionality, the model successfully explains experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability upon stimulus onset, attributing this to convergence towards the nearest attractor state.

In summary, this is a clearly presented and well-written paper that skillfully applies innovative control-theoretic methods to a fundamental problem in neural computation, providing a compelling perspective on biologically plausible associative memory function. While a biological learning rule remains an open question, the authors acknowledge and hint at relevant ongoing research. The creative use of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is a particularly noteworthy aspect of their work. Overall, this paper represents a significant and concise contribution to the field.

I have a few minor suggestions for improvement:

The value of γ = 0.04 used in equation (3) warrants further discussion regarding the sensitivity of the results to this specific parameter choice.
The motivation behind the proposed log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) could be clarified.
The potential for the cost function (4) to yield solutions that deviate from exact fixed-point memories should be quantitatively assessed and discussed in terms of the severity of such violations.
Lines 254-256 require clarification for improved readability.
Addressing the capacity of the network, even with preliminary analysis, would strengthen the work. This is a critical consideration for associative memory models.
Exploring the computational advantages conferred by Dale’s law—whether it enhances computational power under specific conditions—would be a valuable addition. While acknowledging that this may reflect biophysical or evolutionary constraints, relating the findings to this broader context would be beneficial.
