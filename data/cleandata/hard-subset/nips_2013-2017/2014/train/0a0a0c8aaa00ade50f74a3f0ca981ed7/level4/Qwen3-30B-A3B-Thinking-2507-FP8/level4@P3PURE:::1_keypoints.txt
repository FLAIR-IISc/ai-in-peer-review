- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological constraints, including Dale’s Law and non-saturating, graded unit responses.  
- Memories are encoded via synaptic modifications trained with gradient descent constrained by a recent method that ensures stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Simulations show trained fixed points are nearly stationary and achieve the desired stability.  
- Trained networks reproduce two experimentally observed cortical features: synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails, and balanced excitatory/inhibitory input per neuron.  
- The authors argue these features are critical for robust auto-associative memory performance under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- The manuscript is well-written and novel, with a clear historical review and lucid descriptions of network architecture and training.  
- Figures effectively illustrate core results, and demonstrations of robustness to perturbation and noise strengthen the presentation.  
- Major concern: the paper lacks justification for choosing this specific training procedure over alternative methods for enforcing Dale’s Law and graded responses.  
- Reviewer asks whether alternative training methods could achieve equivalent results and requests discussion of why this method is uniquely effective.  
- Suggestion: include (or discuss) a comparison of network capacity (number of memories versus units) against prior models to address a central question in auto-associative memory research.
