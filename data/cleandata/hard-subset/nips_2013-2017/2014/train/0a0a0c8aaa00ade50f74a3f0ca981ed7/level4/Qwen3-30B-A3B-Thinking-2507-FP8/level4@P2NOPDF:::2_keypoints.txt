- Introduces a rate-based neuronal network that stores multiple stable activity patterns while enforcing biological constraints.  
- Resolves two key shortcomings of prior attractor models: violations of Dale’s law and saturation of neural activities.  
- Uses a biologically constrained gradient-descent training procedure minimizing activity changes, penalizing weight norms (Frobenius), and enhancing attractor stability.  
- Trained networks produce synaptic weight distributions that closely match experimental neurophysiological observations.  
- A balanced excitation-inhibition (E/I) ratio spontaneously emerges in memory states due to the imposed biological constraints.  
- Demonstrates maintenance of graded, non-saturated memory representations without violating neurobiological principles.  
- Manuscript is very well written, clearly structured, and explains model architecture, training, and results accessibly.  
- Integration of biological constraints into model design is compelling and directly explains emergent realistic network properties.  
- Advances attractor network theory by showing biological plausibility can be preserved without sacrificing computational stability.  
- Experimental validation is thorough; training reliably produces stable attractors with minimal activity drift.  
- Comparison to cortical weight distributions strengthens empirical credibility and addresses saturation issues in traditional models.  
- Minor suggestions: relate model stability metrics to experimental memory-persistence measures and clarify how the Frobenius norm prevents weight overfitting.
