- Presents a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biologically plausible constraints (Dale’s Law, non-saturating units).  
- Memories are learned via synaptic updates using gradient descent with stability enforced by a stable-linearization method.  
- Trained fixed points exhibit slow (near-fixed) dynamics and the intended stability properties in simulations.  
- Model reproduces cortical-like synaptic weight distributions (zero-centered, heavy-tailed).  
- Network shows balanced excitatory–inhibitory input per unit, matching experimental observations.  
- These biological features support robust memory retrieval under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Paper provides clear, novel contribution and good contextualization of prior auto-associative training methods.  
- Network architecture, training procedure, and figures are well-explained and effectively convey results.  
- Demonstrates robustness to network noise, reinforcing the efficacy of the method.  
- Needs a clarification on why stable-linearization was chosen over alternative methods that also enforce Dale’s Law or graded responses.  
- Questions whether simpler or alternative training procedures could achieve comparable results.  
- Suggests (for future work) comparing memory capacity (retrievable memories vs. network size) to prior models for practical context.  
- Overall assessment: well-executed, technically sound work that bridges theoretical models and biological constraints.
