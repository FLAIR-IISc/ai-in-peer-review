- Revisits associative memory with a fresh perspective and significant new insights.  
- Simultaneously addresses neglected biological constraints (Dale’s law, non-binary and sparse memory representations).  
- Employs a control-theoretic spectral-bounding approach to construct networks under these constraints.  
- Introduces a three-term cost function (fixed-point, stability, small weights) optimized via gradient descent.  
- Directly enforces Dale’s law in weight parameterization, producing balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Single-neuron firing saturation emerges naturally from the optimization without explicit enforcement.  
- Uses excitatory neurons to implement memories and treats inhibitory neurons as optimization resources.  
- Numerical simulations demonstrate noise robustness and strong associative-memory performance.  
- Explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via attractor convergence.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and applies novel control-theoretic tools to a classic problem.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule (only hints at recent proposals).  
- Fails to analyze sensitivity to the chosen γ = 0.04 in Eq.(3); this should be discussed.  
- The log-normal distribution for memory states is unmotivated and needs justification.  
- Cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate exact fixed-pointness; the extent should be quantified.  
- Omits discussion of capacity and whether Dale’s law confers computational benefits under constraints.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-presented, biologically plausible associative-memory construction.  
- Arguments against acceptance: no biologically plausible learning mechanism, no capacity analysis, and unexplored sensitivity to γ.
