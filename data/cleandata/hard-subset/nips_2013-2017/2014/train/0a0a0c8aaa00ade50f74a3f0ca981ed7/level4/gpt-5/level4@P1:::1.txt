Summary:
This paper presents a rate-based autoassociative memory network that satisfies several key biological constraints simultaneously. The model enforces Dale’s law by separating excitatory and inhibitory populations, allows neurons to participate with graded (analog) activity rather than binary responses, avoids saturating firing rates, and operates in a dynamically balanced regime. The authors formalize memory storage as the joint satisfaction of two conditions for each target pattern: (i) the pattern should be a fixed point (or very close to one), and (ii) the dynamics should be locally stable around it. They optimize synaptic weights and the steady-state inhibitory activities using a control-theoretic objective that combines a fixed-point criterion with a robustness term based on the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa that facilitates gradient-based stability optimization.

The target memories are analog activity patterns for the excitatory population drawn from a log-normal distribution (including a uniform baseline state). Inhibitory steady states are treated as auxiliary variables and optimized jointly with the weights. The trained networks exhibit robust recall from noisy cues and in the presence of ongoing noise. Recall performance approaches that of an “ideal observer” that simply selects the nearest stored pattern. The learned connectivity shows a heavy-tailed weight distribution with many small weights; pruning very weak synapses leaves most attractors intact with minimal performance loss. The networks also display tight excitation–inhibition balance across attractors and reproduce the trial-to-trial variability reduction observed in cortex following stimulus onset.

Review:
This is a clearly written and novel contribution that will interest the NIPS community. The paper situates itself well relative to prior work on autoassociative memory, emphasizing limitations of classic Hopfield-type models (symmetry, binary representations), LIF-based approaches that rely on saturation, and models with global inhibition or strong spatial assumptions. The proposed control-theoretic approach directly targets both fixed-point and stability criteria while obeying biological constraints, and the figures communicate the main results effectively. The simulations are thorough: they document recall robustness, the emergence of sparse heavy-tailed synapses, tight E/I balance, and variability quenching, all consistent with experimental observations.

One point that would benefit from clarification is the rationale for the specific optimization framework beyond empirical success. Several strategies have been explored previously to enforce Dale’s law or support graded memories, and the latter is typically more challenging. The paper would be strengthened by explaining why the SSA-based optimization with the chosen parameterization is particularly well suited to this task (e.g., how it avoids saturating solutions and promotes balanced dynamics), and whether alternative training procedures (e.g., different stability surrogates or regularizers) might achieve similar outcomes. The authors already note that the optimized states are “slow points” rather than exact fixed points in the loss, with nearby exact fixed points emerging in practice; a short discussion of why the optimization tends to produce nearby true attractors would improve clarity.

Given the rich history of autoassociative models, a capacity comparison (number of memories vs. network size) would be very informative, even if only approximate or empirical, and would help contextualize the significance of the approach relative to prior graded-memory models. While such a study may be beyond the present scope, a brief discussion of expected capacity, dependencies on the choice of stability margin (ε) and regularization, and scaling behavior would be valuable to readers.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with clear objectives and a principled stability surrogate. Claims are supported by simulations that probe recall accuracy, robustness to cue corruption and ongoing noise, network balance, and synaptic structure. The observation that trained states are slow points but yield nearby true attractors is candid and appropriate.

Clarity: The manuscript is well organized and clearly written. The problem setup, optimization objective, and constraints are described transparently, and the figures are well chosen. Minor clarifications as noted above would further strengthen the exposition.

Originality: The combination of a control-theoretic stability objective (SSA) with biologically constrained, rate-based E/I networks to embed analog attractors is novel and contrasts with previous reliance on saturating nonlinearities, global inhibition, or binary-like codes.

Significance: The results are important for understanding how balanced E/I networks can support analog memory without saturating activity, and the link to experimentally observed variability quenching is compelling. The approach is likely to influence future work on constructing stable attractor networks under realistic constraints.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses multiple key biological constraints simultaneously (Dale’s law, graded activity, balanced E/I, non-saturating units).
- Principled stability optimization using SSA; clear formulation and robust empirical results.
- Emergence of realistic synaptic weight distributions and E/I balance; variability reduction consistent with cortical data.
- Clear writing and strong figures.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of why the chosen optimization framework is particularly appropriate versus alternatives; lack of ablation or comparison across training methods.
- No empirical or theoretical capacity analysis relative to prior models.
- The trained states are optimized as slow points rather than exact fixed points; a more explicit explanation of convergence to nearby true attractors would help.

In sum, this is a strong, well-presented paper with a novel and effective approach. Clarifying the methodological rationale and providing some capacity context would further enhance its impact.
