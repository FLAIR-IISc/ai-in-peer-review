- Revisits associative memory in RNNs from a control-theoretic perspective, enforcing Dale’s law and storing analog attractors without saturation.
- Frames memory storage as an optimization enforcing fixed points, local stability (via smoothed spectral abscissa), and weight regularization; reparameterizes weights to satisfy Dale’s law and treats inhibitory units as auxiliary steady-state variables.
- Positions work relative to Hopfield and bump models: learns arbitrary analog patterns, robust to noise, and reproduces stimulus-driven variability quenching.
- Strength: unified, principled optimization that simultaneously enforces fixed points, stability, and biological constraints.
- Strength: novel and well-motivated use of the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) enabling gradient-based stability optimization.
- Strength: resulting networks operate in balanced E/I regimes with low firing rates and robustness to cue corruption and ongoing noise.
- Strength: interprets inhibitory units as stabilizing auxiliaries while excitatory neurons carry memory content.
- Strength: clear empirical evaluation, including comparisons to an ideal observer and explanation of variability quenching.
- Weakness: limited sensitivity analyses—needs robustness checks for γ, ε (SSA), and regularization strength.
- Weakness: log-normal memory pattern choice is under-motivated and needs empirical justification or tests of alternatives.
- Weakness: optimization often yields “slow points” rather than exact fixed points—requires quantification of residual dv/dt and distance to true fixed points and impact on recall basins.
- Weakness: storage capacity is not characterized—needs at least preliminary scaling analysis (memories vs. network size, sparsity).
- Weakness: unclear passage around lines 254–256 requires revision and clarification.
- Weakness/question: unclear whether enforcing Dale’s law provides computational benefits (stability, generalization, capacity) or is mainly biological realism.
- Quality: method is technically sound; SSA-based stability term and weight parameterization are appropriate; experiments are convincing but would benefit from more quantitative analyses.
- Clarity: generally well written; clarify log-normal motivation, effects of nonzero dv/dt at targets, and the ambiguous passage.
- Originality: novel control-theoretic formulation using SSA for learning analog attractors under Dale’s law.
- Significance: provides a practical recipe for biologically grounded analog attractor networks, connects to variability quenching, and suggests extensions to spiking networks and local learning rules.
- Suggestions: perform sensitivity sweeps for γ, ε, and regularization; justify or test alternative pattern distributions; quantify fixed-point approximation errors and relate to recall; include preliminary capacity estimates; discuss computational trade-offs of Dale’s law; clarify lines 254–256.
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled framework, strong empirical performance, emergence of balanced E/I dynamics, and connection to neural variability phenomena.
- Cons for acceptance: missing capacity analysis, limited sensitivity studies, under-motivated choices (pattern distribution, parameters), and absence of a biologically plausible learning rule.
- Minor comments: discuss sensitivity to γ=0.04; motivate log-normal distribution with data; quantify slow-point severity; rephrase lines 254–256; add preliminary storage capacity discussion; comment on whether Dale’s law confers measurable computational advantages.
- Overall assessment: clear and compelling contribution applying control tools to build biologically grounded analog attractor networks; stronger with added sensitivity and capacity analyses.
