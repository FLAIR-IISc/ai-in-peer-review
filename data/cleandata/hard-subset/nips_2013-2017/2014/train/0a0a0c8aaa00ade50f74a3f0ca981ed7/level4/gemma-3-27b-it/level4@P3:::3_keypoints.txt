- Paper introduces a control-theoretic (smoothed spectral abscissa) approach to build biologically constrained associative memories.
- Addresses prior limitations by enforcing Dale’s law, moving beyond binary memories, and promoting sparsity.
- Optimizes a cost balancing fixed-point attraction, dynamical stability, and weight sparsity via gradient descent.
- Dale’s principle is enforced by weight parameterization, producing naturally balanced excitation and inhibition.
- Saturation of neural activity emerges from optimization rather than being imposed explicitly.
- Inhibitory neurons are treated as auxiliary variables and optimized alongside connectivity—a conceptually novel choice.
- Numerical simulations show robustness to noise and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability as convergence to attractors.
- Paper is clearly written and well-presented, with strong explanatory power and potential field impact.
- Lacks sensitivity analysis for the chosen γ = 0.04; parameter sensitivity should be explored.
- Rationale for using log-normal distributions for memory states is insufficiently justified or compared to alternatives.
- The extent of fixed-point violations from the cost formulation is not quantified and its effect on recall fidelity is unclear.
- Specific text (lines 254–256) requires clarification to make the logic immediately apparent.
- No discussion or estimation of memory capacity is provided, which is a significant omission.
- Potential computational benefits of enforcing Dale’s law are not investigated.
- Biological validation is limited (no detailed comparison to data) and the paper does not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism.
- Reviewer recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add capacity discussion, γ sensitivity analysis, log-normal justification, fixed-point violation quantification, and clarify lines 254–256.
