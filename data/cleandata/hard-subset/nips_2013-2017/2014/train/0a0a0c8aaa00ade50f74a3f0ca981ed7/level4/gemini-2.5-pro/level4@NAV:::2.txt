This paper introduces a rate-based neural network designed to store multiple stable activity patterns, which function as memories. The model is specifically developed to address significant limitations of previous attractor networks, such as the violation of Dale's law and the saturation of neuronal activity in memory states. The network is trained via gradient descent on a cost function that simultaneously minimizes activity changes within the target attractors, minimizes the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, and maximizes the overall stability of the memory states. The principal findings of this work are the emergence of a synaptic weight distribution consistent with experimental observations and the establishment of a balance between excitation and inhibition within the network's attractor states.

The manuscript is clearly written. The descriptions of the model, the training protocol, and the subsequent results are easy to follow and well-presented. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of attractor neural networks by effectively integrating biologically plausible constraints into the model's design, which in turn gives rise to emergent, physiologically relevant network properties after training. In this study, the authors demonstrate a trained rate-based neural network capable of sustaining graded and non-saturated attractor states encoded through both synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity. Notably, the imposition of biologically realistic constraints on both neurons and synapses results in the spontaneous emergence of an excitatory-inhibitory balance across the network.
