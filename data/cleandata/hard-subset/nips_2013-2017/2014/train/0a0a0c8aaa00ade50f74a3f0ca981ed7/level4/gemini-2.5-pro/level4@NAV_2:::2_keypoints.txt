- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to design rate-based excitatory–inhibitory attractor networks that store and retrieve multiple analog memories.  
- Frames graded memory storage as an optimization problem that enforces fixed-point conditions and dynamical stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa.  
- Overcomes prior limitations (violations of Dale’s law, binary memories, saturating firing rates) by producing biologically constrained networks.  
- Optimized networks exhibit robust recall from noisy cues.  
- Networks operate in a tight excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Learned synaptic weights are sparse, matching cortical experimental findings.  
- Model reproduces stimulus-induced variability quenching seen in real neural dynamics.  
- Work is original in applying robust control theory to biologically constrained memory networks.  
- Methodology is technically sound and represents a significant advance for the field.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear, well organized, and well motivated.  
- Main limitations: current optimization is a global offline algorithm; needs exploration of biologically plausible local learning rules.  
- Additional limitation: results shown for a specific network size and load—discussion of scalability and storage-capacity scaling is needed.
