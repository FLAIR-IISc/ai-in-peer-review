This paper revisits the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks from a fresh angle, offering substantial new insight. The authors point out that much prior work either violated Dale’s law, relied on binary codes in rate-based models, or yielded recall states with neurons near saturation. While individual studies have addressed some of these limitations separately, the authors present a unified strategy that simultaneously handles all of them. Concretely, they leverage a recent control-theoretic methodology based on spectral bounding ideas to construct networks that satisfy these biological constraints. Their formulation introduces a cost with three components optimized via gradient methods: one term encourages the desired memories to be fixed points, another enforces dynamical stability of those fixed points, and a regularizer keeps weights small to avoid saturation. By explicitly parameterizing synapses to satisfy Dale’s principle, the resulting networks exhibit naturally balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, although the model does not impose saturating single-neuron nonlinearities, the optimization yields stable, low-rate operating regimes. The memory content is implemented in the excitatory population (consistent with the predominant long-range signaling role of excitatory neurons), while inhibitory units are treated as auxiliary variables to be optimized. The authors validate the approach through simulations, demonstrating robust autoassociative recall under corruption and ongoing noise. Beyond recall, the model captures the widely observed reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, which here arises from convergence toward the attractor closest to the initial condition—a phenomenon broadly consistent with associative dynamics around a spontaneous baseline.

In sum, the paper is clear and well-organized. It addresses a classic question in neural computation with modern control-theoretic tools and provides a compelling illustration of how a biologically plausible associative memory might operate. Although a local, biologically plausible learning rule is not derived, the authors point to recent proposals that could bridge this gap. Treating inhibitory neurons as resources to be allocated by the optimization is an insightful and effective design choice. Overall, this is a concise and meaningful contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues:
- The authors set γ = 0.04 in eq. (3). Please comment on the sensitivity of the results to this choice (likely modest, but worth discussing).
- A log-normal distribution for memory states is introduced around line 115. Please provide additional justification for this choice.
- The cost function (4) can admit solutions that only approximately satisfy the fixed-point condition. How large are these deviations in practice? Please quantify and discuss their impact on recall and stability.
- Lines 254–256 were unclear on first reading; a brief clarification would help.
- Capacity is not discussed. Even a preliminary analysis or empirical scaling result would be valuable.
- Please comment on the computational implications of Dale’s law. Does enforcing sign constraints provide benefits under particular resource or robustness constraints, or is it mainly a biophysical/evolutionary constraint? Relating to this would strengthen the broader perspective.

A clear, well-crafted paper that tackles a classic problem with contemporary control-theoretic techniques and illuminates how a biologically plausible associative memory may operate.
