- Tackles constructing biologically plausible attractor networks that store/retrieve analog patterns while respecting physiological constraints.  
- Builds on prior work (Hopfield, balanced networks) with a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes network parameters.  
- Optimization minimizes time derivative at target patterns to enforce fixed points and uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability.  
- Method enforces Dale’s law and supports graded, non-saturating neural activity without binary encoding or artificial saturation.  
- SSA-based stability optimization solves an intractable Jacobian-based stability problem in prior approaches.  
- Resulting networks show robust memory recall under noise and match experimental firing-rate distributions.  
- Sparse connectivity emerges from stability optimization (not from L2 regularization), acknowledged and analyzed by the authors.  
- Presentation is clear, well-motivated, and detailed enough to enable reproducibility.  
- Integrates Dale’s law, graded analog patterns, and dynamic E/I balance in a unified, original framework.  
- Provides a novel biological insight: explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability for analog memories.  
- Significant advance toward biologically plausible memory substrates, with relevance to cortical memory studies and potential applications.  
- Limitation: no experimental exploration of transferring optimized connectivity to conductance-based spiking networks.  
- Limitation: modest storage capacity reported (0.2 for 30 patterns) compared to theoretical maxima, framed as a trade-off for biological realism.  
- Overall recommendation: methodologically rigorous, addressing a meaningful gap—reviewer recommends acceptance.
