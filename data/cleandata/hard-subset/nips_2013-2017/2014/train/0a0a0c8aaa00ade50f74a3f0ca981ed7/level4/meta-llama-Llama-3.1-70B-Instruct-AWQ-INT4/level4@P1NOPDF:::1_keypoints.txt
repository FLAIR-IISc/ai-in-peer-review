- Paper presents a biologically plausible rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces Dale’s Law.  
- Model encodes memories without requiring units to operate at saturation or binary firing rates.  
- Synaptic updates are derived from a gradient-descent procedure constrained by a recent method that ensures the linearized dynamics around fixed points are stable.  
- Trained networks exhibit slow, stable dynamics around fixed points as demonstrated in simulations.  
- Trained synaptic weight distribution is centered near zero with long tails, matching experimental cortical observations.  
- Network units receive roughly balanced excitatory and inhibitory inputs on average, consistent with cortical data.  
- E/I balance and zero-centered, heavy-tailed weights are identified as crucial for robust memory performance under perturbations and input noise.  
- Manuscript is clearly written, novel, and likely of high interest to the community.  
- Authors provide a thorough literature review and clearly present the network architecture, training procedure, and illustrative figures.  
- Experiments show the network’s performance limitations under noise and highlight the robustness of the learned solution.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on why the specific training procedure was chosen versus alternative methods and whether alternatives could achieve similar results.  
- Reviewer suggests (beyond scope) comparing memory capacity (memories per unit) against previous models.
