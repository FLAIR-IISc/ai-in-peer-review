Summary:
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing autoassociative memory networks that respect key biological constraints. The authors optimize rate-based networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons, enforcing Dale’s law and allowing graded (analog) activity patterns rather than binary codes. Memories are embedded by directly optimizing the network so that designated states are fixed points and locally stable. The fixed-point condition is enforced by minimizing the state derivatives at target patterns; stability is achieved by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based optimization. The resulting networks operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime, avoid firing-rate saturation, and recall memories robustly from noisy cues and under ongoing noise. The learned synaptic weights are heavy-tailed with many small values, and the network exhibits variability reduction after stimulus onset, both consistent with cortical data. The authors also show that many weak synapses can be pruned with little loss of recall performance.

Strengths:
- Clearly written and well structured, with helpful figures that communicate the approach and results.
- Addresses long-standing shortcomings of classic models (e.g., symmetric connectivity, binary memories, saturation, global inhibition) within a single unified framework.
- Technically solid formulation: fixed-point enforcement complemented by stability optimization via SSA provides a principled route to stable attractors.
- Biologically grounded constraints (Dale’s law, balanced E/I, graded responses) are explicitly enforced.
- Robust performance in the presence of significant input noise and during ongoing fluctuations; realistic reduction in across-trial variability following stimulus onset.
- Insightful analysis of learned connectivity: heavy-tailed weights and functional sparsity, with recall supported primarily by a subset of stronger synapses.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Rationale for the specific training method could be clearer. Why is SSA-based optimization particularly well suited to enforcing graded, non-saturating attractors compared to alternative approaches (e.g., other convex relaxations, Lyapunov-based methods, or structured regularization)? A brief justification or ablation would strengthen the argument.
- No direct comparison of storage capacity with prior models. Even a limited empirical capacity curve (memories vs. network size) or a qualitative comparison to classic theoretical limits would be valuable, given the extensive literature on associative memory capacity.
- The target states are “slow points” rather than exact fixed points before convergence, and the width of basins is not detailed. Reporting empirical basin sizes (as a function of cue noise) would help contextualize robustness.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The methods appear technically sound and well-supported by simulations. The SSA-based stability objective is appropriate and implemented carefully. More quantitative comparisons would further bolster the claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized; the link between objectives and method could be explained more explicitly, especially the choice of SSA and the trade-offs introduced by ε.
- Originality: The integration of control-theoretic stability optimization with biologically constrained E/I rate networks for analog attractors is novel and addresses multiple physiological constraints simultaneously.
- Significance: The results advance our understanding of how balanced E/I networks can store and robustly recall analog memories without saturation. The variability reduction result and learned connectivity structure provide compelling connections to cortical phenomenology.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified and principled framework satisfying multiple biological constraints.
- Robust recall from noisy cues, with realistic E/I balance and firing rates.
- Clear exposition and strong empirical demonstrations.
- Potentially influential bridge between control theory and memory network design.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited comparative evaluation (capacity and method ablations).
- Insufficient discussion of why the chosen optimization is uniquely effective versus alternatives.

In sum, by applying SSA-based stability optimization to enforce stable fixed points in an E/I network that obeys Dale’s law and supports graded responses, the paper offers a compelling and biologically grounded model of analog autoassociative memory with robust performance under noise.
