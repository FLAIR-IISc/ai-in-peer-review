**Summary**

The authors present a rate-based neural network model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several key biological constraints. Previous models have often failed to incorporate key biological features, such as Dale's Law (neurons being strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and the ability to encode memories using graded, non-saturating firing rates rather than binary or saturated responses. In this model, memories are encoded via synaptic modifications using a gradient descent procedure. This training is constrained by a recently published method that ensures the stability of the system's dynamics around its fixed points.

Simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of this training procedure, showing that the resulting memory states are stable attractors with slow dynamics, as desired. Notably, the trained network exhibits two features consistent with experimental observations in cortical circuits: a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and a balance between the total excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input received by a typical unit. The authors identify these emergent properties as key to the network's ability to perform robust auto-associative recall, successfully returning to a memory state after being perturbed, even in the presence of stochastic noise.

**Review**

This is a clearly-written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a strong review of the history of auto-associative memory models, effectively highlighting the contributions and shortcomings of prior work. The network architecture and training procedure are presented with clarity, and the figures are well-chosen to illustrate the main results. The demonstration of the network's performance limitations in the presence of noise effectively underscores the robustness of the solution found by the training procedure.

A point for clarification regards the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. The authors note that other methods have been used to enforce Dale's Law or to achieve graded responses in memory networks, with the latter being a particularly challenging goal. While the chosen training procedure successfully produces a network with non-saturating units, the text does not explain why this particular method was selected for this task over others. Could other training procedures achieve a similar result? Further discussion on why this particular method is so effective at producing graded, non-saturating responses would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, while perhaps beyond the scope of the current work, a comparison of this model's memory capacity (i.e., the number of stored memories as a function of network size) against previous models would be of significant interest to the community.
