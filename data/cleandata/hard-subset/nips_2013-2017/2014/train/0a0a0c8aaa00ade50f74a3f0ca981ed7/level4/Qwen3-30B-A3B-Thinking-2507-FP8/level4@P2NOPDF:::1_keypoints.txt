- Introduces a biologically plausible rate-based auto-associative memory model that respects Dale's Law and avoids unit saturation.  
- Trains the network with a gradient-descent procedure constrained to ensure stability of linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Trained fixed points exhibit slow, stable dynamics and robust memory encoding under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Reproduces two experimentally observed cortical features: synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and balanced average excitatory/inhibitory input per unit.  
- Identifies the zero-centered, heavy-tailed weights and E/I balance as essential for robust memory performance.  
- Clear, novel contribution with concise, comprehensive review of prior work and exceptional clarity of presentation and figures.  
- Analysis of performance under network noise effectively demonstrates robustness of the training method.  
- Lacks a clear justification for choosing this specific training procedure over alternative methods and why it uniquely achieves non-saturating, graded responses.  
- Would benefit from a brief comparison of memory capacity (memories vs. network size) to established auto-associative models for context on scalability.  
- Overall strong paper; minor clarifications would improve impact — recommendation: accept.
