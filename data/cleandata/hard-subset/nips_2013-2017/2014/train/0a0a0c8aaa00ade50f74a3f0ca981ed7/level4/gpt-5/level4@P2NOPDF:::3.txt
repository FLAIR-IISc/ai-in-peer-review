This paper revisits associative memory in neural networks with a fresh and rigorous perspective, offering substantial new insight into a long-standing problem. The authors note that much prior work overlooks key biological constraints (e.g., Dale’s law), relies on binary memories within rate-based models, and employs non-sparse memory representations. While each of these issues has been addressed individually before, the paper presents a unified framework that handles all of them simultaneously.

Methodologically, the authors leverage a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding to construct networks that satisfy these constraints. They propose a three-term cost function optimized by gradient descent: one term enforces that stored patterns are fixed points, a second promotes stability of these fixed points, and a third penalizes large weights to avoid saturation. By directly embedding Dale’s principle in the weight parameterization, the network naturally balances excitation and inhibition. Notably, although single-neuron saturation is not explicitly imposed, it emerges from the optimization. The model uses excitatory neurons to store and express the memories—consistent with their role in projecting to other regions—while treating inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized, effectively using inhibition as a resource.

The paper validates the approach through numerical simulations and shows robustness to noise. Beyond standard associative memory behavior, the model reproduces the empirically observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset, which is plausibly explained by rapid convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition. Although the paper does not yet provide a biologically plausible learning rule, it points to recent developments that could be adapted.

Overall, the work is clear, well written, and addresses a classic problem with novel control-theoretic tools, yielding a biologically grounded associative memory model. The use of inhibitory neurons as optimization variables is especially interesting and appears beneficial. The contribution is concise, technically solid, and relevant to neural computation.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Equation (3) uses γ = 0.04. Please discuss sensitivity to this choice and whether performance degrades materially for nearby values.
- Line 115 introduces a log-normal distribution for memory states. Please motivate this choice (e.g., biological evidence for log-normal firing rates, computational benefits for sparsity or dynamic range) and compare with alternatives.
- The cost function in (4) may yield only approximate, not exact, fixed points. Please quantify deviations (e.g., residual dynamics norms, settling times, or error rates) and discuss how they scale with network size, load, and noise.
- Lines 254–256 are unclear. Please rephrase those statements and, if applicable, provide a brief derivation or pointer to the appendix.
- Capacity is not discussed. Even a preliminary analysis (scaling with network size, sparsity, and E/I ratio; comparison to Hopfield-like bounds under your constraints) would strengthen the paper.
- It would be valuable to comment on the computational implications of Dale’s law. Under what constraints does enforcing Dale’s rule enhance, limit, or restructure computational capacity or robustness? Even a qualitative discussion or references to recent theory would help position the work.

In sum, this is a strong and timely contribution that integrates biological realism with principled control-theoretic design to produce a robust associative memory network.
