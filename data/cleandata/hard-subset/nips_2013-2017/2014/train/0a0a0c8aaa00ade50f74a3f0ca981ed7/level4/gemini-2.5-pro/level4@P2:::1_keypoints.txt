- Addresses creating biologically plausible auto-associative memory by enforcing Dale’s Law, analog (non-saturating) patterns, and realistic inhibitory feedback.  
- Proposes a control-theory-based framework that formulates memory storage as an optimization problem ensuring fixed points and dynamic stability.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization to enable gradient-based optimization of network connectivity for robust stability.  
- Simulations show successful embedding and recall of multiple graded analog patterns, with robustness to initial cue noise and ongoing dynamics.  
- Optimization yields emergent biologically realistic features: sparse synaptic weights, tight excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron, and stimulus-induced variability reduction.  
- Strength: original and significant advance by combining Dale’s Law, E/I balance, and analog memory storage in a single model.  
- Strength: technically strong use of SSA from robust control with sound methodology and thorough simulations demonstrating robustness and pruning tolerance.  
- Strength: manuscript is clear, well-motivated, and figures effectively illustrate findings.  
- Weakness: paper needs deeper explanation of why the SSA-based optimization specifically produces graded, non-saturating responses and balanced E/I solutions.  
- Weakness: lacks contextual discussion of memory capacity and scaling (e.g., comparison to Hopfield or other attractor models), even qualitatively.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and technically solid contribution with a few clarifications that would increase impact.
