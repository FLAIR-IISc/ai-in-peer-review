The paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and significant new insights. The authors observe that previous work often overlooked biological constraints such as Dale’s law, employed binary memory representations in rate-based models, and utilized non-sparse memory representations. While these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, this work develops a strategy that tackles them all simultaneously. Specifically, the authors leverage a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that overcome these limitations. Their approach involves a cost function balancing three terms, optimized via gradient descent, to achieve the desired network properties. The first term encourages fixed-point dynamics for the desired memories, the second promotes the stability of those fixed points, and the third encourages small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle through weight parameterization, the authors address the shortcomings of prior work, resulting in a network with naturally balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, saturation of individual neuron firing isn’t explicitly enforced but emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization process. Importantly, the authors utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory—consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically project outputs to other brain regions—while treating inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations, demonstrating robustness to noise. Beyond the network’s good associative memory properties, they also explain experimental findings showing reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset (although this is a general feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing), attributing it to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a fundamental problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory could function. While the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this stage, they reference recent proposals that may lead to such rules. I found their use of inhibitory neurons as tunable resources particularly interesting and beneficial. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to the field of neural computation.

A few minor issues remain:

The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although I anticipate it to be small.

The paper proposes a log-normal distribution for memory states on line 115. Please provide some motivation for this choice.

The cost function (4) could potentially yield solutions that deviate from the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. How significant is this deviation? This should be quantified and discussed.

Lines 254-256 are unclear. Please clarify the statements made there.

The authors do not address capacity limitations. It would be beneficial to include some discussion of this important computational aspect, even if preliminary.

Finally, it would be interesting if the authors could discuss the computational benefits, if any, conferred by Dale’s law. Does this law demonstrably enhance computational power under specific constraints? While it may simply reflect evolutionary or biophysical constraints, relating their work to this broader context would be valuable.
