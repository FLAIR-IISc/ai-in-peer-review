The authors present a novel model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network that adheres to several key biological constraints, including Dale's Law and the ability to encode memories without relying on units that respond at their saturation rate or in a binary manner. This work builds upon previous models of auto-associative memory, which have failed to incorporate some fundamental properties of brain networks. The authors' model utilizes a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters, resulting in a network that operates in the balanced regime, is robust to corruptions of the memory cue and ongoing noise, and explains the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the main ideas, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors' use of a rate-based formulation of the circuit dynamics is well-justified, and their choice of a threshold-quadratic gain function is consistent with experimental observations in cortical areas. The optimization procedure, which uses a variant of the low-storage BFGS algorithm, is well-explained and effectively minimizes the global cost function.

The results presented in the paper are impressive, demonstrating the network's ability to store and recall multiple analog memories as stable fixed points of its dynamics. The authors' use of a smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) to ensure robust stability is a key contribution, as it allows for efficient gradient-based optimization. The network's performance is evaluated using a variety of metrics, including the probability of successful recall, which is comparable to that of an "ideal observer."

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing style, well-organized structure, and effective use of figures to illustrate the main results. The authors' discussion of the results is thorough and provides valuable insights into the network's behavior. The paper's weaknesses are minor, primarily related to the lack of clarity regarding the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives.

One recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors noted, other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, with the latter condition seemingly more difficult to enforce. The chosen training procedure achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but nothing in the text indicates that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly well-suited to this task. Perhaps another network training procedure would achieve the same result? Can anything be said as to why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?

Additionally, a comparison of the trained network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity (number of memories as a function of network units) would be of interest to the community. However, this may be beyond the scope of the current paper.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several key biological constraints.
* The authors' use of a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters is a key contribution.
* The results presented in the paper are impressive, demonstrating the network's ability to store and recall multiple analog memories as stable fixed points of its dynamics.
* The paper is well-written, well-organized, and effectively uses figures to illustrate the main results.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives.
* A comparison of the trained network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity would be of interest to the community, but may be beyond the scope of the current paper.

Overall, the paper is well-suited for publication at NIPS, and its contributions to the field of neural networks and auto-associative memory are significant.
