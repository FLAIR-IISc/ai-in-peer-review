This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and novel insights. The authors observe that previous work has often overlooked key biological features, such as Dale's law, or has relied on binary, non-sparse memory representations in rate-based models. While these issues have been addressed individually in prior literature, this work introduces a unified strategy to tackle them simultaneously.

The core of their method is a control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, used to construct the network. The authors propose a cost function that balances three objectives via gradient descent: the first term establishes the desired memories as fixed points, the second ensures their stability, and the third regularizes the weights to prevent saturation. A key strength of this model is that Dale's principle is directly enforced in the weight parameterization, enabling the network to satisfy the aforementioned biological constraints and achieve a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. Notably, while neuronal saturation is not explicitly imposed, it emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization.

Furthermore, the model implements memories using only excitatory neurons—consistent with their role in projecting to other brain regions—while treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources that shape the network dynamics. The authors validate their approach with numerical simulations, demonstrating the network's associative memory properties and its robustness to noise. The model also accounts for the experimental observation that trial-to-trial variability in neural activity decreases following stimulus onset, explaining this as a general feature of attractor dynamics where the network state converges to the nearest fixed point.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a foundational problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It provides a compelling demonstration of how a biologically plausible associative memory might function. While a biologically plausible learning mechanism is not proposed, the authors hint at recent work that could lead to one. The novel use of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is particularly interesting and effective. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to the field.

**Minor Issues:**
*   The choice of γ=0.04 in Eq. (3) should be justified. A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter would be beneficial, even if it is expected to be low.
*   The motivation for using a log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) should be explained.
*   The cost function in Eq. (4) does not strictly enforce the fixed-point condition. The degree to which the resulting solutions deviate from being true fixed points should be quantified and discussed.
*   The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification.
*   The paper does not address the memory capacity of the network. A preliminary discussion of this important computational property would strengthen the paper.
*   A discussion on the potential computational benefits of Dale's law would be a valuable addition. For instance, does this principle enhance computational power under certain constraints, or is it primarily a result of biophysical and evolutionary factors?
