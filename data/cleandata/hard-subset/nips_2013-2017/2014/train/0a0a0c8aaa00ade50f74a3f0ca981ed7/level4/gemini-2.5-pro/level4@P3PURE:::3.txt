This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks from a fresh perspective, offering significant new insights. The authors observe that previous work has often ignored key biological aspects, such as Dale's law, or used simplified binary or non-sparse memory representations in rate-based models. While these issues have been addressed individually in prior work, this paper presents a unified strategy to tackle them simultaneously.

Specifically, the authors employ a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct the network. Their method involves optimizing a cost function via gradient descent, which balances three key objectives: the first term ensures the desired memories are fixed points, the second enforces the stability of these fixed points, and the third encourages small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle within the weight parameterization, the model successfully incorporates the aforementioned biological constraints and achieves a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. An interesting result is that single-neuron firing rate saturation, a common biological feature, emerges from the optimization without being explicitly enforced.

Importantly, the model implements memories using only excitatory neurons—consistent with their role in projecting to other brain regions—while treating the inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources for stabilizing the network. The authors validate their approach with numerical simulations, demonstrating the network's robustness to noise. Beyond its associative memory properties, the model also accounts for experimental findings that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset. The authors explain this as a consequence of the network converging to the attractor closest to the initial state, although it should be noted that this behavior may be a general feature of attractor networks with spontaneous baseline activity.

In summary, this is a well-written and clear paper that makes a significant contribution to computational neuroscience. By applying novel control-theoretic tools to a classic problem, the authors demonstrate a compelling and biologically plausible mechanism for associative memory. While a biologically plausible learning rule is not presented, the authors point to promising future directions. The conceptualization of inhibitory neurons as an optimizable resource is particularly insightful. Overall, this paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to the field.

**Minor Issues:**
*   The authors set γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3). A discussion of the model's sensitivity to this parameter choice would be beneficial, even if the effect is expected to be small.
*   Could the authors provide a motivation for choosing a log-normal distribution for the memory states (line 115)?
*   The cost function in Eq. (4) encourages, but does not strictly enforce, that memories are exact fixed points. The authors should quantify and discuss the magnitude of the resulting deviation from the exact fixed-point condition in their solutions.
*   The statements on lines 254-256 were unclear. Could the authors please clarify their meaning?
*   The paper does not address the memory capacity of the network. A discussion of this critical computational property, even if preliminary, would strengthen the paper.
*   Could the authors comment on the potential computational benefits of enforcing Dale's law? For instance, does this constraint enhance computational power under certain conditions, or is it better understood as an evolutionary or biophysical constraint without a direct computational advantage? A brief discussion relating the model to this broader question would be valuable.
