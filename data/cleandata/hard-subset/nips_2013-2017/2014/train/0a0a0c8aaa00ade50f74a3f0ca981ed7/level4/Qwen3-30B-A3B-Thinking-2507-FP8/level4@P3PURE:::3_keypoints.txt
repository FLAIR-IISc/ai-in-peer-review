- Revisits associative memory with a novel biological perspective and significant new insights.  
- Identifies three neglected biological constraints—Dale's law, binary memory representations in rate models, and non-sparse encodings—and proposes a unified solution.  
- Leverages a recent control-theoretic framework using spectral bounding to construct networks that satisfy these constraints.  
- Formulates a cost function with three terms to enforce fixed-point memories, ensure fixed-point stability, and penalize large synaptic weights.  
- Optimizes the cost via gradient descent and enforces Dale’s law through weight parameterization that naturally balances excitation and inhibition.  
- Single-neuron firing-rate saturation is not explicitly constrained but emerges from the optimization.  
- Implements memories using excitatory neurons while treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables.  
- Numerical simulations demonstrate noise robustness and associative memory behavior, including reduced trial-to-trial variability from attractor convergence.  
- Clear, well-written contribution applying innovative control-theoretic tools to a foundational neural computation problem.  
- Does not yet provide a biologically plausible learning rule, though relevant recent proposals are cited.  
- Insightful use of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources.
