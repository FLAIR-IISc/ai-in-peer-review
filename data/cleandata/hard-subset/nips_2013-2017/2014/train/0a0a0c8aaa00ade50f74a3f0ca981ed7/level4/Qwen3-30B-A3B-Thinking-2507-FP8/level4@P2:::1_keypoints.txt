- Presents a novel control-theoretic framework for rate-based attractor networks that store analog patterns while enforcing Dale’s law, graded non-saturating activity, and stable low-rate firing.  
- Directly optimizes synaptic connectivity and inhibitory dynamics to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization to impose robust stability and enable gradient-based training despite non-smooth stability constraints.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced excitation–inhibition regime and show noise-robust recall.  
- Model reproduces experimentally observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset (physiological prediction validated in simulations).  
- Sparse synaptic weight distributions emerge naturally from stability constraints rather than imposed regularization.  
- Thorough validation: demonstrates stable fixed points, robust noisy recall, and balanced E/I inputs across attractors.  
- Clear and well-organized presentation with effective figures and concise formalization of methods and stability conditions.  
- Original contribution: uniquely integrates Dale’s law, graded activity, and robust stability in one framework and generalizes to arbitrary analog patterns.  
- Significant advance for mechanistic understanding of memory circuits and provides testable experimental predictions; reported capacity ≈ 0.2 patterns/neuron.  
- Weakness: limited justification for choosing SSA over alternative stability methods (e.g., Lyapunov approaches) and lack of explicit comparison showing SSA’s advantages.  
- Weakness: no direct comparison of network capacity to prior theoretical results, leaving scalability relative to earlier models underexplored.  
- Recommendation: strong acceptance — technically sound, high impact, and well within the conference scope.
