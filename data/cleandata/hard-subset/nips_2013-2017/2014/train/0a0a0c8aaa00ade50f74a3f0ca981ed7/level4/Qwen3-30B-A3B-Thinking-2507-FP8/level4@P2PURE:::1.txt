

Summary: The authors propose a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network that adheres to several biologically plausible constraints. Previous models have neglected key features of biological networks, specifically enforcing Dale's Law (neurons exert strictly excitatory or inhibitory effects on their projections) and avoiding reliance on units that saturate or operate in a binary manner. Memories are encoded via synaptic modifications guided by gradient descent, constrained using a recent method to ensure stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points. Simulations demonstrate that the trained fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics (i.e., they are nearly fixed points but not exact), and are stable as required. The trained network also exhibits two experimentally observed features of cortical networks: synaptic weights distributed around zero with long tails, and average units receiving approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory input. The authors identify these features as critical for robust auto-associative memory performance under perturbation and stochastic noise.

Review: This is a clearly written, novel contribution of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a clear review of prior work on training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, highlighting key contributions and limitations. The network architecture, training procedure, and figures are well-presented and effectively illustrate the main results. The authors demonstrate the network's robustness against perturbations and noise, showcasing the effectiveness of their training procedure.

A key suggestion is to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. While the authors note that prior methods have enforced Dale's Law or graded responses (with the latter being more challenging), the manuscript does not explicitly justify why this specific gradient descent approach was selected for achieving non-saturating units. It remains unclear whether alternative training procedures could achieve the same result, or why this method is particularly effective.

Additionally, though likely beyond the paper's scope, a brief comparison of network capacity (number of memories vs. network size) against prior models would be valuable for the community. Applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in dynamical systems, the authors introduce a new auto-associative memory model that satisfies key biological constraints, particularly enabling units to encode memories without saturation or binary responses.
