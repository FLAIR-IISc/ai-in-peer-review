This is a clearly-written and novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a comprehensive review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting the contributions and limitations of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures are well-chosen to illustrate the main results. The authors demonstrate the network’s performance limitations under noisy conditions, showcasing the robustness achieved through their training procedure.

The paper addresses a longstanding problem in computational neuroscience: building auto-associative memory networks that adhere to biological constraints. Previous models often fall short by violating Dale’s Law (requiring neurons to be either excitatory or inhibitory) or relying on binary or saturating neuronal responses. This work proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to overcome these limitations, directly optimizing networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to stabilize analog patterns as fixed points. The authors successfully demonstrate a network capable of robust memory recall, balanced excitation and inhibition, and reduction of trial-to-trial variability – all features observed in cortical circuits.

One potential area for strengthening the paper lies in a more detailed justification for the chosen training procedure. While the method effectively achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, the manuscript doesn't explicitly address *why* this particular method was selected over alternatives. Previous work has addressed constraints like Dale’s Law and graded memory representations; a discussion of why this approach is particularly well-suited to simultaneously addressing these challenges would be valuable. For instance, does the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa offer advantages in terms of optimization efficiency or stability compared to other methods?

Additionally, a brief comparison of the network’s capacity – the number of memories it can reliably store as a function of network size – relative to previous models would be beneficial. The field has a rich history of investigating network capacity, and placing this work within that context (even with just 1-2 sentences) would strengthen its impact. While a comprehensive capacity analysis may be beyond the scope of the current paper, a concise comparison would provide a valuable benchmark.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of control-theoretic optimization with biological constraints (Dale’s Law, graded activity, balanced excitation/inhibition) is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears well-grounded, and the results are supported by simulations and analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Significance:** The results offer a plausible mechanism for analog memory storage and recall in cortical circuits. The observed properties (balanced activity, robustness to noise, variability reduction) align with experimental observations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Justification of method:** The rationale for choosing the specific optimization method could be more thoroughly explained.
*   **Capacity comparison:** A brief comparison to existing models in terms of network capacity would enhance the paper’s impact.



In summary, this is a strong submission that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-executed approach. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding justification of the chosen method and capacity comparison would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field. I recommend acceptance. Applying a recently developed method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the condition that network units are capable of encoding memories without saturating or binary responses.
