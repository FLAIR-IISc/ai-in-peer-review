The paper revisits the problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and significant new insights into an old problem. The authors note that previous work in the field often neglected biological aspects, such as Dale's law, and used binary memory representations in rate-based models, as well as non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a strategy that allows them to tackle all these problems simultaneously.

The authors employ a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that address these issues. They propose a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed point nature of the desired memories, the second term to the stability of the fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights that prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors are able to address the shortcomings of previous work, leading to a network where excitations and inhibition are naturally balanced.

Interestingly, while the authors do not enforce saturation in the single neuron firing, this arises naturally through the solution of the optimization problem. Importantly, the authors only make use of the excitatory neurons to implement the memory, which is consistent with the fact that it is usually the excitatory neurons that send output to other regions, and view the inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized over.

The authors validate their results through numerical simulations, demonstrating the network's noise robustness. In addition to showing the good associative memory properties of the network, the authors are also able to explain experimental findings that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset. This is explained through convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

The paper is well-written and clear, tackling a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. The authors demonstrate how a biologically plausible associative memory may function, although they do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point. However, they hint at some recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of the inhibitory neurons as resources is an interesting and beneficial aspect of the paper.

A few minor issues need to be addressed. The authors use γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3), and it would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice. Additionally, the log-normal distribution for memory states proposed on line 115 needs to be motivated. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories, and this violation should be quantified and discussed.

Furthermore, the authors do not mention capacity issues, and it would be nice to make some statement, even preliminary, about this important computational aspect. It would also be beneficial to discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, namely, whether this law can be shown to enhance computational power under specific constraints.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation. The authors' approach and results are well-presented, and the paper is a valuable addition to the field.
