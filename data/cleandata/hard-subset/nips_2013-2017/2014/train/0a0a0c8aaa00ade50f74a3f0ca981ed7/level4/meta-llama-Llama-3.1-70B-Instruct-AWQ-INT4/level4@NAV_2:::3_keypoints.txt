- Introduces a novel control-theoretic approach to associative memory that addresses multiple biological constraints simultaneously.  
- Highlights neglect in prior work of biological factors like Dale's law, continuous firing saturation, and sparse/non-binary memory representations.  
- Uses spectral bounding and a three-term cost function optimized by gradient descent to enforce fixed points, stability, and small weights.  
- Enforces Dale's principle directly in weight parameterization, producing natural excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Only excitatory neurons implement memories while inhibitory neurons are treated as optimized resources.  
- Saturation of single-neuron firing emerges from optimization without being explicitly enforced.  
- Demonstrates robustness to noise and good associative memory performance through numerical simulations.  
- Explains experimental reduction of trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via convergence to nearest attractor.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and makes a significant contribution to biologically plausible associative memory modeling.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule, only hints at possible future directions.  
- Sensitivity to the chosen γ = 0.04 in equation (3) is not analyzed and should be discussed.  
- Motivation for using a log-normal distribution for memory states is not provided.  
- The cost function may produce solutions that slightly violate exact fixed-point conditions; the extent of this violation is not quantified.  
- Statements on lines 254–256 need clarification for better understanding.  
- Capacity of the proposed network is not addressed; preliminary remarks on memory capacity are requested.  
- The computational benefits or potential advantages of enforcing Dale's law are not explored.  
- Strengths: novel approach, simultaneous handling of biological constraints, clarity, and significant implications for neural computation.  
- Weaknesses: lack of biologically plausible learning mechanism, missing sensitivity and capacity analyses, and several minor clarifications needed.
