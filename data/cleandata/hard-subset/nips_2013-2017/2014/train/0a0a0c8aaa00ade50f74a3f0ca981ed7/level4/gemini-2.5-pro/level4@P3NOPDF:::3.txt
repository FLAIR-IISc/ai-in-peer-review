This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks from a fresh perspective, offering significant new insights. The authors correctly observe that much previous work has overlooked key biological constraints, such as abiding by Dale's law, or has relied on simplified assumptions like binary memory representations and non-sparse activity patterns. While these issues have been addressed individually in prior work, a key contribution of this paper is a unified framework that simultaneously satisfies all these biological constraints.

To achieve this, the authors employ a novel control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct the network. They formulate an optimization problem with a cost function that intelligently balances three objectives: 1) ensuring the desired memory patterns are fixed points of the dynamics, 2) guaranteeing the stability of these fixed points, and 3) regularizing synaptic weights to prevent saturation.

Crucially, Dale's principle is enforced directly within the weight parameterization, leading to a network with a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. An interesting emergent property of this optimization is that neuronal firing rates saturate naturally without being explicitly constrained. The authors also make the compelling choice to encode memories solely in the excitatory population—consistent with the role of excitatory projection neurons—while treating the inhibitory neurons as a resource to be optimized for achieving stable dynamics.

The model's performance, including its robustness to noise, is validated through numerical simulations. The paper also connects to experimental neuroscience by showing how the model can explain the reduction in trial-to-trial variability observed after stimulus onset, a phenomenon it attributes to convergence towards the nearest attractor state.

In summary, this is a well-written and clear paper that makes an important contribution to computational neuroscience. It successfully applies novel tools from control theory to a foundational problem, demonstrating a principled method for constructing a biologically plausible associative memory. The treatment of inhibitory neurons as optimizable variables to shape network dynamics is particularly insightful. While the authors do not present a biologically plausible learning rule for the weights, they appropriately situate their work by pointing to recent research that could lead to one. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and valuable contribution.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

A few points could be clarified or expanded upon to strengthen the manuscript:

1.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The results are presented for γ=0.04 in eq. (3). It would be helpful to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter choice, even if the effect is expected to be small.

2.  **Motivation for Distribution:** The authors propose a log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115). Could you provide a brief motivation for this specific distributional choice over others, as it is a common model for firing rate distributions in cortex?

3.  **Fixed-Point Accuracy:** The cost function in eq. (4) encourages, but does not strictly enforce, the fixed-point property. It would be valuable to quantify the typical deviation from a true fixed point in the optimized solutions. How close do the memory states get to being perfect fixed points (i.e., how small is the residual term)?

4.  **Clarity on Lines 254-256:** The statements in this section were difficult to parse. Could the authors please clarify or rephrase this part of the text?

5.  **Memory Capacity:** The analysis does not touch upon the memory capacity of the network. A discussion on this critical aspect—even a preliminary one regarding how capacity might scale with network parameters like size or sparsity—would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

6.  **Computational Role of Dale's Law:** Could the authors speculate further on the potential computational benefits of enforcing Dale's law? For instance, does this constraint enhance computational power or efficiency in certain regimes, or is it better understood as a purely biophysical and evolutionary constraint? Any further insight here would be welcome.
