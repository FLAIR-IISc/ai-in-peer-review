- Revisits associative memory with a unified strategy addressing biological constraints (Dale's law) and non-binary, sparse memories simultaneously.  
- Uses a control-theoretic approach with spectral bounding to construct networks.  
- Introduces a gradient-descent cost function with three terms: impose memories as fixed points, ensure stability, and regularize weights to avoid saturation.  
- Enforces Dale's principle directly in weight parameterization, yielding excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Neuronal saturation emerges naturally from optimization rather than being explicitly imposed.  
- Memories are implemented using excitatory neurons; inhibitory neurons are treated as optimizable resources shaping dynamics.  
- Validated with simulations showing associative memory behavior and robustness to noise.  
- Explains decreased trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset as attractor dynamics converging to nearest fixed points.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and offers a novel, biologically plausible associative memory architecture using control-theoretic tools.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism, though related work is noted as a possible route.  
- Minor issue: choice of γ=0.04 in Eq. (3) needs justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Minor issue: motivation for using a log-normal distribution for memory states should be explained.  
- Minor issue: Eq. (4) does not strictly enforce fixed-point conditions; quantify and discuss deviations.  
- Minor issue: statements on lines 254–256 are unclear and require clarification.  
- Minor issue: paper omits discussion of network memory capacity; a preliminary analysis would strengthen the work.  
- Minor issue: lacks discussion of potential computational benefits of Dale's law versus biophysical/evolutionary explanations.
