This paper revisits associative memory in recurrent neural networks from a fresh control-theoretic perspective and offers new insights into a classic problem. The authors address several biological constraints—Dale’s law, continuous rate-based dynamics with non-binary memories, and sparse memory representations—within a single framework. Using recent spectral-bounding techniques, they construct networks that implement multiple fixed-point memories while respecting excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) structure. Their approach optimizes a cost function with three terms: (i) enforcing the desired memories as fixed points, (ii) providing stability margins for those fixed points, and (iii) regularizing weights to prevent saturation. Dale’s principle is imposed directly through the weight parameterization, yielding natural E/I balance. Notably, even without explicitly constraining single-neuron saturation, the optimized networks exhibit saturation-like behavior as an emergent property. Consistent with neuroanatomy, memory content is encoded in excitatory neurons, while inhibitory neurons are optimized as auxiliary variables to stabilize dynamics and balance excitation.

The authors validate the method through numerical simulations, demonstrating robust associative recall in the presence of noise. They also connect the model to experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, which arises here from rapid convergence to the nearest attractor. Although this reduction in variability is a common feature of attractor models with spontaneous baseline activity, the authors show it explicitly in their setting.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized presentation that links control-theoretic tools to questions in neural computation.
- A unified treatment of three biological constraints that are often studied in isolation.
- Principled use of spectral stability constraints to ensure memory robustness, with weight regularization preventing runaway activity.
- Biologically motivated architecture in which excitatory neurons carry memories and inhibitory neurons orchestrate stability/E/I balance.
- Empirical demonstrations of robustness to noise and reduced response variability, aligning with experimental phenomena.
- The conceptual framing of inhibitory neurons as resources to be optimized is particularly compelling.

Limitations and areas for clarification:
- The paper currently lacks a biologically plausible learning rule that could implement the proposed optimization in vivo. The discussion hints at possible directions, but a clearer mapping to synaptic plasticity mechanisms (e.g., local rules, modulatory signals, or network-level approximations to spectral margins) would increase impact.
- The exactness of the fixed-point constraint is relaxed by the cost function; it remains unclear how close the learned attractors are to the target memories under various operating conditions (noise, load, sparsity).
- Memory capacity, a central metric for associative memory models, is not analyzed. Even a preliminary scaling analysis (e.g., capacity as a function of network size, sparsity, and stability margin) would be valuable.

Requests for clarification and suggested analyses:
- Sensitivity to γ in Eq. (3): The paper uses γ = 0.04. Please report sensitivity analyses showing how recall accuracy, stability margins, and firing rate distributions vary with γ. If performance is indeed insensitive within a reasonable range, a brief plot or table would suffice.
- Motivation for log-normal memory states (line 115): Please justify this choice. Is it driven by empirical observations of log-normal distributions in cortical firing rates and synaptic weights, or does it optimize robustness/sparsity? Clarify whether other distributions (e.g., exponential, truncated Gaussian) yield comparable performance.
- Fixed-point accuracy: Because the cost (Eq. 4) may allow small violations of the fixed-point conditions, please quantify the deviation between the target memory and the network’s equilibrium state. Suggested metrics include: average and worst-case fixed-point residuals; overlap between target and retrieved patterns; and the fraction of memories with deviations exceeding a chosen threshold. Reporting these as a function of load and noise would be especially informative.
- Lines 254–256: The statements here are unclear. Please restate with precise definitions (e.g., what measure of variability or stability is referenced, under which input conditions, and how it is computed).
- Capacity: Provide at least a preliminary estimate or bound. For example, explore how the number of storable memories scales with network size under fixed sparsity and stability margins, or compare to classical Hopfield-like benchmarks adapted to Dale’s law. Even empirical scaling curves would greatly strengthen the paper.
- Computational role of Dale’s law: Beyond biological plausibility, can the sign constraint be shown to confer computational advantages under specific constraints (e.g., robustness to perturbations, modularity, control of gain and timescales, or improved generalization via effective regularization)? An ablation (with/without Dale’s constraint, matched for stability margin) could help disentangle its functional benefits from its role as a biological constraint.
- Basins of attraction and noise robustness: Please quantify basin sizes (e.g., as a function of initialization distance or injected noise) and relate them to the chosen stability margins from the spectral bounds. A comparison to networks trained without explicit spectral control would clarify the contribution of the control-theoretic term.

Minor comments:
- Clarify whether the inhibitory population is required to scale with the number of stored memories to maintain stability, or whether its role saturates.
- Report distributions of learned synaptic weights (E→E, E→I, I→E), and whether they are consistent with reported cortical statistics.
- Briefly discuss runtime and optimization stability, including how often training converges to satisfactory attractors versus poor local minima.

Overall assessment:
This is a clear and well-executed paper that tackles a foundational problem in neural computation using modern control-theoretic tools. The integration of Dale’s law, sparse continuous memories, and explicit stability control is novel and timely, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the points above—especially fixed-point accuracy, capacity, and the functional role of Dale’s constraint—would further solidify the contribution. I view this as an important and exciting step toward biologically plausible associative memory and recommend acceptance after minor revisions.
