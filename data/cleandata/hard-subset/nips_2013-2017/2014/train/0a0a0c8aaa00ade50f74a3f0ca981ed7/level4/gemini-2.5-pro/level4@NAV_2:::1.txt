This paper presents a novel framework for creating auto-associative memory networks that conform to several key biological constraints, notably Dale's Law (distinct excitatory and inhibitory neurons) and the ability to store graded, analog activity patterns. Previous models have often failed to incorporate these features simultaneously, resorting to binary representations or relying on neuronal firing rate saturation. The authors formulate memory storage as a control-theoretic optimization problem. They directly optimize synaptic weights to ensure that a set of desired analog patterns become stable fixed points of the rate-based network dynamics, using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to enforce stability. The resulting networks demonstrate robust memory recall from corrupted cues and in the presence of ongoing noise. Furthermore, the optimization process leads to emergent network properties that are consistent with cortical physiology, including a tight balance of excitation and inhibition and a sparse, long-tailed distribution of synaptic weights.

This is a high-quality and original contribution that will be of significant interest to the computational neuroscience community. The paper's main strengths are:

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of robust control theory, specifically SSA minimization, to construct a biologically constrained memory network is a novel and powerful approach. The work successfully addresses major limitations of previous models by enabling the storage of analog memories in a network with separate E/I populations, without relying on rate saturation. The emergence of realistic network features like E/I balance and sparse weights from the optimization is a particularly significant finding.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written. The introduction provides an excellent and concise review of the relevant literature, clearly motivating the need for this work. The methods and results are presented clearly, and the figures are well-designed to illustrate the core findings effectively.
*   **Quality:** The technical work is sound. The simulations convincingly demonstrate that the trained networks are stable and perform robust auto-associative recall. The analysis of the network's properties, including its performance under ongoing noise and the resulting reduction in trial-to-trial variability, adds to the paper's quality.

While the paper is strong, its impact could be enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  The manuscript could provide more intuition about the direct link between the chosen optimization method and the successful encoding of graded, non-saturating responses. While the SSA method effectively enforces stability, it is not immediately obvious why this specific approach is uniquely suited to preventing the network from settling into binary or saturated states, a common failure mode for other models. A deeper discussion on why minimizing the SSA so effectively navigates this challenge would strengthen the paper's core message.
2.  The paper demonstrates storage of 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network. While a full analysis may be beyond the current scope, the authors should consider including a brief discussion on how the storage capacity of this network might scale and compare to classical associative memory models. This would help readers place the performance of this novel architecture within the broader context of the existing literature.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and significant advance in the theory of associative memory networks. The strengths in originality and quality are clear, and the minor weaknesses can be addressed with additions to the discussion.
