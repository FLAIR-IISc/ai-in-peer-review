The paper offers a fresh perspective on the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, providing new insights into an old issue. The authors note that previous work in this field often neglected biological aspects such as Dale's law, employed binary memory representations in rate-based models, and utilized non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a comprehensive strategy to tackle all of them simultaneously. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that address these issues. 

This approach involves proposing a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of the fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network with naturally balanced excitations and inhibitions. Notably, while they do not explicitly enforce saturation in single neuron firing, this emerges naturally through the solution of the optimization problem. 

Importantly, the authors only utilize excitatory neurons to implement the memory, which is consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically send output to other regions. They view inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized over. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's noise robustness. In addition to showcasing the network's good associative memory properties, the authors also explain experimental findings indicating that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset. This is attributed to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory may function, although it does not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is an interesting and beneficial aspect of the paper. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues require attention:

1. The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although it is expected to be small.

2. A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. The authors should provide motivation for this choice.

3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. The severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.

4. The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification.

5. The authors do not mention capacity issues. It would be beneficial to make a preliminary statement about this important computational aspect.

6. It would be interesting if the authors could discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether it can enhance computational power under specific constraints. This may be a result of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints, and relating to this would be beneficial.
