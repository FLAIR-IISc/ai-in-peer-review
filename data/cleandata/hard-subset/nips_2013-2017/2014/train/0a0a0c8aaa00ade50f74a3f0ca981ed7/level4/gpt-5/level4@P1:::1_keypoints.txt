- Presents a rate-based autoassociative memory that enforces Dale’s law, uses graded non-saturating activity, and operates in a dynamically balanced E/I regime.  
- Formalizes memory storage as requiring each pattern to be (i) a fixed point or near one and (ii) locally stable; optimizes weights and inhibitory steady-states jointly.  
- Uses a control-theoretic objective combining a fixed-point loss with the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable stability surrogate for gradient-based optimization.  
- Targets analog excitatory patterns drawn from a log-normal distribution; inhibitory steady states are treated as auxiliary optimized variables.  
- Trained networks show robust recall from noisy cues and ongoing noise, approaching the performance of an ideal nearest-pattern observer.  
- Learned connectivity is heavy-tailed with many weak synapses; pruning very weak weights preserves most attractors with minimal loss.  
- Networks display tight excitation–inhibition balance across attractors and reproduce variability quenching consistent with cortical data.  
- Manuscript is clearly written with well-chosen figures and thorough simulations supporting claims.  
- Novelty: combines SSA-based stability optimization with biologically constrained E/I rate networks to embed analog attractors, differing from Hopfield/LIF/global-inhibition approaches.  
- Significance: demonstrates balanced E/I networks can support analog memory without saturating units and connects to experimental observations.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of why the SSA-based parameterization is especially appropriate versus alternative training procedures or stability surrogates.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical or theoretical capacity analysis (number of memories vs. network size) to contextualize performance.  
- Weakness: trained states are optimized as “slow points” rather than exact fixed points; needs explanation for why nearby true attractors emerge.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify methodological rationale for SSA, include ablations/comparisons with alternative training methods, and provide capacity/scaling discussion or empirical estimates.
