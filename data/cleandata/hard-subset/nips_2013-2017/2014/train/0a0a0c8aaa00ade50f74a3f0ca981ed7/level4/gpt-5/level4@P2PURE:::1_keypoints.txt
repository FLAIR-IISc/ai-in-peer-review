- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological constraints (notably Dale’s Law) and supports graded, non-saturating unit responses.  
- Trains synapses via gradient-descent updates constrained by a recent method that guarantees stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Simulations show learned target states have slow dynamics near fixed points and are stable as intended.  
- Emergent synaptic weight distribution is centered near zero with long tails, matching cortical measurements.  
- Units exhibit approximate excitatory–inhibitory input balance, another biologically consistent emergent property.  
- These emergent features are identified as critical for robust auto-associative retrieval under perturbations and stochastic input noise.  
- Paper is clearly written with a useful overview of prior work; architecture, training, and figures are well presented.  
- Recommendation to clarify why the chosen training method was selected and why it is especially suited to producing graded memories, including whether alternatives could yield similar results.  
- Suggestion (likely beyond scope): compare storage capacity (memories per network size) to prior auto-associative models.  
- Concise summary: applying a stability-enforcing training method, the authors produce a biologically constrained auto-associative network that encodes graded memories without neuron saturation.
