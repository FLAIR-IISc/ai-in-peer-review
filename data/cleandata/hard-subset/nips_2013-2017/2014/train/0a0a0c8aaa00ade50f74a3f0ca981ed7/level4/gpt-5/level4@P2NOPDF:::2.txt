This manuscript presents a rate-based neural network trained to maintain multiple stable activity patterns (memories). It addresses key limitations of prior attractor models—particularly violations of Dale’s law and saturation of neural activity in memory states. The authors employ gradient descent on a cost function that (i) minimizes activity changes at target attractors, (ii) penalizes large synaptic weights via the Frobenius norm, and (iii) enhances attractor stability. The trained networks exhibit synaptic weight distributions consistent with experimental observations and achieve a balanced state of excitation and inhibition within attractors.

The paper is clearly written, and both the model and training procedure are easy to follow. The results are presented in a way that makes their implications transparent. Importantly, the integration of biological constraints into the architecture and learning objective leads to emergent properties—graded, non-saturated attractors and network-level excitation–inhibition balance—that increase the biological plausibility of the model. By constraining synapses and neuronal types in line with neurobiological principles, the study demonstrates how realistic structural assumptions can produce stable, robust attractor dynamics. Overall, the work offers a solid and interesting contribution to our understanding of how biologically grounded rate-based networks can implement memory states without violating Dale’s law or inducing activity saturation.
