Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network that adheres to several biologically plausible constraints, overcoming limitations of previous models. Notably, their model incorporates Dale's Law, which dictates that neurons have strictly excitatory or inhibitory effects on their projections, and allows networks to encode memories without relying on units that respond at saturation rates or in a binary manner. Memories are encoded through synaptic modifications based on a gradient descent procedure, constrained using a recently published method that ensures the linearization of the dynamics around a dynamical system's fixed point is stable. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure with simulations, showing that the trained fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics and are stable, as desired. The trained network also exhibits two features consistent with experimentally observed characteristics of cortical networks: a distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails, and an average network unit receiving approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input. The authors identify these features as crucial to the network's robust auto-associative memory performance, even when perturbed from a fixed point or subjected to stochastic input noise.

Review: This submission is clearly written, novel, and will likely be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a thorough review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are well-presented, and the figures effectively illustrate the main results. The authors also demonstrate the network's performance limitations when noise is included, showcasing the robust solution found by the training procedure.

One recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. While the authors note that other methods have been employed to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, it is unclear why this particular method was chosen. The selected training procedure successfully builds an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but it is not evident that this method was chosen for its suitability to this task. It would be beneficial to discuss whether alternative network training procedures could achieve the same result and why this particular method is effective in achieving the desired goal.

Additionally, although likely beyond the scope of this paper, a comparison of the trained network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity (i.e., the number of memories as a function of network units) would be of interest to the community. Applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the condition that network units are capable of encoding memories without saturating or binary responses.
