- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct Dale’s-law E–I recurrent networks that store multiple analog memories as locally stable attractors.
- Neurons use a rectified quadratic gain; weights reparametrized to enforce Dale’s law; SSA regularization enforces local stability.
- Joint optimization of synaptic weights and per-memory inhibitory steady states (treated as auxiliary variables).
- Resulting networks show tight E–I balance, robust recall from noisy cues and ongoing noise, heavy-tailed/sparse weight distributions, and variability quenching after stimulus onset.
- Empirical demonstrations: 30 patterns in 150 neurons (capacity 0.2), robustness vs. an “ideal observer,” emergence of E–I balance, limited reliance on many weak synapses.
- SSA as a differentiable proxy for spectral abscissa is well motivated and practical for stability regularization.
- Concern: trained target states are “slow points” with nonzero dv/dt; need quantification of distances to exact fixed points and eigenvalue spectra at those fixed points.
- Concern: per-memory optimized inhibitory states raise biological-plausibility questions about how inhibitory configurations are set during recall.
- Request clarification whether inhibitory states during recall arise solely from network dynamics or require memory-specific external inputs; discuss biological mechanisms (inhibitory plasticity, modulatory control).
- Missing analysis of capacity scaling with network size and pattern load; relate to classic graded-memory results (e.g., Treves & Rolls).
- Need to report how SSA regularization affects capacity and basin sizes.
- Basins of attraction are insufficiently quantified; request metrics like critical cue noise at 50% success and distributions across memories compared to the ideal observer.
- Sensitivity/ablation analyses are lacking: hyperparameters (ηs, ηF), SSA smoothing ε, gain nonlinearity, initializations, convergence behavior, and computational cost.
- Recommend ablations (no Dale’s law, alternative regularizers like L1) to test whether sparsity and balance emerge from stability optimization rather than regularization choices.
- Synapse-clipping analysis changed success thresholds post hoc; request consistent metrics or justification and report how attractor locations shift after clipping.
- Claim linking rectified power-law gain to spiking translation is plausible but needs demonstration with matched single-neuron transfer functions or spiking implementation.
- Clarity improvements requested: list all hyperparameters and rationale, provide algorithmic pseudocode and stopping criteria, standardize notation (v vs r), and fix incomplete references.
- Request to report eigenvalue spectra (or SSA/SA histograms) at converged attractors.
- Novelty: direct optimization of E–I networks for arbitrary analog attractors under Dale’s law and use of SSA is an original contribution.
- Significance: promising proof-of-principle with potential impact if scalable; links theory to cortical phenomena like variability quenching.
- Pros summarized: clean fixed-point+stability formulation, enforces Dale’s law, robust recall under noise, emergent sparsity/heavy-tailed weights, clear writing and thorough experiments.
- Cons summarized: per-memory inhibitory optimization hurts biological plausibility, nonzero fixed-point residuals, limited scaling/theory, sensitivity to heuristics, no spiking demo or local learning rule.
- Key questions to address: scaling of capacity/basin size with N and pattern statistics; eigenvalue spectra and distances to true fixed points; whether inhibitory activities are dynamically determined during recall; sensitivity of sparsity/E–I balance to regularization choices; tolerance to structured connectivity constraints or imposed sparsity.  
- Overall assessment: strong, timely proof-of-concept with acceptance leaning, pending additional analyses on scaling, basins, and inhibitory role.
