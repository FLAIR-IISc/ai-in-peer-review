**Summary:**
The authors propose a rate-based neural network model for auto-associative memory that adheres to several biologically plausible constraints. Unlike previous models, this work incorporates two key biological features: Dale's Law, which dictates that neurons are either strictly excitatory or inhibitory, and the ability to store memories using graded, non-saturating firing rates rather than binary or saturated responses. Memories are encoded as stable fixed points of the network dynamics. The authors achieve this by training synaptic weights using gradient descent, incorporating a recently developed constraint that ensures the stability of these fixed points. Through simulations, they demonstrate that the resulting attractors are stable and exhibit slow dynamics, effectively functioning as the desired memory states.

The authors highlight two features of the trained network that are consistent with experimental observations in the cortex: a heavy-tailed distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero, and a balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs to individual neurons. They argue that these properties are crucial for the network's ability to perform robust auto-associative memory, enabling it to return to the correct memory state after perturbations, even in the presence of stochastic noise.

**Review:**
This is a well-written and novel paper that should be of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a clear review of prior work on training auto-associative networks, effectively summarizing the contributions and shortcomings of previous models. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate the main results. The analysis of the network's performance in the presence of noise convincingly demonstrates the robustness of the solution found by the training procedure.

One point for clarification is the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the objective of achieving graded, non-saturating responses. The authors note that while other methods exist to enforce Dale's Law, achieving graded responses has proven more challenging. While the proposed training method successfully produces networks with the desired non-saturating units, the paper does not explain why this particular method is well-suited for this specific task. Could other training procedures achieve a similar outcome? A brief discussion on what makes this specific method effective at preventing saturated responses would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, while perhaps beyond the scope of the current work, a comparison of the network's memory capacity (i.e., the number of memories stored as a function of network size) against previous models would be highly valuable. Given the extensive history of auto-associative networks, such an analysis would provide important context for the community.
