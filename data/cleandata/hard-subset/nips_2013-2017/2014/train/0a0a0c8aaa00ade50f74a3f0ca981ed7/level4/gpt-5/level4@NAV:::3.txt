The paper revisits the longstanding problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, contributing substantial new insight to a classic topic. The authors note that much prior work neglected biological considerations such as Dale’s law, relied on binary memory representations in rate-based models, and employed non-sparse memory codes. While each of these issues has been treated individually elsewhere, the authors present a unified strategy to address them simultaneously. Concretely, they adopt a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding to construct networks that handle all three. Within this approach, they optimize a cost function with three components via gradient descent: the first promotes the desired patterns as fixed points, the second ensures stability of those fixed points, and the third penalizes weight magnitudes to prevent saturation. By directly imposing Dale’s principle through the weight parameterization, the model naturally achieves balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, although single-neuron saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges from the optimization. Importantly, only excitatory neurons are used to encode the memories (consistent with excitatory projections to other regions), with inhibitory neurons treated as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their framework with numerical simulations and demonstrate robustness to noise. Beyond strong associative memory performance, they also account for reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset (though this is generally expected in associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity), attributing it to convergence to the attractor nearest the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper. It tackles a classic problem in neural computation using modern control-theoretic tools and illustrates how a biologically plausible associative memory could operate. While a biologically plausible learning mechanism is not yet provided, the authors point to recent proposals that may lead to such rules. I found the treatment of inhibitory neurons as a resource particularly interesting and advantageous. Overall, the paper offers a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues:
- The authors use γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3). Please discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice (I expect it to be small).
- A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please motivate this choice.
- The cost function (4) may yield solutions that only approximately satisfy the fixed-point conditions. How severe are these deviations? Please quantify and discuss.
- Lines 254–256: The statements here were unclear to me. Please clarify.
- Capacity is not addressed. It would be helpful to provide at least a preliminary statement on this key computational aspect.
- Please discuss the computational implications of Dale’s law. Can it be shown to enhance computational power under specific constraints? This may not hold and could reflect evolutionary or biophysical constraints, but it would be valuable to address.

A strong and well-written paper addressing a time-honored problem in neural computation with novel control-theoretic techniques, demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function.
