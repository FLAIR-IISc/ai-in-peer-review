- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological constraints such as Dale’s Law and graded, non-saturating firing rates.  
- Memories are encoded via synaptic modifications using gradient descent, constrained by a recent method that guarantees stability of fixed points.  
- Simulations show trained memory states are stable attractors exhibiting slow dynamics.  
- Trained networks exhibit emergent synaptic weight distributions centered at zero with long tails and a balance between total excitatory and inhibitory input.  
- The model demonstrates robust auto-associative recall, recovering memories after perturbations and in the presence of stochastic noise.  
- Paper is clearly written and novel, with a strong literature review, clear presentation of architecture/training, and effective figures.  
- The manuscript does not explain why this particular training procedure was chosen over alternatives or why it specifically yields graded, non-saturating responses.  
- It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether other training methods could achieve similar results.  
- A comparison of memory capacity (number of memories vs network size) to prior models would be a valuable additional analysis.
