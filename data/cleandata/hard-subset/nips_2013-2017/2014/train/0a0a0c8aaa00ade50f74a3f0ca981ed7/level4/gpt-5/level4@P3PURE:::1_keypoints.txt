- Presents an auto-associative memory model in a rate-based neural network that enforces biologically plausible constraints.  
- Explicitly incorporates Dale’s Law and produces graded, non-saturating memory representations rather than binary/saturated units.  
- Trains synapses via gradient descent using a recent method that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Simulations show trained target states have slow dynamics near (but not exactly at) fixed points and are stable.  
- Learned synaptic weight distributions are centered near zero with heavy tails, matching experimental observations.  
- Networks display approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance at the level of individual units.  
- The learned networks are robust to perturbations from fixed points and to stochastic input noise.  
- Paper is clearly written, novel, of broad interest, and provides a concise literature review plus well-described architecture, training, and illustrative figures.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on why the chosen training procedure is especially suited to obtaining graded, non-saturating memories and whether alternative methods could achieve the same outcome.  
- Reviewer suggests (likely for future work) comparing network capacity (number of memories vs. network size) to prior auto-associative models.  
- 1–2 sentence summary: Using a stability-enforcing linearization method, the authors produce a biologically constrained auto-associative network that stores graded, non-saturating memories.
