Summary
The paper introduces an auto-associative memory model in a rate-based recurrent network trained under biologically motivated constraints. In contrast to many prior approaches, the network enforces Dale’s law, yields graded (non-binary, non-saturating) persistent activity patterns, and maintains stable fixed points via an optimization objective that constrains the linearization around those points. Simulations demonstrate robust memory recall from perturbed initial conditions and in the presence of stochastic input noise. The learned connectivity exhibits heavy-tailed weight distributions centered near zero and approximate excitation–inhibition balance at the level of individual neurons—both in line with cortical measurements.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and strong motivation rooted in known limitations of classic auto-associative models (e.g., binary saturating units, violations of Dale’s law).
- Elegant training objective that directly enforces local stability of target fixed points, yielding slow but convergent dynamics and robust recall.
- Convincing simulations, with well-chosen figures, showing noise robustness and recovery from perturbations.
- Emergence of two canonical biological features—heavy-tailed synaptic weights and approximate E/I balance—without hand-crafting, which strengthens the biological plausibility of the solution.
- Writing is generally clear, concise, and accessible to the NIPS community.

Weaknesses and questions
- Rationale for the chosen training method: The paper effectively demonstrates that the stability-constrained gradient procedure yields graded, non-saturating memories under Dale’s law. However, it is unclear whether this particular method is uniquely well-suited to the task or merely one viable approach among several. Could the authors articulate why this procedure preferentially leads to graded solutions (e.g., implicit regularization, margin properties around fixed points, proximity to criticality), and whether alternatives (e.g., constrained least-squares, FORCE-like schemes with sign constraints, or Lyapunov-based constructions) would produce similar outcomes?
- Objective–outcome alignment: The manuscript would benefit from an explicit connection between the optimization objective (stability of the linearization) and the observed properties (graded firing, lack of saturation). For example, do the stability constraints implicitly penalize trajectories that rely on saturating nonlinearities to maintain attractors, thereby encouraging solutions in the linear operating regime?
- Quantitative capacity comparison: Given the long history of auto-associative memory models, a capacity analysis would significantly strengthen the work. Even a limited comparison (e.g., memories per neuron at a fixed recall accuracy, or scaling trends as N increases) against classical Hopfield capacity and more recent graded-attractor models would contextualize the contribution. If full characterization is out of scope, please provide preliminary scaling curves or a discussion of expected capacity based on the spectrum of the trained Jacobians.
- Basins of attraction and robustness: The qualitative robustness is compelling, but quantitative measures would improve interpretability. Suggested metrics include: (1) basin size as a function of memory index, (2) recall accuracy versus initial perturbation magnitude, and (3) recall performance under varying noise statistics (variance, temporal correlations).
- Role of constraints and ablations: Since biological constraints are central to the paper, it would help to show ablations. For instance, how do performance and learned structure change if Dale’s law is relaxed, if E/I balance is not enforced, or if weight distributions are explicitly regularized? Similarly, how sensitive are results to network size, nonlinearity choice, and training hyperparameters?
- “Slow” dynamics characterization: The paper notes that trained fixed points are approached slowly. Can the authors quantify this (e.g., dominant eigenvalues of the Jacobian, distribution of time constants across memories) and clarify whether slow convergence is beneficial (e.g., smoothing noisy perturbations) or a byproduct of operating near marginal stability?
- Computational efficiency and scalability: A brief discussion of training complexity (time and memory), convergence behavior, and scalability to larger networks and memory loads would aid reproducibility and practicality assessments.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether excitatory and inhibitory populations are trained jointly with hard sign constraints or via a reparameterization; specify how weight updates are projected to satisfy Dale’s law.
- Report summary statistics of the learned weight distribution (e.g., kurtosis, fraction of near-zero weights) to substantiate “heavy-tailed.”
- Provide details on noise models used in evaluation and any mismatch between training and test noise.
- Where possible, include confidence intervals or multiple random seeds for performance plots.

Overall assessment
This is a timely, well-executed paper that advances biologically grounded auto-associative memory in recurrent rate networks. The combination of Dale’s law, graded activity, explicit local stability constraints, and strong empirical performance is compelling and of clear interest to the community. The primary missing piece is a more explicit connection between the training objective and the emergence of graded, non-saturating attractors, along with at least a preliminary capacity analysis or scaling discussion. Addressing these points—even partially—would materially strengthen the contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
