The paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and significant new insight. The authors observe that prior work often neglected biological constraints such as Dale’s law, employed binary memory representations within rate-based models, and utilized non-sparse memory representations. While each of these limitations has been addressed in isolation by other research, this work develops a unified strategy to address them all. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that overcome these issues. The core of their method is a cost function, optimized via gradient descent, that balances three key terms: one promoting fixed-point dynamics for desired memories, another ensuring the stability of those fixed points, and a third encouraging small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle through the weight parameterization, the authors effectively address the aforementioned shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network where excitation and inhibition are naturally balanced. Notably, saturation of single neuron firing emerges naturally as a consequence of the optimization process, rather than being explicitly enforced. Importantly, the authors utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory – consistent with the typical role of excitatory neurons in transmitting output – while treating inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their approach with numerical simulations, demonstrating robustness to noise. Beyond showcasing the network’s associative memory capabilities, they successfully explain experimental findings related to the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, attributing this to convergence towards the closest attractor given the initial condition (although this effect is characteristic of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity).

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a fundamental problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory can function. While the authors do not currently propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism, they acknowledge and reference recent proposals that may pave the way for such rules. I found their utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources particularly insightful and beneficial. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to the field of neural computation.

Several minor issues could be addressed:

*   The authors employ γ=0.04 in equation (3). A discussion of the results’ sensitivity to this parameter choice would be valuable (though I anticipate the sensitivity to be low).
*   A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide motivation for this specific choice of distribution.
*   The cost function (4) might yield solutions that deviate from exact fixed-point dynamics for the memories. The severity of this deviation should be quantified and discussed.
*   Lines 254-256 are unclear. Please clarify the statements made in this section.
*   The paper does not address capacity limitations. A statement regarding capacity, even if preliminary, would be beneficial given its importance in computational models.
*   It would be interesting if the authors could discuss the potential computational advantages conferred by Dale’s law. Specifically, can it be shown to enhance computational power under certain constraints? While it's possible Dale’s law is primarily a consequence of evolutionary or biophysical factors, relating the work to this broader question would be valuable.
