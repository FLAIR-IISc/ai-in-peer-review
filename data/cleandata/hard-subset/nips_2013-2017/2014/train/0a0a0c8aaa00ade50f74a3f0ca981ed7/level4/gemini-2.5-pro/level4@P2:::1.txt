### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the long-standing challenge of creating biologically plausible models of auto-associative memory. The authors identify several key limitations in previous models, such as the violation of Dale's Law (i.e., neurons being both excitatory and inhibitory), reliance on binary or saturating neural responses to encode memories, and simplified assumptions about inhibitory feedback. To overcome these limitations, the authors propose a novel framework based on control theory to construct a rate-based network of distinct excitatory and inhibitory neurons.

The core of their method is to frame memory storage as an optimization problem with two objectives: ensuring that desired analog activity patterns are fixed points of the network dynamics, and ensuring that these fixed points are dynamically stable. To achieve robust stability, they employ a recently developed technique, the minimization of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), which allows for efficient gradient-based optimization of the network's connectivity matrix.

Through simulations, the authors demonstrate that this procedure successfully embeds multiple graded, analog memory patterns. The resulting networks are robust to noise in both the initial recall cue and the ongoing dynamics. Notably, the optimization process leads to two emergent properties that align with experimental observations in the cortex: 1) a sparse synaptic weight distribution with a peak at zero and long tails, and 2) a tight balance between excitatory and inhibitory inputs received by each neuron, which prevents runaway activity and allows for graded, non-saturating firing rates. The model also reproduces the phenomenon of stimulus-induced variability reduction, a common feature of cortical circuits.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

This is a well-written and technically strong paper that presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and neural networks.

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides a compelling solution to a classic problem. While attractor networks are a foundational concept, creating one that simultaneously respects Dale's Law, operates in a balanced E/I regime, and stably stores *analog* (graded, non-saturating) patterns represents a significant advance. This work moves the theory of associative memory closer to biological reality and will likely be of great interest to both theoretical and experimental neuroscientists.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control theory is a novel and powerful technical approach for this problem. The methodology is sound, and the authors clearly explain how it is used to enforce the stability constraint. The results are well-supported by thorough simulations that convincingly demonstrate the network's capabilities, including its robustness to noise and synaptic pruning. The emergence of biologically realistic features like E/I balance and sparse connectivity from the optimization objective is a particularly strong result.

3.  **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the work by reviewing the limitations of previous models. The methods are described with sufficient detail, and the figures are well-chosen and effectively illustrate the paper's main findings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against Acceptance / Areas for Clarification)**

The paper is very strong, but its impact could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Clarifying the Link Between Method and Outcome:** The training procedure is highly effective at producing an auto-associative network with non-saturating, graded responses. However, the manuscript could benefit from a more detailed discussion on *why* this particular optimization framework is so well-suited for achieving this specific outcome, which has been a major challenge for previous models. Is the emergence of graded responses a general property of enforcing stability via the SSA in networks without explicit saturation, forcing the optimizer to find a balanced E/I solution? Or is it specific to other details of the setup? A deeper insight into this mechanism would strengthen the paper's message.

2.  **Contextualizing Network Performance:** The paper demonstrates successful storage and recall for 30 memories in a network of 150 neurons. While the primary contribution is the model's biological plausibility rather than raw capacity, the community would surely be interested in how the network's performance scales. Given the rich history of work on memory capacity in attractor networks, even a brief discussion comparing this model's capacity to previous models (e.g., Hopfield networks) would be highly valuable. A direct quantitative comparison may be difficult due to the different nature of the memories (analog vs. binary), but a qualitative discussion or preliminary scaling analysis would help place this important contribution in a broader context.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution to a fundamental problem in theoretical neuroscience. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already excellent submission.
