This paper revisits the venerable problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a novel perspective and significant new insights. The authors' primary contribution is a unified framework for designing biologically plausible associative memories that simultaneously satisfy multiple constraints often addressed only in isolation in prior work. These constraints include adhering to Dale's law, using non-binary memory representations in rate-based models, and employing sparse representations for memories.

To achieve this, the authors leverage a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques. They formulate an optimization problem with a cost function designed to balance three key objectives: (1) ensuring the desired memory patterns are fixed points of the network dynamics, (2) guaranteeing the stability of these fixed points, and (3) regularizing the synaptic weights to prevent neuronal saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle within the weight parameterization, the model naturally achieves a balance between excitation and inhibition. Interestingly, while neuronal saturation is not explicitly constrained, it emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization process. The model also incorporates the biological observation that memories are stored primarily by excitatory neurons (which typically send output to other regions), while treating inhibitory neurons as internal variables to be optimized for network stability and function.

The authors validate their model through numerical simulations, demonstrating its effectiveness as an associative memory and its robustness to noise. Furthermore, the model reproduces and explains the experimental finding of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset. This phenomenon is explained as a convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition, a general feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that makes an important contribution to computational neuroscience. By applying novel control-theoretic tools to a foundational problem, the authors demonstrate a principled method for constructing a biologically plausible associative memory. The conceptualization of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources for shaping network dynamics is particularly insightful. While the paper does not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism, the authors rightly point to recent work that may provide a path toward such rules. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and valuable contribution to the field.

**Minor Issues and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The authors use `γ=0.04` in equation (3). A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter's choice would be beneficial, even if the effect is expected to be minor.
*   A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide a brief motivation for this choice.
*   The cost function in equation (4) penalizes deviations from the fixed-point condition rather than strictly enforcing it. It would be valuable to quantify the magnitude of this violation in the resulting solutions and discuss its implications.
*   The statements on lines 254-256 were unclear. The authors are encouraged to revise this passage for clarity.
*   A discussion of the network's memory capacity is notably absent. While a full analysis may be beyond the scope of this work, even a preliminary statement or empirical investigation of this crucial computational property would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   The paper successfully incorporates Dale's law as a biological constraint. It would be interesting if the authors could speculate on the potential computational benefits of this principle. For instance, can this law be shown to enhance computational power under specific constraints, or is it primarily a result of evolutionary and biophysical limitations? A brief discussion relating the model to this broader question would be a welcome addition.
