This is a clearly-written and novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a compelling review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting the contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures are well-chosen to illustrate the main results. The authors appropriately acknowledge the network’s performance limitations with noise, emphasizing the robustness achieved through their training procedure.

The core contribution of this work lies in formulating memory storage as a control problem and leveraging robust control theory (specifically, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa – SSA) to optimize network parameters under physiological constraints. This approach demonstrably overcomes limitations of prior models, achieving stable, graded recall dynamics while adhering to Dale’s Law. The observation that the resulting networks exhibit balanced excitation and inhibition and sparse connectivity further strengthens the biological plausibility of the model.

However, a more detailed justification for the chosen optimization method would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge previous approaches to enforce Dale’s Law and graded memories, they don’t fully articulate *why* the SSA-based optimization is particularly well-suited to this task. What advantages does this method offer compared to alternatives? Does the choice of SSA specifically address challenges inherent in optimizing for stability within the constraints of physiological realism? Expanding on this point would strengthen the paper’s contribution. For example, a brief discussion of why gradient-based methods struggle with directly optimizing the spectral abscissa and how the SSA circumvents these issues would be valuable.

Furthermore, a comparison of the network’s capacity (number of memories as a function of network units) to that of previous models would be informative. While a comprehensive benchmark might be beyond the scope of this paper, even 1-2 sentences contextualizing the model’s capacity relative to existing literature would enhance its impact. The authors demonstrate impressive robustness and adherence to biological constraints; understanding how these factors trade off with capacity would provide a more complete picture. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of control-theoretic principles and robust stability optimization to the problem of auto-associative memory is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and optimization procedure are well-defined and appear rigorously implemented. The results are supported by simulations and analysis.
*   **Biological Plausibility:** The model incorporates key physiological constraints (Dale’s Law, graded responses, balanced excitation/inhibition, sparse connectivity) that are often overlooked in theoretical studies.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible.
*   **Significance:** This work advances our understanding of the neural substrate of memory and provides a framework for building more biologically realistic and robust memory models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Justification of Method:** The rationale for choosing the SSA-based optimization method could be strengthened.
*   **Lack of Capacity Comparison:** The absence of a comparative analysis of network capacity limits the assessment of the model’s overall performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong submission that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The strengths of the paper – its novelty, technical soundness, biological plausibility, and clarity – significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding justification of the optimization method and capacity comparison.
