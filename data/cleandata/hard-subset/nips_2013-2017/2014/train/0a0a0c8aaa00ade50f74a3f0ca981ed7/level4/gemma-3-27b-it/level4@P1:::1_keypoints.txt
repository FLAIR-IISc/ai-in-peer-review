- Presents a biologically plausible auto-associative memory model in a rate-based network that enforces Dale’s Law and uses non-saturating (graded) units.  
- Memories are encoded via synaptic updates from gradient descent, constrained by a recent method that ensures stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Trained fixed points exhibit slow (near-fixed) but stable network dynamics.  
- Trained network shows a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, matching experimental observations.  
- Individual units receive approximately balanced excitatory and inhibitory input, consistent with cortical data.  
- These network features enable robust auto-associative performance under perturbations and stochastic input noise.  
- Manuscript is clearly written, novel, provides a good literature review, and uses clear figures to illustrate results.  
- Authors demonstrate the network’s performance limits under noise, highlighting the robustness of the learned solution.  
- Reviewer requests clarification of the rationale for choosing this specific training procedure relative to stated objectives.  
- Reviewer asks whether alternative training methods could achieve similar results and why this method is particularly effective.  
- Reviewer suggests (possibly for future work) comparing the model’s memory capacity to previous models (number of memories vs. network size).  
- Overall positive assessment: introduces a realistic model that achieves graded memory encoding by enforcing stable fixed points.
