This paper proposes a rate-based recurrent E–I network trained to embed multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while respecting key biological constraints. The authors address common shortcomings of prior attractor models—such as violating Dale’s law, using effectively binary patterns, or relying on high-rate saturation—by directly optimizing synaptic weights and inhibitory steady-state activities so that target analog patterns become stable fixed points. The cost function penalizes drift at the target states, regularizes weights via the Frobenius norm, and enforces stability through robust control methods (smoothed spectral abscissa). The learned networks exhibit realistic, sparse-like weight distributions and operate in a dynamically balanced regime with excitation and inhibition closely matched in each attractor.

The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The modeling assumptions and training protocol are easy to follow, and the results are presented in a way that makes the approach and its implications transparent. The combination of biologically motivated constraints (Dale’s law, graded responses, low firing rates) with a direct stability-optimization procedure is compelling and yields networks with non-saturated attractors and balanced E/I interactions. Overall, the work is an interesting and useful contribution to the study of autoassociative memory networks.

Strengths:
- Clear objective and rigorous optimization framework to enforce both fixed-point and stability conditions.
- Respect for neural constraints (separate E/I populations, graded responses) and resulting balanced dynamics.
- Emergence of realistic weight distributions without explicit sparsity penalties.
- Demonstrated robustness of recall to noisy cues and ongoing noise.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The approach relies on global optimization with access to full network states and SSA gradients; discussion of how local plasticity might approximate this process could be expanded (beyond the brief mention of inhibitory plasticity).
- Analysis of basin sizes is limited; more systematic characterization of recall basins and capacity (beyond a single n = 150, m = 30 example) would strengthen claims about robustness and scalability.
- The rate-based model is biologically motivated, but a demonstration that the learned connectivity transfers to spiking networks with comparable performance would increase significance.
- Some hyperparameter choices (e.g., SSA smoothing ε and regularization weights) appear heuristic; sensitivity analyses would be helpful.

In sum, the paper presents a clear, technically sound framework for constructing analog attractor networks under realistic constraints, with convincing empirical demonstrations of balanced operation and robust recall. It should be of interest to the NIPS community studying recurrent dynamics, memory, and biologically grounded optimization.
