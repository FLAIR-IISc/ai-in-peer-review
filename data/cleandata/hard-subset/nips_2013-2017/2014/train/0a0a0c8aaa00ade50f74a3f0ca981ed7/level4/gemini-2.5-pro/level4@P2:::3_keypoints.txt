- Revisits associative memory, proposing a control-theoretic framework to construct E/I recurrent networks that satisfy multiple biological constraints simultaneously.  
- Optimizes synaptic weights to embed arbitrary analog (graded-rate) patterns as stable attractors.  
- Uses a cost function balancing (1) fixed-point enforcement via minimizing activity derivatives, (2) stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and (3) weight regularization.  
- Enforces Dale’s law by parameterizing weights, which yields emergent E/I balance, low firing rates, and avoidance of saturation without explicit nonlinearity.  
- Treats inhibitory neuron activities as auxiliary optimization variables to gain degrees of freedom for stability.  
- Numerical results show robust autoassociative recall, noise resilience, sparse connectivity, and trial-to-trial variability reduction after cues.  
- Strength: Provides a principled, unified framework addressing Dale’s law, analog memories, low firing rates, and E/I balance together.  
- Strength: Novel application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa from control theory enables gradient-based stability optimization for multiple fixed points.  
- Strength: Biologically relevant properties (tight E/I balance, long-tailed sparse weights, operation far from saturation) emerge without being enforced.  
- Strength: Offers a mechanistic account linking network dynamics to experimentally observed variability reduction.  
- Strength: Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well organized.  
- Weakness: Lacks analysis of storage capacity and how performance scales with number of stored patterns (m) vs. network size (n).  
- Weakness: Does not quantify how closely optimized “slow points” match true fixed points or how drift/error scales with parameters or memory load.  
- Weakness: No biologically plausible, local learning rule is provided; the gap between global SSA-based optimization and plausible synaptic mechanisms needs discussion.  
- Minor clarification: Motivate choice of log-normal memory pattern distribution explicitly in the main text.  
- Minor clarification: Discuss sensitivity of results to the gain parameter γ in Equation (1).  
- Minor clarification: Justify the large relaxation of the recall success threshold after synapse clipping (0.001 → 0.08) and whether this masks performance degradation.  
- Minor clarification: Discuss potential computational benefits or trade-offs of enforcing Dale’s law beyond biological realism.  
- Overall assessment: Clear, innovative, technically sound, and significant contribution to computational neuroscience; strongly recommended for acceptance.
