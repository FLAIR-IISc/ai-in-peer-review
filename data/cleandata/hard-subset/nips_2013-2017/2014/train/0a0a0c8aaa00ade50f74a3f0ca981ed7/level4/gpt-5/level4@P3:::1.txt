Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing autoassociative memory networks that satisfy several key neurobiological constraints simultaneously. The authors optimize a rate-based recurrent network with distinct excitatory and inhibitory populations (enforcing Dale’s law via a reparameterization of synaptic signs) so that a set of arbitrary analog activity patterns become asymptotically stable fixed points. Two objectives drive learning: (i) making each target pattern a fixed point by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at that state, and (ii) ensuring local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target. The approach yields networks that recall analog memories without saturating firing rates, operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime, are robust to perturbations and ongoing noise, and show stimulus-induced variability quenching reminiscent of findings in cortex.

Relative to classic Hopfield-type models that require symmetry and often binary codes, the present work enforces biologically realistic sign constraints and graded patterns. Unlike prior graded-memory theories without explicit inhibition or those relying on LIF saturation or global inhibition calibrated to a binary-like level of excitation, the method uses explicit inhibitory units and robust stability optimization to support heterogeneous analog attractors. The paper also connects to bump attractor models and spike-timing-based graded memory work, while overcoming their reliance on spatial structure or lack of inhibition. The application of SSA from robust control to neural memory networks appears novel and technically well motivated.

Strengths:
- Addresses multiple physiological constraints at once: Dale’s law, graded non-saturating responses, low firing rates, and balanced E/I.
- Clear formulation of memory storage as simultaneous fixed-point and stability optimization; the SSA-based stability objective is principled and computationally tractable.
- Demonstrates robust recall with performance comparable to an “ideal observer,” including tolerance to substantial cue corruption and ongoing noise.
- Emergent synaptic weight distributions are heavy-tailed and sparse, in line with cortical data; ablation suggests that a small fraction of strong synapses dominate recall.
- Balanced-state analysis is compelling and relates attractor-specific E/I co-tuning to stable operation; the stimulus-driven reduction in across-trial variability matches a widely reported cortical phenomenon.
- Writing and figures are clear and informative.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Capacity and scaling: The main demonstration uses n = 150 neurons and m = 30 memories (capacity ~0.2). There is no systematic capacity analysis versus network size or comparison with theoretical/empirical benchmarks from prior graded-memory or balanced network models.
- Basin geometry and drift: The learned states are “slow points” rather than exact fixed points; empirical recall succeeds, but quantitative characterization of basins (size, convergence rates, drift time constants) is limited.
- Algorithmic choices and rationale: While SSA is a sensible stability proxy, the paper does not explain why it is particularly advantageous for this problem relative to alternatives (e.g., margins on real parts of eigenvalues, Lyapunov certificates, contraction metrics, Gershgorin-based constraints, or pole-placement heuristics). Sensitivity to the smoothing parameter ε and regularization weights is only briefly discussed.
- Biological plausibility of learning: The optimization adjusts all synapses jointly with access to target attractors and also treats inhibitory steady-state activities as free variables during optimization. Although this does not inject memory-specific inputs at recall, it raises questions about how local plasticity could implement a similar objective and whether additional degrees of freedom inflate achievable capacity.
- Architectural constraints: Connectivity is optimized without explicit sparsity constraints or structured anatomy; sparsity emerges but is not controlled. It would be useful to test whether the method tolerates pre-specified sparse topologies and how weight clipping or L1 penalties affect performance and stability.
- Spiking generalization: The connection to spiking models is argued on gain-function grounds but not demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a capacity study: scaling of successful recall probability and stability margin with n and m; compare qualitatively with known results for graded memories and balanced attractors.
- Report stability margins: distribution of (smoothed) spectral abscissae at learned attractors, convergence rates, and drift timescales when starting at the target.
- Justify SSA: briefly compare to alternative stability proxies (even via ablations), show sensitivity to ε, ηs, and ηF, and discuss computational cost (runtime, gradient evaluations) versus network size.
- Biological plausibility: clarify the role of inhibitory steady-state variables during learning (do they reflect the network’s self-consistent solution?) and discuss candidate local plasticity mechanisms (e.g., inhibitory plasticity) that could approximate the optimization.
- Robustness tests: stability under synaptic perturbations/noise, pre-imposed sparse connectivity, different gain nonlinearities, and larger networks.
- Optional but valuable: a small demonstration that the learned connectivity supports similar attractors in a matched LIF spiking network.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound optimization framework with convincing simulations. Additional quantitative analysis of capacity, stability margins, and robustness would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: Very well written and organized; figures are effective. A tighter explanation of why SSA is the right tool here would help.
- Originality: High. Applying robust control (SSA) to embed analog memories in Dale-compliant, balanced networks is novel.
- Significance: Potentially substantial, as it offers a general recipe for building realistic analog attractor networks and links to ubiquitous cortical phenomena (E/I balance, variability quenching). The impact would be amplified by capacity benchmarks and spiking demonstrations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled framework meeting multiple biological constraints.
- Strong empirical performance and mechanistic insights (balance, sparsity).
- Clear presentation with connections to influential cortical phenomena.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited capacity/scaling analysis and benchmarking.
- Insufficient justification and sensitivity analysis for the chosen stability objective.
- Learning framework’s biological plausibility and transfer to spiking not yet demonstrated.

Overall, this is a clear, innovative contribution that advances the construction of biologically grounded analog memory networks. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above clarifications, especially around capacity, stability margins, and the rationale for SSA.
