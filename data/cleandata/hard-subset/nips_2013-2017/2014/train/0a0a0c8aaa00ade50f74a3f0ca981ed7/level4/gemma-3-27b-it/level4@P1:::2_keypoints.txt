- Goal: design a rate-based network that stores graded, non-saturating attractors while respecting Dale’s law.  
- Training: gradient descent on a cost that minimizes activity changes in target attractors, penalizes weight norm, and maximizes stability.  
- Framework: uses a control-theoretic approach to jointly optimize synaptic connectivity and inhibitory neuron activity.  
- Stability method: employs the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for differentiable, efficient robust stability optimization.  
- Main result: trained weight distribution is consistent with experimental synaptic statistics.  
- Emergent property: network exhibits a balance of excitation and inhibition within attractor states.  
- Robustness: network robust to noise in recall cues and ongoing dynamics.  
- Additional finding: model reproduces stimulus-induced reduction in trial-to-trial variability seen in cortex.  
- Biological plausibility: incorporates Dale’s law and realistic firing-rate dynamics to increase relevance.  
- Clarity: manuscript is well-written and methods/results are easy to follow.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on Hopfield and subsequent attractor models, addressing symmetry, binary representation, and saturation issues.  
- Novelty: directly enforces fixed-point and stability conditions for arbitrary graded memory patterns.  
- Strength: SSA-based optimization offers a principled, efficient route to robust attractor stability.  
- Limitation: rate-based model abstracts away spiking dynamics, limiting detailed neural mechanism insights.  
- Limitation: parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε) appear heuristic and lack sensitivity analysis or stronger justification.  
- Limitation: memory patterns sampled from a log-normal distribution—limited exploration of other, more complex distributions.  
- Limitation: removing weak weights has little impact overall but requires higher recall thresholds, indicating subtle introduced instability.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, technically sound, biologically grounded approach with significant results.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited applicability due to rate-based abstraction and heuristic parameter tuning.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add discussion of rate-based limitations and justify parameter choices.
