1. Revisits associative memory with a unified, biologically plausible approach that incorporates Dale’s law and non-binary, sparse memories.  
2. Employs a control-theoretic spectral-bounding method to construct the network.  
3. Optimizes a three-term cost (enforce memories as fixed points, ensure stability, penalize large weights) via gradient descent.  
4. Enforces Dale’s principle in the weight parameterization, producing a natural excitation–inhibition balance.  
5. Single-neuron firing-rate saturation emerges from optimization without being explicitly imposed.  
6. Memories are implemented using excitatory neurons; inhibitory neurons serve as optimizable stabilizing resources.  
7. Numerical simulations show robustness to noise and reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset explained by attractor convergence.  
8. Notes that reduced variability may be a general property of attractor networks with spontaneous baseline activity.  
9. Paper is well-written, clear, and makes a significant contribution by applying novel control-theoretic tools to a classic problem.  
10. Does not provide a biologically plausible learning rule but points to promising future directions; conceptualizing inhibitory neurons as a resource is especially insightful.
