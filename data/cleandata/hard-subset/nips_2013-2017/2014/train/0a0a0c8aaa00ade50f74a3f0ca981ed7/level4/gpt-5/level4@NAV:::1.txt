Review- Summary: The authors propose an auto-associative memory model in a rate-based neural network that obeys a set of biologically plausible constraints. Prior models have typically omitted crucial properties of real cortical circuits, in particular strict compliance with Dale’s Law (neurons exert either exclusively excitatory or exclusively inhibitory effects) and the ability to store memories without relying on units that saturate or behave in a binary fashion. Memory patterns are embedded via synaptic updates derived from a gradient descent scheme, coupled with a recently introduced approach that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points. Through simulations, the authors demonstrate that training yields attractor states with slow network dynamics—i.e., trajectories that lie close to, but not exactly at, fixed points—and that these states are stable. They further report two network characteristics consistent with cortical data: a synaptic weight distribution centered near zero with heavy tails, and approximate balance of excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input per unit. These properties are identified as central to the model’s robust auto-associative retrieval when perturbed away from a stored state, even under stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a well-written and original submission that should appeal to a broad audience. The authors provide a succinct and informative overview of prior approaches to training networks for auto-associative memory, clearly identifying their strengths and limitations. Both the architecture and training pipeline are presented clearly, and the figures effectively convey the main findings. The authors also document how performance degrades under injected noise, thereby underscoring the robustness of the learned solution.

One suggestion is to articulate more clearly how the chosen training method aligns with the stated objectives. As noted, alternative techniques have been used to enforce Dale’s Law or to realize graded (non-binary) memory representations, with the latter typically being more challenging. While the proposed procedure successfully constructs an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, the text does not indicate whether this method was selected because it is particularly well-suited to that goal. Could other training strategies yield the same outcome? Can the authors provide intuition for why this specific approach is especially effective?

Finally, although likely beyond the scope of the present work, a comparison of storage capacity (number of memories versus network size) against established models would be valuable given the extensive literature on auto-associative memories.
