- Introduces a novel control-theoretic framework that enforces Dale’s law, non-saturating graded activity, and dynamic excitation–inhibition balance in auto-associative memory networks.  
- Directly optimizes synaptic weights to stabilize arbitrary analog memory patterns as fixed points.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability—a technique not previously applied to memory networks.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced regime and exhibit synaptic sparsity.  
- Demonstrates noise-robust recall and robustness to memory corruption in experiments.  
- Reproduces experimentally observed reductions in trial-to-trial variability, aligning with cortical data.  
- Technically rigorous with comprehensive experiments validating robustness and stability.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized presentation; methods are described with reproducible detail.  
- Original integration of SSA optimization with biological constraints fills gaps left by prior work.  
- Significant potential to explain cortical memory phenomena and advance biologically plausible modeling.  
- Weakness: lacks a comparison of memory capacity (memories per neuron) against prior methods.  
- Weakness: does not explicitly justify choosing SSA over alternative robust-control approaches (e.g., Lyapunov methods).  
- Overall verdict: methodologically sound, biologically relevant, and highly suitable for NIPS, with minor addressable gaps.
