- Novel control-theoretic framework (SSA minimization) used to design recurrent networks that store and recall graded, analog memory patterns.
- Cost function optimized by gradient descent enforces target patterns as fixed points, stabilizes those fixed points, and regularizes synaptic weights.
- Model enforces Dale’s law via parameterization and represents memories only in excitatory population activity.
- Inhibitory neurons treated as optimizable auxiliary variables, using inhibition as a resource to stabilize excitatory memory states.
- Optimized networks are robust to noise in initial cues and ongoing dynamics.
- Networks naturally operate in a balanced excitation–inhibition regime, develop sparse connectivity, and reproduce stimulus-driven reduction in trial-to-trial variability.
- Paper provides a principled synthesis that addresses multiple biological constraints simultaneously, moving beyond prior models that tackled them in isolation.
- Resulting model is biologically realistic: supports graded memories, non-saturating firing rates, and Dale-compliant connectivity.
- Insightful reframing of inhibition’s role as a dynamic, optimizable resource for stability.
- Clear, well-organized presentation with numerical experiments that support the claims.
- Major limitation: the optimization is not a biologically plausible local learning rule, leaving open how biology could implement it.
- Major omission: no analysis of storage capacity or how recall performance scales with number of stored memories.
- Request for sensitivity analysis of neuronal gain parameter γ and robustness to different pattern distributions beyond log-normal.
- Concern that minimizing ||dv/dt||² may yield “slow points” rather than exact fixed points—quantify typical drift magnitudes and distances to true fixed points.
- Minor requests: clarify ambiguous statements (lines 254–256) and discuss whether enforcing Dale’s law provides computational benefits (stability/robustness) or is purely biological.
