

This paper presents a rate-based neuronal network model designed to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while addressing key limitations of prior attractor models. Specifically, the authors overcome two critical shortcomings: violations of Dale's law (which requires neurons to use a single neurotransmitter type) and saturation of neural activities in memory states. The model employs a biologically constrained training procedure based on gradient descent applied to a cost function that simultaneously minimizes activity changes within target attractors, penalizes excessive weight matrix norms via the Frobenius norm, and enhances attractor stability.  

The results demonstrate that the trained network achieves two significant outcomes: (1) synaptic weight distributions closely align with experimental neurophysiological observations, and (2) a balanced excitation-inhibition (E/I) ratio emerges at the network level within memory states. This balance arises directly from the biological constraints imposed during model design, including Dale's law compliance and non-saturating activity dynamics. The authors successfully demonstrate that graded, non-saturated memory representations can be maintained without violating core neurobiological principles.  

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. The model architecture, training methodology, and results are presented with remarkable clarity, making complex concepts accessible. The integration of biological constraints into the model design is particularly compelling, as it directly informs the emergence of realistic network properties—such as the observed E/I balance—rather than being an afterthought. This approach strengthens the paper’s contribution by bridging computational neuroscience with empirical findings.  

The work makes a valuable advancement in attractor network theory. Previous models often sacrificed biological plausibility for computational convenience, but this study shows how adhering to constraints like Dale's law and activity saturation limits can yield both theoretically sound and empirically relevant dynamics. The resulting weight distributions and E/I balance provide strong evidence that the model captures key features of biological memory systems, offering a more viable framework for future studies of neural coding and memory.  

The experimental validation is thorough and appropriately focused. The authors convincingly show that their training protocol consistently produces stable attractors under the imposed constraints, with minimal activity drift in memory states. The comparison to experimental weight distributions (e.g., from cortical recordings) is a significant strength, lending empirical credibility to the model’s predictions. The absence of saturation in attractor states is particularly noteworthy, as it resolves a persistent issue in traditional attractor networks where activity levels often reach pathological maxima.  

Minor suggestions for improvement include: (1) a brief discussion of how the model’s stability metrics relate to experimental measures of memory persistence, and (2) a clearer delineation of how the Frobenius norm term specifically prevents weight matrix overfitting. However, these are minor refinements; the core contribution is robust and well-supported.  

Overall, this paper represents a strong, well-executed contribution to the field. It successfully demonstrates that biologically constrained attractor networks can achieve both theoretical stability and empirical plausibility, advancing our understanding of how neural systems might implement memory storage. The clarity of presentation, technical rigor, and meaningful integration of biological constraints make this a highly valuable addition to NIPS. The work is clearly suitable for publication.
