Summary: The authors present a rate-based auto-associative memory model trained under a set of biologically plausible constraints. Unlike many prior models, it enforces Dale’s Law—each neuron has strictly excitatory or inhibitory outputs—and supports memory encoding without relying on neurons saturating or responding in a binary fashion. Memories are embedded via gradient-descent synaptic updates constrained by a recently published method that ensures the linearized dynamics around fixed points are stable. Simulations show the training procedure produces target states with slow dynamics (i.e., they lie near, but not exactly at, fixed points) that are nonetheless stable, as intended. Two emergent properties align with cortical measurements: a synaptic weight distribution centered near zero with long tails, and approximate balance between total excitatory and inhibitory input to each unit. The authors identify these features as critical to the network’s robust auto-associative performance when perturbed from a stored state, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clearly written, novel submission that will interest the community. The authors provide a helpful overview of prior approaches to auto-associative memory, highlighting key contributions and limitations. The network architecture and training procedure are clearly described, and the figures effectively illustrate the main results. The authors also demonstrate the network’s performance limitations under injected noise, which in turn underscores the robustness of the solution produced by their training procedure.

One recommendation is to clarify the relationship between the chosen training method and the stated objectives. As noted, prior work has enforced Dale’s Law or aimed for graded memories, with the latter being especially difficult. The proposed procedure successfully yields an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but the paper does not explain why this particular method was selected for that goal or why it is especially well-suited to achieving it. Could alternative training approaches yield similar results? Can the authors offer intuition or analysis for why this method is so effective?

Also—while likely beyond the scope of this paper—given the extensive literature on auto-associative memory, a comparison of storage capacity (number of memories as a function of network size) with previous models would be of broad interest.
