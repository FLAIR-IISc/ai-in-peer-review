- Proposes a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model that incorporates biologically relevant constraints, notably Dale’s Law.  
- Encodes graded memories without relying on saturated or binary unit responses.  
- Uses synaptic modifications derived from a gradient descent procedure for training.  
- Ensures stability by linearizing dynamics around a fixed point using a recently developed method.  
- Simulations show the training yields stable, slow network dynamics and effective auto-associative performance.  
- Trained networks display biologically consistent features: centered synaptic weight distribution and E/I balance.  
- These E/I balance and weight distribution properties are crucial for robust memory performance under input noise.  
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation, concise literature review, and effective illustrative figures.  
- Suggests clarifying why the chosen training procedure was selected over alternative methods enforcing Dale’s Law or graded memories.  
- Recommends, if feasible, comparing the trained network’s memory capacity and performance quantitatively with previous models.
