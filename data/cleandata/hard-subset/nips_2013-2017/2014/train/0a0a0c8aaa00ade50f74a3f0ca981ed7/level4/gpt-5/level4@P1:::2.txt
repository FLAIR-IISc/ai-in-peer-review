The authors present a rate-based network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that is trained to sustain multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while respecting key biological constraints. The work targets shortcomings of earlier attractor models, particularly violations of Dale’s law and the tendency for recall states to rely on saturating neuronal responses. Training proceeds via gradient descent on an objective that minimizes temporal drift at the desired attractors, regularizes the synaptic matrix through its Frobenius norm, and explicitly promotes attractor stability. The learned networks exhibit synaptic weight distributions close to experimental observations and achieve balanced excitation and inhibition in the attractor states.

The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. I found both the model and the training procedure straightforward to follow, and the presentation of results is clear. The combination of biologically motivated constraints in the model and the properties that emerge after training make this a compelling contribution to the study of attractor neural networks. In particular, the authors train a rate-based network to embed graded, non-saturated attractors supported jointly by synaptic weights and inhibitory activities, while enforcing excitatory/inhibitory sign constraints for biological plausibility. These constraints naturally yield a dynamically balanced regime of excitation and inhibition at the network level.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a relevant problem in a way that is both principled and biologically grounded. The resulting networks capture experimentally observed features of synaptic weight distributions and balanced dynamics, and the work advances the construction of attractor models that better align with physiological constraints.
