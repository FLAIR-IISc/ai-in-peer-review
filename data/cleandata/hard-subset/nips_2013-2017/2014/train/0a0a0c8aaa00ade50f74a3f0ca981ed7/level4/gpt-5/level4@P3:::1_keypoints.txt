- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to build autoassociative memory networks that respect Dale’s law and explicit E/I populations.  
- Optimizes rate-based recurrent networks so arbitrary analog activity patterns become asymptotically stable via two objectives: minimize dv/dt at targets and minimize the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian.  
- Produces networks that recall graded memories without firing-rate saturation, operate in a balanced E/I regime, are robust to perturbations and noise, and show stimulus-driven variability quenching.  
- Novel relative to prior work by enforcing biologically realistic sign constraints, using explicit inhibition for graded attractors, and applying SSA from robust control.  

Strengths:
- Simultaneously addresses multiple physiological constraints (Dale’s law, graded responses, low firing rates, E/I balance).  
- Clear, principled formulation of memory storage combining fixed-point and SSA-based stability optimization that is computationally tractable.  
- Demonstrates robust recall comparable to an ideal observer and tolerance to severe cue corruption and ongoing noise.  
- Emergent synaptic weights are heavy-tailed and sparse, with a small fraction of strong synapses dominating recall.  
- Balanced-state analysis links attractor-specific E/I co-tuning to stable operation and replicates variability quenching seen in cortex.  
- Writing and figures are clear and informative.  

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited capacity and scaling analysis: main demos use n=150, m=30 (capacity ~0.2) with no systematic scaling or benchmarking.  
- Learned states are slow points rather than exact fixed points; basin geometry, convergence rates, and drift time constants are insufficiently quantified.  
- Insufficient rationale for choosing SSA over alternative stability proxies and limited sensitivity analysis to smoothing/regularization hyperparameters.  
- Biological learning plausibility is unclear because optimization adjusts all synapses jointly, uses target attractors, and treats inhibitory steady-states as free variables.  
- Connectivity is optimized without explicit sparsity/architectural constraints; robustness to pre-specified sparse topologies or weight regularization is untested.  
- Generalization to spiking networks is argued but not demonstrated.  

Suggested improvements:
- Provide capacity scaling studies (successful recall probability and stability margin vs. n and m) and comparisons to prior graded-memory/balanced models.  
- Report stability margins (distribution of SSA), convergence rates, and drift timescales when initialized at targets.  
- Justify SSA choice via ablations or comparisons to alternatives, show sensitivity to ε and regularizers, and report computational cost scaling.  
- Clarify the role of inhibitory steady-state variables in learning and discuss candidate local plasticity mechanisms that could approximate the optimization.  
- Test robustness to synaptic perturbations, pre-imposed sparse connectivity, different gain nonlinearities, and larger networks.  
- Optionally demonstrate that learned connectivity produces similar attractors in a matched LIF spiking network.  

Evaluation (NIPS criteria):
- Quality: technically sound framework with convincing simulations but needs more quantitative capacity and robustness analyses.  
- Clarity: very well written; the rationale for SSA needs tighter explanation.  
- Originality: high—novel use of robust control/SSA in Dale-compliant balanced attractor networks.  
- Significance: potentially substantial; impact would grow with capacity benchmarks and spiking demonstrations.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled framework meeting multiple biological constraints with strong empirical performance and mechanistic insights.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited capacity/scaling benchmarks, insufficient justification and sensitivity analysis for SSA, and unresolved biological plausibility and spiking transfer issues.  

Overall recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance conditional on addressing clarifications, especially capacity analysis, stability margins, and the rationale/sensitivity for SSA.
