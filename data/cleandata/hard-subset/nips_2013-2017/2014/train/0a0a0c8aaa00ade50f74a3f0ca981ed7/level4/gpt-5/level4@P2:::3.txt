Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper revisits associative memory in recurrent neural networks from a control-theoretic perspective, addressing several biological constraints that many classic models neglect. The authors construct rate-based networks with distinct excitatory and inhibitory populations (satisfying Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) activity patterns as stable attractors without driving neurons into saturation. They formulate storage as an optimization problem: target patterns should be fixed points and locally stable. To this end, they minimize a cost combining (i) the norm of dv/dt at the target states (to enforce fixed points), (ii) a stability term based on the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) from robust control (to ensure local stability), and (iii) a weight penalty (to regularize and avoid runaway dynamics). Dale’s law is enforced by a reparameterization of synaptic signs. Inhibitory neurons are treated as auxiliary variables whose steady-state values are optimized jointly with synaptic weights, while memories are encoded in the excitatory population.

The paper situates itself well with respect to classic Hopfield networks and subsequent extensions that use binary patterns, enforce symmetry, or rely on saturating transfer functions and global inhibition. Unlike spatial “bump” attractors, the proposed framework learns arbitrary analog patterns and remains robust to noise. It also reproduces the stimulus-driven reduction in trial-to-trial variability observed in cortex.

Strengths:
- Unified and principled optimization framework that simultaneously enforces fixed points, stability, and biological constraints (Dale’s law) for analog memories.
- Use of SSA is novel in this context and well-motivated; it allows efficient gradient-based optimization of stability.
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced E/I regime, maintain low firing rates without explicit saturation, and show robustness to cue corruption and ongoing noise.
- Attractive interpretation of inhibitory units as auxiliary variables that stabilize recall while excitatory neurons carry the memory content.
- Clear empirical evaluation, including comparisons to an “ideal observer,” and an explanation for variability quenching after stimulus onset.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Sensitivity analyses are limited. It would help to quantify robustness to the choice of the gain parameter γ and other hyperparameters (e.g., ε in SSA, regularization strength).
- The log-normal choice for memory pattern distributions is plausible but needs stronger motivation and/or empirical justification.
- The optimization does not always produce exact fixed points; patterns can be “slow points.” Please quantify the residual dv/dt and the distance from the nearest true fixed point, and assess how this affects recall basins.
- Capacity is not characterized. Even a preliminary scaling analysis (e.g., number of memories vs. network size and sparsity) would be valuable.
- The discussion of the unclear statements around lines 254–256 needs revision; please clarify the intended point and its implications.
- The role of Dale’s law in computational efficiency could be discussed more critically: does enforcing Dale’s law improve stability, generalization, or capacity under certain constraints, or is it primarily a biological realism requirement?

Quality:
The method is technically sound and thoughtfully executed. The SSA-based stability term and the weight parameterization are appropriate, and the experimental evaluation is convincing. Additional quantitative analyses of sensitivity and capacity would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Clarifying the motivation for the log-normal pattern distribution, the impact of nonzero dv/dt at targets, and the noted unclear passage would improve readability.

Originality:
The control-theoretic formulation with SSA-based stability optimization for learning analog attractors under Dale’s law is novel and well positioned relative to prior work.

Significance:
The approach offers a practical recipe for constructing biologically grounded attractor networks with analog memories that are robust and balanced, and it connects to experimental observations on variability quenching. The framework may inspire extensions to spiking networks and local learning rules.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide sensitivity analyses for γ, ε, and regularization. Report performance and stability across ranges.
- Justify the log-normal assumption with data or show results for alternative pattern distributions.
- Quantify the fixed-point approximation error (e.g., maximum dv/dt, spectral gap, or distance to the nearest true fixed point) and relate it to recall success rates and basin sizes.
- Include preliminary capacity estimates (e.g., m/n vs. network size, sparsity, and weight norms), even if empirical.
- Expand the discussion on potential computational benefits or costs of enforcing Dale’s law.
- Clarify the statements around lines 254–256.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and principled framework unifying fixed-point embedding, stability, and biological constraints.
- Strong empirical performance and robustness; balanced E/I dynamics emerge naturally.
- Clear connection to neural variability phenomena.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of capacity analysis and limited sensitivity studies.
- Some methodological choices (pattern distribution, parameter settings) are under-motivated.
- No biologically plausible learning rule is provided, though discussed as future work.

Minor comments:
- Equation: the gain parameter γ = 0.04 is reasonable; please discuss sensitivity to this choice.
- Please motivate the log-normal distribution for memory states (e.g., empirical firing rate statistics).
- Clarify the extent to which the cost can produce slow points rather than exact fixed points, and quantify the severity.
- The statements around lines 254–256 were unclear; please rephrase.
- Consider at least a preliminary discussion of storage capacity.
- Elaborate briefly on whether Dale’s law confers measurable computational advantages or is primarily a biological constraint.

Overall assessment:
A clear and compelling paper that applies modern control tools to a classic problem, yielding biologically grounded analog attractor networks with strong performance. With added analyses on sensitivity and capacity, this would be a strong contribution.
