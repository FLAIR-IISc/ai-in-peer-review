- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological constraints (Dale’s Law) and uses graded, non-saturating neural responses.  
- Memories are encoded as stable fixed points of network dynamics with synaptic weights trained by gradient descent.  
- Training uses a stability-enforcing constraint that guarantees learned fixed points are stable.  
- Simulations show the method produces stable attractors with slow dynamics, robust to perturbations and noise.  
- Trained networks develop synaptic weight distributions centered at zero with long tails.  
- Networks exhibit a balance between total excitatory and inhibitory inputs per neuron.  
- Emergent weight distribution and E/I balance are argued to underlie robust memory recall.  
- Paper is well-written, novel, and relevant to computational neuroscience and machine learning communities.  
- Strong literature review, clear presentation of architecture and training, and effective illustrative figures.  
- Demonstrates robust performance under noisy conditions.  
- Recommendation 1: Explain why the chosen stability-enforcing training method specifically prevents unit saturation and whether alternatives could achieve similar graded responses.  
- Recommendation 2: Include or discuss a comparison of memory capacity/scaling (e.g., versus Hopfield networks) to contextualize performance trade-offs.
