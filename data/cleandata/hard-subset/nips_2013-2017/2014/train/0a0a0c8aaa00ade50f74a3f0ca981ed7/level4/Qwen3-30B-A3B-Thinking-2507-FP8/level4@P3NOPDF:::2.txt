

This manuscript presents a rate-based neuronal network model designed to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while addressing key limitations of prior attractor network approaches. Specifically, the authors overcome two critical shortcomings: violations of Dale's law (where single neurons project both excitatory and inhibitory synapses) and saturation of neural activities in memory states. The model achieves this through a biologically constrained training procedure that employs gradient descent on a cost function balancing three objectives: minimizing activity changes within target attractors, penalizing large weight magnitudes via the Frobenius norm, and maximizing attractor stability. The resulting network exhibits a synaptic weight distribution closely aligned with experimental observations and achieves a balanced excitation-inhibition (E/I) ratio at the network level during memory recall.

The model's design effectively integrates biological plausibility with computational functionality. By enforcing Dale's law through synaptic weight constraints—ensuring each neuron’s outputs are either purely excitatory or inhibitory—the authors avoid a major inconsistency in traditional attractor networks. Simultaneously, the training regimen prevents activity saturation, yielding graded (non-binary) memory states where neuronal firing rates remain within physiologically plausible ranges. This dual constraint leads to a natural emergence of E/I balance during memory maintenance, a feature previously difficult to achieve in biologically motivated attractor models. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these constraints are not merely theoretical but yield quantitatively meaningful results: the learned weight distributions match empirical data from cortical recordings, and the network sustains stable attractors without requiring excessive inhibition or synaptic scaling.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and structured. The technical exposition is clear, with the model architecture, training protocol, and cost function components logically explained without unnecessary jargon. Figures effectively illustrate the training dynamics, weight distributions, and E/I balance, directly supporting the central claims. The results section succinctly connects the imposed biological constraints to the emergent network properties, making the contribution both theoretically rigorous and experimentally relevant. The authors successfully bridge a gap between computational neuroscience and neurophysiology by showing how biological principles (Dale's law, non-saturated firing) can directly shape functional network properties (stable, balanced attractors).

The work represents a significant contribution to attractor network theory. Previous models often sacrificed biological realism for computational simplicity, leading to implausible synaptic architectures or activity patterns. Here, the authors demonstrate that biological constraints can be harmonized with functional requirements—specifically, the ability to store multiple memories robustly—without compromising performance. The resulting model not only aligns with experimental data but also provides a testable framework for understanding how cortical networks maintain stable representations. The emphasis on network-level E/I balance as an emergent property, rather than an imposed parameter, is particularly compelling and advances the field beyond earlier ad-hoc implementations.

Minor suggestions for clarity: The manuscript occasionally uses "attractor states" and "memory states" interchangeably; standardizing to "memory states" would enhance precision. Additionally, a brief discussion of how the model handles memory interference (e.g., during pattern overlap) would strengthen the analysis, though this is not essential to the core contribution. The writing is otherwise polished, with no grammatical errors or ambiguous phrasing.

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances our understanding of biologically plausible neural coding. The model’s ability to simultaneously satisfy Dale’s law, avoid saturation, and produce experimentally consistent weight distributions while maintaining stable attractors addresses a persistent challenge in the field. The clarity of the exposition, combined with the novelty of the approach and the robustness of the results, makes this work highly suitable for publication in NIPS. The authors have successfully demonstrated that biological constraints need not limit functional performance but can instead guide the emergence of realistic neural dynamics.
