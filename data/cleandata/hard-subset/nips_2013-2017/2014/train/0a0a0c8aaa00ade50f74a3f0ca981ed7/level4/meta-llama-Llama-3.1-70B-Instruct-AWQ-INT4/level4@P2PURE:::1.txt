This is a clearly-written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a thorough review of the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are well-presented, and the figures are judiciously chosen to concisely illustrate the main results of the submission. The authors also demonstrate the network's performance limitations when network noise is included, showcasing the robust solution the training procedure finds.

One recommendation for improvement would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. While the authors acknowledge that other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, it is not entirely clear why they chose this particular method. The text does not provide explicit justification for the selection of this training procedure over alternative approaches, despite its success in building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the chosen method is uniquely well-suited to this task or if other network training procedures could achieve similar results.

Additionally, although this may be beyond the scope of the current paper, a comparison of the trained network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity (i.e., the number of memories as a function of network units) would undoubtedly be of interest to the community. Such a comparison would provide valuable context for evaluating the proposed model's efficacy.

In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the condition that network units can encode memories without relying on saturation or binary responses. By applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure in achieving robust auto-associative memory.
