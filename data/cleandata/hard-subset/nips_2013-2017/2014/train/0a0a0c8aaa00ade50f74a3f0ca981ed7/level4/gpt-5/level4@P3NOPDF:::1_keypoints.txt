- Paper introduces a biologically constrained auto-associative memory in rate-based recurrent networks enforcing Dale’s law, producing graded persistent activity and stable fixed points via a local linearization-stability training objective; simulations show robust recall and learned weights with heavy tails and per-neuron E/I balance.

- Strength: clear problem framing and motivation addressing limitations of classic auto-associative models (binary/saturating units, Dale’s law violations).

- Strength: elegant training objective that enforces local stability of target fixed points, yielding slow but convergent dynamics and robust recall.

- Strength: convincing simulations and figures demonstrating noise robustness and recovery from perturbations.

- Strength: emergence of biologically canonical features (heavy-tailed weights, approximate E/I balance) without handcrafting enhances plausibility.

- Strength: writing is clear, concise, and accessible to the community.

- Weakness: unclear why the chosen stability-constrained gradient method is uniquely suited to produce graded, non-saturating memories versus other possible methods.

- Weakness: lack of explicit linkage between the optimization objective (stability of the linearization) and the observed graded, non-saturating firing regimes.

- Weakness: missing quantitative capacity analysis or scaling comparison to Hopfield and other graded-attractor models.

- Weakness: robustness claims are qualitative—need quantitative metrics (basin sizes, recall accuracy vs perturbation, performance under varied noise statistics).

- Weakness: need ablation studies on biological constraints (Dale’s law, E/I balance, weight regularization) and sensitivity to network size, nonlinearity, and hyperparameters.

- Weakness: “slow” approach to fixed points should be quantified (dominant Jacobian eigenvalues, time-constant distributions) and its benefits or origins clarified.

- Weakness: request for discussion of computational efficiency, training complexity, convergence behavior, and scalability.

- Minor: clarify how Dale’s law is enforced during training (hard constraints vs reparameterization) and how updates are projected.

- Minor: report summary statistics of learned weight distributions (kurtosis, fraction near-zero) to substantiate “heavy-tailed.”

- Minor: provide details on noise models used in evaluation and any train/test noise mismatch.

- Minor: include confidence intervals or multiple random seeds for performance plots.

- Overall: timely and well-executed contribution combining Dale’s law, graded activity, and explicit local stability constraints; primary gaps are the mechanistic link between objective and graded attractors and a capacity/scaling analysis—reviewer leans toward acceptance.
