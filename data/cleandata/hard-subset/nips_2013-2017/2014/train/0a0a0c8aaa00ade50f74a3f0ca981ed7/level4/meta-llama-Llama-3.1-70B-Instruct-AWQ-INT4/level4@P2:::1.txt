This is a clearly-written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors nicely review the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are both presented in a clear way and the figures are well-chosen to quickly illustrate the main results of the submission.

The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network subject to a battery of biologically plausible constraints. Previous models of auto-associative memory have failed to include several key features of real biological networks, namely an adherence to Dale's Law that neurons have a strictly excitatory or inhibitory effect on their projections and the observation that networks can encode memories without relying on units that simply respond at their saturation rate or respond in a binary manner. The authors' model overcomes these limitations by employing a control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints.

The authors' training procedure, based on a gradient descent algorithm, is effective in optimizing the network parameters to embed attractor memories. The use of a smoothed spectral abscissa to ensure the stability of the fixed points is a key contribution of the paper. The authors illustrate the effectiveness of their training procedure with simulations, noting that the trained fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics and are stable, as desired.

The authors also identify two features of their trained network that are in line with experimentally observed features of cortical networks, namely a distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails and that an average network unit received approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input. These features are key components to the network's success in performing robust auto-associative memory when the network is perturbed from a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

One recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors noted, other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, with the latter condition seemingly more difficult to enforce. The chosen training procedure achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but nothing in the text indicates that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly well-suited to this task. Perhaps another network training procedure would achieve the same result? Can anything be said as to why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?

Additionally, a comparison of how the trained network performs against previous models in terms of network capacity (number of memories as a function of network units) would surely be of interest to the community. However, this may be beyond the scope of the current paper.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks.
* The authors employ a control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints.
* The training procedure, based on a gradient descent algorithm, is effective in optimizing the network parameters to embed attractor memories.
* The authors identify two features of their trained network that are in line with experimentally observed features of cortical networks.

Weaknesses:

* The relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives is not clearly explained.
* A comparison of how the trained network performs against previous models in terms of network capacity is not provided.

Acceptance:

Based on the strengths of the paper, I would recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel model of auto-associative memory that overcomes several limitations of previous models. The authors employ a control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. The training procedure is effective in optimizing the network parameters to embed attractor memories. While there are some weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall contribution of the paper.
