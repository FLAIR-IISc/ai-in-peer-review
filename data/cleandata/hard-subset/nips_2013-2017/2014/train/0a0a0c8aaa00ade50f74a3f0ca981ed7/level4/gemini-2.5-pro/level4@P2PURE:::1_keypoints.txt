- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological constraints (Dale’s Law and graded, non-saturating firing rates).  
- Encodes memories as stable fixed points of network dynamics and trains synaptic weights with gradient descent plus a stability constraint.  
- Demonstrates via simulations that trained attractors are stable, exhibit slow dynamics, and function as robust memory states.  
- Shows trained networks reproduce cortical-like features: heavy-tailed synaptic weight distribution centered at zero and excitatory/inhibitory input balance.  
- Paper is well-written, novel, and of significant interest, with a clear review of prior work.  
- Network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and figures effectively illustrate key results.  
- Noise-robustness analysis convincingly demonstrates the solution’s ability to recover memories after perturbations and in stochastic conditions.  
- Clarification requested on why the chosen training procedure specifically yields graded, non-saturating responses and whether other methods could achieve similar outcomes.  
- Suggests adding a brief discussion of what aspects of the method prevent unit saturation.  
- Recommends, possibly in future work, comparing memory capacity (memories stored vs. network size) to previous auto-associative models for context.
