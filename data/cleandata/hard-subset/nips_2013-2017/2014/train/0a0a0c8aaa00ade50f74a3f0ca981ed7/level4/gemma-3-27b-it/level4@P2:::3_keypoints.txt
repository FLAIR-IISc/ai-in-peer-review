- Revisits associative memory while incorporating biological constraints (Dale’s law, non-binary representations, sparsity).  
- Identifies limitations of prior models and proposes a unified framework to address them.  
- Leverages control-theoretic spectral bounding techniques to construct constrained networks.  
- Introduces a cost function balancing fixed-point attraction, stability, and weight magnitude, optimized by gradient descent.  
- Enforces Dale’s principle in weight parameterization, yielding balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Saturation of responses emerges naturally from optimization rather than being imposed.  
- Treats inhibitory neurons as auxiliary variables optimized with connectivity; memory encoding concentrated in excitatory populations.  
- Validates approach with numerical simulations demonstrating robustness to noise.  
- Explains reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset as convergence to nearest attractor.  
- Paper is clearly written, technically rigorous, and presents a novel, biologically plausible contribution.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule (acknowledged by authors).  
- Does not analyze network capacity; a preliminary discussion or analysis is recommended.  
- Sensitivity to the chosen γ = 0.04 in equation (3) is not explored and should be tested.  
- Choice of log-normal distribution for memory states needs further justification or linkage to neural data.  
- The cost function may not guarantee exact fixed points; quantify deviations and their effects.  
- Lines 254–256 are unclear and should be rephrased for clarity.  
- Suggest discussing whether Dale’s law confers computational benefits under specific conditions.  
- Overall pros: novelty, significance, technical soundness, clarity, and biological plausibility support acceptance.  
- Overall cons: missing learning rule, absent capacity analysis, and several minor clarifications required.
