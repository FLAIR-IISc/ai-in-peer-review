This manuscript presents a rate-based neuronal network trained to store multiple stable activity patterns, or memories. The authors' primary objective is to develop an attractor network model that overcomes critical limitations of previous work, specifically the violation of Dale's law and the issue of neuronal activity saturation in memory states. To achieve this, the network is trained via gradient descent on a cost function designed to simultaneously: 1) minimize activity changes within the desired attractor states, 2) regularize the synaptic weight matrix by minimizing its Frobenius norm, and 3) explicitly maximize the stability of the attractors. The main results demonstrate that this training procedure yields a network with two notable properties: a synaptic weight distribution that closely mirrors experimental findings, and a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition during the recall of attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written, and the authors present their model, training procedure, and results with excellent clarity. A key strength of this paper is its effective integration of biological constraints into the model's design. By imposing plausible constraints on synapses and neurons—such as enforcing Dale's law and maintaining non-saturated, graded firing rates—the authors show that crucial network-level properties, like excitatory-inhibitory balance, can emerge as a direct consequence of the learning process. This connection between imposed constraints and emergent biological features makes the paper an interesting and valuable contribution to the field of attractor neural networks, offering new insights into their functional principles.
