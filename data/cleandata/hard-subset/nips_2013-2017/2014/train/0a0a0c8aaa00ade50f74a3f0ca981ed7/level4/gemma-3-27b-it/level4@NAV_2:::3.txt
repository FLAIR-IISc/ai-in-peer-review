The paper returns to the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks with a novel and insightful approach, offering substantial contributions to the field. The authors identify key limitations in prior work, including the neglect of biological constraints like Dale’s law, the use of binary representations in rate-based models, and non-sparse memory representations. They present a strategy that simultaneously addresses these issues by leveraging recent advances in control theory, specifically spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks satisfying these constraints. They formulate an optimization problem balancing three terms: fixed point attraction, stability of fixed points, and weight regularization to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle through weight parameterization, they overcome the shortcomings of previous models, resulting in networks with naturally balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, saturation is not explicitly imposed but emerges as a consequence of the optimization process. The authors strategically utilize excitatory neurons for memory representation, treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable auxiliary variables. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated through numerical simulations, exhibiting robustness to noise. Beyond associative memory performance, the model explains experimental observations regarding the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset – a feature inherent to attractor dynamics with spontaneous baseline activity, as the network converges towards the closest attractor. 

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using innovative control-theoretic tools, demonstrating a pathway to biologically plausible associative memory function. While a biologically plausible learning mechanism is not proposed, the authors acknowledge relevant recent work. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is a particularly interesting and beneficial aspect of their approach. Overall, the paper presents a concise, clear, and important contribution to the field of neural computation. 

A few minor points could be addressed:
The authors use γ = 0.04 in Eq. (1). A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter choice would be valuable (although a strong dependence is not necessarily expected).
The rationale for using a log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) could be further elaborated.
The cost function (Eq. 5) may not guarantee exact fixed points. Quantifying the extent of any deviation from fixed-point dynamics and discussing its implications would strengthen the analysis.
Lines 254-256 contain statements that require clarification.
A discussion of memory capacity, even if preliminary, would be a welcome addition, as it is a crucial aspect of associative memory systems.
Exploring potential computational benefits conferred by Dale’s law – whether it enhances computational power under specific conditions or arises from evolutionary/biophysical constraints – could enrich the discussion.
