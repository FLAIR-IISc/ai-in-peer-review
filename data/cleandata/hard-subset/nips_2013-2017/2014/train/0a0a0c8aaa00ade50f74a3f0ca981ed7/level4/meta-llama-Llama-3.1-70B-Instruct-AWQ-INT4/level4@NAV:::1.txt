Summary: The authors propose a novel auto-associative memory model in a rate-based neural network, incorporating key biological constraints that were previously overlooked. Notably, this model adheres to Dale's Law, where neurons have exclusively excitatory or inhibitory effects, and encodes memories without relying on saturated or binary unit responses. The network employs synaptic modifications based on a gradient descent procedure, with stability ensured by a recently developed method for linearizing dynamics around a fixed point. Simulation results demonstrate the effectiveness of this training procedure, yielding stable, slow network dynamics. Furthermore, the trained network exhibits features consistent with experimental observations in cortical networks, including a centered distribution of synaptic weights and balanced excitatory and inhibitory inputs. These characteristics are identified as crucial for robust auto-associative memory performance, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This well-written submission presents a significant contribution to the field, offering a fresh perspective on training neural networks for auto-associative tasks. The authors provide a clear and concise review of previous work, highlighting the main strengths and weaknesses. The network architecture and training procedure are effectively presented, with well-chosen figures that succinctly illustrate the primary results. The authors also demonstrate the network's performance limitations under noisy conditions, showcasing the robustness of their training procedure.

One potential improvement would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. Given that alternative methods have been employed to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, it is unclear why this particular approach was selected. It would be beneficial to discuss whether this method is uniquely suited to this task or if other procedures could achieve similar results.

Additionally, although this may be beyond the scope of the current paper, a comparison of the trained network's performance with previous models in terms of network capacity would be a valuable contribution to the field. The authors' application of a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points has yielded a novel auto-associative memory model that adheres to key constraints of real neural networks, most notably the ability of network units to encode memories without saturating or binary responses.
