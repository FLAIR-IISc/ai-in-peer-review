This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh, control-theoretic perspective that adds substantial insight to an established area. The authors note that much prior work neglects key biological constraints such as Dale’s law, relies on binary memory representations in rate-based models, and uses non-sparse memory encodings. While these issues have been addressed individually in past studies, the authors present a unified strategy that tackles all of them simultaneously.

Concretely, they adopt a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding to construct networks that satisfy these constraints. Within this framework, they optimize a cost function with three terms via gradient descent: one term enforces that the target memories are fixed points, a second promotes stability of those fixed points, and a third penalizes large weights to prevent saturation. By parameterizing weights to explicitly enforce Dale’s principle, the network achieves a natural balance of excitation and inhibition. Notably, even though neuron-level saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges from the optimization. The memory is implemented using only the excitatory population (consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically project to other regions), while inhibitory neurons are treated as variables to be optimized.

The authors validate the approach through numerical simulations and demonstrate robustness to noise. Beyond associative memory performance, they also account for experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset—likely a general feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing—by showing convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

Overall, the paper is clear and well written. It tackles a venerable problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools and offers a compelling, biologically plausible account of associative memory. Although a biologically plausible learning mechanism is not yet provided, the authors point to recent proposals that might lead to such rules. The use of inhibitory neurons as resources to be optimized is particularly interesting and effective. The work is a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

Minor issues and requests for clarification:
- Equation (3) uses γ = 0.04. Please discuss the sensitivity of your results to this choice (I expect it to be small).
- Line 115 proposes a log-normal distribution for memory states. Please motivate this choice.
- The cost function in (4) may produce solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. How severe is this violation in practice? Please quantify and discuss.
- Lines 254–256 were unclear. Please clarify.
- The paper does not discuss capacity. Even a preliminary statement on capacity would be valuable given its importance.
- Please discuss potential computational benefits of enforcing Dale’s law. Can it enhance computational power under specific constraints? While it may primarily reflect evolutionary or biophysical constraints, it would be useful to relate your framework to this question.
