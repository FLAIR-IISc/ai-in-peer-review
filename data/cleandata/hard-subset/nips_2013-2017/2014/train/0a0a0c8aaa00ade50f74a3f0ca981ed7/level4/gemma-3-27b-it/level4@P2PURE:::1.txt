This is a clearly written and novel submission that will be of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a well-structured review of prior work on training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively highlighting both the contributions and limitations of existing approaches. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate the key results. The authors demonstrate the network’s performance limitations under noisy conditions, emphasizing the robustness of the solution achieved through their training procedure.

One recommendation is to further clarify the connection between the selected training procedure and the stated objectives. While other methods have been used to enforce Dale’s Law or achieve graded network memories – the latter often proving more challenging – the text does not explain *why* this particular method was chosen. The procedure successfully creates an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but it is unclear whether the authors believe this method is uniquely well-suited to this task, or if alternative training procedures might yield similar results. Exploring the reasons for this method’s effectiveness would strengthen the paper. 

Additionally, while potentially beyond the scope of this work, given the extensive history of auto-associative network training, a comparison of the trained network’s performance – specifically in terms of network capacity (memories stored as a function of network size) – against previous models would be valuable for the community. In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several biological constraints, notably allowing network units to encode memories without saturating or exhibiting binary responses, by applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system.
