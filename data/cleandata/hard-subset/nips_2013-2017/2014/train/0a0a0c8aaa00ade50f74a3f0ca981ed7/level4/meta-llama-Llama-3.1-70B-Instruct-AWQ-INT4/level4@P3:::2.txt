The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. They directly optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. The resulting networks operate in the balanced regime, are robust to corruptions of the memory cue as well as to ongoing noise, and incidentally explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset that is ubiquitously observed in sensory and motor cortices.

The paper is well-written, and the model and training procedure are easy to understand. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of previous attractor models and how their approach overcomes these shortcomings. The results are well-presented, and the combination of biological constraints and resulting features after training make this paper an interesting contribution to the understanding of attractor neural networks.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework that satisfies a set of relevant physiological constraints, including Dale's law and the presence of recurrent and sparse synaptic connections.
* The resulting networks operate in the balanced regime, which is a key feature of biological neural networks.
* The authors demonstrate that the networks are robust to corruptions of the memory cue and ongoing noise, which is an important aspect of biological memory systems.
* The paper provides a clear explanation of the limitations of previous attractor models and how the proposed approach overcomes these shortcomings.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes a rate-based neuronal network, which may not be directly applicable to spiking neural networks.
* The authors use a gradient descent on a cost function to train the network, which may not be biologically plausible.
* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed approach could be implemented in a biological system.
