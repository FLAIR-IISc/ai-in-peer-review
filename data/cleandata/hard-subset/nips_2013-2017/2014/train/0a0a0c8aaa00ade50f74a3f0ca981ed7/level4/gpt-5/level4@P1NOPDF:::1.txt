Summary: Applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors introduce a rate-based model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several biologically plausible constraints. Prior models often failed to enforce Dale’s law—whereby each neuron is strictly excitatory or inhibitory across its outputs—and typically relied on binary or saturating unit responses. Here, memories are encoded via synaptic modifications using gradient descent, with constraints derived from a recently published technique that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points. Simulations demonstrate that the trained attractors exhibit slow dynamics—trajectories linger near, but are not exactly at, fixed points—and are stable, as intended. The trained networks display two properties consistent with cortical data: synaptic weights are distributed around zero with long tails, and the average unit receives approximately balanced excitatory and inhibitory input. The authors argue that these features are crucial for robust auto-associative retrieval when the system is perturbed from a fixed point, even under stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clearly written and novel submission that will be of broad interest. The paper provides a helpful historical overview of auto-associative memory models, highlighting both the main contributions and the key shortcomings of earlier approaches. The network architecture and training procedure are presented clearly, and the figures effectively convey the core results. The authors also examine performance under injected noise, which both exposes the system’s limitations and underscores the robustness of the learned solution.

A primary recommendation is to clarify the link between the chosen training methodology and the stated objectives. As the authors note, various strategies have been used to enforce Dale’s law and to obtain graded, non-saturating memories, with the latter generally being more challenging. The proposed gradient-based training with stability constraints clearly achieves the desired outcome—an auto-associative network with non-saturating unit responses—but the paper does not explain why this particular method is especially well suited to that goal. Could alternative training procedures (e.g., other constrained optimization schemes, different regularizers, or alternative stability-enforcing techniques) reach the same outcome? Can the authors articulate mechanistic or theoretical reasons why their specific approach so effectively yields graded, stable attractors under Dale’s law and excitation–inhibition balance?

Relatedly, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the stability constraint shapes the learned synaptic structure. For instance, is the emergence of weight distributions centered at zero with heavy tails and near balance of excitation and inhibition a direct consequence of the stability-enforcing constraint, of Dale’s law, of the task structure, or of their interaction? Clarifying this would strengthen the claims about which ingredients are necessary versus incidental for robust retrieval.

Although likely beyond the scope of this paper, a comparison of capacity with prior models would be valuable. Given the rich literature on auto-associative memory, reporting how capacity (number of storable memories as a function of network size) compares to classic or more recent models—especially under the same biological constraints—would be of significant interest to the community.

Overall, this work makes a compelling case that biologically motivated constraints, coupled with stability-aware training, can produce robust, graded auto-associative memories under Dale’s law and excitation–inhibition balance. Clarifying the rationale for the chosen training procedure and, where possible, providing capacity comparisons would further enhance the impact.
