This manuscript presents a rate-based neuronal network model designed to store and maintain multiple stable activity patterns, representing memories. The authors address key limitations of existing attractor network models, specifically violations of Dale’s law and saturation of activity within memory states. Their approach utilizes a gradient descent algorithm to train the network, optimizing a cost function that simultaneously minimizes activity changes within desired attractors and the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, while maximizing attractor stability. The core findings demonstrate a weight distribution closely aligned with experimental observations and a robust balance of excitation and inhibition within the attractor states.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. I found both the model itself and the training procedure remarkably easy to follow, and the results are presented in a straightforward and accessible manner. The strength of this work lies in the thoughtful integration of biological constraints into the model design, and the resulting emergent properties observed after training. Specifically, the authors successfully train a rate-based network to support graded, non-saturated attractors, achieved through constraints on synaptic connections and neuronal properties that promote biological plausibility and a global excitation-inhibition balance. 

The motivation for this work is strong. Existing attractor network models often rely on unrealistic assumptions or exhibit behaviors inconsistent with neurophysiological data. The authors directly confront these issues with a novel training framework and a carefully considered network architecture. The cost function is particularly well-designed; the inclusion of the Frobenius norm regularization encourages sparsity in the weight matrix, which is beneficial for both computational efficiency and biological realism. The simultaneous optimization for attractor stability, minimal disruption of established patterns, and weight regularization appears to be a key ingredient to the success of this model.

The experimental validation, while primarily focused on comparing the learned weight distribution to empirical data, is convincing. The authors effectively demonstrate that the trained network’s synaptic weight distribution exhibits characteristics observed in biological neural circuits. Furthermore, the clear presentation of the resulting excitation-inhibition balance within the attractor states provides compelling evidence for the model’s biological relevance. 

However, some aspects could be further explored. While the authors demonstrate a balance of excitation and inhibition, a more detailed analysis of the network’s response to perturbations or noisy inputs would strengthen the conclusions. How robust are these attractors to external noise?  What is the network’s capacity – how many attractors can be reliably stored before performance degrades? An investigation into the model’s performance on a more complex task, such as pattern completion with incomplete or noisy cues, would demonstrate its utility beyond simply storing and recalling stable states. 

Additionally, the manuscript could benefit from a discussion of the computational cost associated with the training process. Is this method scalable to larger networks with more complex architectures? While the authors mention the biological plausibility of the model, further discussion on the limitations of rate-based models compared to spiking neuron models might be warranted, acknowledging the trade-offs between computational simplicity and biological fidelity.

Despite these minor suggestions, this is a strong contribution to the field of attractor neural networks. The authors have successfully developed a biologically constrained model capable of learning and maintaining multiple stable activity patterns without succumbing to the common pitfalls of previous approaches. The combination of a clear presentation, a well-motivated approach, and promising results makes this paper a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop. I recommend acceptance.
