The paper revisits the venerable problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, adding substantial insight to a classic topic. The authors note that much of the prior work has neglected biological constraints such as Dale’s law, used binary memory representations in rate-based models, and relied on non-sparse memory representations. While each of these issues has been addressed separately in the literature, the authors present a unified strategy that tackles all of them. Specifically, they employ a recent control-theoretic framework, based on spectral-bounding techniques, to construct networks that satisfy these constraints. In this framework, they optimize a cost function via gradient descent that balances three terms: one ensuring that the target memories are fixed points, a second ensuring their stability, and a third penalizing large weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle through the weight parameterization, the network naturally balances excitation and inhibition and overcomes the shortcomings of previous models.

Notably, although single-neuron firing saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges naturally from the optimization. Importantly, the authors use only the excitatory population to implement the memory (consistent with the fact that excitatory neurons typically project to other regions), while treating inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. They validate the approach with numerical simulations and demonstrate robustness to noise. Beyond showing strong associative memory performance, the model also accounts for experimental observations that trial-to-trial variability decreases following stimulus onset (though this appears to be a general feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity). This reduction is explained by convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper. It addresses a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools and demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory might operate. While the paper does not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism, it points to recent proposals that could lead to such rules. I found the use of inhibitory neurons as resources to be both interesting and advantageous. Overall, the work is a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues:
- The authors use γ = 0.04 in eq. (3). It would be good to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice (although I expect it to be small).
- A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide a motivation for this choice.
- The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. How severe is this violation? This should be quantified and discussed.
- Lines 254–256: The statements here were not clear to me. Please clarify.
- The authors do not discuss capacity. It would be helpful to provide at least a preliminary statement about capacity, given its importance for associative memory.
- It would also be useful to discuss potential computational benefits of Dale’s law. Can this constraint be shown to enhance computational power under specific conditions? This is not necessarily expected—it may simply reflect evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints—but it would be valuable to address.
