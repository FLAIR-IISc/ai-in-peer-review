Summary
The paper presents a control-theoretic framework for constructing recurrent rate networks of excitatory and inhibitory (E–I) neurons that store multiple analog (graded, non-binary) memories as stable attractors while respecting key physiological constraints. Neurons follow a rectified quadratic gain; synaptic weights obey Dale’s law via a reparametrization; and dynamics are trained so that prescribed excitatory activity patterns become fixed points with locally stable dynamics. Stability is enforced through minimization of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable proxy for the spectral abscissa from robust control. The method jointly optimizes synaptic weights and the steady-state inhibitory activities associated with each stored memory (treated as auxiliary variables during training). The resulting networks operate in a tightly balanced regime, recall memories robustly from noisy cues and under ongoing noise, exhibit heavy-tailed/sparse weight distributions reminiscent of cortical data, and reproduce a reduction of across-trial variability following stimulus onset. The work connects to classic attractor models (Hopfield; Treves and Rolls), balanced network theory (e.g., Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham), stabilized supralinear dynamics (Ahmadian et al.), and control-theoretic approaches to recurrent dynamics (e.g., Hennequin et al.), while addressing limitations such as violations of Dale’s law, binary memories, and reliance on saturation.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and clearly described. Using SSA to regularize local stability is well motivated and practical; the optimization targets both fixed-point residuals and stability while regularizing weights (Frobenius norm). Empirical evaluation shows: (i) successful recall of 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network (capacity 0.2), (ii) robustness across cue noise levels compared to an “ideal observer,” (iii) emergence of excitation–inhibition balance across memories, and (iv) limited reliance on many weak synapses.

However, several issues merit clarification:
- Fixed points vs. “slow points”: After training, dv/dt at targets remains small but non-zero, with stability inferred via nearby exact fixed points. Please quantify distances from targets to the actual fixed points and report eigenvalue spectra at those fixed points.
- Role of inhibitory “auxiliary” variables: Inhibitory steady states are optimized per memory. Biologically, this implies pattern-dependent inhibitory configurations without specifying how they are set. Clarify whether, during recall, inhibitory states arise purely from network dynamics (no memory-specific external inputs), and discuss mechanisms (e.g., inhibitory plasticity, modulatory control) that could realize this.
- Capacity and scaling: Capacity is reported for a single network size. Provide scaling with network size and pattern load, and relate to classic graded-memory capacity results (e.g., Treves & Rolls). How does SSA regularization affect capacity/basin size?
- Basins of attraction: Beyond success probabilities, quantify basin widths (e.g., critical cue noise σ at 50% success per memory; distributions across memories) and compare to the ideal observer under the same metric.
- Sensitivity/ablation: Analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters (ηs, ηF), the SSA smoothing ε, the gain nonlinearity, and initializations; report optimization convergence and computational cost. Consider ablations (no Dale’s law, alternative regularizers such as L1) to support claims about sparsity and balance emerging from stability optimization rather than from regularization alone.
- Synapse clipping analysis: Changing the success threshold after clipping complicates interpretation. Provide a consistent metric or justify the new threshold, and report how attractor locations shift.
- Link to spiking: The argument that a rectified power-law gain supports translation to spiking is plausible; a demonstration with matched single-neuron transfer functions would strengthen the claim.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well organized. Figures are informative and aligned with the text. Suggestions:
- Specify all hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε scaling) and provide rationale/validation.
- Include algorithmic details (pseudo-code for the optimization loop, stopping criteria).
- Report eigenvalue spectra (or SSA/SA histograms) at converged attractors.
- Standardize notation between potentials v and rates r; ensure units are explicit.
- Some references are incomplete; please correct bibliographic details.

Originality
Directly optimizing E–I recurrent networks to make arbitrary analog patterns locally asymptotically stable while enforcing Dale’s law is novel. The use of SSA for robust stability in this context is an elegant and original contribution that goes beyond traditional Hebbian/Hopfield embeddings and LIF models relying on saturation or global inhibitory calibration. The synthesis of graded memories, balanced dynamics, and biologically constrained connectivity is a notable step forward.

Significance
If scalable, the approach could provide a practical recipe for constructing biologically plausible attractor networks with analog memory states and inspire both theoretical neuroscience and ML work on stability-constrained recurrent networks. The empirical link to variability quenching connects the model to widely observed cortical phenomena. Current limitations are the lack of scaling theory, reliance on nonlocal optimization, and absence of a spiking implementation; nonetheless, the work is a compelling proof of principle.

Pros
- Conceptually clean formulation of storage as fixed-point + stability constraints.
- Enforces Dale’s law and yields balanced E–I dynamics without firing-rate saturation.
- Robust recall from noisy cues and under ongoing noise; variability reduction reproduced.
- Emergent sparse, heavy-tailed weight distributions; few strong synapses suffice.
- Clear writing and thorough experiments for a proof-of-concept paper.

Cons
- Per-memory optimization of inhibitory steady states raises biological plausibility questions.
- Fixed-point residuals remain non-zero; basins and exact stability not fully quantified.
- Limited capacity/scaling analysis; no theoretical bounds.
- Results sensitive to multiple heuristics (SSA ε, thresholds) without systematic study.
- No spiking demonstration or biologically local learning rule.

Questions for the authors
- How do capacity and basin size scale with N and with the statistics/correlation structure of patterns?
- Can you show eigenvalue spectra and distances from targets to the actual fixed points reached after training?
- During recall, are inhibitory activities entirely determined by dynamics, or is any memory-specific drive required?
- How sensitive are sparsity and E–I balance to ηF and to the choice of L2 vs. L1 regularization?
- Can the method tolerate structured connectivity constraints or sparsity masks imposed a priori?

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances constructive methods for analog attractors in biologically constrained E–I networks using robust control tools. With additional analysis on scaling, basins, and the inhibitory role, it would be even more impactful. I lean toward acceptance.
