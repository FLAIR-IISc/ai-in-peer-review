Summary: The author presents a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network, subject to biologically valid constraints. Previous models of auto-associative memory have failed to incorporate several key characteristics of real biological networks, namely, adhering to Dale's law that neurons have a strict excitatory or inhibitory effect on their inputs, and the observation that networks can encode memories without relying on units that only respond to their saturation rate or respond in a binary manner. The author demonstrates the effectiveness of their training process with simulations, noting that trained fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics (i.e., they are close to fixed points, but not exactly there) and are stable, as desired. The author notes two characteristics of their trained network that correspond to experimentally observed characteristics of cortical networks, namely, a distribution of synaptic weights centered on zero with long tails, and that an average network unit received approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic inputs. The author identifies these characteristics as crucial components for the network's success in performing strong auto-associative memory when the network is perturbed by a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clearly written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The author provides a well-reviewed history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. Both the network architecture and the training process are presented in a clear manner, and the figures are well-chosen to quickly illustrate the main results of the submission. The author demonstrates the network's performance limitations when noise is included, highlighting the robust solution found by the training process.

A recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training process and the stated objectives. As the author mentions that other methods have been used in the past to implement Dale's law or to obtain graded network memories, implementing the latter seems more difficult. The chosen training process achieves the desired goal of creating an auto-associative network with non-saturated units, but the text does not indicate anything to suggest that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly suitable for this task. Perhaps no other network training process would achieve the same results? Can anything be said about why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?
