This paper revisits the classical problem of creating associative memory networks, introducing a novel control-theoretic framework that successfully integrates several key biological constraints. Previous models have often addressed these constraints—such as Dale's law (the separation of excitatory and inhibitory neurons), the storage of graded (analog) memory patterns, and the maintenance of low firing rates—in isolation. The main contribution here is a unified approach that handles these aspects simultaneously.

The authors formulate memory storage as an optimization problem. They construct a cost function that is minimized to ensure that a desired set of analog patterns become stable fixed points of the network dynamics. This objective function elegantly combines three components: a term that minimizes the rate of change of neural activity at the target memory states, a stability term based on the recently developed Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) that ensures these states are attractors, and a regularization term on the synaptic weights. Dale's law is enforced by design in the parameterization of the weight matrix. An innovative aspect of the method is that the activity of inhibitory neurons is not prescribed but treated as a set of auxiliary variables to be optimized, allowing them to flexibly support the stability of the user-defined excitatory memory patterns.

Numerical simulations demonstrate that the resulting networks function as robust autoassociative memories, capable of successful recall from noisy cues. The optimized networks operate in a dynamically balanced regime, where strong excitatory and inhibitory currents largely cancel, and they naturally maintain sparse connectivity distributions. Furthermore, the model replicates the widely observed cortical phenomenon of stimulus onset quenching trial-to-trial variability, contextualizing this finding within an attractor network framework for analog memories.

**Strengths:**
*   The application of modern control theory, specifically the SSA, to the design of biologically realistic neural networks is a novel and powerful approach.
*   The work provides a proof of concept for a memory network that simultaneously satisfies a list of important physiological constraints (Dale's law, E/I balance, analog patterns, low firing rates) that are often addressed separately.
*   The treatment of inhibitory neuron activity as an optimizable resource for stabilizing excitatory memories is a particularly insightful and compelling idea.
*   The manuscript is clearly written, well-organized, and the results convincingly support the claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The optimization procedure results in states that are "slow points" rather than exact fixed points. It would be beneficial for the authors to quantify the magnitude of this residual drift and discuss its potential impact on the long-term stability of the memories.
*   The paper does not explore the storage capacity of the network. Even a preliminary analysis or discussion of how capacity might scale with network parameters would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution from a computational perspective.
*   The motivation for using a log-normal distribution for the memory patterns could be stated more explicitly, perhaps by further referencing the in-vivo literature mentioned.
*   The sensitivity of the results to the choice of the gain function parameter γ in Eq. (1) is not addressed. A brief comment on this would be helpful.
*   The explanation for the higher recall performance for the baseline state (lines 254-256) could be clarified. Is this primarily a statistical effect (cues are more likely to be closer to the mean of the distribution) or is it also influenced by the network's initialization in a stable baseline state?
*   It would be interesting if the authors could speculate on the computational role of Dale's law in this context. Does enforcing it provide any functional advantages, or is it purely a biological constraint that the optimization must work around?

In summary, this paper presents an important and elegant contribution to the field of theoretical neuroscience. It provides a principled method for constructing biologically plausible associative memory networks and offers new insights into how cortical circuits might achieve stable memory representations.
