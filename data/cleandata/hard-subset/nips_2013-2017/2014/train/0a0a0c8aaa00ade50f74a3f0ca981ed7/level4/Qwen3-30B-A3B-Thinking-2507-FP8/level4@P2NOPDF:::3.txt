

This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a novel, biologically grounded approach that resolves several longstanding limitations in prior work. The authors identify three key shortcomings: previous models often ignored biological constraints (e.g., Dale's law), employed binary representations in rate-based frameworks, and used non-sparse memory codes. While individual aspects of these issues have been addressed separately, the authors develop a unified strategy using recent control-theoretic methods based on spectral bounding. Their core innovation is a cost function balancing three terms optimized via gradient descent: (1) ensuring fixed-point stability for target memories, (2) enhancing fixed-point stability, and (3) promoting small synaptic weights to avoid saturation. Critically, they enforce Dale's law directly through weight parameterization, naturally balancing excitation and inhibition without explicit constraints. Notably, the model uses only excitatory neurons for memory storage (consistent with biological reality where excitatory neurons typically project to downstream regions) and treats inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. The optimization process inherently yields non-saturating single-neuron firing rates, and numerical simulations demonstrate robust noise tolerance. The authors further explain experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset—attributed to convergence toward the nearest attractor—though this may reflect a general property of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity.  

The work effectively bridges control theory and neuroscience, offering a biologically plausible mechanism for associative memory. While the authors do not yet propose a biologically feasible learning rule (though they reference recent advances that may enable this), their framework provides significant clarity on how such networks could operate. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as tunable resources is particularly insightful and beneficial for achieving balanced dynamics. Overall, the paper is exceptionally clear, concise, and makes a substantial contribution to neural computation.  

**Minor concerns requiring clarification:**  
1.  The choice of γ=0.04 in Eq. (3) lacks sensitivity analysis. The authors should discuss how results depend on this parameter (though small sensitivity is anticipated).  
2.  The log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) requires justification—why is this distribution biologically or computationally motivated?  
3.  The cost function (Eq. 4) may yield solutions deviating from exact fixed points. Quantify the severity of this deviation and discuss its implications for memory fidelity.  
4.  Lines 254–256 are unclear and need elaboration.  
5.  Capacity (number of storable memories) is unaddressed. A preliminary statement on this critical computational aspect would strengthen the work.  
6.  The computational benefits of Dale's law warrant discussion. While it may stem from evolutionary/biophysical constraints, the authors should explore whether it enhances computational power under specific conditions.  

This is a strong, well-executed paper that advances the field by resolving multiple prior limitations through a novel control-theoretic lens. The biological plausibility, technical rigor, and clear exposition make it a valuable contribution. Addressing the minor points above would further solidify its impact.
