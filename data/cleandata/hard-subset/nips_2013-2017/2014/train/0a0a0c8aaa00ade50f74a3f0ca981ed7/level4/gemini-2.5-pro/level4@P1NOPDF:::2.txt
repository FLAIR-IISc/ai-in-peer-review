In this paper, the authors present a rate-based neuronal network designed to store multiple stable activity patterns as memories. The work aims to overcome significant shortcomings of previous attractor models, namely the violation of Dale's law and the saturation of neuronal activity in memory states. The network is trained via gradient descent on a cost function that is carefully constructed to enforce stability by minimizing activity changes within the desired attractors, regularize the synaptic connections by minimizing the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, and explicitly maximize the overall stability of the attractors. The main results of this approach are a network that exhibits a synaptic weight distribution consistent with experimental findings and maintains a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition in its attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written and clear. The model, training procedure, and results are all presented in an easy-to-understand manner. The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating how incorporating key biological constraints into the model's design—specifically, by developing graded, non-saturated attractors and plausible synaptic properties—can give rise to other emergent, realistic network features, such as a balance between excitation and inhibition. This combination of imposed biological constraints and emergent network properties makes this paper an interesting and valuable contribution to the study of attractor neural networks.
