- Proposal: A rate-based autoassociative memory model enforcing biological constraints (Dale’s law, graded non-saturating responses, low firing rates).  
- Method: Memory storage via gradient-based optimization that makes target patterns fixed points and enforces stability by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian.  
- Implementation detail: SSA-based stability is made compatible with Dale’s law through a sign-constrained reparameterization of synaptic weights.  
- Empirical finding: Learned networks produce “slow points” near target patterns and nearby genuinely stable attractors.  
- Physiological consistency: Trained weight distributions show a strong mode near zero with heavy tails, and neurons exhibit tight excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Robustness: The model supports robust autoassociative recall from corrupted cues and resilience to ongoing noise.  
- Strength: The paper is well-written, original, and clearly situated within prior work on associative memory.  
- Strength: Circuit specification, training framework, and figures are presented transparently and effectively.  
- Strength: The characterization of recall under noisy conditions is particularly informative about limitations and robustness.  
- Suggestion: Better justify why this specific optimization framework (SSA-based) was chosen over alternative stability surrogates, constraints, or regularizers.  
- Suggestion: Provide intuition or theoretical arguments for why the SSA objective is especially effective at producing graded, non-saturating attractors.  
- Suggestion: (Beyond scope) Compare storage capacity scaling with prior associative-memory models to benchmark retrievable memory counts under the current constraints.
