- Introduces an auto-associative memory model in a rate-based neural network that respects biological constraints.  
- Enforces Dale’s Law and enables graded, non-saturating memory representations rather than binary/saturated activity.  
- Uses gradient descent learning constrained by a stability-guaranteeing technique for fixed points (linearized dynamics).  
- Simulations show stable memory states with slow dynamics that approach—but do not perfectly reach—static fixed points.  
- Trained networks display a synaptic weight distribution with a zero-centered peak and heavy tails, matching experimental observations.  
- Networks exhibit excitatory–inhibitory input balance to the average neuron, aligning with cortical circuit data.  
- These emergent features are identified as crucial for robust memory recall under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, novel, and provides an excellent overview of prior work on training auto-associative networks.  
- Model architecture, learning algorithm, and figures are well described and effectively convey the results.  
- Inclusion of network noise experiments demonstrates the robustness of the trained solution.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on why the chosen training procedure is especially suited to prevent saturated responses versus alternatives.  
- Reviewer suggests adding a comparison of memory capacity (patterns stored vs network size) to classical auto-associative models.
