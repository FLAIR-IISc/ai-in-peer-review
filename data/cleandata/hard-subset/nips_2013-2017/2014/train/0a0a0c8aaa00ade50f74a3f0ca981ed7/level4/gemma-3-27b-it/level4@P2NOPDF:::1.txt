This is a clearly written and novel submission that will likely be of significant interest to the NIPS community. The authors provide a comprehensive review of previous work on training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, effectively outlining the contributions and limitations of existing approaches. The network architecture and training procedure are both presented in a clear and accessible manner, and the figures effectively illustrate the key findings of the work. The authors appropriately acknowledge and demonstrate the network’s performance limitations under noisy conditions, which underscores the robustness of the learned solution.

A primary recommendation for improvement would be to further clarify the rationale behind the chosen training procedure in relation to the stated objectives. While the authors successfully employ the method to enforce Dale’s Law and achieve graded, non-saturating network responses – a historically challenging goal – the text does not explain *why* this specific procedure was selected. Previous methods have addressed these constraints, and a discussion of why this approach was considered particularly well-suited to building an auto-associative network with these properties would strengthen the paper. Would alternative training procedures yield comparable results? What aspects of this method contribute to its effectiveness in achieving these goals?

Additionally, while potentially beyond the scope of this paper, a comparison of the network’s capacity – quantified as the number of memories stored as a function of network size – against that of previous models would be highly valuable to the community, given the extensive history of research in this area. 

In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that incorporates several biologically plausible constraints, notably enabling network units to encode memories without relying on saturating or binary responses. This is achieved by applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points within a dynamical system.
