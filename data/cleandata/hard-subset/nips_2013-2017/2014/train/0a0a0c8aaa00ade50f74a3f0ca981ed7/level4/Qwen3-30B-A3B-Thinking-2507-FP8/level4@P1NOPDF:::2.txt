

This manuscript presents a rate-based neuronal network trained to maintain multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while addressing key limitations of prior attractor models, specifically violations of Dale's law and saturation of activity in memory states. The authors employ gradient descent on a cost function that minimizes activity fluctuations within target attractors, penalizes large weight magnitudes via the Frobenius norm, and maximizes attractor stability. The results demonstrate a weight distribution aligning with experimental observations and a balanced excitation-inhibition profile within attractor states.  

The manuscript is well-written and clearly structured. The model design, training methodology, and results are presented with sufficient detail for easy comprehension. The integration of biological constraints—such as Dale's law compliance and non-saturating activity—into the network architecture is particularly compelling. These constraints naturally yield emergent properties: the network achieves balanced excitation and inhibition at the network level, and the synaptic weights exhibit distributions consistent with empirical neurophysiological data. This work provides a meaningful advancement in understanding how biological plausibility shapes the dynamics of attractor neural networks.  

The authors successfully bridge theoretical modeling with experimental observations, offering a biologically constrained framework that avoids common pitfalls of simpler attractor models. The training procedure effectively enforces the desired properties without compromising the network’s ability to store multiple distinct patterns. The clarity of the exposition, combined with the model’s alignment with known neural principles, makes this a strong contribution to the field. No major technical flaws were identified in the methodology or results. The paper is suitable for publication in NIPS.
