

This paper addresses a longstanding challenge in computational neuroscience: constructing biologically plausible attractor networks that store and retrieve analog memory patterns while respecting key physiological constraints. The authors build upon prior NIPS work (e.g., Hopfield networks [1], Roudi et al. [12] on balanced networks) by introducing a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes network parameters to satisfy Dale’s law, support graded non-saturating activity, and achieve stable attractor dynamics without relying on binary encoding or artificial saturation mechanisms. Their method minimizes the time derivative of neural activity at target patterns (ensuring fixed points) while using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization for robust stability—a significant improvement over prior approaches that either violated Dale’s law [1, 2] or required saturating neuron models [7, 8].

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical execution is rigorous. The SSA-based stability optimization resolves a critical gap in prior work (where Jacobian-based stability was intractable), and the resulting networks demonstrate robust memory recall under noise (Fig. 3) and match experimental firing rate distributions (Fig. 1c). The authors explicitly address limitations (e.g., sparse connectivity arises from stability optimization, not L2 regularization; Fig. 4), showcasing methodological honesty.  
*Clarity:* The model is well-motivated and clearly presented, with intuitive figures (e.g., Fig. 1, 5) and a transparent optimization procedure. The exposition enables reproducibility, satisfying the criterion that "a superbly written paper provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce its results."  
*Originality:* This work uniquely integrates Dale’s law, graded analog patterns, and dynamic E/I balance into a unified framework—addressing multiple constraints simultaneously where prior NIPS contributions either focused on binary patterns [3, 4] or ignored Dale’s law [5, 6]. The explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability (Fig. 6b) is a novel biological insight not previously demonstrated for analog memories.  
*Significance:* The approach advances the neural substrate of memory by resolving a persistent theoretical gap. The model’s robustness to noise and biological plausibility (e.g., sparse connectivity matching Song et al. [19], balanced E/I [22]) make it highly relevant for future studies of cortical memory circuits, with potential applications in neural prosthetics and understanding cognitive disorders.

**Weaknesses:**  
While the rate-based model is well-justified (gain function matches physiological data [24–26]), the paper does not explore how the optimized connectivity would transfer to spiking networks with conductance-based synapses—a limitation acknowledged in the discussion but not addressed experimentally. Additionally, the storage capacity (0.2 for 30 patterns) is modest compared to theoretical limits for analog patterns [3, 4], though the authors appropriately emphasize practical realizability over theoretical maximums.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:* Unifies multiple physiological constraints (Dale’s law, graded activity, E/I balance) in a single framework; demonstrates robust memory recall and explains a key neural phenomenon (reduced variability); methodologically rigorous with clear optimization procedure.  
*Con:* Limited exploration of spiking network applicability and moderate storage capacity (though justified by biological constraints).  

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a significant contribution to understanding cortical memory mechanisms. It is clearly written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful gap in the field. I recommend acceptance.
