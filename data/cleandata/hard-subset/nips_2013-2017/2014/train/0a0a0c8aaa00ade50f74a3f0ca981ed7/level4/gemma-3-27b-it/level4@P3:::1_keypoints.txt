- Clearly written, novel submission likely to interest the community.  
- Compelling historical review highlighting contributions and shortcomings of prior auto-associative training work.  
- Network architecture, training procedure, and figures are clearly presented and illustrative.  
- Authors acknowledge noise-related performance limits and emphasize robustness achieved via training.  
- Core contribution: formulates memory storage as a control problem and applies robust control theory (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA) to optimize parameters under physiological constraints.  
- Method demonstrably overcomes prior limitations, yielding stable, graded recall while respecting Dale’s Law.  
- Resulting networks show balanced excitation/inhibition and sparse connectivity, enhancing biological plausibility.  
- Major critique: insufficient justification for choosing the SSA-based optimization over alternatives.  
- Recommend explaining why SSA specifically addresses stability optimization under physiological constraints and how it compares to other methods.  
- Suggest clarifying why gradient-based methods struggle with directly optimizing the spectral abscissa and how SSA circumvents these issues.  
- Missing comparison of network capacity (number of memories vs. units) to prior models; even brief contextualization would help.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty (control-theoretic approach), technical soundness, biological plausibility, clarity, and significance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited justification of the SSA method and lack of capacity comparison.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address method justification and capacity context.
