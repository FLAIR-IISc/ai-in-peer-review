This is a clearly-written, novel submission that will be of great interest to the community. The authors provide a thorough review of the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are both presented in a clear and concise manner, and the figures are well-chosen to effectively illustrate the main results of the submission.

The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network subject to several biologically plausible constraints. Unlike previous models, this network adheres to Dale's Law, which states that neurons have a strictly excitatory or inhibitory effect on their projections. Furthermore, the network encodes memories without relying on units that simply respond at their saturation rate or respond in a binary manner. Memories are encoded in the network via synaptic modifications based on a gradient descent procedure, which is constrained using a recently published method for ensuring that the linearization of the dynamics around a dynamical system's fixed point is stable.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure through simulations, noting that the trained fixed points exhibit slow network dynamics and are stable, as desired. The trained network also exhibits two features that are in line with experimentally observed features of cortical networks: a distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails, and the fact that an average network unit receives approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input. The authors identify these features as key components to the network's success in performing robust auto-associative memory when the network is perturbed from a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

One recommendation for improvement would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. While the authors note that other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, it is not clear why this particular method was chosen. The text does not indicate whether the authors believed this method was particularly well-suited to this task, or whether other network training procedures could achieve the same result. Providing additional insight into the reasoning behind the chosen training procedure would strengthen the submission.

Additionally, while it may be beyond the scope of this paper, a comparison of how the trained network performs against previous models in terms of network capacity (i.e., the number of memories as a function of network units) would be of great interest to the community. This comparison would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

In summary, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the condition that network units are capable of encoding memories without saturating or binary responses. By applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors demonstrate a robust solution for training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks.
