The paper presents a fresh perspective on the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering significant new insights into this classic issue. The authors astutely observe that previous work in the field has often neglected crucial biological aspects, such as Dale's law, and has relied on binary memory representations in rate-based models, as well as non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a novel strategy that enables them to tackle all of these challenges simultaneously.

The authors employ a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that address these shortcomings. This approach involves proposing a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term to the stability of the fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights, which prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors are able to create a network where excitations and inhibition are naturally balanced.

Interestingly, the authors do not explicitly enforce saturation in single-neuron firing, yet this emerges naturally through the solution of the optimization problem. Notably, the authors only utilize the excitatory neurons to implement the memory, which is consistent with the fact that it is usually the excitatory neurons that send output to other regions. They view the inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized over. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's robustness to noise.

In addition to demonstrating the good associative memory properties of the network, the authors are also able to explain experimental findings showing that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset. This is attributed to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

The paper is well-written and clear, tackling a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory may function. Although the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point, they hint at some recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is an interesting and beneficial aspect of the paper.

A few minor issues are worth noting. The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3), and it would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice. A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed, but it would be helpful to provide motivation for this choice. The cost function may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories, and it would be useful to quantify and discuss the severity of this violation. The statements on lines 254-256 could be clarified for better understanding. The authors do not mention capacity issues, and it would be nice to make some preliminary statement about this important computational aspect. Finally, it would be interesting if the authors could discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether this law can be shown to enhance computational power under specific constraints.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation. The authors' approach to tackling the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks is novel and insightful, and their results have significant implications for our understanding of biologically plausible associative memory.
