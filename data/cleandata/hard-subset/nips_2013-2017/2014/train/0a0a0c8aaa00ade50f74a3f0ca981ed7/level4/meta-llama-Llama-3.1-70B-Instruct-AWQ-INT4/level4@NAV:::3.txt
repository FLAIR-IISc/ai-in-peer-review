The paper revisits the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective that provides significant new insights into this classic issue. The authors note that previous work in the field has often overlooked crucial biological aspects, such as Dale's law, and has relied on binary memory representations in rate-based models, as well as non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been individually addressed in other studies, the authors develop a comprehensive strategy that tackles all of these concerns simultaneously. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that address these issues. This approach involves a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired outcome through gradient descent. The first term contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of these fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights, preventing saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the aforementioned shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network with naturally balanced excitations and inhibitions. Notably, while saturation in single neuron firing is not explicitly enforced, it emerges naturally from the solution of the optimization problem. Importantly, the authors utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory, consistent with the fact that these neurons typically send output to other regions, and view inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's noise robustness. Furthermore, they explain experimental findings showing reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, attributing this to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this paper is clearly and effectively written. It tackles a classic problem in neural computation using innovative control-theoretic tools and demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory may function. Although the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point, they hint at recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is particularly noteworthy and beneficial. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues require attention:
The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this choice would be beneficial, although the impact is likely to be small.
A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. A clear motivation for this choice would be helpful.
The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. Quantifying and discussing the severity of this violation is necessary.
The statements on lines 254-256 require clarification.
The authors do not address capacity issues. A preliminary statement on this crucial computational aspect would be appreciated.
A discussion on the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether it enhances computational power under specific constraints, would be valuable. While this may not be the case, and may be a result of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints, relating to this would be beneficial.

This paper is a clear and well-written contribution to neural computation, tackling a classic problem using novel control-theoretic tools and demonstrating a biologically plausible associative memory.
