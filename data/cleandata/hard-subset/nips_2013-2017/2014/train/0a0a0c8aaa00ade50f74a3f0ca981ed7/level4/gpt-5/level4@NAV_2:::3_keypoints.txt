- Revisits associative memory with new insights, addressing longstanding biological and modeling limitations.  
- Highlights that prior work often violated Dale’s law, used binary codes in rate models, or produced saturated recall states.  
- Proposes a unified strategy that simultaneously enforces biological constraints rather than addressing them separately.  
- Uses control-theoretic spectral bounding to construct networks that satisfy biological constraints.  
- Introduces a three-term cost optimized by gradient methods: fixed-point enforcement, dynamical stability, and weight regularization.  
- Explicitly parameterizes synapses to satisfy Dale’s principle, yielding naturally balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Optimization yields stable, low-rate operating regimes even without saturating single-neuron nonlinearities.  
- Implements memory content in excitatory populations while treating inhibitory units as auxiliary, optimizable resources.  
- Validates approach with simulations showing robust autoassociative recall under corruption and ongoing noise.  
- Model reproduces reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via attractor convergence from a spontaneous baseline.  
- Paper is clear, well-organized, and a concise, meaningful contribution using modern control-theoretic tools.  
- Does not derive a local biologically plausible learning rule, but points to recent proposals that could bridge this gap.  
- Treating inhibitory neurons as allocatable resources is an insightful and effective design choice.
