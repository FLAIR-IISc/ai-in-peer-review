Summary: The authors present a rate-based model of auto-associative memory that incorporates several biologically plausible constraints. Unlike many prior models, it enforces Dale’s law—neurons are strictly excitatory or inhibitory—and allows memories to be stored and recalled with graded, non-saturating responses rather than binary activity. Memories are encoded via gradient descent on synaptic weights, with constraints derived from a recent method that enforces stability of the linearization around fixed points. Simulations show that the learned attractors are stable and exhibit slow dynamics near the target states (i.e., they are close to, but not exactly, fixed points). The trained networks also display properties consistent with cortical data: a synaptic weight distribution centered near zero with heavy tails, and approximate balance between excitatory and inhibitory inputs to each unit. These features appear crucial for robust recall under perturbations and in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clear, original submission that will interest the community. The authors provide a helpful overview of prior auto-associative models, emphasizing key advances and limitations. The network architecture and training procedure are described clearly, and the figures effectively communicate the main findings. The analysis of network performance under noise is particularly useful, revealing both limitations and the robustness achieved by the training scheme.

One suggestion is to better articulate the rationale for the chosen training method relative to the stated goals. While prior work has addressed Dale’s law and, more challengingly, graded memory representations, it is not clear why this particular approach was selected or why it is especially well-suited to producing non-saturating, graded attractors. Could alternative training frameworks achieve similar outcomes under the same constraints? Can the authors offer mechanistic insight into why this method yields graded memory states so reliably?

A second recommendation—likely beyond the scope of the current paper but valuable to the community—is to compare capacity with prior models (e.g., number of storable memories as a function of network size), even in a limited regime or via scaling arguments. Such a comparison would contextualize the empirical robustness and biological plausibility against classical trade-offs in associative memory models.

In brief, by applying a recent technique that enforces stability of fixed points, the authors develop an auto-associative memory model that satisfies key neurobiological constraints, notably Dale’s law and graded, non-saturating unit responses.
