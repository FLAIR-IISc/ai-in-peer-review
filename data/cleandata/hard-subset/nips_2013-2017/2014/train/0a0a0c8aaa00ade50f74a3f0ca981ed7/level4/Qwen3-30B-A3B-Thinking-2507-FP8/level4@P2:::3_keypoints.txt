- Problem: Builds biologically plausible associative memory networks satisfying multiple cortical constraints simultaneously (Dale’s law, graded representations, low firing rates, stable dynamics).

- Novel method: Uses a control-theoretic gradient optimization with the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a stability metric.

- Memory formalization: Frames storage as requiring fixed points (dv/dt=0) and local stability, enforced via SSA minimization.

- Dale’s law enforcement: Imposes excitatory/inhibitory separation through synaptic weight reparameterization.

- Inhibitory units: Treats inhibitory neurons as auxiliary optimization variables rather than fixed components.

- Balanced regime: Approach naturally produces dynamic E/I balance yielding low, non-saturating firing rates.

- Capacity/result: Networks can store multiple analog patterns (capacity ~0.2 for N=150).

- Robustness: Demonstrates robust memory recall under significant noise in initial cues.

- Variability explanation: Recapitulates reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset without extra assumptions.

- Sparsity: Learned synaptic weights become sparse, aligning with cortical observations.

- Quality and clarity: Strong theoretical grounding, well-motivated optimization, thorough simulations, and clear presentation/figures.

- Originality and significance: Synthesizes disparate constraints, introduces a novel control-theoretic framework, with implications for neuroscience and biologically plausible ANNs.

- Minor issues to address: Sensitivity analysis for γ=0.04; motivation for log-normal pattern generation; quantify how closely fixed points are achieved; fuller discussion of memory capacity; clarify whether Dale’s law aids computation versus being a biological constraint.

- Overall judgment: High-quality, technically sound, significant contribution suitable for NIPS.
