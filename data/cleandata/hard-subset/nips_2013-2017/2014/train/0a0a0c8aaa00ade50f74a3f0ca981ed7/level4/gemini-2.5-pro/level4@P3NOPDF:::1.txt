**Summary**

Applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several key constraints of biological neural networks. This paper presents a novel model of a rate-based network that addresses significant shortcomings of previous models by successfully incorporating Dale's Law (requiring neurons to be strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and, notably, enabling the network to encode memories through graded, non-saturating neural activity rather than binary or saturated states.

Memories are encoded as stable fixed points in the network's dynamics. The authors achieve this by modifying synaptic weights using a gradient descent procedure, which is crucially constrained by a recently developed method that guarantees the stability of the linearized dynamics around each target fixed point. Simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of this training procedure, resulting in stable memories that exhibit slow dynamics. The trained networks also develop two emergent properties consistent with experimental observations in cortical circuits: a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and a balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs to individual neurons. The authors convincingly argue that these features are instrumental to the model's robust performance, particularly its ability to retrieve memories accurately even when perturbed from a fixed point or in the presence of stochastic noise.

**Review**

Overall, this is a well-written and novel submission that makes a significant contribution to the field and will be of high interest to the computational neuroscience community. The paper's strengths are numerous. The authors provide an excellent review of the literature on auto-associative networks, clearly situating their work by highlighting the contributions and limitations of previous models. The network architecture and training algorithm are explained with exceptional clarity, and the accompanying figures are well-chosen and effectively illustrate the paper's main results. A particular strength of the work is the demonstration of the network's robustness. By testing its performance under noisy conditions, the authors effectively underscore the resilience of the solution found by their training procedure.

While the results are compelling, the paper could be strengthened by clarifying the explicit relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. The authors successfully achieve the goal of creating an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, a historically challenging task. However, the text does not fully explain *why* the chosen stability-constrained gradient descent method is particularly well-suited for achieving this specific outcome. The review would benefit from a discussion on this point. For instance, what properties of this method prevent the network from defaulting to binary or saturated solutions, which other approaches might favor? Elucidating this connection would provide deeper insight into the underlying mechanism and bolster the authors' claims about the method's efficacy.

Additionally, while perhaps beyond the scope of the current work, a brief discussion on the network's memory capacity would be a valuable addition. Given the rich history of research in this area, a comparison, even a preliminary one, of how this model's capacity (e.g., number of stored memories as a function of network size) relates to classic models would be of great interest to the community and could be suggested as a direction for future work.

These points notwithstanding, this is a strong paper that presents an elegant and biologically plausible model for auto-associative memory.
