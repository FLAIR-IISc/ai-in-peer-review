- Presents a novel rate-based excitatory–inhibitory network that stores and retrieves analog (graded) memories while respecting physiological constraints like Dale’s law.
- Uses a control-theoretic optimization of synaptic weights and inhibitory firing rates to make stored patterns stable fixed points.
- Optimization minimizes a cost combining attractor stability, spectral-abscissa smoothness, and weight regularization.
- Demonstrates storage and recall of non-saturated graded activity patterns with balanced excitation and inhibition.
- Trained networks show sparse weight distributions similar to experimental observations.
- Network is robust to noise and can recall patterns from corrupted initial cues.
- Overcomes key limitations of past attractor models, notably violation of Dale’s law and activity saturation.
- Control-theoretic training enabling graded attractors is a methodological novelty.
- Results have implications for artificial networks and studying neural substrates of memory.
- Manuscript is well-written, with a clear model description, training procedure, and well-labeled figures.
- Main weakness: rate-based formulation may not directly translate to spiking neural networks.
- Optimization procedure lacks a clear explanation for how it avoids local minima.
- Manuscript needs a more detailed discussion linking the model to experimental findings.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, biological constraints yield realistic features, and potential impact on memory research.
- Arguments for rejection: limited applicability to spiking circuits, unclear optimization robustness, and insufficient experimental contextualization.
