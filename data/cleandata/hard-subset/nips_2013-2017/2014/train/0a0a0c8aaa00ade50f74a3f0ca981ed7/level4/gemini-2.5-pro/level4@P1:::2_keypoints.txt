- Proposes a novel framework to construct biologically plausible recurrent networks that store and recall analog (graded) memory patterns.  
- Addresses key limitations of classic attractor models (violations of Dale’s law, reliance on binary/saturating activity).  
- Formulates memory storage as a control-theoretic optimization problem.  
- Uses a distinct excitatory–inhibitory rate-based network trained by gradient descent to embed desired patterns as stable fixed points.  
- Optimization objective is threefold: enforce fixed-point condition (minimize temporal derivative), ensure local stability (minimize Smoothed Spectral Abscissa of the Jacobian), and regularize synaptic weights.  
- Resulting networks perform autoassociative recall of graded patterns and are robust to noise.  
- Learnt networks operate in a dynamically balanced excitation–inhibition regime, preventing runaway activity without saturating firing rates.  
- Learned synaptic weight distributions are sparse with a long tail of strong weights, matching cortical observations.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, with methods described sufficiently for replication.  
- Principled combination of realistic E–I architecture and robust-control optimization is novel and yields emergent, biologically relevant circuit features rather than imposing them, making a significant theoretical contribution.
