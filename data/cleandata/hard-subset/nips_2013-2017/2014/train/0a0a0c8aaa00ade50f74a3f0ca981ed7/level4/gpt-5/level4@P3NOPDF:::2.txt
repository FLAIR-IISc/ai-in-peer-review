This paper proposes a rate-based recurrent network trained to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories) while addressing two common shortcomings of attractor models: violations of Dale’s law and saturation of neural activities in memory states. The authors optimize a cost function by gradient descent that (i) minimizes activity changes at desired fixed points, (ii) encourages stability of those fixed points, and (iii) regularizes the synaptic matrix via its Frobenius norm. After training, the learned synaptic weights exhibit distributions closer to experimental observations, and network dynamics in memory states display a balance of excitation and inhibition. The manuscript is clearly written; the model, training objective, and results are easy to follow. The combination of explicit biological constraints and the emergent properties of the trained network makes this a noteworthy contribution to the study of attractor neural networks.

Strengths
- Biological plausibility: The model respects Dale’s law by separating excitatory and inhibitory neurons and constraining synaptic signs. Achieving stable, non-saturated attractors under these constraints is nontrivial and valuable.
- Clear objective: The training objective is interpretable and well-motivated. Penalizing deviations from fixed points and directly encouraging stability provides a principled route to constructing attractors without hand-crafting the connectivity.
- Non-saturated graded memories: Demonstrating graded, non-saturated fixed points is important for biological realism, as it avoids ceiling effects in firing rates and permits richer coding.
- Emergent E/I balance and realistic weight statistics: The reported weight distributions and balanced excitation/inhibition in memory states are compelling and align with experimental observations, suggesting the constraints and learning rule capture key features of cortical circuits.
- Exposition: The presentation is concise and accessible, with a good balance between methodological detail and phenomenology.

Weaknesses and questions
- Stability term: The paper should more precisely define how “stability of attractors” is quantified in the loss. Is it based on the Jacobian spectrum (e.g., penalizing positive real parts), a Lyapunov function, or a margin on contraction? Clarifying what is computed, where (at target states only or in their neighborhoods), and how gradients are obtained would strengthen the technical contribution.
- Capacity and scaling: How many memories can be stored as a function of network size and sparsity under Dale-constrained connectivity? Providing scaling plots and a comparison to classical results (e.g., Hopfield-like capacity) would contextualize the approach.
- Basins of attraction: What are the sizes of basins for the stored memories? Quantifying robustness to perturbations of initial conditions and input noise is crucial for assessing usefulness as a memory system.
- Robustness and generalization: How sensitive are results to the choice of nonlinearity, noise, heterogeneity in neuronal gains or thresholds, and random initializations? Do learned attractors persist under synaptic perturbations or pruning? An ablation on the Frobenius norm term (and its strength) would reveal its role in stability, balance, and weight statistics.
- Implementation of Dale’s law: Please detail how sign constraints are enforced during optimization (e.g., reparameterization, projection, or penalization). Are excitatory and inhibitory submatrices treated differently in regularization or initialization?
- Distributional claims: The claim that learned weights match experimental distributions would benefit from quantitative comparisons (e.g., moments, fits to log-normal, Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests) and breakdowns by connection class (E→E, E→I, I→E). It would also help to report sparsity and whether structured motifs emerge.
- Comparison to prior work: A more explicit comparison to classical and modern attractor constructions would help position the contribution—e.g., Hopfield-type networks with E/I segregation, balanced networks, and trained RNNs that impose fixed points or trajectories. How does the proposed method differ in avoiding saturation and respecting Dale’s law? Are there trade-offs in capacity or training complexity?
- Computational cost: What is the complexity of training with the stability term (e.g., Jacobian-related computations) as network size grows? Can the method scale to larger, sparser networks?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Clarify whether “graded and non-saturated attractors in the synaptic weights and inhibitory activities” means graded activity patterns in both excitatory and inhibitory populations with non-saturated rates, while memories are stored in synaptic weights.
- Provide spectra of the Jacobian at learned attractors to visually support stability claims and to illustrate margins.
- Report hyperparameters (learning rates, regularization coefficients), convergence criteria, and initialization schemes to facilitate reproducibility.
- Include ablations: remove the Frobenius norm; relax Dale’s law; vary inhibitory gain. Show impacts on stability, saturation, and E/I balance.
- If possible, demonstrate retrieval from partially cued inputs and quantify performance (e.g., overlap, error) under noise.

Overall assessment
This is a clear and thoughtfully executed study that advances biologically grounded attractor network design by jointly achieving graded, non-saturated fixed points and adherence to Dale’s law, with emergent E/I balance and experimentally plausible weight statistics. While some technical details (especially around the stability term) and empirical characterizations (capacity, basins, robustness, quantitative comparisons) are currently underdeveloped, these seem addressable within the current framework. I view the contribution as solid and of interest to both computational neuroscience and machine learning audiences working on recurrent dynamics. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above clarifications and adding basic capacity/robustness analyses and quantitative comparisons of weight distributions.
