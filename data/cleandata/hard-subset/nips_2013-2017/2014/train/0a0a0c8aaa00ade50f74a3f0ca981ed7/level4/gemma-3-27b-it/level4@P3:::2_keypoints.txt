- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to optimize E/I rate networks to store arbitrary analog patterns as stable fixed points.  
- Uses minimization of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to enforce network stability.  
- Achieves biologically plausible features: Dale’s law compliance, graded activity representations, and stability via E/I balance rather than saturation.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced regime, are robust to noise, and show reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Builds appropriately on prior associative memory and structured/spiking network work and positions the contribution as a general, more plausible framework.  
- Strength: tackles a significant neuroscience problem with a novel, well-motivated method.  
- Strength: clear presentation and informative figures that effectively illustrate methods and results.  
- Strength: compelling empirical findings including robust recall, sparse connectivity emergence, and an “ideal observer” comparison.  
- Weakness: optimization is computationally intensive, limiting scalability to larger networks.  
- Weakness: performance appears sensitive to hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) and lacks thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: reliance on rate-based models limits insights into spiking temporal dynamics and biological validity.  
- Weakness: paper does not evaluate generalization to novel but similar patterns.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-executed approach with compelling results and clear writing; offers promising control-theoretic direction.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, parameter sensitivity, rate-model limitations, and limited generalization weaken practical and biological applicability.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with minor weaknesses; recommendation — Accept.
