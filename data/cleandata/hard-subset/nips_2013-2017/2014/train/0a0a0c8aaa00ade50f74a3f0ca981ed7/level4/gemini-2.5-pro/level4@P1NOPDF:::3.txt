This paper revisits the venerable problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and new insights. The authors correctly observe that much of the previous work in this area has overlooked key biological constraints, such as Dale's law, or has relied on simplified assumptions like binary memory representations in rate-based models and non-sparse activity patterns. While these issues have been addressed individually in prior work, this paper presents a unified strategy that tackles all of them simultaneously.

The core of their contribution is a novel control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, for constructing networks that adhere to these biological constraints. The authors formulate a cost function that balances three key objectives, which is then optimized via gradient descent. The first term in the cost function enforces the fixed-point property of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of these fixed points, and a third regularization term encourages small synaptic weights to prevent saturation. By directly incorporating Dale's principle into the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the aforementioned shortcomings of previous models, resulting in a network with a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. Interestingly, while neuronal saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization process.

A key feature of the model is that only excitatory neurons are used to store and represent memories—consistent with the observation that excitatory neurons typically provide output to other brain regions—while inhibitory neurons are treated as auxiliary variables to be optimized. The authors validate their model through numerical simulations, demonstrating its effectiveness as an associative memory and its robustness to noise. Beyond these core properties, the model also accounts for the experimental finding that trial-to-trial variability in neural activity decreases following stimulus onset. The authors explain this phenomenon as a result of the network dynamics converging to the attractor closest to the initial state, a plausible mechanism for this type of quenching.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation with modern control-theoretic tools. The authors demonstrate how a biologically plausible associative memory can be constructed and analyzed. While a biologically plausible learning rule is not presented, the authors hint at recent work that could provide a path toward one. The treatment of inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is a particularly interesting and effective aspect of their approach. Overall, the paper represents a concise and important contribution to the field of computational neuroscience.

**Minor Issues:**

*   In equation (3), the authors set γ=0.04. It would be helpful to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter choice, although I suspect it is minor.
*   On line 115, a log-normal distribution is proposed for the memory states. The motivation for this specific choice should be clarified.
*   The cost function in equation (4) might allow for solutions that slightly deviate from being exact fixed points. The severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.
*   The statements on lines 254-256 were unclear. Please rephrase for clarity.
*   The paper does not address the storage capacity of the network. It would be beneficial to include some discussion, even if preliminary, on this important computational aspect.
*   It would be interesting if the authors could comment on the potential computational benefits of Dale's law. For instance, could this principle enhance computational power under certain constraints? While it may simply be a result of evolutionary or biophysical constraints, discussing its functional implications would add value to the paper.
