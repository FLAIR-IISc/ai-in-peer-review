This paper presents a method for training a rate-based recurrent neural network to function as an attractor network, storing multiple stable activity patterns as memories. The primary goal is to address significant shortcomings of classical attractor models, namely the violation of Dale's law and the tendency for memory states to exhibit saturated activity. To achieve this, the authors train the network via gradient descent on a carefully constructed cost function. This objective function is designed to: (1) enforce the desired activity patterns as stable fixed points, (2) maximize the stability of these attractors, and (3) regularize the synaptic weights by minimizing their Frobenius norm. The authors demonstrate that this training procedure yields two key emergent properties: a synaptic weight distribution that aligns with experimental neurobiological findings and a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition within the network during memory recall.

The manuscript is well-written and clearly presented. The model, training methodology, and results are easy to follow and understand. A major strength of this paper is its successful integration of biological constraints into a functional learning framework. By imposing constraints for biological plausibility—specifically, enforcing Dale's Law and ensuring neurons exhibit non-saturating, graded responses—the model develops sophisticated network properties that are not explicitly enforced by the learning rule.

The emergence of a balanced excitatory-inhibitory (E/I) state and a realistic synaptic weight distribution from these foundational principles is a compelling and important result. This provides a valuable link between normative learning objectives and observed biological phenomena, making the paper a significant contribution to our understanding of how memory could be implemented in cortical circuits. The work effectively demonstrates how a network, optimized for memory storage under realistic constraints, can self-organize to produce features widely observed in the brain.
