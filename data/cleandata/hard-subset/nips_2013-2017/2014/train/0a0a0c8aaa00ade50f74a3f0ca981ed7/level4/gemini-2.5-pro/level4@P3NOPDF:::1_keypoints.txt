- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces Dale's Law and encodes memories as graded, non-saturating stable fixed points.  
- Uses a stability-constrained gradient descent on synapses to guarantee stability of the linearized dynamics around each target fixed point.  
- Training produces stable memories with slow dynamics that are robust to perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Trained networks exhibit emergent biologically consistent properties: weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and excitatory–inhibitory balance.  
- Provides an excellent literature review situating the work and highlighting limitations of prior models.  
- Network architecture and training algorithm are explained with exceptional clarity and supported by effective figures.  
- Robustness tests under noisy conditions convincingly demonstrate the resilience of the solution.  
- Lacks a clear explanation of why the stability-constrained gradient method specifically prevents the network from converging to binary or saturated solutions.  
- Would benefit from (or should suggest as future work) an analysis/comparison of memory capacity relative to classic models.  
- Overall: a strong, novel, and biologically plausible contribution likely of high interest to computational neuroscience.
