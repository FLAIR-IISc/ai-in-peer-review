In this manuscript, the authors design a rate-based neuronal network trained to store a number of stable activity patterns, representing memories. The goal is to overcome several limitations of previous attractor models, namely the violation of Dale's law and activity saturation in the memory states. For training, the authors employ gradient descent on a cost function that minimizes activity changes in the desired attractors and the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, while maximizing attractor stability. The main results demonstrate a weight distribution consistent with experimental findings and a balance of excitation and inhibition within the attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written and clear. I found the model and training procedure easy to follow, as well as the presentation of the results. The combination of biological constraints incorporated into the model and the resulting network features after training make this paper an interesting contribution to the understanding of attractor neural networks. Specifically, the authors successfully train a rate-based network to support graded, non-saturating attractors by optimizing both synaptic weights and inhibitory activities. Biological plausibility constraints imposed on the synapses and neurons lead to a balance of excitation and inhibition at the network level.

This work builds upon decades of research into attractor networks, starting with the original Hopfield model [1]. While Hopfield’s model provided a foundational framework, it suffered from constraints like symmetrical connectivity (violating Dale’s law) and binary representations [1]. Subsequent work attempted to address these limitations. Treves [2] explored graded neuronal responses in autoassociative memories, and theoretical capacity limits were derived [3, 4], though these proved difficult to realize practically [5, 6]. More recent models incorporated Dale’s law and leaky integrate-and-fire neurons [7], but often relied on saturating dynamics for stability. Others explored dynamic feedback inhibition [8, 9] or spatially structured networks [10, 11], each with its own limitations. Notably, Ref. [12] investigated spike timing-based graded memory networks but lacked inhibitory units.

The authors’ approach distinguishes itself by employing a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters – connectivity and inhibitory neuron activity – to achieve both fixed-point and stability conditions for arbitrary, graded memory patterns. The use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13, 14] for robust stability optimization is a key contribution, offering a differentiable and efficient method compared to directly calculating the spectral abscissa. The resulting network exhibits a balanced state of excitation and inhibition and demonstrates robustness to noise in both the recall cue and ongoing dynamics. The incidental finding that the network also explains the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset observed in cortical circuits [21] further strengthens the biological relevance of the model.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses key limitations of existing models:** The work explicitly tackles the shortcomings of previous attractor network models, including Dale’s law, binary representations, and activity saturation.
*   **Novel control-theoretic framework:** The use of control theory provides a rigorous and principled approach to network design and optimization.
*   **Robust stability optimization:** The application of SSA allows for efficient and reliable optimization of network stability.
*   **Biologically plausible constraints:** The incorporation of Dale’s law and realistic firing rate dynamics enhances the biological relevance of the model.
*   **Emergent balanced state:** The network naturally settles into a balanced state of excitation and inhibition, a hallmark of cortical circuits.
*   **Robustness to noise:** The model demonstrates robustness to noisy recall cues and ongoing network activity.
*   **Explains a behavioral observation:** The model provides a potential explanation for the reduction of trial-to-trial variability observed in cortical circuits.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Rate-based model:** While a rate-based model simplifies analysis, it abstracts away the complexities of spiking dynamics, potentially limiting its applicability to understanding more detailed neural mechanisms.
*   **Parameter tuning:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) appears somewhat heuristic and could benefit from further justification or sensitivity analysis.
*   **Limited scope of memory patterns:** The memory patterns are generated from a log-normal distribution. It would be interesting to explore the performance of the network with more complex or biologically realistic memory distributions.
* **Clipping impact**: While the authors show removing weak weights has little impact, the need to increase the threshold for successful recall suggests a subtle instability introduced.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to building attractor networks that address several key limitations of existing models.
*   The results are significant, demonstrating the feasibility of constructing biologically plausible networks capable of storing and recalling graded memories with robustness to noise.
*   The work advances the state of the art in understanding the neural substrate of memory and provides a potential explanation for observed cortical phenomena.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The rate-based approach may limit the applicability of the model to understanding more detailed neural mechanisms.
*   The heuristic parameter tuning could be improved with a more systematic analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a novel and well-justified approach. While the rate-based model and parameter tuning are areas for potential improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, a discussion of the limitations of the rate-based formulation and a more detailed justification for the parameter choices would be beneficial.
