- Revisits associative memory with a control-theoretic perspective that unifies previously separated biological constraints.  
- Addresses neglected constraints: Dale’s law, non-binary rate representations, and sparse memory encodings simultaneously.  
- Uses spectral-bounding control framework to construct networks satisfying those constraints.  
- Optimizes a three-term cost function (fixed-point enforcement, stability promotion, weight-penalty) via gradient descent.  
- Parameterizes weights to explicitly enforce Dale’s principle, yielding excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Neuron-level saturation emerges from the optimization despite not being explicitly enforced.  
- Memories are stored in the excitatory population while inhibitory neurons are treated as optimizable variables.  
- Validated with numerical simulations showing robustness to noise.  
- Accounts for reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset by showing convergence to the nearest attractor.  
- Paper is clear, well written, and provides a biologically plausible account of associative memory using novel tools.  
- Lacks a biologically plausible learning mechanism, though the authors point to recent proposals that could provide one.  
- The idea of using inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is particularly interesting and effective.  
- Overall judged a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.
