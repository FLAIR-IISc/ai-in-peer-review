- Paper fills a gap by proposing a biologically plausible model for storing/retrieving analog (non-binary) memories in E–I cortical networks.  
- Introduces a control-theoretic framework using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to enforce fixed-point and dynamical stability.  
- Method enforces Dale’s law, achieves graded non-saturating activity, balanced E/I dynamics, and noise robustness.  
- Emergent properties include biologically realistic synaptic sparsity and trial-to-trial variability reduction matching experiments.  
- Technical novelty: SSA optimization circumvents spectral abscissa non-smoothness and enables gradient-based training with Jacobian stability validation.  
- Results show robust recall performance comparable to an ideal observer and balanced dynamics emerge without being imposed.  
- Paper is well-organized and methods/constraints are clearly defined, aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness: lacks quantitative capacity comparison (memories/neuron) to prior graded-memory models like Treves (1990) and Battaglia & Treves (2012).  
- Weakness: target patterns are "slow points" rather than exact fixed points; paper should explain why exact fixed points are unattainable and biological impact.  
- Weakness: sparsity emerges from optimization but mechanism (why stability optimization promotes sparsity) is not fully explained.  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel unified framework with strong validation and biological relevance; minor issues are fixable.  
- Pro: unifies multiple biological constraints and offers testable predictions; Con: limited capacity benchmark and fixed-point precision ambiguity.  
- Revision suggestions: add explicit capacity comparison to prior work, clarify that slow-point drifts are negligible on neural timescales, and justify choice of SSA over alternatives (e.g., Lyapunov-based methods).
