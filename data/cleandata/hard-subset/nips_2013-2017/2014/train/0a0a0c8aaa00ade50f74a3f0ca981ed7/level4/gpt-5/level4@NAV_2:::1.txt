Summary: The authors propose a rate-based autoassociative memory model that enforces a suite of biologically motivated constraints. In contrast to much prior work, the network respects Dale’s law, does not rely on binary pattern representations or saturating unit responses, and supports graded, low-rate activity. Memory storage is achieved by optimizing synaptic weights via gradient-based methods to make designated patterns fixed points and to render those fixed points stable. Stability is enforced by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa of the Jacobian at each target pattern, yielding local asymptotic stability in a manner compatible with Dale’s law through a sign-constrained reparameterization of weights. Simulations demonstrate that the learned solutions are near fixed points (i.e., “slow points” with small residual drift) that are nevertheless accompanied by nearby genuinely stable attractors. The trained networks exhibit two properties consistent with cortical physiology: synaptic weight distributions with a strong mode near zero and heavy tails, and a tight dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition at the single-neuron level. These factors underpin robust autoassociative recall from corrupted cues and robustness under ongoing noise.

Review: This is a well-written and original contribution that should interest the community. The authors situate their work clearly within the literature on autoassociative memory, succinctly outlining key advances and limitations of previous models. Both the circuit specification and the training framework are presented transparently, and the figures effectively convey the main findings. The characterization of recall under noisy conditions is particularly informative, illustrating both the limitations and the robustness conferred by the optimization.

One suggestion is to better articulate why this specific optimization framework was chosen relative to alternatives. Prior approaches have separately addressed Dale’s law and graded memories, with the latter often more challenging. While the current method achieves non-saturating, graded attractors, it is not fully clear whether this choice is uniquely advantageous for that goal. Could alternative training strategies (e.g., different stability surrogates, constraints, or regularizers) obtain comparable results? Can the authors provide intuition or theoretical arguments for why the SSA-based objective is especially effective in this setting?

A second suggestion (likely beyond the present scope) is to include a comparison of capacity with earlier models, i.e., how the number of retrievable memories scales with network size under the current constraints. Given the extensive literature on storage capacity in associative memories, such a benchmark would be of high interest.
