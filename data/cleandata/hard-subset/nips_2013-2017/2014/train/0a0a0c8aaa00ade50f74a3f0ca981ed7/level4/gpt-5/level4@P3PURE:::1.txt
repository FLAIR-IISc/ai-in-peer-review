Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network subject to a set of biologically plausible constraints. Prior models often failed to incorporate key properties of real biological networks, notably adherence to Dale’s Law (neurons are strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and the ability to encode memories without relying on units that saturate or behave in a binary manner. Memories are encoded via synaptic modifications using gradient descent, with constraints imposed by a recently published method that ensures the linearization of the dynamics around a fixed point is stable. Simulations demonstrate that the trained target states exhibit slow network dynamics—close to, but not exactly at, fixed points—and are stable, as desired. The trained networks also display two experimentally observed cortical features: synaptic weight distributions centered near zero with heavy tails, and approximate balance between excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input to each unit. The authors identify these properties as key to the network’s robust auto-associative performance under perturbations from a fixed point, even in the presence of stochastic input noise.

Review: This is a clearly written, novel submission that will be of broad interest. The paper concisely reviews the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and limitations of prior work. The network architecture and training procedure are well described, and the figures effectively illustrate the core results. The authors also characterize performance limitations when network noise is present, further underscoring the robustness of the learned solution.

One recommendation is to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors note, other methods have been used to enforce Dale’s Law or to obtain graded (non-saturating) memories, with the latter being particularly challenging. The proposed procedure successfully yields an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but the paper does not explain why this method was selected for this goal or why it is especially well suited to it. Could other training procedures achieve the same outcome? Can the authors provide intuition for why this particular approach works so effectively?

Finally—although likely beyond the scope of this paper—given the extensive literature on auto-associative memory, a comparison of network capacity (number of memories as a function of network size) with previous models would be of clear interest to the community.
