- Paper revisits associative memory with a novel, biologically constrained perspective, offering significant new insights.  
- Identifies three commonly neglected biological constraints: Dale’s law, binary memory representations in rate models, and non-sparse memory representations.  
- Develops a unified strategy that addresses all three constraints simultaneously.  
- Uses a control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding to construct compliant networks.  
- Introduces a three-term cost function (fixed-point enforcement, stability, and small weights) optimized by gradient descent.  
- Embeds Dale’s law directly in the weight parameterization, producing natural excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Single-neuron saturation is not enforced but emerges from optimization.  
- Memory implementation uses excitatory neurons while inhibitory neurons serve as optimization variables.  
- Numerical simulations show noise robustness and associative memory behavior.  
- Model explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via convergence to nearest attractor.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and presents a biologically plausible associative memory mechanism of significance.  
- Does not yet provide a biologically plausible learning rule, though relevant recent work is cited.  
- Insightful use of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources.  
- Minor concerns: sensitivity to chosen γ value (Eq. 3) needs analysis.  
- Minor concerns: justification required for assuming log-normal distribution of memory states.  
- Minor concerns: quantify how often/strongly the cost yields solutions that violate exact fixed-point requirements.  
- Minor concerns: clarify claims made in lines 254–256.  
- Minor concerns: address capacity limitations (even preliminarily) for computational relevance.  
- Minor concerns: discuss whether Dale’s law confers computational benefits under constraints or is mainly evolutionary/biophysical.
