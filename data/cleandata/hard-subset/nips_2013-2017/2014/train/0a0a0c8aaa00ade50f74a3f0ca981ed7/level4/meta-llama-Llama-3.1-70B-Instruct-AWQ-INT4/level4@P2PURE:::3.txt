The paper revisits the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective that provides significant new insights into an old issue. The authors observe that previous work in this field has often neglected biological aspects, such as Dale's law, and has relied on binary memory representations in rate-based models, as well as non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a comprehensive strategy that tackles all of these challenges simultaneously. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that address these issues. This approach involves a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of these fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors effectively address the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network with naturally balanced excitations and inhibitions. Notably, while they do not explicitly enforce saturation in single neuron firing, this emerges naturally from the solution to the optimization problem. Importantly, the authors utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory, consistent with the fact that these neurons typically send output to other regions, and view inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network's robustness to noise. In addition to showcasing the network's good associative memory properties, the authors also provide an explanation for experimental findings that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset, which they attribute to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a well-written and clear paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools, demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function. Although the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point, they hint at recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is an interesting and beneficial aspect of the paper. Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation.

A few minor issues require attention:

1. The authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3). It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although the impact is expected to be small.

2. A log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide motivation for this choice.

3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories. The severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.

4. The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification.

5. The authors do not mention capacity issues, which are an important computational aspect. It would be nice to include some preliminary statement on this topic.

6. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether this law can enhance computational power under specific constraints. This may not necessarily be the case, and may be a result of evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints, but it would be nice to relate to this.

Overall, the paper provides a clear and well-written contribution to neural computation, tackling a classic problem using novel control-theoretic tools and demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function.
