The paper returns to the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, offering new insights into an established area. The authors observe that much previous work in the field overlooked biological constraints such as Dale’s law, employed binary memory representations in rate-based models, and utilized non-sparse representations for memories. While these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a strategy that simultaneously tackles them all. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach, based on spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that address these limitations. Their approach involves a cost function balancing three terms to achieve the desired outcome through gradient descent: one term ensuring the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, a second promoting the stability of those fixed points, and a third encouraging small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle within the weight parameterization, the authors overcome the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network where excitation and inhibition are naturally balanced. Notably, despite not explicitly enforcing saturation in single neuron firing, this emerges naturally as a consequence of the optimization process. Importantly, the authors utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory (consistent with the typical role of excitatory neurons in outputting signals to other regions), treating inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized. They validate their results through numerical simulations and demonstrate the network’s robustness to noise. Beyond demonstrating the strong associative memory properties of the network, the authors also explain experimental findings regarding the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset (though this appears to be a general feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing). This reduction is attributed to convergence towards the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools, demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory could function. They do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this stage, but point to recent proposals that may facilitate such rules. I found the utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources to be insightful and beneficial. Overall, the paper presents a significant and concise contribution to the field of neural computation.

A few minor points could be addressed:

The authors use γ = 0.04 in Equation (1). It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although I expect it to be minimal.

The use of a log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115. Please provide justification for this choice.

The cost function (Equation 5) may yield solutions that do not perfectly satisfy the fixed-point nature of the memories. How significant is this deviation? This should be quantified and discussed.

Lines 254-256 are unclear. Please clarify the intended meaning.

The authors do not address capacity issues. It would be valuable to include some discussion, even if preliminary, about this important computational aspect.

It would be interesting if the authors could discuss the potential computational advantages of Dale’s law. Specifically, can it be shown to enhance computational power under certain constraints? While it may be a result of evolutionary or biophysical constraints, relating to this aspect would be worthwhile.

This is a clear and well-written paper tackling a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools, and demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function.
