This paper revisits the problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh and insightful perspective on a long-standing challenge. The authors identify key limitations in prior work, including a disregard for biological constraints like Dale’s law, the use of binary representations in rate-based models, and reliance on non-sparse memory representations. While these issues have been addressed individually in previous studies, this work presents a unified strategy leveraging a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding techniques. 

Their approach centers around a cost function balanced across three terms, optimized via gradient descent. The first term promotes the formation of fixed-point memories, the second ensures their stability, and the third encourages small weights to prevent neuronal saturation. Notably, the authors directly incorporate Dale’s principle into the weight parameterization, effectively addressing the aforementioned shortcomings and resulting in a network with inherent excitation-inhibition balance. Interestingly, saturation emerges naturally as a consequence of the optimization process, rather than being explicitly enforced at the single-neuron level. The memory is implemented exclusively through excitatory neurons – consistent with their typical role in outputting signals – while inhibitory neurons are treated as optimization variables.

The authors validate their approach through numerical simulations, demonstrating robust associative memory performance and noise resilience. Beyond successful memory recall, the model also provides a plausible explanation for experimental observations regarding reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, attributing this phenomenon to convergence towards the closest attractor state given the initial network condition. While this variability reduction is a common feature of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity, the model offers a specific mechanistic explanation.

In summary, this is a clear, well-written, and significant contribution to the field of neural computation. It skillfully applies novel control-theoretic tools to a classic problem, proposing a biologically plausible mechanism for associative memory function. The authors acknowledge that a biologically plausible learning rule remains an open challenge, but appropriately point to existing proposals as potential avenues for future work. I particularly appreciated the innovative use of inhibitory neurons as resources for optimizing memory performance. 

However, several minor points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper:

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The authors employ γ = 0.04 in equation (3). A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter choice would be beneficial, even if the expectation is minimal impact.
*   **Log-Normal Distribution:** The introduction of a log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) requires further justification. The authors should provide a rationale for this specific distribution.
*   **Fixed-Point Violation:** The proposed cost function (4) may not perfectly enforce the fixed-point nature of the memories. The extent of this violation should be quantified and discussed, as it could impact performance.
*   **Clarity of Explanation (Lines 254-256):** The statements on lines 254-256 were unclear. Please revise for improved clarity.
*   **Memory Capacity:** The paper does not address memory capacity, a crucial aspect of associative memory systems. Including even preliminary statements regarding capacity limitations would be valuable.
*   **Benefits of Dale’s Law:** Exploring the computational advantages conferred by Dale's law would be interesting. While it may not universally enhance computational power, relating the law to potential benefits under specific constraints, or discussing its origins in biophysical or evolutionary pressures, would enrich the discussion.
