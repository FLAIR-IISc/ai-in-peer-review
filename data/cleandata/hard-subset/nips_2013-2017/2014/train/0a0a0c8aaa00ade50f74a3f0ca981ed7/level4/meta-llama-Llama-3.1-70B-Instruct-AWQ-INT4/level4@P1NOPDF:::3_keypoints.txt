- Presents a fresh control-theoretic perspective on associative memory in neural networks, addressing longstanding issues.  
- Highlights neglect of biological constraints in prior work, specifically Dale's law, non-binary and sparse memory representations.  
- Develops a unified strategy that tackles multiple biological and representation challenges simultaneously.  
- Uses spectral-bounding control-theoretic techniques to construct networks meeting these constraints.  
- Introduces a cost function with three terms: enforce fixed points, ensure stability, and encourage small weights to avoid saturation.  
- Enforces Dale's principle directly in weight parameterization, yielding natural excitation–inhibition balance.  
- Finds single-neuron firing saturation emerges from optimization, without being explicitly enforced.  
- Implements memory using excitatory neurons while treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources.  
- Validates results with numerical simulations showing robustness to noise and good associative-memory performance.  
- Explains experimental finding that trial-to-trial variability decreases after stimulus onset via attractor convergence.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and an important contribution to biologically plausible associative memory modeling.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule yet, though points to recent related proposals.
