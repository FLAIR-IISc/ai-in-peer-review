Summary: The authors propose a novel model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network, incorporating essential features of biological networks that were previously overlooked. Specifically, their model adheres to Dale's Law, which dictates that neurons have a strictly excitatory or inhibitory effect on their projections, and allows networks to encode memories without relying on units that respond at their saturation rate or in a binary manner.

Review: This submission is clearly written, novel, and will likely generate significant interest within the community. The authors provide a thorough review of the history of training neural networks for auto-associative tasks, highlighting the primary contributions and limitations of previous work. The network architecture and training procedure are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the figures effectively illustrate the main results. The authors also demonstrate the network's performance limitations when noise is introduced, showcasing the robustness of the solution obtained through their training procedure.

One potential area for improvement is clarifying the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. While the authors acknowledge that other methods have been employed to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, it is unclear why they selected this particular approach. It would be beneficial to discuss whether this method is uniquely suited to achieving the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, or if alternative training procedures could also be effective.

Additionally, although it may be beyond the scope of this paper, a comparison of the trained network's performance against previous models in terms of network capacity (i.e., the number of memories as a function of network units) would be a valuable contribution to the community. By applying a recent method for ensuring stable fixed points in a dynamical system, the authors have introduced a new model of auto-associative memory that conforms to several constraints of real neural networks, most notably the ability of network units to encode memories without saturating or binary responses.
