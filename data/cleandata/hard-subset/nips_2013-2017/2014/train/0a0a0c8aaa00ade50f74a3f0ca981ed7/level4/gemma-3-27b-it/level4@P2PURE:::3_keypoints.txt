- Revisits associative memory with new control-theoretic perspective and significant insights.  
- Identifies limitations in prior work: neglect of Dale’s law, binary representations in rate models, and non-sparse memories.  
- Proposes a unified solution using a spectral-bounding control-theoretic framework to address these limitations simultaneously.  
- Introduces a gradient-descent-optimized cost function with three terms: enforce fixed-point memories, ensure stability, and penalize large weights.  
- Enforces Dale’s law directly via weight parameterization, yielding naturally balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Saturation of neurons emerges from optimization rather than being hard-coded at the single-neuron level.  
- Uses excitatory neurons to implement memories and treats inhibitory neurons as optimization variables.  
- Numerical simulations demonstrate robust associative memory behavior and noise tolerance.  
- Explains experimentally observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability as convergence to nearest attractors.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and makes an important contribution to biologically plausible associative memory modeling.  
- Does not propose a biologically plausible learning rule, though related recent work is acknowledged for future directions.  
- Treating inhibitory neurons as a computational resource is highlighted as an insightful contribution.  
- Minor issue: sensitivity of results to the chosen γ=0.04 in Eq. (3) should be discussed.  
- Minor issue: need stronger motivation for proposing log-normal distribution for memory states.  
- Minor issue: quantify how much the cost function (4) allows deviations from perfect fixed-point memories.  
- Minor issue: clarify statements in lines 254–256, which are not immediately clear.  
- Minor issue: paper omits discussion of memory capacity; even preliminary observations would be valuable.  
- Minor issue: discuss possible computational advantages (or constraints) imposed by Dale’s law and relate to broader evolutionary/biophysical context.
