

The paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing rate-based attractor networks that store analog memory patterns while satisfying key biological constraints—specifically, Dale’s law, graded neuronal activity without saturation, and stable low-rate firing. Prior work on auto-associative memory networks (e.g., Hopfield [1], Treves [3], LIF-based models [7, 8]) either violated Dale’s law, enforced binary responses, or relied on saturating dynamics for stability. This work overcomes these limitations by directly optimizing synaptic connectivity and inhibitory dynamics to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points, using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization for robust stability. The resulting networks operate in a balanced excitation-inhibition regime, exhibit noise-robust recall, and reproduce the experimentally observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset—a key physiological prediction validated in their simulations (Fig. 6).

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is rigorous. The SSA optimization method (citing [13, 14]) elegantly addresses the non-smoothness of stability constraints, enabling gradient-based training. The authors provide thorough validation: networks achieve stable fixed points (Fig. 2), recall robustly under noise (Fig. 3), and maintain balanced E/I inputs across attractors (Fig. 5). The sparsity of synaptic weights (Fig. 4) emerges naturally from stability constraints, not regularization, strengthening biological plausibility.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The introduction succinctly contextualizes the problem by critiquing prior work’s limitations, and the methods clearly formalize the fixed-point and stability conditions. Figures (e.g., Fig. 1, 5) effectively visualize key results—such as the log-normal activity distributions, optimized inhibitory rates, and E/I balance—without redundancy.  
*Originality*: This is a significant step beyond NIPS proceedings (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 12]). It uniquely integrates Dale’s law, graded activity, and robust stability in a unified framework, resolving a persistent gap in theoretical neuroscience. The method generalizes to arbitrary analog patterns, unlike prior binary or spatially constrained models [10, 11].  
*Significance*: The work advances our mechanistic understanding of memory circuits. The prediction of reduced trial-to-trial variability (Fig. 6) aligns with broad cortical observations [21, 22, 23], offering testable hypotheses for experimental validation. The model’s capacity (0.2 patterns/neuron, Fig. 2) and noise resilience (Fig. 3) demonstrate practical relevance for building biologically plausible memory systems.

**Weaknesses**  
The justification for selecting SSA optimization over alternative stability methods (e.g., Lyapunov-based approaches) is underdeveloped. While the paper states SSA enables "efficient gradient-based optimization," it does not explicitly contrast why this is *necessary* for graded memory networks (where stability is particularly fragile). A brief discussion of how SSA outperforms alternatives in handling the non-smooth spectral abscissa would strengthen the technical contribution. Additionally, the network capacity (number of memories vs. neurons) is not compared to prior work (e.g., [3, 4, 5, 6]), which would contextualize the model’s scalability. A sentence noting that capacity is comparable to theoretical limits [3] but achieved under strict biological constraints would be valuable.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a compelling, high-impact contribution to computational neuroscience. It addresses a fundamental gap in memory network models by satisfying multiple physiological constraints simultaneously, with rigorous theory, clear experiments, and a testable physiological prediction. The method is technically sound, the writing is excellent, and the results are significant for both theory and experiment. While the capacity comparison would be a useful addition, it is not essential for acceptance. The work is clearly within NIPS scope (neural coding, dynamics, learning) and advances the field demonstrably. **Strong acceptance**.
