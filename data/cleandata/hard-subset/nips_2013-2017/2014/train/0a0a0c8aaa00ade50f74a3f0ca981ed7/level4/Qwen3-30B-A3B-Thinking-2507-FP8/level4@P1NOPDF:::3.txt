

This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, offering significant new insights. The authors identify key limitations in prior work: neglect of biological constraints like Dale's law, use of binary memory representations in rate-based models, and non-sparse memory encodings. While individual issues have been addressed separately, the authors present a unified strategy to resolve all three simultaneously. They leverage a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding techniques to construct biologically plausible networks. The core innovation is a cost function balancing three terms, optimized via gradient descent: the first term enforces fixed-point stability for desired memories, the second ensures fixed-point robustness, and the third promotes small weights to prevent saturation. Crucially, Dale's law is directly enforced through weight parameterization, naturally balancing excitation and inhibition. Remarkably, while the model does not explicitly constrain single-neuron firing rates, saturation emerges naturally from the optimization. The authors also adopt a biologically plausible design: using only excitatory neurons for memory storage (consistent with their role in projecting to other regions) and treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. Numerical simulations demonstrate robust associative memory performance and noise resilience. Furthermore, the model explains experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset—a feature attributed to convergence toward the nearest attractor from initial conditions (though this may be a general property of associative memories with spontaneous baseline activity).  

Overall, this is a well-written, clear, and important contribution. The authors effectively apply novel control-theoretic tools to a foundational problem in neural computation, demonstrating a biologically plausible associative memory mechanism. While no explicit learning rule is proposed, the authors appropriately reference recent work that may inform such rules. The strategic use of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources is particularly insightful.  

A few minor points require clarification:  
1. Equation (3) uses γ=0.04. The sensitivity of results to this parameter should be discussed (though small sensitivity is expected).  
2. The log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) requires justification.  
3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions that slightly violate the exact fixed-point requirement. The severity of this violation should be quantified and addressed.  
4. Lines 254–256 are unclear and need elaboration.  
5. Capacity limitations—a critical computational aspect—should be briefly addressed, even preliminarily.  
6. The computational benefits of Dale's law warrant discussion. While it may stem from evolutionary or biophysical constraints, it would be valuable to assess whether it enhances computational capacity under specific constraints.  

The paper makes a clear, concise, and significant contribution to neural computation, effectively bridging control theory and biologically plausible memory models.
