**Summary:** This paper introduces an auto-associative memory model implemented in a rate-based neural network that adheres to several biologically plausible constraints. This work addresses shortcomings in prior auto-associative models by incorporating key biological features often overlooked, such as Dale's Law (requiring neurons to be exclusively excitatory or inhibitory) and the ability to store memories using graded, non-saturating neural responses rather than binary or fully saturated activity. The network learns to store memories through synaptic weight adjustments guided by a gradient descent algorithm. This process is constrained by a recently developed technique that guarantees the stability of the system's dynamics when linearized around its memory-representing fixed points. Through simulations, the authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness, showing that the resulting memory states are stable and characterized by slow dynamics, closely approaching but not perfectly reaching a static fixed point. The paper highlights two emergent properties of the trained network that align with experimental observations in cortical circuits: a synaptic weight distribution with a zero-centered peak and heavy tails, and a balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs to the average neuron. These two features are identified as crucial for the network's ability to perform robust memory recall, successfully returning to the correct memory state after being perturbed, even when subjected to stochastic noise.

**Review:** This is an exceptionally clear and novel paper that addresses a topic of significant interest to the field. The authors provide an excellent overview of the relevant literature on training auto-associative networks, effectively summarizing both the key advances and the limitations of prior models. The model architecture and learning algorithm are described with clarity, and the accompanying figures are thoughtfully selected to effectively convey the paper's primary findings. The inclusion of network noise to test the model's performance boundaries serves to underscore the robustness of the solution achieved by the training method.

One point for clarification concerns the motivation behind choosing this particular training procedure to achieve the stated goals. The authors note that while various methods can enforce Dale's Law, achieving graded, non-saturating memories is more challenging. The selected method accomplishes this, yet the text does not elaborate on why this approach is especially suited for this task compared to potential alternatives. A brief discussion on what makes this method particularly effective for preventing saturated responses would strengthen the manuscript.

Additionally, while perhaps outside the scope of the current work, a comparison of the trained network's memory capacity (i.e., the number of stored patterns relative to network size) against classical auto-associative models would be a valuable contribution and of great interest to the readership.
