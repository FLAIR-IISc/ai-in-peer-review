This paper presents a novel model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network that adheres to several key biological constraints. The authors provide a clear and concise review of the history of training neural networks to perform auto-associative tasks, highlighting the main contributions and shortcomings of previous work. They then introduce their own model, which uses a gradient descent procedure to optimize the network parameters, subject to the constraint that the linearization of the dynamics around the fixed point is stable. This is achieved using a recently published method for ensuring stable fixed points in dynamical systems.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure through simulations, showing that the trained network can perform robust auto-associative memory even in the presence of stochastic input noise. They also highlight two features of their trained network that are in line with experimentally observed features of cortical networks: a distribution of synaptic weights centered at zero with long tails, and the fact that an average network unit receives approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic input.

The paper is well-written, and the figures are well-chosen to quickly illustrate the main results. The authors also provide a clear and detailed description of their methods, making it easy to follow their reasoning and understand their approach.

One recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the stated objectives. As the authors note, other methods have been employed in the past to enforce Dale's Law or achieve graded network memories, with the latter condition seemingly more difficult to enforce. The chosen training procedure achieves the desired goal of building an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but nothing in the text indicates that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly well-suited to this task. Perhaps another network training procedure would achieve the same result? Can anything be said as to why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?

Additionally, while the authors provide some discussion of the limitations of their approach, a comparison of how the trained network performs against previous models in terms of network capacity (number of memories as a function of network units) would be of interest to the community. This would provide a more complete understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed model.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and auto-associative memory. The authors' approach is novel and effective, and their results are well-presented and easy to understand.
