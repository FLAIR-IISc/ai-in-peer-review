### **Review**

**Summary**

The authors present a rate-based neural network model for auto-associative memory that incorporates several biologically plausible constraints. The paper addresses key limitations of previous models by adhering to Dale's Law—requiring neurons to be strictly excitatory or inhibitory—and by demonstrating that memories can be encoded using graded, non-saturating neural responses rather than binary or saturated activity. Memories are stored as stable fixed points in the network's dynamics, with synaptic weights trained via a gradient descent procedure. A crucial component of this procedure is a recently developed constraint that guarantees the stability of the learned fixed points.

Through simulations, the authors demonstrate that their training method is effective. The resulting attractors are stable and exhibit slow dynamics, indicating they are robust fixed points of the system. Notably, the trained network develops two features consistent with experimental observations in the cortex: 1) a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and 2) a balance between the total excitatory and inhibitory synaptic inputs received by a typical neuron. The authors argue that these emergent properties are key to the network's success, enabling robust memory recall even when the system is perturbed or subjected to stochastic noise.

**Critique**

This is a well-written and novel submission that will be of significant interest to the computational neuroscience and machine learning communities. The authors provide an excellent review of the history of auto-associative memory models, effectively situating their work by highlighting the contributions and shortcomings of prior research. The network architecture and training algorithm are presented with clarity, and the figures are well-chosen to compellingly illustrate the paper's main results. Furthermore, the analysis of the network's performance under noisy conditions effectively demonstrates the robustness of the learned solutions.

My primary recommendations are aimed at strengthening the connection between the methods and the stated goals:

1.  **Clarify the Rationale for the Chosen Training Method:** The paper's main success is achieving an auto-associative network with non-saturating, graded responses—a historically challenging objective. While the proposed training procedure achieves this goal, the manuscript does not explain why this particular method is uniquely suited for this task. As the authors note, other techniques have been used to enforce constraints like Dale's Law. Could alternative training procedures also produce graded responses? A discussion on why this stability-enforcing method so effectively prevents units from saturating would add significant insight. Providing this intuition would strengthen the paper's contribution by explaining not just *what* works, but a potential reason *why* it works.

2.  **Comparison of Memory Capacity:** While perhaps beyond the strict scope of the current work, a brief comparison of this model's performance against classic auto-associative memory models would be highly valuable. Given the long history of this research area, a benchmark of memory capacity (i.e., the number of memories that can be stored as a function of network size) would provide the community with a clear metric to evaluate the trade-offs and advantages of this new, more biologically realistic approach. Even a preliminary analysis or a discussion of expected scaling properties relative to models like the Hopfield network would be a welcome addition.

**One-Sentence Summary**

Applying a novel training method that ensures stable fixed points, the authors introduce a new model of auto-associative memory that incorporates key biological constraints, such as Dale's Law and the ability to store memories using graded, non-saturating neural activity.
