The paper offers a fresh perspective on the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks, providing significant new insights into an old challenge. The authors astutely observe that previous work in this field often disregarded biological aspects such as Dale's law, employed binary memory representations in rate-based models, and utilized non-sparse representations for memories. Although these issues have been addressed individually in other studies, the authors develop a comprehensive strategy that tackles all these concerns simultaneously. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that address these issues.

In this approach, the authors propose a cost function that balances three terms to achieve the desired goal through gradient descent. The first term in the cost function contributes to the fixed-point nature of the desired memories, the second term ensures the stability of these fixed points, and the third term encourages small weights to prevent saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle in the weight parameterization, the authors successfully address the shortcomings of previous work, resulting in a network where excitations and inhibitions are naturally balanced. Notably, while they do not explicitly enforce saturation in single neuron firing, this emerges naturally through the solution of the optimization problem.

The authors' decision to utilize only excitatory neurons to implement the memory, consistent with the fact that these neurons typically send output to other regions, is particularly noteworthy. They view inhibitory neurons as variables to be optimized over, which is an interesting and beneficial approach. The authors validate their results through numerical simulations, demonstrating the network's noise robustness and its good associative memory properties. Furthermore, they provide an explanation for experimental findings showing that trial-to-trial variability is reduced following stimulus onset, attributing this to convergence to the attractor closest to the initial condition.

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. The authors demonstrate how a biologically plausible associative memory may function, although they do not propose a biologically plausible learning mechanism at this point. Instead, they hint at recent proposals that may lead to such rules. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as resources is a notable aspect of the paper.

A few minor issues require attention. Firstly, the authors use γ = 0.04 in equation (3); it would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to this choice, although it is expected to be small. Secondly, a log-normal distribution for memory states is proposed on line 115; the authors should provide motivation for this choice. Thirdly, the cost function (4) may yield solutions that violate the exact fixed-point nature of the memories; the severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.

Additionally, the statements on lines 254-256 are unclear and require clarification. The authors do not mention capacity issues, which are an essential computational aspect; it would be beneficial to make a preliminary statement about this. Finally, it would be interesting if the authors could discuss the computational benefits of Dale's law, specifically whether this law can enhance computational power under specific constraints. This discussion could provide valuable insights into the evolutionary and/or biophysical constraints that have shaped the development of this law.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, concise, and important contribution to neural computation, offering a biologically plausible associative memory model that leverages novel control-theoretic tools.
