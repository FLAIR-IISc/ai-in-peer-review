- Paper constructs biologically constrained attractor networks (Dale’s law, sparse/recurrent connectivity, graded analog memories, low firing rates, dynamic E/I balance).

- Memory embedding posed as constrained optimization: minimize dv/dt norm, smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA), and weight L2 regularizer with sign-constrained reparameterized weights.

- Integrates control-theoretic SSA and optimization techniques with prior work on graded attractors and balanced networks.

- Networks exhibit balanced excitation/inhibition, robustness to corrupted cues and ongoing noise, sparse strong synapses, and variability quenching at stimulus onset.

- Technical approach is sound and clearly described, including log-exp reparameterization and differentiable SSA.

- Demonstrated capacity of ~30 analog attractors in n=150 (m/n ~0.2) with near-ideal observer recall across cue noise levels.

- Optimization can produce “slow points” (small nonzero dv/dt) but SSA negativity yields convergence to nearby fixed points in practice.

- Manuscript is clear and well organized; figures effectively support the claims though some passages need clarification.

- Novelty is framing memory embedding as robust stability optimization under biological constraints and operationalizing it with SSA.

- Significance: provides a constructive route to biologically plausible analog memory networks and makes testable predictions, though broader impact depends on scalability, spiking generalization, and learning rules.

- Strength: unified, principled optimization that enforces Dale’s law and yields robust analog attractors.

- Strength: empirical evidence for across-attractor E/I balance and sparse connectivity with a few strong supporting synapses.

- Strength: strong recall performance, robustness to noise, and alignment with observed variability quenching.

- Strength: clear, reproducible SSA-based objective amenable to gradient-based optimization.

- Weakness: learning is algorithmic and nonlocal; no proposed local/plausible plasticity rules approximating the objective.

- Weakness: some targets remain slow points; residual dv/dt magnitude and drift timescales are not quantified.

- Weakness: limited analysis of capacity and scaling with network size, m/n, and pattern statistics.

- Weakness: sensitivity to single-neuron gain γ and SSA smoothing parameter ε is not analyzed.

- Weakness: no spiking-network instantiation to demonstrate transfer from rate to spiking dynamics.

- Request: provide sensitivity analysis for gain γ on recall performance, stability margins, and firing rates.

- Request: justify log-normal pattern statistics with empirical citations and report sensitivity to distributional choices.

- Request: quantify residual dv/dt at targets, estimate drift time constants, and measure distances between targets and realized fixed points.

- Request: rewrite lines on slow points vs fixed points to clearly distinguish training objective from observed dynamics.

- Request: provide preliminary capacity scaling as a function of n, memory statistics, and ε, and discuss theoretical/computational expectations.

- Request: discuss whether Dale’s law affords computational/robustness advantages beyond biological realism.

- Request: justify scaling of ε with network size and show robustness across ε values.

- Request: include a small-scale spiking instantiation (e.g., LIF) to strengthen generalization claims.

- Pros for acceptance: principled, general method linking memory recall, balanced E/I dynamics, and variability quenching with testable predictions.

- Cons for acceptance: lack of biological learning rules, limited capacity/scalability characterization, and rate-only demonstrations without spiking results.

- Overall: clear, technically strong contribution that would benefit from additional sensitivity, capacity, and spiking analyses before publication.
