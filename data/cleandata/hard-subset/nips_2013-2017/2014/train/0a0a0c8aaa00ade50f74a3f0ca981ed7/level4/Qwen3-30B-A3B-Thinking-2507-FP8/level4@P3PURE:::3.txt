

The paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks with a novel biological perspective, offering significant new insights. The authors identify that prior work often neglected key biological constraints—specifically, violations of Dale's law, the use of binary memory representations in rate-based models, and non-sparse memory encodings. While these limitations have been addressed individually in the literature, the authors present a unified strategy to resolve all three simultaneously. They leverage a recent control-theoretic framework based on spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that inherently satisfy these constraints. Their approach formulates a cost function balancing three terms optimized via gradient descent: the first term enforces the fixed-point nature of target memories, the second ensures fixed-point stability, and the third promotes small synaptic weights to prevent neuron saturation. Crucially, Dale's law is directly enforced through weight parameterization, naturally balancing excitation and inhibition. Notably, while the model does not explicitly constrain single-neuron firing rates to avoid saturation, this property emerges naturally from the optimization. The authors utilize only excitatory neurons for memory implementation (aligning with biological observations that excitatory neurons typically drive output), treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables. Numerical simulations demonstrate the network's noise robustness and associative memory properties, including a natural explanation for reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset—attributed to convergence toward the attractor closest to the initial condition (a phenomenon also observed in associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing).  

In summary, this is a clear, well-written contribution that applies innovative control-theoretic tools to a foundational problem in neural computation, demonstrating a biologically plausible associative memory mechanism. While the authors do not yet propose a biologically plausible learning rule (though they reference recent proposals that may enable this), the work provides a compelling foundation. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimization resources is particularly insightful and beneficial. Overall, the paper presents a concise, important, and well-executed advance in the field.  

Minor concerns:  
1. Equation (3) uses $\gamma=0.04$. The sensitivity of results to this parameter should be discussed (though it may be minor).  
2. The log-normal distribution for memory states (line 115) requires justification.  
3. The cost function (4) may yield solutions violating the exact fixed-point property of memories. The severity of this violation should be quantified and addressed.  
4. The explanation on lines 254–256 is unclear and requires clarification.  
5. Capacity limitations—critical for associative memory systems—should be briefly discussed, even preliminarily.  
6. The computational benefits of Dale's law warrant discussion: while it may reflect evolutionary/biophysical constraints, it would be valuable to explicitly relate whether it enhances computational power under specific conditions.
