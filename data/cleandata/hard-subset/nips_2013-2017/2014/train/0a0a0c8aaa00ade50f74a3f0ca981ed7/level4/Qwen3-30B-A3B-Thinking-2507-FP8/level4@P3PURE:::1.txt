

Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network that adheres to multiple biologically plausible constraints. Previous models have failed to incorporate key biological features, specifically Dale's Law (requiring neurons to be strictly excitatory or inhibitory in their projections) and the absence of units that encode memories via saturation or binary responses. Memories are encoded through synaptic modifications guided by gradient descent, constrained via a recent method ensuring stability of the linearization of network dynamics around fixed points. Simulations demonstrate that the trained fixed points exhibit nearly stationary dynamics (close to but not exactly fixed points) and achieve the desired stability. The authors identify two experimentally observed cortical features in their trained network: a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and balanced excitatory and inhibitory input per unit. They posit these features are critical for robust auto-associative memory performance under perturbation and stochastic noise.  

Review: This is a well-written, novel contribution of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a clear historical review of auto-associative network training, effectively highlighting prior work’s key contributions and shortcomings. The network architecture and training procedure are described lucidly, and the figures effectively illustrate core results. The demonstration of the network’s robustness against perturbation and noise further strengthens the presentation.  

A key concern is the justification for the chosen training procedure. While the method achieves the stated goals—enforcing Dale’s Law and preventing unit saturation—it is unclear why this specific approach was selected over alternatives. The manuscript notes prior methods exist for enforcing Dale’s Law or graded responses (with the latter being particularly challenging), yet provides no rationale for preferring this procedure. Could alternative training methods achieve equivalent results? A brief discussion of why this method is uniquely effective would significantly strengthen the argument.  

Additionally, a brief comparison of network capacity (number of memories versus units) against prior models would be valuable, though this may exceed the paper’s scope. Such a comparison would directly address a central question in auto-associative memory research.  

1-2 sentence summary: Applying a recent method for stable fixed points in dynamical systems, the authors introduce a biologically constrained auto-associative memory model where units encode memories without saturation or binary responses.
