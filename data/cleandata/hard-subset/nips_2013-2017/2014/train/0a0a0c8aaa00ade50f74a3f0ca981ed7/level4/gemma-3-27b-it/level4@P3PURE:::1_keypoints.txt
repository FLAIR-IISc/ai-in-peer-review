- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model with biologically plausible constraints (Dale’s Law, graded/non-saturating units).
- Encodes memories via synaptic updates using gradient descent constrained by a recent method to ensure stability of the dynamics’ linearization around fixed points.
- Simulations show stable fixed points with slow dynamics near them, enabling robust auto-associative recall under perturbations.
- Trained networks exhibit synaptic weight distributions centered at zero with long tails and average excitatory/inhibitory balance, matching cortical observations.
- Paper is clearly written, novel, and likely of strong interest to the community.
- Provides a helpful historical review and clearly presents network architecture, training procedure, and illustrative figures.
- Demonstrates network performance limits under noise, underscoring the robustness of the learned solution.
- Reviewer requests clearer rationale for selecting this specific training procedure relative to alternative methods for enforcing Dale’s Law and graded memories.
- Asks whether other training methods could achieve comparable results and requests insight into why the chosen method is effective.
- Suggests, possibly as future work, comparing memory capacity (memories per unit) against prior auto-associative models.
