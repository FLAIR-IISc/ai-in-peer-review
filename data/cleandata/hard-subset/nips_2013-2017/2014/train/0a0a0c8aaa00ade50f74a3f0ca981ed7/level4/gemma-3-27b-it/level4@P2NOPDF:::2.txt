This manuscript presents a rate-based neuronal network designed to store and maintain multiple stable activity patterns, effectively serving as a memory system. The authors address limitations found in prior attractor network models, specifically violations of Dale’s law and activity saturation within stored memories. Their approach utilizes gradient descent to train the network, optimizing a cost function that simultaneously minimizes activity changes within desired attractor states and the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix, while maximizing attractor stability. Key results demonstrate a weight distribution consistent with experimental observations and a balanced excitation/inhibition profile within the attractor states.

The manuscript is well-written and clearly explains both the model and the training procedure. I found the presentation of the results to be particularly accessible. The combination of biologically-motivated constraints incorporated into the model and the resulting network characteristics represents a valuable contribution to the field of attractor neural networks. Specifically, the authors successfully train a rate-based network to support graded, non-saturated attractors through constrained synaptic weights and neuronal activity. By imposing constraints based on biological plausibility, they achieve a network-level balance of excitation and inhibition. 

The strengths of this work lie in its careful consideration of biological realism and its successful mitigation of common issues in attractor network models. The resulting balance between stability, saturation avoidance, and biological constraints is a significant achievement. The experimental validation of the weight distribution further strengthens the findings. 

However, a few points could be addressed to further enhance the manuscript. While the authors demonstrate a balance of excitation and inhibition, a more detailed analysis of *how* these constraints interact to produce this balance would be beneficial. For example, exploring the sensitivity of the balance to variations in the cost function weights (for the Frobenius norm, stability, and activity change terms) could provide further insight.

Furthermore, while the authors mention the improvement over previous models regarding saturation, a direct quantitative comparison—perhaps visualizing activity distributions for the current model versus a comparable, unconstrained model—would more convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. It would also be helpful to briefly discuss the potential implications of this model for more complex cognitive functions beyond simple memory storage.

Overall, this is a strong submission with a clear motivation, well-executed methodology, and promising results. The manuscript's clarity and biological relevance make it a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
