### Summary

The authors present a rate-based model of an auto-associative memory network that adheres to a set of biologically plausible constraints. Previous models have often failed to incorporate key features of biological networks, such as Dale's Law (neurons being strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and the ability to encode graded, analog memories without relying on units that are either saturated or binary. In this work, memories are embedded as stable fixed points in the network's dynamics via synaptic modifications. The learning process uses a gradient-descent procedure that is constrained by a recently developed method from control theory (the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa), which ensures the stability of the dynamics around each target memory state.

The authors use simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their training procedure, showing that the resulting memory states are stable "slow points" of the dynamics (i.e., they are very close to, but not exactly, true fixed points). The authors highlight two emergent properties of the trained network that align with experimental observations in the cortex: a sparse distribution of synaptic weights with a peak at zero and long tails, and a dynamic balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs to each neuron. They identify these features as crucial for the network's ability to perform robust auto-associative recall from corrupted cues, even in the presence of ongoing stochastic noise.

### Review

This is a clearly-written and novel submission that will be of great interest to the computational neuroscience community. The authors provide an excellent review of the history of auto-associative memory networks, effectively highlighting the contributions and shortcomings of previous work. The network architecture and the optimization procedure are presented with clarity, and the figures are well-chosen to illustrate the paper's main results. A particular strength is the demonstration of the network's robustness to ongoing noise, which underscores the quality of the solution found by the training procedure.

My primary recommendation is to clarify the relationship between the chosen training procedure and the paper's stated objectives, particularly the goal of achieving graded, non-saturating memory states. As the authors note, previous methods have successfully enforced Dale’s Law, but achieving stable analog memories has proven more difficult. The proposed optimization method successfully achieves this goal. However, the manuscript could be strengthened by explicitly discussing *why* this particular method is so well-suited for this task. Is there something inherent in optimizing for robust stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa that promotes non-saturating, balanced E-I solutions? A more direct connection between the method and the resulting network properties would make the contribution even more compelling.

Additionally, while likely beyond the scope of the current paper, a brief discussion on how this network compares to previous models in terms of storage capacity (i.e., the number of memories stored as a function of network size) would be a valuable addition for the community.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution. Applying a recent method from control theory for ensuring stable fixed points, the authors have introduced a new model of auto-associative memory that successfully incorporates several key constraints of real neural networks, most notably the ability to store graded memories without saturating or binary responses.
