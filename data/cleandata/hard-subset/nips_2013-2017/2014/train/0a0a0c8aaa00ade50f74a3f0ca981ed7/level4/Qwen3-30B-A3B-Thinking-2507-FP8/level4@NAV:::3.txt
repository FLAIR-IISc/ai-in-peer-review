

The paper revisits the long-standing problem of associative memory in neural networks with a novel perspective, providing significant new insights into this established area. The authors identify that prior work frequently overlooked biological constraints—specifically, neglecting Dale's law, employing binary memory representations in rate-based models, and utilizing non-sparse memory representations. While individual aspects of these limitations have been addressed elsewhere, the authors develop a unified strategy to resolve all three simultaneously. Concretely, they employ a recent control-theoretic framework grounded in spectral bounding techniques to construct networks that overcome these challenges. This approach introduces a cost function balancing three components optimized via gradient descent: the first term ensures the fixed-point nature of target memories, the second term enhances fixed-point stability, and the third term promotes small weights to avoid saturation. By directly enforcing Dale's principle through weight parameterization, the authors resolve the aforementioned limitations, yielding a network where excitation and inhibition are naturally balanced. Notably, while neuron saturation is not explicitly enforced, it emerges naturally from the optimization. The authors utilize only excitatory neurons for memory implementation (aligning with biological observations that excitatory neurons primarily drive output), treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable variables. Numerical simulations validate the approach, demonstrating robustness to noise. Beyond showcasing strong associative memory properties, the authors also explain experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset (a feature common to associative memories with spontaneous baseline firing) through convergence to the nearest attractor from the initial state.  

In summary, this is a clear, well-written paper that addresses a classic neural computation problem using innovative control-theoretic methods, effectively demonstrating a biologically plausible associative memory mechanism. While the authors do not propose a biologically plausible learning rule here, they reference recent work that may inform such mechanisms. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimization variables is particularly insightful and beneficial. Overall, the contribution is clear, concise, and significant for the field.  

Minor concerns:  
- The choice of $\gamma=0.04$ in Eq. (3) warrants discussion of sensitivity (though expected to be minimal).  
- The rationale for proposing a log-normal distribution for memory states on line 115 requires motivation.  
- The cost function (Eq. 4) might yield solutions violating exact fixed-point memory requirements; the severity of this violation should be quantified and discussed.  
- Clarification is needed for the statements in lines 254–256.  
- Capacity limitations should be addressed, even preliminarily, as this is a critical computational aspect.  
- The computational benefits of Dale's law should be discussed: does it enhance computational power under specific constraints, or is it primarily a result of evolutionary/biophysical constraints?  

A clear and well-written paper tackling a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools, and demonstrating how a biologically plausible associative memory may function.
