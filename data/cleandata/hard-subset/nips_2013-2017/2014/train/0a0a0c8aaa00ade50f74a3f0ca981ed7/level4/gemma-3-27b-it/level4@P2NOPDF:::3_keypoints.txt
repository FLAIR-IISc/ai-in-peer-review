- Paper revisits associative memory with a unified, control-theoretic approach addressing prior limitations (Dale’s law, binary rate models, non-sparse representations).  
- Introduces a spectral-bounding framework and optimizes a three-term cost function via gradient descent.  
- Cost terms: enforce fixed-point memories, ensure stability, and penalize large weights to avoid neuronal saturation.  
- Directly incorporates Dale’s principle into weight parameterization, creating an excitation–inhibition balanced network.  
- Memory representations stored in excitatory neurons; inhibitory neurons serve as optimization variables.  
- Neuronal saturation emerges naturally from optimization rather than being enforced at the single-neuron level.  
- Numerical simulations show robust associative recall and noise resilience.  
- Model offers a mechanistic explanation for reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via attractor convergence.  
- Writing is clear and the contribution is significant, applying novel control-theoretic tools to a classic problem.  
- Authors acknowledge biologically plausible learning rules remain open and point to existing proposals for future work.
