Summary: The authors present a model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network, subject to biological constraints. Previous models of auto-associative memory have failed to incorporate several key characteristics of real biological networks, such as adhering to Dale's law, which states that neurons have strictly excitatory or inhibitory effects on their projections, and networks can encode memory without relying solely on units that respond to their saturation rate or binary methods. A recently published method ensures that the dynamics of a dynamic system are linear around a stable point, encoding memory in the network through synaptic changes based on a gradient descent approach. The authors illustrate the effectiveness of their training method through simulations, noting that the trained stable points exhibit slow network dynamics (i.e., they are close to stable, but not exactly stable), and are stable as desired. The authors mention two characteristics of their trained network that are consistent with experimentally observed characteristics of cortical networks, namely a distribution of synaptic weights concentrated in zero with long tails, and an average network unit receiving approximately equal excitatory and inhibitory synaptic inputs. The authors identify these characteristics as key components of the network's success in performing strong auto-associative memory, even in the presence of stochastic input noise, even when these characteristics are scattered from a stable point of the network.

Review: This is a clearly written, novel presentation that will be of great interest to the community. The authors beautifully review the history of neural network training for performing auto-associative tasks, highlighting the major contributions and shortcomings of previous work. Both the network architecture and the training method are clearly presented, and the figures are well chosen to quickly illustrate the main results of the presentation. The authors illustrate the network's performance limitations when network noise is included, highlighting the robust solution obtained by the training method.

A recommendation would be to clarify the relationship between the selected training method and the stated objectives. Since the author mentions that other methods have been used in the past to enforce Dale's law or achieve graded network memory, the latter condition seems more difficult to implement. The selected training method achieves the desired goal of creating an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but there is nothing in the text to suggest that the authors chose this method because they believed it was particularly suitable for this task. Might another network training method achieve the same results? Can anything be said about why this particular method achieves that goal so effectively?

Also (although probably outside the scope of this paper), a rich history of network training for performing auto-associative tasks, a comparison of how trained networks perform compared to previous models in terms of network capacity (the number of memory units as a function), would certainly be of interest to the community. 1-2 sentences: By applying a recent method to ensure stable stable points in a dynamic system, the authors present a new model of auto-associative memory that is consistent with several limitations of real neural networks, in particular the condition that network units are able to respond without saturating or in binary mode.
