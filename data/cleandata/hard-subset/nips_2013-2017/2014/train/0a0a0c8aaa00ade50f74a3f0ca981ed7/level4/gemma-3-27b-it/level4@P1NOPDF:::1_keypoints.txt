- Paper introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model emphasizing biological plausibility (Dale’s Law, non-saturating units).  
- Memories encoded via gradient-descent synaptic updates with a stability-constraining method to enforce stable fixed points.  
- Simulations show trained fixed points with slow, stable network dynamics as intended.  
- Trained networks exhibit synaptic weight distributions centered near zero with long tails, matching experimental observations.  
- Individual units receive approximately balanced excitatory and inhibitory input, another experimentally consistent feature.  
- Network shows robust auto-associative performance under perturbations and stochastic noise.  
- Writing, literature review, architecture description, and figures are clear and effective.  
- Reviewer requests clearer justification for choosing the specific training/stability method over alternatives.  
- Reviewer asks whether other training procedures could achieve similar results and which aspects of the method drive its effectiveness.  
- Suggestion to compare network capacity (memories per network size) against prior auto-associative models for community value.
