- Proposes a rate-based recurrent network trained to store multiple stable activity patterns while enforcing Dale’s law and avoiding saturation of neural activities in memory states.  
- Optimization uses a loss that (i) minimizes deviations at desired fixed points, (ii) encourages fixed-point stability, and (iii) regularizes the synaptic matrix via its Frobenius norm.  
- Trained networks exhibit weight distributions and E/I balance that are closer to experimental observations, and produce graded, non-saturated attractors.  
- Manuscript is clearly written and the model, objective, and results are easy to follow.  

Strengths:
- Biological plausibility: enforces Dale’s law and achieves stable, non-saturated attractors under E/I constraints.  
- Clear, interpretable objective that directly targets fixed points and their stability rather than hand-crafted connectivity.  
- Demonstrates graded (non-saturated) memory states, improving biological realism and coding richness.  
- Emergent E/I balance and realistic weight statistics support the model’s relevance to cortical circuits.  
- Good exposition with balance between methodological detail and phenomenology.  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Stability term is underspecified: unclear how stability is quantified (Jacobian spectrum, Lyapunov, contraction margin), where it is evaluated, and how gradients are computed.  
- Capacity and scaling are not reported: missing analysis of how many memories scale with network size/sparsity and comparison to classical capacities.  
- Basins of attraction are not quantified: sizes and robustness to initial-condition perturbations and input noise are missing.  
- Robustness and generalization analyses are lacking: sensitivity to nonlinearity, noise, neuronal heterogeneity, initializations, synaptic perturbations, pruning, and ablation of Frobenius term.  
- Implementation details of Dale’s law enforcement (reparameterization, projection, penalization) and any differential treatment of E/I submatrices are not described.  
- Distributional claims need quantitative support: require moments, fits (e.g., log-normal), statistical tests, and breakdowns by connection type and sparsity.  
- Comparison to prior work is insufficient: need explicit contrasts with Hopfield-like, balanced, and trained RNN attractor constructions, including trade-offs.  
- Computational cost and scalability of the stability term (e.g., Jacobian computations) are not addressed.  

Minor suggestions:
- Clarify wording about “graded and non-saturated attractors in the synaptic weights and inhibitory activities.”  
- Provide Jacobian spectra at learned attractors to support stability claims and illustrate margins.  
- Report hyperparameters, convergence criteria, and initialization schemes for reproducibility.  
- Include ablations: remove Frobenius norm, relax Dale’s law, vary inhibitory gain, and report impacts on stability and E/I balance.  
- Demonstrate retrieval from partially cued inputs and quantify performance (overlap/error) under noise.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid, well-executed contribution that jointly achieves graded, non-saturated fixed points and Dale’s law with emergent E/I balance; recommend acceptance after addressing stability-term details, capacity/robustness analyses, and quantitative weight comparisons.
