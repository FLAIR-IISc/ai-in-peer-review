This is a well-written and novel contribution that will be of significant interest to the field. The authors provide a thorough overview of prior work on training neural networks for auto-associative memory, effectively outlining the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches. Both the network architecture and training methodology are clearly presented, and the figures effectively communicate the key findings. The authors appropriately acknowledge the network’s performance limitations under noisy conditions, emphasizing the robustness of the learned solution.

I recommend the authors further elaborate on the rationale behind their chosen training procedure in relation to the specific goals of the work. While other methods exist for enforcing Dale’s Law or achieving graded response dynamics, the latter has historically proven more challenging. The current procedure successfully creates an auto-associative network with non-saturating units, but the text does not explain *why* this method was selected as particularly well-suited for this purpose. Exploring whether alternative training procedures might yield comparable results, and discussing the advantages of this specific approach, would strengthen the manuscript.

Additionally, while potentially exceeding the scope of this paper, a comparison of the network’s capacity (memories stored as a function of network size) against that of previous models would be valuable for the community, given the extensive history of research in this area. In summary, the authors present a new model of auto-associative memory that adheres to several biological constraints, notably enabling memory encoding without reliance on saturating or binary neuronal responses, achieved through the application of a recently developed method for stabilizing fixed points in dynamical systems.
