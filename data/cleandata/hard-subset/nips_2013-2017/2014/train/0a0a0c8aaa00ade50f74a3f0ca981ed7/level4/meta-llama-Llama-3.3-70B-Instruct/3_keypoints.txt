- Paper offers a refreshing, significant new perspective on associative memory in neural networks.  
- Highlights overlooked biological aspects (Dale’s law, non-binary and sparse memory representations) that prior models often ignored.  
- Develops a comprehensive strategy that tackles these biological and representational issues simultaneously.  
- Uses a control-theoretic approach with spectral bounding to construct networks satisfying the constraints.  
- Introduces a cost function with three terms optimized by gradient descent to enforce fixed points, stability, and small weights.  
- Direct incorporation of Dale’s principle in weight parameterization yields naturally balanced excitation and inhibition.  
- Neuronal saturation emerges from the optimization rather than being explicitly enforced.  
- Treats excitatory neurons as memory carriers and uses inhibitory neurons as optimized resources.  
- Numerical simulations validate noise robustness and associative memory performance.  
- Explains experimental observation of reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus as convergence to the nearest attractor.  
- Well-structured, clearly written, and an important contribution; points to potential biologically plausible learning rules for future work.
