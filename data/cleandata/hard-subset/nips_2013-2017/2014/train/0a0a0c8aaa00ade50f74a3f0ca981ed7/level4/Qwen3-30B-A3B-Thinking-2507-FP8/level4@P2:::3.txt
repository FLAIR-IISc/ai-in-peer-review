

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in computational neuroscience: constructing biologically plausible associative memory networks that satisfy multiple physiological constraints simultaneously. Previous work has struggled to incorporate key features of cortical circuits, such as Dale's law (separate excitatory and inhibitory neuron classes), graded memory representations (as opposed to binary), low firing rates, and stable dynamics. While some previous studies addressed individual constraints, none have unified them in a single framework. The authors present a novel control-theoretic approach that directly optimizes network parameters to embed arbitrary analog memory patterns as stable fixed points while respecting all these constraints.

The paper's main contribution is a framework that uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a stability metric within a gradient-based optimization procedure. The authors formalize memory storage as requiring two conditions: fixed points (where activity derivatives vanish) and local stability (ensured via SSA minimization). Crucially, they enforce Dale's law through a reparameterization of synaptic weights, and treat inhibitory neurons as "auxiliary" variables to be optimized rather than fixed components. This approach naturally produces networks operating in a balanced regime where excitation and inhibition dynamically adjust to maintain low firing rates without saturation. The authors validate their approach through extensive simulations, demonstrating robust memory recall under noise, explaining the reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset (a widely observed experimental phenomenon), and showing that synaptic weights become sparse—a feature consistent with real cortical circuits.

The paper excels in several dimensions. Quality is strong: the theoretical framework is sound, the optimization procedure is well-motivated, and the experimental validation is thorough. The authors demonstrate that their networks can store multiple analog patterns (capacity ~0.2 for 150 neurons), perform robust recall even with significant noise in the initial cue, and produce balanced E/I dynamics consistent with biological observations. Clarity is excellent: the paper is well-organized, the mathematical formulation is accessible, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (particularly Figures 1-6). Originality is significant: the authors synthesize multiple constraints previously addressed separately, introduce a novel optimization approach using control-theoretic methods, and provide a biologically grounded explanation for an experimental observation. Significance is high: this work represents a step toward understanding the neural substrate of memory, with potential applications in both theoretical neuroscience and the design of more biologically plausible artificial neural networks.

The paper could be strengthened by addressing several minor points. First, the sensitivity of results to the choice of γ=0.04 in Eq. (3) should be discussed—while the authors note this parameter maps to observed firing rates, it would be valuable to quantify how results change with different γ values. Second, the motivation for using a log-normal distribution to generate memory patterns (line 115) needs elaboration; the authors should explain why this distribution better captures neural activity patterns than alternatives like Gaussian. Third, while the authors note that fixed points are achieved approximately (not exactly), they should quantify the degree of violation (e.g., average |dv/dt| at target states) and discuss its implications for memory stability. Fourth, the paper lacks discussion of memory capacity—the number of patterns that can be reliably stored—which is a critical aspect of associative memory models. Finally, while the authors note that Dale's law is biologically observed, they do not address whether this constraint enhances computational power or is merely a consequence of evolutionary/biophysical constraints.

The authors' approach of treating inhibitory neurons as optimization variables is particularly insightful and well-justified. Their explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is compelling, as it emerges naturally from the network dynamics without additional assumptions. The paper's connection to experimental findings (e.g., the widespread reduction in neural variability after stimulus onset) significantly enhances its relevance to neuroscience.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors successfully bridge theoretical neuroscience with experimental observations through a novel, biologically constrained framework. While the paper could benefit from addressing the minor points noted above, these do not detract from the paper's strong contribution. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental question in neural computation with important implications for understanding memory mechanisms. I believe this paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
