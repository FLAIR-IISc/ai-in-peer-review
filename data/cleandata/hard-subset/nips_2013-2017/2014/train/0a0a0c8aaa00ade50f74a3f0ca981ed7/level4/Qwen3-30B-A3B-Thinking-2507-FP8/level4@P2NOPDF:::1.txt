

Summary: The authors introduce a biologically plausible model of auto-associative memory in a rate-based neural network, addressing key limitations in prior work. Existing models often violate biological constraints, particularly Dale's Law (neurons being strictly excitatory or inhibitory) and the requirement that memory encoding avoids units operating at saturation or in binary mode. The authors train the network using a gradient descent procedure constrained by a recent method ensuring stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points. Simulations demonstrate that the trained fixed points exhibit slow, stable dynamics (approaching but not fully reaching fixed points), and the network successfully encodes memories robustly under perturbation and stochastic noise. Crucially, the trained network reproduces two experimentally observed cortical features: a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails, and balanced average excitatory and inhibitory input per unit. The authors identify these features as essential for robust memory performance.

Review: This is a clear, novel contribution of significant interest to the community. The authors provide a concise yet comprehensive review of prior work on auto-associative neural networks, effectively highlighting key advances and shortcomings. The network architecture, training procedure, and results are presented with exceptional clarity, supported by well-chosen figures that efficiently convey core findings. The analysis of performance under network noise effectively underscores the robustness of the proposed training method.

The manuscript would benefit from a clearer justification of the chosen training procedure relative to the stated objectives. While the authors note that prior methods exist for enforcing Dale's Law or achieving graded responses (with graded responses being particularly challenging), the text does not explicitly explain why this specific method was selected or why it proves particularly effective. The authors state that the procedure achieves non-saturating units but do not discuss whether alternative approaches could yield comparable results or why this method is uniquely suited for this task. A brief discussion addressing this would strengthen the work.

Additionally, given the extensive prior literature on auto-associative networks, a brief comparison of the trained network's memory capacity (number of memories as a function of network size) against established models would be valuable. Such a comparison, even if only mentioned in a sentence or two, would provide important context for the community regarding the model's scalability and efficiency relative to existing solutions.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The model successfully integrates critical biological constraints while achieving robust memory performance, and the writing is exceptionally clear. The minor suggestions above would further enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance.
