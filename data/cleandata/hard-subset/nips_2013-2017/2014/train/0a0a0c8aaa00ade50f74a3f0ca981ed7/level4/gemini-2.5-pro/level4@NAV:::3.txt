This work revisits the classic problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a novel perspective that brings significant new insights to this well-established field. The authors correctly identify several limitations in prior research, including the frequent disregard for biological constraints like Dale's law, the reliance on binary representations in rate-based models, and the use of non-sparse memory patterns. Although these limitations have been addressed in isolation in previous studies, the key contribution here is a unified strategy that tackles all of them simultaneously.

Specifically, the paper leverages a modern control-theoretic framework, employing spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that successfully overcome these challenges. Their method involves optimizing a cost function via gradient descent, which carefully balances three distinct objectives. The first objective promotes the desired memories as fixed points, the second ensures the stability of these fixed points, and the third encourages small synaptic weights to prevent neuronal saturation. A key aspect of their model is the direct enforcement of Dale's principle within the weight parameterization, which not only addresses the aforementioned shortcomings but also results in a network with a natural balance between excitation and inhibition. It is noteworthy that single-neuron firing rate saturation is not explicitly enforced but instead emerges as a natural consequence of the optimization process. Crucially, the model assigns memory implementation exclusively to the excitatory population—consistent with their role in projecting to other brain regions—while treating the inhibitory neurons as optimizable variables that shape the network dynamics. The proposed framework is validated through numerical simulations that confirm the network's associative memory properties and demonstrate its robustness to noise. Beyond its core memory function, the model also accounts for experimental observations of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, which is explained by convergence to the nearest attractor (though it should be noted this may be a general property of attractor networks with baseline spontaneous activity).

In conclusion, this is a well-articulated and compelling paper. It applies novel control-theoretic methods to a foundational problem in neural computation, thereby providing a clear demonstration of how a biologically plausible associative memory might operate. While the authors do not present a biologically plausible learning rule at this stage, they acknowledge this limitation and point towards promising recent work that could lead to such a mechanism. The conceptualization of inhibitory neurons as an optimizable resource is particularly insightful and effective. Overall, this paper represents a clear, concise, and significant contribution to the field of neural computation.

A few minor points for consideration:
*   The choice of γ=0.04 in equation (3) should be accompanied by a discussion of the model's sensitivity to this parameter, even if this sensitivity is expected to be low.
*   Please provide a justification for selecting a log-normal distribution for the memory states (line 115).
*   The cost function in (4) could result in solutions where the memories are not exact fixed points. The authors should quantify the magnitude of this deviation and discuss its implications.
*   The statements on lines 254-256 are unclear. Please rephrase for clarity.
*   The paper does not address the storage capacity of the network. A discussion of this critical computational aspect, even if preliminary, would strengthen the work.
*   It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the potential computational advantages of enforcing Dale's law. For instance, does this principle enhance computational power under certain constraints, or is it primarily a reflection of evolutionary and biophysical limitations? A brief engagement with this topic would be welcome.

This is a well-written paper that uses novel control-theoretic tools to address a classic problem, demonstrating a compelling model for a biologically plausible associative memory.
