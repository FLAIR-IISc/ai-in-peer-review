This manuscript proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for building functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. The authors directly optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. The resulting networks operate in the balanced regime, are robust to corruptions of the memory cue as well as to ongoing noise, and incidentally explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset that is ubiquitously observed in sensory and motor cortices.

The manuscript is well-written, and the model and training procedure are easy to follow. The presentation of the results is clear, and the combination of biological constraints going into the model and the resulting features after training make this paper an interesting contribution to the understanding of attractor neural networks.

The authors train a rate-based neuronal network to hold graded and non-saturated attractors in the synaptic weights and inhibitory activities. Constraints imposed on the synapses and neurons for biological plausibility lead to a balance of excitation and inhibition on the network level. The manuscript provides a thorough discussion of the model's limitations and potential extensions, including the applicability of the method to understanding spiking memory networks and the possibility of local synaptic learning rules achieving the stabilization objective.

The quality of the manuscript is high, with well-supported claims and a clear presentation of the results. The authors provide a detailed description of the model and the training procedure, making it easy to reproduce the results. The manuscript is well-organized, and the figures are informative and well-designed.

The originality of the manuscript lies in its ability to overcome several shortcomings of previous attractor models, namely the violation of Dale's law and the saturation of activities in the memory states. The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework that incorporates biological constraints and results in a balance of excitation and inhibition in the attractor states.

The significance of the manuscript is high, as it provides new insights into the neural substrate of memory and the mechanisms that support the storage and retrieval of memories. The results have implications for our understanding of the neural basis of memory and the development of new models of memory circuits.
