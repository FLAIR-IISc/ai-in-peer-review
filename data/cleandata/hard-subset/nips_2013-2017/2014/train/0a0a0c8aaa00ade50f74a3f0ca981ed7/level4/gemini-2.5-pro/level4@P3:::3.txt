This paper revisits the classic problem of associative memory in recurrent neural networks, offering a fresh and powerful perspective. The authors set out to build a model that overcomes several key limitations of previous work, which often ignored biological constraints such as Dale's law, relied on binary memory representations, or resulted in unrealistically high firing rates. While many of these issues have been addressed in isolation, this work presents a unified framework that tackles them simultaneously.

The core contribution is a novel, control-theoretic approach to network construction. Instead of deriving synaptic weights from a local learning rule, the authors directly optimize the network parameters to achieve a desired function. Specifically, they formulate a cost function that forces a set of pre-defined, analog activity patterns to become stable fixed points of the network dynamics. This cost function elegantly balances three objectives: 1) ensuring the target patterns are fixed points by minimizing the rate of change of neural activity at these states; 2) ensuring the stability of these fixed points by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable proxy for the largest real part of the Jacobian's eigenvalues; and 3) maintaining reasonable synaptic weights via a regularization term.

Crucially, the optimization is performed on a network of distinct excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations, strictly enforcing Dale's law. In an insightful design choice, the excitatory neuron activities define the memory patterns, while the corresponding inhibitory neuron activities are treated as free parameters to be optimized, allowing the inhibitory population to flexibly support the stability of each memory. The resulting networks successfully store and recall graded (analog) memories, are robust to noise, and operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime where low firing rates emerge naturally without an explicit saturation mechanism. The authors further demonstrate that their model reproduces the well-documented phenomenon of stimulus-induced variability reduction observed in cortical circuits.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The application of a modern, robust control-theoretic optimization technique (minimizing the SSA) to the problem of constructing attractor networks is highly original. It provides a principled way to engineer stable, biologically constrained networks, moving beyond the limitations of classical approaches.

2.  **Biological Plausibility:** A major strength is the successful integration of multiple, critical biological constraints into a single, functional model. The network respects Dale's law, exhibits a tight balance of excitation and inhibition, maintains low and graded firing rates, and develops a sparse connectivity structure—all of which are hallmarks of cortical circuits.

3.  **Significant Results:** The paper serves as a compelling proof of concept that complex, analog memory patterns can be stably embedded in E-I networks. The demonstration that stability is achieved through a precisely tuned E/I balance for each individual pattern is a particularly important insight. The ability of the model to also account for stimulus-driven variability reduction strengthens its connection to experimental neuroscience.

4.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and technically sound. The methodology is explained with sufficient detail, and the results are presented convincingly through well-designed numerical simulations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or expanded upon to strengthen the contribution further.

1.  **Absence of a Learning Mechanism:** The current framework is a normative one, using a global optimizer to design the network. This is a valid and powerful approach for understanding what is computationally possible. However, the most significant open question is how such a solution could be achieved through biologically plausible, local learning rules. A more in-depth discussion of this gap and potential bridges to synaptic plasticity mechanisms (beyond the brief mention in the Discussion) would be valuable.

2.  **Storage Capacity:** The paper demonstrates successful storage of 30 patterns in a network of 150 neurons (a load of 0.2), but a systematic analysis of the network's storage capacity is missing. How does performance degrade as the number of stored memories increases? How does capacity scale with network size or the statistical properties of the memories? Even a preliminary analysis of this crucial computational aspect would be a welcome addition.

3.  **Fixed-Point Approximation:** The authors rightly note that their optimization procedure results in "slow points" rather than perfect fixed points, as the `dv/dt` term does not reach exactly zero. It would be helpful to quantify the severity of this violation. For example, what is the characteristic timescale of the drift away from the target patterns? This is important for understanding the model's suitability for long-term memory maintenance.

4.  **Parameter Justification:** The motivation for some parameter choices could be clearer. For instance, the choice of a log-normal distribution for the memory patterns is justified by its resemblance to observed firing rate distributions, which is reasonable, but the model's performance on other distributions is not discussed. Similarly, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the gain function parameter `γ` could be briefly addressed.

5.  **Role of Dale's Law:** The model successfully operates under the constraint of Dale's law. It would be interesting for the authors to speculate in the discussion whether this separation of E and I populations confers any computational advantages within their framework (e.g., enhanced stability, greater capacity, or more flexible control) or if it is primarily a biological constraint that the optimization successfully navigates.

**Summary:**

This is a clear, well-written, and technically impressive paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation with novel tools and provides significant new insights. By using a control-theoretic framework, the authors demonstrate how a biologically plausible associative memory network capable of storing analog patterns can be constructed. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the approach and the successful integration of multiple biological constraints—far outweigh the weaknesses, which mostly represent promising avenues for future research. This work is an important contribution to computational neuroscience and is highly likely to inspire future studies on the principles of memory and stability in neural circuits. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
