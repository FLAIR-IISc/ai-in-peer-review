

Summary: The authors present a rate-based neural network model of auto-associative memory that adheres to multiple biologically plausible constraints. Prior models have overlooked key features of real neural networks, including strict adherence to Dale’s Law (where neurons are either purely excitatory or inhibitory) and the avoidance of units that operate at saturation or in binary mode. Memories are encoded via synaptic updates guided by gradient descent, with stability enforced using a recent method ensuring the linearization around fixed points remains stable. Simulations demonstrate that trained fixed points exhibit slow dynamics (near, but not exactly, fixed points) and stability as intended. The model also reproduces two experimentally observed cortical features: synaptic weight distributions centered at zero with heavy tails, and balanced excitatory-inhibitory input per unit. The authors argue these features are critical for robust memory retrieval under perturbations and stochastic noise.  

Review: This paper offers a clear, novel contribution that will interest the computational neuroscience community. The authors effectively contextualize their work by reviewing prior training methods for auto-associative tasks, highlighting key limitations of existing approaches. The network architecture and training procedure are well-explained, and figures efficiently convey core results. The demonstration of robustness against network noise further underscores the effectiveness of their method.  

A minor clarification would strengthen the paper: The authors achieve non-saturating units and Dale’s Law compliance but do not explicitly justify why their specific training procedure (based on stable linearization) was chosen over alternatives that have also addressed these constraints. Previous work has employed other techniques for Dale’s Law enforcement or graded responses—particularly for the latter, which is notably challenging. The paper should clarify whether this method was selected due to its unique suitability for these goals or if its success stems from other properties. Could simpler or alternative procedures achieve comparable results?  

Additionally, while beyond the current scope, a brief comparison of memory capacity (number of retrievable memories relative to network size) against prior models would provide valuable context for the community. Such a comparison would directly address a key practical concern in auto-associative memory systems.  

Overall, this is a well-executed study that advances the modeling of biologically plausible auto-associative memory. The technical approach is sound, and the results align with experimental observations of cortical networks. The authors’ method effectively bridges a gap between theoretical models and biological constraints.
