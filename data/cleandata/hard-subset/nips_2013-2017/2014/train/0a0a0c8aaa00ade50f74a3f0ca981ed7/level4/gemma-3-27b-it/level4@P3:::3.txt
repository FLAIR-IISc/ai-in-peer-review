## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper returns to the fundamental problem of associative memory in neural networks, offering a fresh perspective and significant insights. The authors identify key limitations in prior work – neglecting biological constraints like Dale’s law, employing binary memory representations in rate-based models, and relying on non-sparse representations – and present a unified strategy to address them. Specifically, they leverage a recent control-theoretic approach, utilizing spectral bounding techniques, to construct networks that satisfy these biological constraints. Their method involves optimizing a cost function balancing fixed-point attraction, dynamical stability, and weight sparsity through gradient descent. By directly enforcing Dale’s principle via weight parameterization, they overcome shortcomings of previous models, resulting in networks with naturally balanced excitation and inhibition. Notably, saturation is not explicitly imposed but emerges from the optimization process. The authors treat inhibitory neurons as auxiliary variables optimized alongside the connectivity, a conceptually interesting approach. They validate their results through numerical simulations, demonstrating robustness to noise and explaining the observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset – a phenomenon common in sensory and motor cortices, attributed to convergence to the nearest attractor. 

In summary, this is a clear and well-written paper that tackles a classic problem in neural computation using novel control-theoretic tools. It demonstrates how a biologically plausible associative memory might function. While a biologically plausible learning mechanism isn't proposed, the authors acknowledge recent work suggesting potential avenues. The utilization of inhibitory neurons as optimized resources is a particularly insightful aspect. Overall, the paper represents a significant and valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The application of control-theoretic methods (specifically, Smoothed Spectral Abscissa optimization) to the problem of building biologically realistic associative memories is a significant novelty.
* **Addresses Key Limitations:** The paper directly confronts and overcomes several limitations of previous models, including Dale’s law, binary representations, and lack of sparsity.
* **Biological Plausibility:** The resulting networks exhibit characteristics aligned with biological observations, such as balanced excitation and inhibition, graded activity, and robustness to noise.
* **Explanatory Power:** The model provides a plausible explanation for the observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus presentation.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (1) is not justified with a sensitivity analysis. Exploring the impact of this parameter on network performance would strengthen the findings.
* **Motivation for Log-Normal Distribution:** The rationale behind using a log-normal distribution for the memory states (line 115) is not sufficiently explained. A more detailed justification or comparison to other distribution choices would be beneficial.
* **Fixed-Point Violation Quantification:** While acknowledged, the potential deviation from exact fixed-points (due to the cost function formulation) isn't adequately quantified. Providing a measure of this violation and its potential impact on recall fidelity is crucial.
* **Clarification Needed:** Lines 254-256 require clarification for better understanding. The logic connecting the statements in this section is not immediately apparent.
* **Capacity Analysis:** The paper lacks a discussion of memory capacity. Even a preliminary analysis or estimation of capacity would be valuable.
* **Dale's Law Benefits:**  The potential computational advantages of enforcing Dale's law are not explored. While acknowledging it may be a biophysical constraint, investigating potential computational benefits would be a worthwhile addition.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Technical Contribution:** The paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach to building biologically plausible associative memories.
* **Strong Results:** The model demonstrates impressive performance, exhibiting robustness and explaining relevant experimental findings.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents complex concepts.
* **Potential Impact:**  The work has the potential to influence future research in neural network modeling and our understanding of memory mechanisms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Capacity Analysis:** The absence of a discussion on capacity is a notable omission.
* **Minor Clarifications Needed:**  Addressing the points raised in the "Weaknesses" section regarding parameter sensitivity, distribution justification, and specific explanations would further strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Biological Validation:** While biologically *plausible*, the model lacks direct comparison to more detailed biological data beyond the trial-to-trial variability observation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and addressable. I believe the paper’s strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, I would suggest including a discussion of capacity, a sensitivity analysis of the gamma parameter, a clearer justification for the log-normal distribution, a quantification of the fixed-point violation, and clarification of lines 254-256.
