- Presents a unified control-theoretic framework that integrates biological constraints (Dale's law, analog memory patterns, low firing rates) for associative memory networks.  
- Formulates memory storage as an optimization minimizing a cost that combines a drift-minimization term, Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) stability term, and synaptic regularization.  
- Enforces Dale's law via weight-parameterization and treats inhibitory neuron activities as auxiliary variables to be optimized for stabilizing excitatory memories.  
- Numerical simulations show robust autoassociative recall from noisy cues, dynamic E/I balance, sparse connectivity, and stimulus-onset quenching of trial-to-trial variability.  
- Strength: Novel application of modern control theory (SSA) to design biologically realistic neural networks.  
- Strength: Demonstrates proof of concept for simultaneously satisfying multiple physiological constraints often treated separately.  
- Strength: Treating inhibitory activity as an optimizable resource is an insightful and compelling idea.  
- Strength: Manuscript is clearly written, well-organized, and results support the claims.  
- Weakness: Optimized states are “slow points” rather than exact fixed points; residual drift magnitude and long-term stability are not quantified.  
- Weakness: Storage capacity of the network is not analyzed; discussion or preliminary scaling analysis is missing.  
- Weakness: Motivation for using log-normal pattern distributions should be more explicitly justified with in-vivo references.  
- Weakness: Sensitivity to the gain parameter γ is not addressed and should be commented on.  
- Weakness: Explanation for higher recall performance of the baseline state is unclear—statistical artifact versus network initialization needs clarification.  
- Weakness: The potential computational role or functional advantage of enforcing Dale’s law is not discussed; authors could speculate on whether it helps or merely constrains optimization.
