- Presents a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent E/I rate networks as robust analog autoassociative memories under biological constraints.  
- Models memory storage as an optimization over synaptic weights and inhibitory steady-states to make target analog patterns stable fixed points.  
- Optimization minimizes three terms: fixed-point error (dv/dt), stability via Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and weight regularization.  
- Demonstrates storage and robust recall of graded (log-normal) activity patterns from noisy cues.  
- Emergent properties include tight E/I balance, low non-saturating firing rates, sparse heavy-tailed synaptic weights, and stimulus-induced variability quenching.  
- Strength: Novel methodology—using SSA to directly optimize multi-attractor stability is original and powerful.  
- Strength: High biological realism—enforces Dale’s law, graded activity, and avoids rate saturation; realistic features emerge rather than being imposed.  
- Strength: Strong results and mechanistic insight—shows why E/I balance stabilizes graded firing and connects to cortical variability quenching.  
- Strength: Clear and well-organized manuscript with detailed methods and persuasive figures.  
- Weakness: Learning algorithm is biologically implausible—global, offline L-BFGS optimization requiring full system/Jacobian access.  
- Weakness: Scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity unclear—experiments on 150 neurons/30 patterns and several heuristically chosen hyperparameters need further analysis.  
- Weakness: Optimized states leave small residual dv/dt (“slow points”); implications for long-term stability are not fully discussed.  
- Overall: Strong, original proof-of-concept with high significance for theory and neuroscience despite non-biological learning, and a clear candidate for acceptance.
