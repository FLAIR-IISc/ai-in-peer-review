This paper introduces a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model that is constrained by biological plausibility, incorporating key features such as Dale's Law and non-saturating graded units. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints. The model is trained using a combination of gradient descent and a recent method that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear literature review, architecture/training description, and effective figures illustrating results. The manuscript is novel and of likely strong interest to the community.

The main strengths of the paper are:

1. The authors provide a comprehensive review of previous work on auto-associative memory models, highlighting the limitations of existing models and the need for a more biologically plausible approach.
2. The proposed model is novel and well-motivated, incorporating key features of biological neural networks.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model through simulations, showing that trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow (near-fixed-point) dynamics.
4. The trained networks display biologically consistent features, such as synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron.
5. The trained networks are robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, demonstrating reliable auto-associative recall.

However, there are some potential weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. The authors could clarify why the chosen training procedure was selected and whether it is particularly suited to enforce graded, non-saturating memories versus other methods.
2. It would be useful to discuss whether alternative training procedures could achieve similar results or explain mechanistically why this method succeeds.
3. A comparison of the trained network performance to previous models in terms of capacity (number of memories vs network size) could provide additional insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to auto-associative memory modeling, incorporating key features of biological neural networks. The use of a control-theoretic framework and the combination of gradient descent with a method that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points are new and interesting contributions.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance our understanding of the neural substrate of memory and to provide a more biologically plausible approach to auto-associative memory modeling. The results presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model and its potential for robust and reliable auto-associative recall.

Overall, the paper is well-written, novel, and of likely strong interest to the community. With some minor revisions to address the suggested areas for improvement, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and cognitive science.

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to auto-associative memory modeling.
