- Revisits associative memory in excitatory/inhibitory networks while enforcing biological constraints (Dale's law, non-binary representations, sparse memories).  
- Introduces a unified strategy using control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques.  
- Proposes a three-term cost function targeting fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation; enforces Dale’s principle in weight parameterization.  
- Uses excitatory neurons to encode memories and treats inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources.  
- Single-neuron saturation emerges naturally from optimization despite not being explicitly enforced.  
- Numerical simulations show associative memory behavior, noise robustness, and reduced trial-to-trial variability via convergence to nearest attractors.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and an important contribution.  
- Concern: sensitivity to the choice gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (3) is not discussed or justified.  
- Concern: assumption of log-normal distribution for memory states lacks motivation and exploration of alternatives.  
- Concern: authors do not quantify how much the cost function (4) violates exact fixed-point constraints or the impact on performance.  
- Concern: unclear wording on lines 254–256 about stabilization via a “tight dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition” and its relation to the cost function.  
- Concern: paper lacks at least a preliminary discussion of memory capacity and scalability with number of memories.  
- Strength/Originality: novel use of control-theoretic spectral-bounding and addressing often-ignored biological constraints.  
- Weakness: no biologically plausible learning rule presented (only hinted).  
- Recommendation: accept conditionally—authors should address the specific issues raised (gamma sensitivity, distribution choice, fixed-point constraint violations, clarification of E/I balance, and memory capacity).
