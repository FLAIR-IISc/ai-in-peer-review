- Introduces a novel control-theoretic framework for biologically plausible associative memory networks satisfying Dale’s law, non-binary representations, and non-saturated firing rates.  
- Directly optimizes synaptic weights to stabilize arbitrary analog memory patterns as fixed points using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization for local stability.  
- Enforces Dale’s law through weight parameterization and treats inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources rather than fixed elements.  
- Shows that saturation avoidance emerges naturally from the optimization, removing the need for explicit saturation constraints.  
- Numerical results demonstrate robust memory recall, noise tolerance, and reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset consistent with cortical data.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and advances beyond binary Hopfield models and recent Dale’s-law-compliant LIF networks.  
- Strength: unified handling of multiple biological constraints fills a major gap in prior work.  
- Strength: elegant and effective use of SSA for stability optimization with strong empirical validation.  
- Weakness: lacks a biologically plausible learning rule, offering only a brief hint.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to the choice of γ = 0.04 needs justification and robustness analysis.  
- Weakness: motivation for using a log-normal distribution of memory states (Fig. 1a) requires deeper explanation.  
- Weakness: paper should quantify fixed-point violations (non-zero ‖dv/dt‖) and clarify related statements (lines 254–256).  
- Weakness: needs preliminary analysis of memory capacity scaling with network size.  
- Weakness: should discuss computational versus evolutionary explanations for imposing Dale’s law.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and novel; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the specified technical points.
