- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to build auto-associative E/I networks that obey Dale’s law, have recurrent connectivity, graded non-saturating responses, low firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Uses a rate-based model with rectified quadratic gain; memories are arbitrary analog patterns on excitatory neurons.  
- Enforces Dale’s law by parameterizing weights with a softplus transform and optimizes inhibitory steady-state activities per memory.  
- Optimization minimizes squared dv/dt at targets plus the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian, with L2 regularization.  
- Simulations show learned memories are locally stable (negative SSA) and near fixed points.  
- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues with performance comparable to an ideal observer.  
- Shows approximate single-neuron E/I balance across attractors, heavy-tailed weight distributions, robustness to ongoing noise, and variability quenching after stimulus onset.  
- Positions contribution relative to Hopfield/binary-memory models, saturation/global-inhibition E/I models, and bump/spatial models; leverages robust control methods (SSA) as novel.  
- Technical soundness: careful formulation, appropriate use of SSA, clean Dale’s-law parameterization, and empirical results supporting main claims.  
- Main limitation: only local stability is enforced; basin sizes, global dynamics, and exact fixed-pointness (dv/dt ≈ 0 but not exactly zero) are not analyzed.  
- Capacity is reported only for one network (m/N ≈ 0.2) with no systematic scaling or comparison to prior graded-memory capacities.  
- Learning procedure is offline and nonlocal (BFGS), lacking a biologically plausible plasticity mechanism.  
- Connectivity is effectively dense (many small weights); effects of true sparsity or fixed connection probability are not tested.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (ε, ηs, ηF) and initialization is not systematically explored.  
- Spiking implementation is discussed but not demonstrated; portability to spiking remains unvalidated.  
- Clarity: manuscript is well written, well organized, with effective figures and appropriate related-work context; minor clarifications suggested.  
- Originality: novel application of SSA/robust-control optimization to construct graded, biologically constrained attractor networks.  
- Significance: useful methodological advance offering a recipe for analog attractors under biological constraints; impact depends on scaling and spiking validation.  
- Pros: satisfies multiple biological constraints, introduces SSA-based stability objective, yields robust recall and variability quenching, and produces biologically plausible weight statistics.  
- Cons: limited capacity/sparsity analysis, offline/nonlocal optimization, only local stability guarantees, no spiking instantiation, and unreported hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze capacity scaling and basins (retrieval vs load and cue noise), ablate SSA vs alternatives, test sparse connectivity, provide spiking instantiation with matched f–I curves, explore alternative/online training methods, clarify inhibitory fixed-point optimization, and release code/pseudocode.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — novel and compelling approach but would be strengthened by capacity scaling, sparsity tests, and spiking validation.
