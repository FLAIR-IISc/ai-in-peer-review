## Review: Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons

This paper presents a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model constrained by biological plausibility, specifically incorporating Dale’s Law and utilizing graded, non-saturating neuronal units. The authors tackle the longstanding challenge of building biologically realistic memory networks by framing memory storage as a control-theoretic problem – forcing desired activity patterns to be stable fixed points through optimized synaptic weights, leveraging recent advances in robust control (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa). Simulations demonstrate stable fixed points with slow dynamics, biologically consistent weight distributions (zero-centered with long tails), approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance, and robustness to perturbations. 

The work builds upon decades of theoretical neuroscience concerning auto-associative memory, directly addressing limitations in previous models like the original Hopfield network (binary memories, violation of Dale’s Law) and more recent efforts (saturation issues, global inhibition). The approach distinguishes itself by simultaneously addressing multiple biological constraints within a unified framework, moving beyond individual fixes for specific issues. The clear presentation of the architecture, training procedure, and compelling visualizations of results—demonstrating both synaptic weight distributions and recall dynamics—are significant strengths. The observation of reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset, mirroring empirical findings, is particularly noteworthy and strengthens the biological relevance.

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed justification for the chosen training procedure. While the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa is well-motivated for stability, a more explicit comparison to alternative optimization methods (e.g., directly optimizing the spectral abscissa, or using different learning rules) would strengthen the argument that this approach is particularly well-suited for enforcing graded, non-saturating memories. Specifically, a discussion of *why* this method succeeds where others might fail to achieve stable, graded recall would be valuable. 

Furthermore, while not necessarily within the immediate scope, a comparison of the trained network’s capacity (number of storable memories relative to network size) against existing models would provide a valuable benchmark. This would help contextualize the model’s performance and highlight potential trade-offs between biological realism and storage capacity. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and biologically plausible approach to auto-associative memory.
*   Clear and well-written manuscript with effective visualizations.
*   Strong simulation results demonstrating stability, robustness, and biologically realistic features.
*   Potential for significant impact in the field of computational neuroscience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of why the chosen training method is optimal compared to alternatives.
*   Lack of comparison to existing models in terms of storage capacity.

**Overall Recommendation:** Accept. Despite the minor weaknesses, the originality, clarity, and promising results presented in this paper make it a strong contribution to the field and worthy of presentation at the conference. Addressing the suggestions in a revised version would further strengthen the work.
