## Review: Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons

This paper addresses the longstanding problem of building biologically plausible attractor networks for memory storage and recall. The authors tackle key limitations of prior models – violation of Dale’s Law and activity saturation – by formulating memory storage as a control problem, optimizing network parameters to establish desired activity patterns as stable fixed points. They employ a novel approach leveraging the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to ensure robust stability during gradient descent training. The resulting network exhibits balanced excitation and inhibition, achieving graded, non-saturated attractor states closely matching experimental synaptic distributions and demonstrating robustness to noise.

The work builds on decades of research in attractor networks, starting with the Hopfield model (Hopfield, 1982) and subsequent attempts to address its limitations (Treves, 1986; Battaglia & Treves, 2002). Notably, it surpasses recent models (Latham & Nirenberg, 2003; Roudi & Latham, 2007) by explicitly incorporating both Dale’s Law and realistic, non-saturating activity dynamics. The authors’ use of a control-theoretic framework and the SSA for stability optimization represents a significant methodological advancement, distinguishing this work from previous approaches that relied on approximations or limited stability guarantees. The finding of intrinsic sparsity arising from the optimization process and the demonstration of variability reduction following stimulus onset are also compelling connections to experimental observations (Churchland et al., 2010).

**Strengths:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents a complex model and its results. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the experimental validation through comparisons to biological data (synaptic distributions, E/I balance) is strong. The originality lies in the combination of biological constraints with a robust control-theoretic optimization framework, leading to a demonstrably more plausible and functional attractor network. The significance is high; this work provides a substantial step forward in understanding the neural substrate of memory and offers a pathway for building more biologically realistic computational models. 

**Weaknesses:** While the results are compelling, the computational cost of the optimization process remains a potential limitation. Scaling this approach to significantly larger networks will require further investigation. Furthermore, the model employs a rate-based formulation, potentially limiting its direct applicability to spiking neural networks – although the authors address this to some extent in the discussion.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental problem in neuroscience with a novel and effective approach.
*   Combines biological constraints with a rigorous mathematical framework.
*   Demonstrates strong results, aligning with experimental observations.
*   Exceedingly clear and well-written manuscript.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of the optimization process.
*   Reliance on a rate-based model, potentially limiting its direct translation to spiking networks.

**Overall:** I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a significant contribution to the field, offering a promising new direction for understanding memory and building biologically realistic neural networks. The strengths of the work far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the clarity of the presentation ensures its impact on the broader research community.
