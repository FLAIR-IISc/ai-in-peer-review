This paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based recurrent networks of excitatory and inhibitory (E–I) neurons that store and robustly retrieve multiple analog memories as stable attractors, while obeying physiological constraints (Dale’s law, low firing rates, balanced E/I). The authors parameterize synaptic weights to enforce Dale’s law, define target analog patterns for the excitatory population, and treat inhibitory steady-state activities as auxiliary variables determined by optimization. They minimize a composite objective: (i) the drift ‖dv/dt‖2 at each target pattern (to make it a fixed point), (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target (to enforce local stability), plus (iii) an L2 weight penalty. Networks trained this way store tens of graded patterns with non-saturating rates, exhibit strong E/I balance across attractors, recall reliably from noisy cues and under ongoing noise, and show a reduction in trial-by-trial variability upon stimulus onset. Synaptic weights acquire a heavy-tailed, sparse distribution; pruning small weights leaves recall largely intact.

Relation to prior work: Classical Hopfield-type models [1,2] often use binary patterns and/or symmetric connectivity that violates Dale’s law. Subsequent graded pattern analyses [3–6] gave capacity predictions but typically assumed instantaneous global inhibition or did not directly ensure robust stable dynamics. E–I network models with LIF neurons [7–9] often relied on saturation or global inhibition, effectively inducing binary attractors. Spatially structured bump attractors [10,11] impose strong geometric constraints; spike-timing based models [12] lacked explicit inhibition. This work’s novelty lies in directly optimizing robust stability (via SSA [13,14]) in an E–I network with a rectified power-law transfer function [15,24–26], yielding balanced, graded, low-rate attractors that also reproduce variability quenching [21–23].

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and well motivated. The SSA-based objective provides a practical surrogate for stability, and the drift term addresses fixed-point consistency. Empirical results support the main claims: successful recall from substantial cue corruption, robustness to ongoing noise, balanced currents across attractors, and heavy-tailed weight distributions matching experimental observations [19,20]. The demonstration that pruning small weights barely degrades performance strengthens the interpretation that a minority of strong synapses dominate recall. However, several aspects would benefit from deeper analysis: (i) fixed points are “nearby” because ‖dv/dt‖ is small but not zero; quantifying the distance to the true fixed point (e.g., by Newton iterations) and reporting actual spectral abscissae would tighten the claims; (ii) capacity is shown for n=150, m=30 (≈0.2) but the scaling with network size, pattern statistics, and hyperparameters is not analyzed; (iii) the nonconvex optimization may introduce sensitivity to initialization/ε/ηs/ηF—ablation/sensitivity analyses would improve confidence; (iv) global dynamical landscape (spurious attractors, basins) is not characterized.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and places the work in context. The objective, parameterization enforcing Dale’s law, and SSA rationale are described plainly. Figures are informative. Suggestions: include pseudocode or algorithm boxes; report computational cost per iteration and convergence diagnostics; explicitly note whether hi is uniform and how external inputs are used at recall; provide more detail on SSA gradient computation and conditioning.

Originality: Using robust control tools (SSA) to directly enforce stability of multiple analog attractors in a biologically constrained E–I network is original and timely. The joint satisfaction of Dale’s law, graded non-saturating activity, balanced currents, and variability quenching within one framework is a distinctive contribution relative to prior associative memory models.

Significance: The approach advances our understanding of how cortical-like networks could implement analog attractor memories under realistic constraints and may provide a general recipe for constructing stable, balanced dynamics for other computations (e.g., movement generation [14]). It meaningfully connects dynamical stability, balance, and variability quenching. Broader impact would be strengthened by capacity scaling, robustness, and spiking instantiations.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean formulation of memory storage as fixed-point plus stability optimization under Dale’s law.
- Demonstrated graded, low-rate, non-saturating attractors with tight E/I balance and variability quenching.
- Robust recall under cue corruption and ongoing noise; performance approaches an “ideal observer.”
- Emergent heavy-tailed, sparse-like synaptic distributions consistent with cortical data; recall largely supported by a minority of strong synapses.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Provide evidence that target states correspond to true fixed points (e.g., explicit root-finding; report SA as well as SSA).
- Capacity and basin-size scaling with n and pattern load; compare to classical results [3–6].
- Sensitivity to ε in SSA, regularization strength, initialization; report variability across runs.
- Analyze spurious attractors and confusion matrix across memories, not just success rates.
- Explore structural sparsity constraints (fixed in-degree) or L1 penalties; quantify performance under systematic pruning.
- Compare with alternative training paradigms (e.g., FORCE-like stabilization) and with LIF/spiking realizations using matched gain [24–26].
- Clarify biological plausibility of optimizing inhibitory steady-state rates during training; emphasize that runtime dynamics are fully determined by fixed weights and inputs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-executed integration of robust control with biologically grounded constraints.
- Clear empirical demonstrations of balanced analog attractors and variability quenching.
- Potentially influential methodology for constructing robust cortical computations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scaling analysis and lack of theoretical capacity bounds.
- Reliance on SSA without direct verification of actual spectral abscissae and fixed points.
- Evaluation on relatively small networks with few memories; limited comparisons to alternative training schemes and spiking models.

Overall, this is a strong and thought-provoking paper that makes a substantive contribution to the design and analysis of biologically plausible analog memory networks. With additional analyses on scaling, fixed-point verification, and robustness, it would be even more compelling.
