- Paper revisits autoassociative memory using a control-theoretic approach to directly optimize recurrent E–I rate networks under Dale’s law.  
- Objective combines a fixed-point term (minimize dv/dt at targets), a stability term using smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA), and an L2 weight penalty.  
- Excitatory units encode analog memories; inhibitory units are auxiliary variables optimized per memory to ensure stability and E/I balance.  
- Learned E/I balance prevents single-neuron saturation despite supralinear, non-saturating activations.  
- Simulations show robust recall from noisy cues, resilience to ongoing noise, sparse effective synapses, and trial-to-trial variability quenching.  
- Advances over classic models by enforcing Dale’s law, supporting analog (non-binary) memories, and avoiding hard output saturation.  
- Integrates robust control (SSA) and stabilized supralinear network analyses into a general, pattern-agnostic framework.  
- Strength: conceptual advance — unified control-theoretic strategy enforcing fixed-point existence, stability, and biological constraints.  
- Strength: technical novelty — first practical use of SSA for stability optimization in high-dimensional recurrent networks.  
- Strength: biological realism — strict Dale reparameterization, explicit E/I populations, balanced regime, and analog memories.  
- Strength: empirical validation — thorough numerical demonstrations of recall, robustness, sparsity, and variability quenching.  
- Strength: clear exposition and careful literature positioning.  
- Weakness: learning rule plausibility — optimization is global/offline; no concrete local synaptic learning rule provided.  
- Weakness: capacity and scaling — only one example (m/n ≈ 0.2); no systematic scaling with network size, nI/nE, or pattern statistics.  
- Weakness: fixed-point approximation — targets have dv/dt ≠ 0 but consequences and magnitudes are not quantified.  
- Weakness: distributional assumptions — memories are log-normal but sensitivity to other pattern statistics is untested.  
- Quality: technically sound with appropriate SSA use and simulations, but capacity and fixed-point accuracy need quantitative analysis/ablations.  
- Clarity: well written and organized; methods are clear though a few statements require precision.  
- Originality: high — novel combination of SSA-based training, Dale compliance, and non-saturating balanced attractors.  
- Significance: meaningful for computational neuroscience and recurrent network design; likely to inspire follow-ups.  
- Request: analyze sensitivity to gain γ (hold W,h fixed and vary γ; re-optimize for multiple γ and compare metrics).  
- Request: justify log-normal memory choice with references and test robustness to other distributions (truncated Gaussian, Gamma, uniform).  
- Request: quantify fixed-point violations per memory: ||dv/dt||, distance to nearest true fixed point, drift time scale, and SA/SSA at that fixed point.  
- Request: clarify the claim that exact stable fixed points exist near targets and provide constructive/technical justification.  
- Request: provide at least empirical capacity scaling: recall vs m/nE across sizes and nI/nE, pattern distributions, and failure-mode analysis.  
- Request: ablation comparing unconstrained vs Dale-constrained W to assess computational benefit or cost of Dale’s law.  
- Request: expand discussion on plausible local learning rules and connections to inhibitory plasticity, equilibrium propagation, or implicit differentiation.  
- Additional suggestions: report synapse pruning sensitivity, show SSA vs SA during training, and state computational costs for reproducibility.  
- Pros: unified principled framework, strong empirical validation, biologically faithful constraints, links to variability quenching.  
- Cons: lacks capacity analysis, relies on global optimization without plausible local rules, limited sensitivity studies.  
- Overall assessment: clear, original, important contribution; recommend acceptance pending empirical analyses on capacity, γ/log-normal sensitivity, and fixed-point quantification.
