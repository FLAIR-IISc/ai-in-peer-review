

This paper presents a significant advance in biologically plausible memory modeling by introducing a rate-based auto-associative network that rigorously enforces Dale’s Law and non-saturating graded activity. The authors address a critical gap in prior work (e.g., Hopfield networks violating Dale’s Law, binary-only representations, or saturation issues in LIF models) by optimizing synaptic weights and inhibitory dynamics via gradient descent combined with Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization to ensure stable fixed-point attractors for analog memory patterns. Their method successfully generates networks with biologically consistent features: synaptic weight distributions centered near zero with long tails, excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron, and robust recall under noise and perturbations. The manuscript is exceptionally clear, with thorough literature context, well-justified architecture, and compelling figures (e.g., Fig. 1c, 5b).

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* Technically rigorous; stability is validated via SSA minimization and empirical dynamics (Fig. 2–3), with robustness to noise demonstrated (Fig. 6).  
*Originality:* Novel integration of control-theoretic stability optimization (SSA) into neural memory models, resolving longstanding biological constraints.  
*Clarity:* Excellent organization, precise figures, and accessible explanation of the method.  
*Significance:* Directly addresses a core neuroscience challenge (how graded memories are stored/stable in cortex), with implications for understanding variability reduction in sensory/motor systems (Fig. 6b).  

**Weaknesses:**  
The justification for choosing SSA-based training over alternatives (e.g., alternative stability metrics or learning rules) is underdeveloped. The authors should clarify *why* this method uniquely enables graded, non-saturating memories (e.g., contrast with methods requiring binary thresholds or saturation). A brief mechanistic explanation of *why* SSA succeeds would strengthen the contribution. Capacity comparisons (e.g., vs. [5, 6]) would further contextualize significance but are beyond the core contribution.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept.** The paper makes a strong, well-executed contribution to biologically grounded neural modeling. The minor gap in training procedure justification is easily addressable in revision and does not undermine the compelling results. The work is clearly novel, technically sound, and highly relevant to NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful computational neuroscience. It advances the field beyond prior binary/saturating models and provides a robust framework for future work on memory mechanisms. The clarity and quality of presentation further support acceptance.
