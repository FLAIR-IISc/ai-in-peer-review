Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents a control-theoretic approach for constructing rate-based recurrent networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons (obeying Dale’s law) that can store and robustly retrieve multiple analog memories as stable fixed points. The authors formulate two core storage criteria: (i) each target pattern must be a fixed point of the dynamics, and (ii) the fixed point must be locally stable. They optimize synaptic weights (with a nonnegative/negative reparameterization to enforce Dale’s law) and per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities to minimize the drift at target states and the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian for robust stability, with an L2 regularizer on weights. The resulting networks operate in a balanced E/I regime, exhibit sparse-long-tailed weight distributions reminiscent of cortical data, retrieve graded patterns without saturation, are robust to cue corruption and ongoing noise, and naturally reduce across-trial variability at stimulus onset.

Relative to classic Hopfield-like autoassociative memories [1,2] (binary/symmetric and violating Dale’s law), subsequent graded-memory models [3–6] lacking explicit inhibition, and E/I spiking/rate models relying on saturation or global inhibition [7–9], this work unifies several physiological constraints (Dale’s law, graded non-saturated activity, balanced regime) within a single constructive framework. It differs from spatial bump attractors [10,11] by storing arbitrary analog patterns without assuming spatial structure, and from spike timing–based schemes lacking inhibitory units [12]. The use of SSA from robust control [13,14] to enforce stability of multiple attractors in an E/I network appears novel in this context. The findings dovetail with stabilized supralinear network ideas [15] and observed firing-rate distributions [18–20], and connect to variability quenching phenomena [21–23].

Evaluation

Quality
- Technical soundness: The two-part objective (fixed-point drift and SSA) is well motivated; SSA offers a differentiable proxy for local stability with guarantees (SSA ≥ SA and SSA ≤ 0 ⇒ SA < 0). The optimization uses L-BFGS and enforces Dale’s law via reparameterization.
- Empirical support: Experiments on n=150 with m=30 (capacity ≈0.2) show robust retrieval under substantial cue noise, performance close to an ideal observer baseline, balanced E/I currents at steady state, emergent sparse-long-tailed weight distributions, and robustness to ongoing noise with stimulus-onset variability reduction.
- Limitations: Fixed-point drift is reduced but not eliminated (patterns become “slow points”); stability is argued via SSA and empirical recall but not by directly verifying SA at the final operating points for the learned weights. Basin sizes are probed indirectly via cue noise rather than systematically analyzed. Capacity and scaling with n, m, and ε are not characterized theoretically. Training hyperparameters and computational cost/sensitivity are only briefly discussed.

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written and well organized, with figures that support the claims (e.g., recall performance vs noise, E/I balance plots, weight distributions).
- Suggestions: Provide more details on SSA computation and gradients, sensitivity to ε and ηs/ηF, and reporting of optimization convergence and runtime. A short algorithmic summary (steps, initialization, stopping criteria) would aid reproducibility. Clarify how nonzero dv/dt at target states impacts the location of the realized attractors and how SA was checked post-training.

Originality
- Introducing robust control (SSA) to shape multiple stable attractors in a biologically constrained E/I rate network is novel. The combination of Dale’s law, graded memories without saturation, and balanced dynamics optimized directly for stability is original relative to prior attractor constructions.

Significance
- Conceptual: Provides a constructive recipe to embed analog attractors in balanced E/I networks, plausibly bridging theoretical memory models and cortical physiology.
- Practical/methodological: The optimization framework could be adopted in other circuit-design problems (e.g., motor dynamics [14]) and extended to spiking models leveraging rectified power-law gains [24–26].
- Caveats: Without capacity analysis, scaling results, or comparisons against alternative learning schemes (e.g., classical Hebbian, stabilization via inhibitory plasticity [27]), the immediate impact on state-of-the-art memory capacity is unclear. Still, the biological plausibility and control-theoretic approach are promising.

Strengths
- Unifies key biological constraints (Dale’s law, balanced E/I, graded non-saturated rates).
- Robust control–based stability objective (SSA) is principled and differentiable.
- Empirical demonstrations of robust recall, ideal-observer-level performance, E/I balance, and variability quenching.
- Emergent sparse-long-tailed synaptic distribution aligning with cortical data.
- Clear writing and compelling figures.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Capacity and scaling: Provide systematic scaling with n and m, memory load vs performance curves, and sensitivity to ε. Any theoretical bounds?
- Fixed-point accuracy: Report SA (not just SSA) at converged states and quantify dv/dt residuals; demonstrate the nearby true fixed points explicitly.
- Basins of attraction: Characterize basin sizes across memories beyond a single σ parameter; e.g., distance-to-basin-boundary statistics.
- Biological constraints: While Dale’s law is enforced, structural sparsity (connection probabilities) is not; consider training with fixed sparse masks. Clarify the role of optimizing per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables—does this effectively add degrees of freedom relative to models that only specify E targets?
- Comparisons: Include baselines using standard Hebbian or balanced-memory constructions [8,9], and inhibitory plasticity approaches [27].
- Generality: Explore other gain exponents and show robustness; discuss steps towards spiking implementations beyond qualitative arguments.

Fit to NIPS
- Squarely within NIPS topics (theory of neural computation, learning dynamics in recurrent networks) and of broad interest to computational neuroscience and machine learning communities exploring stable recurrent computation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled method to construct biologically plausible analog attractor networks.
- Strong empirical evidence of robust recall and balanced dynamics under multiple constraints.
- Clear exposition; potential methodological impact beyond memory networks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited capacity/scaling analysis and lack of theoretical guarantees beyond local stability proxies.
- Fixed points not enforced exactly; reliance on SSA without post hoc SA checks.
- Sparse set of comparisons to alternative learning rules or architectures.
- Practical training details (complexity, sensitivity) underreported.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The conceptual and methodological contributions outweigh the current limitations; with added analyses on capacity, scaling, and stability verification, this could be a strong contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
