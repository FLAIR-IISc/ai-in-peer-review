This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of associative memory, revisiting the topic with a fresh perspective and addressing limitations of prior work. The authors develop a control-theoretic framework to construct attractor networks satisfying biological constraints – Dale’s law, non-binary representations, and avoiding neuronal saturation – simultaneously. The resulting networks demonstrate robust memory recall and explain observed phenomena like reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset. The work is clearly written, well-organized, and represents a substantial advancement. However, the lack of a biologically plausible learning rule is a notable weakness.

The paper correctly identifies shortcomings in previous models, such as the violation of Dale’s law in the original Hopfield network [1] and the limitations imposed by binary representations [2]. While subsequent work attempted to address these issues [3, 4, 7, 12], they often fell short, either by relying on unrealistic assumptions or failing to incorporate all relevant biological constraints. The authors’ approach, leveraging recent advances in control theory [13, 14], offers a unified and elegant solution. The cost function (Eq. 5) is well-motivated, directly targeting fixed-point stability and preventing saturation through a regularization term.

A significant strength is the enforcement of Dale’s principle via the reparameterization of weights (Eq. 2), which naturally leads to an excitation/inhibition balance. Treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is also a clever strategy. The emergence of single-neuron saturation as a consequence of optimization, rather than direct enforcement, is a compelling result. Numerical simulations convincingly validate the approach, demonstrating both associative memory properties and noise robustness. The explanation for reduced trial-to-trial variability, linking it to convergence to the nearest attractor, is insightful and connects the model to experimental observations [21].

However, several points require clarification and further investigation.

**Regarding specific parameters:** The choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (1) feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors justify it based on observed firing rates in cat V1 [16], a sensitivity analysis exploring different values of γ and their impact on network performance and stability would be beneficial. How does the network behave with a different gain function shape?

**Motivation for log-normal distribution:** The authors’ rationale for assuming a log-normal distribution for memory states (Fig. 1a) – to roughly capture the skewed and heavy-tailed nature of firing rate distributions observed *in vivo* [18] – feels slightly weak. More rigorous justification or comparison to other plausible distributions is warranted. What specific characteristics of the log-normal distribution are crucial for the observed network behavior?

**Cost function and fixed-point constraints:** The authors acknowledge that the optimization may not achieve exact fixed points (p. 8). Quantification of the degree to which the cost function (4) deviates from the strict fixed-point constraints would be valuable. How close are the "slow points" to true fixed points, and what is the impact of this deviation on network performance?

**Clarification of lines 254–256:** The statement on lines 254–256 regarding the network's tendency to fall into the baseline state despite the ideal observer’s performance suggests a potential bias in the learning process. Further elaboration on this point is needed. Is this a consistent observation, and if so, what mechanisms contribute to it?

**Memory capacity:** A crucial aspect missing from the analysis is a discussion of memory capacity. While the authors state a capacity of 0.2 for the example shown, a more systematic exploration of how capacity scales with network size and other parameters is essential. What are the theoretical limits on capacity given the imposed constraints? How does the observed capacity compare to theoretical predictions for similar models [3, 4]?

**Dale's Law: Computational Benefit vs. Biophysical Constraint:** The authors rightly emphasize the importance of incorporating Dale’s law. However, the paper would benefit from a discussion of whether Dale’s law provides computational benefits beyond merely being an evolutionary or biophysical constraint. Does the separation of excitation and inhibition offer advantages in terms of stability, learning, or information processing?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful approach to associative memory.
*   Addresses key limitations of previous models.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and compelling numerical results.
*   Connects model to experimental observations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a biologically plausible learning rule.
*   Limited discussion of memory capacity.
*   Unjustified parameter choice (γ=0.04).
*   Insufficient justification for the log-normal distribution.
*   Requires clarification regarding the deviation from exact fixed points and the baseline state bias.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential to advance the field. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the contribution and make it even more impactful. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
