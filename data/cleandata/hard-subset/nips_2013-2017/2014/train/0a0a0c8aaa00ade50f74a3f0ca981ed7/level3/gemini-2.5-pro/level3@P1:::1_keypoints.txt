- Introduces a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize synapses in separate excitatory and inhibitory neurons, enforcing Dale's Law.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to enable differentiable, gradient-based stability optimization.  
- Embeds arbitrary, graded (analog) activity patterns as stable fixed points without requiring neuronal firing-rate saturation.  
- Contrasts with Hopfield-like models that violated Dale's law, used binary patterns, or needed saturation for stability.  
- Joint optimization formulation (fixed-point condition + stability) is clear, well-motivated, and technically sound.  
- Simulation results convincingly demonstrate robust recall from noisy cues, learned weight structure analysis, E/I balance, and robustness to ongoing noise.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with a concise literature review, clear methods, and informative figures.  
- Several biologically realistic features emerge from optimization: sparse-like weight distribution (peak at zero, long tails), tight per-neuron E/I balance, and stimulus-induced variability quenching.  
- Weakness: paper lacks mechanistic explanation for why SSA minimization is uniquely effective versus other stability-promoting methods.  
- Weakness: missing quantitative benchmarking of storage capacity and scaling comparisons to prior models or theoretical limits.  
- Pro-acceptance summary: novel, powerful, biologically plausible attractor framework that meets multiple constraints and yields emergent cortical-like properties.  
- Con-acceptance summary: would be stronger with deeper mechanistic insight and capacity comparisons, but weaknesses are minor relative to its contributions.
