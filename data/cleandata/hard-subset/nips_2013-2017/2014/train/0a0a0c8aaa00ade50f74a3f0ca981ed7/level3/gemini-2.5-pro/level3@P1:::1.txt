This paper presents a novel and compelling method for constructing autoassociative memory networks that adhere to a number of crucial biological constraints. The central contribution is a control-theoretic framework for directly optimizing the synaptic weights of a network of separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons, thereby respecting Dale's Law. This optimization procedure is designed to embed a set of arbitrary, analog (graded) activity patterns as stable fixed points of the network dynamics. This work stands in contrast to a long line of research stemming from the original Hopfield network, which often violated Dale's law, relied on binary memory patterns, or required neuronal firing rates to be near saturation for stability. The authors address the difficult problem of ensuring stability by using a recently developed, differentiable measure from robust control theory, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), which allows for efficient gradient-based optimization. The resulting networks are shown to successfully store and recall graded patterns, operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime, and exhibit several features observed in cortical circuits, such as sparse-like weight distributions and stimulus-induced reduction of trial-to-trial variability.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a principled method to build functional memory networks that simultaneously satisfy multiple, non-trivial biological constraints. The application of the SSA minimization technique to stabilize fixed points in a rate-based E-I network is highly novel and appears to be a powerful approach for overcoming the stability issues that plague recurrent networks, particularly those without saturating nonlinearities. The ability to store graded, low-rate analog patterns moves the field of attractor networks significantly closer to biological reality.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The formulation of memory storage as a joint optimization of a fixed-point condition and a stability condition is clear and well-motivated. The methods are described in sufficient detail, and the simulation results convincingly support the paper's claims. The demonstrations of robust recall from noisy cues (Fig. 3), the analysis of the learned weight structure (Fig. 4), the verification of the E/I balanced state (Fig. 5), and the robustness to ongoing noise (Fig. 6) constitute a thorough validation of the proposed model.

3.  **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent and concise review of the relevant literature, clearly positioning the current work and motivating its approach. The methods section is clear, and the results are presented logically with figures that are informative and easy to interpret.

4.  **Emergent Properties:** A particularly strong aspect of this work is that several biologically realistic features emerge from the optimization procedure rather than being explicitly built in. The resulting synaptic weight distribution, with a peak at zero and long tails, resembles experimentally observed distributions and arises naturally from the stability optimization (and not just the L2 regularizer). Similarly, the tight E/I balance for each neuron across different memory patterns is an emergent property that ensures stability without requiring neuronal saturation. The demonstration of stimulus-induced variability quenching further connects the model to robust experimental findings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Mechanistic Insight into the Optimization:** While the paper successfully demonstrates *that* the SSA-based optimization works, it would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of *why* this particular method is so effective for achieving the desired outcome of stable, non-saturating, graded memories. Is the success primarily due to the ability of the optimization to find solutions where inhibition precisely counteracts excitation? Could alternative stability-promoting regularizers or training methods (e.g., variants of contrastive Hebbian learning) achieve similar results, or is there something unique about minimizing the SSA that is critical here? A more mechanistic explanation would provide deeper insight and clarify the specific contribution of the chosen technical approach.

2.  **Quantitative Performance Benchmarking:** The paper demonstrates successful storage and recall for a network of 150 neurons storing 30 patterns (a capacity of p/N_E = 0.3 if considering only excitatory neurons, or p/N = 0.2 for the whole network). While this is a good proof of concept, the work would be more impactful if it included a comparison of its storage capacity against previous models, even idealized ones. How does the capacity scale with network size? How does it compare to the theoretical limits of networks storing analog patterns? While a full capacity analysis might be beyond the scope of this paper, providing some context would help readers gauge the performance of this new class of models.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and powerful framework for building biologically plausible attractor networks.
    *   Successfully incorporates several key constraints (Dale's Law, graded patterns, low firing rates) that have been difficult to address simultaneously.
    *   The model produces several emergent, biologically realistic properties (E/I balance, sparse weights, variability quenching).
    *   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are well-supported by simulations.
    *   The work is of high significance and is likely to be of great interest to both computational neuroscientists and the machine learning community.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The manuscript could provide a deeper mechanistic explanation for why the chosen optimization procedure is particularly successful.
    *   A lack of quantitative comparison to the storage capacity of other models makes it slightly difficult to assess the performance in a broader context.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a clear and significant advance in our understanding of how memory could be implemented in neural circuits. The weaknesses identified are better framed as suggestions for improving an already excellent manuscript. I strongly recommend acceptance.
