- Proposes a novel framework for auto-associative memory networks that respect biological constraints (Dale’s Law, E/I separation, graded non-saturating activity).  
- Models distinct excitatory and inhibitory rate neurons and formulates memory storage as an optimization problem.  
- Trains networks with gradient descent to make desired analog patterns fixed points by minimizing the activity time derivative.  
- Enforces dynamic stability using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable control-theoretic proxy for linearized stability.  
- Demonstrates via simulations reliable storage and recall of dozens of graded patterns, robust to large cue corruption and ongoing stochastic noise.  
- Trained networks spontaneously exhibit biologically plausible features: weight distributions centered at zero with long tails and pattern-specific E/I balance.  
- Strength — originality/significance: advances attractor memory theory and overcomes limits of prior models (e.g., Hopfield) by incorporating biological constraints.  
- Strength — technical quality: claims well-supported by thorough, convincing simulations and careful analyses (e.g., role of weak synapses, E/I balance).  
- Strength — clarity: manuscript is well-written, organized, and figures effectively illustrate results.  
- Weakness 1: Lacks a clear justification for selecting the SSA-based training method and discussion of alternative stability-enforcing approaches.  
- Weakness 2: Does not provide a mechanistic explanation for why the optimization yields sparse weights and E/I balance.  
- Weakness 3: Storage capacity (0.2; 30 memories/150 neurons) needs benchmarking or contextualization against previous associative memory models.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and well-executed work with minor weaknesses that can be addressed to strengthen impact.
