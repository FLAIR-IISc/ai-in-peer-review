- Addresses building biologically plausible networks that store/retrieve analog (graded) memory patterns under physiological constraints.
- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes rate-based networks to embed arbitrary analog memories as stable fixed points.
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable stability metric for gradient-based optimization of local stability.
- Enforces biological constraints: Dale’s law for synapses and a non-saturating gain function for firing rates.
- Formulates memory storage as two conditions: fixed-point equality (dv/dt = 0) and local stability (spectral abscissa < 0).
- Demonstrates stable fixed points with near-zero dv/dt (Fig. 2).
- Shows biologically consistent synaptic weight distributions (Fig. 4).
- Exhibits robust recall from noisy cues (Fig. 3).
- Achieves balanced excitatory-inhibitory input currents (Fig. 5).
- Reduces trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset (Fig. 6).
- Clear writing, strong organization, effective figures, and sufficient method detail for reproducibility.
- Advances prior work by avoiding saturating dynamics, global inhibitory shortcuts, and strong structural assumptions of bump attractor models.
- Suggestions: more explicitly justify SSA versus alternatives and discuss whether other training procedures could yield similar graded memories.
- Suggestion: provide direct comparison of memory capacity (memories vs. network size) to previous models.
- Suggestion: elaborate how the rate model and findings would translate to spiking network dynamics to strengthen biological relevance.
- Overall recommendation: Accept — technically rigorous, biologically plausible, novel, and significant contribution.
