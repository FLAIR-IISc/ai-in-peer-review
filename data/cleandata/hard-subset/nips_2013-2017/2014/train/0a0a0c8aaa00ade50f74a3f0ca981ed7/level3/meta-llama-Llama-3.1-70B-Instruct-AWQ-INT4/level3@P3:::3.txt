This paper revisits the concept of associative memory with a fresh and insightful perspective, providing important contributions to the field. The authors develop a unified strategy to address biological constraints, such as Dale's law, non-rate/binary representations, and non-sparse memories, using recent control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques.

The paper is well-written, clear, and an important contribution to the field. The authors propose a cost function with three terms targeting memory fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation. They enforce Dale's principle in weight parameterization, producing natural excitation/inhibition balance. The use of excitatory neurons to encode memories and treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources is a novel approach.

The results demonstrate that the proposed framework can store and recall multiple analog memories as stable fixed points of the dynamics, even in the presence of ongoing noise and corruption of the recall cues. The authors also show that neuronal activities do not saturate, and dynamic feedback inhibition ensures that each neuron can fire at a relatively low rate during recall.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset, which is a ubiquitous observation in sensory and motor cortices. The authors demonstrate that this phenomenon can be explained by the convergence of the network state to the nearest attractor.

However, there are some areas that require further discussion and clarification. Firstly, the choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3) seems arbitrary, and the authors should provide a motivation for this choice. Secondly, the assumption of a log-normal distribution for memory states requires further justification. The authors should provide a clear explanation for why this distribution was chosen and how it relates to the underlying biology.

Additionally, the authors should provide a quantification of how much the cost function (4) may violate exact fixed-point constraints. This would help to understand the trade-offs between the different terms in the cost function and how they affect the overall performance of the network.

The statements on lines 254-256 require clarification, as they seem to be inconsistent with the rest of the paper. The authors should provide a clear explanation of what they mean by "the system will eventually drift away" and how this relates to the stability of the fixed points.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a discussion of memory capacity and how it relates to the number of neurons and the complexity of the memories being stored. The authors should provide some preliminary results or simulations that demonstrate the scalability of their approach.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques from control theory and neuroscience. The use of spectral-bounding techniques to optimize the network parameters is a new approach to the problem of associative memory, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in storing and recalling multiple analog memories.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to provide a new framework for understanding the neural substrate of memory. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be used to explain a range of phenomena, from the reduction of trial-to-trial variability to the robustness of memory recall in the presence of noise.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach and results. However, there are some areas that require further discussion and clarification, and the authors should address these in future work.
