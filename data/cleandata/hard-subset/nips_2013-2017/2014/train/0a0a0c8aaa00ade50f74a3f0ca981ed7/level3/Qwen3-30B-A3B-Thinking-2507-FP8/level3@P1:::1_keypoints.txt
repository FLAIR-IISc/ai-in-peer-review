- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct biologically plausible attractor networks that store and retrieve analog (graded) memory patterns.  
- Addresses three key limitations of prior models: enforces Dale’s law, supports graded (non-binary) memories, and avoids firing-rate saturation during recall.  
- Directly optimizes synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron states to stabilize graded activity patterns as fixed points using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced excitation–inhibition regime and show noise-robust autoassociative recall.  
- Model explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset, matching a key cortical physiology observation.  
- Strength: bridges theoretical neuroscience and biological constraints with a principled SSA-based stability solution enabling non-saturating graded activity.  
- Strength: biological plausibility demonstrated—Dale’s law via reparameterization, sparse heavy-tailed weight distributions matching cortex, and per-neuron E/I balance across attractors.  
- Strength: experiments validate robustness to cue noise, ongoing noise, and synaptic pruning; figures and exposition are clear and informative.  
- Weakness: paper does not sufficiently justify why SSA is uniquely necessary for graded-memory storage compared to prior methods that achieved binary stability.  
- Weakness: lacks capacity benchmarking—reports 30 patterns in 150 neurons (capacity 0.2) but does not compare to prior graded-memory models to assess competitiveness.  
- Originality/significance: unifies multiple biological constraints in one framework and links network dynamics to experimentally observed variability reductions, offering testable predictions.  
- Quality/clarity: technical execution is sound, optimization and stability verification are rigorous, and the manuscript provides reproducible details.  
- Recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to justify SSA choice and include capacity comparisons.
