

This paper presents a compelling framework for constructing biologically plausible rate-based memory networks that store arbitrary analog patterns as stable fixed points while respecting key physiological constraints. The authors address longstanding limitations in theoretical neuroscience: previous models either violated Dale's law (e.g., Hopfield networks [1]), restricted memories to binary states [2,3], or relied on saturating neuron models [7,8]. Their approach directly optimizes synaptic weights using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13,14] to enforce both fixed-point conditions and local stability, circumventing the non-smoothness of the spectral abscissa. Crucially, the resulting networks operate in a balanced E-I regime with graded, non-saturating activity, robust to noise and memory cue corruption—features consistently observed in cortical physiology.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality & Originality:* The method is technically sound, leveraging robust control theory to solve a persistent neuroscience challenge. The SSA optimization is elegantly adapted to neural dynamics, and the experimental validation is thorough: networks achieve stable fixed points (Fig. 2), exhibit realistic synaptic distributions (Fig. 4), demonstrate E-I balance per neuron (Fig. 5), and replicate the stimulus-induced reduction in trial-to-trial variability observed *in vivo* (Fig. 6b). This work significantly advances beyond prior rate-based models [3,4,7] by simultaneously satisfying Dale’s law, graded activity, and stability without saturation.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written. The methods are precisely defined (Eqs. 1–5), key results are clearly visualized (Fig. 1–6), and the narrative logically connects constraints to solutions. The literature review situates the work within NIPS history (e.g., contrasting with Hopfield [1], LIF models [7], and spatial bump models [10,11]).  
*Significance:* The framework provides a new pathway to model cortical memory substrates with physiological fidelity. The emergent E-I balance and variability reduction directly explain empirical phenomena [21], suggesting broader applicability to sensory/motor systems. The approach could inform future work on learning rules for inhibitory plasticity [27].

**Weaknesses & Suggestions:**  
*Justification for SSA choice:* The paper states SSA was chosen for its differentiability but does not contrast it with alternatives (e.g., Lyapunov-based stability optimization [28]). It would strengthen the argument to clarify why SSA is *particularly suited* for graded memory storage versus methods used in prior work (e.g., [10,11] for binary patterns). A brief discussion of trade-offs (e.g., SSA’s ε-parameter sensitivity) would help readers assess the method’s robustness.  
*Capacity comparison:* The storage capacity (0.2 for 150 neurons) is stated but not benchmarked against prior analog-memory models. The authors should compare this to theoretical limits [3,4] or empirical results from similar rate-based networks [6], as capacity is a key metric for memory systems. This would clarify the method’s practical advantage.  
*Training procedure:* While the optimization is effective, the paper does not explicitly link the SSA-based training to the *mechanism* enabling graded activity (e.g., whether E-I balance arises from the SSA objective itself or the rate model). A short mechanistic discussion would deepen the contribution.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality, original contribution with strong significance for computational neuroscience. The method resolves a critical gap in biologically plausible memory models, and the results are rigorously validated. The weaknesses noted are minor and addressable via targeted revisions. The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on principled, impactful advances in neural computation. **I recommend acceptance.** The authors should clarify the SSA choice and capacity comparison in the revision to further solidify the work’s novelty. The manuscript is already clear and compelling, and these additions would make it a landmark contribution to the field.
