- Revisits associative memory with a unified strategy addressing biological constraints using control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques.  
- Proposes a cost function with three terms targeting memory fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation.  
- Enforces Dale's principle via weight parameterization, producing natural excitation/inhibition balance.  
- Novel approach: excitatory neurons encode memories while inhibitory neurons are treated as optimizable resources.  
- Demonstrates storing and recalling multiple analog memories as stable fixed points despite noise and corrupted cues.  
- Shows neuronal activities do not saturate and dynamic feedback inhibition keeps firing rates low during recall.  
- Explains reduction of trial-to-trial variability as convergence of network state to the nearest attractor.  
- Choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3) appears arbitrary and requires justification.  
- Assumption of a log-normal distribution for memory states needs further biological justification.  
- Request to quantify how much cost function (4) may violate exact fixed-point constraints to understand trade-offs.  
- Statements on lines 254–256 are unclear/inconsistent and need clarification about “the system will eventually drift away.”  
- Paper lacks discussion and preliminary results on memory capacity and scalability with neuron count and memory complexity.  
- Originality: novel combination of familiar control-theoretic and neuroscience techniques, applying spectral-bounding to associative memory.  
- Significance: provides a new framework for understanding neural memory phenomena and demonstrates robustness of recall.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/insightful approach, effective storage/recall of analog memories, robustness to noise, and theoretical significance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: needs clarifications on γ, log-normal assumption, cost-function violations, and empirical evidence for capacity/scalability.
