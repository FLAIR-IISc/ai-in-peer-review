- Introduces a novel, biologically plausible attractor network that enforces Dale’s Law and stores graded, non-saturating memories.  
- Employs a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes synaptic connectivity and uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa for robust fixed-point stability.  
- Demonstrates networks that are robust to noise and partial cues, produce graded activity patterns, and show balanced excitation/inhibition consistent with physiology.  
- Clearly written and well-organized with informative figures and thorough literature situating.  
- Theoretical framework and experiments are technically sound and the evaluation is careful and comprehensive.  
- Original contribution by integrating control-theory-based optimization with biological constraints.  
- Significant potential impact on understanding neural memory representation and retrieval.  
- Strength: strong biological plausibility (Dale’s Law, avoidance of neuronal saturation).  
- Strength: methodological novelty (SSA-based robust stability optimization).  
- Strength: empirical robustness and emergence of balanced E/I dynamics.  
- Strength: convincing simulation results for storage and recall of graded memories.  
- Weakness: training procedure needs stronger mechanistic justification compared to alternative learning rules.  
- Weakness: lacks a capacity comparison to prior models and theoretical limits.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, offers a novel sound solution, strong results, clear presentation, high relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient justification of training method and missing capacity benchmarking.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance; strengths substantially outweigh minor weaknesses.
