- Presents a novel control-theoretic framework that builds attractor networks respecting Dale’s law and supporting graded activity patterns.  
- Formulates memory storage as a control problem focused on fixed-point stability and optimizes robustness using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).  
- Directly optimizes excitatory and inhibitory networks to stabilize multiple graded activity patterns and enable robust memory recall.  
- Explains stimulus-induced reductions in trial-to-trial variability and reproduces excitation–inhibition balance observed in cortex.  
- Explicitly enforces Dale’s law via synaptic weight reparameterization, increasing biological plausibility.  
- Technically rigorous: the mathematical formulation and optimization procedure are well-justified.  
- Clear and well-written presentation with informative figures that make the work accessible.  
- Original integration of control theory, robust optimization, and biological constraints represents a significant advance.  
- Results are significant and comparable to an “ideal observer” in network performance.  
- Computational cost and scalability to larger, more realistic networks are a concern and not fully discussed.  
- Cost-function parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε) seem heuristic and lack systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Use of a rate-based model abstracts away spiking dynamics, limiting generalizability to real neural circuits.  
- Limited direct biological validation beyond firing-rate statistics; more experimental comparison would strengthen claims.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, novel, and technically sound work with important contributions; strong recommendation for acceptance.
