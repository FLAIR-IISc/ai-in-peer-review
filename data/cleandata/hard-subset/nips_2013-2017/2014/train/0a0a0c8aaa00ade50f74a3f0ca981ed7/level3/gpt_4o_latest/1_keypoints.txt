- Presents a biologically plausible auto-associative memory model that obeys Dale’s Law and avoids binary/saturating neuron responses.  
- Employs a control-theoretic framework and gradient descent to optimize synaptic weights for stable fixed points.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for stability optimization, demonstrating robustness to noise and perturbations.  
- Successfully encodes graded memory patterns in an excitatory/inhibitory network with robust retrieval performance.  
- Network reproduces experimental features: balanced excitation/inhibition, synaptic weight distributions centered near zero, sparsity, and variability reduction.  
- Technically sound with solid theoretical analysis, thorough simulations, and clear, reproducible methodology.  
- Novel contribution in unifying multiple physiological constraints within a single memory-modeling framework.  
- Strength: SSA-based stability optimization is innovative and well-justified.  
- Weakness: Needs clearer justification for using the chosen gradient-descent training versus alternative methods.  
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison of memory capacity with prior models (e.g., Hopfield networks, graded-memory systems).  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of how the rate-based model would translate to spiking network dynamics.  
- Recommendation: Strong candidate for acceptance, contingent on addressing training rationale and capacity comparison.  
- For acceptance: novelty, robust validation, clear writing, and relevance to neuroscience.  
- Against acceptance: missing capacity benchmarks, insufficient training-method justification, and limited spiking extensions.
