- Presents a novel framework combining Dale’s law, graded analog patterns, and low firing rates using a control-theoretic optimization.  
- Core method: cost function with terms for fixed-point matching, stability via Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and synaptic regularization.  
- Optimization yields networks where excitatory neurons encode memories and inhibitory neurons act as tunable auxiliaries.  
- Emergent features include dynamic E/I balance, sparse weights, and robust autoassociative recall without explicitly enforcing those properties.  
- Model reproduces experimentally observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability, supporting biological relevance.  
- Original and significant: first application of SSA to ensure stability of multiple graded patterns under Dale’s law.  
- Technical quality is high: sound analysis and clear numerical simulations that support claims.  
- Manuscript clarity is excellent and makes complex methods accessible.  
- Major limitation: training uses global gradient descent, lacking a biologically plausible, local synaptic learning rule.  
- Request to analyze sensitivity to the choice of γ = 0.04 and its impact on network behavior.  
- Ask for stronger motivation for assuming log-normal memory state distributions beyond matching firing-rate statistics.  
- Clarify how close final states are to true fixed points since optimization minimizes ||dv/dt||² rather than enforcing zero.  
- Suggest adding a preliminary analysis of memory capacity, a key metric for associative memory models.  
- Recommend discussing whether Dale’s law confers computational advantages in this framework versus being solely a biological constraint.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — important, original contribution despite lack of a plausible learning mechanism.
