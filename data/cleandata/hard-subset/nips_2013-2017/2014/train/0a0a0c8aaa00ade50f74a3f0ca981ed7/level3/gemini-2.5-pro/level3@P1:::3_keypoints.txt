- Introduces a control-theoretic framework that optimizes synapses and inhibitory firing rates to build associative memory networks under biological constraints.
- Targets storage of graded (analog) memory patterns as stable fixed points while enforcing Dale’s law and avoiding unrealistically high firing rates or binary representations.
- Optimization minimizes a cost combining fixed-point error (||dv/dt||) and stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).
- Trained networks successfully store and recall analog patterns, are robust to noise, and operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime.
- Neuronal saturation and sparse connectivity emerge from the optimization rather than being hard-coded.
- Model reproduces stimulus-induced reduction in trial-to-trial neural variability, linking to experimental observations.
- Methodological novelty: first application of robust control theory (SSA minimization) to enforce stability in Dale-obeying attractor networks.
- Significant advance over prior models by enabling analog memory storage without relying on saturation or effectively binary neurons.
- Technical execution is sound: well-formulated problem, justified cost function, appropriate optimization, and convincing numerical simulations.
- Results are validated with thorough analyses, metrics, and clear visualizations.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and motivates the problem with a strong literature review.
- Main weakness: learning procedure is not biologically plausible—optimization uses global information and is not Hebbian/local.
- Question on parameter sensitivity: impact of neuronal gain parameter (γ = 0.04) on stability and E/I balance is unclear.
- Question on pattern statistics: rationale for using log-normal memory patterns and whether performance generalizes to uniform or Gaussian patterns.
- Fixed-point accuracy concern: residual ||dv/dt|| after convergence is non-zero; magnitude and long-term drift implications are not quantified.
- Synaptic pruning/clipping experiment needs clarification on why some attractors lose stability and whether affected attractors were outliers.
- Storage capacity is not systematically analyzed; only α = 0.2 demonstrated, lacking discussion of performance versus number of memories.
- Computational role of Dale’s law is not explored: unclear if separable E/I populations aid optimization or are merely constraints to satisfy.
- Arguments for acceptance: original SSA-based methodology, unified solution to Dale’s law and analog memory storage, emergence of biological features, and link to variability quenching.
- Arguments against acceptance: absence of biologically plausible learning mechanism and limited discussion/analysis of storage capacity and parameter sensitivity.
