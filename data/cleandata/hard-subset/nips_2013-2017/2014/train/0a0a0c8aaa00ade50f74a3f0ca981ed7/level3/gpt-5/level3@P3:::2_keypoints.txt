- Proposes a control-theoretic method to train E–I rate attractor networks that store multiple graded memories while enforcing Dale’s law, low-rate fixed points, and E/I balance.
- Enforces Dale’s law via synaptic-sign reparameterization, stabilizes low-rate fixed points with feedback inhibition, and trains with a composite loss (fixed-point drift + SSA + L2).
- Trained networks exhibit graded, non-saturated attractors, heavy-tailed weight distributions with a peak near zero, E/I balance during recall, robustness to cue/ongoing noise, and variability quenching.
- Addresses key limitations of prior attractor models: symmetric connectivity, effectively binary memories, and reliance on saturating transfer functions.
- Novel use of robust-control tools (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to explicitly stabilize graded attractors in biologically structured E–I networks.
- Technical formulation is clear and principled; combining dv/dt penalties and SSA is a tractable compromise between analysis and biological realism.
- Empirical validation is thorough: recall approaches ideal-observer bounds, attractor residence under noise is stable, E/I currents balance tightly, and learned weight statistics match experiments.
- Clipping experiments indicate only a minority of strong synapses are functionally essential.
- Optimization produces “slow points” (small nonzero dv/dt) rather than provable exact fixed points; empirical evidence suggests nearby fixed points but basin analysis is lacking.
- Empirical capacity is modest (~0.2 with N=150, m=30) and no theoretical capacity or scaling analysis is provided.
- Training relies on global, nonlocal gradient optimization and per-pattern inhibitory steady-state variables, which are not biologically local learning rules.
- Network is trained densely; explicit structural sparsity or spatial cortical structure are not imposed.
- SSA is applied with a heuristic ε scaling; sensitivity to ε and relative loss weights is not fully characterized.
- Transfer to spiking (LIF) networks is discussed but not demonstrated empirically.
- Manuscript is very well written and organized; objectives, reparameterization, SSA, and protocols are clearly described and figures effectively support claims.
- Suggested additions: report attractor basin sizes vs. cue noise, provide computational cost and convergence diagnostics, and perform sensitivity analysis for ε, ηs, and ηF.
- Suggested clarification: interpret the role of per-attractor inhibitory steady-state variables during training versus recall.
- Originality: the combination of biologically grounded constraints with SSA-driven stability optimization for E–I attractors is novel in this literature.
- Significance: provides a template for constructing biologically plausible recurrent networks that embed graded memories and reproduce synaptic/dynamical signatures; likely to influence future work.
- Pros for acceptance: principled objective, strong empirical validation, emergent biological plausibility, robustness, and reproducibility detail.
- Cons for acceptance: no capacity theory/scaling, global nonlocal learning rule, extra degrees of freedom from per-pattern inhibitory variables, dense connectivity, and limited SSA hyperparameter analysis.
- Open questions: how capacity scales with N/pattern stats, whether explicit sparsity can be incorporated without losing stability, sensitivity to gain functions, transferability to LIF networks, and whether local plasticity can approximate SSA gradients.
- Recommendation: Strong accept — careful, original work with clear limitations and actionable follow-up directions.
