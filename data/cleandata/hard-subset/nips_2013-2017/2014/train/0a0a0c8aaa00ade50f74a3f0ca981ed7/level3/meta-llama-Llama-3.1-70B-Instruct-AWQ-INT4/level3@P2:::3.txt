This paper revisits the concept of associative memory in a network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons, addressing several key biological constraints that have been often ignored in previous work. The authors develop a unified strategy that incorporates Dale's law, non-binary representations, and sparse memories using recent control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques. The paper proposes a cost function with three terms targeting memory fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation, and enforces Dale's principle in weight parameterization, producing a natural excitation/inhibition balance.

The authors use excitatory neurons to encode memories and treat inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources, and demonstrate that single-neuron saturation emerges naturally from optimization despite not being directly enforced. The approach is validated with numerical simulations demonstrating associative memory properties and noise robustness. The paper also explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via convergence to the nearest attractor.
