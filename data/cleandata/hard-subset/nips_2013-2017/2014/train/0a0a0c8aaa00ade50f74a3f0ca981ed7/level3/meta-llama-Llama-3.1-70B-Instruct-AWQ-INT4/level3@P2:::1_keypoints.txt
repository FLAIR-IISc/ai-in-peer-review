- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to build attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.  
- Introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model enforcing Dale’s Law and non-saturating graded units.  
- Encodes memories via gradient-descent synaptic updates combined with stability enforcement around fixed points.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization.  
- Provides a thorough literature review and clearly describes architecture and training.  
- Simulations show trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow, near-fixed-point dynamics.  
- Trained networks display biologically consistent features: weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate E/I balance per neuron.  
- Networks are robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, demonstrating reliable auto-associative recall.  
- Manuscript is clearly written, figures are effective, and methods are detailed and reproducible.  
- Strength: novel, biologically plausible control-theoretic approach overcoming prior model limitations.  
- Strength: demonstration of stable, robust memory recall under noise and perturbations.  
- Weakness: unclear justification for the chosen training procedure and why it’s suited for graded, non-saturating memories.  
- Weakness: absence of discussion on alternative training procedures or mechanistic explanation for the method’s success.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to previous models in capacity (number of memories vs network size).  
- Recommendation: provide a clear explanation and justification for the chosen training procedure.  
- Recommendation: discuss whether alternative training methods could achieve similar results and explain mechanisms of success.  
- Recommendation: compare trained network performance to prior models in terms of capacity and performance.  
- Overall: significant contribution with potential impact, but requires revisions addressing the noted weaknesses.
