

This paper presents a significant advance in modeling neural attractor networks by directly addressing long-standing biological constraints that have plagued prior work. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework to train rate-based E-I networks to store multiple analog memory patterns as stable fixed points, rigorously satisfying Dale’s law, avoiding activity saturation, and achieving dynamic E-I balance—key limitations in Hopfield-style models and recent LIF-based approaches. The core innovation lies in optimizing synaptic weights via gradient descent on a cost function that minimizes activity drift at target patterns, penalizes weight magnitude, and enforces stability through the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a robust control-theoretic measure. Crucially, the resulting weight distributions and firing rate statistics closely mirror experimental findings (e.g., log-normal rate distributions, synaptic sparseness), and the network demonstrates robust memory recall under noise and stimulus perturbations, while naturally explaining reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset—a hallmark of cortical dynamics.

**Quality**: Exceptionally strong. The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical grounding (SSA for stability optimization) and comprehensive validation through both synthetic and biologically motivated experiments. The authors carefully address limitations (e.g., rate-based simplification) and provide thorough ablation studies (e.g., synaptic pruning tests). Claims are fully supported by results.  
**Clarity**: The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow, precise definitions, and effective figures (e.g., Fig. 1c, 5b) that directly illustrate biological plausibility. The methods and results are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  
**Originality**: Highly original. The paper uniquely combines Dale’s law, graded attractors, and stability optimization via control theory—a novel synthesis not present in prior NIPS work (e.g., [12, 22] lack E-I balance or graded patterns; [7, 8] rely on saturation). It directly tackles a gap identified in the literature.  
**Significance**: Major contribution. The model provides a biologically grounded mechanism for analog memory storage, resolving key theoretical inconsistencies. Its ability to match empirical data (synaptic weights, rate distributions, variability reduction) elevates it beyond abstract models, offering a tangible framework for future experimental and computational neuroscience.  

This work is a notable step toward biologically plausible memory networks and meets all NIPS quality criteria. It is highly recommended for acceptance.
