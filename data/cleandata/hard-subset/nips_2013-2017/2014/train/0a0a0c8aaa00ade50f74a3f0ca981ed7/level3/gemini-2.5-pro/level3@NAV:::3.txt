This paper presents a novel and insightful framework for constructing associative memory networks that adhere to several key biological constraints. The authors revisit this classic problem by simultaneously incorporating Dale’s law (distinct excitatory and inhibitory neurons), graded analog memory patterns, and low firing rates without explicit saturation—limitations often ignored or addressed in isolation by prior work (e.g., Hopfield, 1982; Treves & Rolls, 1991). The core contribution is a unified, control-theoretic approach to optimize network parameters. The authors formulate a cost function with three terms: one to ensure memory patterns are fixed points of the dynamics, another to enforce stability using the recently developed Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and a third to regularize synaptic weights. This optimization produces networks where excitatory neurons encode memories, inhibitory neurons act as tunable auxiliary variables, and a dynamic balance between excitation and inhibition emerges naturally.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper’s main strength lies in its novel synthesis of modern control theory with classical neuroscience problems. Applying the SSA to ensure the stability of multiple graded memory patterns in a Dale’s law-abiding network is a significant and original contribution. This work provides a powerful proof of concept and advances the state of the art in modeling memory circuits, connecting theoretical models more closely to biological reality.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by clear numerical simulations. The authors demonstrate that the resulting networks exhibit robust autoassociative recall, and they compellingly link the model's dynamics to the experimentally observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability, bolstering the model’s relevance. The emergence of properties like E/I balance and sparse weights without being explicitly enforced is a strong result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a technically complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The primary limitation is the lack of a biologically plausible learning rule. The network is trained via global gradient descent, and while the authors acknowledge this, the work would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how local, synaptic rules might approximate this optimization. Furthermore, several points could be clarified to improve the manuscript:
1.  Could the authors discuss the sensitivity of the model to the choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3)? How critical is this parameter for the network's behavior?
2.  Please provide a stronger motivation for assuming a log-normal distribution for memory states beyond its resemblance to observed firing rates.
3.  Since the optimization minimizes ||dv/dt||² rather than enforcing it to be zero, please quantify or discuss how much the final states may deviate from being true fixed points.
4.  A preliminary discussion of the network's memory capacity would be a valuable addition, as this is a critical metric for associative memory models.
5.  A brief discussion on whether Dale’s law provides a computational benefit in this framework, versus being a purely biological constraint, would be very insightful.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that provides a fresh, principled perspective on a fundamental problem. Despite the absence of a plausible learning mechanism, its novel methodology and strong results make it an important contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
