This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing attractor networks that store graded, analog memory patterns while respecting key biological constraints. The authors aim to address several long-standing limitations of previous attractor network models, such as the violation of Dale’s law, the reliance on binary activity patterns, and the need for neuronal firing rates to saturate for stability. The core idea is to frame the problem of memory storage as a direct optimization of network parameters. A cost function is designed to enforce two conditions for each desired memory pattern: 1) that the pattern is a fixed point of the network dynamics (by minimizing the rate of change of neural activity), and 2) that the fixed point is stable. To achieve the second condition in a tractable way, the authors employ the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable proxy for stability from robust control theory. They apply this method to a rate-based network of distinct excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations. The optimization successfully produces networks that can store multiple analog patterns as stable attractors. The resulting networks exhibit several emergent, biologically plausible properties, including a tight balance of excitation and inhibition, and a sparse, long-tailed distribution of synaptic weights, similar to what is observed in cortical circuits.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of memory storage as an optimization problem is elegant, and the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa to ensure stability is a clever and appropriate technical choice for this non-trivial problem. The claims are well-supported by numerical experiments. The authors demonstrate that the trained networks successfully perform pattern completion from noisy cues, with performance comparable to an ideal observer. They also carefully analyze the properties of the learned networks, providing convincing evidence for E/I balance and emergent synaptic sparsity. The work is presented as a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-written. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of prior research, clearly motivating the need for a new approach. The methods section provides a detailed and understandable description of the network model and the optimization procedure, sufficient for an expert to grasp the implementation. The results are presented logically, and the figures are informative and well-designed, effectively illustrating the key findings.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While attractor networks are a classic topic, the approach taken here is novel. Instead of relying on pre-defined learning rules (e.g., Hebbian) or statistical mechanics approaches, the authors use modern optimization techniques from control theory to directly sculpt the network dynamics. This allows them to simultaneously satisfy a set of biological constraints (Dale's law, non-saturating graded rates, E/I populations) that have proven difficult to integrate in previous models. The demonstration that optimizing for stable analog memories leads to emergent properties like E/I balance and realistic weight distributions is a novel and important finding.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be influential. The work provides a powerful proof of concept that functional and robust memory networks can be built from biologically realistic components. By showing that a single optimization objective can give rise to multiple distinct, experimentally observed phenomena (graded persistent activity, E/I balance, sparse connectivity, stimulus-induced variability reduction), the paper offers a unifying perspective on cortical circuit function. This framework could serve as a valuable tool for generating new, testable hypotheses about the relationship between network structure and dynamics in memory circuits. Researchers and practitioners interested in building functional neural circuits, both for neuroscience modeling and for neuromorphic applications, are likely to build upon these ideas.

A minor weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is that the optimization algorithm itself is not proposed as a biologically plausible learning mechanism. It serves as an existence proof that networks with these properties can exist. The question of how such a solution could be learned by local, synaptic plasticity rules remains an important direction for future work.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** The paper introduces a highly original and powerful control-theoretic framework for designing biologically plausible attractor networks.
2.  **Integrates Multiple Constraints:** It successfully overcomes several key limitations of prior models by simultaneously satisfying Dale's law, allowing for graded (non-binary, non-saturating) firing rates, and explicitly modeling E and I populations.
3.  **Biologically Plausible Emergent Properties:** The model demonstrates that optimizing for robust stability leads to emergent network features, such as E/I balance and sparse synaptic weight distributions, that closely match experimental findings in the cortex.
4.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The work is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by evidence, and the manuscript is written with exceptional clarity.
5.  **Significant Contribution:** The paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of how memory could be implemented in cortical circuits and provides a valuable new tool for theoretical neuroscience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Non-biological Learning:** The gradient-based optimization of the full connectivity matrix is not a plausible model of biological learning. However, the authors frame the work as a proof of concept for the existence of such functional networks, which is a valid and valuable contribution in itself.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It presents a novel, elegant, and significant contribution to the field of theoretical and computational neuroscience.
