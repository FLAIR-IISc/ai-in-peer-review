Summary
This paper introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory network composed of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that obeys Dale’s law and supports non-saturating, graded firing rates. The authors formulate memory storage as imposing two requirements: (1) the desired patterns are fixed points of the dynamics and (2) the linearized dynamics around those points are stable. They train the recurrent weights by minimizing a composite objective that drives the time derivative dv/dt toward zero at each memory and enforces local stability using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable surrogate for the spectral abscissa from robust control. The resulting networks exhibit stable and slow dynamics near each stored pattern, robust auto-associative recall under noisy cues and ongoing noise, balanced excitation/inhibition, and synaptic weight distributions with a peak near zero and long tails, in line with cortical data. The manuscript situates the work relative to Hopfield-type models, graded memories, balanced spiking networks, and spatial bump attractors, highlighting that prior approaches often violate Dale’s law, assume binary codes, or rely on saturation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization objective cleanly separates fixed-point and stability requirements; using SSA is a principled and practical approach to robust stability. The training enforces Dale’s law via a signed softplus reparameterization. Simulations convincingly demonstrate: (i) negativity of SSA at trained patterns, (ii) slow near-fixed-point dynamics and eventual convergence, (iii) robust recall across cue noise, (iv) E/I balance per neuron and across memories, and (v) robustness to ongoing stochastic input. The analysis of weight distributions and clipping weak weights is informative.
- Caveats and clarifications:
  - The dv/dt terms remain small but nonzero at trained patterns; convergence is then to nearby true fixed points. It would strengthen the claims to either (a) numerically locate the actual fixed points reached by integration and verify their SA < 0, or (b) include a line-search refinement stage that zeroes dv/dt at each stored pattern.
  - The training treats inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ as per-memory free variables during optimization. This is reasonable as an auxiliary device, but a short discussion of its biological interpretation (e.g., interneuron recruitment profiles) and whether constraining v_inh^μ (or learning only W) changes outcomes would be useful.
  - Computational aspects: SSA and Jacobian computations scale with n and m; a brief complexity analysis and scaling experiment (e.g., runtime vs n, m) would help readers gauge practicality.
  - Robustness across seeds/hyperparameters: Report variance of recall performance and stability across initializations and η_s, η_F.
- Reproducibility: The training objective and parameterization are described clearly; including code or further details on SSA implementation, ε scaling, and optimization settings would facilitate replication.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and motivates the constraints and design choices. The figures effectively illustrate target patterns, training progress (SSA/SA/dv/dt), recall trajectories, weight distributions, E/I balance, and noise robustness. Minor suggestions:
- Explicitly report eigenvalue spectra or timescales (e.g., distribution of real parts of leading eigenvalues) at stored memories to quantify “slow” dynamics.
- Summarize E/I balance with quantitative metrics (per-neuron correlation coefficients and their mean/variance).
- Collect hyperparameters (η_s, η_F, ε) and their rationales in the main text or a concise table.

Originality
The core novelty is importing SSA-based robust stability optimization into biologically constrained attractor memory design with graded, non-saturating units obeying Dale’s law. This provides a distinct alternative to classical energy-based Hopfield constructions, pseudo-inverse rules, LIF saturation-stabilized models, and spatially structured bumps. The combination of control-theoretic stability enforcement with biological constraints is original and compelling. Related work that could be briefly discussed for contrast includes RLS/FORCE training of RNNs with stability control (Sussillo & Abbott, 2009), Lyapunov- or contraction-based stabilization, and Gershgorin/spectral norm constraints on Jacobians.

Significance
Storing analog memories in a balanced E–I network without relying on saturation addresses a long-standing gap between theory and cortical physiology. The demonstrations of robustness, realistic synaptic distributions, and per-neuron E/I balance strengthen the case. The framework is likely to influence future work on memory in biologically plausible networks and may transfer to spiking models via matched gain functions, as discussed.

Suggestions for the authors
- Clarify why SSA-based training was chosen over alternatives and whether it is especially suited to non-saturating graded memories. For example, compare to:
  - Direct SA minimization heuristics or spectral radius constraints on W⊙g′(vμ).
  - Contraction metrics (bounding the largest singular value of the Jacobian).
  - Lyapunov-function-based optimization or energy-function constructions adapted to Dale’s law.
  - RLS/FORCE-style training with stability regularizers under sign constraints.
  Provide either empirical comparisons (even small-scale) or mechanistic arguments explaining why SSA succeeds here (e.g., better conditioning, gradient informativeness, robustness to non-normality).
- Capacity: Provide a systematic capacity analysis versus network size n (e.g., critical m/n at fixed recall accuracy), with basin size characterization and comparison to classic binary (≈0.14N), graded Treves–Rolls predictions, and modern balanced-network capacities.
- Explore alternative nonlinearities (rectified power-law with different exponents, sigmoidal with high dynamic range) to show robustness of the approach to single-neuron gain choices.
- Report performance after enforcing structural sparsity (fixed connection probability) in addition to magnitude sparsity emerging from training.
- Analyze the role of weak weights more fully (e.g., pruning curves and stability margins), and quantify how clipping shifts attractor locations.

Pros and cons
Pros:
- Clear, rigorous framework marrying control-theoretic stability with biological constraints.
- Demonstrated stable, graded attractors without saturation; robust recall under noise.
- Emergent E/I balance and realistic synaptic weight statistics.
- Well-written with effective figures and solid literature grounding.

Cons:
- Limited analysis of capacity and scaling.
- Reliance on per-memory inhibitory steady-states during training warrants discussion.
- Minor gap between target “fixed points” and observed slow points not fully resolved analytically.
- Comparisons to alternative stabilization/training methods are largely absent.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is novel, technically sound, clearly presented, and likely of broad interest to the NIPS community working at the interface of control, machine learning, and computational neuroscience.
