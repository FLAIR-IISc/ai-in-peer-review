## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper presents a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model that addresses key biological constraints often overlooked in previous work, namely Dale’s Law and the representation of memories with graded, non-saturating neuronal activity. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters – synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity – to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points, leveraging recent advances in robust control (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to ensure stability. Simulations demonstrate stable fixed points with slow dynamics, biologically plausible synaptic weight distributions, approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance, and robust recall performance even with noisy inputs. 

The work builds upon the foundational Hopfield network [1] and subsequent attempts to address its limitations [2, 3, 4]. It distinguishes itself by simultaneously incorporating Dale’s Law, graded activity, and a biologically realistic network structure, overcoming issues present in models relying on binary representations [5] or saturating dynamics [7, 8]. The demonstrated robustness to noise and the observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset [21] are particularly compelling, linking the model to observed cortical phenomena. The clarity of the manuscript is excellent, with a well-structured presentation, effective figures, and a comprehensive literature review. The originality of combining control theory with biologically constrained network optimization is significant, and the potential impact on our understanding of memory mechanisms is substantial.

A key strength is the thorough exploration of network properties post-training, revealing features consistent with biological observations (weight distributions, E/I balance). However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed justification for the chosen training procedure (minimizing SSA) and a discussion of why this method is particularly well-suited for enforcing graded, non-saturating memories compared to alternative optimization techniques. Exploring whether other training procedures could achieve similar results, or mechanistically explaining why this approach succeeds, would strengthen the argument. 

Furthermore, while the authors demonstrate robust recall, a comparison of the network’s capacity (number of memories vs. network size) to previous models would be valuable. The suggestion to compare against existing models, as noted by my supervisor, would provide a benchmark for evaluating the model’s efficiency and scalability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining control theory and biologically constrained network optimization.
*   Demonstrated stability, robustness, and biologically plausible network properties.
*   Clear writing, comprehensive literature review, and effective visualizations.
*   Potential to advance our understanding of the neural substrate of memory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed justification for the chosen training procedure.
*   Absence of a capacity comparison to existing models.

**Overall:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, addressing a fundamental problem in neuroscience with a novel and well-executed approach. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance the impact of this work.
