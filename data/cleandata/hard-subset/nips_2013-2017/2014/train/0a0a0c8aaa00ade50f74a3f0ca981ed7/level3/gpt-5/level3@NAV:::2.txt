This paper trains a rate-based recurrent E-I network to store multiple stable, graded activity patterns while satisfying key biological constraints. The authors pose memory storage as a constrained control problem: they jointly minimize activity drift at target patterns (to create fixed points), a smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) cost (to ensure local stability), and an L2 weight penalty; Dale’s law is imposed via a log-exp reparameterization. The learned networks recover autoassociative recall without activity saturation, exhibit E/I balance in attractor states, and produce synaptic weight distributions with sparse, heavy tails consistent with cortical measurements. The model robustly recalls from noisy cues, resists ongoing noise, and reproduces stimulus-onset variability quenching. Relative to classic Hopfield models (binary, symmetric, Dale-violating), LIF-based attractors that rely on high-rate saturation, and spatial bump attractors, this work unifies multiple physiological constraints and uses control-theoretic stability (SSA; Vanbiervliet et al.) in the attractor learning objective. It complements balanced-memory theories (e.g., Roudi & Latham) and control ideas previously used for transient dynamics (Hennequin et al.) by targeting stable analog memories.

Quality: The methodology is technically careful: SSA provides a differentiable proxy for stability, results are validated with recall success and perturbation robustness, and the emergent weight statistics and E/I balance are well-analyzed. Limitations include: (i) fixed points are sometimes “slow points,” and basin sizes are not quantified; (ii) capacity and scaling with N, m, and sparsity are not systematically compared to graded-capacity theory (e.g., Treves & Rolls); (iii) sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF and initialization is not explored; (iv) networks are dense during training and structural sparsity constraints are not explicitly tested; (v) spiking instantiations are discussed but not demonstrated.

Clarity: The manuscript is well written and organized; figures clearly support claims. Adding ablations (hyperparameter sensitivity, computational complexity, scaling curves) would strengthen reproducibility.

Originality: The combination of SSA-based stability optimization with Dale-constrained analog attractors and inhibitory co-optimization is novel and differentiates from prior attractor-learning and control formulations.

Significance: By jointly enforcing multiple biological constraints, the paper advances the design and interpretation of biologically plausible attractor networks and links recall to variability quenching. Impact will hinge on scalability and translation to local/plausible learning rules.

Pros:
- Unified, biologically grounded formulation; strong empirical support
- Emergent E/I balance and realistic weight statistics
- Robust analog attractors without saturation; clear writing

Cons:
- Limited capacity/scaling analysis
- No spiking validation
- Hyperparameter and basin-of-attraction analyses are light

Overall, a strong and timely contribution; I lean toward acceptance.
