- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to directly optimize excitatory–inhibitory networks to stabilize analog patterns as fixed points.  
- Adheres to biological constraints (Dale’s law) and avoids binary memories or saturating neuronal responses.  
- Leverages the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to enforce stability of the learned fixed points.  
- Resulting networks are robust to noise and operate in a balanced excitation/inhibition regime reminiscent of cortical circuits.  
- Demonstrates stable storage and recall with biologically plausible synaptic weight distributions.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and situates the work with a thorough literature review.  
- Methods section provides detailed, reproducible descriptions of architecture, optimization, and mathematical formulation.  
- Results are clearly presented with informative figures and show trial-to-trial variability reduction consistent with experiments.  
- Work is original: a non‑incremental combination of control theory and biologically realistic models for memory.  
- Significant potential impact for understanding cortical memory and inspiring new computational memory models.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for choosing SSA over alternative stability/optimization methods and lack of discussion of trade-offs (e.g., computational cost, parameter sensitivity).  
- Weakness: absence of a direct quantitative comparison of memory capacity to existing models; capacity should be contextualized.  
- Overall assessment: strongly recommended for acceptance, with minor improvements suggested.
