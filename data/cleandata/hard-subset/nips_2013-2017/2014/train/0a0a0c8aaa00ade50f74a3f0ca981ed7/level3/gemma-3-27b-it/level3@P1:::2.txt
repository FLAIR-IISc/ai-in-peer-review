This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building functioning attractor networks that adhere to key physiological constraints, addressing limitations of previous models. The authors aim to create a network of excitatory and inhibitory (E-I) neurons capable of storing and retrieving multiple analog memory patterns as stable fixed points, avoiding issues like Dale’s law violations and activity saturation common in earlier work (Hopfield networks [1], Treves [3, 4], Latham & Nirenberg [7]). They achieve this through a gradient-based optimization procedure, minimizing a cost function that promotes stability and fixed-point conditions while respecting Dale’s law and realistic firing rate dynamics. The resulting networks demonstrate robust recall, balanced E-I activity, and a reduction in trial-to-trial variability reminiscent of observations in sensory and motor cortices.

The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing theoretical neuroscience literature. It meticulously details shortcomings in earlier attractor network models, acknowledging work on binary memories [1, 2], limitations in capacity [5, 6], and challenges in modelling inhibitory neurons [8, 9, 10]. The authors clearly articulate how their approach overcomes these challenges. Specifically, they highlight the novelty of their control-theoretic framework and the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13, 14] for robust stability optimization, distinguishing it from previous methods.  The method allows for the creation of graded memories, moving beyond binary representations, and importantly, avoids saturating neuronal responses.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically robust and well-motivated. The use of control theory and the SSA provides a principled way to achieve stable fixed points. The authors clearly define the objective function and explain the optimization process.
* **Biological Realism:** Incorporating Dale's law and striving for balanced E-I activity are significant steps towards biological plausibility. The resulting network properties, such as sparse synaptic weights and the reduction of trial-to-trial variability, align with experimental observations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The methods section is detailed and provides sufficient information for reproducibility. Figures are informative and effectively illustrate the key findings. 
* **Novelty:** The combination of control theory, robust stability optimization, and physiological constraints represents a novel approach to building attractor networks.  The resulting network, capable of storing graded memories without saturation, is a significant advancement.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated robustness to noise and the ability to explain observed phenomena like trial-to-trial variability reduction suggest that this work could be impactful.  The framework provides a potentially valuable tool for understanding the neural basis of memory.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the method is theoretically sound, the computational cost of optimizing network parameters for even a moderate number of neurons could be substantial. The paper doesn’t explicitly address the scalability of this approach.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters like ηs and ηF in the cost function (Equation 5) seems somewhat heuristic. A more systematic exploration of parameter sensitivity might be valuable.
* **Rate-Based Model:** The use of a rate-based model simplifies neuronal dynamics and may not fully capture the complexities of spiking networks. While acknowledged by the authors, the limitations of this simplification should be further discussed. Specifically, the effect of spike timing on the learning rules is not explicitly considered.
* **Limited Exploration of Learning Rules:** The paper focuses on optimizing network parameters *given* target memory patterns.  It would be beneficial to discuss how synaptic plasticity rules could be used to *learn* these patterns in a more biologically realistic setting, building upon prior work on plasticity and stability [27].



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant advance in the field of attractor network modeling, addressing key limitations of previous approaches.
* The method is technically sound, biologically plausible, and clearly presented.
* The results are compelling and demonstrate the potential of this framework to explain important neural phenomena.
* The originality of combining control theory with biological constraints is noteworthy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and parameter sensitivity of the optimization procedure may limit the applicability of the method.
* The reliance on a rate-based model may not fully capture the complexities of real neural networks.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of potential learning rules.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of attractor networks. The strengths of the work clearly outweigh its weaknesses. While the computational cost and the reliance on a rate-based model are limitations, they do not detract from the novelty and importance of the findings. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and demonstrates a deep understanding of the relevant literature. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and potential learning rules.  A brief discussion about the computational scalability could also be added.
