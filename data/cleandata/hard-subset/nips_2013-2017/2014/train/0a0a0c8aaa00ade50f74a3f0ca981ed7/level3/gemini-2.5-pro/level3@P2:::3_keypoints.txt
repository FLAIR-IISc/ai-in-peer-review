1. Proposes a novel framework to design associative memory networks that satisfy Dale's law, use analog (graded) activities, and avoid saturation via direct optimization from robust control theory.

2. Defines a cost combining three objectives: enforce fixed points (minimize dv/dt), ensure stability via Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and regularize synaptic weights.

3. Optimizes inhibitory neuron activities as auxiliary variables, producing emergent E/I balance that stabilizes low-rate graded memories.

4. Shows robust autoassociative recall from noisy cues and reproduces stimulus-induced quenching of trial-to-trial variability.

5. Technically rigorous and complete, with clear numerical simulations supporting claims and honest framing as a design proof-of-concept rather than a learning model.

6. Highly original: co-optimizing synapses and inhibitory activity under biological constraints and applying SSA minimization in this context is novel.

7. Significant contribution: provides a principled method for building biologically plausible circuit models linking structure, dynamics, and function, likely to inspire follow-ups.

8. Primary limitation: the approach is an offline, global optimization, not a biologically plausible synaptic learning rule.

9. Missing a systematic memory capacity analysis beyond the demonstrated 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network.

10. Some model choices (e.g., log-normal pattern distribution, gain parameter gamma) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.

11. Optimization minimizes ||dv/dt||^2 without forcing it to zero; residual drift at learned attractors should be quantified to confirm true fixed points versus slow regions.

12. The weight-clipping procedure and subsequent adjustment of recall success threshold (lines 254–256) seem ad-hoc and need explanation regarding effects on the attractor landscape.

13. Arguments for acceptance: original framework, integration of key biological constraints, demonstration of emergent E/I balance, and strong technical presentation.

14. Arguments against acceptance: lack of a plausible learning rule and absence of a systematic capacity study.

15. Overall recommendation: strong, novel, and significant paper deserving acceptance despite clear directions for future work.
