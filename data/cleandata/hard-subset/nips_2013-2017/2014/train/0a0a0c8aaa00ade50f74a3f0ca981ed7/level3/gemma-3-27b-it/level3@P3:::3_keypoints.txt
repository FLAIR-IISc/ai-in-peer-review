- Introduces a control-theoretic framework for attractor networks that satisfies biological constraints (Dale’s law, non-binary representations, avoids neuronal saturation).  
- Resulting networks show robust associative memory recall and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and represents a substantial advancement.  
- Major weakness: no biologically plausible learning rule is provided.  
- Correctly identifies prior models' shortcomings and offers an elegant unified solution using a well-motivated cost function targeting fixed-point stability and anti-saturation.  
- Enforces Dale’s law via weight reparameterization, yielding excitation/inhibition balance and treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources.  
- Single-neuron saturation emerges naturally from optimization; numerical simulations validate memory properties and noise robustness.  
- Require sensitivity analysis for the arbitrary-seeming γ = 0.04 and exploration of alternative gain-function shapes.  
- Need stronger justification or comparison for assuming a log-normal distribution of memory states and which distribution features matter.  
- Quantify how closely optimized “slow points” approach true fixed points and the impact of deviations on performance.  
- Clarify the reported baseline-state bias (lines 254–256): consistency of the effect and underlying mechanisms.  
- Missing systematic analysis of memory capacity scaling with network size/parameters and comparison to theoretical limits and prior models.  
- Suggest discussion on whether Dale’s law yields computational benefits (stability, learning, information processing) beyond being a biophysical constraint.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
