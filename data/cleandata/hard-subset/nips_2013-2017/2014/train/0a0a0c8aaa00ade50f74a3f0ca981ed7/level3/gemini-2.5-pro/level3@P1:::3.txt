### Summary

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to constructing associative memory networks that respect a number of key biological constraints often overlooked in previous models. The authors revisit the classic problem of storing graded (analog) memory patterns as stable fixed points in a recurrent neural network. They correctly identify that prior work has struggled to simultaneously incorporate Dale's law (separate excitatory and inhibitory populations), avoid unrealistically high firing rates or binary representations, and maintain stability. The core contribution is a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes network parameters—synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron firing rates—to satisfy these constraints. This is achieved by minimizing a cost function that includes terms for making target patterns fixed points of the dynamics (minimizing `dv/dt`) and for ensuring the stability of these fixed points using the recently developed Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA). The resulting networks successfully store and recall analog patterns, are robust to noise, and operate in a balanced excitation-inhibition regime where neuronal saturation emerges dynamically rather than being hard-coded. The model also compellingly reproduces the reduction in trial-to-trial variability observed experimentally upon stimulus presentation.

### Strengths and Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of theoretical neuroscience and neural computation.

**Originality & Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its fresh and powerful methodology. While attractor networks are a well-trodden subject, the application of robust control theory, specifically the minimization of the SSA, to enforce stability in a network obeying Dale's law is highly novel. This provides a unified framework to address several long-standing challenges that were previously tackled in isolation or ignored. The ability to store arbitrary *analog* patterns without relying on neuronal saturation is a particularly important advance over models that are effectively binary. The finding that key biological features, such as E/I balance and sparse connectivity, emerge naturally from the optimization objective rather than being explicitly enforced is an elegant and impactful result. Furthermore, the paper provides a mechanistic explanation for the widely observed phenomenon of stimulus-induced variability quenching, connecting the model directly to experimental data.

**Quality:** The technical work is sound and well-executed. The problem is clearly formulated, the cost function is well-motivated, and the optimization approach is appropriate. The numerical simulations are convincing and systematically demonstrate the key properties of the trained networks: successful associative recall, robustness to noise, the emergence of a balanced state, and sparse weight distributions. The analysis is thorough, and the authors are careful to validate their claims with appropriate metrics and visualizations. The paper represents a complete and polished piece of research.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem by reviewing the limitations of previous models in the introduction. The methods section clearly explains the network dynamics, the parameterization to enforce Dale's law, and the structure of the optimization objective. The results are presented logically and are easy to follow, with figures that are both informative and visually clear.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

While the paper is strong, there are several points that could be clarified or expanded upon to further increase its impact.

1.  **Biologically Plausible Learning:** The most significant limitation is that the learning process itself is not biologically plausible. The optimization is performed algorithmically with global access to the network state and parameters. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion and suggest inhibitory plasticity as a potential avenue. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how local, Hebbian-like rules might approximate or implement the objectives of this global optimization. This is a crucial step for connecting the model to biological memory formation.

2.  **Parameter Sensitivity and Choices:**
    *   The choice of `γ = 0.04` in the neuronal gain function (Eq. 1) seems specific. How sensitive are the results, particularly the stability and the nature of the E/I balance, to this parameter?
    *   The memory patterns are drawn from a log-normal distribution to mimic in-vivo rate distributions. What is the motivation for this choice from a memory encoding perspective? Would the network perform similarly if memories were drawn from a uniform or Gaussian distribution? Does the skewed distribution facilitate storage?

3.  **Fixed-Point Accuracy:** The results show that `||dv/dt||` after convergence is small but non-zero. Could the authors quantify the magnitude of this residual drift? Does it have any practical implications for the long-term stability of the memories, for instance, causing them to slowly drift over very long timescales?

4.  **Clarification on Synaptic Pruning:** The experiment described in lines 254–256 where weak synapses are clipped is interesting. When one attractor loses stability, is there anything special about that attractor or the connections supporting it? Was it, for example, an outlier in the pattern distribution? A little more detail here would be helpful.

5.  **Storage Capacity:** The paper demonstrates successful storage for a load of α = 30/150 = 0.2, but a systematic analysis of the storage capacity is missing. While a full capacity analysis may be beyond the scope of this work, a preliminary discussion or simulation showing how performance degrades as the number of stored memories increases would be a valuable addition.

6.  **Computational Role of Dale's Law:** The model correctly enforces Dale's Law as a biological constraint. It would be interesting to see a discussion on whether this constraint provides any computational benefits (e.g., does separating populations into E and I roles make the stability optimization easier?) or if it is simply a biophysical constraint that the optimization must work around.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and powerful methodology using control theory (SSA) to build biologically realistic attractor networks.
*   Provides a unified solution to several long-standing problems: enforcing Dale's Law, storing analog patterns, and avoiding saturation.
*   Demonstrates that key features like E/I balance and sparse connectivity emerge naturally from the optimization.
*   Offers a compelling mechanistic explanation for the experimentally observed reduction in neural variability.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and represents a significant advance in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The primary weakness is the lack of a biologically plausible learning mechanism; the optimization is purely algorithmic.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of storage capacity and sensitivity to key parameters.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is an insightful and important contribution that is likely to inspire future research into the dynamics and learning mechanisms of memory networks in the brain.
