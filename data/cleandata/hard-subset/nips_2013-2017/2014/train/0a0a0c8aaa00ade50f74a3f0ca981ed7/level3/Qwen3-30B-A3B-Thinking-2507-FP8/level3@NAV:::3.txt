

This paper presents a significant contribution to computational neuroscience by introducing a novel control-theoretic framework for building biologically plausible associative memory networks that simultaneously satisfy key physiological constraints—Dale’s law, non-binary representations, and non-saturated firing rates—previously neglected in prior work. The authors directly optimize synaptic weights to stabilize arbitrary analog memory patterns as fixed points while ensuring local stability via Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) minimization. Crucially, they enforce Dale’s law through weight parameterization, treat inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources (not fixed), and demonstrate that saturation avoidance emerges naturally from the optimization, eliminating the need for explicit saturation constraints. Numerical results confirm robust memory recall, noise tolerance, and a compelling explanation for reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset—aligning with empirical cortical observations. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and advances the field beyond binary Hopfield models and recent Dale’s-law-compliant LIF networks.

Strengths include the unified handling of multiple biological constraints (a major gap in prior work), elegant use of SSA for stability optimization, and strong validation of memory properties. The approach represents a substantial step toward understanding the neural substrate of memory. However, several weaknesses warrant attention. First, the absence of a biologically plausible learning rule (only a brief hint is provided) limits the paper’s translational impact. Second, the sensitivity to the choice of γ = 0.04 (Eq. 3) and the motivation for the log-normal distribution of memory states (Fig. 1a) require deeper justification. Third, the paper should quantify how much the cost function (4) violates exact fixed-point constraints (given non-zero ‖dv/dt‖ in results) and clarify statements on lines 254–256. Additionally, a preliminary discussion of memory capacity (e.g., scaling with network size) is needed, and the computational versus evolutionary rationale for Dale’s law should be addressed.

The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a significant problem in a demonstrably novel way. While the missing learning rule is a notable gap, the core contributions are robust and impactful. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the specified technical points. This paper advances both the theory and biological plausibility of memory networks, making it highly significant for NIPS.
