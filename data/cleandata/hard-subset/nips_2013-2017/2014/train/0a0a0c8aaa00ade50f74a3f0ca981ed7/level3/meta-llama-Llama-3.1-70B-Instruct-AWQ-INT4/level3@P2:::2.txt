This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for building functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints, overcoming the limitations of previous models. The authors aim to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories) in a rate-based network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons, addressing prior issues such as the violation of Dale's law and activity saturation in memory states.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the context of attractor networks and memory models. The authors build upon the work of Hopfield (1982) and subsequent models that have attempted to incorporate more realistic neuronal dynamics and physiological constraints. However, these models have often fallen short of satisfying all the relevant constraints simultaneously. The current paper proposes a new approach that directly optimizes network parameters to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics.

The strengths of the paper include its clear presentation of the model and training results, as well as its ability to combine biological constraints to produce biologically plausible features. The learned synaptic weight distribution closely matches experimental findings, and the network exhibits excitation-inhibition balance in attractor states. The authors also demonstrate the ability to achieve graded, non-saturated attractors encoded in synaptic weights and inhibitory activities.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints. The use of a smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization is also a new contribution. The paper's results constitute a step forward in understanding the neural substrate of memory and provide a new approach to building functioning attractor networks.

The paper is well-written, and the model and training results are clearly presented. The authors provide a detailed description of their methods and results, making it easy to follow their argument. The paper also includes a thorough discussion of the implications of their results and potential avenues for future research.

However, there are some potential weaknesses to the paper. One concern is that the model's ability to generalize to more complex and realistic scenarios is not fully explored. The authors demonstrate the model's performance on a specific task, but it is unclear how well it would perform on more challenging tasks or in more realistic environments. Additionally, the model's reliance on gradient descent optimization may limit its scalability and applicability to larger networks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.
