## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to building attractor networks that address several limitations of prior work in the field. The authors revisit associative memory with a fresh perspective, explicitly acknowledging and tackling issues often overlooked, such as adherence to Dale’s law, the use of non-binary representations, and the avoidance of saturated neuronal activity. Their unified strategy, leveraging recent control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques, is a significant contribution. The proposed cost function, targeting fixed points, their stability, and weight minimization, is well-motivated and effectively implemented. 

A key strength lies in the elegant enforcement of Dale’s principle through weight parameterization, naturally leading to excitation/inhibition balance. Treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources, rather than fixed components, is a clever design choice. The emergence of single-neuron saturation as a consequence of optimization, rather than direct enforcement, is a particularly noteworthy result. The validation through numerical simulations, demonstrating associative memory properties and noise robustness, further strengthens the work. The explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset provides a biologically plausible link to observed cortical phenomena. 

The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and logically organized, making it easy to follow the complex theoretical framework. It represents a substantial step forward in understanding the neural substrate of memory. However, the lack of a concrete, biologically plausible learning rule is a notable weakness. The authors acknowledge this, hinting at potential avenues for future research, but a more detailed discussion would be beneficial. 

I have several specific questions for the authors. First, could they discuss the sensitivity of their results to the choice of γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3)? What impact does this parameter have on network dynamics and performance? Second, what is the motivation for assuming a log-normal distribution for memory states? Is this based on empirical observations, or is it a simplifying assumption? Third, a quantification or discussion of how much the cost function (4) may violate exact fixed-point constraints would be valuable. Fourth, clarification of the statements on lines 254–256 regarding the relationship between the SSA and the SA would be appreciated. Finally, a preliminary discussion of memory capacity, even if limited, is crucial for understanding the scalability of this approach. 

Furthermore, it would be insightful to discuss whether Dale’s law provides genuine computational benefits, or if it is primarily an evolutionary/biophysical constraint. Exploring this distinction could further strengthen the paper’s theoretical foundation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach, strong theoretical grounding, clear writing, significant contribution to the field, biologically plausible results.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of a concrete learning rule, limited discussion of memory capacity.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential impact. Addressing the questions and suggestions above would further enhance its value. I recommend acceptance.
