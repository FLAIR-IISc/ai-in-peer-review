This paper presents a principled, control-theoretic framework for training a rate-based network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to store multiple, graded activity patterns as stable attractors. The work aims to address longstanding limitations of classic attractor models, such as the Hopfield network, by explicitly enforcing Dale’s law and avoiding reliance on neuronal saturation for stability. The authors formulate a cost function that is minimized via gradient descent, simultaneously ensuring that target patterns are fixed points of the dynamics, that these fixed points are stable (using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa), and that synaptic weights are regularized.

The paper has several significant strengths. Its primary contribution is the demonstration that by incorporating a few key biological constraints from the outset, the trained network naturally exhibits several other important, biologically plausible features. These emergent properties include a tight E-I balance during memory recall, a sparse and long-tailed synaptic weight distribution consistent with experimental observations in the cortex, and the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following a stimulus cue. The manuscript is of high quality and is exceptionally clear; the methods are well-described, and the claims are convincingly supported by the simulation results. The originality lies in the novel combination of robust control theory with attractor network design to overcome prior modeling limitations in a unified manner.

The main weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is the biological implausibility of the learning rule itself. The gradient-based optimization of a global cost function requires information that is not locally available at the synapse, positioning this work as a normative or algorithmic-level contribution rather than a mechanistic model of synaptic plasticity. Future work will be needed to determine if local, Hebbian-style rules could approximate this optimization objective.

In summary, this work provides a powerful proof of concept and a significant step forward for the field of attractor networks. By showing that a stable, balanced network capable of storing analog memories can be constructed through principled optimization, the paper offers a valuable new perspective on the computational principles that may underlie memory function in the brain. The results are important and likely to inspire future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
