## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to constructing attractor networks that address several limitations of prior work. The authors revisit the problem of associative memory, focusing on biologically realistic constraints often overlooked—Dale’s Law, non-binary representations, and avoiding saturated neuronal activity. They introduce a unified control-theoretic framework, leveraging recent advances in spectral bounding techniques to optimize network parameters for stable analog memory storage. The resulting networks exhibit a balanced E/I dynamic, robustness to noise, and explain observed reductions in trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset.

The strength of this work lies in its holistic approach. Previous models typically focused on one or two of these constraints, often at the expense of others. By formulating the problem as an optimization problem with a carefully crafted cost function—targeting fixed points, stability, and weight minimization—the authors successfully navigate these challenges simultaneously. Enforcing Dale’s principle via weight parametrization is particularly elegant, resulting in a natural excitation/inhibition balance. The emergent property of single-neuron saturation, arising from optimization rather than explicit enforcement, further enhances the biological plausibility of the model. Validation through numerical simulations strengthens the claims, and the explanation for reduced variability is a significant and insightful observation. 

However, the paper isn't without limitations. While the authors hint at potential learning rules, a concrete proposal is absent, which is a significant omission. The sensitivity of the results to the specific value of gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (3) requires further investigation. The motivation for choosing a log-normal distribution for memory states, while acknowledged as capturing observed firing rate distributions, could benefit from more detailed justification. It would also be valuable to quantify, even approximately, the extent to which the cost function (4) deviates from perfectly enforcing fixed-point constraints. Clarification regarding statements on lines 254-256 is needed. Crucially, the paper lacks a discussion of memory capacity.  Finally, exploring whether the benefits of enforcing Dale’s Law are purely computational or stem from underlying evolutionary/biophysical necessities would deepen the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a significant advance in the field of associative memory, offering a theoretically sound and biologically plausible model. Its originality, clarity, and potential impact are high.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of a concrete learning rule and the omission of a capacity analysis are notable weaknesses. 

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised regarding the learning rule, sensitivity analysis (gamma), distribution justification, cost function deviation, clarification of lines 254-256, and a preliminary discussion of memory capacity. This is a strong contribution with the potential to significantly influence future research in this area.
