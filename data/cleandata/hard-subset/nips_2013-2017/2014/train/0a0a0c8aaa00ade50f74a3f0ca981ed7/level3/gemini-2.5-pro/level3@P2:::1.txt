### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing autoassociative memory networks that adhere to several key biological constraints. The authors build a rate-based network of distinct excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons (obeying Dale's Law) whose single-neuron gain functions are non-saturating. The central contribution is an optimization procedure that directly sculpts the synaptic weight matrix to ensure that a set of pre-defined, analog (graded) activity patterns become stable fixed points of the network dynamics. This is achieved by minimizing a cost function that combines two objectives: 1) forcing the time derivative of the neural activity to be zero at the target patterns, and 2) ensuring local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound on the largest real part of the Jacobian's eigenvalues.

The authors demonstrate through simulation that this procedure successfully embeds multiple graded memories. The resulting networks are robust to noise in the initial cue and can perform reliable recall. Remarkably, the optimization process leads to emergent network properties that are biologically plausible but were not explicitly enforced: a tight balance of excitatory and inhibitory currents onto individual neurons, and a sparse-like synaptic weight distribution with a peak near zero and long tails. The paper also shows that the network dynamics replicate the phenomenon of stimulus-induced reduction in trial-to-trial variability, a common finding in cortical recordings. This work provides a significant step forward from classical models like the Hopfield network, which typically violate Dale's law or are limited to binary patterns, and from more recent models that often rely on saturating neuronal nonlinearities to ensure stability.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by thorough simulations. The authors are careful in their analysis, for instance by noting that the learned states are technically "slow points" rather than perfect fixed points, and then demonstrating that this is sufficient for robust recall. The investigation of emergent properties like E/I balance and weight sparsity, as well as the demonstration of robustness to ongoing noise, significantly strengthens the paper.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear and concise review of prior work, effectively motivating the current approach. The methods section is detailed enough to understand the model and the optimization framework. The figures are clean, informative, and effectively illustrate the main results, from the structure of the memory patterns to the dynamics of recall and the E/I balance.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building attractor networks is a classic problem, the application of a robust control-theoretic tool (the SSA) to directly optimize for the stability of analog patterns in a Dale's Law-abiding, non-saturating network is a novel and powerful idea in this context. This approach successfully circumvents the limitations of previous models that relied on specific assumptions like synaptic symmetry, global inhibition, or neuronal saturation to achieve stability.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance to both the computational neuroscience and machine learning communities. The paper addresses the long-standing question of how robust memories can be stored in biologically realistic recurrent circuits. The finding that key physiological features like E/I balance can emerge directly from an optimization principle for stable memory is profound. This work not only provides a powerful new tool for constructing functional neural circuit models but also offers a principled, normative explanation for why cortical circuits might be structured the way they are. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers studying memory, recurrent network dynamics, and cortical computation.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and powerful method for constructing biologically plausible attractor memory networks.
*   Successfully embeds analog (graded) memories, moving beyond the limitations of traditional binary models.
*   The trained networks exhibit important emergent biological features, including E/I balance and sparse-like connectivity, without these being explicitly enforced.
*   The model is shown to be robust to noise and demonstrates reliable auto-associative recall.
*   The work is technically solid, clearly communicated, and addresses a problem of fundamental importance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper does not provide a systematic analysis of the storage capacity of the network, which is a standard benchmark for comparing memory models.
*   The justification for why the chosen optimization method is uniquely suited for this problem, compared to other potential learning rules or stability criteria, could be strengthened.

### Detailed comments and suggestions for the authors

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. The approach is innovative, the results are convincing, and the conclusions are significant. My comments below are intended as suggestions to further strengthen an already impressive manuscript.

1.  **Justification of the Optimization Method:** The authors have clearly shown that their method works very well. To make the contribution even more impactful, it would be beneficial to discuss *why* this specific control-theoretic approach is so effective, or perhaps necessary, for achieving stable analog memories in non-saturating networks. Could alternative, simpler methods (e.g., training with a standard RNN algorithm like BPTT with a loss on the final state, combined with a simple weight decay or other regularizer) achieve similar results? A brief discussion explaining mechanistically why the direct optimization of stability via SSA succeeds where other methods might fail (e.g., by succumbing to runaway excitation) would provide deeper insight. Is it the explicit control over the Jacobian's spectrum that is the crucial ingredient for finding solutions in this dynamically sensitive regime?

2.  **Storage Capacity:** A classic question for any associative memory model is its storage capacity (how many patterns `m` can be stored as a function of network size `n`). While a full-blown capacity analysis may be beyond the scope of this paper, it would be useful to comment on this. Even showing results for a few different network sizes or memory loads could give the reader a sense of how the performance scales. This would also facilitate a more direct comparison with the theoretical capacities of classical models.

3.  **The Role of Sparsity:** The authors show that the learned weight matrix is sparse-like and that removing the weakest ~50% of synapses has a minor impact on performance. This is a very interesting result. Does the L2 regularizer on the weights play a significant role here, or does sparsity emerge purely from the stability constraints? Replacing the L2 norm with an L1 norm is a standard way to promote sparsity; it would be interesting to know if doing so results in even sparser networks that are equally stable, potentially making the connection to biology even stronger.

Overall, this is a fantastic contribution that elegantly bridges control theory and theoretical neuroscience to provide new insights into the neural basis of memory.
