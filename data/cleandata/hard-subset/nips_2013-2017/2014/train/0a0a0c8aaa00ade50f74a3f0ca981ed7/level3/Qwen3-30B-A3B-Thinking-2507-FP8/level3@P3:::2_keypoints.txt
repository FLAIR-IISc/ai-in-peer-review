- Addresses building attractor networks that store multiple analog memory patterns while respecting biological constraints (Dale's law, E/I balance, non-saturated activity).  
- Proposes a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes synaptic weights to make arbitrary analog patterns stable fixed points.  
- Introduces a cost function minimizing activity changes, penalizing weight norm, and promoting stability via Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization.  
- Demonstrates learned synaptic weight distributions that are sparse and long-tailed, matching experimental observations.  
- Shows attractors exhibit excitation-inhibition balance with graded, non-saturated activity encoded across excitatory and inhibitory populations.  
- Mathematical formulation is sound with clear definitions of fixed points and stability conditions.  
- Provides a practical solution to optimizing stability through SSA and careful experimental validation.  
- Experimental results include memory recall performance, noise robustness, synaptic sparseness analysis, and reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, well-organized presentation, informative figures, and reproducible methods.  
- Novelty lies in integrating multiple biological constraints and using SSA for stability in attractor networks.  
- Significant contribution: offers a biologically plausible mechanism for analog memory storage aligning with cortical data.  
- Limitation: study is restricted to rate-based networks; spiking implementations are suggested but not explored.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong, original, clearly presented, and recommended for acceptance.
