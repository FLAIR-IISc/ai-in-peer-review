The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints, overcoming the limitations of previous models. They directly optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. The resulting networks operate in the balanced regime, are robust to corruptions of the memory cue as well as to ongoing noise, and incidentally explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset that is ubiquitously observed in sensory and motor cortices.

The paper is well-written, and the model, training, and results are clearly presented. The authors provide a thorough review of previous work on attractor networks, highlighting their limitations and how their proposed framework addresses these issues. The use of a rate-based network trained to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories) is a significant contribution to the field.

The authors' goal is to fix prior attractor-model issues, including the violation of Dale's law and activity saturation in memory states. They achieve this by training the network via gradient descent on a cost that minimizes activity changes in target attractors, penalizes weight Frobenius norm, and promotes attractor stability. The learned synaptic weight distribution closely matches experimental findings, and the network exhibits excitation-inhibition balance in attractor states.

The results show that the network achieves graded, non-saturated attractors encoded in synaptic weights and inhibitory activities. The authors demonstrate the robustness of the network to ongoing noise and corruption of the recall cues, as well as the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to building attractor networks, its ability to satisfy physiological constraints, and its robustness to noise and corruption. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their results, including the distribution of synaptic weights, the balance of excitation and inhibition, and the reduction of trial-to-trial variability.

The paper's weaknesses include the use of a rate-based model, which may not be directly applicable to spiking networks. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that the connectivity generated by their model could still be used in a spiking model, provided the gain function matches that of the single spiking neurons.
