This paper revisits associative memory with a fresh and insightful perspective, making important contributions to the field. The authors develop a unified strategy to address key biological constraints, including Dale's law, non-rate/binary representations, and non-sparse memories, using recent control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology. The authors propose a cost function with three terms targeting memory fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation. They also enforce Dale's principle in weight parameterization, producing a natural excitation/inhibition balance.

The authors use excitatory neurons to encode memories and treat inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources. Single-neuron saturation emerges naturally from optimization despite not being directly enforced. The approach is validated with numerical simulations demonstrating associative memory properties and noise robustness.

The paper explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via convergence to the nearest attractor. However, there are some areas that require further discussion. For instance, the authors should discuss the sensitivity to the choice of gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (3). Additionally, the motivation for assuming a log-normal distribution for memory states should be clarified.

The authors should also quantify and discuss how much the cost function (4) may violate exact fixed-point constraints. Furthermore, the statements on lines 254-256 require clarification. A preliminary discussion of memory capacity would also be beneficial.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset via convergence to the nearest attractor. However, a weakness is the lack of a biologically plausible learning rule, with the authors only hinting at one.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of associative memory and is well-written and clear. However, there are some areas that require further discussion and clarification.
