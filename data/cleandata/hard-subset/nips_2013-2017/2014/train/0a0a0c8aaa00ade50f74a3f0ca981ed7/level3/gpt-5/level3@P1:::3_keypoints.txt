- Proposal: control-theoretic method to build rate-based E/I recurrent networks that store arbitrary analog memories as stable attractors under physiological constraints.
- Core idea: jointly enforce fixed-point conditions for target patterns and local stability by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian.
- Dale’s law: enforced via a log-exp reparameterization of weights; inhibitory units treated as auxiliary variables optimized per attractor.
- Results: networks operate in a dynamically balanced regime, recall from corrupted cues near ideal-observer performance, are robust to ongoing noise, and show variability quenching.
- Synthesis: integrates ideas from associative memory, balanced networks, bump attractors, and robust control while addressing biological shortcomings of prior models.
- Strength: principled unified formulation using SSA for smooth objective and gradient-based optimization under biological constraints.
- Strength: explicit enforcement of E/I separation, low-rate unsaturated activity, and E/I balance without ad hoc saturation.
- Strength: demonstrates robust autoassociative recall for analog patterns close to an ideal baseline.
- Strength: emergent realistic weight statistics (heavy-tailed, many weak synapses) without explicit sparsity priors; many weak synapses appear non-critical.
- Strength: provides a mechanistic link between attractor dynamics and stimulus-driven variability quenching.
- Weakness: synaptic and per-attractor inhibitory optimizations use global gradients; no biologically plausible local learning rule is provided.
- Weakness: capacity is only illustrated for one scale (n=150, m=30, ~0.2) with no scaling laws or dependence on SSA smoothing ε.
- Weakness: residual fixed-point violations (nonzero ‖dv/dt‖ at targets) are noted but not quantified (distance to true fixed point, basin size, drift time).
- Weakness: SSA hyperparameters (ε, ηs) and their tuning are heuristic; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Weakness: treating inhibitory steady states as free per-attractor parameters raises interpretability and biological implementation questions.
- Weakness: assumptions on single-neuron gain (γ=0.04) and memory statistics (log-normal) are untested and unjustified for robustness.
- Quality: method is technically sound, objective well-motivated and tractable; experiments convincing at explored scale but need quantitative capacity and sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized with explicit setup and supportive figures, but some statements and numeric metrics need clarification.
- Originality: novel combination of SSA-based robust control with E/I attractor design and per-memory inhibitory auxiliary variables.
- Significance: approach could impact cortical memory modeling if scalable; noise robustness and variability quenching are compelling, but lack of plausible learning rule limits immediate impact.
- Requested analyses: sweep γ to test sensitivity, justify/test log-normal memory statistics, quantify fixed-point violations and basin sizes, and clarify convergence claims (lines 254–256).
- Requested analyses: provide preliminary capacity scaling with n, m, pattern variance and examine ε/ηF effects and failure modes as m increases.
- Requested analyses: ablation comparing Dale’s law enforcement versus unconstrained signs to test computational benefits.
- Requested explanation: propose mechanisms for per-attractor inhibitory steady states (plasticity, interneuron diversity, modulatory gating) and test performance without v_inh optimization.
- Requested analyses: report sensitivity to SSA ε and weight regularization ηF, and include results on larger networks.
- Pros for acceptance: principled novel framework, strong empirical demonstrations, clear writing and literature connections.
- Cons for acceptance: no biologically plausible learning rule, uncharacterized capacity and parameter robustness, and unquantified fixed-point approximation errors.
