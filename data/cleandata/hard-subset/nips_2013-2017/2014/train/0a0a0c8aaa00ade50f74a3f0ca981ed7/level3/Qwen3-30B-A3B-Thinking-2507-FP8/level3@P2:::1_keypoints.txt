- Presents a framework for biologically plausible rate-based memory networks that store arbitrary analog patterns as stable fixed points while respecting physiological constraints.  
- Addresses prior model limitations (violations of Dale’s law, binary-only memories, reliance on saturating neurons).  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to enforce fixed-point conditions and local stability, avoiding spectral abscissa non-smoothness.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced excitatory-inhibitory (E-I) regime with graded, non-saturating activity.  
- Networks are robust to noise and corrupted memory cues, matching cortical physiology observations.  
- Technical contribution is strong: adapts robust control theory (SSA) to neural dynamics effectively.  
- Experimental validation is thorough: demonstrates stable fixed points, realistic synaptic distributions, per-neuron E-I balance, and reduced trial-to-trial variability.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, with precise methods (Eqs. 1–5) and well-presented figures (Figs. 1–6).  
- Significant advance: provides a physiologically faithful pathway to model cortical memory substrates and may inform inhibitory plasticity learning rules.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for choosing SSA over alternatives (e.g., Lyapunov-based methods) and lack of discussion on SSA trade-offs like ε-parameter sensitivity.  
- Weakness: storage capacity (0.2 for 150 neurons) is reported but not benchmarked against prior analog-memory models or theoretical limits.  
- Weakness: training procedure lacks a mechanistic explanation linking SSA-based optimization to the emergence of graded activity and E-I balance.  
- Recommendation: recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify SSA choice and provide capacity comparisons to strengthen claims.
