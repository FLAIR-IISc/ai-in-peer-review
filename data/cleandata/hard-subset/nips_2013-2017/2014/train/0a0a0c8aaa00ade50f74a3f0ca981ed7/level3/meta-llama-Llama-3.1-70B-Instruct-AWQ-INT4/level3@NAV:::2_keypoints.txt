- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to train excitatory–inhibitory attractor networks that store arbitrary analog patterns as stable fixed points, operate in a balanced regime, are noise-robust, and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Quality: technically sound, well-motivated, with thorough theoretical and experimental analysis and careful evaluation of strengths and weaknesses.  
- Clarity: clearly written and well-organized, providing enough detail for expert reproduction; figures effectively illustrate key points.  
- Originality: novel combination of familiar techniques that is distinct from prior work and properly situated in the literature.  
- Significance: addresses a difficult problem better than previous approaches and offers new insights into neural memory storage and retrieval.  
- Pro-acceptance: presents a novel, physiologically constrained approach; results are well-supported; accessible presentation; potential to advance the field.  
- Con-acceptance: uses a rate-based formulation that may not generalize to spiking networks; lacks a clear account of how local synaptic learning rules could implement the derived stabilization objective.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution—recommend acceptance.
