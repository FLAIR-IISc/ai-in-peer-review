This paper introduces a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model that explicitly addresses several limitations of previous work, namely incorporating Dale’s Law, utilizing graded neuronal responses, and achieving stable dynamics. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters for storing and retrieving analog patterns as fixed points, leveraging recent advances in robust control (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa). Simulations demonstrate stable fixed points with slow dynamics, biologically plausible synaptic weight distributions, excitatory/inhibitory balance, and robustness to noise. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results. It presents a novel and significant contribution with high potential impact on the field.

The work builds upon a long history of auto-associative memory models, beginning with the Hopfield network [1]. While Hopfield’s work established the foundation, it suffered from binary representations and violations of Dale's Law. Subsequent attempts to address these limitations, such as those incorporating graded responses [3, 4] or LIF neurons [7], often struggled with stability or practical capacity limitations [5, 6]. More recent efforts [8, 9, 12] introduced aspects of biological realism but still faced issues with binary-like memories or limited applicability. This paper successfully integrates many of these considerations, representing a substantial step forward. The clear literature review effectively contextualizes the contribution within this broader landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is mathematically well-defined and grounded in control theory. The use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa is a clever approach to tackle the stability problem, and the results clearly demonstrate stable fixed points and slow dynamics.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The concepts are explained intuitively, and the figures effectively illustrate the key findings.
* **Originality:** The combination of a rate-based model, biological constraints (Dale’s Law, graded responses, E/I balance), and a control-theoretic optimization framework is novel and represents a significant departure from previous approaches.
* **Significance:** The results are important for understanding the neural mechanisms underlying memory and could have implications for building more biologically realistic and robust artificial memory systems. The observed features—synaptic weight distributions and E/I balance—are consistent with neurophysiological data. The demonstration of robustness to noise is also highly significant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Training Procedure Justification:** While the chosen training procedure, based on minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, appears effective, the paper could benefit from a more detailed justification of *why* this method was selected over other optimization techniques. Specifically, it would be valuable to discuss whether this approach is particularly well-suited for enforcing graded, non-saturating memories, and how it differs in its properties from alternative gradient-based methods. A more mechanistic explanation of why this method succeeds in achieving stability with graded units would strengthen the work.
* **Alternative Training Procedures:** It would be beneficial to address whether alternative training procedures could achieve similar results. Exploring the parameter space of different optimization algorithms or loss functions could reveal the robustness of the findings.
* **Capacity Comparison:** A comparison of the trained network’s performance (in terms of capacity – number of memories stored vs. network size) to that of previous models would be valuable. While the authors mention capacity briefly, a quantitative comparison with established models would provide a better understanding of the model’s strengths and limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of auto-associative memory models.
* The results are significant, demonstrating stable memory storage and retrieval in a biologically plausible network.
* The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.
* The work addresses several key limitations of previous models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The justification for the specific training procedure is somewhat lacking.
* A quantitative comparison of capacity to other models is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The originality, technical soundness, and potential significance of the work far outweigh the areas for improvement. Addressing the suggested clarifications would further enhance the paper, but are not critical for acceptance. The manuscript is of high quality and a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
