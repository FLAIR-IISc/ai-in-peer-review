- Proposes a control-theoretic optimization to build rate-based excitatory/inhibitory (Dale’s law) networks that store and robustly retrieve multiple analog memories as stable fixed points.
- Formulates two storage criteria: each pattern must be a fixed point and that fixed point must be locally stable.
- Optimizes synaptic weights (with sign reparameterization for Dale’s law) and per-memory inhibitory steady-states to minimize fixed-point drift and the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), with L2 regularization.
- Uses SSA from robust control as a differentiable proxy for local stability, implemented with L-BFGS optimization.
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced E/I regime, retrieve graded patterns without saturation, and show sparse, long-tailed weight distributions similar to cortical data.
- Demonstrates robustness to cue corruption and ongoing noise, with stimulus-onset variability reduction and near–ideal-observer recall performance in experiments (n=150, m=30; capacity ≈0.2).
- Novelty: applying SSA/robust-control methods to shape multiple attractors in biologically constrained E/I networks is original.
- Situates work relative to Hopfield models, graded-memory models, bump attractors, and spike-timing schemes, unifying several physiological constraints in one framework.
- Technical soundness: two-part objective is well motivated and SSA provides theoretical guarantees (SSA ≥ SA and SSA ≤ 0 ⇒ SA < 0).
- Empirical support is convincing for the reported regime, with figures illustrating recall vs noise, E/I balance, and weight statistics.
- Limitation: fixed-point drift is reduced but not eliminated—learned patterns become “slow points” rather than exact fixed points.
- Limitation: stability argued via SSA and empirical recall without directly verifying the true Spectral Abscissa (SA) at final operating points.
- Limitation: basin sizes are probed only indirectly via cue noise; systematic basin characterization is missing.
- Limitation: capacity and scaling with network size, number of memories, and parameters (n, m, ε) lack theoretical analysis and thorough empirical scaling.
- Clarity: manuscript is well written with helpful figures, but needs more detail on SSA computation/gradients, optimization convergence, runtime, and algorithmic steps for reproducibility.
- Suggest reporting SA post-training, quantifying residual dv/dt at target states, and demonstrating nearby true fixed points explicitly.
- Suggest characterizing basin sizes more systematically (e.g., distance-to-boundary statistics) and testing sensitivity to hyperparameters ε and regularizers.
- Suggest exploring structural sparsity (fixed sparse masks), role of per-memory inhibitory variables, other gain exponents, and steps toward spiking implementations.
- Suggest comparing against baselines (Hebbian, balanced-memory constructions, inhibitory-plasticity approaches) to contextualize performance and capacity.
- Significance: conceptually bridges attractor memory models and cortical physiology; methodologically useful for other circuit-design problems.
- Caveat: immediate impact limited by lack of capacity/scaling analysis, sparse comparisons, and underreported training details.
- Recommendation: weak accept—novel and promising but needs added analyses on capacity, scaling, stability verification, and comparative baselines.
