- Proposes a principled control-theoretic framework to train rate-based excitatory–inhibitory (E–I) networks to store multiple graded activity patterns as stable attractors.  
- Explicitly enforces Dale’s law and avoids relying on neuronal saturation for stability.  
- Formulates a cost function minimized via gradient descent that ensures target patterns are fixed points, enforces stability using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, and regularizes synaptic weights.  
- Shows that incorporating a few biological constraints yields emergent, biologically plausible features (tight E–I balance during recall).  
- Emergent synaptic properties include a sparse, long-tailed weight distribution consistent with cortical data.  
- Demonstrates reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus cues in the trained networks.  
- Manuscript is clear, methods well-described, and simulation results convincingly support the claims.  
- Original contribution: novel integration of robust control theory with attractor network design to overcome prior modeling limitations.  
- Main weakness: the gradient-based learning rule is biologically implausible and requires nonlocal information at synapses.  
- Positions the work as a normative/algorithmic-level contribution and calls for future development of local, Hebbian-like approximations.  
- Overall assessment: a strong proof of concept that significantly advances attractor network modeling and merits acceptance.
