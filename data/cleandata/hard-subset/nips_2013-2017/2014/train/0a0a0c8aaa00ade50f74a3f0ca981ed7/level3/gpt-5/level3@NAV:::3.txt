This paper revisits associative memory with a fresh control-theoretic approach that simultaneously addresses several biological constraints too often isolated in prior work. The authors construct rate-based E–I networks that obey Dale’s law via a sign-constrained weight parameterization, encode analog (non-binary) memories in excitatory neurons while treating inhibitory neurons as optimizable resources, and achieve stability through minimization of the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA). The objective combines three terms that target (i) fixed points (small dv/dt), (ii) fixed-point stability (negative SSA), and (iii) small weights to avoid saturation. Resulting networks operate in a dynamically balanced regime without explicit saturation in the neuronal nonlinearity, exhibit robust autoassociative recall under cue corruption and ongoing noise, and offer a mechanistic account of stimulus-induced reductions in trial-to-trial variability as convergence to the nearest attractor. This unifies strands from Hopfield-style memory, balanced networks, and recent spectral-bounding techniques from control.

Quality: Technically solid and carefully executed; the stability analysis and numerical validations are convincing, and the emergence of E/I balance and non-saturation is noteworthy. A limitation is the lack of a biologically plausible learning rule; only speculative avenues are discussed.

Clarity: The paper is clear and well organized. Please clarify the statements on lines 254–256 and provide more guidance on hyperparameter choices.

Originality: High. Bringing SSA-based spectral shaping to associative memory under realistic E/I constraints is novel and insightful.

Significance: Strong. The framework is likely to influence both computational neuroscience and control-informed neural modeling and could catalyze new work on biologically grounded associative memories.

Requests for revision:
- Sensitivity to gain parameter: Please report how results (stability, balance, recall) depend on γ = 0.04 in Eq. (3), and whether re-tuning ηs/ηF is required when γ varies.
- Pattern statistics: Motivate the log-normal assumption for memory states; assess robustness to alternative distributions (e.g., truncated Gaussian, exponential).
- Fixed-point accuracy: Since dv/dt is not driven exactly to zero, quantify residual drift per attractor at convergence and its impact on recall (link to Eq. (4)’s constraints versus the surrogate in Eq. (5)).
- Capacity: Provide at least preliminary capacity estimates (m/N scaling, dependence on sparsity and SSA ε), and compare to classical bounds for analog patterns.
- Dale’s law: Discuss whether Dale’s constraint yields computational benefits (e.g., robustness, energy shaping, generalization) beyond biophysical plausibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified, principled treatment of stability with realistic E/I structure
- Robust analog recall and variability reduction explained mechanistically
- Clear writing and strong simulations

Cons for acceptance:
- No biologically plausible learning rule
- Limited analysis of capacity and hyperparameter sensitivity
- Assumption of log-normal memory statistics not fully justified
