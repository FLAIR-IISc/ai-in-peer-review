- Frames memory storage as a control problem to build biologically plausible attractor networks that respect Dale’s Law and avoid activity saturation.  
- Introduces the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to enforce robust stability during gradient-descent optimization.  
- Produces a balanced excitatory-inhibitory network with graded, non-saturated attractor states.  
- Learned networks match experimental synaptic distributions and show robustness to noise.  
- Optimization yields intrinsic sparsity and reproduces variability reduction after stimulus onset.  
- Advances prior attractor models by explicitly incorporating Dale’s Law and realistic non-saturating dynamics.  
- Mathematical formulation is rigorous and the manuscript is exceptionally well written.  
- Strong experimental validation through comparisons to biological data (synaptic distributions, E/I balance).  
- Originality lies in combining biological constraints with a control-theoretic SSA optimization framework.  
- High significance: provides a promising new direction for biologically realistic memory models.  
- Weakness: computational cost of the optimization may hinder scaling to much larger networks.  
- Weakness: reliance on rate-based formulation may limit direct applicability to spiking networks (partly discussed).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, offers rigorous methods, strong empirical alignment, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational expense and limited direct translation to spiking models.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
