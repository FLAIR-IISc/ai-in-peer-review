This paper presents a novel approach to modeling auto-associative memory in a rate-based network of excitatory and inhibitory (E-I) neurons. The authors introduce a control-theoretic framework that overcomes several limitations of previous models, including the incorporation of Dale's law, non-saturating graded units, and the ability to store and retrieve analog patterns.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough literature review. The authors effectively describe their architecture and training procedure, and the figures are informative and easy to understand. The manuscript is novel and likely to be of strong interest to the community.

The authors' approach to modeling memory storage and retrieval is based on optimizing network parameters to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. They use a Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to perform robust stability optimization, which is a more suitable stability measure than the traditional spectral abscissa. The results show that the trained networks operate in the balanced regime, are robust to corruptions of the memory cue as well as to ongoing noise, and incidentally explain the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset.

The simulations demonstrate that the trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow dynamics. The trained networks also display biologically consistent features, including a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron. The networks are robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, demonstrating reliable auto-associative recall.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to incorporate key physiological constraints in a unified way. The authors' approach to modeling memory storage and retrieval is based on optimizing network parameters to force sets of arbitrary analog patterns to become stable fixed points of the dynamics. This approach allows for the incorporation of Dale's law, non-saturating graded units, and the ability to store and retrieve analog patterns.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion on why the chosen training procedure was selected and whether it is particularly suited to enforce graded, non-saturating memories versus other methods. The authors should consider discussing alternative training procedures and explaining mechanistically why their method succeeds.

Another potential weakness is the lack of comparison to previous models in terms of capacity (number of memories vs network size). While the authors provide a thorough literature review, they do not explicitly compare their model to previous models in terms of capacity. This comparison would be useful in understanding the strengths and limitations of their approach.
