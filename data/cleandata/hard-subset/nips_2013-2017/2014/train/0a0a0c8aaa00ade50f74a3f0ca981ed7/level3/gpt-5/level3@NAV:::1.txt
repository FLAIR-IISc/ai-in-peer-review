This paper introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory network that enforces key biological constraints (Dale’s Law, explicit E/I populations, non-saturating graded units) while directly optimizing stability of target fixed points. The authors formulate storage as jointly minimizing drift at desired states and the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian, a control-theoretic surrogate that enables gradient-based stabilization. Simulations demonstrate that learned fixed points are locally stable, exhibit slow near-attractor dynamics, robust auto-associative recall under large cue corruption and ongoing noise, and produce cortical-like phenomena: E/I balance, heavy-tailed weight distributions centered near zero, and stimulus-onset variability quenching. The work sits at the intersection of classic associative memory (Hopfield; Treves & Rolls), balanced networks and stabilized supralinear dynamics, and recent robust control tools (SSA), and compares favorably to an “ideal observer” in recall performance.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and carefully executed. The SSA-based training is appropriate for enforcing local stability and is evaluated across multiple metrics (stability, robustness, emergent statistics). Some claims (e.g., “near fixed points”) are supported empirically but would benefit from more systematic quantification of basin sizes and scaling.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written, with an informative literature review, transparent objectives, and effective figures. A few methodological choices merit further explanation (see below).

Originality: The combination of graded, non-saturating units with strict Dale constraint and a differentiable stability objective is novel and timely. The emergence of balanced dynamics without saturating nonlinearities is particularly interesting.

Significance: Likely to be of strong interest to computational neuroscience and control-oriented ML. The framework could influence how attractor dynamics are trained in biologically constrained RNNs.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Please clarify why SSA-based training was chosen over alternatives (e.g., Lyapunov/energy shaping, contraction metrics, Jacobian spectral norm or Gershgorin constraints, FORCE-like schemes), and whether it is uniquely suited to achieving graded, non-saturating memories.
- Provide a mechanistic account of why the approach succeeds (eigenvalue spectra and modes at attractors; role of inhibition; link to stabilized supralinear network theory).
- Capacity: report scaling with network size and compare to prior graded-memory capacities (e.g., Treves-Rolls), including basin size and interference analyses.
- Ablations: sensitivity to ε, regularization strength, initializations; effect of clipping weak synapses; sparsity origins.
- Discuss computational cost and scalability to larger networks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong biological plausibility; robust recall with noise.
- Clear, well-motivated control-theoretic training.
- Emergent E/I balance and realistic synaptic statistics.
- High clarity and novelty.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited capacity/scale benchmarks and theoretical guarantees on basins.
- Comparative analysis with alternative stabilization methods is missing.
- Scalability and computational cost not quantified.
