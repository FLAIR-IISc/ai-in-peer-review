- Presents a novel, unified framework that builds associative memory networks satisfying multiple biological constraints simultaneously.  
- Uses control-theoretic tools (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to enforce stability of arbitrary analog attractors.  
- Parameterizes connectivity to explicitly enforce Dale’s law during optimization.  
- Optimization yields networks with balanced E/I dynamics, sparse connectivity, robustness to noise, and low non-saturating firing rates.  
- Technical formulation is clear and sound; SSA is an effective solution for the non-smooth eigenvalue optimization.  
- Numerical simulations convincingly demonstrate autoassociative recall, robustness to cue degradation, and emergent biological features.  
- Several realistic properties (tight E/I balance, low firing rates, sparse weight distribution) emerge from optimization rather than being enforced.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clearly presented.  
- Major weakness: learning via global L-BFGS is not biologically plausible; local synaptic plasticity approximations are not provided.  
- Missing systematic analysis of memory capacity and how performance scales with increasing number of memories.  
- Target patterns are optimized to “slow points” rather than true fixed points; residual drift (`||dv/dt||` > 0) should be quantified and discussed.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters needs clarification (e.g., choice and effect of gain γ = 0.04) and justification for log-normal memory pattern distribution.  
- Request elaboration on functional implications of high E/I input correlation across memories (computational advantages or coding consequences).  
- Suggest discussing whether enforcing Dale’s law provides computational benefits (stability or performance) versus being purely biophysical.  
- Overall recommendation: high-quality, original contribution likely to inspire future work; recommend acceptance despite noted analyses to strengthen impact.
