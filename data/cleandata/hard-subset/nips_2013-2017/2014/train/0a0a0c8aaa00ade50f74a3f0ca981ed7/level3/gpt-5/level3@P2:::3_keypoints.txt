- Proposes a control-theoretic method to build rate-based E-I networks that store robust analog memories while enforcing Dale’s law, sparse connectivity, low non-saturating firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance.
- Optimizes synaptic weights via a softplus reparameterization to enforce Dale’s law and uses a fixed-point objective plus a Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) stability objective with L2 regularization.
- Treats inhibitory steady-state activities per attractor as auxiliary optimization variables; learned networks show robust autoassociative recall, neuron-by-neuron E/I balance, and stimulus-driven variability quenching.
- Novelty: first to apply SSA-based robust stability optimization to construct multiple analog attractors in biologically constrained E-I networks.
- Technical soundness: two-part objective and SSA are appropriate and enable gradient-based training; Dale’s-law reparameterization is practical and clean.
- Empirical results: stores 30 analog patterns in a 150-neuron network (α=0.2), robust to cue corruption/noise, shows balanced currents, variability quenching, and emergent sparse effective connectivity.
- Limitation: capacity demonstrated empirically but lacks theoretical analysis and scaling laws.
- Limitation: basins of attraction are probed empirically but not quantitatively characterized.
- Limitation: learning is global/algorithmic (not biologically local), raising biological interpretability concerns.
- Limitation: sensitivity analyses are limited (notably for gain γ, SSA ε, nI/nE, and pattern statistics).
- Concern: treating inhibitory activities as per-attractor free variables aids optimization but raises questions about capacity, biological plausibility, and scalability.
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized, but needs clarification in a few spots and a centralized listing of training hyperparameters and scaling rules.
- Significance: strong — provides a constructive recipe for biologically plausible analog attractors with robust dynamics and links to cortical variability phenomena.
- Request: provide sensitivity analysis for gain γ (sweep with rescaling of W/h) showing effects on recall, stability margins, and firing-rate distributions.
- Request: justify log-normal memory-rate choice and report performance for alternative pattern statistics (Gaussian, bimodal, heavy-tailed) and impact on E/I balance and sparsity.
- Request: quantify fixed-point accuracy — residual drift magnitudes, distances to nearest true fixed points, and frequency/magnitude of recall deviations from targets.
- Request: clarify how “exact, stable fixed points near targets” was verified (e.g., numerically locating fixed points and checking Jacobians).
- Request: provide at least preliminary capacity analysis (scaling with n, nE/nI, m; effects of ε and L2), and compare to graded Hopfield limits under Dale’s law and non-saturating gains.
- Request: discuss role and regularization of inhibitory degrees of freedom; report effects of constraining or removing per-attractor inhibitory optimization and varying nI/nE.
- Request: give more SSA details — choice and scaling of ε with n, and report both SSA and spectral abscissa (SA) at convergence for all attractors.
- Request: discuss whether Dale’s-law constraint yields computational benefits beyond biological realism (e.g., stability, balancing, generalization).
- Request: quantify sparsity across runs (fraction of near-zero weights) and report recall/basin performance before/after clipping under fixed success criteria.
- Request: improve reproducibility by providing optimizer hyperparameters, stopping criteria, initialization ranges, and consider releasing code.
- Pros summary: unified constructive method, elegant SSA-based robustness, strong empirical demonstrations, emergent sparsity, clear writing and neuroscience relevance.
- Cons summary: missing theoretical capacity/basin analysis, global learning rule, limited sensitivity studies, interpretability of per-attractor inhibitory variables, and some clarity gaps about fixed points.
- Overall recommendation: Accept — substantial, novel contribution with strong empirical support; further analyses on capacity, sensitivity, and fixed-point claims would strengthen the paper.
