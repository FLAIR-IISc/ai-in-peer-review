Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to constructing rate-based recurrent E-I networks that store and robustly recall analog (graded) memories while respecting key physiological constraints: Dale’s law, sparse recurrent connectivity, low firing rates without imposed saturation, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors directly optimize synaptic weights (via a softplus parameterization that enforces Dale’s law) so that designated excitatory patterns become (asymptotically) stable fixed points. They combine a fixed-point objective (minimizing drift at target states) with a robust stability objective based on the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control, plus L2 regularization. Inhibitory activity patterns at each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables during optimization; after learning, the network exhibits robust autoassociative recall, balanced E/I currents, and stimulus-onset quenching of variability.

This work addresses limitations of classic Hopfield-style models [1,2], graded-capacity analyses [3–5], spiking/balanced network memory models [6–9], spatial “bump” attractors [10,11], and spike-timing models lacking explicit inhibition [12]. Unlike much of that literature, the authors jointly enforce Dale’s law, analog memories, and low-rate operation without saturations, and they explicitly optimize local stability rather than relying on indirect mechanisms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-founded. The two-part objective (fixed point + SSA-based stability) directly targets what is required for attractor memories. The SSA is an appropriate smooth proxy for the spectral abscissa [13,14], enabling gradient-based optimization. The reparameterization of W to enforce Dale’s law is clean and practical.
- Empirical validation: The paper demonstrates (i) successful storage and recall of 30 analog patterns in a 150-neuron network (α = 0.2), (ii) robustness to cue corruption and ongoing noise, (iii) balanced E/I currents neuron-by-neuron across attractors, and (iv) stimulus-driven reduction of trial-to-trial variability consistent with [21–23]. The sparsity of effective synapses and robustness to clipping small weights are informative.
- Limitations: Capacity is demonstrated but not analyzed; basins of attraction are probed empirically but not quantified; learning is global/algorithmic rather than biologically grounded; sensitivity analyses are limited (e.g., gain parameter γ). Treating inhibitory steady-state activities as per-attractor free variables during optimization is powerful but raises questions about biological interpretability and capacity scaling.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The modeling choices are transparent, and the figures are helpful. A few spots need clarification (see questions below), and it would help to gather key training hyperparameters and scaling rules (e.g., ε for SSA) in one place for reproducibility.

Originality
High. Using SSA-based robust stability optimization to directly construct multiple analog attractors in a biologically constrained E-I network is novel relative to Hopfield-type learning, graded-capacity theory without explicit inhibition, and prior balanced/spiking models that effectively reintroduce binarization or rely on saturation.

Significance
Strong. The method provides a unifying, constructive recipe for building biologically plausible analog attractor networks with robust dynamics and balanced operation. The link to variability quenching offers an appealing systems-level explanation. If capacity and scalability can be better characterized, this could become a widely used design/analysis tool in theoretical neuroscience and neuromorphic control.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Sensitivity to gain (γ = 0.04): Please provide a sensitivity analysis (e.g., sweeping γ while compensating with rescaling of W and h) showing robustness of recall, stability margins (SA/SSA), and firing-rate distributions.
- Distributional assumptions: You motivate log-normal memory rates (in line with in vivo firing rate distributions [18]). Please (i) expand the motivation and (ii) report how performance changes for alternative pattern statistics (e.g., Gaussian, bimodal, heavier-tailed) and whether E/I balance and sparsity persist.
- Fixed-point accuracy: Since ‖dv/dt‖ at targets remains non-zero, please quantify: the residual drift magnitudes; distances to the nearest true fixed point; and how often recalls converge to a point measurably different from the target (and by how much). A table or histogram would help.
- Clarify lines 254–256: The text claims “an exact, stable fixed point had in fact been created very near each target pattern” despite small residual drift. Please clarify how this was verified (e.g., by numerically locating fixed points and evaluating their Jacobians).
- Capacity: Provide at least a preliminary analysis (scaling with n, nE/nI, and m; effect of SSA parameter ε and L2 regularization). How does capacity compare to classic graded Hopfield limits [3–5] once Dale’s law and non-saturating gains are imposed? How do basins scale with load α?
- Role of inhibitory degrees of freedom: Since vinhµ are optimized per attractor, please discuss how this affects capacity and robustness. Are there regularizers on inhibitory activity magnitudes? What happens if vinhµ are not optimized (or are constrained), or if nI/nE varies?
- SSA details: Provide more detail on choosing ε and its scaling with n, and report both SSA and SA at convergence for all attractors to show the gap and robustness margins.
- Dale’s law: Beyond biological realism, do your results suggest computational benefits (e.g., improved stability/balancing or generalization)? A brief discussion weighing computational vs biophysical origins would be valuable.
- Sparsity and clipping: Since L2 does not induce sparsity, it would help to quantify across runs the fraction of near-zero weights, and to report recall/basins before/after clipping under a fixed success criterion (rather than changing the threshold).
- Reproducibility: Please provide implementation details sufficient to reproduce results (optimizer hyperparameters, stopping criteria, initialization ranges), and consider releasing code.

Pros
- Unified, principled construction of analog attractors in E-I networks honoring Dale’s law.
- Robust stability via SSA is technically elegant and effective.
- Strong empirical demonstrations: recall under noise, E/I balance, variability quenching.
- Emergent sparse effective connectivity without explicit L1.
- Clear writing and relevance to both theory and systems neuroscience.

Cons
- No theoretical capacity analysis or basin-size characterization.
- Learning is global/algorithmic; biological plasticity is only discussed speculatively.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (γ, ε, nI/nE, pattern statistics).
- Use of per-attractor inhibitory variables during training requires further interpretation.
- Some clarity gaps around residual drift vs exact fixed points.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a fresh and substantial contribution that advances the construction and understanding of biologically plausible analog memory networks, with clear empirical support and compelling connections to cortical dynamics. My enthusiasm would be further increased by additional analyses on capacity, sensitivity, and clarification of the fixed-point claims.
