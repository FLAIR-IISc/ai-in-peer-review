- Proposes a novel control-theoretic optimization framework to construct attractor networks that store and retrieve analog memory patterns while obeying biological constraints.  
- Simultaneously enforces Dale’s law, graded activity (analog patterns), low firing rates, and dynamic E–I balance—constraints often violated in prior models.  
- Formulates learning as minimizing a cost combining fixed-point conditions (minimizing activity derivatives) and stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa.  
- Method naturally yields sparse connectivity and balanced excitation–inhibition without explicit regularization for those properties.  
- Numerical experiments show robust storage and retrieval of analog patterns, resilience to noisy cues, and explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Demonstrates capacity of 30 patterns (capacity ≈ 0.2) for n = 150 in reported simulations.  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and clear mathematical definitions (e.g., Eq. 4).  
- Use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa for stability optimization is novel in a neuroscience context.  
- Exceptionally well-written, with clear figures and reproducibility details (parameters and protocols).  
- Major weakness: lacks a biologically plausible learning rule; suggested inhibitory plasticity is speculative without analysis.  
- Insufficient justification for specific choices: gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (1) and the log-normal distribution of memory states.  
- Limited discussion and quantification of memory capacity beyond the single reported value.  
- Does not fully address whether Dale’s law confers computational advantages versus being an evolutionary/biological constraint.  
- Reviewer suggestions: justify gamma, motivate log-normal priors, quantify fixed-point constraint violation, clarify specified manuscript lines, add preliminary capacity analysis, and discuss computational role of Dale’s law.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; strong, significant contribution to theoretical neuroscience.
