- Introduces a novel control-theoretic optimization framework to construct attractor networks that store graded, analog memory patterns under biological constraints.  
- Addresses prior limitations by enforcing Dale’s law, non-binary graded firing rates, and separate excitatory/inhibitory populations.  
- Formulates memory storage via a cost function that enforces each pattern to be a fixed point and to be stable.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control as a differentiable proxy to enforce stability tractably.  
- Applies the method to rate-based E/I networks and successfully produces networks that store multiple stable analog attractors.  
- Trained networks perform pattern completion from noisy cues with performance comparable to an ideal observer.  
- Learned networks exhibit emergent, biologically plausible properties: tight E/I balance and sparse, long-tailed synaptic weight distributions.  
- Results also show stimulus-induced variability reduction consistent with cortical observations.  
- Technical execution is high-quality, with well-supported claims and convincing numerical experiments.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear, well-written, and provides sufficient methodological detail and informative figures.  
- Approach is highly original, using modern control/optimization rather than traditional learning rules or statistical mechanics.  
- Demonstrates that optimizing for robust stability can unify multiple experimentally observed circuit phenomena.  
- Significant contribution with potential influence on theoretical neuroscience and neuromorphic design.  
- Main weakness: the gradient-based full-connectivity optimization is not presented as a biologically plausible learning rule.  
- Authors position the method as an existence proof; deriving local/plausible learning mechanisms remains future work.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, integrates many constraints, yields biologically plausible emergent features, high quality and clarity, and significant impact.  
- Argument against acceptance: lack of biological plausibility of the optimization algorithm (acknowledged by authors).
