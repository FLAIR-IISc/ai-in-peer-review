## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that address limitations of prior models, specifically violations of Dale’s law and activity saturation. The authors directly optimize a network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to stabilize a set of arbitrary analog patterns, achieving this through gradient descent on a cost function incorporating attractor stability, weight regularization, and fixed-point conditions. The resulting network exhibits biologically plausible features, including excitation-inhibition balance and graded, non-saturated attractor states, with learned synaptic weight distributions closely matching experimental findings.

The work builds upon decades of research into attractor networks (Hopfield, Treves, Amit) by explicitly addressing key physiological constraints often overlooked. Unlike earlier models that relied on binary representations or saturated firing rates, this approach generates graded attractors within a biologically realistic firing rate regime. The use of a smoothed spectral abscissa for stability optimization is a particularly clever solution to a challenging problem, and the resulting network’s robustness to noise and initial conditions is a significant strength. The finding that the network incidentally explains the reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is a compelling connection to experimental observations. 

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents a complex model and its results. The methods section is thorough, and the results are presented with supporting figures. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and discuss future directions, such as exploring how local learning rules might implement the proposed optimization framework. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of control-theoretic optimization with biological constraints (Dale’s law, balanced E/I activity) is a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:** The model addresses fundamental questions about the neural substrate of memory and offers a plausible mechanism for graded, robust memory recall.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, with clear theoretical justification and compelling simulation results.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   While the results are promising, the model is still relatively abstract. Further investigation into the impact of more biologically realistic neuron models (e.g., spiking neurons) and network architectures would strengthen the findings.
*   The computational cost of the optimization procedure could limit its scalability to larger networks.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of attractor networks. The originality, quality, and clarity of the work outweigh the minor limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability concerns and potentially discuss future work on bridging the gap to spiking neural networks.
