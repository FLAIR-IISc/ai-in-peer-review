- Introduces a control-theoretic framework to construct autoassociative memory networks that respect Dale’s Law and use non-saturating neuron gains.  
- Optimizes synaptic weights so predefined analog activity patterns become stable fixed points by minimizing a loss combining zero time-derivative at targets and the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).  
- Demonstrates via simulations that multiple graded memories are successfully embedded and reliably recalled from noisy cues.  
- Shows learned networks exhibit emergent biological features—tight E/I balance and sparse-like weight distributions—without being explicitly enforced.  
- Network dynamics reproduce stimulus-induced reduction in trial-to-trial variability seen in cortical recordings.  
- Advances beyond classic Hopfield and recent models by handling analog patterns, obeying Dale’s law, and avoiding saturating nonlinearities.  
- Paper is technically sound, thoroughly simulated, and careful about nuances (e.g., learned states are “slow points” rather than perfect fixed points).  
- Manuscript is very clear and well organized, with informative figures and a detailed methods section.  
- The approach is highly original: applying SSA-based control-theoretic optimization to recurrent memory networks is novel and powerful.  
- Results are significant for computational neuroscience and machine learning, offering a normative account for cortical-like E/I balance and connectivity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, analog memory embedding, emergent biological features, robustness to noise, technical rigor and clarity.  
- Main criticisms: no systematic analysis of storage capacity (m vs. n), limiting comparison to other memory models.  
- Main criticisms: need stronger justification of why this specific optimization/SSA approach is uniquely appropriate versus alternatives (e.g., BPTT with regularization).  
- Suggestion: discuss mechanistically why direct control of the Jacobian spectrum is crucial and how other learning rules might fail in this regime.  
- Suggestion: provide at least some empirical scaling results or commentary on storage capacity across different network sizes.  
- Suggestion: investigate the origin of sparsity—role of L2 regularizer vs. stability constraints—and test L1 regularization to promote/assess sparser solutions.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance; the paper is an excellent, impactful contribution with only a few suggested additions.
