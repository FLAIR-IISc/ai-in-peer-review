- Novel control-theoretic approach for modeling auto-associative memory in a rate-based E-I network.  
- Overcomes prior limitations by incorporating Dale's law, non-saturating graded units, and analog pattern storage/retrieval.  
- Well-written, clearly organized manuscript with a thorough literature review and informative figures.  
- Training optimizes network parameters so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points of the dynamics.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization, argued superior to traditional spectral abscissa.  
- Trained networks operate in the balanced regime and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow dynamics consistent with memory retention.  
- Learned synaptic weights are centered at zero with long tails and show approximate per-neuron E/I balance.  
- Networks are robust to cue corruption, perturbations, and stochastic input noise, enabling reliable auto-associative recall.  
- Strength: integrates key physiological constraints in a unified modeling framework.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of why the chosen training procedure was selected or why it specifically enforces graded, non-saturating memories.  
- Weakness: no explicit comparison of memory capacity (number of memories vs. network size) to previous models.  
- Quality: technically sound with claims supported by theory and experiments; authors are measured about limitations.  
- Significance: results are important and likely to attract strong community interest.  
- Recommendations: discuss alternative training procedures and mechanistic reasons for success, compare capacity to prior models, and provide more optimization/hyperparameter details.  
- Overall: novel, solid work that with revisions could be a strong contribution to the field.
