This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to build functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints, overcoming the limitations of previous models. The authors introduce a rate-based auto-associative memory model constrained by biological plausibility, including Dale's Law and non-saturating graded units. Memories are encoded via synaptic updates from gradient descent combined with a recent method that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.

The authors provide a thorough literature review, discussing the limitations of previous models and the importance of incorporating physiological constraints. They clearly describe the architecture and training procedure, which involves optimizing network parameters to satisfy both the fixed-point and stability conditions for a collection of arbitrary, graded memory patterns. The use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to perform robust stability optimization is a notable contribution.

The simulations show that the trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow (near-fixed-point) dynamics. The trained networks display biologically consistent features, including a synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron. The networks are also robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, demonstrating reliable auto-associative recall.

The manuscript is clearly written, and the figures effectively illustrate the results. The authors provide a detailed description of the methods and materials, making it easy to follow and reproduce their work.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of a novel control-theoretic framework that overcomes the limitations of previous models.
* The use of a rate-based auto-associative memory model constrained by biological plausibility.
* The demonstration of stable and robust memory recall in the presence of noise and perturbations.
* The display of biologically consistent features in the trained networks.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The lack of a clear explanation for why the chosen training procedure was selected and whether it is particularly suited to enforce graded, non-saturating memories versus other methods.
* The absence of a discussion on whether alternative training procedures could achieve similar results or explaining mechanistically why this method succeeds.
* The limited comparison of the trained network performance to previous models in terms of capacity (number of memories vs network size).

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel and biologically plausible model for auto-associative memory.
* The results demonstrate stable and robust memory recall in the presence of noise and perturbations.
* The manuscript is clearly written, and the figures effectively illustrate the results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of a clear explanation for the chosen training procedure and its suitability for the task.
* The limited comparison to previous models in terms of capacity and performance.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clear explanation for the chosen training procedure and its suitability for the task.
* Discuss whether alternative training procedures could achieve similar results or explaining mechanistically why this method succeeds.
* Compare the trained network performance to previous models in terms of capacity (number of memories vs network size).

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and auto-associative memory. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, it has the potential to be a strong and impactful paper.
