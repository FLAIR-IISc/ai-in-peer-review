- Introduces a control-theoretic framework to build attractor memory networks that respect Dale's law and low, graded firing rates.  
- Reframes memory storage as an optimization over synaptic weights and inhibitory activity to ensure desired analog excitatory patterns are stable fixed points.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for stability, enabling gradient-based optimization.  
- Demonstrates reliable recall from noisy cues in optimized networks.  
- Emergent tight excitation–inhibition balance arises from optimization, matching cortical dynamics.  
- Learned synaptic weight distributions are sparse with long tails (log-normal-like), despite using L2 regularization.  
- Model reproduces stimulus-onset variability quenching observed in cortex.  
- Method is novel and principled, avoiding heuristic learning rules or strong connectivity assumptions.  
- Successfully reconciles separate E/I populations, analog pattern storage, and low firing rates in one model.  
- Manuscript is clear, well-motivated, and well-presented with informative figures.  
- Main weakness: learning uses a global, offline BFGS optimization that is biologically implausible and lacks a local plasticity implementation.  
- Reliance on rate-based dynamics; no demonstrated mapping to stable spiking (e.g., LIF) networks.  
- Limited analysis of storage capacity and scaling (only shown for m=30, n=150); unclear critical capacity and robustness as patterns increase.  
- Overall evaluation: technically sound, original, significant — an important existence proof that E-I networks can store graded memories while showing biologically realistic features.
