Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to building rate-based attractor networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons that store multiple graded (analog) memories while satisfying key biological constraints. The model explicitly enforces Dale’s law via a reparameterization of synaptic signs, avoids activity saturation by stabilizing low-rate fixed points through feedback inhibition, and achieves balanced excitation–inhibition during recall. Training uses gradient-based optimization of a composite cost: minimizing activity drift at target attractors (fixed-point condition), minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to promote local stability, and an L2 (Frobenius norm) penalty on synaptic weights for regularization. The resulting networks exhibit: (i) graded, non-saturated attractors; (ii) synaptic weight distributions with a sharp peak near zero and a heavy tail that closely matches cortical measurements; (iii) excitation–inhibition balance within attractor states; (iv) robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise; and (v) variability quenching after stimulus onset.

This work addresses long-standing limitations of Hopfield-style and subsequent attractor models: violations of Dale’s law (symmetric connectivity), binary memories or effectively binary recall, and reliance on saturating transfer functions at high firing rates for stability. It complements prior balanced network and LIF-based attractor approaches by explicitly stabilizing non-saturated graded attractors with realistic E–I structure and by importing tools from robust control (SSA). The paper also connects to empirical findings on synaptic weight statistics and trial-to-trial variability reductions.

Quality
Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and principled. Enforcing fixed-point conditions via dv/dt penalties and stabilizing via SSA is a compelling compromise between tractability and biological realism. Empirical results are thorough: recall from noisy cues approaches an ideal-observer bound; ongoing-noise experiments show stable residence near the correct attractor; E/I currents reveal tight balancing across attractors; and learned weight statistics align with experimental reports. The clipping experiment suggests that only a minority of strong synapses are functionally essential.

Limitations and assumptions: (i) The optimization produces “slow points” with small but nonzero dv/dt; stability is then verified via SA/SSA, and behavior suggests nearby exact fixed points—this is convincing empirically but would benefit from a more systematic basin-of-attraction analysis. (ii) The capacity (∼0.2 with N=150 and 30 patterns) is modest, and there is no theoretical capacity characterization or scaling analysis. (iii) The method relies on global, nonlocal gradient optimization over all patterns and auxiliary inhibitory steady-state variables, which is not a biologically local learning rule. (iv) The network is dense; while the learned weights are effectively sparse/heavy-tailed, explicit structural sparsity and spatial structure typical of cortex are not imposed. (v) SSA is used with a heuristic ε scaling; sensitivity of performance to ε and to the relative weights of loss terms is not fully characterized. (vi) Transfer to spiking networks is discussed but not demonstrated.

Clarity
The manuscript is very well written and organized. The optimization objective, reparameterization for Dale’s law, SSA, and experimental protocols are clearly described. Figures effectively support the claims (e.g., weight distributions, E/I balance, variability quenching). Suggestions:
- Report basin sizes or convergence radii for attractors as a function of cue noise σ.
- Provide computational cost and convergence diagnostics (e.g., gradient norms, runtime vs. N/m).
- Add a sensitivity analysis for ε, ηs, and ηF.
- Clarify the role and interpretation of per-attractor inhibitory steady-state variables during training vs. recall.

Originality
The combination of biologically grounded constraints (Dale’s law, graded rates, non-saturating gain) with robust control-based stability optimization is novel. SSA-driven training of attractors in E–I networks is, to my knowledge, new in the NIPS literature. The finding that these combined constraints naturally yield heavy-tailed weight distributions and balanced-state dynamics in recall is an insightful contribution to our understanding of biologically plausible attractor networks.

Significance
This work advances attractor modeling by showing that multiple graded memories can be embedded in balanced E–I networks without saturating activity, while reproducing realistic synaptic statistics and dynamical signatures (variability quenching). It offers a template for constructing biologically plausible recurrent networks by directly optimizing both fixed-point and stability properties—an approach likely to influence future theoretical and computational neuroscience research. The main caveats concern capacity, scalability, and biological learnability; addressing these would further increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear advance over prior attractor models: respects Dale’s law and avoids saturation while supporting analog memories.
- Principled training objective (fixed-point + SSA) with solid empirical validation.
- Emergent biological plausibility: heavy-tailed weights, E/I balance during recall, variability reduction.
- Robust recall near an ideal-observer bound; robustness to ongoing noise.
- Well written and easy to follow; code-like detail sufficient for reproduction in principle.

Cons for acceptance
- No theoretical capacity analysis; limited empirical scaling beyond N=150, m=30.
- Global, nonlocal optimization; no local/plausible plasticity rule provided.
- Use of per-pattern inhibitory steady-state variables during training gives extra degrees of freedom; biological interpretation could be clearer.
- Dense connectivity; structural sparsity and spatial constraints not explored.
- SSA hyperparameters and sensitivity not extensively analyzed; “slow point” vs. exact fixed point formalization is heuristic.

Questions and suggestions
- How does capacity scale with N and with the statistics of patterns? Any failure modes as m/N increases?
- Could the approach incorporate explicit structural sparsity constraints without degrading stability?
- How sensitive are results to the gain function (e.g., different power exponents or rectified-linear)?
- Can the learned rate connectivity be transferred to LIF networks with matched f–I curves and maintain attractors?
- Can inhibitory (and/or excitatory) local plasticity rules approximate the gradients of the SSA term?

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper presents a carefully executed and original approach that resolves key biological shortcomings of classic attractor models and yields several experimentally consistent phenomena. The limitations are well bounded and open clear avenues for follow-up work.
