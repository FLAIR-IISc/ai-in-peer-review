- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework to build attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.  
- Directly optimizes excitatory–inhibitory rate networks so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced E–I regime and exhibit experimentally matching synaptic weight distributions.  
- Networks show graded, non-saturated attractors encoded in synaptic weights and inhibitory activity.  
- Models are robust to recall cue corruption and to ongoing noise, and reproduce stimulus-triggered reduction in trial-to-trial variability.  
- Paper is well-written with clear presentation of model, training, and results.  
- Provides a thorough review of prior attractor-network work and clearly highlights how the new framework overcomes previous limitations.  
- Training minimizes activity change in attractors, penalizes weight norm, and promotes attractor stability to enforce Dale’s law and avoid saturation.  
- Technical analysis and experimental results support the authors’ claims; the work is technically sound.  
- Main weakness: uses a rate-based model, which may not directly generalize to spiking networks (authors acknowledge this).  
- Authors argue learned connectivity could be transferred to spiking models if single-neuron gain functions match.  
- Originality: introduces a significant, novel control-theoretic approach to constructing attractor networks.  
- Significance: addresses a difficult problem more effectively than prior work, with implications for neural memory mechanisms.  
- Strengths summarized: novel approach, physiological plausibility, robustness, and thorough analysis.  
- Cons summarized: limited direct applicability to spiking networks and not a complete solution but a substantial advance.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance based on novelty, rigor, clarity, and impact.
