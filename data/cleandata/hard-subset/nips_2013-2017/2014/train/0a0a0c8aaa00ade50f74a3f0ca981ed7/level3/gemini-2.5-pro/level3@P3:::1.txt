This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for constructing auto-associative memory networks that adhere to several key biological constraints, including Dale’s Law and the ability to store graded, non-saturating activity patterns. The authors model a network of distinct excitatory and inhibitory rate-based neurons and formalize memory storage as an optimization problem. The core of their contribution is a training procedure that uses gradient descent to simultaneously ensure that desired analog memory patterns are fixed points of the network dynamics (by minimizing the activity's time derivative) and that these fixed points are dynamically stable. To enforce stability, the authors leverage a recent method from control theory, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), which provides a differentiable proxy for the stability of the linearized dynamics.

The authors demonstrate through simulations that this procedure is highly effective. The resulting networks can reliably store and recall dozens of graded memory patterns. The recall process is robust, showing resilience to significant corruption in the initial cue as well as to ongoing stochastic noise in the dynamics. Importantly, the trained networks spontaneously develop features that are strikingly consistent with biological observations. These include synaptic weight distributions that are centered at zero with long tails, and a dynamic balance of excitatory and inhibitory currents arriving at each neuron, which is fine-tuned for each specific memory pattern.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** This work represents a significant advance in the theory of attractor memory networks. By providing a method to construct functional networks that respect multiple fundamental biological constraints (E/I separation, graded non-saturating activity), it overcomes major limitations of previous models, such as the classic Hopfield network. The application of control-theoretic optimization techniques (specifically, the SSA) to directly enforce stability in this context is novel and powerful. The results are of strong interest to the computational neuroscience community and provide a new, principled way to think about the neural substrate of memory.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by thorough and convincing simulations. The experiments effectively demonstrate the stability of the learned memories, the robustness of the recall process, and the emergence of biologically plausible network properties. The authors are careful in their analysis, for example, by testing the functional relevance of weak synapses and examining the E/I balance in detail.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The introduction provides a concise and accurate review of related work, which effectively motivates the authors' approach. The description of the network architecture and the training procedure is precise, and the figures are high-quality and informative, clearly illustrating the main findings of the paper.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, its contribution could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Justification of the Training Method:** The paper convincingly shows that the chosen training procedure works, but it would benefit from a clearer explanation of *why* this particular method was selected and whether it is uniquely suited for the problem. For instance, is the SSA-based stability constraint particularly important for networks of non-saturating units where runaway excitation is a primary failure mode? A discussion of alternative stability-enforcing methods and why they might be less suitable would help clarify the specific advantages of the proposed approach.

2.  **Mechanistic Explanation of Emergent Properties:** The authors demonstrate that biologically realistic features like sparse weights and E/I balance emerge from the optimization process. This is a fascinating result. The paper would be more impactful if it offered a mechanistic explanation for *why* this occurs. How does the combined objective of creating fixed points and ensuring their stability through the SSA constrain the network parameters to yield these specific properties? Providing this deeper insight would elevate the work beyond a successful proof-of-concept.

3.  **Performance Benchmarking:** The paper demonstrates a storage capacity of 0.2 (30 memories in a 150-neuron network). While this is impressive given the biological constraints, the significance is difficult to assess without context. The authors might consider discussing, even briefly, how this capacity compares to previous models of associative memory. While a direct comparison is complicated by the different assumptions, some form of benchmark would help readers appreciate the performance of the trained network more fully. This could be framed as a suggestion for future work if space is limited.

In summary, this is a novel, well-executed, and clearly presented piece of research that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of memory in neural circuits. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to providing deeper justification and insight, which can be addressed in a revised version. The paper is of high quality and is likely to have a strong impact on the field.
