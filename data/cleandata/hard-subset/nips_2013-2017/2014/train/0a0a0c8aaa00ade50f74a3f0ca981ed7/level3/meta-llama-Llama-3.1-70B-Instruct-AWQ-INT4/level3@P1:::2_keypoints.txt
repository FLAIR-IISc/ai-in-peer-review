- Novel control-theoretic framework for attractor networks satisfying physiological constraints (Dale’s law, sparsity, graded activity, low firing rates).
- Rate-based excitatory–inhibitory network with synaptic weights and inhibitory activities optimized to store multiple stable activity patterns.
- Training via gradient descent on a cost that promotes attractor stability, penalizes weight Frobenius norm, and minimizes activity changes in target attractors.
- Learned synaptic weight distribution closely matches experimental findings and shows excitation–inhibition balance in attractor states.
- Network achieves graded, non-saturated attractors encoded in weights and inhibitory activity and performs high-accuracy memory recall.
- Demonstrates robustness to noise in recall cues and ongoing network dynamics.
- Key contribution: combining multiple biological constraints to produce biologically plausible attractor features.
- Strength: novel, well-formulated approach that addresses prior model shortcomings and is clearly presented.
- Strength: detailed description of model, training algorithm, and cost function.
- Weakness: rate-based formulation may not directly apply to spiking memory networks.
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability to larger networks.
- Weakness: limited discussion of potential limitations, such as requirement for large neuron/synapse counts for robust recall.
- Paper quality: well-written, organized, and results are clear and easy to follow.
- Argument for acceptance: biologically plausible, novel method with strong, experimentally consistent results.
- Argument against acceptance: applicability to spiking networks, unclear scalability/computational cost, and unaddressed limitations regarding network size.
