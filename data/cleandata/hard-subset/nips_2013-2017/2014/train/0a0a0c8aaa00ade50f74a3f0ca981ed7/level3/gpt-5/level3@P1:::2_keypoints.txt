- Proposes a control-theoretic method to build rate-based E–I recurrent networks that store and robustly retrieve multiple analog memories while enforcing physiological constraints (Dale’s law, low firing rates, E/I balance).  
- Parameterizes synaptic weights to enforce Dale’s law and treats inhibitory steady-state activities as auxiliary variables optimized during training.  
- Optimizes a composite objective: drift norm at targets (fix-point consistency), Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for local stability, and an L2 weight penalty.  
- Demonstrates storage of tens of graded, non-saturating patterns with strong E/I balance and reliable recall from noisy cues and ongoing noise.  
- Shows trial-by-trial variability quenching upon stimulus onset, matching experimental observations.  
- Finds emergent heavy-tailed, sparse synaptic weight distributions; pruning small weights largely preserves recall performance.  
- Positions work relative to prior models: extends beyond Hopfield/symmetric, instantaneous-inhibition, saturation-based, bump, and spike-timing models by directly optimizing robust stability in a Dale-constrained E–I network.  
- Methodology is technically sound; SSA is a practical surrogate for stability and the drift term addresses fixed-point consistency.  
- Empirical results support claims about recall robustness, balance, and weight statistics, strengthening biological plausibility.  
- Main concern: target states have small but nonzero drift — fixed points aren’t explicitly verified; recommend root-finding and reporting true spectral abscissae.  
- Scaling concern: capacity shown only for n=150, m=30 (≈0.2); scaling with network size, pattern statistics, and hyperparameters is not analyzed.  
- Optimization concern: nonconvex training may be sensitive to initialization and SSA/regularization hyperparameters; ablation/sensitivity studies are missing.  
- Dynamical landscape concern: spurious attractors, basin sizes, and confusion among memories are not characterized.  
- Clarity is high: writing, figures, and rationale are clear; recommends adding pseudocode, computational cost, convergence diagnostics, and SSA gradient details.  
- Originality: novel use of robust control (SSA) to enforce multi-attractor stability in biologically constrained E–I networks.  
- Significance: advances understanding of how cortical-like networks could implement analog attractors and links stability, balance, and variability quenching.  
- Strength: clean formulation combining fixed-point and stability optimization under Dale’s law with empirical demonstrations of biologically relevant phenomena.  
- Strength: robust recall close to an ideal observer, and biologically consistent emergent weight distributions where a few strong synapses dominate recall.  
- Weakness / suggestions: verify true fixed points and actual spectral abscissae, analyze capacity scaling and basin sizes, test sensitivity to hyperparameters, explore structural sparsity and spiking realizations.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, well-executed integration of control tools with biological constraints and compelling empirical results.  
- Rejection arguments: limited scaling/theoretical analysis, reliance on SSA without direct verification, small networks and limited comparisons to alternatives.  
- Overall: strong, thought-provoking contribution that would be more compelling with additional analyses on fixed-point verification, scaling, and robustness.
