- Introduces a novel rate-based, biologically constrained auto-associative memory model enforcing Dale’s Law and non-saturating graded units.  
- Uses a control-theoretic framework to construct attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.  
- Trains networks via gradient descent combined with a method enforcing stability of linearized dynamics around fixed points.  
- Manuscript is well-written with clear literature review, architecture/training description, and effective figures.  
- Novel contribution likely of strong interest to the community.  
- Comprehensive review of prior auto-associative models and clear motivation for a more biologically plausible approach.  
- Demonstrates that trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow near-fixed-point dynamics in simulations.  
- Trained networks show biologically consistent synaptic weight distributions and approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron.  
- Networks are robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, enabling reliable auto-associative recall.  
- Needs clarification on why the chosen training procedure was selected and whether it specifically enforces graded, non-saturating memories.  
- Should discuss whether alternative training procedures could produce similar results or provide mechanistic explanations for success.  
- Lacks a comparison of memory capacity (memories vs. network size) to previous models to contextualize strengths and limitations.  
- Originality: introduces a new combination of control-theoretic design and stability-enforcing training—novel and interesting.  
- Significance: potential to advance understanding of neural memory substrates and provide a more biologically plausible modeling approach.  
- Overall recommendation: well-written, novel, and impactful with minor revisions; Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.
