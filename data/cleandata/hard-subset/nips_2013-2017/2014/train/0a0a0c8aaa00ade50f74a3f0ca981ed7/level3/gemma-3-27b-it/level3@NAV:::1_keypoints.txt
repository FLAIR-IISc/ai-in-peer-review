- Introduces a novel rate-based auto-associative memory model that enforces biological plausibility (Dale’s Law, graded non-saturating units).  
- Frames memory storage as a control-theoretic problem, using optimized synaptic weights to make desired patterns stable fixed points.  
- Leverages the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa from robust control to enforce stability during training.  
- Simulations show stable fixed points with slow dynamics, matching expectations for analog memory recall.  
- Trained weight distributions are biologically consistent: zero-centered with long tails and approximate E/I balance.  
- Network exhibits robustness to perturbations and reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset, echoing empirical findings.  
- Builds on and addresses limitations of prior models (Hopfield networks, saturation, global inhibition) within a unified framework.  
- Clear presentation, well-explained architecture/training procedure, and compelling visualizations are major strengths.  
- Main weakness: insufficient justification and comparison for the chosen training method versus alternatives (e.g., direct spectral abscissa optimization or other learning rules).  
- Secondary weakness: no comparison of memory capacity (number of storable patterns relative to network size) against existing models.  
- Suggested improvements: provide explicit comparisons to alternative optimization methods and evaluate storage capacity to contextualize trade-offs.  
- Overall recommendation: accept—original, clear, and promising work with minor weaknesses that can be addressed in revision.
