This paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to construct biologically plausible autoassociative memory networks that store and recall analog (graded) patterns as stable attractors while enforcing Dale’s law, low firing rates without artificial saturation, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors directly optimize synaptic weights in a rate-based E–I network by (i) minimizing the drift at desired activity patterns to make them fixed points (or near fixed points), and (ii) minimizing a smooth upper bound on the spectral abscissa (the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA) at those points to ensure local stability. The framework treats inhibitory steady-state activities in each attractor as auxiliary variables during training and respects sign constraints via a reparameterization that enforces Dale’s law. The trained networks exhibit robust recall in the presence of corrupted cues and ongoing noise, balanced excitation and inhibition per neuron across memories, heavy-tailed synaptic weight distributions with many weak synapses, and stimulus-onset variability quenching akin to in vivo observations.

Relation to prior work: The approach bridges classic associative memory models (e.g., Hopfield with binary or quasi-binary coding [1,2]) and graded-memory extensions [3,4,5,6], while addressing Dale’s law and realistic E/I separation lacking in many earlier models. It improves on LIF-based balanced memory models that often rely on saturation or global inhibition [7–9] and on spatial “bump” attractors that assume topographic structure [10,11]. It complements timing-based graded memories without explicit inhibition [12]. The stability formulation leverages robust control tools (SSA) previously used for transient control in balanced networks [14], now targeted at embedding multiple stable analog attractors.

Quality: The optimization objective is well-motivated and technically sound: combining a fixed-point constraint with SSA-based stability offers a principled way to shape local dynamics around desired memories. The use of sign-constrained reparameterization is appropriate, and the experiments demonstrate: (i) successful creation of stable attractors (negative SSA; small drift), (ii) robust recall under cue noise with performance comparable to an ideal observer, (iii) resilience to ongoing noise, (iv) emergence of balanced E/I currents and heavy-tailed synaptic weights, and (v) limited dependence on weak synapses. Limitations include: (a) no theoretical capacity bounds or scaling analysis; (b) attractors are sometimes “slow points” rather than exact fixed points, with no formal guarantees about basin sizes; (c) choice and sensitivity of ε in SSA and of the trade-off hyperparameters are heuristic; (d) reliance on a global, nonlocal, offline optimization that is not biologically plausible; and (e) evaluation is limited to moderate network sizes.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written, well-organized, and carefully relates to prior literature. The figures effectively illustrate optimization progress, recall performance, weight distributions, E/I balance, and variability quenching. Some clarifications would help: the rationale for SSA versus alternative surrogates (e.g., Lyapunov functions, spectral radius constraints), sensitivity of results to ε and regularization weights, convergence behavior and computational cost, and a more precise characterization of basin geometry and recall time scales.

Originality: The integration of robust stability optimization (SSA) with Dale’s law and graded, non-saturating rate dynamics for memory storage is novel. The framework provides a fresh algorithmic route to stable analog attractors while matching several physiological hallmarks (E/I balance, variability quenching, heavy-tailed synaptic strengths).

Significance: The work is a meaningful step toward unifying associative memory with realistic circuit constraints and may stimulate follow-up studies on learning rules for stability, spiking instantiations, and scaling laws. If generalized to larger networks and spiking models, it could become a reference approach for constructing analog memories in biologically grounded circuits.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Justify the choice of SSA: compare against alternative smooth stability proxies (e.g., spectral norm penalties, Lyapunov constructions, Gershgorin-based constraints), and show that SSA better supports low-rate, non-saturating graded memories.
- Provide capacity and scaling analyses: plot success probability and basin sizes versus m/n across several n; compare to graded-memory predictions [3–6].
- Report sensitivity analyses: ε, ηs, ηF, initializations, and optimizer settings; include runtimes and convergence statistics.
- Ablations: remove the SSA term, fix inhibitory steady-state variables, or alter regularization (L1 vs L2) to show which components are necessary for stability, sparsity, and balance.
- Basin geometry and dynamics: quantify basin volumes, recall times, and robustness to synaptic perturbations/pruning (beyond thresholding small weights).
- Spiking realization: demonstrate that the learned connectivity yields analogous attractors and E/I balance in an LIF network with matched gain.
- Clarify the role of per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables during training and argue why this does not undermine biological plausibility at recall.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a long-standing problem (stable analog memories) under key biological constraints (Dale’s law, low rates, E/I balance).
- Conceptually novel and methodologically principled (SSA-based stability).
- Strong and diverse empirical evidence (robust recall, variability quenching, realistic weight statistics).
- Clear exposition and relevance to both neuroscience and machine learning communities.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scaling and capacity characterization; no theoretical bounds.
- Offline, nonlocal optimization lacks a plausible synaptic learning interpretation.
- No spiking implementation; rate-only demonstrations may limit impact for some readers.
- Heuristic choices (ε, hyperparameters) and incomplete sensitivity analyses.

Overall recommendation: Strong accept. The paper offers a compelling, well-executed framework that advances our ability to construct biologically plausible analog attractor memories, with clear empirical validation and impactful connections to robust control, despite open questions about scalability, theory, and learning mechanisms.
