- Introduces a Dale’s-law-compliant excitatory–inhibitory rate network supporting graded, non-saturating memory attractors trained via a composite objective for fixed points and local stability.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable surrogate to enforce local linear stability of stored patterns.  
- Training enforces Dale’s law via a signed softplus reparameterization and yields slow, near-fixed-point dynamics and robust recall under cue and ongoing noise.  
- Emergent network properties include per-neuron E/I balance and synaptic weight distributions with peaks near zero and long tails, matching cortical data.  
- Technical soundness: objective cleanly separates fixed-point vs stability goals and SSA is a principled, practical choice; simulations convincingly demonstrate key claims.  
- Caveat: dv/dt at trained patterns remains small but nonzero, so dynamics converge to nearby rather than exact specified fixed points.  
- Caveat: inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ are treated as per-memory free variables during optimization; biological interpretation and effects of constraining them need discussion.  
- Caveat: computational cost—SSA and Jacobian computations scale with network size; a complexity analysis and runtime scaling would help assess practicality.  
- Caveat: robustness to random seeds and hyperparameters (η_s, η_F) is not reported—variance of recall/stability should be provided.  
- Reproducibility: objective and parameterization are clear, but code/implementation details for SSA, ε scaling, and optimization settings should be supplied.  
- Clarity: paper is well written with effective figures; minor additions could include eigenvalue spectra/timescale distributions and quantitative E/I balance metrics.  
- Clarity: collect hyperparameters (η_s, η_F, ε) and rationales in a concise table or main text.  
- Originality: novelty lies in applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to biologically constrained graded attractors under Dale’s law.  
- Significance: fills a gap by storing analog memories in balanced E–I networks without saturation, producing realistic synaptic statistics and robust recall.  
- Suggestion: compare SSA to alternatives (direct SA heuristics, spectral radius constraints, contraction/Lyapunov methods, RLS/FORCE with sign constraints) to justify choice.  
- Suggestion: provide a capacity analysis versus network size (critical m/n), basin sizes, and comparisons to classic/balanced-network predictions.  
- Suggestion: test robustness across alternative single-neuron nonlinearities and under enforced structural sparsity (fixed connection probability).  
- Suggestion: analyze the role of weak weights (pruning curves, stability margins) and consider refining trained patterns to exact fixed points (line-search or numerical root-finding).  
- Pros: rigorous control-theoretic framework, biologically plausible constraints, robust graded attractors, emergent E/I balance and realistic weights, clear writing.  
- Cons: limited capacity/scaling analysis, reliance on per-memory inhibitory variables during training, small but unresolved gap between targets and achieved fixed points, few comparisons to alternative stabilization methods.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — novel, technically sound, well-presented, and likely influential for control, ML, and computational neuroscience communities.
