- Introduces a novel rate-based auto-associative memory that enforces Dale’s Law, graded responses, and stable dynamics.  
- Uses a control-theoretic framework and Smoothed Spectral Abscissa to directly optimize networks for storing analog patterns as fixed points.  
- Simulations show stable fixed points with slow dynamics, biologically plausible synaptic weight distributions, E/I balance, and robustness to noise.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivating and explaining methods and results.  
- Integrates and advances prior auto-associative work (Hopfield and successors) by combining biological realism and stability.  
- Technical soundness: mathematically well-defined methodology grounded in control theory.  
- Clarity: concepts and figures are intuitive and effectively presented.  
- Originality: novel combination of rate-based model, biological constraints, and control-theoretic optimization.  
- Significance: advances understanding of neural memory mechanisms and could impact realistic artificial memory systems.  
- Weakness — training justification: lacks detailed explanation for choosing Smoothed Spectral Abscissa over other optimization methods.  
- Weakness — alternative procedures: should explore whether other training algorithms or loss functions yield similar results.  
- Weakness — capacity comparison: missing quantitative comparison of memory capacity versus prior models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, original, significant results, well-written, addresses key prior limitations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient justification for training choice and missing capacity benchmarks.  
- Overall assessment: strong recommend for acceptance; weaknesses are minor and addressable but don’t outweigh contributions.
