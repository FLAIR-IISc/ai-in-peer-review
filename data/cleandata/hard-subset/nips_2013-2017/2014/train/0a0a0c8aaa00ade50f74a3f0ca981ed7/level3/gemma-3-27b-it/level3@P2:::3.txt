This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to building attractor networks for analog memory storage, addressing several limitations of prior work. The authors tackle the challenge of creating biologically plausible memory networks by explicitly incorporating crucial physiological constraints – Dale’s law, graded neuronal representations, and balanced excitation/inhibition – within a control-theoretic framework. This is a significant contribution, moving beyond simplified models and offering a potentially more realistic pathway towards understanding the neural substrate of memory.

The core idea of formalizing memory storage as optimizing for fixed points and their stability, coupled with the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization, is particularly insightful. The resulting networks exhibit desirable properties such as robustness to noise, avoidance of neuronal saturation, and the ability to explain observed phenomena like reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, and the paper is well-organized, facilitating comprehension of the complex methodology.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** This work introduces a fresh perspective on associative memory, tackling long-standing issues in the field. The combined focus on biological constraints and control-theoretic optimization is a genuinely novel approach. The potential to explain observed neural phenomena (variability reduction) adds to the significance.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears technically sound, building upon established control theory principles and applying them in a creative manner to network neuroscience. The use of the SSA is a clever way to address the challenges of stability optimization.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. Complex concepts are explained in an accessible manner, making the work understandable even to those not deeply familiar with control theory.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide thorough numerical validation, demonstrating the network's memory recall performance, robustness, and balanced activity.  The analysis of synaptic weight distribution and the impact of weight pruning strengthens the conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Learning Rule:** While the optimization framework is well-developed, the paper lacks a biologically plausible learning rule to implement the proposed parameter adjustments. The authors acknowledge this and hint at future directions, but this remains a significant gap.  It's unclear how a biological system could achieve the required optimization.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of γ = 0.04 in Equation (1) is somewhat arbitrary.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter, and the rationale behind this specific value, would be beneficial.
* **Memory Distribution Motivation:** The authors motivate the use of a log-normal distribution for memory states based on observed firing rate distributions *in vivo*.  However, a more detailed justification for this specific choice, perhaps comparing its properties to other plausible distributions, would strengthen the argument.
* **Fixed-Point Approximation:** The cost function (Equation 5) doesn't guarantee perfect fixed points, only approaching them. A more quantitative discussion of the degree to which the fixed-point constraints are violated, and how this impacts the overall performance, is needed.
* **Capacity Analysis:** The paper demonstrates the ability to store a limited number of memories (30 in the example). However, a preliminary discussion of memory capacity – the maximum number of memories that can be reliably stored – would be a valuable addition.  Scaling of performance with network size would also be insightful.
* **Dale’s Law Discussion:** The paper highlights the importance of adhering to Dale’s law. A discussion exploring whether Dale’s law provides specific *computational* advantages (beyond its biological reality) would add further depth.  Is there a functional reason why separating excitation and inhibition is beneficial for memory networks?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in computational neuroscience with a novel and well-supported approach.
* The technical contributions are significant, particularly the application of control theory to memory network design.
* The results are compelling and demonstrate the potential of the proposed framework to explain observed neural phenomena.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a biologically plausible learning rule is a significant limitation.
* The limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and memory capacity leaves some questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. While the absence of a biologically plausible learning rule is a notable weakness, the novelty, technical soundness, clarity, and comprehensive evaluation outweigh this concern.  The work is likely to stimulate further research and represents a significant step forward in our understanding of the neural substrates of memory.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns outlined above regarding parameter sensitivity, memory distribution motivation, fixed-point approximation, capacity analysis, and the functional role of Dale’s law. Clarification regarding statements on lines 254–256 would also be appreciated.
