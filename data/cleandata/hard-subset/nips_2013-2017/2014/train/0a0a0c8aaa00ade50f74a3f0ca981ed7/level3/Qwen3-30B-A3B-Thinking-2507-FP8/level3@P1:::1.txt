

The paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing biologically plausible attractor networks that store and retrieve analog memory patterns. It addresses key limitations in prior memory models: violation of Dale’s law (neurons must be exclusively excitatory or inhibitory), reliance on binary memory representations, and saturation of firing rates during recall. The authors directly optimize synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron states to stabilize arbitrary graded activity patterns as fixed points of the dynamics, using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The resulting networks operate in a balanced excitation-inhibition regime, exhibit noise-robust autoassociative recall, and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset—a key experimental observation in cortical physiology.

**Strengths.** The work makes a significant contribution to bridging theoretical neuroscience and biological constraints. The control-theoretic approach (SSA minimization) elegantly solves the stability challenge without requiring artificial saturation mechanisms, directly enabling non-saturating graded activity. This resolves a long-standing issue in models like Hopfield networks (which violate Dale’s law) and LIF-based models (which rely on saturation for stability). The biological plausibility is rigorously demonstrated: (1) networks satisfy Dale’s law via weight reparameterization; (2) synaptic weights follow a sparse, heavy-tailed distribution matching cortical data (Fig. 4); (3) E/I balance is precisely tuned per neuron across attractors (Fig. 5), explaining reduced variability post-stimulus (Fig. 6b)—a phenomenon observed in sensory/motor cortices. The experiments comprehensively validate robustness to cue noise (Fig. 3), ongoing noise (Fig. 6a), and synaptic pruning (Fig. 4d). The manuscript is exceptionally clear, with a thorough literature review, well-structured methods, and figures that effectively convey key insights (e.g., Fig. 1c shows biologically realistic firing rate distributions).

**Weaknesses.** While the SSA-based optimization is well-motivated for stability, the paper does not sufficiently justify why this method is uniquely suited for *graded* memory storage versus alternatives. Prior work (e.g., [6, 8]) achieved binary memory stability without SSA; the authors should clarify how SSA specifically enables the non-saturating graded dynamics central to their contribution. A minor but important gap is the lack of capacity comparison. The paper demonstrates storage of 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network (capacity 0.2), but does not benchmark against prior graded-memory models (e.g., [3, 4]) to quantify whether this capacity is competitive or superior. Such a comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

**Originality and Significance.** The work stands out by unifying multiple biological constraints (Dale’s law, graded activity, non-saturation, E/I balance) in a single framework—a critical advance over fragmented prior approaches. It moves beyond binary memory models to address the ubiquity of graded activity in cortex. The explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability (Fig. 6b) is particularly compelling, as it links network dynamics to a well-documented experimental phenomenon, suggesting testable predictions for neural recordings. This represents a meaningful step toward understanding the neural substrate of memory, with potential implications for both computational neuroscience and neural engineering.

**Quality and Clarity.** The technical execution is sound: the optimization procedure (Eq. 5) is well-defined, stability is rigorously verified via SSA (not just qualitative observations), and experiments cover key validation metrics (noise robustness, synaptic sparseness, E/I balance). The manuscript is exceptionally clear, with minimal jargon, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., parameter choices in Table 1, gain function definition). The literature review adequately contextualizes the work against NIPS proceedings (e.g., [12] for spike-timing models).

**Recommendation.** This is a strong contribution to NIPS. The paper successfully addresses a core challenge in theoretical neuroscience with a novel, biologically grounded method that demonstrates robust performance and explains key experimental observations. The minor weaknesses (training procedure justification, capacity benchmark) are easily resolvable in revision and do not undermine the core contribution. The work is technically rigorous, clearly written, and advances the field by providing a unified framework for analog memory networks that respects physiological constraints. **Accept.**
