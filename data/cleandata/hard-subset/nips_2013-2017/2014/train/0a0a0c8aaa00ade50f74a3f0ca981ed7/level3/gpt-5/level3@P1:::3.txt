This paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to constructing rate-based recurrent E–I networks that store arbitrary analog memories as stable attractors under realistic physiological constraints. The key idea is to jointly enforce (i) fixed-point conditions for a set of graded target patterns and (ii) local stability around each target by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian. Dale’s law is enforced via a log-exp reparameterization of weights; inhibitory units are treated as auxiliary variables whose steady-state values in each attractor are optimized along with synapses. The resulting networks operate in a dynamically balanced regime, recall from corrupted cues comparably to an ideal observer, are robust to ongoing noise, and exhibit stimulus-driven reductions in across-trial variability. The work synthesizes ideas from associative memory (Hopfield; Treves & Rolls), balanced networks and stability (Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham), bump attractors (Ben-Yishai et al.), and robust control (Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.), while addressing common biological shortcomings of prior memory models (binary codes, symmetric or sign-inconsistent connectivity, saturating recall states).

Strengths
- Unified and principled formulation of memory storage as simultaneous fixed-point and stability design under Dale’s law, using SSA to obtain a smooth objective and gradient-based optimization.
- Clear enforcement of biology-relevant constraints (E/I separation; low-rate, unsaturated operation; balanced E/I currents) without relying on ad hoc saturation.
- Demonstrates autoassociative recall for analog patterns with robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise; performance is close to an ideal observer baseline.
- Emergence of realistic weight statistics (heavy-tailed, many weak/silent synapses) without explicit sparsity priors; ablation suggests many weak synapses are non-critical.
- Provides a mechanistic link between attractor dynamics and variability quenching after stimulus onset, connecting to widespread cortical observations.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Learning rule plausibility: the work optimizes synapses and per-attractor inhibitory steady states via global gradient-based methods; no local/plausible rule is provided beyond brief pointers to inhibitory plasticity.
- Capacity is only illustrated (n=150, m=30, ~0.2) but not characterized. There is no scaling law with network size, pattern statistics, or SSA smoothing ε.
- The residual violation of fixed-point constraints (‖dv/dt‖ > 0 at targets) is acknowledged but not quantified in terms of distance to the nearest true fixed point, basin size, or time to drift.
- The role and tuning of SSA parameters (ε, ηs) are heuristic; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Treating inhibitory steady-state voltages per attractor as free parameters during optimization is powerful but raises interpretability questions: what circuit mechanisms would implement this attractor-specific inhibitory configuration?
- Assumptions about single-neuron gain (γ=0.04 in g(v)=γ[v]+^2) and memory statistics (log-normal) are plausible but not justified or tested for robustness.

Quality
The method is technically sound and the control-theoretic formulation is appropriate. The optimization objective is well-motivated and numerically tractable. Experiments are convincing for the scale explored and cover recall robustness, weight structure, E/I balance, and variability quenching. However, quantitative analyses of capacity, parameter sensitivity (γ, ε, ηs, ηF), and fixed-point approximation error are needed to fully substantiate the claims and guide reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem setup, cost function, and constraints are explicit. Figures effectively support the claims (recall, balance, sparsity). Some statements need clarification (see below), and it would help to report exact quantitative metrics (e.g., distributions of SA/SSA after learning; drift timescales).

Originality
The combination of robust control (SSA minimization) with E–I network design for analog attractors under Dale’s law is novel relative to classic Hopfield-style and more recent LIF-based approaches relying on saturation or global inhibition. Using inhibitory units as optimized auxiliary variables per memory is also a fresh angle. The work connects attractor design with balanced dynamics in a principled way.

Significance
If scalable, this approach could influence how cortical memory circuits are modeled, offering a bridge between biological constraints and functional attractor computation without saturation. The noise robustness and variability quenching phenomena are compelling. The absence of a plausible learning mechanism tempers immediate impact but opens a clear research direction (e.g., inhibitory plasticity).

Specific requests and suggestions
- Sensitivity to γ=0.04: Please report how recall performance, balance, and stability depend on γ (e.g., sweep over γ with compensatory external input adjustments). Is the framework robust to different supralinear gains or exponents?
- Log-normal memory statistics: Provide motivation and empirical support for the choice (beyond heavy tails), and test robustness to other pattern distributions (e.g., Gaussian with positive truncation, mixture distributions).
- Quantify fixed-point violations: For each stored pattern, report ‖dv/dt‖, SA/SSA, the distance to the nearest actual fixed point (found by local search), and an estimate of basin size and drift timescale. This will substantiate the statement that “an exact, stable fixed point had been created very near each target pattern.”
- Clarify the claim around lines 254–256: The manuscript states that despite nonzero ‖dv/dt‖ at targets, the system converges to nearby exact fixed points. Please make this precise (e.g., via continuation analyses or explicit fixed-point solves) and indicate how often targets map to distinct nearby attractors versus merging/splitting.
- Capacity: Provide preliminary scaling with n, m, and pattern variance; how do ε and ηF influence capacity and stability margins? Report failure modes as m increases (e.g., SA crossing zero, basin shrinkage).
- Role of Dale’s law: Discuss whether enforcing sign constraints yields computational benefits (e.g., expanded stability margins, robustness to noise/perturbations) beyond biological plausibility. An ablation comparing to unconstrained signs would be informative.
- Inhibitory auxiliary variables: Comment on how per-attractor inhibitory steady states could arise through plasticity (e.g., Vogels et al., 2011), interneuron diversity, or modulatory gating, and whether similar performance is achievable without optimizing vinh per memory.
- Report sensitivity to SSA parameters (ε) and weight regularization (ηF), and include results on larger networks.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel framework unifying biological constraints with attractor design.
- Strong empirical demonstrations of recall, robustness, balance, and variability quenching.
- Clear writing and connection to prior literature.

Cons for acceptance
- No biologically plausible learning rule; inhibitory “auxiliary” treatment needs mechanistic grounding.
- Capacity and parameter robustness not characterized.
- Fixed-point approximation not quantified; some claims need clarification.

Overall, this is an important and timely contribution that advances the design of biologically realistic analog memory networks. Addressing the quantitative and mechanistic gaps above would significantly strengthen the paper.
