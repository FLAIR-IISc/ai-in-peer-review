Summary and relation to prior work
This paper revisits autoassociative memory with a modern control-theoretic lens. The authors directly optimize recurrent E–I rate networks so that a set of analog activity patterns become asymptotically stable fixed points, while enforcing Dale’s law via a sign-constrained weight parameterization. Their objective combines: (i) a fixed-point term that minimizes dv/dt at each target pattern, (ii) a stability term based on the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA), and (iii) an L2 penalty on weights to discourage saturation and promote well-conditioned solutions. Excitatory units encode memories; inhibitory units act as flexible auxiliary variables optimized per memory to secure stability and E/I balance. Despite the supralinear, non-saturating activation, single-neuron saturation is avoided through learned E/I balance. Simulations show robust recall from noisy cues, resistance to ongoing noise, natural sparsity of effective synapses, and a reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset, consistent with cortical data.

Relative to classic Hopfield and follow-ups, the paper addresses multiple biological omissions simultaneously: strict Dale’s law, non-binary analog memories, and non-saturating units without relying on hard output saturation. It builds on and unifies strands from robust control (SSA) and stabilized supralinear network analyses, complementing prior work with global inhibition or binary-like recall states. The approach extends beyond spatial-bump models and spike-timing constructions by offering a general, pattern-agnostic framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: a unified, control-theoretic strategy that jointly enforces fixed-point existence, stability, and biological constraints.
- Technical novelty: use of SSA for stability optimization in high-dimensional recurrent networks, with clear practical benefits.
- Biological realism: strict Dale’s law via reparameterization, explicit E/I populations, balanced regime across attractors, analog memories.
- Empirical validation: strong numerical demonstrations of associative recall, robustness to cue and dynamical noise, sparsity of functional weights, and variability quenching after stimulus onset.
- Clear exposition and careful positioning with respect to the literature.

Weaknesses and questions
- Learning rule plausibility: optimization is global and offline; a local synaptic rule is only hinted at.
- Capacity and scaling: beyond one example (m/n ≈ 0.2), there is no analysis of capacity scaling with network size, number of inhibitory units, or memory statistics.
- Fixed-point approximation: the paper acknowledges dv/dt ≠ 0 at targets; the magnitude and consequences are not quantified.
- Distributional assumptions: memory patterns are log-normal; sensitivity to this choice is not assessed.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate use of SSA and thorough simulations. Claims are mostly supported, though capacity and fixed-point accuracy would benefit from quantitative analysis and ablations.

Clarity
Well written and organized. Methods and objectives are explained clearly; figures are informative. A few statements would benefit from clarification (see below).

Originality
High. The control-theoretic, SSA-based training of Dale-compliant analog attractor networks that avoid saturation and achieve balanced operation is a fresh and valuable combination of ideas.

Significance
Significant for computational neuroscience and recurrent network design. The framework is likely to inspire follow-up work on biologically realistic associative memories and control-based training of dynamics.

Specific requests for the authors
- Sensitivity to γ = 0.04 in Eq. (1)/(3): Please analyze how solutions depend on the gain scale γ. There is a near scale-invariance if weights are rescaled by 1/γ (since J involves W g′ and g′ ∝ γ), but the L2 penalty and fixed external inputs h break exact invariance. We suggest: (a) hold learned W, h fixed and vary γ to show stability margins and recall degrade; (b) re-optimize for γ ∈ {0.02, 0.04, 0.08} and compare recall, firing rate ranges, E/I balance, SA/SSA margins, and weight norms. This will clarify whether γ is a benign hyperparameter or requires careful tuning.
- Motivation for log-normal memory states: You cite heavy-tailed in vivo firing distributions. Please strengthen this with references and, crucially, test robustness to memory statistics: e.g., truncated Gaussian, Gamma, or uniform-in-range rates with the same mean/variance. Report how these choices affect learned E/I balance, sparsity, recall probability vs noise, and capacity. Does the heavy tail help or hinder stability/recall?
- Quantify fixed-point violations: You state dv/dt is “small but non-zero” yet an exact fixed point exists nearby. Please report, for each memory: (i) ||dv/dt|| at the target; (ii) the distance from the target to the nearest true fixed point (e.g., by root-finding/Newton from the target); (iii) the drift time scale from the target (v/||dv/dt||); and (iv) SA/SSA at the nearest true fixed point. A histogram across memories would substantiate the claim and bound the approximation error.
- Clarify lines 254–256: The statement that “an exact, stable fixed point had in fact been created very near each target pattern” despite nonzero dv/dt needs a precise justification. Do you have a constructive procedure to locate the fixed point? Under what conditions on J and Lipschitz constants does the argument hold?
- Preliminary capacity analysis: Please provide at least an empirical scaling: recall probability vs m/nE for several network sizes and nI/nE ratios, under matched noise levels, and for multiple pattern distributions. Report failure modes (loss of stability vs attractor interference) and how SSA margins shrink with load. Even a rough linear scaling regime and its dependence on ηF and ε would be valuable.
- Dale’s law: computational benefit or constraint? Include an ablation comparing (i) unconstrained W, (ii) Dale-constrained W. Do Dale constraints improve robustness, reduce saturation, or promote sparsity/E–I balance that aids stability, or are they neutral/harmful but biologically mandated? This will strengthen the case that Dale’s law is not merely an evolutionary artifact but a useful computational inductive bias.
- Biological learning: Expand the discussion on plausible local rules. Could inhibitory plasticity plus homeostatic excitatory mechanisms approximate the SSA objective? Are there connections to equilibrium propagation or implicit differentiation through fixed points that could yield local approximations?

Additional suggestions
- Report how often weak synapses can be pruned without harming performance, alongside the effect on SA/SSA and recall basins.
- Provide SSA vs SA curves during training to illustrate how ε trades smoothness for tightness; note if any solutions have negative SA but positive SSA at convergence.
- State computational costs (problem sizes, iterations, wall-clock) to inform reproducibility.

Pros
- Unified, principled framework; strong empirical validation; biologically faithful constraints; insightful link to variability quenching.

Cons
- Lacks capacity analysis; relies on global optimization with no plausible local rule; limited sensitivity analyses.

Overall assessment
A clear, original, and important contribution that advances associative memory under realistic constraints. I recommend acceptance after addressing the (mostly empirical) analyses requested above, particularly capacity, γ/log-normal sensitivity, and fixed-point quantification.
