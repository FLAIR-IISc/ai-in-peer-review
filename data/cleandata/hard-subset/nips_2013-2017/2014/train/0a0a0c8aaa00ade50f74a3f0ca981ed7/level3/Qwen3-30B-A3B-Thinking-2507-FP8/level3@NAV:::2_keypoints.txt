- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to train rate-based excitatory–inhibitory (E–I) networks that store multiple analog memory patterns while satisfying Dale’s law, avoiding saturation, and achieving dynamic E–I balance.  
- Core innovation: optimize synaptic weights by gradient descent on a cost that minimizes activity drift at target patterns, penalizes weight magnitude, and enforces stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).  
- Resulting weight distributions and firing-rate statistics (e.g., log-normal rates, sparse synapses) closely match experimental observations.  
- Network demonstrates robust memory recall under noise and stimulus perturbations and naturally explains reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Technical approach is sound with clear theoretical grounding (SSA) and comprehensive validation on synthetic and biologically motivated experiments.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., rate-based simplification) and provide thorough ablation studies (including synaptic pruning).  
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear and reproducible, with effective figures and detailed methods.  
- Highly original synthesis combining Dale’s law, graded attractors, and control-theoretic stability optimization not found in prior work.  
- Major significance: delivers a biologically grounded mechanism for analog memory storage that aligns with empirical data and advances the field.  
- Strong recommendation for acceptance; meets NIPS quality criteria.
