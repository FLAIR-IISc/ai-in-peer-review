- Introduces a novel control-theoretic framework that builds biologically constrained attractor networks, addressing Dale’s Law and activity saturation.
- Directly optimizes network parameters (using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to stabilize analog patterns while enforcing E/I balance.
- Positions the work as an advance over prior attractor models (Hopfield and successors) by explicitly handling stability and physiological constraints.
- Technical soundness: mathematically well-defined approach with a clever SSA-based solution to non-smooth stability criteria.
- Methods are detailed and sufficiently explain the optimization procedure and network parameters.
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and supported by informative figures.
- Originality: novel integration of control theory with explicit biological constraints to produce stable, analog attractors.
- Significance: provides a plausible memory mechanism consistent with E/I balance, sparse connectivity, robustness to noise, and reduced trial-to-trial variability.
- Weakness: optimization may be computationally expensive and scalability to large/brain-sized networks is unclear.
- Weakness: parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε) seem heuristic; lacks systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: rate-based model limits direct applicability to spiking networks and requires more discussion of translation challenges.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental neuroscience problem, presents a theoretically sound and biologically plausible approach, matches experimental features, and advances the field.
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about computational cost/scalability, heuristic parameter choices, and reliance on rate-based formulation.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with strengths outweighing minor weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
