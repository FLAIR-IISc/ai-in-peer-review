This paper revisits the concept of associative memory in neural networks with a fresh perspective, addressing several key biological constraints that were previously ignored. The authors note that prior work often failed to incorporate important physiological properties, such as Dale's law, non-binary representations, and sparse memories. To overcome these limitations, they develop a unified strategy that uses recent control-theoretic spectral-bounding techniques to optimize network parameters.

The authors propose a cost function with three terms that target memory fixed points, fixed-point stability, and small weights to avoid saturation. They enforce Dale's principle in weight parameterization, which naturally produces a balance between excitation and inhibition. Excitatory neurons are used to encode memories, while inhibitory neurons are treated as optimizable resources. Notably, single-neuron saturation emerges naturally from optimization, despite not being directly enforced.

The paper validates the approach through numerical simulations, demonstrating associative memory properties and robustness to noise. The authors also explain the reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset as a result of convergence to the nearest attractor.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it an important contribution to the field. However, it lacks a biologically plausible learning rule, only hinting at one. Additionally, the authors could benefit from discussing the sensitivity of their results to the choice of gamma = 0.04 in Eq. (3). Furthermore, they assume a log-normal distribution for memory states without providing motivation for this choice.

The authors should also quantify and discuss how much the cost function (4) may violate exact fixed-point constraints. Clarification is needed for statements on lines 254-256, and a preliminary discussion of memory capacity would be beneficial. Finally, it would be interesting to discuss whether Dale's law provides computational benefits or is primarily evolutionary/biophysical in origin.
