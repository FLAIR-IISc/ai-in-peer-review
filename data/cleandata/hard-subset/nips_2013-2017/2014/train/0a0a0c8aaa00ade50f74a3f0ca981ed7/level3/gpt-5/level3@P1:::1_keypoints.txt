- Proposes a control-theoretic method to train rate-based E–I networks that store and recall analog (graded) patterns while enforcing Dale’s law.
- Optimizes synapses by minimizing drift at target patterns to create (near) fixed points.
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to impose local stability at stored patterns.
- Treats inhibitory steady-state activities per attractor as auxiliary variables during training.
- Enforces sign constraints via reparameterization to respect Dale’s law.
- Trained networks show robust recall from corrupted cues with performance near an ideal observer.
- Networks tolerate ongoing noise and exhibit balanced excitation/inhibition at the single-neuron level across memories.
- Learned synaptic weight distributions are heavy-tailed with many weak synapses and limited dependence on those weak synapses.
- Reproduces stimulus-onset variability quenching similar to in vivo observations.
- Bridges classic binary/quasi-binary associative models and graded-memory extensions while addressing E/I separation and Dale’s law.
- Advances beyond prior LIF balanced-memory models that relied on saturation or global inhibition, and complements timing-based graded memories without explicit inhibition.
- Quality: objective is well-motivated and technically sound, combining fixed-point constraints with SSA-based stability.
- Experimental evidence supports successful attractor creation (negative SSA, small drift) and multiple physiological hallmarks.
- Limitations: no theoretical capacity bounds or scaling analysis provided.
- Limitations: some attractors are “slow points” rather than true fixed points, with no formal basin-size guarantees.
- Limitations: choice and sensitivity of SSA smoothing ε and trade-off hyperparameters are heuristic and not fully characterized.
- Limitations: training uses a global, nonlocal, offline optimization that lacks biological plausibility.
- Limitations: evaluations are limited to moderate network sizes.
- Clarity: manuscript is well-written and figures effectively illustrate results but needs clarifications on SSA choice, sensitivity analyses, convergence, and basin geometry.
- Originality: novel integration of robust stability optimization (SSA) with Dale’s law and graded, non-saturating rate dynamics.
- Significance: meaningful step toward biologically constrained associative memory and a potential framework for spiking instantiations and scaling studies.
- Suggested improvements: justify SSA versus alternatives, provide capacity/scaling plots, and report sensitivity to ε, regularizers, initializations, and runtimes.
- Suggested improvements: perform ablations (remove SSA, fix inhibitory variables, vary regularization) and quantify basin geometry, recall times, and robustness to synaptic perturbations/pruning.
- Suggested improvements: demonstrate a spiking (LIF) realization and clarify the role and plausibility of per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables during training.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a long-standing problem under biological constraints, is methodologically principled, and provides diverse empirical evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks scaling/theoretical bounds, uses nonlocal offline optimization, has no spiking implementation, and contains heuristic hyperparameter choices.
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — compelling, well-executed framework with clear empirical validation despite open questions on scalability, theory, and learning mechanisms.
