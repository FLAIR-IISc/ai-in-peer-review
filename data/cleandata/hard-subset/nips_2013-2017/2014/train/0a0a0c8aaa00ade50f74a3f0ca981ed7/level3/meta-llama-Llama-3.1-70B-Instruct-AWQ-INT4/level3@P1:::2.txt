This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for building functioning attractor networks that satisfy a set of relevant physiological constraints, including Dale's law, sparse synaptic connections, graded activity patterns, and low firing rates. The authors propose a rate-based network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons, where the synaptic weights and inhibitory activities are optimized to store multiple stable activity patterns (memories). The goal is to overcome prior attractor-model issues, such as the violation of Dale's law and activity saturation in memory states.

The paper is well-written, and the model, training, and results are clearly presented. The authors use a gradient descent algorithm to minimize a cost function that promotes attractor stability, penalizes weight Frobenius norm, and minimizes activity changes in target attractors. The learned synaptic weight distribution closely matches experimental findings, and the network exhibits excitation-inhibition balance in attractor states.

The results show that the network achieves graded, non-saturated attractors encoded in synaptic weights and inhibitory activities. The authors also demonstrate that the network is robust to noise in both the recall cue and the ongoing dynamics, and that it can perform memory recall with high accuracy.

One of the notable contributions of this paper is the combination of biological constraints to produce biologically plausible features. The authors' approach is a significant step forward in understanding the neural substrate of memory, and it has the potential to inspire new research in the field of theoretical neuroscience.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes a rate-based formulation of the circuit dynamics, which may not be directly applicable to spiking memory networks.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm or the scalability of the approach to larger networks.
* The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the approach, such as the requirement for a large number of neurons and synapses to achieve robust memory recall.
