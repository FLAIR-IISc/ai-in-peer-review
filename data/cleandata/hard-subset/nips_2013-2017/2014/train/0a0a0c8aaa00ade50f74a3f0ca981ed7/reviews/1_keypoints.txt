- Paper introduces a rate-based auto-associative memory model constrained by biological plausibility (Dale’s Law, non-saturating graded units).
- Memories encoded via synaptic updates from gradient descent combined with a recent method that enforces stability of the linearized dynamics around fixed points.
- Simulations show trained fixed points are stable and exhibit slow (near-fixed-point) dynamics.
- Trained networks display biologically consistent features: synaptic weight distribution centered at zero with long tails and approximate excitatory/inhibitory balance per neuron.
- The trained networks are robust to perturbations and stochastic input noise, demonstrating reliable auto-associative recall.
- Manuscript is clearly written, novel, and of likely strong interest to the community.
- Authors provide a clear literature review, architecture/training description, and effective figures illustrating results.
- Review recommends clarifying why the chosen training procedure was selected and whether it is particularly suited to enforce graded, non-saturating memories versus other methods.
- Reviewer suggests discussing whether alternative training procedures could achieve similar results or explaining mechanistically why this method succeeds.
- Suggestion (possibly beyond scope) to compare trained network performance to previous models in terms of capacity (number of memories vs network size).
