- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework that builds memory networks satisfying multiple physiological constraints (Dale's law, graded activity, low firing rates, E/I balance, sparse connectivity).  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to directly enforce stability of desired analog memory fixed points.  
- Advances prior theoretical work by enabling stable analog patterns while respecting biological constraints that previous models violated.  
- Technically sound: clear math formulation, justified use of SSA, and reproducible methods.  
- Experimental results comprehensively validate claims: memory storage, robust recall, sparse connectivity, balanced E/I dynamics, and reduced trial-to-trial variability.  
- Acknowledges limitations: target patterns are "slow points" (small drift) rather than exact fixed points.  
- Well-written and organized with effective figures; mathematical explanations are accessible to neuroscience readers.  
- Some clarity issues: need more detail on NLopt implementation parameters, ε-scaling heuristic, and earlier definition of terms like "auxiliary" inhibitory neurons.  
- Highly original use of SSA to achieve robust stability for arbitrary analog patterns under physiological constraints.  
- Significant contribution: more biologically plausible memory framework and explanation for reduced variability after stimulus onset.  
- Strength: simultaneous satisfaction of key physiological constraints with strong theoretical grounding.  
- Strength: novel SSA-based optimization and comprehensive experimental validation.  
- Weakness: rate-based model may not directly translate to spiking networks.  
- Weakness: optimization is computationally intensive and may not scale to very large networks.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of memory interference when multiple memories are active and relatively low theoretical capacity (~0.2 in example).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, technically sound, novel, and well-validated; explains important physiological phenomena.  
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about translation to spiking networks, modest storage capacity, and computational complexity.  
- Overall recommendation: acceptance — significant, well-supported contribution to theoretical neuroscience.
