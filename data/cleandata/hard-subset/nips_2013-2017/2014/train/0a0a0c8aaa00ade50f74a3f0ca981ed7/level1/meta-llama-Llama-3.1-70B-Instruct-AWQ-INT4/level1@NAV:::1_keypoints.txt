- Introduces a novel control-theoretic framework to build attractor networks that satisfy physiological constraints.  
- Proposes a method to directly optimize excitatory–inhibitory networks so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced regime and are robust to corrupted cues and ongoing noise.  
- Model explains the observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset in sensory and motor cortices.  
- Paper is technically sound with clear, detailed methods and supporting theoretical analysis and experiments.  
- Manuscript is well-written, organized, and figures aid understanding.  
- Approach is original and distinct from prior contributions in attractor-network design.  
- Results are significant and could advance understanding of neural memory substrates and retrieval mechanisms.  
- Arguments for acceptance: originality, technical rigor, theoretical and experimental support, and potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: uses rate-based dynamics which may not generalize to spiking networks.  
- Additional concern: reliance on optimal control may lack biological plausibility.  
- Paper does not clarify how local synaptic learning rules could achieve the stabilization objective.  
- Suggestions: discuss applicability to spiking memory networks, elaborate biological plausibility and links to synaptic plasticity, and explain possible local learning-rule implementations.
