- Proposes a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes network parameters to create stable fixed points while enforcing physiological constraints (Dale’s law, graded activity, E/I balance).  
- Leverages Smoothed Spectral Abscissa from robust control to ensure stability and noise robustness.  
- Demonstrates networks robust to noise and reproducing neural phenomena such as reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Builds on and addresses limitations of prior autoassociative memory models (binary memories, capacity realization, saturating neurons, global inhibition).  
- Differentiates from spatially connected and spike-timing models by offering a more general constrained framework.  
- Technical soundness: solid mathematical formulation, justified optimization approach, and clear rationale for gain function and Dale re-parameterization.  
- Novelty: combines control theory with physiologically plausible memory network design and directly optimizes for fixed points and stability.  
- Significance: offers a plausible mechanism for robust analog memory storage/recall and links model behavior to observed neural dynamics.  
- Clarity: well-written, organized paper with clear equations and informative figures.  
- Computational cost: scalability and optimization expense for large networks are not adequately addressed.  
- Parameter sensitivity: lack of systematic exploration of hyperparameter effects (ηs, ηF, ε, etc.).  
- Biological realism: rate-based model and simplified synaptic/temporal dynamics limit biological plausibility (no spike-timing, detailed synapses, or plasticity mechanisms).  
- Limited learning exploration: focuses on storage/optimization but lacks concrete biologically plausible learning rules for implementing connectivity.  
- Recommendation: overall positive—paper is a strong, novel contribution and should be accepted, though scalability, parameter sensitivity, and learning implementation need future work.
