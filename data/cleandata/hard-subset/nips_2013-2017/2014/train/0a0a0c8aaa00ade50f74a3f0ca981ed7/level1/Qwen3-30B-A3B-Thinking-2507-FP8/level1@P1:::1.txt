

# Peer Review

This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing memory networks that simultaneously satisfy multiple key physiological constraints: Dale's law, graded activity patterns, low firing rates, dynamic excitation-inhibition balance, and sparse connectivity. The authors directly optimize network parameters to make desired analog memory patterns stable fixed points of the dynamics, using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable stability measure. Their approach differs from previous work in the field, which typically violates one or more of these physiological constraints.

The paper builds on but significantly advances previous theoretical neuroscience work on memory networks. While Hopfield networks (1982) established the foundational framework, they violate Dale's law and use binary patterns. Later work by Treves (1988), Amit (1989), and others explored graded patterns but still relied on saturation or violated Dale's law. Roudi & Latham (2007) proposed a balanced network but with effectively binary patterns. The authors' key innovation is using SSA optimization to achieve stable fixed points for arbitrary analog patterns while respecting all physiological constraints.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the fixed-point and stability conditions, and they properly justify their use of SSA as a surrogate for spectral abscissa optimization. Their experimental results are comprehensive and address the core claims: they demonstrate successful memory storage (Fig. 2), robust recall performance (Fig. 3), sparse connectivity (Fig. 4), balanced E/I dynamics (Fig. 5), and explain reduced trial-to-trial variability (Fig. 6). The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, noting that target patterns aren't exact fixed points but "slow points" (with small non-zero drift). The optimization procedure is well-motivated and the results are reproducible given the clear description of methods. The paper is complete and not merely a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The figures (particularly Fig. 1-6) are excellent visual aids that effectively illustrate key findings. The mathematical formulations are precise and accessible to a neuroscience audience. The paper could be strengthened by providing more detail about the NLopt implementation parameters and the heuristic for ε scaling (beyond just "ε = 0.01 · 150/n"). Some technical terms (like "auxiliary" inhibitory neurons) could be clarified earlier in the text.

**Originality:** This work is highly original in its approach to satisfying multiple physiological constraints simultaneously. The use of SSA optimization to achieve robust stability for analog patterns represents a significant advance over previous work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior contributions by explicitly addressing each constraint and showing how their approach overcomes limitations of previous models. The authors' careful comparison to related work in the introduction and discussion (citing 26 references) demonstrates thorough understanding of the field.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to theoretical neuroscience by providing a more biologically plausible framework for memory networks. The results explain a key physiological phenomenon (reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset) that was previously unaccounted for in memory models. The framework could guide future experimental work and potentially inform the design of more biologically realistic artificial neural networks. The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating that analog memory storage is possible while respecting all key physiological constraints.

**Strengths:**
- Simultaneous satisfaction of multiple physiological constraints (Dale's law, graded activity, low firing rates, balanced E/I)
- Novel use of SSA optimization for stability
- Comprehensive experimental validation of all claims
- Explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability
- Clear comparison to previous work
- Good theoretical grounding

**Weaknesses:**
- The rate-based model doesn't directly address how this would translate to spiking networks (though the authors acknowledge this in discussion)
- The optimization procedure is computationally intensive and might not scale to very large networks
- The paper doesn't explore how their approach would handle memory interference when multiple memories are simultaneously active
- The theoretical capacity (0.2 in their example) is relatively low compared to some theoretical limits

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental problem in theoretical neuroscience with multiple physiological constraints
- Provides a novel, technically sound solution that advances the state of the art
- Demonstrates robust memory storage and recall with realistic properties
- Explains a key physiological phenomenon (reduced variability)
- Well-written with strong experimental validation

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The rate-based model may not directly translate to spiking networks
- The storage capacity (0.2) is modest compared to some theoretical limits
- The computational complexity of the optimization might limit applicability

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of theoretical neuroscience, advancing our understanding of how memory might be implemented in cortical circuits. The authors have successfully built a model that satisfies multiple physiological constraints simultaneously while demonstrating robust memory storage and recall. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The paper's significance lies in its ability to explain a key physiological phenomenon (reduced trial-to-trial variability) while providing a more biologically plausible framework for memory networks.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong case for its novelty and significance, and the results are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The limitations noted are well-understood and do not undermine the main contribution. This work should be of interest to both computational neuroscientists and machine learning researchers interested in biologically plausible neural network models.
