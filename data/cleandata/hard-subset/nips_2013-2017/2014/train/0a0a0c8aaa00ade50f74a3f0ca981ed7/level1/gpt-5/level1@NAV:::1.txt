This paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based recurrent networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons (respecting Dale’s law) that embed multiple analog memory patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points. The authors jointly optimize synaptic weights and per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables so that (i) the target patterns are (approximate) fixed points and (ii) the Jacobian has negative smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) around each, borrowing tools from robust control (Vanbiervliet, 2009). The trained networks exhibit E/I balance, robust recall under noisy cues and ongoing noise, sparse heavy-tailed synaptic weights, and trial-to-trial variability quenching upon stimulus onset (Churchland et al., 2010). The work positions itself against classic Hopfield-style and Treves-Rolls graded memories (often binary or violating Dale’s law), LIF attractor models relying on saturation or global inhibition (e.g., Latham & Nirenberg, 2004; Roudi & Latham, 2007), and structured bump attractors, offering a general construction that preserves physiological constraints.

Quality: The optimization objective is well-motivated; using SSA is an appropriate smooth proxy for local stability, and empirical checks with the spectral abscissa are reassuring. That said, targets often become “slow points,” not exact fixed points, and no formal basin-of-attraction guarantees or robustness margins are provided. Capacity claims are limited (30 memories in n=150; 0.2) without scaling analyses or bounds. Allowing per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables as free parameters during optimization increases degrees of freedom and may inflate apparent capacity; an ablation fixing these or constraining them via shared inhibition would clarify biological plausibility.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. Some key implementation details (hyperparameters in Table 1, SSA smoothing schedule, gradient computation, and code availability) should be expanded to aid reproducibility. Notation is consistent, and figures convey core claims.

Originality: Using robust stability optimization to engineer balanced analog attractors under Dale’s law is novel and a valuable synthesis bridging control and computational neuroscience. The connection to variability quenching is insightful.

Significance: If scalable, this approach could become a practical design tool for biologically constrained memory circuits and inform spiking implementations (cf. stabilized supralinear networks; Ahmadian et al., 2013; inhibitory plasticity, Vogels et al., 2011). Demonstrations on larger networks, theoretical capacity/robustness characterizations, and a spiking instantiation would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, novel use of SSA to enforce local stability in Dale-compliant analog attractors
- Robust recall with E/I balance and variability quenching consistent with cortex
- Emergent sparse, heavy-tailed synaptic weights without explicit sparsity penalties
- Clear positioning relative to classic and modern attractor models

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited capacity and lack of scaling theory or bounds
- Reliance on per-memory inhibitory variables during training raises plausibility concerns
- No spiking validation; transfer is argued but not demonstrated
- Local stability only; basins and robustness margins not quantified
