- Introduces a method to build E–I rate networks that store/recall analog memories while respecting biological constraints (Dale’s law, low firing rates, E/I balance).
- Frames network design as a control-theoretic optimization enforcing fixed-point and stability conditions.
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for dynamical stability enabling gradient-based optimization.
- Shows networks successfully store and robustly recall dozens of analog patterns and are noise-robust.
- Learned networks exhibit emergent properties matching experiments: tight E/I balance, sparse heavy-tailed synapses, and variability quenching.
- High technical quality: precise formulation, justified methods, and extensive simulation evidence.
- Exceptionally clear presentation with lucid methods, clean figures, and strong logical flow.
- Highly original synthesis of control theory and biologically constrained networks; SSA application is a key novel contribution.
- Significant impact: provides a normative proof-of-concept linking connectivity, E/I balance, and analog memory with experimental phenomena.
- Main weakness: the learning algorithm is non-biological (centralized, offline gradient-based), so the work is normative rather than a model of biological learning.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — strengths and insights far outweigh the limitation on biological plausibility.
