This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that adhere to physiological constraints such as Dale’s law and graded neuronal activity. The authors directly optimize network parameters – synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron firing rates – to stabilize analog memory patterns, resulting in networks operating in a balanced regime. The work builds upon decades of theoretical neuroscience research on memory networks (e.g., Hopfield [1], Treves [2,3]) by addressing limitations such as binary memory representations, saturating neuronal responses, and lack of explicit inhibitory neuron modeling. The proposed method leverages recent advances in robust control theory, specifically the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13, 14], to ensure stability.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The control-theoretic approach is well-motivated and the mathematical formulation is clear. The use of SSA for stability optimization is a clever application of techniques from control theory to neuroscience. The authors carefully address the challenges of achieving both fixed-point and stability conditions. The experimental results, while based on simulations, appear convincing and demonstrate the network's ability to store and recall analog memories robustly. The inclusion of ongoing noise and the observation of variability reduction following stimulus onset further strengthens the biological relevance of the model. However, the reliance on heuristic parameter settings (e.g., ηs, ηF, ε) weakens the reproducibility and generalizability of the results, and a more systematic exploration of these parameters would be beneficial.  The method is complete in terms of presenting a functional model; it’s not merely a position paper.  The authors are careful to note limitations (e.g., the fixed point condition is not *exactly* met, but close enough).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the limitations of previous work. The methods section provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand the model and optimization procedure. The results section is clearly presented, with illustrative figures. However, the mathematical notation could be improved for readability. For example, defining variables like *vµexc* and *vµinh* more explicitly before use would improve understanding. The connection between Eq. (5) and the biological intuition could also be strengthened with more explanatory text.

**Originality:** The combination of control theory, robust stability analysis (SSA), and biologically realistic network constraints (Dale’s law, graded activity) is novel. While previous work has touched upon some of these aspects individually, this paper provides a unified framework for building and optimizing memory networks that satisfy all these constraints simultaneously. The application of SSA to neural network optimization is a significant contribution.  The work is clearly differentiated from previous NIPS publications on memory networks, which typically focus on different aspects or utilize different techniques. The authors adequately reference related work, providing a comprehensive overview of the field.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant. The proposed framework offers a promising approach to understanding the neural substrate of memory. The demonstration of robust memory recall and variability reduction in the presence of noise aligns with experimental observations in cortical circuits. The work could inspire new algorithms for building artificial memory systems. The insight that network performance and sparsity are linked (demonstrated in Fig. 4) is valuable. Although the model is still simplified compared to real biological networks, it represents a step forward in bridging the gap between theory and experiment. The observation of variability reduction after stimulus onset is particularly compelling.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of control theory, robust stability analysis, and biological constraints.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrated ability to store and recall analog memories robustly.
*   Results align with experimental observations (variability reduction).
*   Potential to advance our understanding of the neural substrate of memory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on heuristic parameter settings may limit generalizability.
*   Mathematical notation could be improved for readability.
*   The model is still a simplification of real biological networks.
*   The claim of a "novel" framework might be overstated, as some components have appeared in isolation in prior work.
