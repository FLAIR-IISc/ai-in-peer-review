- Proposes a control-theoretic optimization to embed multiple graded analog memory patterns as stable attractors in recurrent E/I networks.  
- Enforces biological constraints: separate excitatory/inhibitory populations (Dale’s Law), graded firing rates, low non-saturating rates, and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to make stability differentiable and optimizable alongside a fixed-point condition and weight regularization.  
- Demonstrates successful recall from noisy cues with performance near an ideal observer.  
- Optimized networks show emergent biological features: sparse connectivity, tight E/I current balance, and stimulus-induced variability quenching.  
- High technical quality: sound optimization framework, careful analyses, and thorough simulations.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized presentation with reproducible methods and informative figures.  
- Highly original application of robust control (minimizing SSA) to store analog memories in Dale’s-law networks.  
- Significant impact: provides a principled proof-of-concept that biologically realistic networks can support robust analog associative memory.  
- Main weakness: learning algorithm is global, offline, and biologically implausible (L-BFGS), so it models what’s possible rather than how the brain learns.  
- Limited scalability/capacity analysis: shown on 150 neurons storing 30 patterns without systematic study of scaling or capacity limits.  
- Residual drift: optimization produces small nonzero dv/dt at targets—distance to true fixed points is not fully quantified.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel methodology, significant advance, biologically relevant emergent properties, and high quality/clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: non-plausible learning rule and insufficient scalability/capacity evaluation.  
- Conclusion: strong recommendation for acceptance as an important, original, and well-executed proof-of-concept likely to inspire future work.
