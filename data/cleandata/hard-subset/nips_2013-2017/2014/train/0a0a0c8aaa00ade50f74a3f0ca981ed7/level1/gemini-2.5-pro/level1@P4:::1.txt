### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing recurrent neural networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons that can robustly store and recall multiple, graded (analog) memory patterns. The central problem the authors address is how to build such networks while respecting several key biological constraints simultaneously: Dale's Law (separate E and I populations), low, non-saturating firing rates, and a dynamic balance between excitation and inhibition.

The proposed method frames memory storage as an optimization problem. The goal is to tune network parameters (synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity levels) such that a desired set of analog activity patterns become stable fixed points of the network dynamics. This is achieved by minimizing a cost function composed of two main terms for each pattern: (1) a term that drives the time derivative of neural activity to zero, ensuring the patterns are fixed points, and (2) a term that ensures the local stability of these fixed points. To make the stability criterion (a negative spectral abscissa of the Jacobian) amenable to gradient-based optimization, the authors employ the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound from robust control theory.

This work distinguishes itself from previous literature on attractor networks. Unlike classic Hopfield networks, it adheres to Dale's Law and stores analog patterns. In contrast to subsequent models that incorporated more realism (e.g., explicit inhibition or spiking dynamics), this work avoids the common pitfalls of relying on neuronal saturation, which leads to effectively binary recall states, or assuming specific spatial structures for connectivity and memory patterns. The proposed framework provides a principled method for generating networks that exhibit E/I balance for each distinct analog memory, a significant advance over models where inhibition is global or tuned for a single level of excitation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its novel and powerful approach to a long-standing problem in theoretical neuroscience. The synthesis of robust control theory (specifically, SSA optimization) with the construction of biologically constrained memory networks is highly original. The work provides a compelling proof of concept that a network can simultaneously exhibit Dale's Law, E/I balance, low firing rates, and robust analog memory recall. This is a significant step beyond previous models, which typically satisfied only a subset of these constraints. The emergent properties of the optimized networks, such as sparse connectivity and stimulus-induced variability quenching, further strengthen the model's relevance by connecting it to well-known experimental observations in the cortex.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The methodology is technically sound and rigorously formulated. Using the SSA to create a differentiable objective for stability is an elegant solution to a difficult optimization problem. The claims are well-supported by thorough numerical experiments that demonstrate successful storage, robust recall in the presence of noise, the balanced nature of the network dynamics, and the functional relevance of the learned sparse connectivity. The authors are careful in their analysis, for instance, by acknowledging that the optimized states are technically "slow points" rather than perfect fixed points, although this does not appear to affect performance in practice.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear and concise review of prior work, effectively motivating the need for the proposed model by highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. The methods are explained clearly, and the results are presented logically. The figures are informative and directly support the main conclusions of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Biological Plausibility of Learning:** The most significant limitation is that the proposed method is a non-local, algorithmic optimization procedure, not a biologically plausible learning rule. The optimization requires global information about the network state and Jacobian for all memory patterns to compute gradients for all synaptic weights. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, framing the work as a normative approach that defines a computational objective that local learning rules might strive to achieve. While this is a valid and important contribution, it means the paper explains *what* functional state the network needs to achieve, but not *how* biological processes like synaptic plasticity could lead it there.

2.  **Heuristic Parameterization:** The optimization relies on several hyperparameters (e.g., ηs, ηF in the cost function and the SSA smoothing parameter ε) that are set heuristically. While the results appear robust, a more systematic analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters or a more principled way of setting them would strengthen the work. For instance, the heuristic rule for scaling ε with network size `n` could benefit from further justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**

*   Presents a novel and powerful framework for building biologically realistic analog attractor memory networks.
*   Successfully unifies several key physiological constraints (Dale's Law, E/I balance, non-saturating rates) that previous models struggled to incorporate simultaneously.
*   Technically sound, clearly written, and provides a significant conceptual advance in the field of theoretical neuroscience.
*   The model reproduces emergent features of cortical circuits, such as sparse connectivity and variability quenching, linking function to structure and dynamics.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**

*   The learning mechanism is not biologically plausible, limiting its explanatory power for how memories are formed in the brain.
*   The selection of some model and optimization parameters is heuristic.

In summary, this paper makes a highly significant contribution by providing a new, principled way to construct functional and biologically constrained attractor networks. Despite the limitation regarding the learning mechanism's biological plausibility, the work successfully demonstrates that robust analog memory can emerge from networks respecting key cortical design principles. It represents a clear advance over the state of the art and is likely to inspire future research into both the function of balanced circuits and the learning rules required to achieve such function.
