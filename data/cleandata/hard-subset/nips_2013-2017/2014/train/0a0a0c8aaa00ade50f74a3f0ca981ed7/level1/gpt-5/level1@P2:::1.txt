Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based recurrent networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations that store multiple analog patterns as stable fixed points. The network respects Dale’s law via a softplus parameterization of synaptic signs, uses a rectified quadratic gain with no upper saturation, and is trained by minimizing a composite objective: (i) small velocity at the target patterns to approximate fixed-point conditions, (ii) robust local stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), and (iii) L2 weight regularization. The inhibitory steady-state activities are optimized per memory as auxiliary variables. The authors show: robust recall from noisy cues close to a log-normal prior; operation in a dynamically balanced regime with tight E/I cancellation; an apparent sparsification of effective connectivity; and a stimulus-onset reduction of trial-to-trial variability that matches a ubiquitous cortical phenomenon.

This situates well against classic Hopfield-style autoassociative memories and later graded-response/generalized attractor models that often violate Dale’s law or use binary patterns. It also relates to balanced networks (e.g., Roudi & Latham) and LIF-based attractors that rely on saturating nonlinearities. The key novelty is to directly optimize for fixed-point existence and robust local stability under explicit E/I constraints, borrowing tools from robust control (SSA), extending this group’s prior use of SSA for transient control to storage/recall.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is principled: fixed-point conditions are approximated by minimizing ||dv/dt|| at the targets; robust stability is handled via SSA minimization, with careful discussion of the ε trade-off. The Dale-constrained parameterization is clean and differentiable. Empirical results demonstrate successful recall, balanced E/I currents, and variability quenching, and include a useful “ideal observer” baseline.
- Support for claims: The results support robust local stability and recall for a moderate network (n=150) with m=30 patterns (capacity ~0.2). The E/I balance is illustrated across attractors. The claim that small weights are not essential is partially supported via synapse clipping, though threshold choices are ad hoc and one attractor is lost.
- Limitations: (i) Fixed points are “near” the target patterns (small but nonzero velocities), and SA is not directly minimized; SSA negativity is used as a proxy. The authors acknowledge this and argue practical equivalence, but a direct verification of SA<0 at convergence would strengthen the stability claim. (ii) Capacity and basin-size scaling are not characterized systematically across N, m, and statistics of patterns; a single working point is shown. (iii) The balanced regime is demonstrated qualitatively; quantitative metrics (e.g., input cross-correlation, balance indices, operating points across attractors) would be welcome. (iv) Practical scalability and computational cost of SSA-based training are not assessed. (v) The generality across initializations/hyperparameters and robustness to different pattern distributions is not systematically reported.
- Biological plausibility: The model respects Dale’s law, uses reasonable single-neuron gain, and yields balanced E/I. However, the learning procedure is global, offline, and nonlocal; a discussion of how inhibitory plasticity or biologically plausible rules could approximate the SSA objective is only briefly sketched. The inhibitory steady-state rates are treated as free per-memory variables during optimization; while they become part of the learned fixed points, it would be helpful to constrain them to physiological ranges and report those distributions.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The objective is explicit, the role of SSA is explained, and the experimental protocol (noise parameter σ, normalized distance dμ) is clear. Some details are deferred to Supplementary Material (e.g., mean-field initialization), and readers would benefit from:
- A brief derivation or pseudocode for SSA and its gradient use in L-BFGS.
- Reporting computational complexity and wall-clock times.
- More explicit ranges for inhibitory steady-state rates per attractor.

Originality
High. Directly optimizing an E/I network for analog attractors with robust stability via SSA under Dale’s law is, to my knowledge, new. Prior graded or balanced-memory models either relax biological constraints, rely on saturating nonlinearities, or do not tackle robust local stability explicitly across multiple analog patterns.

Significance
Moderate to high. If scalable, this approach provides a constructive recipe for building balanced analog memory networks with realistic constraints and makes a compelling link between recall dynamics and variability quenching. It could influence how we design and analyze cortical-like attractor circuits and seed future work on local learning rules that approximate robust stability objectives. Demonstrating capacity scaling and a spiking instantiation with the same learned W would substantially increase impact.

Strengths
- Unified treatment of fixed-point existence and robust stability using SSA.
- Enforces Dale’s law and yields low-rate, balanced E/I dynamics without saturating nonlinearities.
- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues and accounts for stimulus-induced variability reduction.
- Emergent sparsity of effective synapses and informative analysis via synapse clipping.
- Clear positioning relative to Hopfield-style, balanced, and LIF attractor models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Validate stability by computing the true spectral abscissa (SA) at learned attractors and report basin sizes.
- Characterize capacity and robustness across network sizes, pattern counts, and pattern statistics; compare to known bounds for graded memories.
- Quantify E/I balance (e.g., ratio metrics, correlation coefficients) and operating regime across attractors; report inhibitory rate distributions and plausibility.
- Assess sensitivity to ε and regularization parameters; discuss guidelines for selecting ε as N scales.
- Report computational complexity and scalability; include larger networks if feasible.
- Provide a spiking control experiment using the learned W to test transfer from rate to LIF dynamics.
- Explore structural sparsity constraints and compare with emergent functional sparsity.
- Clarify the success threshold choice and avoid changing it post hoc after synapse clipping.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled method with strong conceptual clarity.
- Compelling demonstrations of balanced analog memory recall and variability quenching.
- Addresses key biological constraints that many prior models relax.
- Potentially influential bridge between robust control and neural memory modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope (single N, modest m) and no systematic capacity/basin analysis.
- Stability evaluated via SSA proxy without direct SA verification.
- Scalability and computational practicality are unclear.
- Biological learning plausibility not addressed beyond discussion; no spiking validation.

Overall recommendation
A strong and original contribution with clear strengths in method and biological grounding. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing (at least partially) stability verification (SA), capacity/basin characterization, and reporting computational scalability.
