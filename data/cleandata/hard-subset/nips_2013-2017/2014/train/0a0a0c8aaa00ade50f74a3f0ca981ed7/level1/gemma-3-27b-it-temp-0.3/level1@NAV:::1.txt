## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of building biologically plausible attractor networks for memory storage and recall. The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework to optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points, adhering to key physiological constraints like Dale’s law and avoiding saturation. They leverage the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization, resulting in networks operating in a balanced regime, robust to noise, and exhibiting a reduction in trial-to-trial variability post-stimulus onset – a phenomenon observed in cortical circuits.

The work builds upon decades of research in attractor networks, directly addressing limitations of previous models like the original Hopfield network (binary memories, violation of Dale’s law [1]) and subsequent attempts to incorporate graded responses [2, 3] or LIF neurons [7] which often relied on saturating dynamics. The authors effectively synthesize recent advances in robust control theory [13, 14] with established neuroscience principles, offering a significant step forward in addressing the complexities of biological memory networks. The connection to observed variability reduction [21] is a particularly compelling result.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined optimization procedure. The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories under noisy conditions, with a compelling link to the balanced excitation/inhibition regime observed in cortex. The inclusion of inhibitory neurons and avoidance of saturation are significant improvements over prior work. The analysis of synaptic weight distributions and their impact on recall performance adds valuable insight.

**Weaknesses:** While the method is promising, the computational cost of the SSA optimization could be a limitation for larger networks. The reliance on a specific gain function (threshold-quadratic) might limit generalizability. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity of the optimization process (e.g., the choice of ηs and ηF in Eq. 5). While the authors acknowledge the rate-based formulation, a more thorough discussion of the implications for spiking networks would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of control theory to a fundamental neuroscience problem.
*   Addresses key limitations of existing attractor network models.
*   Demonstrates robust memory storage and recall with biologically plausible constraints.
*   Provides a potential explanation for observed cortical phenomena (variability reduction).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of optimization may limit scalability.
*   Reliance on a specific gain function.
*   Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant potential to advance our understanding of the neural basis of memory. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work represents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
