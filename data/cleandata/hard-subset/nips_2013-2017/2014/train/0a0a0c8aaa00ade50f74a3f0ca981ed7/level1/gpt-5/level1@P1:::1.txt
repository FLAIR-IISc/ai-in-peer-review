Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to constructing recurrent neural networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) rate units that store and retrieve multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable fixed points, while satisfying key biological constraints: Dale’s law, graded responses, low firing rates without saturating single-neuron nonlinearity, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors formulate storage as two coupled objectives per memory: (i) the target activity pattern must be a fixed point (dv/dt≈0), and (ii) that fixed point must be locally stable (negative spectral abscissa of the Jacobian). They enforce Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization of weights and use the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control for differentiable stability optimization, combined with an L2 weight penalty. They jointly optimize all synapses and, importantly, allow the inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory to be free variables during training. They demonstrate recall robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, E/I balance across memories, sparsity-like weight distributions, and a quenching of trial-to-trial variability at stimulus onset consistent with cortical data.

The work builds on and addresses limitations of Hopfield-type autoassociative networks [Hopfield ’82, ’84], non-binary/graded formulations and capacity analyses [Treves, Rolls; Battaglia & Treves], balanced attractor networks and stability analyses that typically used implicit/global inhibition or binary-like patterns [Amit & Brunel; Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham], spatial bump attractors [Ben-Yishai et al.], and spike-timing based graded memories without inhibitory units [Lengyel et al.]. The use of SSA follows robust control optimization [Vanbiervliet et al.] and is related to recent control-theoretic shaping of transient dynamics in balanced networks [Hennequin et al., Neuron 2014]. The variability quenching connects to Churchland et al. and computational explanations by Litwin-Kumar & Doiron and Deco & Hugues.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and principled: (a) minimize dv/dt at target states to encourage fixed points; (b) minimize SSA to ensure local stability; (c) enforce Dale’s law by construction; (d) regularize weights. Simulations verify that SSA<0 is achieved for all targeted patterns and that recall succeeds with performance near an “ideal observer” nearest-neighbor bound. The balanced regime is characterized convincingly via E/I current cancellation plots across memories. Ongoing noise tests show retention near attractors and stimulus-induced variability quenching.
- Caveats: (1) The inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ are optimized per memory, adding n_I×m degrees of freedom. While inhibitory rates are not clamped during recall, using them as auxiliary free variables raises concerns about over-parameterization and whether the learned stability depends on memory-specific inhibitory states that are not constrained by synaptic structure. An ablation constraining inhibitory activity statistics or penalizing across-memory variance would strengthen claims. (2) Capacity and scaling are not systematically analyzed. Demonstrated capacity is ~0.2 patterns/neuron for n=150; it is unclear how this scales with n, m, and ε in SSA. Given SSA’s cubic costs in n per memory (eigendecompositions), the computational scalability is a practical limitation. (3) The fixed-point condition is only approximately satisfied (dv/dt small but non-zero), with the claim that true fixed points lie nearby. More systematic quantification of basin sizes and fixed-point distances would help. (4) The gain function is a rectified quadratic without saturation; while this is supported by supralinear regimes, many cortical neurons exhibit effective saturation at higher rates. A sensitivity analysis to the gain shape (e.g., rectified power-law exponent, soft saturation) would be informative. (5) Comparisons are limited. In addition to the ideal observer bound, comparisons to alternative E/I attractor constructions (e.g., clustered balanced networks) and to trained RNN approaches (e.g., FORCE learning in recurrent spiking networks) would contextualize performance and requirements.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and organized, with precise definitions of dynamics, constraints, and objective function. The rationale for SSA and its properties is clearly explained, and figures illustrate training progress, recall robustness, weight distributions, E/I balance, and variability quenching. Some aspects could be clearer: computational complexity and scaling; the biological interpretation of memory-specific inhibitory steady states; and how ε should scale with network size (currently heuristic).

Originality
The combination of (i) explicit local stability optimization via SSA, (ii) E/I separation satisfying Dale’s law, (iii) embedding arbitrary analog patterns with low-rate, non-saturating units, and (iv) demonstrating balanced dynamics and variability quenching, is novel and timely. Prior memory models rarely satisfied all these constraints simultaneously, and stability has typically been implicit or reliant on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibition. Bringing robust control tools to bear on autoassociative memory construction is a fresh and potentially influential perspective.

Significance
The work offers a constructive recipe for building physiologically constrained analog attractor networks and bridges robust control and computational neuroscience. If scalable, it could impact how memory networks are designed and analyzed, and it provides mechanistic links to E/I balance and variability quenching. However, significance is tempered by the current scale (n=150), modest capacity and lack of scaling analysis, and the reliance on per-memory inhibitory auxiliary variables. Demonstrations in larger networks and/or spiking implementations using matched gains would substantially enhance impact.

Strengths
- Principled formulation tying fixed-point and stability conditions to optimization.
- Enforces Dale’s law; yields low-rate, balanced E/I dynamics without saturating nonlinearities.
- Robust recall comparable to a nearest-neighbor ideal observer; resilience to ongoing noise.
- Emergent sparsity-like weight distributions; weak weights shown largely non-essential.
- Demonstrates stimulus-induced variability quenching across trials, matching cortical data.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Over-parameterization via per-memory inhibitory steady states; suggest constraining/regularizing their across-memory variability, or training with fixed inhibitory parameters, to show robustness.
- Limited capacity and scaling analysis; report capacity vs. n and m, computational cost, and how ε scaling affects SSA-SA gap and success rates.
- Fixed-point accuracy and basin sizes not quantified; provide measures of fixed-point proximity and attraction basins.
- Sensitivity to gain function; test other exponents and soft saturation.
- Comparative baselines; include comparisons to clustered balanced architectures and trained RNNs (e.g., Sussillo & Abbott, FORCE) to contextualize trade-offs.
- Path to spiking networks; a proof-of-concept translation using the learned weights and matched f–I curves would strengthen biological relevance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled method integrating robust control with biologically constrained memory networks.
- Clear demonstrations of balanced dynamics, robust analog recall, and variability quenching.
- Addresses longstanding limitations of autoassociative models (Dale’s law, graded patterns, non-saturating regimes).

Arguments against acceptance
- Scalability and capacity not established; experiments limited to small networks.
- Reliance on per-memory inhibitory auxiliary variables raises concerns about biological and algorithmic parsimony.
- Limited comparisons to alternative approaches; unclear relative advantages at scale.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and technically solid paper with innovative methodology and convincing qualitative results. With additional analyses on scalability, inhibitory constraints, and comparisons, it would make a strong contribution.
