## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of building biologically plausible attractor networks for memory storage and recall. The authors propose a novel control-theoretic framework to optimize networks of excitatory and inhibitory (E-I) neurons to stabilize arbitrary analog patterns as fixed points, adhering to key physiological constraints like Dale’s law and avoiding saturated firing rates. This work builds upon decades of research in this area, notably addressing limitations of the original Hopfield network [1] and subsequent extensions [3, 7] which often struggled with physiological realism or practical capacity. The authors cleverly leverage the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) [13, 14] to achieve robust stability optimization, a significant methodological contribution.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The approach is technically sound, utilizing a well-defined optimization problem and demonstrating successful storage of analog memories in a balanced E-I network. The explicit consideration of physiological constraints – Dale’s law, graded activity, and balanced excitation/inhibition – distinguishes this work from many prior models. The results showing robustness to noise and the intriguing link to observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability [21] following stimulus onset add significant value. The clarity of the presentation is good, with a logical flow and helpful figures, though the mathematical details in the Methods section could benefit from further elaboration for a broader audience.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the proof-of-concept is compelling, the network size (n=150) is relatively small. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, and scaling this approach to more realistic network sizes will be a crucial next step. The choice of hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) feels somewhat arbitrary and a more systematic exploration of parameter space would be beneficial. Finally, the authors don't deeply explore the limitations of their rate-based approach or discuss how readily it translates to spiking networks beyond acknowledging the gain function similarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel control-theoretic framework addresses a fundamental problem in computational neuroscience.
*   Strong theoretical grounding and clear mathematical formulation.
*   Successfully demonstrates stable storage and recall of analog memories in a biologically plausible network.
*   Provides insights into the neural basis of trial-to-trial variability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Relatively small network size limits generalizability.
*   Hyperparameter choices feel somewhat arbitrary.
*   Limited discussion of the transition from rate-based to spiking networks.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the technical rigor, and the potential for future impact outweigh the limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding network size and parameter exploration.
