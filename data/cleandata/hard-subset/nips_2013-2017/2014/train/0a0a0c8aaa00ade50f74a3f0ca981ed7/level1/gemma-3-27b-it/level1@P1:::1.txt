This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for building attractor networks that satisfy several physiological constraints, including Dale’s law, graded activity representations, realistic firing rates, and a balanced excitation-inhibition regime. The authors directly optimize network parameters to create stable fixed points corresponding to arbitrary analog patterns. The resulting networks demonstrate robustness to noise and explain observed reductions in trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.

This work builds upon decades of research on associative memory, starting with the Hopfield network [1]. Unlike earlier models, it addresses limitations related to binary representations [2], impractical capacity limits [3, 4, 5, 6], and reliance on saturating neuron dynamics [7, 8, 9]. While recent work [10, 11, 12] has attempted to address some of these issues, this paper distinguishes itself by providing a unified framework addressing multiple constraints simultaneously, leveraging robust control theory [13, 14]. The method is novel in its direct optimization of network parameters for stability and fixed-point dynamics, rather than relying on inherent properties of specific network architectures. The focus on a balanced E/I regime and its connection to observed neural phenomena is also a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses Key Limitations:** The paper directly tackles limitations of previous models, offering a more biologically plausible framework for understanding memory networks.
* **Novel Approach:** The use of control theory and optimization for network construction is a novel and promising approach.  The Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) is a clever way to address stability optimization.
* **Physiological Constraints:**  Explicitly incorporating Dale’s law, graded activity, and balanced E/I dynamics makes the model more realistic and relevant to biological observations.
* **Robustness:** The demonstrated robustness to noise is a crucial property for any biologically plausible memory model.
* **Connection to Neural Data:**  The explanation of reduced trial-to-trial variability is a compelling connection to experimental findings [21].
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** Optimization, even with BFGS, is likely computationally expensive, especially for larger networks. The paper doesn't discuss scalability.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) appears heuristic. A more systematic exploration of parameter space and a justification for these choices would be beneficial.
* **Rate-Based Model:** While acknowledged, the reliance on a rate-based model limits the direct applicability to spiking neuron dynamics.  More discussion on bridging this gap would be helpful.
* **Limited Biological Validation:** While the connection to trial-to-trial variability is strong, further validation with more detailed biological data (e.g., synaptic distributions, plasticity rules) would strengthen the claims.
* **Implicit Baseline State:**  The pre-conditioning of the network with a stable baseline state simplifies the optimization. While acknowledged, the potential impact on the generality of the results should be discussed.  Is the learning process fundamentally different if the baseline is not pre-set?
* **Sparsity Explanation:** While the observed sparsity is noted, the explanation solely attributing it to the optimization objective is potentially incomplete. Network structure, parameter initialization and the choice of gain function likely contribute.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant advancement in the field of neural memory models by addressing limitations of previous approaches.
* The novel control-theoretic framework and optimization strategy are well-motivated and technically sound.
* The results demonstrate robustness and a connection to observed neural phenomena, indicating potential biological relevance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost and parameter sensitivity of the method could limit its practical application.
* The rate-based model and limited biological validation raise questions about the direct applicability to real neural systems.
* The heuristic choices for key parameters could be seen as a weakness.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and is worthy of acceptance. The novelty of the approach, the thoroughness of the analysis, and the connection to biological data outweigh the weaknesses. However, I recommend the authors address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, computational scalability, and the gap between rate-based models and spiking neuron dynamics in a revised version.  Specifically, providing a more systematic analysis of the parameter space and discussing potential strategies for scaling the optimization would significantly strengthen the paper.
