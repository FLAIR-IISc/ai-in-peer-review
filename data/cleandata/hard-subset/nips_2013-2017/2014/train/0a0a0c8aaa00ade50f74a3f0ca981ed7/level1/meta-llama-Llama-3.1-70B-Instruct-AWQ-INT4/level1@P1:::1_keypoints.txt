- Proposes a novel control-theoretic framework that builds attractor networks satisfying physiological constraints.  
- Directly optimizes excitatory–inhibitory rate networks so arbitrary analog patterns become stable fixed points.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced regime and are robust to cue corruption and ongoing noise.  
- Model accounts for stimulus-onset reduction in trial-to-trial variability observed in cortex.  
- Situates work relative to autoassociative memory literature and highlights prior models’ violations of Dale’s law and binary/saturated representations.  
- Strength: uses a rate-based E–I formulation enabling biologically plausible excitatory/inhibitory separation.  
- Strength: novel control-theoretic optimization to ensure desired activity states are stable.  
- Weakness: assumes a rectified power-law gain function that may not generalize across neuron types.  
- Weakness: assumes network homogeneity and random connectivity, which may be unrealistic.  
- Originality: introduces a new optimization-based perspective for building functioning attractor networks.  
- Significance: could materially advance understanding of memory storage/retrieval and inspire applications for memory disorders.  
- Recommendation: accept the paper for publication.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant contribution, novel methodology, more realistic memory network model, potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on simplifying assumptions, limited realism of connectivity, unclear pathway to therapeutic applications.  
- Recommendations: explicitly discuss assumptions/limitations, clarify applicability to treatments, and consider more realistic neuron and connectivity models.
