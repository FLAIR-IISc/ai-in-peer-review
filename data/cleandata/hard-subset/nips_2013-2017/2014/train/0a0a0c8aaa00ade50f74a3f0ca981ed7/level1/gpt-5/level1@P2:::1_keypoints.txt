- Proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based E/I recurrent networks that store multiple analog patterns as stable fixed points while enforcing Dale’s law via a softplus parameterization.

- Uses rectified quadratic neuron gains (no upper saturation) and trains by minimizing a composite loss: small velocity at targets, Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust local stability, and L2 weight regularization; inhibitory steady-state activities are optimized per memory.

- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues, operation in a dynamically balanced regime with tight E/I cancellation, emergent sparsification of effective connectivity, and stimulus-onset variability reduction matching cortical data.

- Positions itself relative to Hopfield-style and graded attractor models and balanced/LIF attractor work; novelty is direct optimization for fixed-point existence and robust stability under explicit E/I constraints using SSA.

- Technical formulation is principled: velocity minimization approximates fixed points, SSA addresses robust stability, and the Dale-constrained parameterization is differentiable and clean.

- Empirical results support robust local stability and recall for networks of size N=150 storing m=30 patterns (capacity ~0.2) and illustrate E/I balance across attractors.

- Claim that small weights are nonessential is partially supported by synapse clipping experiments, though threshold choices are ad hoc and one attractor was lost.

- Key limitation: fixed points are only “near” targets (nonzero velocities) and SA is not directly verified; SSA negativity is used as a proxy.

- Capacity and basin-size scaling are not systematically characterized across N, m, and pattern statistics; only a single working point is shown.

- Balanced regime is demonstrated qualitatively but lacks quantitative balance metrics (e.g., input cross-correlations, balance indices) across attractors.

- Practical scalability and computational cost of SSA-based training are not assessed.

- Generality across initializations, hyperparameters, and pattern distributions is not systematically reported.

- Biological plausibility: model respects Dale’s law and produces low-rate balanced dynamics, but learning is global/offline and nonlocal; inhibitory steady-states are free per memory and should be constrained to physiological ranges.

- Clarity is high overall, but readers would benefit from SSA derivation/pseudocode, computational complexity/wall-clock times, and ranges for inhibitory steady-state rates.

- Originality: high — directly optimizing E/I networks for analog attractors with SSA under Dale’s law is novel compared to prior models that relax constraints or rely on saturating nonlinearities.

- Significance: moderate-to-high if scalable; method links recall dynamics to variability quenching and could guide biologically grounded attractor circuit design and learning-rule development.

- Strengths include unified treatment of fixed-point existence and robust stability, enforcement of Dale’s law, balanced E/I dynamics without saturation, robust recall, variability quenching, and emergent functional sparsity.

- Main suggestions: verify true spectral abscissa at learned attractors, quantify basin sizes and capacity scaling, report quantitative E/I balance metrics and inhibitory rate distributions, test sensitivity to ε and regularization, and report computational scalability.

- Recommended extensions: spiking (LIF) validation using learned W, explore structural sparsity constraints, avoid post-hoc success-threshold changes after synapse clipping.

- Reviewer verdict: leans toward acceptance as a strong, original contribution contingent on partial fixes (SA verification, capacity/basin analysis, and computational reporting); concerns remain about empirical scope, stability verification, scalability, and biological learning plausibility.
