- Introduces a novel control-theoretic framework for attractor networks that store and retrieve analog (graded) memory patterns.  
- Frames memory storage as an optimization problem to make desired activity patterns stable fixed points.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control theory to directly optimize connectivity for stability.  
- Enforces biological constraints: Dale’s law with distinct excitatory/inhibitory populations and non-saturating graded firing rates.  
- Achieves stable recall via a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition emerging from optimization.  
- Optimization also yields realistic features like sparse connectivity and balanced E/I states.  
- Technical approach is sound, clearly explained, and supported by thorough computational experiments.  
- Demonstrates robust autoassociative recall, strong performance vs an ideal observer, and resilience to cue corruption and noise.  
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized with informative figures.  
- Main weakness: learning rule (global gradient descent on SSA) is not biologically plausible; local synaptic mechanisms are not provided.  
- Scalability concern: experiments on 150 neurons; discussion of computational complexity and scaling to larger networks is limited.  
- Overall assessment: substantial, original contribution linking control theory and neuroscience with strengths outweighing minor weaknesses.
