- Proposes a control-theoretic method to train Dale-compliant excitatory/inhibitory rate networks that embed multiple analog memory patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points.  
- Jointly optimizes synaptic weights and per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables, using smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) to enforce local stability.  
- Empirical checks with the true spectral abscissa support the SSA-based stability proxy.  
- Trained networks show E/I balance, robust recall from noisy cues and ongoing noise, sparse heavy-tailed synaptic weights, and stimulus-triggered variability quenching.  
- Novel and principled synthesis of robust-control tools with biological constraints; connects to variability quenching literature.  
- Clarity and organization are good, with consistent notation and informative figures.  
- Key implementation details (hyperparameters, SSA smoothing schedule, gradient computation, code availability) need expansion for reproducibility.  
- Targets often become “slow points” rather than exact fixed points; no formal basin-of-attraction guarantees or robustness margins are provided.  
- Capacity is limited in experiments (30 memories in n=150, ~0.2) with no scaling analyses or theoretical bounds.  
- Allowing per-memory inhibitory variables during training increases degrees of freedom and may artificially inflate capacity; ablation or constrained/shared inhibition needed to assess plausibility.  
- No spiking-network validation or demonstration of transfer to spiking models is provided.  
- If scalable and extended (larger networks, theory, spiking instantiation), the approach could become a practical design tool for biologically constrained memory circuits.  
- Pros: principled SSA use, biologically plausible E/I-balanced attractors, emergent sparse weights, clear positioning vs. prior attractor models.  
- Cons: limited capacity and scaling theory, reliance on per-memory inhibitory parameters, lack of spiking validation, and only local stability (no basins/robustness quantified).
