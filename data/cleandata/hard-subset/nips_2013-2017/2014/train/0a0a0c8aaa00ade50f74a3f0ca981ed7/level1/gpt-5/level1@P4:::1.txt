Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct rate-based attractor networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations (respecting Dale’s law), recurrent connectivity, graded (analog) memory patterns, and low non-saturating firing rates. Memory storage is defined by two conditions at each target pattern: zero time derivative (fixed point) and local asymptotic stability. The authors jointly optimize synaptic parameters (via a sign-constrained reparameterization) and the steady-state inhibitory activities for each memory to minimize (i) the squared drift at the target states and (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian as a differentiable surrogate for stability, plus an L2 weight penalty. They demonstrate: robust recall of analog patterns from noisy cues with success comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor classifier; emergence of tight dynamic E/I balance across memories; apparent sparsity of trained weights; and stimulus-induced reduction of across-trial variability under ongoing noise.

This work explicitly addresses limitations of classical Hopfield models (binary patterns, symmetric weights violating Dale’s law) and later graded variants that either lack explicit inhibition or rely on saturation in LIF-based recall. It differs from global-inhibition, effectively binary attractor models and from spatially structured “bump” attractors by allowing arbitrary analog patterns and inhibition that is tuned per memory through optimization. It leverages robust control techniques (SSA) recently used in neuroscience contexts to control dynamics. The contribution is complementary to theories of balanced networks and supralinear stabilization, and to prior demonstrations of variability quenching.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The dynamical system is standard and clearly specified. Dale’s law is enforced via a smooth reparameterization. The fixed-point residual and SSA are sensible objectives; SSA is a principled differentiable upper bound to the spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based optimization.
- Optimization: Nonconvexity is acknowledged. Gradients of SSA are computable; NLopt/L-BFGS is an appropriate choice. The heuristic scaling of ε with network size is reasonable but unvalidated.
- Stability and recall: Training reduces both SSA and SA at target patterns and small residual drifts. Although the fixed-point residuals are nonzero, the authors report nearby true fixed points; examples suggest this is the case, but a quantitative bound or explicit verification (e.g., Newton refinement to zero residuals) would strengthen claims.
- Robustness: Recall performance as a function of cue noise is compared to an ideal observer and remains competitive; robustness to ongoing noise is shown; E/I balance is quantified by correlated E/I currents across memories.
- Capacity and generalization: Demonstrations at n=150 with m=30 (capacity 0.2) are encouraging, but there is no systematic scaling analysis versus network size, pattern load, or pattern correlations. Basins of attraction and global stability (away from trained points) are not characterized.
- Biological realism: Rate model with rectified quadratic nonlinearity aligns with measured power-law f–I curves. However, optimizing inhibitory steady-state values separately for each memory is a strong, nonlocal design choice; whether such pattern-specific inhibitory configurations emerge from local plasticity is left open.
- Reproducibility: Key ingredients are described; some details (e.g., Table 1 parameters, supplement mean-field initialization) are referenced; code/data availability is not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and generally clear. Definitions of objectives, Jacobian, and SSA are explicit. Figures (as described) align with claims. Minor issues: the reliance on supplemental material for initialization details; arbitrary thresholding to define “silent” synapses; and occasional informal phrasing could be tightened. Adding pseudocode for training and a brief SSA primer would improve accessibility.

Originality
The use of robust stability optimization (SSA) to engineer multiple analog attractors in a sign-constrained recurrent network is novel. The combination of (i) arbitrary graded patterns, (ii) explicit E/I separation with balanced operation, and (iii) differentiable stability control is a distinctive contribution relative to prior attractor and balanced network models. The connection to variability quenching is a useful byproduct.

Significance
As a constructive framework, the work shows existence and design principles for analog memory in balanced E/I networks under physiologically relevant constraints. It may inform future mechanistic models and learning-rule development. Practical impact is moderated by the lack of capacity scaling, basin analysis, and spiking validation, and by reliance on offline, global optimization.

Strengths
- Clear optimization framework that enforces both fixed-point and local stability conditions.
- Respect for Dale’s law and emergence of tight E/I balance at low rates without saturation.
- Robust recall from noisy cues; robustness to ongoing noise; demonstration of variability quenching.
- Sparse-like trained connectivity without explicit L1 penalties.
- Careful choice of gain function matching cortical f–I curves.

Weaknesses
- No systematic capacity or scaling analysis; only n=150, m=30 shown.
- Fixed-point residuals remain nonzero; nearby exact fixed points are not explicitly demonstrated or quantified.
- Basins of attraction and robustness to pattern correlations are not analyzed.
- Inhibitory steady-state activities are optimized per memory (“auxiliary variables”), reducing biological plausibility and potentially inflating effective capacity.
- Lack of comparison to alternative training approaches (e.g., pseudoinverse/Hopfield variants, FORCE/RLS for fixed points) and to different stability surrogates.
- “Sparsity” claims rely on an arbitrary weight threshold; no statistical metrics (e.g., Gini, kurtosis) provided.
- No spiking network validation using the learned connectivity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide capacity analyses versus n, m, and pattern statistics; report basin sizes and failure modes.
- Verify existence of exact fixed points near targets (e.g., Newton refinement) and report distances.
- Analyze the effect of removing per-memory inhibitory optimization; quantify performance drop.
- Compare against baselines trained without SSA, with alternative regularizers (L1), or with known methods for fixed-point embedding.
- Explore heterogeneity in neuron time constants and gain functions; test transfer to spiking LIF networks with matched f–I.
- Quantify sparsity with standard metrics; assess robustness to weight pruning under principled thresholds.
- Include more seeds and report variability; release code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a principled, differentiable stability objective (SSA) to construct analog attractors under biologically relevant constraints.
- Demonstrates robust recall, E/I balance, and variability quenching without saturating nonlinearities.
- Provides a general framework that could be extended and adopted by others.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scope of experiments and lack of capacity/basin scaling limits confidence in generality.
- Biological plausibility is weakened by optimizing inhibitory states per memory.
- No direct comparison to alternative training paradigms or spiking validation.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel framework with promising results, but would benefit from broader quantitative evaluation and stronger biological and algorithmic baselines.
