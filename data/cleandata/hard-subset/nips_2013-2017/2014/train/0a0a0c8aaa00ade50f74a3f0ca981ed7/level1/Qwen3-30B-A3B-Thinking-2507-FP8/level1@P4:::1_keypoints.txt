- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to store/retrieve analog memories in excitatory–inhibitory (E–I) networks while enforcing physiological constraints (Dale’s law, graded activity, no saturation).  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable stability measure to directly optimize fixed-point stability.  
- Resulting networks operate in a balanced E–I regime, show robust recall, and naturally explain reduced trial-to-trial variability after stimulus onset.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous mathematical formulation of fixed-point and stability conditions and effective use of SSA.  
- Experimental validation is comprehensive (memory capacity, noise robustness, sparsity, E–I balance).  
- Originality: novel application of SSA to neural memory networks and unified handling of multiple physiological constraints.  
- Significance: provides a more physiologically plausible mechanism for analog memory and links to experimental observations of variability reduction.  
- Clarity: well-written, organized, clear mathematics and figures, with sufficient detail for expert reproducibility.  
- Weakness — limited treatment of how optimized rate-based connectivity would translate to spiking network implementations.  
- Weakness — scalability concerns: demonstrated capacity (30 patterns, 150 neurons, capacity 0.2) without theoretical analysis of scaling or limits.  
- Weakness — learning rule gap: algorithmic optimization lacks a biologically plausible local learning rule; inhibitory plasticity only briefly mentioned.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically robust, novel, thoroughly validated, and advances theoretical understanding of neural memory.  
- Arguments against acceptance: significant gap to biologically plausible learning, insufficient scalability analysis, and limited connection to spiking dynamics.  
- Overall: significant and valuable contribution with notable but common theoretical-neuroscience limitations; recommended for acceptance.
