- Paper introduces a control-theoretic framework to design E/I recurrent networks as autoassociative memories for analog (graded) patterns, addressing biological constraints like Dale’s law and low firing rates.
- Builds on attractor-network literature (Hopfield, Treves, Amit & Brunel, Latham & Nirenberg) and targets shortcomings such as instantaneous inhibition and saturation-based stability.
- Key innovation: cast memory storage as optimization forcing desired analog patterns to be stable fixed points by minimizing a cost combining fixed-point and stability terms.
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based stability optimization.
- Reparameterization enforces Dale’s law; threshold-quadratic, non-saturating gain demonstrates E-I balance can stabilize activity without neuronal saturation.
- Technical methodology is elegant, simulations are compelling, and analyses show robust recall, sparse/long-tailed weights, and variability quenching consistent with experiments.
- Paper is very clear, well-organized, and situates contributions in prior literature effectively.
- Major weakness: the L-BFGS global optimization is not biologically plausible and requires non-local information, so the work is more network design than a model of synaptic learning.
- Treating inhibitory steady-state potentials as free optimization parameters simplifies the problem but raises questions about post-optimization self-consistency of inhibitory activity.
- Scalability concerns: experiments use 150 neurons; discussion needed on computational cost scaling with network size n and number of memories m and on the ε heuristic for SSA smoothing.
- Optimized states are often “slow points” with small nonzero dv/dt rather than exact fixed points; authors should comment on whether this reflects numerical limits or a cost trade-off.
- Overall assessment: significant methodological advance and strong evidence that biologically-constrained analog attractors can be built; strengths outweigh limitations.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel SSA application, satisfies multiple biological constraints, demonstrates E-I balance stability, emergent properties match data, technically strong and well-written.
- Argument against acceptance: non-biological optimization limits claims about learning mechanisms.
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
