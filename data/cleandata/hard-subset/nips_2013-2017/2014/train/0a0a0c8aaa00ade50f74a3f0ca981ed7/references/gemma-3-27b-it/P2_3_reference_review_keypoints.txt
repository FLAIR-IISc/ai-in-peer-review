- Proposes a control-theoretic framework using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to optimize E-I rate networks for stable analog memory attractors.  
- Optimizes synaptic weights and inhibitory firing rates under Dale’s Law to store arbitrary graded memory patterns.  
- Demonstrates successful storage and recall of analog memories with robustness to cue noise and ongoing dynamics.  
- Shows emergence of dynamic excitation–inhibition balance consistent with cortical circuits.  
- Positions work relative to Hopfield and other memory models, highlighting limitations of prior binary, noise-sensitive, or inhibition-absent models.  
- Novel application of robust control theory (SSA) to biologically constrained neural memory models.  
- Builds on and contrasts with models using dynamic feedback inhibition and spatial structure by offering a more general solution.  
- Biologically plausible constraints (Dale’s Law, realistic firing rates) increase model relevance.  
- Thorough evaluation including recall performance, synaptic sparseness, and E/I balance analyses.  
- Optimization via SSA is innovative but computationally complex and potentially expensive.  
- Heuristics for parameter selection (ηs, ηF, ε) are under-justified and feel ad-hoc, especially ε scaling with network size.  
- Rate-based model simplifies computation but omits spike-level temporal dynamics, limiting applicability to complex cognitive processes.  
- Limited exploration of generalization — network tested on predefined patterns without assessing novel similar-pattern recall.  
- Sensitivity to synapse clipping: removing “silent” synapses can destabilize attractors, indicating fragile dependence on small weights.  
- Paper is technically sound with well-motivated theory supported by convincing simulations.  
- Mathematical details of SSA optimization are dense and could use clearer intuition and elaboration.  
- Original contribution: synthesizing robust control theory with biological constraints is novel and significant.  
- Results could broadly impact computational neuroscience and neuromorphic algorithm design.  
- For acceptance: novel, biologically plausible, robust, and technically strong contribution.  
- Against acceptance: scalability concerns, rate-model limitations, and synapse-clipping sensitivity need more investigation.  
- Recommendation: strong accept, with suggested clarifications of optimization heuristics, discussion of rate-model limits, and exploration of generalization to new patterns.
