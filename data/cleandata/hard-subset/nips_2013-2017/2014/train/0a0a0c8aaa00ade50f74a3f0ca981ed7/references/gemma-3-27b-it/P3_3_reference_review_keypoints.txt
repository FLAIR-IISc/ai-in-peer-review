- Presents a novel framework that formulates analog memory storage as a control problem, optimizing connectivity and inhibitory activity to create stable fixed points.  
- Leverages Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to ensure stability under biological constraints (Dale’s Law, realistic firing rates).  
- Demonstrates successful graded memory storage and recall, robustness to cue and ongoing noise, and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Situates work relative to Hopfield and subsequent models, generalizes dynamic feedback inhibition, and departs by using control-theoretic SSA.  
- Well-motivated connection to robust control theory and literature on E/I balance in cortex.  
- Tackles a fundamental neuroscience question about biologically plausible memory representation.  
- Novel integration of control theory with biologically constrained recurrent networks.  
- Incorporates Dale’s Law, realistic firing-rate dynamics, and explicit inhibitory neurons for increased biological realism.  
- Shows robustness to noise in both recall cues and network dynamics.  
- Clear writing, well-presented mathematics, and helpful figures.  
- Thorough evaluation including synaptic weight analysis, E/I balance, and recall performance.  
- Code availability stated, supporting reproducibility.  
- SSA optimization is computationally expensive, limiting scalability to larger networks.  
- Key parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) are chosen heuristically with limited systematic exploration or adaptive tuning.  
- Network architecture is simplistic (fully connected); more realistic connectivity patterns not explored.  
- Rate-based model limits direct translation to spiking networks beyond a gain-function mapping.  
- Lacks comparison to alternative stability/robustness methods (e.g., regularization techniques).  
- Overall assessment: strong, innovative, and biologically plausible contribution with important limitations on scaling and parameterization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: methodological novelty, thorough evaluation, biological plausibility, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, arbitrary parameter choices, and limited architecture exploration.  
- Recommendation: Strong Accept.  
- Rating: 8/10.
