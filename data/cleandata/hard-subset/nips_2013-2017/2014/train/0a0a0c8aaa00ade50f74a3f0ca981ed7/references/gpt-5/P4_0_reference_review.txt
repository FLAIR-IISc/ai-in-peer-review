Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an optimization-based framework to embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points in a recurrent network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons obeying Dale’s law. Neurons follow rate dynamics with a rectified-quadratic gain. The network is trained by minimizing a cost that combines (i) a fixed-point residual term (the norm of dv/dt evaluated at desired patterns), (ii) a robust stability term computed via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) applied to the Jacobian at each target state, and (iii) L2 weight regularization. Dale’s law is enforced through a log(1+exp) reparameterization of synaptic weights by presynaptic cell type. The inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory are treated as auxiliary variables optimized jointly with the weights. After optimization, the network recalls from noisy cues, is robust to ongoing noise, exhibits tight excitation–inhibition balance, and shows emergent weight sparsity. A demonstrated example stores 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network (capacity ≈0.2), with performance comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor “observer.”

This work addresses limitations of classical Hopfield networks (symmetric connectivity, binary memories), graded-response variants that do not model explicit inhibition, and models relying on saturating single-neuron nonlinearities or global inhibition calibrated for binary patterns. It connects to control-theoretic tools—SSA from robust control—and prior use of SSA-like ideas in balanced network stabilization. Compared to balanced memory networks with binary patterns and models with spatial bump attractors, it supports arbitrary graded memories without assuming spatial structure and with explicit E/I populations.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The objective aligns with necessary conditions for storing memories as attractors: small dv/dt at target states and negative spectral abscissa (via its smooth upper bound). The use of SSA is appropriate to obtain gradients in a nonconvex setting and to bias solutions toward robust local stability. Empirical results support that SSA < 0 is achieved across memories, recall is successful for a range of cue noise levels, and dynamics remain stable under ongoing noise. The analyses of E/I balance and weight distributions are consistent with known cortical features.
- Limitations: (1) The fixed-point condition is enforced approximately; targets become “slow points,” and exact fixed points are argued to lie nearby. This is plausible but should be verified more directly (e.g., by finding the actual fixed points and reporting their SA). (2) Inhibitory steady-states are optimized per memory as free variables. While the final network dynamics will determine these inhibitory states, treating them as per-memory design variables partially shifts the learning burden away from recurrent interactions and may complicate biological interpretation. (3) No analysis of basin sizes or capacity scaling versus network size and pattern statistics is provided; capacity is shown for one regime (0.2), which is modest. (4) The approach’s scalability and computational cost are not characterized; SSA optimization and L-BFGS in high dimensions could be challenging. (5) The link to spiking implementations is discussed but not validated. (6) The emergent sparsity is interesting, but its dependence on the objective (SSA vs. L2 regularization) and on hyperparameters is not dissected (e.g., ablations or L1 comparisons).

Clarity:
The presentation is generally clear: the model, constraints (Dale’s law), objective, and evaluation are explained, and figures (as described) support the claims. For reproducibility, more detail is needed: exact hyperparameters (ηs, ηF), SSA implementation details and ε scheduling, stopping criteria, optimizer settings, initialization means/variances (Table 1 is referenced), computational budget, and data/code availability. The role of auxiliary inhibitory variables should be emphasized and clarified.

Originality:
Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to construct multi-attractor, graded memory networks with explicit E/I separation and no saturating nonlinearity appears novel relative to cited autoassociative memory literature and balanced binary-memory models. The combination of control-theoretic stability optimization with biologically constrained network structure is a notable contribution.

Significance:
If scalable, the framework can serve as a general recipe for constructing E/I-balanced, analog associative memories with robust stability. The empirical demonstrations (robust recall, E/I balance, variability quenching, synaptic sparsity and pruning tolerance) are compelling. However, the current scale/capacity, lack of basin/capacity analysis, and absence of a biologically plausible or local learning rule limit immediate impact. A spiking instantiation or learning-rule perspective would strengthen significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear formulation integrating fixed-point and robust stability criteria using SSA.
- Enforces Dale’s law and achieves low-rate, non-saturating recall with tight E/I balance.
- Demonstrates robust recall under cue and ongoing noise; variability quenching matches cortical phenomenology.
- Emergent sparse weights; recall robust to pruning of small weights.
- Bridges neuroscience and robust control in a principled way.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Fixed points are only approximately enforced; exactness and stability margins near learned targets are not directly quantified.
- Inhibitory steady-states optimized per memory may reduce biological plausibility and blur what the network “learns.”
- No systematic analysis of basin sizes, capacity scaling, or sensitivity to pattern statistics; demonstrated capacity is modest.
- Scalability and computational cost not assessed; limited network sizes.
- No spiking validation; no local/plausible learning mechanism.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Verify that exact fixed points exist near targets and report SA/SSA margins and basin sizes; provide convergence diagnostics.
- Analyze capacity scaling with n, pattern variance, and sparsity; compare against classical and recent baselines (e.g., graded Hopfield variants, balanced binary-memory models).
- Ablate SSA (e.g., optimize without it or with different ε) to show its necessity; study sensitivity to ηs, ηF; report runtime and memory costs.
- Replace auxiliary inhibitory variables with fully derived inhibitory states during training, or justify their use (e.g., as dual variables); explore constraints tying inhibitory states to excitatory patterns.
- Provide a spiking instantiation with matched f–I curves, or a path toward local/plausible synaptic plasticity achieving similar stabilization.
- Expand methodological detail (SSA computation, optimizer settings) and provide code to enhance reproducibility.
