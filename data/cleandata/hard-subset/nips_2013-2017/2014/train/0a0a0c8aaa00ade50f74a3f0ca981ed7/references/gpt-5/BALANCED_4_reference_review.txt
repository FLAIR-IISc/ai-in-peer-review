Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the construction of analog (graded) attractor memories in recurrent networks that respect key biological constraints: separate E/I populations (Dale’s law), low/moderate firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors pose memory storage as an optimization problem with two objectives: (i) make desired activity patterns fixed points (minimize the norm of dv/dt at those states), and (ii) make those fixed points locally stable (minimize the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). Connectivity obeys Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization; inhibitory neurons’ steady-state activities for each memory are treated as auxiliary variables and optimized jointly with synaptic weights. The model uses rectified-quadratic transfer functions and a rate-based dynamical system.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate: successful storage of m=30 patterns in n=150 neurons (capacity ≈0.2), recall from noisy cues with performance comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor classifier, dynamic E/I balance around attractors, robustness to ongoing noise and stimulus-evoked variability quenching, and that many small weights can be pruned with limited loss of performance. They argue this overcomes limitations of classic Hopfield-type models (symmetry; binary states) and of subsequent work that relied on saturating nonlinearities, instantaneous or global inhibition, or strong spatial priors. The primary methodological novelty is the use of robust control tools (SSA) to enforce stability, building on Vanbiervliet et al. and prior work applying SSA to neural circuit design (Hennequin et al., 2014).

Strengths
- Clear formulation of storage as a joint fixed-point and stability optimization; principled use of SSA to obtain differentiable stability surrogates.
- Networks obey Dale’s law and operate at low rates without upper saturation, achieving balance-driven stabilization.
- Solid demonstrations: recall-vs-noise curves with ideal-observer upper bound; robustness to ongoing noise; trial-to-trial variability quenching reminiscent of cortical data.
- Interesting and perhaps counterintuitive finding that many small weights are functionally dispensable.
- Conceptually bridges control-theoretic stability tools and neural memory models; potentially a reusable design framework.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Only local stability is guaranteed. Basins of attraction are not characterized beyond empirical success/failure. Suggest quantifying basin sizes (e.g., along random and pattern-aligned directions), measuring spurious attractors, and reporting global landscape features where possible.
- Capacity is demonstrated at a single scale (n=150, m=30). No scaling analysis with n, m, or distributional overlap. Suggest a systematic capacity study across n and m with recall performance and basin sizes, and contextualize versus classic O(n) capacities (even if strictly incommensurate).
- The claim that sparsity “cannot be accounted for by L2” is overstated. L2 does shrink weights toward zero; while it doesn’t yield exact zeros, the observed peak near zero could be largely regularization-induced. Suggest ablations varying ηF, and exploring L1 or explicit structural sparsity masks to meet cortical connectivity probabilities.
- The optimization scales poorly: each iteration evaluates SSA and its gradient per memory, involving Jacobian operations that are O(n^3). There is no runtime or convergence analysis. Suggest reporting computation time per iteration, number of iterations to convergence, and scaling with n and m.
- Biological plausibility: learning is fully nonlocal and offline. While the Discussion mentions inhibitory plasticity, no constructive link is made. A useful addition would be a local-rule surrogate or a teacher-student setup demonstrating approximate realization with local plasticity (e.g., inhibitory synaptic plasticity stabilizing fixed points).
- Spiking realization is only argued, not shown. Given the emphasis on Dale’s law and balance, a minimal LIF instantiation (using the same W) demonstrating recall and E/I balance would significantly strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but several implementation details are missing or scattered: the exact gradient computation for SSA; step sizes/optimizer settings; τi, hi distributions; noise magnitudes in rate units; convergence criteria; sensitivity to ε in SSA. Including these (or providing code) would improve reproducibility.
- The decision threshold for success (d<0.001) is quite stringent; when pruning weights, the threshold is changed to 0.08. This confound should be addressed by using consistent criteria or explaining the metric’s calibration more thoroughly.
- Some references are incomplete or lack bibliographic details; please fix.

Originality
- The core conceptual novelty is applying SSA to construct analog attractors in EI networks under Dale’s law. This extends prior SSA-based control of transients (Hennequin et al., 2014) to fixed-point storage with graded patterns. The approach is interesting but reads as an application of existing robust control machinery rather than a fundamentally new theory of capacity or learning.

Significance
- If scalable and transferable, this framework could influence how memory networks are designed and analyzed, offering a route to stable analog attractors with balance and biologically realistic sign constraints. However, the current evidence is limited to small networks, lacks capacity scaling, and omits spiking demonstrations or local learning. As such, impact is promising but not yet compelling.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated method to enforce stability in EI attractor networks with graded memories.
- Demonstrates balance, low-rate operation, robustness, and variability quenching.
- Bridges robust control and neural memory design; the SSA-based recipe could be useful to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- No capacity scaling, basin characterization, or runtime analysis; unclear generality and scalability.
- Biological realism is partial: no structural sparsity, no local learning rule, no spiking validation.
- Incremental relative to existing SSA-based circuit design; limited theoretical advances.
- Some clarity and reference issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is thoughtful and technically competent, but to meet the NeurIPS bar I would like to see (i) capacity and basin analyses across scales, (ii) scalability/runtime characterization, (iii) either a spiking instantiation demonstrating recall and balance or a concrete local learning proxy, and (iv) explicit control over structural sparsity. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen both the scientific contribution and the potential impact.

Constructive suggestions
- Quantify capacity vs n and m, basin sizes, and spurious attractors; compare to ideal observer and to baselines without SSA.
- Provide scaling of optimization time and sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF; include code.
- Impose structural sparsity (fixed connection probability masks) and assess performance.
- Ablate the SSA term to show its necessity; compare to spectral norm or Lyapunov-based surrogates.
- Demonstrate a spiking counterpart using the learned W and matching f–I curves.
- Explore a local inhibitory plasticity scheme to approximate the stabilization objective.
- Clean up and complete references.
