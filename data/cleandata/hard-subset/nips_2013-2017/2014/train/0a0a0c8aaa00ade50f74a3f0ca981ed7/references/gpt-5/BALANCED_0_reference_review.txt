Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the classic problem of storing and recalling multiple attractor memories in recurrent neural networks, but with key biological constraints: separate excitatory and inhibitory neuron populations (Dale’s law), recurrent sparse connectivity, graded (analog) activity patterns, low firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors propose an optimization framework that enforces two conditions for each stored memory: (i) the state is a fixed point (minimizing the norm of dv/dt at the target) and (ii) the fixed point is locally stable (minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). Dale’s law is enforced via a monotonic reparameterization of weights, the single-neuron nonlinearity is threshold–quadratic (supralinear), and inhibitory neuron steady-state activities in each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables to be optimized. On small networks (n=150; nE=100, nI=50), the method yields: robust recall from noisy cues, attractor stability under ongoing noise, emergent sparsity of weights, and tight E/I balance across attractors, with performance comparable to an “ideal observer.”

This work sits at the intersection of associative memory (Hopfield and many follow-ups), balanced networks and stabilization via inhibition (e.g., Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham; Litwin-Kumar & Doiron), and recent control-theoretic approaches to shaping dynamics (SSA from Vanbiervliet; transient control in balanced networks from Hennequin et al.). It also complements graded/continuous attractor models and balanced E–I rate/spiking networks, as well as low-rank recurrent constructions that embed manifolds/attractors. The novelty here is to directly optimize fixed-point conditions and robust stability under Dale’s law, without relying on saturating nonlinearities or handcrafted global inhibition.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of memory storage as joint fixed-point and stability optimization; use of SSA is principled and practical.
- Enforces Dale’s law with a simple reparameterization; achieves low-rate, non-saturating dynamics via E/I balance rather than hard nonlinear saturation.
- Demonstrates robust autoassociative recall, including under ongoing noise; variability quenching after stimulus onset aligns with experimental observations.
- Emergent sparsity (without L1) and demonstration that many small weights are dispensable for recall.
- The inhibitory population is not just global gain—its activity patterns vary across memories, yielding more flexible stabilization than many prior E–I models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited scale and characterization: results shown for n=150 and m=30 (capacity m/n=0.2). No scaling analysis with n, no capacity vs. pattern load curves, and no systematic basin-of-attraction quantification beyond the σ-cue experiments. Suggest adding scaling experiments (n from a few hundred to a few thousand), memory interference analyses, and basin size measurements.
- Fixed point vs. “slow point”: dv/dt remains nonzero at targets, and stability is assessed at the target rather than the true fixed point. Please verify existence/uniqueness and distance to the nearest actual fixed point (e.g., via root-finding initialized at the target), and report how often the network converges to the nearest fixed point versus drifting.
- Role and necessity of optimizing inhibitory steady-state activities per memory: while biologically plausible that inhibitory ensembles vary across memories, allowing vinh^μ as free variables increases degrees of freedom. An ablation constraining inhibitory activity (e.g., low-rank parametrization across memories, or constrained variance) would clarify how essential this flexibility is for stability/recall.
- Comparisons and ablations: it would strengthen the case to compare against (i) networks stabilized by saturating nonlinearities, (ii) global inhibition baselines, (iii) modern associative memory formulations (e.g., continuous/graded Hopfield variants), and (iv) low-rank recurrent constructions embedding multiple fixed points/manifolds. Ablate SSA (e.g., use SA heuristics or Lyapunov-based surrogates) to show it is necessary, and study sensitivity to ε.
- Biological/engineering realism and reproducibility: connectivity is dense during training; sparsity is post hoc. Consider training with explicit sparsity, wiring-cost, or sign/magnitude constraints, and report current distributions and firing-rate ranges across states. Provide implementation details (hyperparameters in Table 1, SSA/gradient computation details, initialization seeds) and code to enable reproduction.
- No spiking validation: since the argument rests on matching rate-level power-law gains, a minimal spiking instantiation (e.g., LIF with comparable f–I) would materially strengthen the claim.

Quality
- Technically sound and well-motivated use of SSA; the approach is coherent and the demonstrations are convincing at small scale. However, theoretical guarantees (existence/robustness of nearby fixed points) and scaling/capacity characterization are missing. Comparisons and ablations are limited.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized; figures are informative. Please provide the missing Table 1 and supplementary details, and clarify the definition of capacity. Minor editing: spacing/typos (e.g., “dimensions.To”), and make explicit all hyperparameters.

Originality
- Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to embed multiple analog attractors in a Dale-constrained E–I network is novel. The work meaningfully departs from global inhibition or saturation-based stabilization.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a constructive recipe for building biologically plausible analog memory networks; links control theory and neuroscience. Current significance is limited by scale, lack of capacity analysis, and absence of spiking validation; addressing these would considerably increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled optimization framework (SSA) for stable analog memories under Dale’s law.
- Empirical demonstrations of robust recall, E/I balance, and variability quenching consistent with neurophysiology.
- Emergent sparsity and minimal reliance on saturating nonlinearities.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited scale and no capacity/basin analysis; unclear generality.
- Stability assessed at targets with nonzero drift; no verification of nearby fixed points.
- Few comparisons/ablations and no spiking validation; reproducibility details incomplete.

Questions for the authors
- How does performance and stability scale with n and m? What are basin sizes as a function of load?
- How sensitive are results to ε, ηs, ηF, and the choice of nonlinearity?
- Can you constrain inhibitory activity across memories (e.g., low-rank) and maintain performance?
- Do networks generalize to interpolated patterns between stored memories?
- Can you demonstrate a spiking instantiation with comparable recall?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and well-executed at small scale, but the paper needs stronger empirical characterization (scaling/capacity/basins), ablations/comparisons, and preferably a minimal spiking validation to meet the NeurIPS bar. I encourage resubmission with these additions; the approach is compelling and could have significant impact with a more comprehensive evaluation.
