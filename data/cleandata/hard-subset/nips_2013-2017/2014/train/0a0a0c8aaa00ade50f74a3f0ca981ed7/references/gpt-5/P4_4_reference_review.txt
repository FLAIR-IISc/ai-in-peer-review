Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a control-theoretic method to construct recurrent rate networks with separate excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) populations (obeying Dale’s law) that embed multiple graded (analog) attractor memories as asymptotically stable fixed points. Neuron rates follow a rectified quadratic transfer function; recurrent weights are reparameterized via a signed softplus to enforce sign constraints. Given a set of desired excitatory activity patterns, the method optimizes synaptic weights and the inhibitory steady-state activities per memory by minimizing a composite objective: (i) a fixed-point term that penalizes the norm of dv/dt at each target; (ii) a robust stability term based on the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target; and (iii) an L2 weight penalty. The SSA provides a differentiable upper bound to the spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based stability optimization [13,14]. The authors demonstrate recall performance in a 150-neuron network storing 30 patterns (capacity ≈ 0.2), robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, emergent E/I balance across attractors, and a heavy-tailed synaptic weight distribution with many near-zero weights.

This work positions itself relative to: classical Hopfield models [1,2] (symmetric weights, effectively binary memories); graded-response models with high theoretical capacity but limited practicality and implicit inhibition [3–6]; E/I networks with balancing/saturation mechanisms but typically binary-like attractors or global inhibition [7–9]; spatial continuous attractor models [10,11]; and spike-timing-based graded memory without explicit inhibition [12]. The key novelty is an algorithm that directly enforces fixed-point and stability conditions for arbitrary graded patterns in a Dale-constrained, recurrent E/I network using SSA-based robust stability optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of storage as joint fixed-point and local stability constraints is sound. Dale’s law is enforced via a smooth, differentiable parameterization. The use of SSA to obtain gradients of a stability surrogate is appropriate and well-cited [13,14]. Simulations demonstrate that after optimization, SSA (and often SA) at target states is negative, and recall is robust to cue noise and ongoing perturbations. However, several limitations merit attention:
  - The fixed-point term is not always driven to zero; targets become “slow points.” The empirical claim that true stable fixed points lie nearby is plausible, but would benefit from a systematic verification (e.g., solving for fixed points from the learned dynamics and reporting their distances to targets).
  - Capacity is demonstrated in one regime (n=150, m=30). No scaling analysis versus network size, pattern variance, or m/n is provided; thus, storage capacity and basin-size scaling remain unclear.
  - The optimization includes per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables vinhμ, increasing degrees of freedom by nI·m. While biologically inhibitory states are memory-dependent, treating them as independent optimization variables could inflate feasible storage compared to a strictly parameter-shared network, and this impact is not quantified.
  - Connectivity sparsity is not explicitly constrained; the network is dense pre-clipping. Emergent sparsity of small weights is shown, but true structural sparsity is only produced via post hoc thresholding.
  - No ablation on SSA (e.g., replacing with SA where differentiable, or other stability surrogates) or sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF is provided.

- Empirical evaluation: The cue-noise sweep and comparison to an “ideal observer” nearest-neighbor baseline are useful. Robustness to ongoing noise and variability quenching are convincing qualitative signatures. Additional quantitative tests of basin sizes (e.g., σ at 50% recall), perturbation robustness around each attractor, and generalization across pattern distributions would strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally well-organized. The optimization objective and biological constraints are explained. Several details are referenced to Table 1/Supplement (hyperparameters, initial weights). Some clarifications would help:
- Provide a brief description of SSA computation/gradients and sensitivity to ε; justify the heuristic ε scaling with n.
- State clearly whether SA is evaluated at convergence for all memories and report its distribution.
- Distinguish “weak/silent” (near-zero) from exactly zero weights, and clarify that structural sparsity was not explicitly enforced.
- Ensure references are complete (years/pages missing for several entries).

Originality
The combination of: (i) Dale-constrained E/I rate networks; (ii) direct multi-pattern fixed-point constraints; and (iii) robust stability optimization via SSA, to realize analog attractors, is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior associative memory and balanced network work [1–12,14,15]. The framing of inhibitory activities as auxiliary variables optimized jointly with weights is also distinctive (though its implications deserve further analysis).

Significance
Methodologically, the paper offers a constructive recipe to synthesize biologically constrained analog attractor networks, potentially useful for studying recall dynamics and E/I balance. The results suggest that tight E/I balancing can stabilize non-saturating rate regimes across multiple graded memories. However, significance is tempered by the lack of capacity scaling, basin analyses, explicit sparsity/connectivity constraints, and comparisons to alternative stabilization methods. The current contribution is a solid proof-of-concept with promising implications but limited quantitative scope.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, constructive method to embed graded memories in Dale-constrained E/I networks.
- Appropriate and novel use of SSA for stability optimization; differentiable and scalable to moderate sizes.
- Empirical demonstrations of robust recall, E/I balance across attractors, and variability quenching.
- Emergent heavy-tailed weight distribution consistent with cortical data.

Cons for acceptance
- No capacity or basin-size scaling; results shown for a single network size and load.
- Per-memory inhibitory variables may inflate storage feasibility; impact not quantified.
- No explicit structural sparsity; connectivity is dense pre-clipping.
- Fixed points not always exact; claim of nearby true fixed points not systematically validated.
- Limited comparisons/ablations of stability surrogates and hyperparameter sensitivity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide scaling of recall performance and basin sizes versus m/n and network size; report σ at 50% success per memory.
- Explicitly solve for fixed points post-training and report distances to targets; verify SA < 0 at those fixed points.
- Quantify the effect of optimizing vinhμ: compare performance/capacity when vinhμ are not free parameters.
- Explore explicit sparsity constraints (L1 or fixed connection probabilities) and evaluate impact on performance.
- Add sensitivity analyses for ε, ηs, ηF; ablate SSA term to show necessity.
- Consider a spiking instantiation with matched gain to demonstrate transferability.
- Complete and correct references.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-presented proof-of-concept with meaningful ideas; strengthening the quantitative analyses and controls would substantially increase its impact.
