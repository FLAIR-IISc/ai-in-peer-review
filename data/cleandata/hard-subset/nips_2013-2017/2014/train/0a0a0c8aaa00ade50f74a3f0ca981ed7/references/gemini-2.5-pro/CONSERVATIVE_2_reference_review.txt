### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel method for constructing recurrent neural networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons that can store and recall multiple analog (graded-rate) memory patterns. The authors frame the problem of memory storage as an optimization problem with two key objectives: ensuring the desired memory patterns are fixed points of the network dynamics, and ensuring these fixed points are dynamically stable. To achieve this, they propose a control-theoretic approach. The fixed-point condition is met by minimizing the temporal derivative of neural activity at each target pattern. The stability condition, which is more challenging due to the non-smooth nature of the standard stability metric (the spectral abscissa, SA), is addressed by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound on the SA. The authors optimize the synaptic weights (respecting Dale's law) and the firing rates of the inhibitory neurons for each pattern. The resulting networks are shown to perform robust auto-associative recall from noisy cues, operate in a dynamically balanced E/I regime, exhibit sparse connectivity, and reproduce phenomena like stimulus-induced variability quenching, all without relying on neuronal firing rate saturation.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a Significant and Unsolved Problem:** The paper addresses the important challenge of creating biologically plausible attractor networks that can store analog, rather than binary, memories. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous models, such as violations of Dale's law in classic Hopfield networks, the reliance on saturation in many subsequent models, or the use of global, non-specific inhibition which is ill-suited for stabilizing graded patterns.

2.  **Novel Methodological Contribution:** The core idea of framing memory storage as a direct optimization of dynamics using tools from robust control theory is novel and powerful. The use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) is a clever way to incorporate a differentiable stability constraint into a gradient-based optimization framework. This provides a principled, if non-biological, method for constructing networks with desired dynamical properties.

3.  **Biologically Relevant Features:** The resulting model successfully incorporates several key features of cortical circuits. It strictly adheres to Dale's law, operates with low firing rates without an explicit saturation mechanism, and crucially, demonstrates a tight E/I balance that is tuned for each specific memory pattern. The emergent properties of sparse connectivity and stimulus-induced variability reduction further strengthen the model's relevance to neuroscience.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of a Plausible Learning Mechanism:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its core methodology. The proposed method is a global, offline optimization procedure (L-BFGS) that requires full knowledge of all desired attractor states and the ability to directly set synaptic weights and even the target firing rates of inhibitory neurons. This is fundamentally an engineering approach to *construct* a network with desired properties, rather than a model of how a network could *learn* these properties through local, activity-dependent processes. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, suggesting inhibitory plasticity as a future direction, but this does not mitigate the fact that the current work offers little insight into the biological mechanisms of memory formation. It provides an existence proof, but not a process model.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** While the paper effectively critiques prior work in the introduction, it fails to quantitatively compare its model's performance against any established baselines. The comparison to an "ideal observer" is an interesting internal benchmark for recall robustness, but it is not a comparison to an alternative mechanistic model. How does the storage capacity (presented as 0.2 for the example) or recall fidelity compare to other, perhaps less biologically detailed, models capable of storing analog patterns (e.g., modern continuous Hopfield networks)? Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the complex optimization framework offers a tangible performance advantage over simpler, more established approaches.

3.  **Scalability and Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments are conducted on a relatively small network (150 neurons). The optimization procedure's scalability to larger, more cortically realistic network sizes is not demonstrated or discussed. Furthermore, the cost function (Eq. 5) includes several hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) that are set "heuristically." The robustness of the results to the choice of these parameters is not explored, raising concerns about the generality of the findings and the potential need for extensive fine-tuning for any new application. The paper also concedes that the optimization does not yield true fixed points (where dv/dt = 0) but rather "slow points." While the practical effect may be minor, it represents a deviation from the stated theoretical goal.

4.  **Novelty of the Approach vs. Established Principles:** While the use of SSA is new in this context, the general idea of using gradient descent to train recurrent networks to have specific fixed points is not entirely new (e.g., see work on FORCE learning or other target-based training methods). The paper positions itself as a significant departure, but its core is still a form of supervised, target-based network training. The main innovation is the explicit and differentiable stability constraint, but the overall framework feels more like an incremental, though technically sophisticated, advance on established engineering principles rather than a revolutionary new paradigm for understanding memory networks.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a novel and technically sound application of control theory (SSA) to the difficult problem of stabilizing multiple analog fixed points in a neural network.
*   The resulting network model is compellingly realistic, incorporating Dale's law and E/I balance, and reproducing several non-trivial experimental observations.
*   Addresses a clear and important gap in the theoretical neuroscience literature concerning analog memory storage.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   The core method is a biologically implausible, offline global optimization, offering little insight into how such memory networks could be learned by the brain.
*   The work lacks quantitative comparisons to alternative baseline models, making it difficult to evaluate its performance in a broader context.
*   Concerns about scalability and sensitivity to hyperparameters are not addressed, limiting the perceived robustness and generality of the approach.
*   The contribution, while technically interesting, feels more like a sophisticated network construction/engineering technique than a new scientific model of memory.

### Conclusion

This paper introduces an elegant and technically interesting method for constructing biologically plausible E-I networks that store analog memories. The application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa is a notable contribution. However, the work's primary weakness is its reliance on a global, offline optimization scheme that is far removed from any known principles of biological learning. While it provides a valuable "proof of concept" that such networks can exist, it does not explain how they might arise. Coupled with a lack of comparison to alternative models and unaddressed questions about scalability, the paper falls short of providing a truly significant advance in our understanding of memory in the brain. It is more of an engineering solution than a scientific one. Given my preference for methods that are either well-established or, if new, rigorously validated against existing approaches and grounded in plausible mechanisms, I find this work interesting but ultimately unconvincing as a major contribution to the field at this stage.
