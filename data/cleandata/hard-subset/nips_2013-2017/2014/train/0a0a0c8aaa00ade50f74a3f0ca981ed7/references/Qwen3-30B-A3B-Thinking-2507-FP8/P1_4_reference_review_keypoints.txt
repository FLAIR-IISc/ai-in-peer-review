- Proposes a control-theoretic framework that enforces target analog activity patterns as stable fixed points in E-I networks while respecting Dale’s law and low firing rates.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable surrogate to optimize stability, enabling direct parameter optimization for stable memories.  
- Demonstrates networks that store multiple graded patterns with balanced excitation-inhibition dynamics and realistic low firing rates (5–10 Hz).  
- Shows robust memory recall under noisy inputs and analyzes effects of sparsity and robustness in experiments.  
- Extends prior work by combining graded patterns, Dale’s law, non-saturating gains, and E/I balance within a single framework.  
- Technical approach is rigorous with a precise mathematical formulation and comprehensive experimental validation.  
- Novel application of SSA for neural-network stability optimization and clear differentiation from Hopfield/binary/spatial models.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and accessible to both computational neuroscience and machine learning audiences.  
- Significant contribution with potential to bridge theoretical models and empirical neuroscience and to inspire biologically plausible artificial networks.  
- Lacks discussion of how the optimized networks could be learned via biologically plausible synaptic plasticity rules.  
- Storage capacity is modest (≈0.2, e.g., 30 patterns in 150 neurons) and scalability/capacity limits are not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Connection to spiking network implementations is speculative; no concrete spiking demonstrations are provided.  
- Overall assessment: strong, technically sound contribution with important future directions; recommendation to accept.
