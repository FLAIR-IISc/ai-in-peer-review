- Summary: Proposes a control-theoretic method to embed multiple graded attractors in Dale-compliant E/I rate networks by jointly optimizing weights and per-memory inhibitory steady-states using fixed-point and Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) objectives.

- Novelty: Casting memory storage as constrained stability optimization and using SSA for gradient-based stability control is conceptually new for graded E/I attractors.

- Methodological strength: Practical use of SSA provides a smooth, differentiable surrogate for spectral abscissa enabling high-dimensional gradient training for local stability.

- Biological plausibility: Network enforces E/I populations, non-saturating transfer, balanced recall, realistic firing-rate patterns, and heavy-tailed learned synapses.

- Empirical demonstrations: Shows robust recall from noisy cues, variability quenching under ongoing noise, tight E/I balance at recall, and resilience to pruning small weights.

- Clear objective: Loss transparently combines fixed-point constraints, local stability, and weight regularization.

- Capacity/scaling limitation: Experiments limited to n=150, m=30 (capacity ~0.2) with no systematic scaling analysis or theoretical capacity characterization.

- Free inhibitory degrees of freedom: Treating per-memory inhibitory steady-states as free parameters (n_I × m) may over-parameterize and bias feasibility; necessity not established.

- Basin and global-dynamics gap: Analysis focuses on local stability; basin sizes, spurious attractors, and global recall geometry are insufficiently characterized.

- Robustness and sensitivity missing: No systematic tests of robustness to synaptic perturbations, sensitivity to SSA smoothness ε, regularization, initialization, or convergence statistics across runs.

- Sparsity/biological constraint issue: Connectivity is dense pre-pruning and sparsity is not enforced; heavy-tailed weights may result from L2 regularization rather than true sparse structure.

- Comparison gap: Lacks comparisons to other trained E/I stabilizing methods (Lyapunov, low-rank RNNs, FORCE, prior balanced-memory models) for recall performance and capacity.

- Reproducibility and cost concerns: No computational complexity analysis; per-iteration cost dominated by m Jacobians/SSA and scalability guidance is missing.

- Technical soundness: Formulation and experiments are technically appropriate and SSA is well-motivated, but key claims need stronger quantitative support.

- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-organized, but missing quantitative summaries (e.g., distributions of SA/SSA, convergence/failure rates, computational cost).

- Originality: High — novel combination of graded memories, Dale’s law, non-saturating gains, and SSA-based stability optimization.

- Significance caveat: Potentially important for balanced E/I memory models, but impact limited by lack of scaling, robustness, and comparative analyses.

- Suggested experiments: Provide capacity vs. N scaling, quantify basin sizes and spurious attractors, ablate or penalize per-memory v_inh optimization, test synaptic perturbation robustness, and run sensitivity analyses for ε and regularization.

- Suggested comparisons/designs: Compare to alternatives without SSA, Lyapunov constraints, and other E/I memory constructions; consider explicit sparsity or structured connectivity.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel principled method, biologically-aligned emergent phenomena, and technically interesting SSA use.

- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient capacity/scaling characterization, potential over-parameterization via v_inh^μ, limited robustness/basin analyses, and unclear reproducibility/scalability.

- Overall: Promising and creative contribution that requires further quantitative analyses of capacity, robustness, and the role of inhibitory degrees of freedom to fully establish significance.
