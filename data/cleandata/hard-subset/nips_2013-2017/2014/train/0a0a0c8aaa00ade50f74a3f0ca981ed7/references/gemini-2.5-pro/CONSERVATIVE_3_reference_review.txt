This paper presents a novel framework for constructing recurrent neural networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons capable of storing and recalling analog, or graded-rate, memory patterns. The authors frame the problem of memory storage as an optimization task, aiming to simultaneously satisfy two conditions for a set of desired activity patterns: that they are fixed points of the network dynamics, and that these fixed points are dynamically stable. To achieve this, they propose minimizing a cost function that includes terms for the velocity of the system state (`dv/dt`) and, crucially, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the system's Jacobian. The SSA, a tool from robust control theory, serves as a differentiable proxy for the spectral abscissa, allowing for gradient-based optimization of network stability. The synaptic weights and the activity levels of inhibitory neurons for each pattern are optimized to embed the memories. The authors demonstrate that the resulting networks successfully recall noisy versions of the stored patterns, operate in a régime of tight E/I balance, and exhibit emergent properties like sparse connectivity and stimulus-induced variability quenching, which are qualitatively consistent with biological observations.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem in theoretical neuroscience: how to create associative memory networks that store analog patterns while respecting fundamental biological constraints such as Dale's law and E/I balance. The departure from classic binary Hopfield-type models is well-motivated.
2.  **Novel Technical Approach:** The application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to directly optimize for the stability of multiple attractors is an original and interesting contribution. This provides an engineering solution to a difficult network design problem.
3.  **Emergent Properties:** The model successfully produces networks that exhibit several non-trivial and biologically-relevant properties without them being explicitly enforced. The emergence of a tight E/I balance that is fine-tuned for each neuron and each memory pattern is particularly compelling. The resulting sparse weight distributions are also noteworthy.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is laid out clearly. The motivation and goals are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Biologically Implausible "Learning":** The central method relies on a global, offline optimization of all network parameters using an L-BFGS algorithm. This procedure requires full knowledge of the system's state, its dynamics, and its Jacobian matrix for all desired patterns simultaneously. While this is a valid method for *constructing* a network with desired properties, it cannot be interpreted as a model of biological *learning*. The paper provides a proof-of-existence for such networks but sidesteps the fundamental question of how a brain, through local and activity-dependent rules, could ever converge to such a solution. The authors relegate this critical issue to future work, which significantly limits the paper's impact as a contribution to computational neuroscience.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The performance of the network is compared only to a statistical "ideal observer." This is insufficient. A proper evaluation requires benchmarking against other established models of associative memory. For example, how does the storage capacity or robustness of this network compare to modern continuous Hopfield networks or other rate-based models, even those that do not satisfy all the same biological constraints? Without such comparisons, the claims of the paper are difficult to contextualize, and the advantages of this complex optimization-based approach over simpler, more established methods remain unquantified.
3.  **Methodological Soundness and Parameter Sensitivity:** The proposed cost function relies on several hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) that are set "heuristically." The paper offers no sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values. It is unclear how robust the method is to changes in these parameters. For a method that hinges entirely on an optimization procedure, this lack of rigorous analysis is a notable weakness. Furthermore, the analysis of the network's properties is sometimes cursory. For instance, the conclusion that weak synapses are not necessary for recall is based on a single experiment with one arbitrary clipping threshold, which is not sufficient evidence for such a general claim.
4.  **Scope and Originality:** While the application of SSA is novel in this context, the overall approach of direct optimization to achieve network function has been explored before (e.g., [14]). The primary contribution is thus the application of this specific technique to the analog memory problem in E-I networks. However, without a plausible learning story or a clear performance advantage over existing models, the significance of this contribution is limited.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**For Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and unresolved problem of storing analog memories in biologically constrained networks.
*   The use of SSA optimization for attractor stability is a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The emergent properties of the trained networks (E/I balance, sparse weights) are compelling and align with experimental data.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The core method is an engineering solution based on global optimization, which is biologically implausible and offers little insight into synaptic learning mechanisms.
*   The paper fails to compare its model against any relevant established computational baselines, making it impossible to assess its performance, capacity, or utility relative to prior work.
*   The methodology lacks a rigorous analysis of its sensitivity to key hyperparameters.
*   The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete scientific study with thorough validation.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents an interesting and original technical approach to designing networks with desirable properties, its contributions are undermined by major weaknesses. The reliance on a biologically implausible optimization scheme and the complete absence of comparisons to established baseline models are critical flaws. As a reviewer who values methodological rigor and validation against existing work, I find that the paper does not meet the standards for publication at NeurIPS. The approach is too unconventional to be accepted without a much more thorough demonstration of its advantages and a more serious engagement with the question of how such a network could be learned. I therefore recommend rejection.
