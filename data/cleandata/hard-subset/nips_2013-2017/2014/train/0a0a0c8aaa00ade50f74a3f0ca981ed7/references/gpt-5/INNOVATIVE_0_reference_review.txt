Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent E-I rate networks (respecting Dale’s law) that embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as asymptotically stable attractors with low, biologically plausible firing rates. The authors jointly optimize synaptic weights and the inhibitory steady-state activities associated with each memory so that: (i) each target pattern is (approximately) a fixed point (minimizing ||dv/dt|| at those states), and (ii) each is locally stable (minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). The networks operate in a balanced regime where excitation and inhibition tightly cancel; recall is robust to noise in cues and ongoing dynamics; synaptic weights become sparse without explicit sparsity regularization; and weak weights can be pruned with minimal loss. Variability quenching upon stimulus onset emerges naturally.

Relative to classic Hopfield networks and graded-response variants (Treves & Rolls), this work enforces Dale’s law and obtains genuinely graded attractors at low rates without relying on saturation. Unlike many previous models with implicit/global inhibition or spatially structured “bump” attractors, inhibition here is explicit and the framework allows arbitrary analog patterns. The use of SSA from robust control to directly optimize dynamical stability in neuroscience settings builds on prior robust stability work (Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.) but is applied here to multi-attractor memory storage with Dale constraints—this integration is, in my view, the key conceptual novelty.

Quality
- Soundness: The optimization objective is clear and justified; the SSA is an appropriate differentiable surrogate for spectral abscissa, and the authors also report SA to validate stability. Empirical results demonstrate recall, robustness, balance, sparsity, and variability quenching.
- Caveats: (i) The fixed-point condition is not always met exactly (“slow points”), though the authors argue nearby true fixed points exist; more systematic verification would strengthen claims. (ii) The training treats inhibitory steady-state activities per memory as free variables; while plausible as auxiliary variables in an optimization, this increases degrees of freedom (nI·m) and raises questions about over-parameterization and biological realizability via local plasticity. (iii) No analysis of capacity scaling, basin volumes, or spurious attractors is provided; experiments are limited to n=150, m=30. (iv) The method is offline and nonlocal (L-BFGS), with unclear scalability. (v) The sparsity claim is suggestive but would benefit from ablations (e.g., different regularizers) and reporting of actual network density after pruning.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The definition of the SSA and its ε-scaling heuristic are clear enough for reproduction; use of NLopt/L-BFGS is appropriate. Some presentation details could be improved: reference formatting is inconsistent/incomplete; justify thresholds used for success and for pruning; clarify whether SA is negative for all stored patterns at convergence; and report hyperparameters (ηs, ηF) and sensitivity.

Originality
High. The idea of directly optimizing a stability surrogate (SSA) to synthesize multi-attractor, graded, low-rate, balanced E-I networks under Dale’s law is, to my knowledge, new in this combination. Prior work either relied on binary patterns, global inhibition, saturation mechanisms, or spatially structured attractors.

Significance
If scalable, this approach could influence how we construct and analyze attractor memory circuits that are both dynamically and structurally realistic. The bridge between robust control and memory storage is promising and could inspire new learning rules or constraints for spiking networks. Even as a “constructive existence proof,” it suggests new directions for inhibitory plasticity and balanced computations.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold synthesis of robust control (SSA) with biologically constrained attractor networks.
- Genuinely graded attractors with low firing rates and explicit E/I balance; no reliance on saturation.
- Robust recall to cue corruption and ongoing noise; variability quenching emerges.
- Dale’s law enforced; sparse effective connectivity arises; weak-synapse pruning largely preserves performance.
- Clear demonstrations and practical optimization framework.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Capacity and scaling: Provide experiments beyond n=150; report capacity vs. n and m, basin size estimates, and prevalence of spurious attractors. Analyze SSA–SA gap as n grows.
- Over-parameterization: Discuss the role and necessity of optimizing v_inh^μ. Are comparable results achievable without treating these as free variables? Do inhibitory patterns generalize or overfit?
- Biological learning: Outline pathway to local/plausible plasticity (e.g., inhibitory plasticity, homeostasis) that could approximate the SSA objective; even a toy demonstration would help.
- Fixed points: More systematically verify that true fixed points exist near all targets (e.g., Newton refinement, root-finding from slow points).
- Comparisons: Baseline against classical autoassociative constructions under Dale’s law, and against simpler stability proxies (e.g., bounding the symmetric part of J, Gershgorin-based constraints).
- Spiking networks: A small-scale LIF instantiation using the learned W would bolster biological relevance.
- Clarity: Clean up references; report hyperparameters and optimization details; justify thresholds (0.001, 0.08) and pruning criterion.

Questions for the authors
- How does performance and the SSA–SA discrepancy scale with network size and number of memories?
- Do spurious (non-trained) attractors emerge? How often?
- Can you achieve similar performance without optimizing v_inh^μ explicitly?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of activation (e.g., different power-laws) and ε-scaling?
- What is the computational cost, and can you exploit structure (e.g., block-Dale constraints) for scalability?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual novelty and a fresh methodological direction.
- Addresses long-standing gaps: Dale’s law, graded memories, balanced low-rate dynamics.
- Empirical demonstrations are compelling and connect to cortical phenomena (variability quenching).
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work at the ML–neuroscience–control interface.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited scale and lack of capacity/basin/spurious-attractor analysis.
- Nonlocal offline optimization; unclear path to biological or scalable learning.
- Auxiliary inhibitory variables per memory inflate parameter count.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction, with a bold and promising framework that outweighs the current empirical and theoretical incompleteness.

Confidence
High (familiar with attractor networks, balanced E/I dynamics, and control-based stability methods).
