### **Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"**

#### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing recurrent neural networks that can store and recall analog (graded-activity) memory patterns. The authors address several key limitations of previous models by explicitly incorporating biologically plausible constraints, including Dale's Law (separate excitatory and inhibitory populations), low firing rates without saturation, and dynamic E-I balance. The core contribution is to frame the problem of memory storage as a direct optimization problem from control theory. Specifically, they design a cost function that simultaneously enforces two conditions for a set of desired memory patterns: 1) that they are fixed points of the network dynamics (by minimizing the temporal derivative of activity), and 2) that these fixed points are dynamically stable. To overcome the non-differentiability of standard stability measures (the spectral abscissa), they leverage a recently developed, smooth proxy called the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA). By optimizing the synaptic weights and the activity of inhibitory neurons using gradient-based methods, they demonstrate that the resulting networks can robustly recall dozens of analog patterns. The trained networks exhibit emergent properties observed in biological circuits, such as sparse connectivity and a tight balance between excitatory and inhibitory currents.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel and powerful methodological approach. Framing memory storage as a robust control problem and using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa to directly optimize for dynamical stability is a significant departure from traditional Hebbian-based or statistical mechanics approaches. This provides a principled "proof of concept" that networks with multiple, complex biological constraints can indeed support stable, graded memories. This work opens a new and promising avenue for designing and understanding functional recurrent circuits, connecting theoretical neuroscience with modern optimization and control theory.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and the methodology is well-executed. The formulation of the cost function (Eq. 5) is clear and directly addresses the core objectives. The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the main claims. Key results, such as the robustness of recall to noise (Fig. 3), the emergent sparsity of weights (Fig. 4), the detailed E-I balance (Fig. 5), and the reduction of trial-to-trial variability (Fig. 6), are clearly presented and align well with both theoretical expectations and experimental observations in neuroscience.

3.  **Addresses a Key Problem in Neuroscience:** The paper tackles a long-standing and difficult problem: how can the brain store memories that are not binary "on/off" states, but rather patterns of graded neural activity? By successfully embedding analog patterns while respecting fundamental constraints like Dale's law and E-I balance, the work makes a substantial contribution to the theory of associative memory. The fact that the network achieves stability through dynamic inhibition rather than artificial neuron saturation is particularly noteworthy and biologically relevant.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of a Biologically Plausible Learning Mechanism:** The most significant weakness is that the network parameters are found using a global, offline optimization algorithm (L-BFGS) that requires full knowledge of the system's state and Jacobian. This demonstrates that a functional connectivity matrix *can exist*, but provides no insight into how such a configuration could be learned by a biological system through local, activity-dependent synaptic plasticity. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, but it remains a major gap between the model and biological reality. The work is thus more of a normative or engineering solution than a mechanistic model of learning.

2.  **Scalability and Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments are conducted on a network of 150 neurons. While this is sufficient for a proof of concept, questions remain about how the optimization procedure scales to much larger networks. The authors note that the SSA smoothness parameter ε must be scaled with network size, but a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and potential challenges in larger systems would be beneficial. Furthermore, several key hyperparameters (ηs, ηF) are set "heuristically." The paper would be stronger if it included a sensitivity analysis showing how the quality of the solution depends on these choices.

3.  **Imperfect Fixed Points:** The optimization does not drive the `dv/dt` term to exactly zero (Fig. 2), meaning the target patterns are technically "slow points" rather than true fixed points. The authors argue that a stable fixed point exists nearby, which seems plausible and is supported by the recall simulations. However, this raises questions about long-term drift. A more formal analysis of the distance between the target pattern and the actual learned fixed point, and how this distance scales with network load (number of memories), would strengthen the claims.

#### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the methods are explained in sufficient detail for an expert to understand, and the figures are illustrative. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the limitations of prior work.
*   **Relation to Previous Work:** The paper does a good job of situating its contributions within the existing literature on associative memory networks.
*   **Sparsity:** The emergence of sparse connectivity (Fig. 4) from an L2 regularizer is an interesting result. The authors attribute this to the stability optimization itself. Could they elaborate on the intuition for why minimizing the SSA would promote sparsity? Is it because a dense network with many small weights is more likely to have complex, unstable eigenmodes?
*   **Capacity:** The paper demonstrates storage of 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network (capacity α = m/nE = 0.3 if nE=100). How does recall performance degrade as the number of stored memories increases? An analysis of the network's storage capacity would be a valuable addition.

#### **Overall Evaluation and Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, powerful, and significant contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and machine learning. The application of robust control theory to design biologically constrained memory networks is highly original and yields impressive results that connect with known biological phenomena. While the reliance on a global optimization algorithm limits its direct biological plausibility as a model of *learning*, it provides an invaluable proof of concept and a new theoretical framework for understanding the *function* of such circuits. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance far outweigh the weaknesses.

**I recommend accepting this paper.**
