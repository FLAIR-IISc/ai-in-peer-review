- Proposes a novel framework to store analog memories in a balanced rate-based E-I network that obeys Dale’s law.  
- Formulates memory storage as a control problem and uses the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization.  
- Combines biologically realistic constraints (Dale’s law, graded firing rates, recurrent/sparse connectivity) with robust control methods.  
- Demonstrates successful storage and recall of analog memories, robustness to noise, and tight E-I balance in simulations.  
- Provides strong empirical results and compares performance to an “ideal observer” benchmark.  
- Clear and thorough presentation with helpful figures (e.g., Fig. 1).  
- Explores synaptic sparseness and shows resilience to removal of weak synapses.  
- Builds appropriately on prior work, leveraging recent advances in robust control theory.  
- Computational cost of the BFGS-based optimization is high, raising scalability concerns.  
- Many parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε, initial conditions) appear heuristic and lack systematic justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Rate-based model omits spiking temporal dynamics; robustness of learned connectivity in spiking networks is unclear.  
- Limited exploration of memory capacity and scaling relationships (30 memories in 150 neurons shown).  
- No validation against real neural data, leaving biological relevance unverified.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, requesting discussion of optimization complexity, parameter justification/sensitivity, spiking translation, and acknowledgment of need for empirical validation.
