Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog memories as stable attractors in a recurrent rate-based network that obeys key biological constraints: separate excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) populations with Dale’s law, recurrent connectivity, low firing rates without saturating single-neuron nonlinearities, and dynamically balanced E/I inputs. The core idea is to optimize synaptic weights (via a sign-constrained softplus reparameterization) and the inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory so that (i) the desired activity patterns are (approximately) fixed points and (ii) these points are locally asymptotically stable. Because the spectral abscissa (SA) is non-smooth, the authors use the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to produce a differentiable surrogate objective, minimized with (L-)BFGS. They demonstrate recall robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, E/I balance across attractors, and emergent sparsity of effective synapses.

This work connects to classic attractor memory models (Hopfield and graded-response extensions) but addresses several shortcomings: adherence to Dale’s law, explicit inhibitory neurons with dynamics rather than instantaneous feedback, graded (log-normal) patterns rather than effectively binary coding, and low-rate operation without saturating input-output curves. It also relates to balanced networks and clustered connectivity models, as well as to prior uses of SSA for stabilizing dynamics.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Casting memory embedding as a joint fixed-point and stability optimization problem, with robust control machinery (SSA) to directly shape local stability, is elegant and broadly useful.
- Biological plausibility: Respect for Dale’s law, low-rate regimes, dynamic E/I balance, and graded firing rate targets is compelling for computational neuroscience.
- Robustness: The network recalls from highly noisy initializations, is resilient to ongoing noise, and shows stimulus-evoked variability quenching consistent with in vivo observations.
- Clear empirical methodology: Sensible normalization of recall error, comparison to an “ideal observer” upper bound, and analyses of E/I currents and emergent weight sparsity.
- Practical insight: Weak synapses can be pruned with limited performance loss, hinting at robustness and potential for sparse implementations.

Weaknesses and questions
- Capacity and scaling: Results are shown for n=150 with m=30 (capacity ~0.2). There is no systematic scaling analysis (e.g., m vs n, basin sizes vs m, robustness vs N), nor a theoretical capacity characterization. This limits claims about generality and competitiveness with prior high-capacity models.
- Degrees of freedom in training: The inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ are optimized per-memory alongside synapses. This increases the number of free parameters by n_I·m and may inflate storage ability. While biologically plausible (inhibitory neurons participate in attractors), it would help to quantify how much capacity and robustness depend on allowing v_inh^μ to vary freely versus being determined by W alone.
- Fixed-point approximation: The dv/dt residuals are nonzero post-training; stability metrics (SSA/SA) are computed at the target points rather than the actual fixed points. Although simulations suggest nearby true fixed points exist, a more direct validation (e.g., locating the fixed point and reporting its distance to the target, or verifying SA at the converged point) would strengthen the claim.
- Sensitivity and reproducibility: Hyperparameters (η_s, η_F, ε) are set heuristically; convergence properties, sensitivity analyses, and computational cost are not reported. Given SSA’s ε–smoothness tradeoff and scaling rule, robustness of outcomes to these choices should be documented. Public code would greatly aid reproducibility.
- Comparisons: The main baseline is the ideal observer; comparisons to alternative network constructions that enforce E/I separation or stabilize graded patterns (e.g., clustered balanced networks, spatial bump models without spatial structure assumptions) would better position the contribution.
- Biological readouts: Claims of “tight balance” are mostly qualitative; quantifying balance indices, correlation coefficients across attractors, and scaling with network size would make the physiological interpretation more convincing.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall. The SSA-based objective directly targets stability and is appropriate. Claims are supported by simulations showing robust recall and dynamics consistent with E/I balance and variability reduction. The main technical gaps are the absence of scaling/capacity analysis, limited sensitivity studies, and the fixed-point approximation caveat.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The methodological pipeline (objective, reparameterization, optimization) is clear. Some key choices (hyperparameters, ε scaling, initializations) are heuristic; more guidance and/or supplementary details and code would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Applying robust control tools (SSA) to build analog attractor memories in E/I networks obeying Dale’s law is novel and nontrivial. The combination of constraints and objectives distinguishes the work from classic Hopfield-style models and from prior balanced network constructions.
- Significance: The framework is promising for constructing biologically grounded attractor networks with graded activity, a long-standing challenge. If shown to scale and generalize (including to spiking implementations), it could influence both theory and practical design of stable recurrent systems. Current empirical scope (single network size, modest capacity) tempers immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide scaling experiments (vary n, m, report success rates, basin sizes, training time).
- Quantify the effect of optimizing v_inh^μ: compare against training where v_inh^μ are not free parameters, or regularize their deviation from baseline.
- Locate and analyze the actual fixed point reached after training/simulation; report SA/SSA there and its distance from the target.
- Add sensitivity analyses for ε, η_s, η_F and initialization; report optimization stability and computational cost.
- Include comparisons with alternative E/I models targeting graded memories, and discuss pros/cons.
- Release code or detailed pseudo-code and parameter settings for reproduction.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled use of robust stability optimization to embed analog memories under biological constraints.
- Clear empirical demonstrations of robust recall, E/I balance, variability quenching, and emergent sparsity.
- Potentially general framework with implications beyond this specific model.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited capacity and lack of scaling/theoretical analysis.
- Additional degrees of freedom via per-memory inhibitory activities complicate interpretation of memory storage.
- Fixed-point approximation and sensitivity to hyperparameters not fully addressed.
- Comparisons to alternative network approaches are sparse.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a novel and promising framework with solid initial results and clear relevance to computational neuroscience. Strengthening the scaling analysis, fixed-point validation, and sensitivity/comparison studies would make it a strong accept.
