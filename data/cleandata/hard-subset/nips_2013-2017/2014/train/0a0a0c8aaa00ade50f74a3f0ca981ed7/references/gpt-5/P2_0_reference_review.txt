Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic method to construct recurrent rate networks with separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations that store multiple analog (graded) attractor memories. The network obeys Dale’s law via a reparameterization of synaptic signs, uses a rectified-quadratic gain without saturation, and achieves stability via dynamic E/I balance rather than saturating nonlinearities. The core technical contribution is an optimization objective that (i) makes the desired patterns approximate fixed points by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at each target, and (ii) enforces local stability by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at those states. The authors show robust autoassociative recall in the presence of noise, emergent sparsity of synaptic weights, tight E/I balance across attractors, and stimulus-induced variability quenching. Results are demonstrated in networks of n=150 with m=30 memories (capacity ≈0.2), and compared to an “ideal observer.”

This sits between classic Hopfield-style models (binary or effectively binary memories, no I population, symmetry constraints) and more biophysically grounded E/I networks (often relying on saturation, global inhibition, or spatial structure). It leverages robust control methods (SSA) previously used for transient control in balanced networks, extending them to the storage of multiple graded attractors under Dale’s law. Related work on clustered balanced networks and graded bump attractors is discussed; here, memories are non-spatial and non-binary, learned via optimization rather than hard-wired structure.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent: Dale’s law is enforced by parametrization; fixed-point proximity is handled by minimizing dv/dt; local stability is addressed via SSA, a smooth upper bound on the spectral abscissa with computable gradients. The demonstration of recall, E/I balance, sparsity, and variability quenching is consistent with the design. However:
  - Fixed points are not enforced exactly; dv/dt remains small but non-zero at targets. The claim that “true fixed points exist nearby” is plausible given negative SSA, but not proven or quantified (e.g., by finding the actual fixed point via a solver and reporting distances/basin sizes).
  - The inhibitory steady-state activities for each attractor are treated as free optimization variables. While justified as auxiliary variables that the network can realize, this increases the degrees of freedom considerably (nI m parameters) and risks “cheating” unless the solutions are shown to satisfy the fixed-point equations tightly. The small-but-nonzero dv/dt makes this a central concern: how close are the optimized inhibitory rates to those achieved by the learned dynamics?
  - There is no analysis of capacity scaling with network size, distributional statistics, or SSA ε, and no theoretical guarantees on stability margins or basin sizes. Results are mostly for n=150 and a single choice of pattern statistics.
  - Computational practicality is unclear: optimizing SSA across m patterns requires repeated Jacobian spectral calculations; complexity and scaling are not discussed.
- Empirics: The demonstrations are careful and relevant (recall vs. cue noise; comparison to an ideal observer; pruning weak synapses; E/I balance; variability quenching with ongoing noise). But additional quantitative diagnostics would strengthen claims: retrieval times, success as a function of m/n, basin radii, and sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, and the method is clearly explained. The role of SSA is laid out, and the tradeoff introduced by ε is discussed.
- Some implementation details are relegated to a table/supplement (e.g., exact parameters, mean-field initialization). For reproducibility, more explicit reporting of nE/nI, time constants, external inputs h, and code availability would help.
- Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted; please fix.

Originality
- Novel combination: Bringing robust control (SSA minimization) to the storage of multiple graded attractors in Dale-constrained E/I networks with non-saturating gain is original and timely. Prior graded memory/storage models typically avoid explicit I populations or rely on saturation/global inhibition; spatial bumps assume strong structure. This paper enables arbitrary analog patterns without saturation and with local E/I balance.
- The idea of optimizing inhibitory patterns per attractor is less standard; whether this is an acceptable modeling freedom or an unrealistic degree of freedom depends on how tightly the fixed-point conditions are met.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the approach offers a new, principled way to synthesize E/I circuits with multiple analog attractors that are dynamically stabilized by balance. This is relevant for theoretical neuroscience and could inform machine-learning models that need structured attractor dynamics.
- The current scope (n=150, capacity ~0.2, offline heavy optimization) limits immediate impact. Lack of scaling analysis, absence of spiking validation, and reliance on global optimization over per-attractor inhibitory variables weaken the broader significance.

Strengths
- Satisfies key biological constraints (Dale’s law, E/I balance, low rates, no saturation, sparse recurrence).
- Clear control-theoretic formulation; appropriate use of SSA with gradients.
- Empirical demonstrations of recall robustness, emergent sparsity, and variability quenching.
- Comparison to an ideal observer establishes an upper bound and shows competitive performance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fixed-point enforcement: quantify the distance from targets to actual fixed points found by solving dv/dt=0; report basin sizes and retrieval times.
- Degrees of freedom: justify optimization over {vinhμ} by showing the learned network indeed reaches those inhibitory states; report residuals in dv/dt per neuron.
- Capacity and scaling: provide m/n curves, sensitivity to ε and regularization, and computational cost scaling.
- Comparisons: include baselines with global inhibition or saturating gains to highlight advantages.
- Spiking validation: demonstrate a spiking instantiation with matched f-I curves, even on a smaller network.
- Reproducibility: provide full parameter details and, ideally, code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and well-motivated application of robust control to realistic analog memory storage under Dale’s law.
- Empirical evidence of robust recall and balanced dynamics without saturating nonlinearities.
- Potentially influential for designing structured dynamics in neuroscience and ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key aspects unquantified (exact fixed points, basins, capacity scaling, computational complexity).
- Possible over-reliance on per-attractor inhibitory degrees of freedom and nonzero dv/dt at targets.
- Limited scale and absence of spiking validation reduce generalizability.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is strong and the approach is promising, but the current empirical and theoretical support is not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. Addressing fixed-point exactness, scaling/capacity, and either a spiking validation or stronger comparative baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
