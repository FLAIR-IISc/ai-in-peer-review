Summary
This paper proposes an optimization-based framework to store multiple analog (graded) attractors in a recurrent rate network that respects Dale’s law, exhibits dynamic E/I balance, and avoids high-rate saturation. Neurons have rectified quadratic transfer functions; the connectivity is parameterized to enforce sign constraints via a softplus reparameterization. The authors pose storage as a joint fixed-point and local stability optimization problem: they minimize the deviation of dv/dt at target patterns and use the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to promote asymptotic stability. Inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory are treated as free auxiliary variables during optimization. The method yields networks that recall target patterns from noisy cues at low firing rates, show E/I balance, apparent sparsity of effective synapses, and variability quenching under ongoing noise. Results are demonstrated in a 150-neuron network storing 30 patterns (capacity ~0.2), with comparisons to an “ideal observer.”

Relation to prior work
The approach is cast as overcoming constraints in Hopfield-like models (symmetry, binary patterns), LIF-based models that rely on saturation or global inhibition, and spatial bump attractors. Use of robust control tools (SSA) for network stability follows recent work (e.g., Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al. on movement generation), but applied here to analog memory storage with Dale’s law. The paper positions itself against theoretical capacity claims for analog patterns and “global inhibition” models, but does not provide systematic capacity scaling.

Strengths
- Formulates memory storage as a control problem that enforces both fixed-point and stability constraints with Dale’s law.
- Elegant use of SSA for differentiable stability optimization; practical smoothing heuristic.
- Demonstrates graded attractors with low-rate recall and robust E/I balance without saturating nonlinearities.
- Empirical recall quality close to an ideal observer across noise levels for the given setup.
- Interesting observation that effective sparsity emerges without explicit L1 penalty; weak synapse pruning largely preserves performance.
- Variability quenching under ongoing noise mirrors cortical phenomenology.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major technical/conceptual:
- Auxiliary inhibitory steady-states per memory: Treating v_inh^μ as free parameters substantially relaxes the problem (n_I m additional degrees of freedom). Biologically, during recall the inhibitory activities must be determined by network dynamics, not memory-specific “setpoints” chosen by an optimizer. This choice risks overfitting and undermines claims about implementable storage. It also inflates apparent capacity.
- Fixed points not actually satisfied: After training, dv/dt at target states is “small but non-zero,” yet stability is assessed via SSA/SA on the Jacobian computed at those non-fixed points. Negative SSA there does not guarantee existence or stability of a true nearby fixed point. The paper asserts nearby fixed points “in practice” but provides no proof, continuation/root-finding verification, or contraction argument.
- Capacity and scaling: Only one network size and one m are shown. No capacity-vs-n curves, no scaling with m/n, no effect of SSA ε, no ablations (η_s, η_F), no runtime/complexity analysis. Calling 0.2 a “storage capacity” without such analysis is premature.
- Basin geometry and spurious attractors: No quantitative analysis of basins, robustness margins, or spurious fixed points. Failures at high noise suggest interference but this is not characterized.

Modeling/biological plausibility:
- The rectified quadratic nonlinearity lacks saturation; stability depends on precise balance. No exploration of robustness to neuron model choice or mapping to spiking (despite claims).
- “Sparsity” is defined via an arbitrary |W|<0.01 threshold; no units or sensitivity analysis. L2 regularization does not promote sparsity; the claim that sparsity “must be” due to stability optimization needs quantitative support.
- External inputs hi are constant but not specified; choice may be critical to baseline stability.

Methodological clarity/reproducibility:
- SSA smoothing parameter ε chosen by a heuristic (0.01·150/n); no sensitivity or justification beyond a brief remark.
- Training uses L-BFGS on a highly nonconvex problem; no convergence diagnostics, initialization sensitivity, or multiple seeds.
- Evaluation thresholds are ad hoc (recall success at 0.001; later increased to 0.08 after pruning due to attractor shifts), which makes comparisons fragile.
- Figures and referenced Table 1/Supplementary are not present; many details (n_E/n_I split, τ_i, hi values, step sizes, stopping criteria) are missing.

Presentation and minor issues:
- Several typographical/formatting issues (e.g., figure panel headers, “Hz m em or ie s”), ambiguous notation for positive/negative parts in defining h_exc/h_inh, and inconsistent units.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted; many entries lack years/volumes/pages.

Quality
Technically interesting but currently undermined by the auxiliary inhibitory variables, lack of verified fixed points, limited evaluation, and insufficient analysis of scaling/basins.

Clarity
Readable but significant missing details for reproduction and several notational/formatting issues.

Originality
Combining SSA-based stability optimization with Dale’s law for analog attractors is novel. However, the biological viability is weakened by the auxiliary inhibitory degrees of freedom.

Significance
Potentially impactful if the approach can be made implementable and scalable with verified fixed points and realistic constraints. As is, significance is limited by the above concerns.

Questions and requested revisions
- Remove v_inh^μ as free variables, or justify biologically how inhibition could realize memory-specific steady states; alternatively, optimize only W and hi and verify that the learned network dynamics produce the inhibitory steady states.
- Verify that actual fixed points exist: perform root-finding/continuation from v^μ, report ||dv/dt|| and SA at the true fixed points, and quantify their basins.
- Provide capacity scaling with n and m, sensitivity to ε, η_s, η_F, initializations, and multiple seeds; report computational cost.
- Characterize spurious attractors and basins; provide robustness margins to ongoing noise across amplitudes.
- Justify the sparsity claim with thresholds, units, and sensitivity; avoid changing success criteria midstream.
- Clarify all missing hyperparameters and provide code or detailed pseudo-code; fix formatting and references.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a principled stability tool.
- Achieves graded attractors with Dale’s law, balanced regime, and encouraging recall.

Cons
- Core design choice (v_inh^μ as free parameters) undermines plausibility.
- Fixed points not verified; stability claims rely on Jacobians at non-fixed states.
- Limited experimental scope and missing reproducibility details.
- Presentation/reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the current version has substantial methodological and clarity shortcomings that preclude acceptance. I encourage a revision addressing the above points. Confidence: high.
