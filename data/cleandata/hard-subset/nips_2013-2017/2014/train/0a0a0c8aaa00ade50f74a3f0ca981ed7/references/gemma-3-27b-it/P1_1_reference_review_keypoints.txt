- Proposes a novel framework framing analog memory storage as a control problem, using fixed-point stability constraints.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to optimize synaptic weights and inhibitory firing rates.  
- Enforces biological constraints (Dale’s law, realistic firing rates) and models excitatory-inhibitory (E-I) dynamics.  
- Demonstrates robust analog memory recall performance, including resilience to noisy inputs.  
- Networks operate in a dynamically balanced E-I regime and show reduced trial-to-trial variability during recall.  
- Provides detailed analysis of learned connectivity, including sparsity patterns.  
- Situates work well within prior literature, addressing limitations of Hopfield models and recent graded-memory approaches.  
- The application of SSA is a novel and well-justified connection to control theory.  
- Model moves beyond binary or saturating graded representations toward more biologically plausible memories.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and explains complex methodology effectively.  
- SSA optimization may be computationally expensive, raising scalability concerns.  
- Key hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) are set heuristically without thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Experiments focus on a 150-neuron network, leaving scalability to larger networks uncertain.  
- Rate-based model omits spiking dynamics; extensions to spiking networks are not explored.  
- No comparison to alternative stability methods (e.g., Lyapunov-based approaches) is provided.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, uses a novel control-theory approach, shows strong empirical results, and has potential high impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, heuristic parameter choices, and lack of scaling analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong, innovative contribution; recommended for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept; Confidence: 8/10.
