- Proposes a control-theoretic method to design recurrent networks that store and recall analog (graded) memories as stable fixed points.  
- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to enforce robust stability while respecting Dale’s law and realistic rate dynamics.  
- Demonstrates storage and recall of multiple analog memories with robustness to input and dynamical noise.  
- Positions work relative to Hopfield and later approaches, arguing prior models fail to represent graded memories without saturating dynamics or global inhibition.  
- Connects and contributes to literature on balanced E-I networks and recent robust control applications.  
- Novel application of SSA to neural network parameter optimization, offering formal stability guarantees.  
- Incorporates biologically plausible constraints (Dale’s law, E-I balance), increasing relevance to neuroscience.  
- Clear presentation and helpful figures that explain motivation, methods, and results.  
- Comprehensive evaluations including synaptic weight distributions, role of weak synapses, and E-I balance dynamics.  
- Computational cost of SSA optimization is likely high and not thoroughly quantified, raising scalability concerns.  
- Parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε) are heuristic and lack systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Does not demonstrate how optimized parameters could be learned via local synaptic plasticity rules.  
- Rate-based model simplification may miss important spiking dynamics despite suggested bridging paths.  
- Lacks direct empirical comparison to other modern optimization/architecture approaches (e.g., energy-based models, reservoir computing).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, biologically plausible, robust, and thoroughly evaluated.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and absence of learning-rule implementation.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution that substantially advances recurrent memory modeling; merits acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommended revisions: discuss SSA computational cost and scaling, explore parameter sensitivity, propose local learning mechanisms, address rate-model limitations vs. spiking, and add comparisons to recent alternatives.
