## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for constructing analog memory networks using a balanced rate-based model of excitatory and inhibitory (E-I) neurons. The authors address limitations of traditional memory models (e.g., Hopfield networks) by incorporating Dale’s law, recurrent/sparse connectivity, graded activity levels, biologically plausible firing rates, and dynamic E-I balance. They formulate memory storage as a control problem, optimizing network parameters (connectivity and inhibitory neuron activity) to create stable fixed points corresponding to desired memory patterns, using a smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization.  The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories, robustness to noise, and a tight dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the existing literature on memory models. It correctly identifies shortcomings of early Hopfield networks (binary states, symmetry constraints) and subsequent attempts to address them (e.g., [3, 7, 8]).  It builds upon recent advances in robust control theory [13, 14] to tackle the stability problem, a key bottleneck in previous approaches. The novelty lies in the combination of these elements – a biologically realistic E-I network, analog memory patterns, and robust stability optimization – to create a system that addresses multiple limitations simultaneously. The references provided are relevant and demonstrate awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a core problem in neural network models of memory:** Achieving stable storage and recall of graded, analog memories in a biologically plausible setting has been a long-standing challenge. This work offers a promising approach.
* **Novel framework:**  Formulating memory storage as a control problem and using the SSA for robust stability is a clever and innovative idea.
* **Biologically realistic constraints:** The adherence to Dale’s law and the use of a rate-based model with realistic firing rates are important steps towards building more biologically plausible models.
* **Strong empirical results:** The simulations demonstrate successful memory storage and recall, robustness to noise, and a balanced E-I state. The comparison to an “ideal observer” provides a valuable benchmark.
* **Clear and thorough presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the complex methodology in a comprehensible manner. The inclusion of figures (especially Fig. 1) is helpful.
* **Exploration of synaptic sparseness:** Investigating the role of weak synapses and demonstrating resilience to their removal adds depth to the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational cost:** The optimization process (using BFGS) is computationally expensive, potentially limiting scalability to larger networks. While acknowledged implicitly, this is a significant practical concern.
* **Heuristic parameter choices:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε, initial conditions) appears largely heuristic. More systematic exploration of parameter space and justification for the chosen values would strengthen the work.
* **Rate-based model limitation:**  While the rate-based approach simplifies analysis, it loses the temporal dynamics inherent in spiking neurons.  The link to spiking networks, while discussed, could be more thoroughly explored.  How robust are the connectivity patterns found here when implemented in a spiking network?
* **Limited exploration of capacity:** The paper demonstrates successful storage of 30 memories in a network of 150 neurons (20% capacity). Further investigation into the capacity limits of this approach is needed.  Do scaling relationships emerge as network size increases?
* **Lack of validation with real neural data:** While aiming for biological realism, the model is not directly validated against experimental data.  Testing the predictions of this model against electrophysiological or imaging data would be a crucial next step.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in computational neuroscience with a novel and technically sound approach.
* The results are compelling and demonstrate the feasibility of storing and recalling analog memories in a biologically plausible setting.
* The work has the potential to advance our understanding of memory mechanisms and inspire new approaches to neural network design.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the optimization process could limit the scalability of the approach.
* The reliance on heuristic parameter choices weakens the robustness of the findings.
* The lack of validation with real neural data leaves open the question of biological relevance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. While the computational cost and heuristic parameter choices are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the compelling results, and the clarity of the presentation outweigh these concerns. The work represents a significant step forward in the development of biologically plausible memory models.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity of the optimization process more explicitly and explore potential methods for improving scalability.
2.  Provide more justification for the choice of parameters and potentially conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results.
3.  Elaborate on the potential for translating the learned connectivity patterns to spiking networks.
4.  Acknowledge in the discussion section the need for validation with real neural data in future work.
