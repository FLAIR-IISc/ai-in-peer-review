Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog attractor memories in a recurrent, rate-based E–I network that respects Dale’s law. Neurons follow rectified-quadratic transfer functions; dynamics are dv/dt = −v + W g(v) + h. Storage is formulated as (i) approximate fixed-point constraints (minimizing ||dv/dt|| at each target pattern) and (ii) local stability via minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at those targets. Dale’s law is enforced by a softplus reparameterization ensuring fixed presynaptic sign. Inhibitory steady-state potentials per memory (vinhμ) are treated as free “auxiliary” variables during optimization. Using LBFGS to minimize a weighted sum of the fixed-point residual, SSA, and an L2 penalty on W, the authors demonstrate recall of 30 graded patterns in a 150-neuron network, robustness to initial cue noise and ongoing fluctuations, dynamic E/I balance, and an apparently sparse weight distribution with many near-zero synapses.

Relation to prior work
The work situates itself against Hopfield-style networks (binary memories, symmetric weights), graded-response models with high theoretical capacities but impractical dynamics, and E–I LIF networks relying on saturation or global inhibition. It leverages robust control (SSA) to stabilize graded attractors—an interesting connection. Related neuroscience models of balanced supralinear networks are cited. However, comparisons to modern E–I attractor formulations with structured inhibition and graded activity (beyond the cited works) are limited, and capacity/scaling comparisons remain anecdotal.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of memory storage as a multi-objective optimization problem (fixed point + local stability) under Dale’s law.
- Use of SSA is principled, differentiable, and computationally tractable; the control-theoretic framing is novel for this application.
- Empirical demonstrations: noise-robust recall, dynamic E/I balance, variability quenching after cue—phenomena consonant with cortical data.
- Simple, interpretable neuron model (rectified power law) with biologically plausible parameterization.
- Observations that performance approaches a nearest-neighbor “ideal observer” on the prescribed cue corruption task.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Methodology/assumptions
- Free inhibitory variables per memory. Treating vinhμ as free parameters adds nI×m memory-specific degrees of freedom that are not encoded in W or h, effectively allowing pattern-specific inhibitory set points during training. This dramatically increases the parameter count and may inflate apparent capacity or stability. The authors assert that dynamics reproduce these inhibitory states, but no constraint ensures consistency, nor is there an ablation showing performance without these auxiliary variables. This is a major concern for biological plausibility and fair capacity comparison.
- Fixed points versus “slow points.” Minimizing ||dv/dt|| rather than enforcing dv/dt=0 yields non-zero residuals; the claim that “an exact, stable fixed point had in fact been created very near each target” is not substantiated (no root-finding or Poincaré analysis, no demonstration that the nearby fixed point has the same code). This weakens the central claim of stable attractors.
- Capacity and scaling. Only one configuration (n=150, m=30; capacity ≈0.2) is shown, with no systematic study of scaling with N, m/N, or robustness margins. Without scaling laws or bounds (empirical or theoretical), significance is limited.
- Sparsity claim. Although the weight histogram is heavy-tailed, the connectivity remains dense; sparsity is defined post hoc via an arbitrary magnitude threshold (|W|<0.01). This does not satisfy the stated goal of “sparse recurrent connections.” An L1 or explicit structural sparsity constraint and reporting of actual density would be more convincing.
- Stability assessment. SSA is a local measure. There is no analysis of basin sizes, multistability interactions, or global behavior (e.g., via Lyapunov constructions). The heuristic ε scaling (ε=0.01·150/n) is not justified beyond anecdote; gradients of SSA can be ill-conditioned—no diagnostics are reported.
- Comparators. No comparison to learnable E–I attractor baselines (e.g., balanced attractor networks with structured inhibition, stabilized supralinear motifs with tuned feedback) on identical tasks. The “ideal observer” is a nearest-neighbor upper bound on cue disambiguation, not a dynamical baseline.
- Hyperparameters and reproducibility. Key choices (ηs, ηF, ε, τi, h, initial weight statistics) are deferred to Table 1/Supplement; these are missing here, hindering reproducibility. Optimization details (stopping criteria, gradient checks, runtime/complexity) are absent.
- Biological realism. External inputs h are constant and unspecified; inhibitory specificity per memory during training undermines the claim of biologically grounded stabilization; no structural sparsity; no heterogeneity in τi or noise statistics beyond a single amplitude; no discussion of learned E→I/I→E motifs.

Experimental evaluation
- Performance metrics. Success is defined by an extremely tight distance threshold (0.001), then relaxed to 0.08 after clipping. This inconsistency complicates interpretation.
- Ongoing noise. Noise amplitude is specified indirectly (“std 0.33 in an isolated neuron”), units unclear, with no sweep over noise levels.
- Weight clipping. The threshold (0.01) is arbitrary; removing many small weights slightly shifts attractors—more systematic pruning curves and stability margins would be helpful.

Clarity and presentation
- Missing tables/figures/supplementary. Table 1 and figure specifics are referenced but not available here.
- Notational issues: ambiguous use of brackets in the definition of hexci and hinhi; bWijc+ notation is undefined; some equations have inconsistent normalization; minor typographical errors.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (years/pages missing).

Quality: The approach is technically interesting but currently under-supported by experiments and ablations. Key modeling choices (free inhibitory per memory, non-zero fixed-point residuals) weaken the main claims.

Clarity: Generally understandable, but missing details and notation/formatting issues impede reproducibility.

Originality: The use of SSA to train E–I graded attractors under Dale’s law is novel.

Significance: Potentially impactful, but without scaling analysis, principled sparsity, and stronger validation, practical significance is limited.

Pros
- Novel control-theoretic training objective (SSA) for E–I attractor networks
- Robust recall with graded patterns and balanced E/I dynamics
- Potentially general framework extendable to spiking models

Cons
- Free inhibitory variables per memory undermine plausibility and capacity claims
- No capacity/scaling analysis; limited experiments
- Dense connectivity; sparsity claims based on arbitrary thresholds
- Fixed-point conditions not enforced; lack of proof of nearby true attractors
- Reproducibility gaps; inconsistent metrics; missing details/figures

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission after substantial revision)

Suggestions for improvement
- Remove or strongly justify vinhμ as free parameters; alternatively, constrain them via the dynamics or a learned inhibitory subspace shared across memories.
- Enforce dv/dt=0 exactly at targets (e.g., via constrained optimization or root-finding inner loops) and verify existence/location of true fixed points.
- Provide capacity and scaling studies (vary N, m, E/I ratios), basin-size analyses, and noise robustness curves.
- Impose and report true structural sparsity; compare L1 vs L2 regularization; analyze learned motifs.
- Add baselines (e.g., stabilized supralinear networks with tuned inhibition) and ablations (SSA vs SA proxies; ε sensitivity).
- Include all hyperparameters, code/pseudocode, stopping criteria, and computational costs.
- Clean up notation, units, and references; define all symbols consistently.
