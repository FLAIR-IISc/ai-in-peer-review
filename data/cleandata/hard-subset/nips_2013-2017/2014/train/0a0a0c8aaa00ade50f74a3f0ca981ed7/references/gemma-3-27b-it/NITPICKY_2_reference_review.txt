This paper presents a control-theoretic framework for constructing balanced E-I networks capable of storing and recalling analog memories. The approach optimizes network parameters – connectivity and inhibitory neuron firing rates – to satisfy fixed-point and stability conditions for target activity patterns. While the core idea of applying robust control techniques to memory networks is intriguing and addresses limitations of previous models (binary memories, global inhibition, sensitivity to noise), the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in presentation, methodological detail, and justification of design choices.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the shortcomings of existing approaches. However, the phrasing is often overly assertive ("overcomes all of the above limitations"), which isn’t fully warranted by the presented results. The visual depiction of the analog patterns in Fig. 1 is acceptable but lacks quantitative information (e.g., distribution parameters). The claim of achieving "graded activities" requires more rigorous demonstration beyond visual inspection.

The Methods section is where the majority of concerns reside. The threshold-quadratic gain function (Eq. 1) is standard, but the choice of γ = 0.04 feels arbitrary and lacks justification. Similarly, the reparameterization of synaptic weights (Eq. 2) – while enforcing Dale's law – introduces a non-linearity and its impact on optimization isn’t fully explored. The justification for excluding autapses is weak. The discussion of runaway excitation is acknowledged, but the reliance on the optimization algorithm to “naturally seek stable solutions” is insufficient. A more detailed explanation of why the optimization *should* achieve stability is needed.

The formulation of the optimization problem (Eqs. 4 and 5) is complex. The rationale behind minimizing the squared deviation from the fixed-point condition rather than directly enforcing it is not clearly articulated. The use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) is a strength, but the choice of ε = 0.01 (and the scaling heuristic) appears ad hoc and lacks sensitivity analysis. The impact of different ε values on the results (and the trade-off between smoothness and accuracy) should be investigated. The L2 regularization term’s weight (ηF) is also chosen heuristically, and its effect on sparsity and stability isn’t discussed. The parameters in Table 1 are presented without clear explanation of their derivation or sensitivity.

The Results section is difficult to follow due to a lack of quantitative rigor. Figure 2 demonstrates stability optimization, but the presented curves offer limited insight. What specifically are the attractorspecific sub-objectives? What are the scales of the cost function components? The claim of “robustness to noise” (Fig. 3) is supported, but the normalization in Eq. 6 is complex and its interpretation isn’t clear. The comparison to the “ideal observer” is useful, but the observation about the baseline state requires more explanation. The sparsity analysis (Fig. 4) is interesting, but the clipping experiment and its conclusion regarding the necessity of weak synapses are not convincing. The performance decrease after clipping needs more detailed statistical analysis. The discussion of balanced state (Fig. 5) is qualitative and lacks quantitative measures of E/I balance. The observation that E and I inputs are correlated isn’t novel and requires further context. The discussion of trial-to-trial variability reduction (Fig. 6) is promising but requires a more rigorous statistical analysis of the variability reduction.

The Discussion section summarizes the work and suggests future directions. The acknowledgement of the rate-based formulation’s limitations and the need for linking to spiking networks is appropriate. The suggestion regarding inhibitory plasticity is insightful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of robust control theory to memory network design.
*   Addresses limitations of existing models (binary memories, global inhibition).
*   Demonstrates stable storage and recall of analog memories.
*   Potential for advancing understanding of memory and its neural substrate.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous methodological details are presented without sufficient justification or analysis (γ, β scaling, ε choice, ηF, initialization).
*   Results lack quantitative rigor and rely heavily on visual inspection.
*   Claims are often overly assertive and not fully supported by evidence.
*   The optimization procedure is complex and the impact of different parameters isn't investigated.
*   Writing could be significantly improved for clarity and precision.
*   The heuristics used throughout the parameter space are troubling.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper's numerous shortcomings in methodological detail, quantitative analysis, and justification of design choices outweigh its strengths. The lack of rigor and the reliance on ad hoc choices raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results. I recommend **rejection**. Significant revisions addressing the points raised above, including a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices and a more rigorous quantitative evaluation, would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper feels like a proof-of-concept with many details glossed over rather than a fully developed and carefully validated contribution.
