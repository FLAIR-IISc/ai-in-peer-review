- Presents a novel optimization-based method to build recurrent rate networks that store and recall analog memories while respecting Dale’s law and E-I balance.
- Formulates memory storage as a direct optimization of connectivity and inhibitory firing rates to create stable fixed (slow) points corresponding to target patterns.
- Introduces the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control to enforce stability and robustness to noise in the designed networks.
- Demonstrates successful recall and balanced E-I dynamics in a 150-neuron network, showing robustness to noisy cues and ongoing dynamics.
- Positions the work as advancing associative-memory models by addressing graded representations, realistic firing rates, and excitation-inhibition constraints.
- Builds on robust control and prior associative memory literature while differing from saturating dynamics and global inhibition approaches.
- Strength: tackles biological plausibility issues (Dale’s law, graded activity, realistic rates, E-I balance).
- Strength: proposes a principled, novel optimization framework for memory storage rather than ad-hoc tuning.
- Strength: shows robustness to input and dynamical noise in recall performance.
- Strength: demonstrates emergence and analysis of balanced excitation-inhibition across attractors.
- Strength: clear writing, well-organized presentation, and informative figures/equations.
- Weakness: optimization may be computationally expensive and scalability is not addressed.
- Weakness: choice of hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) appears heuristic without systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: experiments limited to a relatively small network (n=150), so scalability to larger networks is unclear.
- Weakness: reliance on rate-based models abstracts away spiking dynamics; link to spiking networks needs elaboration.
- Weakness: attractors are “near” fixed points (slow points) rather than exact fixed points, which warrants more discussion on implications.
- Qualitative scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses fundamental memory problems with a novel, theoretically sound approach and promising results.
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about scalability, heuristic parameters, and limited biological realism due to rate-based modeling.
- Overall recommendation: accept, with encouragement to address scalability and parameter-sensitivity concerns in future work.
