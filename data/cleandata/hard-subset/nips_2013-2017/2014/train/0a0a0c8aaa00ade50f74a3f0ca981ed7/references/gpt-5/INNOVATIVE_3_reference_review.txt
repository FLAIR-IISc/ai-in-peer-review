Summary
This paper proposes a principled, control-theoretic framework for embedding multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a biologically constrained rate network with explicit excitatory and inhibitory populations and Dale’s law. The core idea is to jointly optimize synaptic weights and the steady-state inhibitory activities associated with each attractor so that: (i) the desired patterns are (near) fixed points by minimizing the activity drift at those points; and (ii) those fixed points are locally stable by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable upper bound of the spectral abscissa from robust control. The networks exhibit balanced E/I currents, low-rate activity, sparse effective connectivity, robust recall from noisy cues (approaching an “ideal observer” bound), robustness to ongoing noise, and stimulus-driven variability quenching. The work positions itself against Hopfield-type models (binary or effectively binary), LIF-based models leveraging saturation, spatial bump attractors, and spike-timing models without inhibition, and emphasizes achieving graded memories with realistic E/I structure and dynamics without relying on rate saturation.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: Importing SSA-based robust stability optimization into memory network construction is a fresh and compelling idea that addresses a longstanding gap: stable analog attractors in strictly Dale-compliant, recurrent E/I circuits with low firing rates. This feels like a meaningful new direction beyond incremental tweaks to Hopfield-type formulations.
- Principled objective: The combination of drift minimization and SSA offers a clean way to jointly enforce fixed-point proximity and local stability, sidestepping non-differentiability issues of the spectral abscissa.
- Biological realism: Dale’s law, recurrent E/I architecture, supralinear (rectified-quadratic) gain, balanced currents, low rates, and heavy-tailed firing distributions align well with cortical data.
- Empirical demonstrations: 
  - Robust recall vs. noise, with performance close to an ideal observer baseline.
  - Ongoing noise robustness and reduction of across-trial variability after cue onset, echoing widely reported cortical phenomena.
  - Emergent sparsity of effective weights (few strong synapses suffice), consistent with cortical measurements.
- Clarity of goals and limitations: The paper transparently notes that target states become “slow points” with small residual drift, and argues that nearby true fixed points emerge in practice.

Weaknesses and points to improve
- Capacity and scaling: Results are shown for n=150 and m=30 (capacity ≈ 0.2 per neuron or 0.3 per excitatory neuron), but there is no systematic capacity analysis, scaling with n, dependence on pattern statistics, or tradeoffs with the SSA smoothness parameter. This is a major missing piece for a memory model.
- Role of inhibitory auxiliary variables: Inhibitory steady-state activities are optimized per attractor. While mathematically convenient, the biological constraints on inhibitory rates are not enforced explicitly (e.g., via penalties), and it is not fully demonstrated that the learned v_inh for each attractor corresponds to the steady state implied by the final W and fixed external inputs. A stronger check of consistency would help.
- Ablations and sensitivity: No ablations are shown to establish the necessity of the SSA term (vs. drift-only optimization), the role of ε, ηs, ηF, or the initializations. How robust is training to these choices?
- Basins of attraction: Only local stability is optimized. Basin sizes are probed implicitly via recall under noise, but a more systematic analysis would be valuable (e.g., overlap curves, energy-like Lyapunov function if any, or empirical basin maps).
- Comparisons: While prior work is cited, there are no head-to-head comparisons with E/I balanced memory networks or clustered networks on the same tasks, nor with recent graded-memory approaches. Even one such comparison would strengthen claims.
- Practical details: Training time, computational cost, gradient evaluation details, and reproducibility specifics (hyperparameters in the main text) are sparse.

Quality
Technically sound and careful overall, with a clear optimization objective and meaningful empirical checks. The reliance on SSA is appropriate for differentiability, and the results are convincing at the demonstrated scale. However, the absence of capacity/scaling analysis and ablations leaves open key questions about generality and robustness.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Figures communicate the main points. Some aspects (SSA scaling with n, explicit training hyperparameters, constraints on inhibitory rates) would benefit from additional detail in the main text.

Originality
High. The use of SSA for constructing analog attractor networks under strict biological constraints is a distinctive contribution and likely to catalyze new work at the interface of control theory and neural memory models.

Significance
Promising and impactful. If the approach scales and can be adapted to spiking networks (as argued), it could reshape how we construct and analyze biologically grounded memory circuits. Even as a rate-model proof-of-concept, it opens a useful design methodology.

Questions for the authors
1) Capacity and scaling: How does storage capacity scale with n and with the statistics of patterns? Can you provide curves for m/nE across sizes?  
2) Consistency of inhibitory states: After training, if you initialize the network at the learned excitatory pattern but with arbitrary inhibitory states, do dynamics converge to the same inhibitory steady state v_inh^μ used by the optimizer?  
3) SSA ablation: What happens if you minimize drift-only, or vary ε by an order of magnitude?  
4) Constraints on inhibitory rates: Do rates ever become unrealistically large during optimization? Would adding penalties on inhibitory rates change performance or balance?  
5) Basins: Can you quantify basin sizes (e.g., overlap or noise robustness thresholds) across attractors?  
6) Comparisons: Can you compare to a balanced memory network or clustered network baseline on the same analog recall task?  
7) Spiking validation: With the learned W, how does a corresponding LIF network perform?

Pros
- Conceptually novel and ambitious; clear advance in constructing analog memories in biologically constrained networks.  
- Strong empirical demonstrations: recall robustness, balance, variability quenching, emergent sparsity.  
- Methodologically clean and likely reusable by others.

Cons
- No capacity/scaling analysis; limited ablations.  
- Auxiliary inhibitory variables and lack of explicit constraints may raise realism concerns.  
- No head-to-head baselines; limited training/runtime details.

Recommendation
Accept. The conceptual novelty and potential impact outweigh the gaps in capacity analysis and ablations. Adding even modest scaling/ablation results in the camera-ready would further strengthen the contribution.

Reviewer confidence
High.
