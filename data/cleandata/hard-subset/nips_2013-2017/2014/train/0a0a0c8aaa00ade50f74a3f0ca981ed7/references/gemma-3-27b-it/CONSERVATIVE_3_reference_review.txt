This paper proposes a novel method for constructing recurrent neural networks with stable, analog memories, explicitly addressing limitations of prior work regarding Dale's law, graded activity, low firing rates, and excitation-inhibition balance. The approach frames memory storage as a control-theoretic problem, optimizing network parameters to ensure fixed points and stability, utilizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The authors demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog patterns with reasonable performance and a degree of robustness to noise.

The core idea – leveraging control theory for memory network design – is interesting, and the attempt to address multiple known limitations of traditional models is commendable. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivations and methodology. The inclusion of biological constraints (Dale’s law, realistic firing rates) is a positive aspect. The results, while not groundbreaking, demonstrate that the proposed method *can* achieve the stated goals.

However, my primary concern lies with the novelty and significance of the work, and the extent of validation. While the combination of control theory and neural networks *sounds* novel, the core principles of stabilizing recurrent networks are well-established. The application of SSA is a technical detail, and its advantage over more standard stability analysis techniques isn't convincingly demonstrated – particularly given the computational cost likely involved.  The authors state the method “overcomes all of the above limitations,” which is a strong claim that isn’t fully supported by the presented evidence. Many previous models *attempted* to address these limitations, and the authors don’t clearly establish a substantial improvement in any specific area. The comparison to an "ideal observer" is useful, but doesn’t demonstrate superiority to other, more established memory network approaches. 

Furthermore, the validation is limited. The network size (n=150) is relatively small, and the storage capacity (0.2) is modest. The robustness tests, while present, are not exhaustive. The ablation study removing weak weights is a good step, but only reveals a fragility in *one* attractor.  The parameter tuning (ηs and ηF) is described as “heuristic,” which raises concerns about the sensitivity of the results to these choices. The authors acknowledge that the optimization may not achieve true fixed points, settling for “slow points,” which further weakens the claim of stable memory recall.  The results are presented without thorough comparison to existing rate-based memory models; performance metrics are not reported against established benchmarks.  I find the explanation for improved performance on the baseline memory questionable - simply claiming it's due to initialization seems insufficient.

The claim of sparsity emerging as a consequence of stability optimization is intriguing, but the authors don’t fully explore this – is the observed sparsity optimal, or a byproduct of the specific optimization procedure?  The analysis of excitation-inhibition balance is descriptive, but lacks a deeper mechanistic explanation of *why* this balance arises in the optimized networks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in computational neuroscience.
* Integrates control-theoretic principles into neural network design.
* Demonstrates a functional memory network with desired properties.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited novelty and significance – doesn't represent a substantial advancement over existing approaches.
* Insufficient validation – small network size, modest capacity, limited comparisons to benchmarks.
* Parameter tuning described as “heuristic.”
* Claim of overcoming all previous limitations is overstated.
* Stability is not rigorously demonstrated (reliance on “slow points”).



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a reasonable, but not particularly compelling, contribution.  The authors need to significantly strengthen the validation by: 1) comparing their results to existing state-of-the-art memory models (e.g., those based on liquid state machines or reservoir computing) using standard benchmarks, 2) scaling up the network size and demonstrating comparable performance, 3) performing a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters, and 4) providing a more rigorous analysis of the stability properties of the optimized networks. A more detailed explanation of the emergence of sparsity and the underlying mechanisms of excitation-inhibition balance would also be beneficial.  The authors should also tone down the strong claims about overcoming all previous limitations.
