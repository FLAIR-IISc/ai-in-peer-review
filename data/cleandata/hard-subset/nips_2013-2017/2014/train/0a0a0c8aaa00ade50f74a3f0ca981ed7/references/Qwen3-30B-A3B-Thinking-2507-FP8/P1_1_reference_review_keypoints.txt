- Novel control-theoretic framework to design balanced E-I rate networks that store analog memory patterns by enforcing fixed points and stability.

- Uses Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to achieve robust stability while preserving dynamic E/I balance and non-saturating firing rates.

- Extends prior work (e.g., Roudi & Latham) to graded patterns and addresses limitations of Hopfield and other previous models.

- SSA-based stability optimization elegantly resolves the trade-off between stability and realistic firing regimes.

- Model respects biological constraints: Dale’s law via weight reparameterization, threshold-quadratic gain, and balanced E/I dynamics.

- Thorough experimental validation across memory capacity, recall robustness to noise, sparsity effects, and E/I balance with an ideal-observer benchmark.

- Insight that many weak synapses can be removed without performance loss informs functional roles of synaptic sparsity.

- Clear presentation, well-organized mathematics, and informative figures that support reproducibility.

- Rate-based formulation limits direct applicability to spiking neural systems; link to spiking implementations is underdeveloped.

- Demonstrations are on a relatively small network (n=150, m=30); scalability to larger, more realistic networks is not shown.

- Does not provide mechanisms or evidence for how local synaptic learning rules could achieve the global SSA optimization.

- Limited direct comparison to other rate-based memory models that use different stability metrics or gain functions.

- Quality: technically sound and rigorous SSA framework with solid simulations (Rating 9/10).

- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized with reproducible detail (Rating 9/10).

- Originality: novel biologically plausible approach for storing analog patterns using SSA (Rating 9/10).

- Significance: advances modeling of robust, balanced analog memory in cortical-like networks (Rating 9/10).

- Arguments for acceptance: fills a key gap for biologically plausible analog memory, presents a novel technical solution, shows robust performance, and is well-validated and written.

- Arguments against acceptance: rate-based limitation, unclear scalability, lack of local learning-rule implementation, and limited model comparisons.

- Overall recommendation: strong accept for NIPS; technically sound and significant contribution.

- Suggestion: expand discussion linking the rate model to spiking implementations and the gain function’s spiking correspondence.

- Suggestion: discuss SSA optimization scaling to larger networks (e.g., 1,000+ neurons).

- Suggestion: add comparisons to other rate-based models that incorporate Dale’s law or balanced dynamics.

- Suggestion: contextualize reported memory capacity (0.2) relative to theoretical limits for analog memory networks.

- Suggestion: clarify Figure 5’s depiction of inhibitory neurons having a target rate of 0 to avoid confusion.
