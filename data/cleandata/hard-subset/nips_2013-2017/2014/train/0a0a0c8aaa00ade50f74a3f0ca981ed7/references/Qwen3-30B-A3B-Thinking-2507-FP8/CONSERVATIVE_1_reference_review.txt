

# Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing rate-based neural networks with E-I balance that can store multiple analog memory patterns as stable fixed points. The authors claim to overcome limitations of prior work by explicitly enforcing Dale's law, using realistic firing rate dynamics, and achieving stable recall of graded patterns without relying on saturating neuron models. The key innovation is using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for stability optimization, combined with a reparameterization to enforce Dale's law.

The paper makes several important claims: 1) It demonstrates a method for embedding arbitrary analog patterns as stable fixed points in E-I networks; 2) The networks maintain a tight balance of excitation and inhibition; 3) The networks exhibit robust memory recall despite noise; 4) The learned connectivity resembles biological networks in its sparsity.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors have made a genuine effort to address several important biological constraints that many prior neural network models neglect (Dale's law, graded activity, low firing rates). The use of SSA for stability optimization is technically sound and represents a reasonable adaptation of control theory to neural network design. The results on memory recall performance and balanced E/I inputs are compelling and demonstrate the method's feasibility.

**Weaknesses:** Despite these strengths, the paper fails to adequately justify its core claim that this is a significant advance over existing methods. The authors acknowledge that "most of the work following [Hopfield] up" has limitations, but they provide minimal comparison to established baselines. For example, they don't compare their method to recent work on balanced networks (e.g., [12, 22, 23]) or to standard approaches for analog memory storage. The paper presents no ablation studies showing why SSA is necessary versus simpler stability criteria, nor does it demonstrate scalability to larger networks or memory sets.

The most concerning weakness is the lack of rigorous validation. The paper claims "robust memory recall" but provides no evidence that their method outperforms existing approaches in any meaningful way. Figure 3b compares to an "ideal observer" (which is not a standard baseline), but doesn't compare to any realistic neural network model. The paper states that "the network performance is also compared to an 'ideal observer'" without providing any statistical analysis of whether the difference is significant.

The paper also makes a claim that "the resulting networks perform memory recall that is robust to noise," but the noise experiments (Figure 6) are limited to specific noise levels and don't explore the full range of conditions under which memory recall might fail. The authors don't provide any analysis of how their method's performance scales with memory load or network size, which is critical for assessing practical utility.

Furthermore, the paper's connection to neuroscience remains superficial. While it claims to respect biological constraints, it doesn't provide evidence that the networks they construct are biologically plausible beyond the basic E-I balance. The authors don't discuss how their method relates to known neural mechanisms of memory storage, nor do they provide any experimental data to support their claims.

The paper also fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, the authors note that "‖dv/dt‖ after convergence is small but non-zero" (Figure 2), meaning the target patterns aren't exact fixed points, yet they don't discuss how this affects the practical utility of their method. The paper doesn't consider how their method would scale to more complex memory structures or how it would interact with learning rules in biological systems.

**Recommendation:** This paper presents a technically interesting approach that addresses some limitations of prior models, but it falls short of demonstrating that it represents a significant advance over existing methods. The lack of rigorous comparison to established baselines, minimal validation of the claimed advantages, and superficial connection to neuroscience make it difficult to assess the true novelty and value of the work. While the approach is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that it would be useful to the broader neural network or neuroscience communities.

Given the NIPS community's emphasis on rigorous validation and clear demonstration of advantages over existing methods, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from extensive comparisons with established baselines, more thorough analysis of performance scaling, and stronger connections to biological evidence before being considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should also address the limitations they acknowledge (non-zero velocity at fixed points) rather than treating them as minor issues.

The paper's core technical contribution (using SSA for stability optimization) is interesting but not sufficiently novel or validated to warrant publication at NIPS in its current form. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental validation and comparative analysis to meet NIPS's high standards for methodological soundness and significance.
