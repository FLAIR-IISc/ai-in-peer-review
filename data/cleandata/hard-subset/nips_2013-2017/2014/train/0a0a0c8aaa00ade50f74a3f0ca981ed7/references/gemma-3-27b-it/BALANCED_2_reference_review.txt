This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing memory networks based on a control-theoretic framework, optimizing for both fixed-point attractors and robust stability in a balanced network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons. The authors address several limitations of previous models, including Dale’s law, graded activity representations, low firing rates, and the dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition. They utilize the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to achieve robust stability optimization, resulting in networks capable of storing and recalling analog memories with resilience to noise.

The core idea is strong and addresses a significant gap in the field. Existing memory models often struggle to reconcile biological realism with robust performance, particularly regarding the E/I balance and graded activity patterns. The authors’ use of robust control theory, specifically the SSA, is a clever and effective way to tackle the stability problem, which is often a bottleneck in network optimization. The results demonstrate impressive performance, with recall accuracy comparable to an ideal observer and a plausible network structure exhibiting sparsity and E/I balance. The ablation study showing the redundancy of weak synapses is also valuable, offering insights into the network’s organization. The demonstration of variability reduction following stimulus onset further strengthens the biological plausibility of the model.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the method is demonstrably effective, the parameter tuning (ηs, ηF, ε, Gamma distribution parameters, etc.) feels somewhat heuristic and lacks a clear justification beyond achieving good results. A more systematic exploration of parameter space and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The optimization process itself, while utilizing an established algorithm (BFGS), is not detailed enough; the computational cost and scalability to larger networks are not discussed.  The claim of a “general” control-theoretic framework is perhaps overstated. The specific gain function (threshold-quadratic) and network architecture (fully connected within subpopulations) are still assumptions that may limit generalizability. Although the authors acknowledge the rate-based approach, a more detailed discussion of the implications of this simplification, and potential routes to extending the framework to spiking networks, would be helpful.  Finally, the connection to specific biological systems is somewhat loose. While the authors cite relevant physiological findings (e.g., firing rate distributions, synaptic connectivity), a more concrete discussion of how the model could account for specific experimental observations in real brain circuits would enhance its impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of robust control theory (SSA) to memory network optimization is a genuinely novel approach.
*   **Significance:** The model addresses a critical challenge in computational neuroscience: constructing biologically realistic and high-performing memory networks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is well-defined and the experimental results are compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the key concepts are clearly explained.
*   **Results:** The recall performance and robustness to noise are impressive.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Parameter Tuning:** The parameter selection process lacks sufficient justification and systematic exploration.
*   **Scalability:** The computational cost and scalability to larger, more realistic network sizes are not addressed.
*   **Generalizability:** The model relies on specific assumptions about the gain function and network architecture.
*   **Biological Specificity:** The connection to specific biological systems could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the compelling results, outweighs the weaknesses. While some areas could benefit from further investigation and clarification, the work is of high quality and warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, specifically regarding parameter tuning justification, scalability discussion, and strengthening the link to biological data. The authors should consider adding a short discussion of the computational resources required for the optimization.
