Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a rate-based recurrent network that respects Dale’s law and exhibits excitation–inhibition (E/I) balance. Neurons have rectified quadratic transfer functions without saturation; synaptic weights are parameterized to enforce sign constraints. Memory storage is cast as: (i) making target patterns fixed points (by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at those states) and (ii) ensuring local asymptotic stability (by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). The optimization variables include all synaptic parameters and, importantly, the steady-state inhibitory activities for each memory. Empirically (n=150, m=30; capacity ≈0.2), the method yields robust recall from noisy cues, E/I balance, sparse weight distributions, and reductions in trial-by-trial variability under ongoing noise. Performance is compared to an “ideal observer” upper bound.

Relation to prior work: The paper aims to overcome limitations of classical Hopfield-style models (symmetric weights; effectively binary memories), as well as more recent balanced/LIF models with global inhibition or saturating transfer functions. It leverages robust control ideas (SSA) previously used for transient control in balanced networks to enforce stability at desired attractors. It is closest to balanced memory networks (e.g., Roudi & Latham) and graded-memory analyses (Treves & Rolls; Treves; Battaglia & Treves), but claims to combine Dale’s law, graded memories, recurrent E and I populations, and low-rate non-saturating activity.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of storage as simultaneous fixed-point and stability constraints; using SSA for differentiable stability optimization is interesting and technically appropriate.
- Enforces Dale’s law and obtains dynamically balanced E/I inputs without saturating transfer functions.
- Demonstrates recall robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise; shows variability quenching consistent with cortical data.
- Empirically observes sparse weight distributions and tests functional impact of pruning small weights.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Critical modeling choice: the inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ are treated as free optimization variables per memory. This greatly increases degrees of freedom (n_I m additional parameters), effectively adding memory-specific “auxiliary knobs.” It undermines biological plausibility and inflates apparent capacity; inhibitory steady states should be consequences of W and the dynamics, not per-memory targets. An ablation where v_inh^μ are not optimized (or are constrained by a single set of parameters) is essential.
- Capacity and scaling are not characterized. Results are limited to n=150, m=30. No study of capacity vs load (m/n), basin sizes, or how SSA–SA gaps and performance scale with n. This is a major omission given extensive prior theory on capacity in graded/balanced networks.
- Validation lacks strong baselines. Comparing to an “ideal observer” is not a neural/dynamical baseline. Missing comparisons to: (i) standard graded Hopfield-like constructions under Dale’s constraints; (ii) balanced memory networks with global inhibition; (iii) a version optimizing fixed points only (no SSA) to show the added value of stability optimization.
- Fixed-point condition is not satisfied exactly (dv/dt ≠ 0 at targets); reliance on “nearby” fixed points is asserted but not rigorously demonstrated (e.g., via root finding or continuation). Basin-of-attraction characterization is absent; success criteria (e.g., dμ<0.001 then relaxed to 0.08 post-pruning) appear ad hoc.
- SSA choice and guarantees: ε is selected heuristically, with limited discussion of the SSA–SA gap and potential failure modes. Only local (linearized) stability is enforced; no analysis of global properties or robustness margins.
- Sparsity claim is overstated. L2 regularization plus the softplus/log reparameterization can induce many small (near-zero) weights; calling this “genuine” sparsity without quantitative measures (e.g., distributional fits; sensitivity to η_F; comparing to L1) is not convincing.
- Biological plausibility: rate model only; the assertion that it should transfer to spiking if gains match is speculative without demonstration. Inhibition being “auxiliary” during training weakens the argument.
- Reproducibility details are thin: Table 1 and supplementary derivations are referenced but not provided here; scalability and optimizer settings (convergence criteria, computational cost) are unclear.

Quality
- Technically sound in setting up the objectives and using SSA, but important methodological choices (per-memory inhibitory variables, approximate fixed points) and the lack of ablations/baselines reduce confidence in the conclusions. The empirical study is limited in scale and scope.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. However, the role and interpretation of v_inh^μ as optimization variables should be made explicit up front, including implications for plausibility and capacity. The claims about sparsity need more careful wording and quantification.

Originality
- Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to construct analog attractors in Dale-constrained balanced networks is a novel synthesis. However, several ingredients (graded memories, balanced networks, Dale’s law enforcement) are known; the main novelty is the optimization framework.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a constructive method for building stable analog attractors. Current evidence is preliminary. Without capacity/scaling analysis, stronger baselines, and removal of per-memory inhibitory DoF, the impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Remove or tightly constrain per-memory inhibitory variables; show results when only W (and global parameters) are learned. Alternatively, justify biologically how inhibition could implement memory-specific steady states without extra supervision.
- Provide capacity vs load curves, basin-size estimates, and scaling to larger n. Report SSA–SA gaps and robustness margins.
- Add baselines (e.g., balanced network with global inhibition; fixed-point-only optimization; graded Hopfield with Dale constraints) and ablations (no SSA; different ε; different regularizers).
- Verify exact fixed points post-optimization and quantify convergence domains. Standardize success thresholds.
- Demonstrate transfer to a spiking LIF network with matched gain, or temper claims.
- Clarify sparsity claims with quantitative analyses and sensitivity to η_F.

Pros
- Interesting optimization-based framework; respects Dale’s law; demonstrates balanced recall with graded patterns; solid connection to robust control.

Cons
- Per-memory inhibitory DoF; small scale; no capacity/scaling; weak baselines; approximate fixed points; overclaimed sparsity; limited biological plausibility without spiking validation.

Overall recommendation: 5 (borderline/lean reject)
Confidence: 0.7
