Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent rate-based networks with explicit excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations (respecting Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) attractor memories as stable fixed points. The network uses a rectified-quadratic gain function without saturation and aims to achieve stability via precise dynamic E/I balance rather than neuronal saturation. Memory storage is formulated as a joint optimization problem over synaptic parameters and the steady-state inhibitory activities in each attractor. Two conditions are targeted: (i) near-zero velocity at the desired states to approximate fixed points; (ii) asymptotic stability, enforced by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each desired state. The authors use L-BFGS to minimize a composite objective over all memories with an L2 regularizer; Dale’s law is enforced via a softplus reparameterization. They demonstrate: robust recall of multiple log-normal firing-rate patterns at low firing rates; E/I balance tightly matched across attractors; partial sparsification of synaptic weights without explicit L1; robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise; and across-trial variability quenching upon stimulus onset.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to Hopfield-style associative memory networks and their graded generalizations (Treves & Rolls), balanced network theory (Amit & Brunel; Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham), spatially-structured bump attractors (Ben-Yishai), and inhibitory plasticity for stability and balance (Vogels et al.). Prior high-capacity analog memory models often lacked explicit inhibitory units or relied on saturating transfer functions or global inhibition; recent spiking models with balance achieve stability but mainly for binary or stereotyped patterns. Here, a key novelty is importing robust control tools (SSA optimization) to construct E/I balanced, rate-based networks with graded memories and realistic constraints (Dale’s law), differing from previous heuristic or mean-field constructions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and principled. The SSA is an appropriate smooth surrogate for spectral abscissa, enabling gradient-based stability optimization. The reparameterization enforces Dale’s law.
- Empirical support: Simulations show convergence to target attractors, noise robustness, E/I balance, and limited reliance on weak synapses. The comparison to an ideal observer is appropriate as an upper bound.
- Caveats:
  - Fixed points are only approximate (dv/dt small but non-zero), with an empirical claim that nearby exact fixed points exist; a more formal argument or constructive correction step would strengthen the claim.
  - Capacity is demonstrated on n=150 with m=30 (≈0.2) but no scaling analysis with n or m, nor comparisons to theoretical capacity bounds for graded memories.
  - SSA parameter ε is set heuristically; stability guarantees depend on ε and system size. Sensitivity analyses are limited.
  - Biological plausibility of learning is limited (global, nonlocal optimization using Jacobians), though the authors acknowledge this and point to inhibitory plasticity as a biological hypothesis.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The optimization objective and architectural choices are clearly specified. It would benefit from:
- Explicit reporting of all hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) and sensitivity analyses.
- Details on computational cost (complexity per iteration, wall-clock time) and convergence behavior.
- Inclusion of Table 1 and supplementary details referenced (initialization, mean-field derivations).

Originality
The use of SSA-based robust stability optimization to design multi-attractor, graded E/I networks under Dale’s law is novel and interesting. Prior work did not combine a smooth spectral proxy with explicit E/I structure to embed arbitrary analog patterns. The observation that partial sparsity emerges without imposing L1 is also noteworthy.

Significance
If robust and scalable, this framework could influence how computational neuroscientists construct biologically grounded associative memories and how control-theoretic tools are applied to neural circuit design. Demonstrations of balanced operation at low rates across multiple attractors are compelling. However, claims would be strengthened by capacity scaling, basin-of-attraction quantification, and demonstrations in spiking networks using the learned connectivity.

Strengths
- Principled formulation aligning fixed-point existence with robust local stability.
- Explicit E/I separation with Dale’s law; low-rate operation via E/I balance rather than saturation.
- Robust recall of graded, log-normal patterns; good match to ideal observer performance.
- Clear E/I balance characterization across attractors; variability quenching consistent with neurophysiology.
- Emergent partial sparsity and robustness to pruning of small weights.

Weaknesses and questions
- Capacity and scaling: How does performance scale with n, m, and E/I ratio? Can the method achieve capacities competitive with graded-memory theory when stability is enforced?
- Fixed-point accuracy: Can the residual dv/dt be driven closer to zero, or can a post-optimization Newton step project onto exact fixed points while maintaining stability?
- SSA sensitivity: How sensitive are results to ε and ηs/ηF? Provide eigenvalue spectra before/after optimization and SSA vs SA discrepancies.
- Connectivity structure: Despite emergent small weights, the graph is effectively dense. Can explicit structural sparsity constraints be added without losing performance?
- Biological plausibility: Any steps toward local learning approximations (e.g., inhibitory plasticity) that improve the SSA objective? Can the optimized rate connectivity be transplanted to a spiking network with matched f–I curves?
- Robustness: Test with colored noise, heterogeneity in τi, and variability in external inputs hi.
- Reproducibility: Please provide code, random seeds, and precise hyperparameters.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add scaling plots (success probability vs m/n; basin size vs noise σ).
- Include spectral analyses and SSA/SA convergence behavior across memories.
- Quantify basin volumes and dwell times under ongoing noise.
- Explore explicit L1 or structured sparsity penalties and compare to emergent sparsity.
- Demonstrate transfer to a spiking network with similar gain and show recall and balance.
- Report compute time and optimization diagnostics.

Pros
- Novel control-theoretic approach to stabilizing graded memories in E/I networks.
- Biologically relevant features: Dale’s law, balanced operation, low rates, variability quenching.
- Strong empirical demonstrations with thoughtful baselines.

Cons
- Limited analysis of capacity and scaling.
- Approximate fixed points without formal guarantees.
- Heuristic choices (ε) without sensitivity analysis.
- Learning rule is nonlocal and offline.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological novelty and biological relevance outweigh the current limitations. With added analyses on scaling, stability sensitivity, and fixed-point accuracy (and, if possible, a spiking demonstration), this could be a strong contribution bridging control theory and neural circuit design.
