### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing recurrent neural networks that can store and recall multiple analog (graded-activity) memory patterns. The authors frame memory storage as an optimization problem with two key objectives: 1) ensuring the desired memory patterns are fixed points of the network dynamics, and 2) ensuring these fixed points are dynamically stable. The network model incorporates several key biological constraints, including separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations (Dale's Law), and a non-saturating neuronal transfer function. A central contribution is the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a differentiable measure from robust control theory, to create a smooth objective function for stability that is amenable to gradient-based optimization.

Previous work on attractor networks, such as the Hopfield model, typically stored binary patterns and often violated Dale's Law. While subsequent models incorporated analog patterns or spiking neurons, they often relied on neuronal saturation at high firing rates, assumed instantaneous or global inhibition, or required highly structured connectivity, which limited their ability to store arbitrary, graded patterns at low firing rates. This work overcomes these limitations by directly optimizing the connectivity matrix and the activity patterns of inhibitory neurons to achieve stable, low-rate analog attractors within a dynamically balanced E/I network. The authors demonstrate that the resulting networks exhibit robust recall from noisy cues, generate sparse-like weight distributions, operate in a balanced E/I regime, and reproduce experimentally observed phenomena like stimulus-induced variability quenching.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by the presented optimization results and simulations. The formulation of the problem is sound, and the use of the SSA to address the non-smoothness of the standard stability criterion (the spectral abscissa) is a technically sophisticated and appropriate solution. The authors are careful in their evaluation, for instance, by acknowledging that the optimized patterns are technically "slow points" rather than perfect fixed points, although the practical difference appears minimal. The analysis of the resulting network properties—such as the E/I balance and weight distributions—is thorough and provides convincing evidence that the optimization yields biologically relevant network structures and dynamics.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by situating it within the existing literature and clearly stating the limitations of prior models. The methods section provides a concise but sufficient description of the network model, the optimization objective, and the use of the SSA. The results are presented logically, with figures that are well-designed and illustrative of the main findings (e.g., the convergence of the optimization, recall performance, and E/I balance). The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the core methodology.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The primary novel contribution is the application of modern control-theoretic tools (specifically, the SSA) to the classic neuroscience problem of creating stable attractor memories. This provides a new, principled, and powerful method for *constructing* networks with desired dynamical properties, moving beyond the limitations of both Hebbian-style learning rules and earlier stability analyses. While individual components (rate models, Dale's law, E/I balance) are familiar, their synthesis via this optimization framework to achieve stable *analog* memories at low rates without saturation is a significant and novel combination.

**Significance:** The results are significant for the field of computational neuroscience. The paper provides a compelling proof of concept that networks respecting several key biological constraints can support complex computational functions like analog memory. This moves the field beyond the common dichotomy of either highly abstract models that ignore biology or more detailed models that are difficult to analyze or train for specific functions. The emergent properties of the optimized networks, such as sparse connectivity and stimulus-driven variability reduction, connect the model to experimental data and suggest that robust stability may be a powerful principle for organizing circuit properties. Researchers are likely to build upon this control-theoretic approach to design networks for other computational tasks.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Methodology:** The application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to stabilize multiple analog attractors in a biologically constrained network is a highly novel and powerful contribution.
2.  **Significant Problem:** The paper successfully addresses the long-standing challenge of storing graded, low-rate memories in networks that respect Dale's law and operate in a balanced state, a significant advance over prior models.
3.  **Strong Results:** The optimized networks demonstrate robust recall, and the emergent properties (E/I balance, sparse weights, variability quenching) align well with biological observations, strengthening the model's relevance.
4.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, technically sound, and presents its contributions in a clear and compelling manner.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Biologically Implausible "Learning":** The optimization algorithm (L-BFGS on a global cost function) is a network construction method, not a model of biological learning. It requires global information about the Jacobian matrix for all desired states. While valuable from an engineering and normative perspective, this limitation should be acknowledged more centrally as it constrains the model's direct biological interpretation as a learning process.
2.  **Scalability and Parameter Sensitivity:** The results are demonstrated for a single network size (N=150) and memory load (m=30). The paper would be strengthened by an analysis of how the method scales with network size and what the storage capacity limits are. Furthermore, key hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) are set "heuristically," and a discussion of the sensitivity to these parameters is missing.
3.  **Role of Inhibitory Neurons:** Inhibitory neuron activities are treated as free parameters optimized for each memory pattern. This is a powerful degree of freedom but lacks a clear biological interpretation. A deeper analysis of the structure of these learned inhibitory patterns and the implications of this modeling choice would be beneficial.

Overall, the strengths, particularly the novelty of the methodology and the significance of the problem addressed, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The paper introduces a powerful new tool for theoretical neuroscience and provides a compelling model of analog memory that is more biologically grounded than many predecessors.
