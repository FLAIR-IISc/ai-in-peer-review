- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to build recurrent rate networks with explicit E/I populations (Dale’s law) that store multiple graded attractor memories via rectified-quadratic gains and precise dynamic E/I balance.
- Formulates memory storage as joint optimization over synaptic parameters and steady-state inhibitory activities, enforcing near-zero velocity and asymptotic stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).
- Implements L-BFGS with L2 regularization and softplus reparameterization to enforce Dale’s law.
- Demonstrates robust recall of multiple low-rate log-normal firing patterns, tight E/I balance across attractors, emergent partial sparsity, robustness to noisy cues/ongoing noise, and variability quenching on stimulus onset.
- Novelty: first to apply SSA-based robust stability optimization to embed graded memories with explicit E/I separation under Dale’s law.
- Technical soundness: clear formulation; SSA is an appropriate smooth surrogate enabling gradient-based stability optimization; Dale’s-law reparameterization is effective.
- Empirical support: simulations validate convergence, noise robustness, E/I balance, and provide an ideal-observer reference.
- Main caveat: fixed points are only approximate (dv/dt small); no formal proof or constructive correction to exact fixed points is provided.
- Capacity/scaling limitation: results shown for n=150, m=30 (~0.2) with no scaling analysis or comparison to theoretical capacity for graded memories.
- SSA tuning concern: smoothing parameter ε chosen heuristically with limited sensitivity analysis and unclear scaling guarantees.
- Biological plausibility issue: learning is global and nonlocal (Jacobian-based), not biologically realistic, though inhibitory plasticity is proposed as a hypothesis.
- Clarity: paper is well written but needs explicit reporting of hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε), computational cost, convergence diagnostics, and inclusion of referenced supplementary materials (Table 1, initializations).
- Originality: combining a smooth spectral proxy with explicit E/I structure to embed arbitrary analog patterns is novel; emergent sparsity without L1 is notable.
- Significance: method could influence biologically grounded associative memory design and control-theoretic neural circuit design if shown robust and scalable.
- Strength: principled alignment of fixed-point existence with robust local stability and explicit E/I separation enabling low-rate operation via balance rather than saturation.
- Strength: good match to ideal observer, E/I balance characterization, and neurophysiological-like variability quenching.
- Weakness/question: How does performance scale with network size, memory load, and E/I ratio; can capacities match graded-memory theory under stability constraints?
- Weakness/question: Can residual dv/dt be reduced (e.g., via post-optimization Newton step) while preserving stability?
- Weakness/question: How sensitive are results to ε and learning rates; provide eigenvalue spectra before/after optimization and SSA vs SA discrepancies.
- Weakness/question: Connectivity remains effectively dense despite small weights—can explicit sparsity or structured connectivity be imposed without performance loss?
- Weakness/question: Can learned rate connectivity transfer to spiking networks; test robustness to colored noise, heterogeneous time constants, and input variability?
- Reproducibility request: provide code, random seeds, and exact hyperparameters.
- Suggested improvements: add scaling plots (success vs m/n), basin-size and dwell-time quantification, spectral analyses, explicit sparsity penalties, spiking-network transfer, and compute-time/optimization diagnostics.
- Pros: novel control-theoretic approach, biologically relevant constraints, strong empirical demonstrations with thoughtful baselines.
- Cons: limited capacity/scaling analysis, approximate fixed points without guarantees, heuristic ε choices, and nonlocal offline learning rule.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on additional analyses of scaling, stability sensitivity, fixed-point accuracy, and preferably a spiking demonstration.
