- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog memories as stable attractors in E/I recurrent rate networks obeying Dale’s law and low-rate regimes.
- Optimizes synaptic weights (sign-constrained softplus reparameterization) and per-memory inhibitory steady-states so target patterns are approximate fixed points and locally asymptotically stable.
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable surrogate for spectral abscissa, optimized with (L-)BFGS.
- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues, resilience to ongoing noise, E/I balance across attractors, variability quenching, and emergent sparsity of effective synapses.
- Connects to Hopfield/graded-response attractor literature while addressing Dale’s law, explicit inhibitory dynamics, graded patterns, and low-rate operation.
- Conceptual contribution: framing memory embedding as joint fixed-point and stability optimization using robust control tools.
- Biological plausibility: model respects E/I separation, dynamic balance, and graded firing targets.
- Empirical methodology: sensible normalization of recall error, ideal-observer baseline, and analyses of E/I currents and weight sparsity.
- Practical insight: weak synapses can be pruned with limited performance loss, suggesting robustness and potential for sparse implementations.
- Capacity and scaling are underexplored: experiments limited to n=150, m=30 (~0.2 capacity) with no systematic scaling or theoretical capacity analysis.
- Training degrees of freedom: optimizing per-memory inhibitory activities (v_inh^μ) increases free parameters and may inflate storage—impact not quantified.
- Fixed-point approximation concern: residual dv/dt is nonzero post-training and stability metrics are evaluated at targets rather than true converged fixed points.
- Sensitivity and reproducibility issues: hyperparameters (η_s, η_F, ε) set heuristically; no sensitivity analysis, convergence properties, computational cost, or public code provided.
- Comparisons are limited: lacks baseline comparisons to other E/I-separating or graded-memory network constructions (e.g., clustered balanced or bump models).
- Biological readouts: claims of "tight balance" are qualitative; quantitative balance indices, correlations, and scaling analyses are missing.
- Quality assessment: technically sound SSA objective and supporting simulations, but gaps in scaling, sensitivity, and fixed-point validation.
- Clarity assessment: generally well written and organized, though heuristic choices need clearer justification and supplement details.
- Originality assessment: novel application of robust control (SSA) to build analog attractors under biological constraints.
- Significance assessment: promising framework with potential impact if shown to scale and generalize (e.g., to spiking models).
- Suggested improvements: add scaling experiments, quantify effect of optimizing v_inh^μ, locate actual fixed points and report SA/SSA there, perform sensitivity analyses, compare to alternative models, and release code.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, principled approach with solid initial empirical demonstrations and broader applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited capacity/scaling analysis, extra inhibitory degrees of freedom, fixed-point approximation caveats, hyperparameter sensitivity, and sparse comparisons.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — promising work that needs stronger scaling, validation, and reproducibility evidence.
