- Presents a biologically plausible analog memory network combining control-theoretic SSA optimization with an E/I-separated, Dale’s-law-constrained rate model.  
- Frames memory storage as a fixed-point stability/control problem and uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa to ensure robust stability across multiple memories.  
- Extends prior work by providing principled, optimization-based stability across many memories rather than single-level inhibition tuning, and generalizes beyond spatial or spike-specific models.  
- Novel application of robust control theory to neural memory storage, offering a mathematically grounded framework.  
- Enforces biological constraints (Dale’s Law, E/I separation, recurrent connectivity) and achieves a dynamically balanced E/I state.  
- Demonstrates robustness to noise in recall cues and ongoing dynamics with comprehensive experiments and comparisons to an ideal observer.  
- Technical and mathematical presentation is rigorous and well-written, though some SSA explanations are dense for non-control-theory readers.  
- Main weakness: SSA optimization is computationally expensive and may not scale to brain-size networks.  
- Optimization success depends on careful parameter tuning (ηs, ηF, ε); parameter sensitivity and robustness need more study.  
- Focuses on a design/optimization approach without detailing biologically plausible learning rules (e.g., Hebbian/STDP) for implementation.  
- Uses a rate-based model, limiting direct applicability to spiking network dynamics; connection to spiking models is underexplored.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, biologically plausible, robust results, clear presentation with significant potential impact.  
- Against acceptance: computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and reliance on rate-based modeling.  
- Overall recommendation: Strongly accept; encourage addressing parameter sensitivity and learning-rule limitations in future work.
