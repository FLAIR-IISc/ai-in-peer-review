

# Peer Review: "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in computational neuroscience by proposing a biologically plausible framework for storing and recalling analog (graded) memory patterns in networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons. The authors successfully overcome several limitations of previous memory models: they respect Dale's law, incorporate explicit inhibitory neurons, avoid binary neuron responses, achieve low firing rates through dynamic balance, and do not rely on saturation mechanisms. Their key innovation is using robust control theory (specifically, minimization of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) to optimize network parameters such that desired analog activity patterns become stable fixed points of the network dynamics. The paper demonstrates that their approach can successfully store multiple graded patterns (e.g., 30 patterns in a 150-neuron network) with robust recall performance, balanced E-I activity, and sparse connectivity similar to biological networks.

This work builds upon and significantly extends several strands of previous research: Hopfield's early work on associative memory (which violated Dale's law), more recent attempts to incorporate Dale's law and inhibitory neurons (which often relied on saturating neuron models), and the growing interest in balanced networks for cortical function. The paper effectively situates itself within this literature, clearly articulating how it advances the state of the art beyond previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Methodological Contribution:** The authors' use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for stability optimization is a significant technical innovation in the context of memory networks. This cleverly addresses the key challenge of achieving stability without relying on saturating neuron models, which has been a persistent limitation in prior work. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for why SSA is suitable for this problem.

2. **Biological Plausibility:** The paper successfully integrates multiple biological constraints simultaneously—Dale's law, sparse connectivity, graded activity patterns, and balanced E-I dynamics—something few prior models have accomplished. The resulting network architecture and dynamics are consistent with known cortical properties.

3. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including memory recall performance under noise, analysis of synaptic sparsity, examination of E-I balance, and demonstration of reduced variability following stimulus onset. The comparison to an "ideal observer" is particularly insightful.

4. **Clear Theoretical Framework:** The paper establishes a solid theoretical foundation by formally defining the conditions for memory storage (fixed point and stability) and providing a principled optimization approach. The mathematical formulation is precise and well-motivated.

5. **Strong Experimental Results:** The results are convincing and well-presented. Figure 2 clearly shows the convergence of the optimization process, Figure 3 demonstrates robust recall performance, and Figure 5 provides compelling evidence of balanced E-I dynamics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper demonstrates success with only 30 memories in a 150-neuron network (storage capacity of 0.2), but doesn't sufficiently address how this would scale to larger networks or higher storage capacities. In Figure 3b, the recall performance drops significantly as the number of memories increases (though this is only shown for 30 memories). The authors should provide more analysis on the scaling properties of their method.

2. **Spiking Network Connection is Underdeveloped:** While the authors briefly discuss the potential for applying their rate-based model to spiking networks, this connection remains speculative. They state that "the gain function we have used here is likely an appropriate choice" but provide no empirical evidence that the rate model's gain function accurately captures the input-output relationship of spiking neurons in the relevant range. A more detailed discussion or preliminary experiments would strengthen this claim.

3. **Limited Analysis of Memory Interference:** The paper doesn't address how the network handles memory interference when multiple patterns are stored. This is a critical aspect of memory systems, especially as the number of memories increases. The authors should investigate how the network performs when presented with cues that are close to multiple stored patterns.

4. **Lack of Comparison to Other Biologically Plausible Models:** While the paper adequately reviews prior work, it could strengthen its contribution by more directly comparing to recent biologically plausible memory models like those in [12] (which the authors cite but don't compare to in detail) or the work by [8,9] on dynamic inhibition. A quantitative comparison would better establish the novelty of their approach.

5. **Optimization Procedure Details:** The paper mentions using "a variant of the low-storage BFGS algorithm" but provides insufficient detail about the computational complexity and scalability of the optimization procedure. For a model with 150 neurons, the optimization involves tuning approximately 150×149 + 150×30 = 24,600 parameters. The authors should discuss the computational feasibility of this approach for larger networks.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of how memory might be implemented in biological neural networks. The paper successfully addresses multiple limitations of previous models simultaneously, providing a theoretically sound and biologically plausible framework for analog memory storage. The results are robust and the methodology is innovative.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of scalability and the underdeveloped connection to spiking networks. However, these are important concerns that could be addressed in future work rather than undermining the current contribution. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a compelling case for its approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing a novel, biologically plausible framework for analog memory storage that overcomes multiple limitations of previous approaches. The paper is technically sound, the results are well-supported, and the significance of the work is clear for both neuroscience and computational modeling communities.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the discussion of scalability, particularly how the storage capacity (number of memories) scales with network size. Include some analysis of the trade-offs between network size, memory capacity, and performance.

2. Provide more concrete evidence for the connection to spiking networks, perhaps by comparing the rate model's gain function to known spiking neuron properties or by showing preliminary results with a spiking network using the optimized connectivity.

3. Include a brief analysis of memory interference—how the network handles cues that are similar to multiple stored patterns.

4. Add a comparison table to the related work section that explicitly shows how the current approach differs from key prior models in terms of the specific limitations it addresses.

5. Provide more details about the optimization procedure, including the computational cost and any practical limitations for larger networks.

This paper represents an important step forward in building biologically plausible models of memory and has the potential to influence both theoretical neuroscience and the development of more brain-inspired machine learning architectures.
