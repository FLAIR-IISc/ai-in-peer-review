Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent rate-based networks with separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations (Dale’s law) that store multiple analog attractor memories as stable fixed points. Neurons have rectified quadratic input-output gains with no upper saturation; stability and bounded activity arise from a dynamically maintained E/I balance rather than saturation. The key technical contribution is an optimization objective that enforces, for each desired memory pattern: (i) near-fixed-point constraints by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at the target pattern, and (ii) asymptotic local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian, a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa (SA). The network parameters include all synaptic weights (parametrized via a softplus to enforce Dale’s law) and, importantly, the inhibitory steady-state potentials for each memory (treated as auxiliary variables during training). The authors demonstrate recall performance in a 150-neuron network storing 30 analog patterns drawn from a log-normal distribution (capacity ≈ 0.2), robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise, sparsity in the learned synapses, and tightly balanced E/I inputs across attractors. They compare recall to an “ideal observer” that picks the closest stored pattern to the cue, and show comparable accuracy. They also show that many small weights can be clipped with minimal degradation.

Relation to prior work
The paper explicitly situates itself with respect to classical Hopfield networks (symmetry, binary memories), continuous-valued attractors [Treves, Rolls], spiking networks and balanced E/I networks [Amit & Brunel; Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham], spatial “bump” attractors, and spike timing-based memory models lacking inhibition. The novelty here is the joint enforcement of Dale’s law, graded analog patterns at low rates without saturation, and robust stability via SSA optimization, rather than relying on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibitory feedback calibrated to a single excitation level. The use of SSA from robust control theory for RNN stability optimization (related to Hennequin et al. 2014) is leveraged here specifically to embed multiple graded attractors in E/I networks.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: Poses memory embedding as a stability optimization problem and uses SSA to obtain differentiable stability constraints under Dale’s law.
- Biological plausibility constraints: Separate E/I populations, sparse recurrent connectivity, graded rates, low firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance (demonstrated across attractors).
- Empirical thoroughness: Recall performance vs cue noise with a principled baseline (ideal observer), ongoing noise robustness, and variability quenching upon stimulus onset, qualitatively matching cortical observations.
- Emergent sparsity: Despite L2 regularization, learned synaptic weights are heavy-tailed; pruning small weights minimally affects performance (except for one attractor).
- Clear exposition and intuitive figures (as described), with practical training details (reparameterization, choice of ε scaling, L-BFGS optimization).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Capacity and scaling: Results are shown for n=150, m=30 (m/n≈0.2). There is no systematic scaling analysis of capacity with network size or pattern statistics. Given the long-standing interest in capacity, a plot of performance vs m/n across sizes and noise levels would significantly strengthen the paper.
- Fixed point vs slow point: The objective minimizes dv/dt at target states but does not enforce exact fixed points; SSA<0 only ensures local stability if the point is on the dynamics’ manifold. The claim that a nearby stable fixed point exists is supported empirically but not guaranteed. A constructive analysis (e.g., running the network from the target and reporting convergence to a numerically found fixed point and its distance) or a bound relating residual ‖dv/dt‖, Jacobian stability margin, and distance to a true fixed point would increase rigor.
- Auxiliary inhibitory variables: Treating vinh for each memory as free variables during training raises questions about biological plausibility and identifiability (one could “assign” memory-specific inhibitory setpoints). It would help to clarify that these serve only to adjust the learned connectivity and that during recall the network dynamics uniquely determine inhibitory activity. An ablation in which vinh are not free (or are regularized across memories) would be informative.
- Basins of attraction: Local stability (SA/SSA) does not quantify basin size. Some characterization (e.g., success probability vs cue noise for each attractor, Lyapunov-like energy surfaces, or empirical basins along random directions) would address concerns about robustness and interference.
- Comparisons: While the ideal-observer baseline is appropriate for cue-noise, it would be valuable to compare to alternative network constructions: e.g., continuous Hopfield-type models without Dale’s law, balanced memory networks with global inhibition, or learned low-rank RNN constructions embedding multiple fixed points, to quantify the benefits and trade-offs of SSA-based training.
- Regularization and sparsity: The claim that sparsity “must be” a consequence of stability optimization (and not L2) is plausible but could be supported by ablations varying ηF and adding L1 penalties to disentangle mechanisms.
- Clipping experiment: Changing the success threshold from 0.001 to 0.08 to accommodate attractor shifts complicates interpretation. Reporting the actual distribution of distances at convergence, or re-centering to the new fixed points before evaluation, would be cleaner.
- Reproducibility details: Some critical training choices are deferred to Table 1/Supplementary (e.g., τi, learning hyperparameters, gradients of SSA). Explicit gradient expressions for the SSA term and Jacobian with respect to the β parameters, and code or pseudocode, would aid reproduction.

Quality: The paper is technically sound overall and careful about claims, but would benefit from more rigorous fixed-point verification, capacity scaling, and basin analyses.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Minor issues: references are incomplete (missing years/pages in several entries), and some implementation specifics are deferred.

Originality: High in its combination of SSA-based stability optimization with Dale’s law for graded attractors and the demonstration of E/I balance across memories.

Significance: Potentially impactful for both theoretical neuroscience and machine learning/control communities interested in stable memory embeddings in realistic RNNs. Demonstrations are convincing but limited in scale; broader adoption would be helped by capacity/scaling analysis and open-source code.

Questions for authors
- Can you empirically locate the true fixed points near each target and report their distances and stability margins?
- How does capacity scale with n and with the variance of the pattern distribution?
- What happens if vinh are not optimized per memory (e.g., constrained by a shared affine map of vexc)?
- How sensitive are results to ε and the weighting ηs, ηF?
- Can the learned connectivity be ported to a spiking LIF network with matched gain to validate the rate-to-spike transfer?

Pros
- Novel and principled stability-based training under biological constraints
- Robust recall of analog patterns with E/I balance and variability quenching
- Emergent sparsity and pruning tolerance
- Clear relation to prior theory

Cons
- No capacity/scaling study; small networks only
- No guarantee/verification of exact fixed points or basin sizes
- Per-memory inhibitory variables raise plausibility questions
- Limited comparisons and ablations; some evaluation choices ad hoc

Recommendation: Weak accept. The approach is original and promising, with solid empirical demonstrations; addressing capacity, fixed-point verification, and ablations would elevate it further.
