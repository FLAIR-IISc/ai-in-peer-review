

# Review: Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons

This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing biologically plausible neural networks that can store and recall analog (non-binary) memory patterns. The authors address key limitations of previous memory models, particularly those that violate Dale's law, rely on binary activity states, or require saturating neuron models. By formalizing memory storage as requiring both fixed points and stability conditions, and using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control theory to optimize for stability, they create networks with realistic E-I balance, graded activity, and low firing rates.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper builds on classic Hopfield network models [1] and recent advances in balanced network models [7-9, 12], but addresses critical shortcomings. Previous work either violated Dale's law [1], assumed binary activity states [2-6], or relied on saturating neuron models [7-9]. The authors' approach differs from spatially connected networks [10-11] by not requiring specific spatial structure in connectivity or memory patterns. Their use of SSA optimization (from control theory [13-14]) to achieve stability represents a novel application to neuroscience that bridges theoretical control and neural modeling.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Quality**: The paper presents a sound mathematical formulation of memory storage requirements (fixed points and stability) and effectively leverages SSA optimization to address the stability challenge. The theoretical derivation of the cost function (Eq. 5) is rigorous, and the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproduction.

2. **Biological Plausibility**: The authors successfully demonstrate a network that respects key biological constraints: Dale's law (via weight reparameterization), graded activity patterns (log-normal distributions), low firing rates (5-10 Hz), and dynamic E/I balance. The empirical results (Fig. 5) compellingly show how E/I inputs precisely balance to maintain target firing rates.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation, including robust recall performance (Fig. 3), sparsity analysis (Fig. 4), and demonstration of variability reduction (Fig. 6), all of which support their claims. The comparison to an ideal observer provides a meaningful performance benchmark.

4. **Novel Methodological Contribution**: The application of SSA optimization to neural network memory design is a significant methodological advance that could be applied to other neural modeling problems.

## Weaknesses
1. **Scalability and Capacity Limitations**: The paper demonstrates storage capacity of 0.2 (30 memories in 150 neurons), but doesn't thoroughly explore how this capacity scales with network size or memory pattern complexity. The authors note that "the Jacobian-based stability criterion remains valid" even when ‖dv/dt‖ is small but non-zero, but this could be problematic for larger networks.

2. **Biological Plausibility of Optimization Process**: While the final network is biologically plausible, the optimization process itself is algorithmic rather than learning-based. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how this optimal connectivity could be achieved through local synaptic plasticity rules, which is a critical step toward biological relevance. This gap is acknowledged in the discussion, but remains a significant limitation.

3. **Spiking Network Connection**: The authors briefly mention that the rate-based model could be applied to spiking networks, but provide no evidence or analysis of this connection. The gain function (Eq. 1) is justified as appropriate for cortical neurons, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that the optimized rate model would function similarly in a spiking implementation.

4. **Analysis of Memory Interference**: The paper shows that removing weak synapses affects one attractor's stability, but doesn't thoroughly analyze the mechanisms behind this interference or how it might be mitigated in larger networks.

## Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The optimization framework is rigorously derived and validated through comprehensive experiments. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., non-zero ‖dv/dt‖ at fixed points).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear figures that effectively illustrate key concepts. The mathematical derivations are accessible to the target audience.

**Originality**: The work represents a significant advance over previous memory models by simultaneously addressing multiple biological constraints. The use of SSA optimization is particularly novel in this context.

**Significance**: The framework provides a promising new approach for modeling memory in biologically plausible networks. It could inspire future work on neural circuit modeling and potentially inform experimental studies of memory mechanisms. The demonstration of graded activity patterns and E/I balance in memory recall represents a meaningful step beyond binary models.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of computational neuroscience by providing a novel, biologically plausible framework for analog memory storage. The technical approach is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the significance of the work is clear. However, the lack of connection to local learning rules and insufficient exploration of scalability represent important limitations that would need to be addressed for broader impact.

**Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions**

**Suggested Revisions**:
1. Expand the discussion of scalability by analyzing how storage capacity changes with network size or number of memories.
2. Provide more concrete suggestions for how the optimization could be implemented through local learning rules (even if speculative), addressing the gap between the algorithmic approach and biological plausibility.
3. Include a brief discussion of how the rate-based model might translate to spiking implementations, perhaps with a simple simulation example.
4. Clarify the implications of the non-zero ‖dv/dt‖ at fixed points for larger networks or more memories.

The paper is strong in its core contribution and makes a meaningful advance toward biologically plausible memory models. With the suggested revisions to address the scalability and biological plausibility gaps, it would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.
