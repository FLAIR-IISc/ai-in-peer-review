Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct rate-based recurrent networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations that store multiple analog (graded) fixed-point memories. The network enforces Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization of synaptic weights, uses a rectified quadratic nonlinearity, and includes constant external inputs. The authors define a composite objective to (i) make desired patterns fixed points by minimizing the norm of the time derivative at those states, and (ii) enforce local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target pattern. Both the weights and the inhibitory neurons’ steady-state activities for each attractor are learned using gradient-based optimization (L-BFGS). On a 150-neuron network (100 E, 50 I) with 30 stored patterns (capacity 0.2), the authors report robust recall from noisy cues, ongoing-noise robustness, E/I balance across attractors, and an emergent sparse-like distribution of synaptic weights. They also show that pruning small weights degrades only one attractor.

Relation to prior work
This work is positioned against Hopfield-type models (binary or effectively binary recall), LIF implementations that rely on saturation, and spatial bump networks. The incorporation of Dale’s law, graded patterns, and non-saturating transfer, together with explicit stability optimization via SSA, is the claimed advance. Related control-theoretic approaches for robust stability (Vanbiervliet) and prior work on balanced networks and stabilized supralinear networks are cited. However, comparisons to established baselines with similar constraints (e.g., balanced E/I rate networks trained for fixed points, FORCE/other RNN training with Dale constraints, or low-rank stabilized attractor constructions) are not provided.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting memory storage as joint fixed-point and stability optimization is clean and well-motivated. Using SSA is a principled, differentiable surrogate to SA.
- Biological constraints: explicit Dale’s law, E/I separation, graded firing rates, low-rate operation via dynamic E/I balance, and demonstration of balance across attractors.
- Empirical demonstrations: recall from noisy cues; variability quenching under ongoing noise; qualitative sparsity of weight distributions; partial robustness to weight pruning.
- Potentially general methodology: the framework could be reused for other structured fixed points or control objectives.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Fixed points vs “slow points”: The method does not enforce dv/dt=0 exactly; the authors admit learned states are “slow points” and claim an exact fixed point lies nearby. This should be verified rigorously (e.g., Newton solver from learned targets to find true fixed points; report residual norms and Jacobian spectra at those points). As is, the central claim (stable fixed points) is not demonstrated conclusively.
- Use of inhibitory steady-state variables as free, pattern-specific parameters: Treating v_inh^μ as m×n_I free variables greatly increases degrees of freedom and may make storage substantially easier than in a biologically plausible setting, where inhibitory activity must be a deterministic function of the learned weights and excitatory inputs. This needs stronger justification and ablations (e.g., learn weights only; or constrain inhibitory targets to be predicted by a parametric function of excitatory patterns).
- Limited validation and scaling: Results are shown for a single network size (n=150) and one capacity point (0.2). No scaling of capacity with network size, no basin-of-attraction quantification, no capacity comparison to established baselines with E/I constraints, and no systematic sensitivity to ε, η_s, η_F, or initialization. Without these, it is difficult to assess robustness, generality, and significance.
- Sparse connectivity claim: While weight magnitudes are peaked near zero, connectivity appears dense; no explicit sparse topology is enforced. The claim of “sparse synaptic connections” is therefore not substantiated as a structural property (as opposed to small weights). Please clarify and, if desired, train with an explicit sparsity mask and report results.
- Pruning analysis: Success criterion is relaxed (distance threshold increased) post-pruning, which confounds interpretation. The pruning threshold and “silent synapse” definition are ad hoc. A more principled analysis (fixed success criterion; pruning curves; random vs magnitude pruning; effect on spectra) is needed.
- Comparisons and baselines: No empirical comparisons to: (i) RNN training methods achieving fixed points under Dale constraints; (ii) stabilized supralinear network constructions of graded attractors; (iii) Hopfield-type graded models with explicit inhibition. An “ideal observer” upper bound is helpful, but not a substitute for algorithmic baselines.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Key hyperparameters (Table 1) and figures are referenced but not provided here; no algorithmic pseudocode or complexity analysis; no report of multiple random seeds; no ablation removing the SSA term; no analysis of SSA vs SA gap across patterns. Several references are incomplete.

Quality
Technically interesting but several central claims are insufficiently supported. The stability claim hinges on SSA and approximate fixed points; there is no rigorous verification of true fixed points and their basins. The reliance on per-attractor inhibitory targets inflates the parameterization and weakens the biological and algorithmic significance.

Clarity
The narrative is generally clear, but reproducibility is hindered by missing tables/figures and incomplete references. The paper would benefit from algorithmic details (objective gradients, optimization schedule, complexity) and a clearer statement of constraints actually enforced (Dale yes; structural sparsity unclear).

Originality
Combining SSA-based robust stability optimization with Dale-constrained, graded-memory embedding is a novel and interesting synthesis. However, the paper does not sufficiently contrast against related E/I-constrained training and modern RNN methods.

Significance
Potentially useful for constructing biologically constrained attractor networks, but current empirical scope and lack of strong baselines limit impact. Capacity 0.2 at n=150 is modest, and scalability is unaddressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Verify true fixed points: numerically solve for fixed points near targets, report residuals and SA/SSA; quantify basins (e.g., volume estimates vs σ).
- Remove or justify auxiliary inhibitory variables: show results when inhibitory targets are not free per attractor, or constrained by a learned mapping from excitatory patterns.
- Provide scaling analyses: capacity vs N, m/N curves, and sensitivity to ε and regularization.
- Add baselines: E/I-constrained RNN training to fixed points; SSN-based attractor networks; classical graded Hopfield variants with inhibition.
- Structural sparsity: enforce a sparse mask and report whether the method still succeeds; compare to L1-regularized training.
- Pruning: keep success criterion fixed; provide pruning curves and spectral analyses pre/post pruning.
- Reproducibility: include all hyperparameters, pseudocode, seeds, and code.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is promising and the control-theoretic angle is appealing, but the empirical validation is too limited, key claims (exact stable fixed points) are not convincingly demonstrated, and the use of pattern-specific inhibitory targets raises questions about plausibility and difficulty. With stronger validation, scaling analyses, and fair baselines, this could become a solid contribution.
