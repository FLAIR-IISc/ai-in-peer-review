- Proposes a control-theoretic framework using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to build Dale-constrained recurrent rate networks that store multiple analog fixed-point memories.  
- Neurons use rectified power-law input–output functions; training jointly optimizes synapses (softplus reparam.) and per-memory inhibitory steady-states with a quasi-Newton optimizer.  
- Objective combines squared velocity at targets (approximate fixed points), SSA of Jacobians (local stability), and L2 weight penalty.  
- Empirical demonstration on N=150 (≈100 E, 50 I) embedding m=30 analog patterns plus baseline uniform state.  
- Shows autoassociative recall from noisy cues with performance comparable to an ideal-observer nearest-neighbor baseline.  
- Demonstrates robustness to ongoing noise and trial-to-trial stimulus-onset variability reduction consistent with cortical data.  
- Networks exhibit tight dynamic E/I balance across attractors and emergent heavy-tailed weight distributions with many small-magnitude synapses.  
- Clipping weak weights largely preserves recall performance.  
- Conceptual novelty: principled use of robust stability optimization (SSA) to enforce local asymptotic stability under Dale’s law.  
- Biological plausibility: separate E/I populations, supralinear rectified gains, low firing rates without explicit saturation.  
- Practicality: SSA objective is differentiable and compatible with gradient-based methods; ε-scaling heuristic is reasonable and reported.  
- Limited capacity/scaling analysis: results restricted to N=150, m=30 (capacity ~0.2) with little exploration of scaling or dependence on memory statistics.  
- Fixed-point verification incomplete: velocity minimized but not driven to zero; no systematic Newton solver or bounds on distance to exact fixed points.  
- Spurious attractors and basin structure not characterized beyond occasional non-target convergence and recall success plots.  
- Sensitivity and hyperparameter dependence (ε, ηs, ηF, initialization) not analyzed; no ablation to show SSA necessity versus simpler proxies.  
- Sparsity claim overstated: L2 regularizer does not induce true structural sparsity and “silent” synapses use an arbitrary threshold.  
- Learning plausibility limited: training is global/offline with per-memory inhibitory auxiliaries, lacking a biologically plausible local learning rule.  
- Comparisons limited: only an ideal-observer cue classifier shown; no comparison to alternative Dale-constrained design methods or spiking implementations.  
- Technically sound as a constructive design method; SSA use is well motivated and empirical claims are broadly supported by experiments.  
- Clarity is good overall but some details (hyperparameters, runtime, SSA gradient computation, incomplete refs) are missing or deferred.  
- Originality high: novel application of robust control tools to design analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks.  
- Significance moderate-to-high as a methodological contribution if shown to scale and transfer to spiking networks.  
- Suggested improvements: scaling experiments, explicit fixed-point verification and basin quantification, sensitivity/ablation studies, stronger sparsity controls, spiking transfer tests, comparisons to alternative methods, and code/hyperparameter release.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, principled stability-based construction with clear demonstrations of recall, balance, robustness, and variability quenching.  
- Rejection arguments: limited scope on scaling and basins, no explicit fixed-point verification, overstated sparsity, and limited biological learning plausibility.  
- Overall recommendation: leaning toward weak accept as a solid proof-of-concept contingent on strengthening scaling, fixed-point verification, and claims about sparsity and robustness.
