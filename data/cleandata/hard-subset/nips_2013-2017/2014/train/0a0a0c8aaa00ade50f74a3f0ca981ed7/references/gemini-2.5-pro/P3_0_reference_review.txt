### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing recurrent neural networks that can store and recall multiple analog (graded-activity) memory patterns. The authors' primary contribution is to design a network that respects several key biological constraints simultaneously: Dale's law (separate excitatory and inhibitory populations), low firing rates without artificial saturation, and dynamic balance between excitation and inhibition. The core of their method is a control-theoretic approach to memory storage. They formalize the problem as an optimization task with two main objectives for each desired memory pattern: 1) ensuring the pattern is a fixed point of the network dynamics (i.e., time derivatives of neuron potentials are zero), and 2) ensuring this fixed point is dynamically stable. To tackle the challenging stability constraint, they leverage a recently developed technique from robust control theory, the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), which provides a differentiable upper bound on the largest real part of the Jacobian's eigenvalues. By minimizing a cost function that incorporates both the fixed-point and SSA stability terms, they optimize the network's synaptic weights and the activity patterns of the inhibitory neurons.

The authors demonstrate that this procedure successfully produces a network of 100 excitatory and 50 inhibitory neurons capable of storing 30 distinct graded patterns. The resulting network exhibits robust recall from noisy initial cues, with performance comparable to an "ideal observer." Importantly, the optimization leads to several emergent, biologically realistic properties: the learned synaptic weight distribution is sparse with a long tail, the network operates in a tightly balanced E/I regime, and it replicates the phenomenon of stimulus-induced variability quenching, which has been widely observed in cortical recordings.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Powerful Methodology:** The application of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to stabilize attractor states in a neural network is a significant methodological innovation. This approach elegantly sidesteps the non-differentiability of the standard spectral abscissa, allowing for efficient, gradient-based optimization of stability. This provides a principled way to construct stable networks without relying on common but less realistic mechanisms like hard saturation of firing rates.

2.  **High Degree of Biological Realism:** The model successfully integrates a combination of biological constraints that are rarely addressed together in theoretical work on attractor networks. The adherence to Dale's law, the achievement of stable, low firing rates through dynamic E/I balance, and the emergence of sparse connectivity are all major strengths that bring the model closer to cortical reality than classic models (e.g., Hopfield) or even more recent spiking network models that often rely on global inhibition or binary states.

3.  **Important Emergent Properties:** A compelling aspect of the work is that key features like synaptic sparsity and E/I balance are not explicitly forced by the optimization procedure (beyond a simple L2 regularizer) but emerge as a consequence of optimizing for stable analog memory storage. This suggests a deep functional link between these biological features and the computational requirements of robust memory.

4.  **Connection to Experimental Neuroscience:** The demonstration that the network exhibits stimulus-induced variability quenching is a strong point. This connects the model's dynamics directly to a well-documented cortical phenomenon, adding to its explanatory power and relevance for understanding brain function.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Lack of a Biologically Plausible Learning Mechanism:** The primary weakness is that the learning process is entirely non-local and algorithmic. The optimization is performed by a global, offline algorithm (L-BFGS) that has access to the full network state, connectivity matrix, and Jacobian for all memory patterns. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the discussion, but it is a critical one. The work provides a powerful "proof of concept" of what *can* be learned but offers no insight into *how* it could be learned by local, synaptic plasticity rules. This makes it more of a normative/engineering solution than a descriptive model of biological learning.

2.  **The Role of Inhibitory Neurons:** The treatment of inhibitory neuron firing rates (`vµinh`) as free parameters to be optimized for each memory is a clever simplification that makes the problem tractable. However, it is biologically questionable. It implies that for each memory, the network "knows" the precise target firing rate for every inhibitory neuron. A more realistic model would have these rates emerge from the learned E-I and I-I connectivity. This choice reduces the complexity of the optimization but sidesteps a fundamental aspect of how inhibitory circuits self-organize.

3.  **Scalability and Capacity Analysis:** The paper presents a single, successful example with N=150 neurons and m=30 memories. While this is a non-trivial result, the study would be strengthened by a more systematic analysis of the model's limitations. How does the optimization performance scale with the number of neurons (N) and memories (m)? Is there a clear capacity limit (e.g., p/N_E) beyond which the algorithm fails to find a stable solution? How sensitive is the solution to the hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε)?

### **Detailed Comments**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of the problem is sound, and the use of the SSA is appropriate and well-motivated. The experiments conducted are relevant and convincingly support the main claims. The analysis of the resulting network properties (weight distribution, E/I balance) is thorough and insightful.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly lays out the limitations of previous models and motivates the current approach. The methods section provides a clear, step-by-step description of the model and the optimization objective. The figures are clean, informative, and effectively illustrate the key results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the long tradition of attractor network models, its core methodological contribution—using robust control theory (SSA) to directly optimize for the stability of graded patterns in a Dale's Law-abiding network—is novel. It represents a significant departure from previous approaches that relied on statistical mechanics, mean-field theory, or specific architectural motifs (like bump attractors). It clearly distinguishes itself from related work by simultaneously addressing graded patterns, E/I separation, and stability without rate saturation.

**Significance:** The paper is highly significant for the field of computational neuroscience and potentially for neuromorphic engineering. It provides a powerful new normative framework for understanding the functional principles of memory circuits in the brain. It suggests that complex properties like E/I balance and sparse connectivity may be necessary consequences of the need to robustly store analog information. The framework could inspire new directions in research aimed at finding local learning rules that can approximate this global optimization objective. The work advances the state of the art by providing a concrete, functional model of an analog memory system that is more biologically constrained than most of its predecessors.
