Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes to construct recurrent rate networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations (Dale’s law), whose dynamics admit a set of prescribed analog activity patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points. Neurons use a rectified quadratic nonlinearity. The authors optimize synaptic weights and (crucially) the inhibitory steady-state activities associated with each memory to (i) drive the time-derivative dv/dt to zero at each target and (ii) enforce local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian around each target, plus L2 weight regularization. They demonstrate recall from noisy cues, robustness to ongoing white noise, emergent E/I balance at attractors, and that many small weights can be pruned with limited performance loss. Experiments are on small networks (n=150; ~100 E, 50 I) with m=30 memories (capacity ≈0.2 per E neuron, if counted that way), patterns drawn from a log-normal firing-rate distribution. They compare recall to an “ideal observer” that returns the nearest stored pattern.

The work relates to Hopfield-style attractor networks, extensions with graded responses, LIF networks with inhibition, and balanced networks. Enforcing Dale’s law and using a supralinear gain follows stabilized supralinear network (SSN) ideas. The novelty is the control-theoretic use of SSA for multi-attractor stabilization under Dale constraints, building on [Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.] but applied here to storage of multiple analog memories.

Strengths
- Clear, principled objective for embedding multiple graded attractors: fixed-point residual + robust local stability via SSA.
- Dale’s law enforced; no saturation invoked—the networks rely on dynamically balanced E/I, consistent with balanced network literature.
- Empirical demonstrations of recall robustness, E/I input correlations across attractors, and across-trial variability quenching upon stimulus onset.
- Interesting observation that many small weights can be pruned without catastrophic loss.
- The control-theoretic lens (SSA) is potentially useful and differentiable, offering a practical route to stability-constrained design.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Degrees of freedom and per-memory inhibitory variables: The optimization treats the inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ as free parameters per memory, in addition to all weights. This substantially inflates the parameter count (n(n−1) + n_I m) relative to constraints, raising concerns that capacity/performance are driven by over-parameterization rather than a principled storage mechanism. A control showing performance without per-memory inhibitory free variables (or with constrained inhibitory statistics) is needed.
- Modest capacity and limited scaling analysis: With n≈150 and m=30, capacity is small, and there is no systematic study of capacity vs N, E/I ratio, pattern statistics, or regularization. No theoretical capacity bounds or basin size quantification are provided.
- Fixed points not actually satisfied: The training often leaves nonzero dv/dt at target states; the claim that “an exact stable fixed point exists nearby” is not demonstrated rigorously. Stability and recall are assessed at the target rather than at the actual fixed point found by the dynamics.
- Evaluation limited and lacks established baselines: The only baseline is an ideal nearest-neighbor “observer.” There is no quantitative comparison to existing Dale-constrained attractor constructions (e.g., SSN-based fixed-point embedding, LIF networks with feedback inhibition) or graded-memory models. No ablations on SSA vs SA, ε sensitivity, or the weight regularizer.
- Biological plausibility gaps: While Dale’s law is enforced and weight sparsity emerges, the approach is an offline, nonlocal global optimization with memory-specific inhibitory variables. No local learning rules or data-driven validation are provided, and generalization to spiking networks is asserted but not demonstrated.
- Robustness and generality: Results are shown for a single network size and one log-normal pattern distribution. Ongoing noise is simplistic (white Gaussian). No tests of parameter noise, heterogeneity in time constants, or spurious attractor incidence.
- Reporting issues: Important details (Table 1) and supplementary derivations are referenced but not included. Several references are incomplete. Reproducibility (code, exact hyperparameters) is unclear.

Quality
Technically competent in optimization and stability assessment; however, key claims (true fixed points, capacity, basin sizes) are not supported by theory or comprehensive experiments. The reliance on per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom is a central concern for the validity of storage claims.

Clarity
Generally well written; the control-theoretic setup is explained clearly. Missing tables/supplementary details and incomplete references hinder reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Using SSA for stability-constrained design in neural dynamics is known in related contexts; applying it to graded attractor storage under Dale’s law is a reasonable extension. The per-memory inhibitory optimization is unconventional but raises concerns rather than adding clear novelty.

Significance
Potentially interesting for theoretical neuroscience and for cross-fertilization with robust control, but current validation is too limited to conclude practical impact. Without capacity scaling, basin characterization, or comparisons to established E/I attractor models, significance is uncertain.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Remove or strongly constrain the per-memory inhibitory variables and report how capacity and recall change.
- Provide capacity scaling with N and E/I ratio, and quantify basin sizes and spurious attractors.
- Compare against established Dale-constrained attractor constructions and SSN-based fixed-point embeddings; include LIF simulations using the learned weights if claiming spiking relevance.
- Report sensitivity to ε in SSA, η_s, η_F, and the effect of hitting dv/dt≈0 exactly.
- Test different pattern distributions and structured patterns; analyze robustness to parameter perturbations and heterogeneity.
- Include all hyperparameters, optimization details, and code for reproducibility; complete references.

Pros
- Principled stability objective; neat use of SSA.
- Dale’s law respected; balanced E/I dynamics shown.
- Demonstrates recall of analog patterns and variability quenching.

Cons
- Over-parameterized (per-memory inhibitory latents).
- Limited capacity and no scaling theory/experiments.
- No comparative baselines beyond an oracle nearest-neighbor.
- Fixed points not actually attained; basin sizes uncharacterized.
- Reproducibility and reporting gaps.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Interesting direction, but the evaluation is insufficient and the reliance on per-memory inhibitory parameters undermines the central claim. Strengthening the empirical and theoretical case could make this a valuable contribution.
