Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to build recurrent rate-based networks with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations (respecting Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) activity patterns as stable fixed points. Neurons have a rectified power-law input–output function without an explicit saturation. The authors jointly optimize all synaptic weights (via a softplus reparameterization to enforce Dale) and the steady-state inhibitory activities associated with each memory. The objective minimizes: (i) the squared velocity at each target state (to enforce approximate fixed points), (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target (a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa, to enforce local asymptotic stability), and (iii) an L2 weight penalty. Optimization uses a quasi-Newton method.

Empirically (N=150, with ~100 E and ~50 I), they embed m=30 analog patterns (plus a baseline uniform state), show autoassociative recall from noisy cues with performance comparable to an “ideal observer” nearest-neighbor baseline, demonstrate robustness to ongoing noise, tight dynamic E/I balance across attractors, and an emergent heavy-tailed weight distribution with many small-magnitude synapses. Clipping weak weights mostly preserves recall. The network also exhibits stimulus-onset variability reduction across trials, consistent with cortical observations.

This work builds on and seeks to remedy limitations of Hopfield-type models (symmetric weights; typically binary memories), graded-response variants with optimistic but hard-to-realize capacities, and LIF-based E/I networks where stability often relies on saturation or global inhibition. It is related to balanced network theory and variability quenching in cortex, and is novel in leveraging robust control tools (SSA) to construct stable analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Clear and principled use of robust stability optimization (SSA) to enforce local asymptotic stability of multiple analog attractors under Dale’s law and with realistic rate nonlinearities.
- Biological plausibility constraints: Separate E/I populations, rectified supralinear gain, balanced E/I dynamics, low firing rates without saturating nonlinearities.
- Solid empirical demonstrations: Recall vs. cue noise against an ideal-observer upper bound; robustness to ongoing noise; variability quenching; E/I input correlations across attractors; weight distributions reminiscent of cortical data.
- Practicality: The SSA-based objective is differentiable and compatible with standard gradient methods; the heuristic scaling of ε is reasonable and clearly reported.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Capacity and scaling: Results are presented for N=150 and m=30 (capacity ~0.2) with little exploration of scaling with network size, number of memories, or memory statistics. This makes it hard to assess generality and how the SSA margin and recall basins scale.
- Fixed points vs. slow points: The method minimizes the velocity but does not make it exactly zero; claims that nearby exact fixed points exist are plausible but not quantified. No systematic verification (e.g., Newton solver to locate exact fixed points) or bounds on the distance from targets are provided.
- Spurious attractors and basins: The approach enforces local stability at targets but does not analyze spurious attractors, saddle structures, or basin volumes beyond recall success plots. Occasional convergence to non-target attractors is reported but not characterized.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: Performance and stability likely depend on ε in the SSA, ηs, ηF, initialization, and the balance of terms; there is no sensitivity analysis or ablation (e.g., SSA vs. spectral norm proxies) to show the necessity of SSA.
- Claimed sparsity: The L2 regularizer does not promote true structural sparsity; “silent” synapses are defined by an arbitrary magnitude threshold. Many connections remain nonzero, and structural sparsity (binary connectivity) is not enforced. Claims about sparsity should be tempered or supported by explicit sparsity-inducing penalties and connectivity statistics.
- Biological plausibility of learning: The optimization is global and offline, with inhibitory steady-state activities treated as per-memory auxiliary variables during training; no local/plausible learning rule is provided. While posed as a constructive proof-of-principle, this limits immediate neurobiological interpretability.
- Comparisons: Beyond the ideal-observer cue classifier, there is no comparison to alternative network-design methods (e.g., pseudo-inverse learning with E/I constraints, contraction/Lyapunov-based constructions, other stability surrogates) or to spiking implementations.

Quality
Technically sound as a constructive design method; the use of SSA is appropriate and well-motivated. Empirical evidence supports claims of stable recall, E/I balance, and robustness. However, capacity/scaling, existence of exact fixed points, and sensitivity analyses are underdeveloped, and the “emergent sparsity” claim is overstated without structural sparsity controls.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The modeling choices are explained, and figures (as described) are informative. Some details are missing or deferred (hyperparameters in Table 1, runtime/complexity, exact SSA/gradient computation details). Several references are incomplete; please fix bibliographic entries.

Originality
High: bringing robust control (SSA) to the explicit construction of analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks is novel and interesting for NeurIPS’ computational neuroscience community.

Significance
Moderate to high as a methodological contribution. If shown to scale and to transfer to spiking networks, this could become a useful tool for building realistic memory circuits and neuromorphic designs. The present empirical scope is modest.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide scaling experiments: capacity vs. N, memory statistics, and stability margins; report computational complexity and runtimes.
- Verify fixed points explicitly (e.g., Newton–Raphson from targets) and quantify distances and basin sizes; report spurious attractors if present.
- Sensitivity/ablation: vary ε, ηs, ηF; compare SSA to simpler proxies; show why SSA is necessary.
- Clarify the “sparsity” claim; consider L1/group sparsity or explicit structural sparsity constraints and report connection probabilities.
- Test transfer to spiking LIF networks with matched gain; report recall performance and E/I balance.
- Compare to alternative design pipelines under Dale constraints.
- Release code and hyperparameters; complete and correct references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled stability-based construction under realistic constraints.
- Clear demonstrations of analog recall, balance, robustness, and variability quenching.
- Potentially impactful methodological bridge between control theory and neural circuit design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scope and scaling; capacity and basin analyses are thin.
- No explicit verification of exact fixed points or study of spurious attractors.
- Overstated sparsity and limited comparisons/baselines.
- Biological learning plausibility not addressed beyond a high-level discussion.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward weak accept as a solid proof-of-concept methodological paper with clear novelty and promising results, contingent on strengthening scaling analyses, fixed-point verification, and clarifying claims about sparsity and robustness.
