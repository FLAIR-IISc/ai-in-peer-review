Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a recurrent network that respects key biological constraints: separate excitatory/inhibitory populations (Dale’s law), recurrent sparse connectivity, realistic non-saturating rate nonlinearities, low rates, and dynamic E/I balance. The network dynamics are rate-based with a rectified-quadratic transfer function. The authors directly optimize the synaptic weights (constrained via a sign-respecting reparameterization) and the steady-state inhibitory activities for each memory, to (i) make each target state a fixed point (by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at that state), and (ii) ensure local stability (by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). They demonstrate recall performance, robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise, balanced E/I inputs, emergent sparsity of effective synapses, and variability quenching after stimulus onset. The approach is related to classical and modern memory models (Hopfield-type networks, balanced networks), but differs by using robust control tools (SSA) to stabilize graded memories in E/I networks without saturating nonlinearities.

Quality
- Soundness: The use of SSA as a differentiable surrogate for the spectral abscissa is appropriate; ensuring α̃ε ≤ 0 is a sufficient condition for local asymptotic stability. The fixed-point condition is handled by minimizing the velocity at targets, which in practice produces “slow points.” The authors claim that true fixed points exist nearby and that dynamics converge; the recall experiments support this, but a more direct check (e.g., root-finding from the target initialization, or showing SA < 0 and dv/dt ≈ 0 at the converged equilibrium) would strengthen the claim. The optimization includes a regularizer and a heuristic ε scaling with n; this is reasonable but could use sensitivity analyses.
- Empirics: The main demonstration uses n=150 and m=30 (capacity ~0.2) and shows recall-vs-noise curves that track an ideal observer, ongoing-noise robustness, and balanced E/I. The pruning experiment suggests many small weights are dispensable. These are compelling demonstrations. However, there is no scaling analysis of capacity, basins of attraction, or computational complexity with N, E/I ratio, sparsity, or pattern statistics. Given the longstanding importance of capacity and basins in associative memory, this is a notable omission.
- Assumptions: Inhibitory steady-state rates are optimized per memory alongside weights. This is justified because the inhibitory units are part of the dynamics; nevertheless, it inflates the parameter count by nI×m and highlights the gap to local learning rules (which authors discuss). External inputs h are fixed and memory-independent, which keeps recall cue-based. The non-saturating nonlinearity is realistic under balanced operation; stability enforcement via SSA is what prevents blowup.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a coherent narrative linking objectives, methods, and results. Some details are deferred to a table/supplement (e.g., full hyperparameters ηs, ηF, nE/nI, τi, h, SSA gradient and implementation details). For reproducibility, please provide:
- Exact parameter settings and initialization distributions (means per synapse class, τi, h).
- SSA computation and ε selection details, plus sensitivity to ε and ηs/ηF.
- Code or pseudocode for the optimization loop and termination criteria.
References need editorial cleanup (missing years/volume info).

Originality
Using robust control (SSA) to directly co-optimize fixed points and local stability of multiple graded attractors in an E/I network that obeys Dale’s law and operates in a balanced regime is novel relative to prior memory models. Prior balanced memory models typically relied on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibition and effectively binary patterns; here graded patterns with non-saturating gain are stabilized via learned E/I feedback matching per attractor. The Dale reparameterization is known, but the combination with SSA-based stability optimization is new and of interest.

Significance
The work offers a principled, constructive way to build biologically constrained analog attractor networks with robust recall and balanced E/I, and it reproduces variability quenching phenomena. This bridges control theory and computational neuroscience and could inform both theory and engineered recurrent systems. The absence of capacity scaling, basin characterization, and spiking instantiations limits immediate impact; nonetheless, the framework is promising and likely to spur follow-up work.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of storage as joint fixed-point and stability optimization.
- Biologically grounded constraints (E/I, Dale, balanced state; low-rate operation).
- Robust control tool (SSA) used effectively; differentiable stability objective.
- Empirical demonstrations: recall vs noise; ongoing noise robustness; variability quenching; emergent sparsity and pruning robustness.
- Thoughtful discussion of extensions and biological plausibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- No systematic capacity scaling or basin size analysis; results shown for a single modest-sized setting.
- Fixed-point satisfaction is approximate; a more direct verification of true fixed points would be valuable.
- Optimization includes per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables (nI×m parameters), raising questions about scalability and biological learnability; no local learning rule provided.
- Limited analysis of failure modes (spurious attractors, interference between memories).
- Reproducibility requires more implementation detail; references are incomplete.

Questions for authors
- How does capacity scale with N, E/I ratio, sparsity of connectivity, and pattern statistics? What are basin sizes as a function of noise level?
- How sensitive are results to ε in SSA, ηs/ηF, and regularization strength? Do different ε alter convergence and stability guarantees in practice?
- Can you directly locate and report the actual fixed points after training (dv/dt ≈ 0) and their SA? Are there spurious attractors?
- How much of the emergent sparsity depends on the L2 term vs stability optimization? What happens if ηF→0?
- Do results hold with heterogeneous τi/h and with different nonlinearities (e.g., rectified power p≠2)?
- Can the trained rate connectivity transfer to an LIF spiking network with matched static gain and preserve recall?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled approach; addresses biologically realistic constraints.
- Strong conceptual contribution linking robust control and memory networks.
- Convincing demonstrations of balanced analog recall and robustness.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of scaling/capacity analysis and basin characterization.
- Approximate fixed-point satisfaction without direct verification.
- Heavy reliance on global optimization with many parameters; limited biological plausibility of the learning mechanism.
- Reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological contribution and biological relevance are compelling, and the results are solid at the demonstrated scale. Addressing fixed-point verification, adding capacity/basin analyses, and improving implementation details would strengthen the paper further.
