Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable attractors in a recurrent rate-based network that respects key biological constraints: separate excitatory and inhibitory populations (Dale’s law), sparse recurrent synapses, low firing rates without saturation, and dynamically balanced E/I inputs. The network uses a threshold-quadratic nonlinearity without an upper bound; stability is achieved not by saturation but via inhibition balancing excitation. Memories are encoded as fixed points with local stability: the authors minimize, for each target pattern, the squared velocity at that state to enforce a fixed point, and the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to enforce robust local stability. Weights are parameterized to enforce Dale’s law via a softplus transform. The cost also includes an L2 regularizer and includes the steady-state inhibitory potentials as auxiliary variables to be optimized per memory. Using L-BFGS, the authors show recall with good robustness to noise, sparse learned weights, tight E/I balance across attractors, and variability quenching upon stimulus onset.

Relative to classic Hopfield-style models (largely binary, symmetric weights) and subsequent graded-rate models often lacking explicit inhibition or relying on saturating nonlinearities, this work provides a constructive routine that jointly enforces fixed points and stability under Dale’s law. It also relates to balanced network theory and prior “balanced memory” work that typically stored binary patterns with global inhibition. The use of SSA from robust control to optimize stability in neural memory networks appears novel. The demonstrations connect to literature on variability reduction in cortex during stimulus onset.

Quality
- Soundness: The optimization objective directly targets the fixed-point condition (via small dv/dt) and local stability (via SSA). The choice of SSA is appropriate and practically differentiable; the authors also track the true spectral abscissa (SA) empirically, which helps substantiate stability claims. Still, since dv/dt remains non-zero at targets, the paper relies on the existence of nearby stable fixed points inferred from dynamics; this could be made more rigorous by solving for fixed points post-optimization (e.g., root-finding) and reporting their distances to targets.
- Empirical support: The paper shows successful storage and recall for n=150 and m=30 (capacity roughly 0.2 by their definition), robustness to cue noise and to ongoing dynamical noise, emergent weight sparsity and strong E/I balance. The “ideal observer” baseline is a reasonable upper bound for a nearest-neighbor decoder.
- Limitations and missing analyses: Capacity and scaling are not systematically quantified (e.g., m versus n, coding level, E/I ratio). Computational complexity and runtime of SSA-based training are not discussed; SSA computations can become expensive for large n. Stability basins are not characterized beyond recall success probabilities. The role of optimizing inhibitory steady states per attractor should be clarified (are these only used for training Jacobians, or do they reflect the actual steady-state inhibitory rates that the network dynamics reach at recall?). There is no spiking validation; although the authors argue the gain nonlinearity is appropriate, a concrete spiking instantiation would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear and well-organized. The method is described at a level that an expert could implement, though some details are referred to a Supplement (e.g., initial mean-field parameter setting). Several hyperparameters are specified heuristically; a short sensitivity analysis would help. Some reference entries are incomplete or poorly formatted. Define precisely the notion of “capacity” used, and report nE/nI explicitly.

Originality
The combination of (i) enforcing Dale’s law with a biologically plausible nonlinearity that does not rely on saturation, (ii) optimizing for both fixed-pointness and robust stability via SSA, and (iii) demonstrating graded analog memories with balanced E/I is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior graded memory models either lacked explicit inhibition or relied on saturation/global inhibition; balanced memory models typically targeted binary patterns. Importing SSA from robust control into neural memory network design is a fresh and potentially useful idea.

Significance
If scalable, this framework offers a practical way to synthesize biologically plausible analog attractor networks with robust recall and E/I balance, which could impact both computational neuroscience and dynamical systems approaches to memory. The present empirical scope is modest (n=150) with limited capacity analysis; more systematic scaling would increase significance. The work may also inspire local/plasticity-based approximations to the presented objective.

Pros
- Addresses multiple biological constraints simultaneously (Dale’s law, E/I balance, low rates) without saturating nonlinearities.
- Uses a principled stability objective (SSA) with practical gradients; shows SA tracking.
- Demonstrates robust recall under cue and ongoing noise; variability quenching is compelling.
- Emergent sparsity and analysis of functional impact of small weights.
- Clear articulation of the control-theoretic perspective.

Cons
- No systematic capacity and scaling analysis; unclear how performance scales with n and m.
- Global, nonlocal optimization with per-attractor inhibitory variables; biological plausibility and online learnability left open.
- Fixed points not exactly enforced; reliance on “nearby” attractors not quantified.
- No spiking validation; limited comparisons to alternative E/I models beyond narrative.
- Computational cost and convergence reliability not characterized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a capacity study varying n, nE/nI, m, and pattern statistics; report scaling of SSA-optimized stability and recall.
- Explicitly compute the nearest true fixed point after training and quantify its distance to the target; report SA at that point.
- Clarify how per-attractor inhibitory steady states are used during training versus recall, and show that the learned network dynamics reach those inhibitory rates without extra control.
- Report computational complexity, wall-clock time, and convergence reliability across random initializations.
- Include an ablation: train without SSA (only dv/dt term), or with global inhibition only, to highlight the contribution of robust stability optimization and E/I structure.
- Consider a spiking instantiation (even small-scale) with matched f–I curves to validate transferability.
- Improve reference formatting and consider adding classic balanced networks (van Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky), continuous attractor literature (e.g., neural integrators), and related stability-optimization work if any.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a novel and promising control-theoretic approach to building analog attractor networks under strong biological constraints, with solid demonstrations at modest scale. Addressing capacity/scaling and fixed-point verification would strengthen the case and broaden impact.
