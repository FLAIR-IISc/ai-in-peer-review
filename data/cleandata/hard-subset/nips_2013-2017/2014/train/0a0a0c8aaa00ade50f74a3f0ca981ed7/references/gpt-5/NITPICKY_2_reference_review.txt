Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an optimization-based framework to embed multiple graded (analog) attractor memories in a recurrent rate network with explicit excitatory and inhibitory populations and Dale’s law. Each memory is specified over excitatory neurons; inhibitory steady-state activities are treated as auxiliary variables to be optimized per memory. The network uses a threshold-quadratic nonlinearity without explicit saturation; stability is enforced by minimizing a composite objective: (i) squared drift at the target state to approximate the fixed-point condition, (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian to encourage local asymptotic stability, and (iii) an L2 weight penalty. Weights are reparameterized with a softplus and a fixed sign to enforce Dale’s law. Experiments on n=150 neurons (≈100 E, 50 I) with m=30 memories show recall robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, E/I balance at steady-state, and that clipping small weights has limited impact on recall except for one attractor. The authors relate to Hopfield-type models (binary vs graded), Dale-constrained spiking models, stabilized supralinear networks, and prior control-theoretic SSA work.

Strengths
- Addresses a longstanding gap: graded attractors with E/I separation and Dale’s law, without relying on saturating f–I curves, achieving low-rate, balanced states.
- Methodologically interesting: importing SSA-based robust stability optimization into memory embedding; explicit joint optimization of fixed-point proximity and stability.
- Empirical findings: recall probability vs noise approaches an “ideal observer” baseline; demonstration of E/I input correlation across memories and stimulus-driven variability quenching.
- Enforces Dale’s law with a smooth parameterization; basins of attraction assessed via recall success under substantial perturbations.
- Insight on connectivity: heavy-tailed weight magnitudes emerge without L1 sparsity; removing small weights largely preserves recall.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Inhibitory “auxiliary variables”: optimizing vinh per memory introduces nI×m additional degrees of freedom during training, potentially inflating capacity and reducing biological plausibility. In a real network, inhibitory rates must emerge from the dynamics given weights and E activity, not be free memory-specific parameters. A control where vinh is not optimized per memory (or is constrained by a learned inhibitory subnetwork) is needed to quantify how much this assumption contributes to success.
- Stability assessment: the objective uses SSA; the text reports SSA < 0 post-optimization but does not consistently report the true spectral abscissa (SA) being negative for all memories. Given SSA is only an upper bound with ε-dependent looseness, explicit SA values and sensitivity to ε are necessary. No link between SSA minimization and basin size is provided.
- Fixed-point approximation: dv/dt at target states remains nonzero; claims of “nearby exact fixed points” are qualitative. Please quantify the distance to the nearest fixed point and demonstrate that the linearization at that fixed point is stable (SA < 0).
- Capacity and scaling: only one configuration (n=150, m=30, “capacity” 0.2) is shown. No scaling with n, m, or parameter sweeps (ηs, ηF, ε, τi) are reported, nor statistics across multiple random seeds. This severely limits conclusions about generality and robustness.
- Structural sparsity: the introduction emphasizes sparse connectivity as a brain constraint, but the trained network is dense with many small-magnitude weights. Emergent near-zero weights are not true structural sparsity. A version with explicit sparsity constraints (L0/L1, fixed connection probability) or masked connectivity is needed to substantiate the claim.
- Clipping analysis: choosing |W|<0.01 as “silent” is arbitrary, and success thresholds are changed post hoc (0.001→0.08) to accommodate attractor shifts, confounding comparison. Show performance using a consistent criterion, vary the clipping threshold, and report which attractors fail and why.
- Noise robustness: only one noise level is shown; no systematic noise-vs-performance curves or time-to-recall analyses. Units of noise (“potential SD 0.33 in an isolated neuron”) are unclear relative to the dynamic range.
- Reproducibility: numerous key values are deferred to “Table 1/Supplementary” (τi, hi, ηs, ηF) without enough in-text detail to reproduce results. Algorithmic details (NLopt settings, initialization statistics per synapse class, stopping criteria) are under-specified.

Clarity/presentation
- Notational inconsistencies: v denotes “potentials” yet panel 1b mentions “inhibitory neuronal firing rates” when v is used; please disambiguate v vs r. The notation b·c+ (positive/negative parts) in the E/I input definitions is not defined.
- Figures: axes/units/labels are sometimes missing or unclear (e.g., dynamics timescales). The text occasionally contains typos and awkward formatting (e.g., “Hz m em or ie s”).
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/pages/venues, inconsistent capitalization). This is below NeurIPS standards.

Originality
- The use of SSA to stabilize targeted dynamics is not new per se (see Hennequin et al., Neuron 2014); applying it to graded memory embedding with Dale constraints is a reasonable extension, but the leap in novelty is moderate. The framing as a “general control-theoretic framework” feels stronger than what is demonstrated.

Significance
- Conceptually interesting, but empirical scope is narrow (single small network, limited ablations). No comparison to Dale-respecting graded-attractor baselines or to simpler global-inhibition alternatives is provided. Without capacity/scaling analyses or spiking instantiations, impact is limited.

Questions and requested clarifications
- Report SA (not only SSA) for all memories post-training; include sensitivity to ε.
- Quantify the contribution of optimizing vinh per memory: remove that degree of freedom or constrain it, and show performance.
- Provide scaling of recall performance vs m for fixed n, and vs n for fixed load; include multiple seeds.
- Add experiments with explicit structural sparsity constraints and masked connectivity.
- Use consistent success criteria across ablations; justify thresholds.
- Specify all hyperparameters and optimization details sufficient for reproduction.
- Provide time constants, stimulus amplitudes, and noise units relative to v and r dynamic ranges.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem: graded attractors with E/I balance under Dale’s law.
- Methodologically clean formulation linking fixed-point and stability optimization.
- Empirical evidence of balanced dynamics and variability quenching.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on per-memory inhibitory variables; unclear biological plausibility and contribution to capacity.
- Insufficient stability guarantees (SSA vs SA), limited analysis of basins.
- Sparse claims not supported by structurally sparse networks.
- Narrow experimental scope; no scaling, few ablations, limited baselines.
- Clarity and referencing issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the cumulative technical and presentation issues, limited empirical scope, and reliance on per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom make it premature for NeurIPS. A revised version addressing the above concerns could become a strong submission.
