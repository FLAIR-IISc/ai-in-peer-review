Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog attractor memories in a recurrent rate-based network that obeys Dale’s law and exhibits balanced excitation and inhibition. The network consists of excitatory and inhibitory units with a rectified-quadratic nonlinearity. Memory storage is framed as satisfying two conditions per target pattern: (i) making the target a fixed point (by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at that state), and (ii) ensuring local stability (by minimizing the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). Parameters optimized include the synaptic weights (constrained by Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization) and the steady-state inhibitory activities associated with each memory (used as auxiliary variables during optimization). The authors demonstrate recall robustness to noisy cues and to ongoing noise, show near-ideal-observer performance on recall probability versus cue noise, report emergent sparsity in the learned weights, E/I balance at steady state across attractors, and trial-to-trial variability reduction after stimulus onset. A representative network (n=150; ~100 E, 50 I) stores m=30 patterns (capacity ~0.2).

Relation to prior work: Classical Hopfield-type models rely on symmetric weights and often binary patterns. Prior graded-response studies derived high capacities but struggled with practical stability, and many omitted explicit inhibitory units or relied on global inhibition or saturation. Balanced E/I spiking and rate models have been used to stabilize dynamics, but often with effectively binary memories or fixed excitation levels. This work brings a robust-control tool (SSA) to directly optimize stability of analog attractors under Dale’s law and shows balanced dynamics without saturating neuronal transfer functions.

Strengths
- A clear, principled optimization formulation that jointly targets fixed-point consistency and local stability, with a differentiable stability surrogate (SSA) enabling gradient-based learning.
- Enforces Dale’s law throughout and demonstrates balanced E/I inputs that finely track across attractors, aligning with known cortical motifs.
- Empirical demonstrations of recall robustness to cue corruption and to ongoing noise; variability quenching after stimulus onset mirrors experimental observations.
- Emergent weight distributions with many small weights and heavy tails qualitatively resembling cortical synapses; analysis of recall after clipping small weights is informative.
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized; the methodology and goals are stated clearly.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validation against established baselines is limited. Comparison is only to an ideal-observer upper bound, not to strong model baselines that support graded memories under similar biological constraints (e.g., graded-rate attractor models with explicit inhibition, balanced memory networks, clustered networks). Without rigorous baselines and systematic ablations, it is hard to assess the advantage of SSA-based optimization beyond proof-of-concept.
- Capacity and scaling are not characterized. Only one configuration (n=150, m=30) is reported. No analysis of capacity versus network size, excitatory/inhibitory ratio, or pattern statistics; no comparison to known theoretical capacity bounds for graded patterns. Claims of overcoming prior limitations would be far stronger with scaling curves and sensitivity analyses.
- Fixed-point condition is not actually satisfied. The method minimizes ‖dv/dt‖ at target patterns but often leaves nonzero residual drift, while optimizing SSA at those same points. This does not, in principle, guarantee stability of any actual fixed point. The paper claims nearby true fixed points are induced, but this is not demonstrated rigorously. A constructive check (e.g., Newton iterations from each target to the nearest fixed point, then verifying SA<0 there, and quantifying distances and basins) is needed.
- Sparsity claims are weak. Connectivity is dense; small weights are numerous but not zero. Emergent “sparsity” is based on an arbitrary |W|<0.01 threshold. A biologically motivated sparsity constraint or an L1/structured regularizer would be more convincing; and performance versus true sparsity level should be reported.
- Biological plausibility of learning. The approach uses global, nonlocal optimization (L-BFGS) with full knowledge of all patterns and the Jacobian spectra; no path to a synaptically local rule is provided beyond a brief discussion. While NeurIPS need not demand biological plausibility, the claimed neuroscientific relevance would be stronger with a learning rule closer to established plasticity mechanisms or at least a demonstration that the solution is accessible via local rules.
- Robustness and hyperparameter sensitivity are underexplored. The choice of SSA smoothing ε and regularization coefficients is heuristic; no ablations show how results change with ε, ηs, ηF, or with the gain nonlinearity parameters. Given that SSA is only a surrogate, sensitivity analyses are important.
- Scope and significance for NeurIPS: The paper is more a modeling contribution than an ML method likely to be used by practitioners. Computational cost and scalability are not discussed (SSA requires spectral computations per pattern during optimization). The advance is interesting, but the empirical scope feels narrow for NeurIPS.

Quality: Technically competent and internally consistent, but critical guarantees are indirect (fixed points not exactly satisfied, SSA surrogate), and evaluation is limited to small-scale experiments without strong baselines or scaling analyses.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. However, key settings are deferred to a supplement (e.g., mean-field initialization, Table 1), hindering reproducibility in the current form.

Originality: Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to construct Dale-compliant analog attractors appears novel in this context. The overall ingredients (rate networks, E/I balance, attractor memories) are established; the optimization framing is the main novelty.

Significance: Potentially useful for computational neuroscience, but limited empirical breadth and lack of comparisons reduce impact. For ML audiences, practical relevance and scalability are unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous fixed-point verification: locate the nearest true fixed point to each target (e.g., by root-finding) and report SA there; quantify distances and basins.
- Add baselines: e.g., Hopfield-style graded networks with explicit inhibitory feedback, balanced memory networks, or networks with global inhibition tuned per memory, and compare recall, capacity, and firing-rate regimes under the same constraints.
- Capacity/scaling study: m versus n (and versus number of synapses), influence of E/I ratio, sparsity, and pattern statistics.
- Sensitivity analyses: ε in SSA, regularization weights, gain function slope/exponent; report computational cost.
- Clarify and justify sparsity claims; consider enforcing explicit sparsity and testing performance.
- Consider a spiking instantiation using the learned connectivity to support the biological claims.

Decision: Weak reject
The approach is interesting and the paper is well written, but the empirical validation is too narrow, key guarantees are indirect, and comparisons to established methods are missing. With stronger baselines, scaling analyses, and fixed-point verification, this could become a solid contribution.
