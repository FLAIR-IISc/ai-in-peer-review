- Proposes a control-theoretic method to construct Dale-constrained E/I recurrent networks that store multiple analog (graded) attractor memories using rectified-quadratic gains and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Core technical contribution: optimization objective that minimizes dv/dt at target patterns and minimizes the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian to enforce local stability.  
- Empirical demonstrations show robust autoassociative recall with noise, emergent synaptic sparsity, tight E/I balance, and stimulus-induced variability quenching; experiments use n=150, m=30 (capacity ≈0.2) and compare to an ideal observer.  
- Situates work between Hopfield-style models and biophysically grounded E/I networks by applying robust control (SSA) to graded attractors under Dale’s law.  
- Technical formulation is coherent—Dale’s law enforced by parametrization, and SSA provides smooth gradients for stability optimization.  
- Fixed points are only approximately enforced (dv/dt small but nonzero), and the claim that true fixed points exist nearby is plausible but unproven and unquantified.  
- Optimization treats inhibitory steady-states per attractor as free variables, inflating degrees of freedom and risking “cheating” unless shown to match learned dynamics.  
- Lacks analysis of capacity scaling with network size, sensitivity to pattern statistics and SSA ε, and theoretical guarantees on stability margins or basin sizes.  
- Computational practicality is unclear because optimizing SSA across many patterns requires repeated Jacobian spectral calculations; complexity and scaling are not discussed.  
- Empirical results are relevant and careful but would benefit from more quantitative diagnostics: retrieval times, success vs m/n, basin radii, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.  
- Paper is generally clear and explains SSA’s role, but many implementation details are relegated to supplements; reproducibility needs explicit reporting of nE/nI, time constants, inputs, and code.  
- Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted and should be corrected.  
- Originality: novel and timely combination of robust control (SSA minimization) with Dale-constrained E/I networks to store arbitrary analog patterns without saturating nonlinearities.  
- The per-attractor optimization of inhibitory patterns is less standard and its biological or modeling legitimacy depends on demonstrating tight adherence to fixed-point equations.  
- Significance: promising new approach for synthesizing balanced E/I circuits with analog attractors, but immediate impact limited by small scale, offline heavy optimization, and no spiking validation.  
- Strengths: satisfies biological constraints (Dale’s law, E/I balance, low rates), clear control-theoretic formulation with SSA gradients, and solid empirical demonstrations including ideal-observer comparison.  
- Key weaknesses and suggestions: quantify distances to true fixed points and basins, report dv/dt residuals per neuron, justify per-attractor inhibitory variables, provide capacity/scaling curves and computational costs, add baselines (global inhibition/saturation), and include spiking validation and code.  
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptually novel, well-motivated application of robust control, and empirical evidence of stable balanced dynamics without saturation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: crucial aspects unquantified (exact fixed points, basins, capacity), potential overreliance on extra inhibitory degrees of freedom, limited scale, and lack of spiking experiments.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject—idea is strong and promising but needs additional empirical/theoretical support (fixed-point exactness, scaling/capacity, and spiking or stronger baselines) before acceptance.
