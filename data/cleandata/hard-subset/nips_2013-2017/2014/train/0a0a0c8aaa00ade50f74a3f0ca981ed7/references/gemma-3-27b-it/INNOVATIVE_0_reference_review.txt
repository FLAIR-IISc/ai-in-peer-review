This paper presents a novel and ambitious framework for constructing analog memories within a balanced network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons. The core idea – framing memory storage as a robust control problem and optimizing for stability using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) – is a significant departure from traditional approaches and represents a bold attempt to bridge theoretical neuroscience with control theory. The authors successfully demonstrate the feasibility of their approach, showcasing the creation of stable, graded memories and robust recall even in the presence of noise. This is particularly noteworthy, as it addresses several limitations of prior work, including the reliance on binary representations, the difficulty of achieving high capacity, and the lack of biologically plausible dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The conceptual leap of applying robust control principles to memory network design is genuinely innovative. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the shortcomings of existing models. The results are compelling, demonstrating stable memory states and robust recall performance that rivals an “ideal observer.” The inclusion of Dale’s Law and the resulting parametrization, while increasing complexity, adds to the biological plausibility of the model. Furthermore, the analysis of synaptic weight distributions and the demonstration of sparsity are interesting observations. The finding that a significant fraction of seemingly ‘silent’ synapses aren’t critical for recall is also a nuanced and valuable insight. Finally, the demonstration of reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is a compelling connection to experimental observations.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the results are promising, the network size (n=150) is relatively small, and scaling to larger, more realistic networks remains an open question. The authors acknowledge this implicitly. The choice of hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) feels somewhat heuristic, and a more systematic exploration of their impact on performance would strengthen the conclusions. The connection to biological learning rules isn’t fully explored, which is a crucial step towards translating this theoretical framework into a more biologically realistic model. While the authors state their gain function is appropriate for spiking neurons, the transition and validation in a spiking implementation would further solidify this claim. The analysis of the fixed point condition is a little hand-wavy; they admit the fixed point isn’t *exactly* achieved, but deem it "close enough". More rigorous quantification of this deviation would be beneficial. Finally, while the SSA provides a robust stability measure, a deeper understanding of the *mechanism* by which the network achieves this stability beyond just E/I balance would be illuminating.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High novelty:** The control-theoretic framework for memory storage is a genuinely novel and potentially transformative approach.
* **Addresses significant limitations:** The paper tackles several key shortcomings of existing memory models.
* **Compelling results:** The simulations demonstrate stable memory states and robust recall performance.
* **Biological plausibility:** The inclusion of Dale’s Law and the balanced E/I dynamics enhance the model's biological relevance.
* **Potential impact:** This work could open new avenues for understanding memory and its neural substrate.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Small network size:** Scaling to larger networks needs to be addressed.
* **Heuristic hyperparameters:** More systematic hyperparameter tuning is needed.
* **Limited exploration of learning rules:** The link to biological learning mechanisms is not fully developed.
* **Imprecise fixed-point condition:** The deviation from true fixed points warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the originality and potential impact of this work are substantial. The authors have presented a bold and innovative approach to a fundamental problem in neuroscience. While further investigation is needed to address the limitations, the current work represents a significant step forward and warrants acceptance. The paper prioritizes novelty and a new direction, even with some methodological imperfections. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly concerning hyperparameter sensitivity and the quantification of the fixed-point deviation. I would be very interested to see this work extended to larger networks and integrated with more biologically realistic learning rules.
