

# Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing rate-based neural networks with balanced E-I dynamics that can store and recall analog memory patterns. The authors address limitations of previous models by explicitly incorporating Dale's law, graded activity patterns, realistic firing rates, and dynamic E-I balance. They optimize network parameters to satisfy fixed-point conditions and stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), a robust control theory measure.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the neuroscience of memory by providing a method to create structurally realistic networks with graded activity patterns. The demonstration of E-I balance and robustness to noise is compelling. The use of SSA for stability optimization is novel and well-justified theoretically. The results showing sparse connectivity and balanced E-I inputs align with biological observations.

**Major Technical Concerns:**

1. **Biologically Implausible Gain Function (Equation 1):** The authors define a threshold-quadratic gain function r_i = γv_i^2 for v_i > 0 (with γ=0.04). This is problematic because:
   - Quadratic functions lead to unbounded firing rates without inhibitory feedback, contradicting the paper's claim that "the gain function has no upper saturation" (p.5).
   - The reference [15] cited for this gain function does not appear to support this specific quadratic form. In cortical neurons, gain functions are typically linear or saturating, not quadratic.
   - The paper states "neuronal activities do not saturate" (p.12), but the quadratic gain function would cause saturation without inhibition, making this claim misleading.

2. **Mathematical Errors and Inconsistencies:**
   - In Equation (4), the stability condition is written as "α(J^μ) < 0" but spectral abscissa stability requires α(J^μ) ≤ 0 for asymptotic stability.
   - Equation (3) has a formatting error: "τ_i dvi dt" should be "τ_i dv_i/dt".
   - In Equation (2), the Dale's law reparameterization has a typo: "sj = { +1 if j ≤ nE −1 otherwise" should be "sj = { +1 if j ≤ nE, −1 otherwise".
   - The paper states "the trained network contains 52% of silent excitatory synapses" but defines "silent" as |W_ij| < 0.01 without justification for this threshold.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology and Presentation:**
   - Figure 1a claims "only 30 of the 100 excitatory neurons are shown" but the text states n_E = 100, while Figure 4a shows 100 excitatory neurons.
   - The paper contains a typo: "Hz m em or ie s" appears in the figure caption for Figure 1.
   - The claim that "the network was initialized prior to learning with the baseline as the single global attractor" (p.8) lacks explanation of how this initialization was achieved.

4. **Unjustified Claims:**
   - The paper claims "neuronal activities do not saturate" (p.12), but with the quadratic gain function, saturation would occur without feedback, making this claim false.
   - The ideal observer comparison (Figure 3b) is not clearly explained or justified as a fair benchmark.

5. **Methodological Ambiguities:**
   - The heuristic rule for ε = 0.01 · 150/n lacks justification. Why 150? Why scale with n in this specific way?
   - The paper states "the Jacobian-based stability criterion remains valid" when ‖dv/dt‖ is small but non-zero (p.6), but this is not theoretically justified.

**Additional Minor Issues:**
- The reference format is inconsistent (some use "et al.", some don't).
- Some grammatical errors ("the possibility for each neuron to sustain graded levels of activity in different memories" should be "the possibility for each neuron to sustain graded activity levels across different memories").
- The paper claims to overcome "the possibility for each neuron to sustain graded levels of activity in different memories" as a limitation of previous work, but this is not a limitation of previous work - it's a feature they're introducing.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents an interesting approach to modeling analog memory storage in balanced E-I networks, the fundamental flaw in the gain function (quadratic with no saturation) undermines the biological plausibility and theoretical soundness of the model. The mathematical errors and inconsistencies further weaken the paper's technical quality. The authors claim to create networks with "realistic firing rate dynamics" (p.5), but the quadratic gain function makes this impossible without inhibitory feedback, which the authors acknowledge is present but don't adequately explain.

The paper's significance is overshadowed by these technical flaws, particularly the biologically implausible gain function. The claim that "neuronal activities do not saturate" is contradicted by the model itself. The methodological shortcomings make it difficult to evaluate whether the proposed approach truly represents a significant advancement over previous work.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting insights, the fundamental methodological flaw in the gain function, combined with multiple technical errors and inconsistencies, prevents this from being a scientifically sound contribution. The authors should completely revise the model to use a biologically plausible gain function before considering resubmission.
