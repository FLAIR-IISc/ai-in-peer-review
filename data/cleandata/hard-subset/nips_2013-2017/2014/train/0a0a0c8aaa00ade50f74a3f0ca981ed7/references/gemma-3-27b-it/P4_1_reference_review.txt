This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing analog memory networks with balanced excitation and inhibition (E/I). The core idea is to directly optimize network parameters—synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron firing rates—to achieve stable fixed points corresponding to desired memory patterns, leveraging the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The authors address several limitations of previous memory models, including Dale’s law, graded activity patterns, realistic firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the shortcomings of existing models (Hopfield networks, LIF neuron models, spatially connected networks, spike-timing-based approaches). It positions the work within a rich body of literature and convincingly argues for the need for a new approach. The proposed method appears sound, utilizing established control theory principles (SSA optimization) within a biologically plausible network framework. The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories, robustness to noise, and a dynamically balanced E/I state. The detailed analysis of synaptic weights (sparseness) and the impact of weak connections adds further insight. The connection to observed neural phenomena like stimulus-induced variability reduction strengthens the biological relevance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of control theory (SSA optimization) with a biologically realistic E/I network model for analog memory storage is novel and addresses significant limitations of prior work.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the use of SSA is a well-established technique for ensuring stability. The method demonstrably achieves stable memory states.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the methods are explained in sufficient detail, and the results are presented in a logical manner. Figures are helpful and well-labeled.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential to advance our understanding of how memories are encoded and retrieved in the brain, particularly regarding the role of E/I balance and graded activity patterns. The observed reduction in trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is a particularly compelling result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** Optimization using SSA appears computationally expensive, which is not explicitly discussed. Scalability to larger networks with more memories is an open question.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) appears heuristic and lacks a systematic investigation of their impact on network performance.
*   **Gain Function:** The chosen gain function, while biologically plausible, lacks saturation. The authors argue stability arises from E/I balance, but the lack of saturation could still pose challenges in more complex scenarios.
*   **Limited Biological Validation:** While the authors highlight the biological relevance, the model is still a simplification of real neural circuits. Further validation against empirical data (e.g., relating the optimized weights to experimentally measured synaptic strengths) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Detailed Discussion of Failure Cases:** The authors primarily focus on successful storage and recall. A more detailed discussion of scenarios where the method *fails* (e.g., very high memory load, strongly correlated memories) would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in neuroscience (memory encoding) with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories with robust stability and E/I balance.
*   The work has the potential to inspire new research directions in both computational neuroscience and control theory.
*   The clarity and organization of the paper facilitate understanding and evaluation of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational cost of the method is a potential limitation for scalability.
*   The parameter selection process appears heuristic and could benefit from a more systematic investigation.
*   The lack of more extensive biological validation weakens the claims of biological relevance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and addresses several limitations of previous models. While some weaknesses exist (parameter sensitivity, computational cost), the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the potential impact of the work is substantial. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, explore the sensitivity of the results to parameter variations, and consider adding a section discussing potential failure cases.
