Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to build rate-based recurrent networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons that store multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable fixed points. The network respects Dale’s law via a reparameterization of synaptic weights, operates at low/moderate firing rates with a threshold-quadratic gain function, and achieves dynamic E/I balance. Storage is formulated as two objectives: (i) the target activity patterns are fixed points (minimize the norm of dv/dt at each target), and (ii) the fixed points are locally stable (minimize the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian at each target). Parameters (synapses and per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities) are optimized using gradient-based methods with an L2 penalty.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate: robust recall from noisy cues with performance comparable to an “ideal observer,” emergent sparsity in learned weights with many small-magnitude synapses that can be clipped with limited impact, tight dynamic E/I balance across attractors, and reduced trial-to-trial variability upon stimulus onset. They store 30 analog attractors in a 150-neuron network (capacity ~0.2) drawn from log-normal rate distributions.

This work connects to classical associative memory (Hopfield 1982), graded-response attractors (Treves & Rolls), balanced networks and recall dynamics (Amit & Brunel; Roudi & Latham), and spatial continuous attractors (Ben-Yishai et al.). It differs by explicitly enforcing Dale’s law, using a supralinear non-saturating transfer, and optimizing a robust stability surrogate (SSA) from control theory (Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.) to obtain multiple graded, non-binary memories with dynamic E/I balance. It also relates to recent efforts on constructing E/I-stable rate networks via optimization, and, to a lesser degree, to dense/modern Hopfield models (albeit those typically rely on symmetric weights and energy functions and often do not enforce Dale’s law).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Poses memory storage as a joint fixed-point and robust stability optimization problem with a differentiable stability surrogate (SSA), enabling gradient-based design of multi-attractor, Dale-compliant networks with analog memories.
- Biological realism: Explicit E/I separation, adherence to Dale’s law, low firing rates, emergent E/I balance, and heavy-tailed weight distributions, all consistent with cortical phenomenology.
- Empirical robustness: Good recall under noisy initialization; variability quenching upon stimulus onset parallels cortical data.
- Clear objective formulation and practical procedure: Cost combines fixed-point residuals, SSA, and weight regularization; implementation with L-BFGS is straightforward.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Limited scaling analysis: Capacity is shown for a single network size (n=150, m=30). There is no systematic scaling (capacity vs n, effect of pattern statistics, E/I ratios) or theoretical capacity bounds. Basin sizes are probed indirectly; more thorough quantification is missing.
- Stability surrogate: SSA guarantees local stability only as an upper bound to the spectral abscissa and does not ensure basin size or robustness to parameter noise; sensitivity analyses to ε, ηs, and ηF, and comparisons to other stability proxies (e.g., log-spectral radius of the Jacobian, Lyapunov-based certificates) are absent.
- Optimization degrees of freedom: Treating inhibitory steady-state activities per memory (vinhμ) as free variables increases the number of parameters linearly with m. Although the synapses are shared across memories, this choice risks overfitting the fixed point locations and obscures how much “storage” is truly carried by the recurrent weights alone.
- Sparsity claim: Connectivity is dense with many small weights; L2 regularization does not enforce true sparsity or wiring constraints. Biological sparse connectivity (fixed connection probability) is not explicitly imposed.
- Baselines: No direct comparison to alternative constructions that enforce E/I balance or to classic graded-response associative memories implemented with symmetric weights; no comparison to models with global inhibitory feedback calibrated per memory.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but some notation and presentation issues: occasional figure labeling (e.g., Hz memories), missing details on convergence reliability, run times, and sensitivity to initialization; references are incomplete/inconsistent in formatting.
- Reproducibility: No code or explicit algorithmic details (e.g., gradient computation for SSA, stopping criteria) are provided; no reporting of variance across multiple runs/inits.

Originality
- The use of SSA for stabilizing multiple target fixed points in a Dale-compliant network is a fresh combination of tools. However, the overall idea of constructing attractor networks by optimizing fixed points and Jacobian spectra is thematically related to control-theoretic approaches and prior work using supralinear gains and E/I balance. The paper would benefit from sharper positioning against these.

Significance
- The approach could become a useful design principle for building biologically grounded associative memories with graded representations and controlled E/I dynamics. Currently, significance is tempered by the lack of scaling, theoretical guarantees, and stronger empirical comparisons. As a proof of concept, it is promising.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide capacity and scaling analyses: vary n, m, pattern statistics, and E/I ratios; report recall probability vs load; characterize basin sizes (e.g., critical σ at 50% success for each memory).
- Ablations: quantify the contribution of the SSA term vs fixed-point residuals; evaluate different ε; compare to alternative stability surrogates; report sensitivity to ηs and ηF.
- Limit degrees of freedom: repeat experiments without per-memory vinhμ as trainable parameters, or constrain them with shared parameters, and compare performance.
- Enforce structural sparsity: add connection probability masks and/or L1 penalties; show the effect on recall and E/I balance.
- Baselines: compare to (i) graded Hopfield-style networks (even if violating Dale’s law); (ii) networks with global inhibitory feedback calibrated to target rates; (iii) spatial continuous attractors (where applicable).
- Robustness: test weight perturbations, noise in external inputs, and mismatches in single-neuron gain; report stability margins (spectra) at learned attractors.
- Clarity and reproducibility: improve references, provide code or pseudocode, document optimization details, and report statistics over multiple random seeds.
- Bridging to spiking: demonstrate that learned rate connectivity carries over to an LIF instantiation with matched f–I curves.

Pros
- Elegant control-theoretic formulation using SSA; principled handling of stability.
- Dale-compliant, E/I-balanced analog memories with robust recall and variability quenching.
- Emergent heavy-tailed weights; minimal assumptions on pattern structure.

Cons
- Limited scale and lack of capacity theory; no comprehensive baselines.
- Potential over-parameterization via per-memory inhibitory variables.
- Dense connectivity with small weights rather than true sparse wiring.
- Reproducibility and reference clarity need work.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a compelling proof of concept with clear potential. Strengthening the empirical evaluation (scaling, ablations, baselines), reducing degrees of freedom, and improving clarity/reproducibility would make it much more competitive. Even if not accepted, the ideas are promising and worth pursuing.
