- Presents a control-theoretic method to store multiple analog memory patterns as stable fixed points in rate-based E-I networks.  
- Key innovation: uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for stability, enabling gradient-based training under Dale’s law.  
- Demonstrates embedding of 30 analog patterns in a 150-neuron network with sparse connectivity, E-I balance, low firing rates, and robust recall from noisy cues.  
- Builds on robust control theory and extends Hopfield/balanced network models to support graded activity under biological constraints.  
- Technical execution is sound with a rigorous optimization formulation and appropriate use of SSA.  
- Empirical results are thorough and support the main claims, but theoretical explanation of why SSA yields stable fixed points is limited.  
- Optimization does not exactly meet fixed-point conditions (‖dv/dt‖ remains non-zero), creating a conceptual tension in claims about exact stable fixed points.  
- Choice of SSA smoothing parameter ε = 0.01 is heuristic and needs sensitivity analysis; scaling rule provided but not fully justified.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with replicable methods and effective figures.  
- Needs clearer discussion of inhibitory neuron “auxiliary” states and how they could be implemented biologically.  
- Original contribution: first to combine SSA-based stability optimization with Dale’s law to store analog patterns in balanced E-I networks.  
- Significant for computational neuroscience: biologically plausible analog memory storage, matches empirical E-I balance and firing-rate statistics.  
- Strengths include addressing multiple prior limitations, demonstrating robustness to noise, and showing weak-synapse pruning without performance loss.  
- Weaknesses: incomplete theoretical justification for SSA’s effect, arbitrary ε choice, unclear scalability to much larger networks, no biologically plausible learning rule, and no treatment of interference/forgetting.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify fixed-point claims, expand theoretical stability analysis (possibly with simple analytical examples), perform ε sensitivity tests, discuss scalability and capacity, and outline potential local learning rules.  
- Final recommendation: Accept — strong, novel, well-executed work with important implications despite some theoretical and scalability gaps.
