Summary
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct rate-based recurrent E/I networks that store multiple analog (graded) memories as asymptotically stable fixed points while respecting Dale’s law. Neuron rates follow a threshold–quadratic nonlinearity without saturation; synaptic weights are parameterized to enforce sign constraints; and the network dynamics are optimized so that (i) the desired patterns are fixed points (by minimizing the velocity norm at each target) and (ii) those fixed points are locally stable (by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA). Importantly, the inhibitory neurons’ steady-state activities in each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables optimized jointly with the synaptic weights. The method yields networks that recall target patterns from noisy cues, maintain low firing rates via dynamic E/I balance, show emergent heavy-tailed weight distributions, remain robust to ongoing noise, and exhibit stimulus-onset variability quenching. The main experimental demonstration uses n=150 neurons storing m=30 patterns (capacity ~0.2), shows recall accuracy comparable to an ideal observer baseline, and indicates that clipping small-magnitude synapses leaves most attractors intact.

Relative to prior work, the paper aims to overcome limitations of classic Hopfield-type attractor networks (symmetry, binary patterns), graded-response models lacking explicit inhibitory populations, and spiking models where stability often relies on saturation and/or global inhibition. It draws on robust control (SSA) to provide a differentiable stability surrogate, complementing earlier work on balanced networks and graded gain functions.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: casting memory embedding as joint fixed-point and robust stability optimization under Dale’s law and without saturating nonlinearities.
- Thoughtful use of SSA provides a practical, differentiable stability criterion; the link to robust control is novel and technically interesting.
- Biological plausibility improved relative to many attractor models: explicit E/I populations, realistic supralinear gain, low-rate regime via dynamic E/I balance, and variability quenching akin to cortical data.
- Empirical rigor: recall assessed against an ideal observer; robustness tested against cue noise and ongoing noise; analysis of E/I input correlations across memories.
- Emergent heavy-tailed/sparse-ish weight distributions and an informative ablation by clipping small weights.
- Writing is generally clear about the objectives, the optimization problem, and the empirical evaluations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Capacity and scaling. Only a single network size and storage load (n=150, m=30) are presented. There is no systematic scaling with n, no estimate of information per synapse, nor a comparison to classical and recent capacity results for analog/balanced networks. Suggest reporting:
  - Capacity vs m/n across multiple n.
  - Basin sizes (as a function of cue noise σ) and how they scale with load.
  - Spurious attractors or mis-retrieval statistics beyond ideal-observer comparison.
- Stability guarantees and transients. SSA is an upper bound on SA and is sufficient but not necessary; gradients can be ill-behaved as ε→0. While you report SSA and SA at convergence, the analysis would benefit from:
  - Robustness margins (e.g., worst-case real part, distance to instability, ε sensitivity).
  - Transient amplification/non-normality (pseudospectra, H∞ gains), as balanced networks often exhibit strong transients that can harm recall.
  - Quantification of basin geometries beyond local stability.
- Biological plausibility of learning. The optimization is fully offline and nonlocal. The per-attractor inhibitory steady-state variables v_inh^μ are optimized independently and scale with m, which gives the learner considerable degrees of freedom that are not clearly implementable synaptically. It would strengthen the paper to:
  - Demonstrate that the inhibitory steady states emerging from the trained W during recall match the optimized v_inh^μ.
  - Constrain or regularize v_inh^μ (e.g., distributional priors) and assess capacity/performance impact.
  - Explore local or semi-local learning approximations (e.g., inhibitory plasticity stabilizing fixed points) or provide a roadmap bridging the optimal algorithm to plausible rules.
- Sparsity claims. The observed “sparsity” is defined by a threshold on weight magnitude; with an L2 penalty and soft sign-constrained parameterization, weights are not truly sparse nor structurally sparse as in cortex (low connection probability). Please clarify:
  - Fraction of exact zeros (structural sparsity) vs small weights.
  - Sensitivity of performance to the clipping threshold; provide a sweep and quantify attractor stability loss.
  - Results with explicit sparsity constraints (L1 or fixed sparsity masks) and their effect on stability/recall.
- Comparisons and baselines. The paper would be more compelling with comparisons to:
  - Balanced attractor/spiking E/I networks that store discrete/graded patterns with global or local inhibition (e.g., Roudi & Latham; Amit & Brunel; Treves & Rolls follow-ups).
  - Alternative training approaches for stable dynamics (e.g., Jacobian regularization, Lyapunov-based methods) and discussion of pros/cons relative to SSA.
- Practical aspects. Missing or limited:
  - Runtime, convergence behavior, and sensitivity to η_s, η_F, ε.
  - Effect of network heterogeneity (τ_i, external inputs h_i) and whether h_i were fixed or optimized.
  - Robustness to synaptic noise/perturbations post-training (e.g., weight dropout; multiplicative noise).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled use of robust stability optimization to build analog attractor networks under Dale’s law with balanced low-rate dynamics.
- Empirically solid demonstration of recall robustness and cortical-like variability reduction.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scope of evaluation: no capacity/scaling analysis, limited stability/transient characterization, and no comparisons to strong baselines.
- Learning procedure is powerful but biologically implausible; per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom risk inflating performance without clear implementation.
- Sparsity and biological realism claims need stronger evidence or more appropriate constraints.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core idea is interesting and relevant to NeurIPS, but the current evidence falls short of the standards for significance and completeness. I encourage the authors to add capacity/scaling analyses, robustness/transient characterizations, stronger sparsity/biological plausibility results (or more modest claims), and comparative baselines. These additions would substantially strengthen the contribution.
