This paper addresses the important problem of building biologically plausible memory networks that can store and recall graded, analog memories. The authors propose a control-theoretic framework to directly optimize network parameters – including synaptic connectivity and inhibitory neuron firing rates – to achieve stable fixed points corresponding to desired memory patterns. The key innovation lies in employing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to ensure robust stability during optimization, alongside constraints reflecting Dale’s law and realistic firing rate dynamics. The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories with robustness to noise, exhibiting a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly contextualizing the limitations of previous approaches (Hopfield networks, LIF models, spatially connected networks, spike-timing based models) and positioning their work as a significant step forward. The introduction effectively highlights the biological constraints that are often overlooked in theoretical models and the importance of addressing these. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The application of robust control theory (SSA optimization) to the problem of memory storage is a genuinely novel and promising approach. This provides a systematic and principled way to address the stability issues that plague many existing models.
* **Biological Realism:** The incorporation of Dale’s law, graded neuronal responses, and a focus on dynamic balance between excitation and inhibition significantly enhances the biological plausibility of the model.
* **Robustness:** The demonstrated robustness to noise in both the recall cue and ongoing dynamics is a crucial feature, highlighting the practical relevance of the proposed framework.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 are helpful in visualizing the key aspects of the model and its performance.
* **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the network’s behavior, including the sparsity of synaptic weights, the E/I balance, and the reduction of trial-to-trial variability, which strengthens the conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention using the BFGS algorithm, the computational cost of optimizing the network parameters, especially for larger networks, is not adequately discussed. This could be a significant limitation for scaling up the model.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε, γ) appears somewhat heuristic, and a more systematic exploration of their influence on network performance would be beneficial. The impact of varying the smoothness parameter ε on the trade-off between stability and gradient behavior could be better elaborated.
* **Limited Network Size:** The model currently utilizes a network of only 150 neurons. While this is sufficient for demonstrating the feasibility of the approach, it remains unclear how well it will scale to more realistic network sizes (e.g., thousands or millions of neurons).
* **Fixed Gain Function:** The use of a fixed threshold-quadratic gain function (Eq. 1) may be a simplification. While justified by experimental observations, exploring the sensitivity of the results to different gain function shapes could be insightful.
* **Lack of Comparison to Other Methods:** While the paper acknowledges previous work, a more direct comparison of performance (e.g., storage capacity, recall accuracy) against state-of-the-art methods, specifically addressing the limitations the authors highlight, would strengthen the claim of advancement. A comparison with models that attempt to balance E/I ratios (e.g., [8,9]) would be particularly relevant.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to building biologically plausible memory networks.
* The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories with robustness to noise.
* The incorporation of biological constraints (Dale’s law, graded responses, E/I balance) is a significant contribution.
* The clear presentation and thorough analysis enhance the value of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and parameter sensitivity remain potential limitations.
* The limited network size raises concerns about scalability.
* A more direct comparison to existing methods would strengthen the claims of advancement.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. While there are some limitations regarding computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and network size, the overall contribution is significant. The use of robust control theory to stabilize memory networks is a promising direction, and the results demonstrate the feasibility of building biologically plausible models with enhanced robustness.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:

1. **Discuss computational cost:** Elaborate on the computational challenges associated with scaling up the model and potential strategies for mitigating them.
2. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Provide a more systematic exploration of the influence of key parameters on network performance.
3. **Scalability Discussion:**  Address the expected performance and challenges associated with scaling the model to more realistic network sizes.
4. **Comparison to Existing Methods:** Include a more direct comparison of performance against state-of-the-art methods, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.
