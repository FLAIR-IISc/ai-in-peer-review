Summary
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent rate-based networks of excitatory and inhibitory neurons (obeying Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points. The key idea is to jointly optimize synaptic weights and, for each memory, the steady-state inhibitory activities so that: (i) the desired patterns are fixed points (by minimizing the drift ‖dv/dt‖ at each target state), and (ii) those fixed points are stable (by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian). The network uses a rectified quadratic transfer function without saturation; stability is achieved through dynamically balanced E/I interactions rather than output saturation. The authors demonstrate recall performance and robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, emergent sparsity and pruning robustness of the learned connectivity, and stimulus-induced variability quenching. They show a proof-of-concept with n=150 neurons storing m=30 analog patterns (capacity ~0.2).

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to Hopfield-style associative memories, noting violations of Dale’s law and effective binarization, and to EI models with global inhibition or saturating nonlinearity, as well as spatial “bump” attractors. It builds on robust control tools (SSA) previously used for transient control in balanced networks and claims novelty in using SSA to directly enforce stability of multiple, arbitrary graded attractors in EI networks with Dale’s constraints. The approach differs from prior EI attractor models by not relying on saturation or global inhibition and by handling arbitrary analog patterns with dynamic E/I balance.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: using SSA-based robust stability optimization to design EI balanced networks that store arbitrary analog patterns is bold and original.
- Biological realism: Dale’s law, separate E/I populations, graded responses, low rates, dynamic E/I balance, and variability quenching are all addressed.
- Clear empirical demonstrations: recall probability vs. cue noise, comparison to an ideal observer, robustness to ongoing noise, pruning tolerance, and balanced inputs at fixed points.
- Insightful result: sparsity emerging without L1 suggests stability objectives alone can induce heavy-tailed weight distributions.

Weaknesses
- Capacity and scaling: Only a single network size (n=150) and capacity ~0.2 are shown; there is no systematic scaling analysis with N, m/N, or pattern statistics.
- Auxiliary inhibitory variables: Treating vinh per memory as free variables increases degrees of freedom during optimization. While the fixed points are legitimate, a discussion of whether this inflates effective capacity or constraints on inhibitory manifolds would be helpful.
- Fixed-point accuracy and basins: The ‖dv/dt‖ terms remain non-zero post-training; reliance on nearby exact fixed points is argued empirically. Basin sizes are not quantified beyond recall probability curves; more formal analysis would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons: No direct comparison to strong EI baselines that support graded activity (e.g., clustered/balanced EI attractors) under matched conditions.
- Reproducibility and details: Heuristic choices for ε, ηs, ηF; no code; no complexity/runtime analysis; incomplete references in the bibliography.

Quality
Technically sound as a proof-of-concept. The optimization is well-motivated, and stability evidence (SSA < 0, SA < 0) is encouraging. Experiments support claims of recall and robustness. However, theoretical capacity bounds, scaling, and basins are not analyzed; some choices (ε scaling, thresholds for success and pruning) are heuristic. The “auxiliary inhibitory” design choice is reasonable but should be discussed more critically.

Clarity
Generally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow. Figures are informative. Exposition of SSA could be expanded slightly (intuition, sensitivity to ε). Please fix incomplete citations and specify all hyperparameters and initializations; consider releasing code.

Originality
High. The combination of Dale-constrained EI networks with direct stability optimization via SSA for arbitrary analog memories is, to my knowledge, new and impactful.

Significance
Promising direction that bridges robust control and computational neuroscience. If scalable and paired with local/plausible learning rules, this could influence how analog memories are modeled in realistic EI circuits. Even as an algorithmic design principle, it’s valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel idea (SSA-driven design of analog EI attractors) with biological plausibility.
- Robust empirical demonstrations (recall, noise robustness, E/I balance, variability quenching).
- Emergent sparse, heavy-tailed weight distributions without L1.
- Opens a new avenue linking robust control and memory network design.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited scope of evaluation: no scaling or capacity analysis, small networks only.
- Use of per-memory inhibitory steady states as trainable variables raises questions about effective degrees of freedom.
- Lack of comparisons to alternative EI graded-attractor approaches.
- Heuristic choices and incomplete bibliographic details; no code.

Suggestions for the authors
- Provide scaling experiments (capacity vs N, effect of pattern statistics).
- Analyze basin sizes and recall robustness more systematically.
- Clarify the role and constraints of vinh per memory; discuss whether adding constraints changes capacity/performance.
- Compare to at least one alternative EI attractor model under matched conditions.
- Explore transfer to spiking LIF networks with matched gain functions (even a small demo).
- Release code and document optimization settings and computational cost.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a bold and original framework likely to stimulate follow-up work despite some empirical gaps. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the incomplete validation.
