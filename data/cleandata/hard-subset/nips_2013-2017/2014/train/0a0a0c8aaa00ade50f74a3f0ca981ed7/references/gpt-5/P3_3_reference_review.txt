Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent E–I rate networks (obeying Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) activity patterns as asymptotically stable fixed points. The network uses rectified-quadratic neuronal input–output functions without explicit saturation. The authors formulate storage as (i) making the desired patterns fixed points and (ii) rendering those fixed points locally stable. They optimize the synaptic weights (reparameterized to enforce Dale’s law) and, critically, the steady-state inhibitory activities for each memory, by minimizing a composite objective that includes the squared drift at the target points and the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian as a differentiable proxy for stability. They demonstrate autoassociative recall with probabilistic success vs cue noise, robustness to dynamic noise, tight E/I balance across memories, emergent sparsity of weights, and variability quenching upon stimulus onset. They report qualitative comparison to an ideal observer and ablative pruning of small weights.

This work connects to classic Hopfield models and their graded-response extensions (Treves & Rolls), balanced/spiking memory networks (Amit & Brunel; Roudi & Latham), stabilized supralinear networks (Ahmadian et al.), and to control-inspired stability shaping (Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.). Compared to earlier E–I networks where recall relied on saturating I/O nonlinearities or global inhibition with effectively binary attractors, the authors aim to realize truly graded attractors under Dale’s law with robust stability certification via SSA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is plausible and the use of SSA is appropriate for smooth stability optimization. The reparameterization for Dale’s law is standard. Simulation results are coherent: convergence of SSA, recall probabilities, E/I balance, and variability reduction. However, several methodological choices weaken the strength of claims:
  - The optimization treats the inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ as free parameters per memory, in addition to the synaptic weights. While biologically the inhibitory rates are not preset, this increases the number of learned degrees of freedom linearly with m and directly influences both fixed-point equations and Jacobian slopes g′(v). This confounds capacity claims and may overfit stability by “tuning” inhibitory gains per memory during training. It is essential to clarify whether these v_inh^μ are uniquely determined by the fixed-point constraints given W (they should be), or whether the optimization uses them as auxiliary knobs beyond what the dynamics would self-consistently yield.
  - The fixed-point term is not driven to zero; the authors posit an actual fixed point nearby but provide no constructive proof or tolerance analysis. A post-hoc Newton step or fixed-point solver to locate the actual attractor, and reporting its distance to the target, would substantiate the claim.
  - Capacity and scaling are not characterized. m/n≈0.2 at n=150 is modest relative to classic graded models; there is no scaling vs n, m, nI, or pattern statistics, nor basin-size analysis beyond empirical σ sweeps.
  - Computational complexity is not discussed. SSA evaluation and gradients typically require eigen/schur computations; per-μ costs likely scale superlinearly with n. Practical scalability to biologically meaningful sizes is unclear.
  - “Sparse” connectivity is inferred from many small-magnitude weights under an L2 penalty. L2 does not induce true sparsity; the chosen 0.01 threshold is arbitrary. A comparison to L1 regularization, and reporting exact zeros vs near-zeros, would clarify whether sparsity is an outcome of the task or the regularizer.
- Experiments are mostly single-setting; sensitivity to ε (SSA smoothing), η_s/η_F, gain exponent, and random seeds is not reported. The ideal-observer comparison is helpful but indirect; comparisons to more standard autoassociative baselines (e.g., graded Hopfield with Dale’s projection, global inhibition variants) would contextualize gains.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with a clear objective function and method. Some clarifications would improve reproducibility:
  - Precisely state whether v_inh^μ are entirely determined by dv/dt=0 given W and v_exc^μ; if not, what prevents degeneracy?
  - Provide algorithmic details: SSA definition used, gradient computation, per-iteration complexity, stopping criteria, and typical wall-clock time.
  - Specify noise units/magnitudes (e.g., SD=0.33 in what units/time constant?) and integration details.
  - Reference formatting is incomplete and should be fixed.

Originality
- The key novelty is the application of SSA-based robust stability optimization to construct multi-attractor analog memories in E–I networks under Dale’s law, without relying on saturating f–I curves. The explicit joint optimization of fixed-point and stability constraints for arbitrary graded patterns is a valuable perspective. While related balanced/graded memory ideas exist, and E–I balanced dynamics are well studied, leveraging SSA to directly shape local stability across multiple target states appears new and interesting.

Significance
- If scalable and validated, the approach could provide a recipe for embedding analog memories with biological constraints, and a bridge between control-theoretic tools and neural circuit design. The demonstrations of E/I balance and variability quenching are compelling qualitative signatures. However, the current scope (n=150), lack of capacity scaling, and the ambiguity around per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom limit immediate impact. A stronger empirical characterization (scaling, basins, robustness across hyperparameters) and a demonstration in spiking networks with matched gains would materially increase significance.

Pros
- Clear and principled optimization framework using SSA; respects Dale’s law.
- Stores genuinely graded patterns; robust, low-rate recall stabilized by E/I balance.
- Demonstrates variability quenching and robustness to dynamic noise.
- Emergent heavy-tailed weight distribution; recall robust to pruning small weights.

Cons
- Per-memory optimization of inhibitory steady-states inflates degrees of freedom and clouds capacity claims.
- No scaling analyses (capacity vs n, computational cost), limited network sizes.
- Fixed-point errors remain nonzero; no verification of nearby true fixed points.
- Sparsity claim relies on L2 and arbitrary thresholds; limited ablations.
- Incomplete comparisons to established baselines; reference formatting issues.

Suggestions and questions for authors
1. Clarify the role of v_inh^μ: Are these uniquely determined by dv/dt=0 given W and v_exc^μ? If multiple solutions exist, how are they constrained? Can you optimize W only (or penalize deviations of v_inh^μ from those implied by dynamics) and report capacity?
2. Provide scaling results: capacity vs n, basin sizes, runtime vs n and m, sensitivity to ε, η_s, η_F, and nI/nE.
3. Verify existence and proximity of exact fixed points post-training (e.g., Newton solver), and report distances.
4. Compare to baselines: graded Hopfield with projected Dale constraints, global-inhibition models, and LIF implementations using the learned W.
5. Revisit sparsity: include L1 or elastic-net variants; report exact zero counts; assess performance after structured pruning.
6. Report failures/spurious attractors and inter-run variability across seeds.
7. Improve citation completeness and methodological details sufficient for reproduction; consider releasing code.

Overall evaluation
A technically interesting and potentially impactful approach that brings robust control tools to a classic problem with biological constraints. The main concerns are the additional per-memory degrees of freedom on inhibitory steady-states, lack of scaling/capacity characterization, and limited ablations. With clarifications and additional experiments, this could be a strong contribution.

Recommendation: Weak accept if authors can address the inhibitory-parameter and scaling concerns in rebuttal; otherwise, borderline.
