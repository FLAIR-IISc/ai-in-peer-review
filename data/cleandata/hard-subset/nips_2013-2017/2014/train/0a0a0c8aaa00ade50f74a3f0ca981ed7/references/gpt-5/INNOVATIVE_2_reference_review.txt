Summary
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct balanced rate-based E–I recurrent networks that store multiple analog attractor memories under biological constraints (Dale’s law, low rates, graded responses). The dynamics use rectified-quadratic transfer functions. The authors optimize synaptic weights (with a softplus reparameterization to enforce Dale’s law) and per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities to satisfy two criteria: (i) the target states are fixed (minimize squared velocity at each target), and (ii) they are locally stable (minimize the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian at each target). An L2 penalty regularizes weights, and optimization uses L-BFGS.

On n=150 neurons, they embed m=30 analog memories (capacity ~0.2). They demonstrate robust recall from noisy cues, performance close to an “ideal observer” (nearest stored pattern), tight E/I balance across attractors, robustness to ongoing noise, and across-trial variability quenching after stimulus onset—consistent with cortical data. Trained weights exhibit a heavy-tailed distribution; clipping small weights preserves most attractors, suggesting recall relies on a sparse backbone.

Relation to prior work
This work addresses limitations of classical Hopfield models (symmetry, binary codes) and recent Dale-compliant networks that rely on saturating f–I curves or global inhibition, often yielding effectively binary memories. It differs from spatial bump models (no spatial structure assumed) and from spike-timing-based graded memory work without inhibitory units. The key novelty is importing robust control tools (SSA minimization) to directly optimize stability of analog attractors in E–I networks while maintaining low-rate balance—bridging computational neuroscience and control.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Uses SSA minimization to enforce stability of multiple analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks. This is a bold, promising direction that could impact how we design stable RNNs with biological structure.
- Biological realism and phenomena: Explicit E/I populations, balanced regime at low rates across attractors, and stimulus-driven variability quenching.
- Empirical robustness: Successful recall under substantial cue noise, ongoing process noise; performance near an ideal observer bound.
- Emergent sparsity and interpretability: Heavy-tailed synaptic weights; many weak synapses are dispensable for recall.
- Clear demonstration that balance—not saturation—stabilizes graded rates.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Fixed-point verification: The velocity at target states remains nonzero; stability is inferred from SSA at the target rather than proven at an exact equilibrium. Please locate the actual fixed points near each target (e.g., via Newton or root-finding initialized at the target), evaluate SA there, and report distances to the targets.
- Capacity and scaling: Only one setting (n=150, m=30). No scaling curves vs. n, m, E:I ratio, or pattern statistics. The achieved capacity (~0.2) is modest relative to classic (though less constrained) models; discussion and empirical scaling would strengthen claims.
- Structural sparsity: Connectivity is effectively dense during training; “sparsity” is thresholded post hoc and depends on an arbitrary cutoff. It would be valuable to train with a sparsity mask and report connection probabilities closer to cortex.
- Training plausibility: The method is a global offline optimization with access to all targets; no local learning rule is proposed. Even a constrained experiment (e.g., optimizing only inhibitory synapses or using biologically plausible plasticity on I→E) would increase impact.
- Robustness to parameter perturbations: No tests with synaptic noise, heterogeneity in τ, or mismatch in single-neuron gain. These would be important for claims of robust stability.
- Algorithmic details: Limited reporting on computational cost, convergence behavior, sensitivity to initialization, and the role of ε, ηs, ηF. An ablation vs. alternative stabilizers (e.g., spectral radius penalties, Lyapunov/contraction-based objectives) would contextualize SSA’s advantages.

Questions for the authors
- Do the true fixed points near the target states have negative SA? How far (in norm) are they from the targets?
- How much does freeing v_inh^μ (per-memory inhibitory steady rates) ease the problem? If you only target E activities and optimize W, does performance drop substantially?
- What is the largest m you reliably store for n=150, and how does recall degrade with m? How do results change with pattern variance and mean rate?
- Sensitivity to single-neuron nonlinearity: If you change the exponent or add saturation, does the approach still work, and does balance persist?
- How does ε scaling with n affect optimization stability and the gap between SSA and SA in practice?

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Please include all hyperparameters (Table 1), τi and hi, full sizes of E/I populations, and the precise SSA implementation details (and code, if possible). Clarify the threshold used to define “silent” synapses and report sensitivity.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual novelty and boldness in combining robust control with biologically constrained memory networks.
- Clear empirical demonstrations of analog attractors under E/I balance and robustness phenomena linked to cortical dynamics.
- Potential to inspire follow-up work in both neuroscience and machine learning on stability-aware RNN design.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scaling/capacity analysis and incomplete verification of exact fixed points.
- Lack of structural sparsity and biological learning mechanisms.
- Missing robustness ablations and computational details.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-strong). Despite some gaps, the core idea is original and significant, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing fixed-point verification and adding a brief capacity/scaling analysis would materially strengthen the final version.
