Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic method to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a recurrent rate-based network that respects key biological constraints: distinct excitatory and inhibitory populations (Dale’s law), recurrent connectivity, low firing rates without saturation, and dynamically balanced E/I. Unlike classic Hopfield models (symmetric weights, effectively binary memories) or later LIF-based balanced networks where stability often relies on saturation and/or global inhibition calibrated to a single excitation level, the authors optimize the network to make a set of target patterns fixed points and locally stable by jointly minimizing (i) the norm of dv/dt at the targets and (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each pattern. They enforce Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization of weights and treat inhibitory steady-state activities at each attractor as auxiliary variables to be optimized. They demonstrate recall robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, E/I balance at the neuron and attractor level, and that only a minority of strong synapses are functionally critical. Capacity demonstrated is ~0.2 (30 memories in a 150-neuron network). This extends prior autoassociative memory work by bringing robust control tools (SSA) to explicitly enforce local stability in EI networks with graded patterns. It relates to balanced networks and memory models (e.g., Roudi & Latham), spatial-bump models, and graded-response Hopfield extensions, but differs by constructing stability algorithmically without relying on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibition.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization objective is coherent: driving dv/dt at targets towards zero ensures near-fixed points; minimizing SSA (a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa) enforces local asymptotic stability. The Dale constraint is cleanly implemented via a log(1+exp) parameterization. The demonstrations are consistent: SSA and SA decrease across training; recall follows basins comparable to an “ideal observer” predictor; ongoing noise does not disrupt recall; E/I balance emerges and is quantified. However:
  - Fixed points are not enforced exactly; dv/dt remains small but nonzero. The claim that “an exact, stable fixed point exists very near each target” is plausible but unverified. A post-hoc root finding (e.g., Newton or fixed-point iteration) to locate actual fixed points given the learned W and then checking SA there would strengthen this.
  - Biological plausibility of learning is limited (global offline LBFGS; inhibitory steady-state values per memory optimized as free variables). While acceptable for a constructive proof-of-principle, this weakens claims about mechanisms.
  - Capacity, basins, and scaling are not characterized theoretically, and empirical scale (n=150, m=30) is modest. SSA computation scales poorly with n and m; no runtime/memory report.
  - Comparisons are limited. Beyond the ideal observer, there is no experimental comparison to alternative training methods (e.g., graded Hopfield with sign-constrained weights; FORCE/RSNN approaches; Lyapunov-based constructions; modern Hopfield networks).
  - The “sparsity” finding is mostly that many weights are small, not structurally zero; clipping shows some robustness but is heuristic (|W|<0.01 threshold) and one attractor is lost.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The objective is precisely stated, the SSA choice is explained, and figures are interpretable. A few issues:
  - Some details are referenced to Table 1/Supplement; those should be included (hyperparameters ηs, ηF, ε-scaling, initialization means/variances).
  - Reporting exact dv/dt residuals at convergence, exact SA values at the converged fixed points, and run-time complexity would improve reproducibility.
  - Citations are sometimes incomplete; related prior work in control for neural dynamics and alternative attractor-learning methods could be expanded.

Originality
- Using SSA-based robust stability optimization to embed analog attractors in EI-balanced networks is, to my knowledge, novel. The combination of Dale-constrained parameterization, graded patterns without saturating single-neuron transfer, and explicit Jacobian stabilization is original relative to classical autoassociative memory and balanced network literature. The notion of treating inhibitory steady-state activity as auxiliary variables during optimization is also a distinctive modeling choice.

Significance
- As a constructive demonstration that analog memories can be stabilized at low rates in EI-balanced circuits via explicit stability optimization, the contribution is interesting and could spur further cross-fertilization between robust control and computational neuroscience. It clarifies how graded attractors can exist without saturation or global inhibition. That said, the practical impact is limited by lack of scalability analysis, absence of theoretical capacity/basin results, and the biological irrelevance of the optimization procedure. For NeurIPS’ computational neuroscience track, this is still a meaningful, if incremental, contribution.

Pros
- Clear formulation of memory embedding as a joint fixed-point and local stability optimization.
- Satisfies key biological constraints (Dale’s law, low rates, dynamic E/I balance).
- Novel use of SSA for differentiable stability control; good empirical demonstrations (noise robustness, ideal observer comparison, variability quenching).
- Insight that few strong synapses dominate recall.

Cons
- No proof of exact fixed points post-training; SA evaluated at approximate target states.
- Modest scale; no capacity/basin scaling theory or benchmarks against alternatives.
- Learning procedure is global and not biologically plausible; inhibitory activities per memory treated as free variables.
- Sparsity claims are qualitative; clipping threshold ad hoc; one attractor lost.
- Limited related-work comparison; reference list formatting incomplete.

Requests for clarification/suggestions
- Locate exact fixed points after training and report SA there; quantify basin sizes.
- Provide scaling experiments (n up to 1–2k, m varied), with runtime and memory; examine ε-sensitivity.
- Compare to sign-constrained graded Hopfield-style training and to a Lyapunov/energy-based construction.
- Explore constraints encouraging structural sparsity and/or biologically realistic connection probabilities.
- Discuss how local inhibitory plasticity could approximate the SSA-driven objective.

Overall recommendation
- Weak accept for the NeurIPS computational neuroscience track: technically sound and novel in method, clearly presented, but limited in scale and comparative depth. Confidence: medium.
