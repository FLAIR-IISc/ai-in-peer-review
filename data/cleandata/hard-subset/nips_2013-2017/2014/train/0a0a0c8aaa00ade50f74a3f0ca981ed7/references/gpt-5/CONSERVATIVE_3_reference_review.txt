Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an optimization-based framework to construct rate-based recurrent E/I networks (obeying Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) memories as locally stable fixed points. The key technical elements are (i) a cost term that drives the time-derivative of the state to zero at a set of prescribed excitatory patterns (thus encouraging fixed points), and (ii) a robust-control-inspired stability term based on the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian evaluated at those target states, ensuring local asymptotic stability. Synaptic weights are parametrized via a softplus with fixed sign to enforce Dale’s law; inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory are treated as free variables during optimization. The paper reports: successful recall for m=30 patterns in a network of n=150 (nE=100, nI=50), robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise, emergent weight “sparsity,” balanced E/I inputs, and variability quenching upon stimulus onset. The authors situate their proposal relative to Hopfield-type models (binary or graded), balanced LIF networks, and ring attractors, and leverage control-theoretic tools (SSA) previously used in neural control design.

Strengths
- Addresses a longstanding gap: autoassociative storage of non-binary patterns in networks that obey Dale’s law and operate at low rates without relying on saturating transfer functions.
- Conceptually appealing use of robust control (SSA) to enforce local stability; this could be of broader interest for dynamical systems design in neuroscience.
- Empirical demonstrations include robustness to noise, E/I balance across attractors, and variability reduction—phenomena of interest in systems neuroscience.
- The optimization framework is clearly described, and the sign-constrained parametrization is standard and appropriate.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evidence and scope: The results are largely proof-of-concept on a small network (n=150) with one capacity point (m=30; “capacity 0.2”). There is no systematic capacity analysis (scaling with n, m, nE/nI, statistics of patterns) or characterization of basin volumes. Without scaling curves and comparisons to established baselines, it is difficult to assess significance.
- Over-parameterization via inhibitory latent variables: Treating the steady-state inhibitory activities vinh^μ as free per-memory parameters increases degrees of freedom linearly with m. This effectively introduces auxiliary latent variables per pattern and makes the feasibility of storing patterns much easier. The paper should quantify how much of the storage/stability hinges on this choice, and provide results with more constrained inhibitory parametrizations (e.g., only synaptic weights learned; no per-memory inhibitory “targets”; or a shared inhibitory manifold). As-is, capacity claims are hard to interpret or compare to classical results.
- Fixed points vs “slow points”: The dv/dt term does not converge to zero; hence target states are not fixed points. The paper asserts that true stable fixed points “very near” the targets emerge, but does not verify this rigorously. At minimum, one should locate the actual fixed point reached from the target neighborhood (e.g., via Newton or simulation convergence), compute the Jacobian there, and report the spectral abscissa at that point. Evaluating SA/SSA at a non-fixed point is not a standard stability guarantee.
- Baselines and prior work: There is no quantitative comparison to classical continuous-attractor/Hopfield-like models with graded patterns (e.g., Treves & Rolls, subsequent graded-response analyses), E/I decompositions of rate networks, or more recent balanced network models storing structured patterns. The “ideal observer” upper bound is not a meaningful algorithmic baseline for network recall.
- Robustness and ablative analyses: The stability hinges on SSA with a heuristic ε scaling; sensitivity analyses over ε, ηs, ηF, initialization distributions, and network size are not reported. Training complexity and convergence reliability are unclear. The sparsity claim is qualitative; given the L2 penalty, near-zero weights are expected but do not imply true structural sparsity. The clipping test changes the success threshold by two orders of magnitude (0.001 to 0.08), undermining the conclusion that weak weights are unnecessary.
- Biological relevance: The model is purely rate-based with a supralinear input-output; claims of transferability to spiking neurons are speculative without a spiking instantiation. The learning procedure presumes global access to all parameters and per-memory inhibitory targets—far from local plasticity. The paper acknowledges this but provides no concrete path or experiments toward local rules.
- Clarity/reproducibility: Some key hyperparameters are in “Table 1” and Supplementary, which are not included. The exact number of runs, variance across runs, and code availability are not stated.

Questions for the authors
1. How does capacity (success probability vs m/nE) scale with network size and pattern statistics? Please provide curves with error bars across seeds.
2. What is the impact of removing the per-memory inhibitory variables from the optimization? Can you constrain vinh^μ to be the dynamical solution given W and h, rather than optimizing them independently?
3. Can you locate the actual fixed points after training and report SA/SSA there? Also report basin sizes (e.g., maximum σ for which recall succeeds) per memory.
4. How sensitive are results to ε in SSA, the regularization weights, and initialization? What is the computational cost?
5. Provide comparisons to at least one established graded memory model adapted to satisfy Dale’s law (and, ideally, to a LIF E/I implementation).
6. Can you demonstrate a small spiking instantiation with matched f–I curves to support the biological plausibility claim?

Pros
- Interesting, potentially useful use of SSA for neural dynamical design.
- Addresses Dale’s law and balanced operation for analog memories.
- Empirical phenomena (noise robustness, E/I balance, variability quenching) are appealing.

Cons
- Limited validation, no scaling/capacity analysis, and weak baselines.
- Reliance on per-memory inhibitory latent variables undermines interpretability of capacity.
- Stability claims are not rigorously verified at the actual fixed points.
- Biological and learning plausibility remain speculative.

Overall assessment
The paper presents an intriguing optimization-based approach with a novel control-theoretic angle, but the empirical evidence is not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. The lack of capacity scaling, rigorous stability verification at true fixed points, and meaningful baselines, together with over-parameterization via per-memory inhibitory variables, make it hard to assess the contribution relative to established models. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the hope that a revised version adds thorough scaling analyses, stronger baselines, constrained inhibitory parametrization (or a clear latent-variable framing), stability verification, and preferably a spiking demonstration.
