

# Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing neural networks that can store and recall analog memory patterns while respecting biological constraints (Dale's law, graded activity, low firing rates, and E-I balance). The authors optimize synaptic weights using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to ensure both fixed-point conditions and stability for desired analog patterns, claiming this overcomes limitations of previous approaches like Hopfield networks (which violate Dale's law) and LIF-based models (which rely on saturation).

## Strengths

The paper demonstrates technical rigor in its mathematical formulation, particularly in the application of SSA as a differentiable proxy for stability optimization. The method is well-motivated by the limitations of existing models, and the experimental results show that the networks can indeed store multiple analog patterns with robust recall performance. The demonstration of sparse connectivity and dynamic E-I balance aligns with biological observations, and the paper's figures effectively illustrate these findings. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how their model differs from prior work, particularly regarding the handling of graded activity patterns.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** While the mathematical formulation is sound, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The comparison to an "ideal observer" is inadequate as a benchmark—this is not a neural network model but a theoretical upper bound. The authors do not compare their approach to relevant existing memory models that handle graded activity, such as those by Treves (1985, 1988) or more recent work on analog associative memories (e.g., Battaglia & Treves, 2001; Latham & Nirenberg, 2004). Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a meaningful improvement over established techniques. Additionally, the paper does not address the computational complexity of the optimization procedure or how it scales with network size, which is critical for practical applications.

**Originality:** The application of SSA to neural network stability optimization is somewhat novel, but this is a methodological detail rather than a significant conceptual advance. The core idea of optimizing networks for stable fixed-point attractors is not new (see Hopfield, 1982; Amit, 1989), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing work on analog memory networks. The most significant novelty appears to be the use of SSA as a stability proxy, but this is a technical refinement rather than a fundamental breakthrough. The paper also fails to properly contextualize its contribution relative to recent NIPS work on neural dynamics and memory (e.g., the 2020 paper "Stabilized Supralinear Networks" by Ahmadian et al.).

**Significance:** The paper claims to "open new routes to the understanding of memory," but it provides no evidence that this approach leads to new biological insights or practical applications. It doesn't demonstrate how this model could be applied to real neural data or used to make testable predictions about memory function. The focus on rate-based networks without addressing the implications for spiking networks (despite a brief mention in the discussion) limits the paper's relevance to neuroscience. The "robustness" claims are based on limited noise conditions, and the paper doesn't explore failure modes or boundary conditions where the method might not work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and technically clear, with good figures that effectively illustrate the key findings. However, the lack of comparison to established baselines undermines the clarity of the contribution's significance. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this method differs from and improves upon existing approaches, rather than just listing the limitations of previous work.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution that lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and fails to demonstrate significant practical or theoretical advantages over existing methods. While the method is well-formulated, the paper does not provide compelling evidence that it offers a meaningful improvement over prior work, particularly given the absence of thorough comparative experiments. The claimed advantages over existing models are not substantiated with rigorous benchmarking.

The paper's primary weakness is the insufficient evidence to support its central claim that this approach "overcomes all of the above limitations" of previous work. Without direct comparisons to relevant baselines that address the same biological constraints, it's impossible to evaluate whether the proposed method is truly novel or merely a slight variation on existing techniques.

For NIPS, this paper falls short of the expected standard for significance and novelty. While the technical execution is good, the lack of meaningful comparison to established approaches and the limited demonstration of practical advantages make it difficult to justify acceptance over more established methods. The paper would benefit from extensive benchmarking against relevant baselines and a clearer articulation of the specific advantages of the SSA-based optimization approach.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of novelty and significance, despite the paper's technical quality. The authors should first establish that their method provides measurable improvements over existing approaches before claiming it "overcomes" the limitations of prior work.
