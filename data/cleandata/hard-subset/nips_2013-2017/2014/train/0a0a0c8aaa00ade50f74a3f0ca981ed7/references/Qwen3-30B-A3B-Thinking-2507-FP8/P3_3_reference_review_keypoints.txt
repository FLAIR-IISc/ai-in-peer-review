- Goal: construct biologically plausible networks that store graded (analog) memories while respecting Dale's law, sparse connectivity, E-I balance, and low firing rates.
- Core methodological contribution: use Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) to enable gradient-based stability optimization in E–I networks.
- Main result: optimized E–I networks with Dale's law and sparse connectivity can store multiple analog memories as stable fixed points.
- Biological plausibility: networks exhibit realistic sparse connectivity, dynamic E–I balance, and low baseline firing rates.
- Evaluation: thorough empirical tests including recall under noise, synaptic sparseness analysis, and robustness to ongoing noise.
- Theoretical grounding: precise mathematical formulation and explicit incorporation of biological constraints.
- Weakness — scalability: experiments limited to 30 memories in 150 neurons (capacity 0.2); no analysis of scaling to larger networks or memory loads.
- Weakness — spiking link superficial: limited evidence that rate-based optimization transfers to spiking models; gain function physiological match under-justified.
- Weakness — learning rules: reliance on global BFGS optimization rather than biologically plausible local/inhibitory plasticity mechanisms.
- Weakness — literature comparison: limited comparison to recent analog memory models (e.g., refs [28–30]).
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant; advances analog memory modeling in balanced E–I networks.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scalability and strengthen the spiking-network connection.
- Pro acceptance: SSA novelty, strong biological plausibility, comprehensive validation, clear theoretical contribution.
- Con acceptance: limited scalability analysis, superficial spiking discussion, and no proposed biologically plausible learning mechanism.
