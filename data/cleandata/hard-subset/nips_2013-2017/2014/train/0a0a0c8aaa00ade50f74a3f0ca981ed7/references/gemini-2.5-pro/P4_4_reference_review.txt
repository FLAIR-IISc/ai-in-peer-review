### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing rate-based recurrent neural networks capable of storing and recalling multiple analog (graded) memory patterns. The network model incorporates several key biological constraints: separate populations of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons adhering to Dale's law, non-saturating neuronal transfer functions, and the objective of maintaining reasonably low firing rates.

The central idea is to frame the problem of memory storage as a direct optimization problem based on control theory. The authors define two conditions for a memory pattern to be a stable attractor: 1) the pattern must be a fixed point of the network dynamics (i.e., the time derivative of neuronal activity is zero), and 2) the dynamics must be locally stable around that fixed point. To achieve this, they formulate a cost function that minimizes both the deviation from the fixed-point condition and a measure of instability.

A key technical contribution is the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable proxy for the true spectral abscissa (the largest real part of the Jacobian's eigenvalues), which is typically non-smooth and difficult to optimize. By minimizing the SSA, the authors can use gradient-based methods to tune network parameters—specifically, the synaptic weights and the firing rates of inhibitory neurons for each memory—to ensure stability.

This approach departs from traditional Hebbian learning rules and statistical mechanics analyses of attractor networks. It overcomes several limitations of prior models, such as the reliance on symmetric connectivity (violating Dale's law) in classic Hopfield networks, the instability or need for saturating non-linearities in previous analog memory models, and the use of global, non-specific inhibition in many balanced network models. The authors demonstrate that networks optimized with this method can robustly recall graded patterns from noisy cues and that these networks exhibit emergent properties observed in biological circuits, such as a tight balance between excitation and inhibition, sparse connectivity, and stimulus-induced reduction of neural variability.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, leveraging a well-established tool from robust control theory (the SSA) to address the challenging problem of ensuring stability in a high-dimensional dynamical system. The claims are well-supported by numerical experiments. The authors demonstrate that the optimization successfully drives the SSA to negative values, indicating stability. The recall performance is quantitatively evaluated and benchmarked against an ideal observer, showing near-optimal performance. Furthermore, the analysis of the resulting network properties (weight distributions, E/I balance, noise robustness) provides convincing evidence that the method produces networks with desirable and biologically-relevant characteristics. The work is presented as a complete piece, moving from theoretical formulation to implementation and detailed analysis of the results.

#### Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of previous attractor network models and motivates the need for a new approach. The methods section provides a precise mathematical description of the network model, the optimization objective (Eq. 5), and the use of the SSA. The results are presented logically with clear figures that effectively illustrate the main findings, such as the optimization process (Fig. 2), recall performance (Fig. 3), and emergent network properties (Figs. 4, 5, 6). The description is sufficient for an expert to understand the core methodology.

A minor point for improvement could be a slightly more detailed justification for the heuristic used to set the smoothness parameter ε. While the chosen rule is reasonable, a brief discussion of its sensitivity or rationale would strengthen the manuscript.

#### Originality

The originality of this work is high. While the components (rate-based E-I networks, attractor memory, stability analysis) are familiar, their combination within a direct stability optimization framework using the SSA is novel in the context of memory networks. This represents a significant departure from classical approaches. Previous work on stabilizing network dynamics with control-theoretic methods (e.g., [14]) has typically focused on generating specific transient dynamics or stabilizing a single baseline state, rather than embedding a multitude of distinct, graded attractor states. This paper applies these modern tools to solve a classic problem in theoretical neuroscience, successfully integrating multiple biological constraints that have been difficult to satisfy simultaneously in past models.

#### Significance

The results presented are significant for both computational neuroscience and potentially for machine learning.
1.  **For Neuroscience:** The paper provides a powerful proof of concept that networks respecting key biological constraints can support robust analog memory. It offers a normative model for what a "solution" to the analog memory problem could look like at the circuit level. The emergent properties—particularly the pattern-specific E/I balance and sparse functional connectivity—provide a mechanistic link between network optimization and widely observed cortical phenomena. This framework can serve as a valuable tool for generating hypotheses about the structure and function of biological memory circuits.
2.  **For Machine Learning:** The problem of creating stable and robust dynamical systems is central to the design of recurrent neural networks. This work presents a principled method for embedding multiple stable fixed points with graded values, which could be relevant for applications requiring robust associative memory or discrete state representations in continuous-time RNNs.

A limitation, which the authors correctly acknowledge, is that the optimization procedure is non-local and therefore not a model of biological synaptic learning. However, as a normative framework that demonstrates *what* connectivity structure is required to achieve a computational goal, it provides a crucial benchmark against which future work on plausible learning rules can be compared.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Methodology:** The application of SSA-based stability optimization to the problem of storing multiple analog memories in a biologically constrained network is highly original and powerful.
*   **Strong Results:** The paper demonstrates robust recall performance and shows that the resulting networks exhibit several emergent properties (E/I balance, sparse weights, variability quenching) that align with experimental observations.
*   **Significant Contribution:** The work provides a compelling solution to a long-standing problem in theoretical neuroscience—how to store graded patterns in stable, biologically plausible networks—and opens new avenues for studying the principles of neural computation.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the evidence provided.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Non-Biological Learning:** The optimization method is algorithmic and not a plausible synaptic learning rule. This is a primary limitation, although the authors are transparent about it and frame the work as a normative study.
*   **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The paper demonstrates the method on a single network size and memory load. A more systematic analysis of storage capacity and how it scales with network parameters would have strengthened the contribution.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novel and effective methodology and its significant implications for understanding memory circuits, substantially outweigh its limitations. It represents a solid and important contribution to the field.
