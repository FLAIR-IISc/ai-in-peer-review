The paper proposes a novel framework for constructing analog memory networks based on a control-theoretic approach, aiming to overcome limitations of previous models regarding Dale’s law, graded activity, low firing rates, and excitation-inhibition balance. The core idea is to directly optimize network parameters—synaptic connectivity and inhibitory neuron firing rates—to ensure desired activity states are fixed points of the dynamics and are locally stable, leveraging the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization. The authors demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog patterns with robust performance even with noisy inputs, and observe balanced excitation and inhibition in the optimized networks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and a well-defined optimization objective. The use of the SSA for stability optimization is a strong point, addressing a critical challenge in neural network design. The experimental results, although presented on a relatively small network size (n=150), demonstrate successful storage and recall. The ablation study on synaptic weights provides valuable insights into the importance of sparsely connected networks. However, the claim of overcoming *all* limitations of previous models may be slightly overstated – the model still relies on rate-based dynamics, and the extension to spiking networks is only briefly discussed. The regularization term in the cost function and the heuristic choices for its parameters (ηs, ηF) could benefit from further justification. While the paper demonstrates successful recall, a more thorough analysis of the network's capacity – i.e., how many memories it can reliably store – would strengthen the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the motivation and limitations of existing approaches. The methods section provides sufficient detail for understanding the model and optimization procedure, although the mathematical notation is dense and might be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with control theory. The results section is well-structured and effectively uses figures to illustrate the key findings. One minor point: the explanation of the re-parameterization of the synaptic weights (Eq. 2) could be more intuitive.

**Originality:** The combination of control-theoretic methods, robust stability optimization (SSA), and explicit consideration of biological constraints (Dale’s law, realistic firing rates, E-I balance) represents a novel approach to memory network design. While previous work has explored some of these aspects individually, this paper integrates them into a comprehensive framework. The finding that sparsity emerges naturally from the optimization process is also noteworthy. Related work is adequately cited, covering key contributions in the field.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate a promising approach for building more biologically realistic and robust memory networks. The ability to store and recall graded analog patterns with balanced E-I dynamics is a significant advance over traditional binary models. The findings could have implications for understanding how memories are encoded and retrieved in the brain, and could inspire new algorithms for building artificial intelligence systems. The observed reduction of trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset is also an intriguing result with potential relevance to neural coding. However, the current work focuses on relatively small networks, and further research is needed to scale up the approach and assess its applicability to larger, more complex systems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to memory network design.
*   Successful demonstration of analog memory storage and recall with robust performance.
*   Integration of biological constraints and control-theoretic methods.
*   Emergence of sparsity and balanced E-I dynamics.
*   Potential for significant impact on understanding memory and building AI systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Relatively small network size limits the generalizability of the results.
*   Heuristic choices for regularization parameters require further justification.
*   Limited discussion of the extension to spiking networks.
*   Lack of a thorough analysis of network capacity.
*   The claim of overcoming *all* prior limitations may be overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural network modeling, addressing important limitations of existing approaches and offering a novel and promising framework for building more biologically realistic and robust memory networks. While some aspects could be further explored, the originality, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work justify acceptance to NIPS.
