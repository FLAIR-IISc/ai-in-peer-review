- Proposes a method to store and recall analog (graded) memories in recurrent networks while respecting biological constraints.  
- Addresses limitations of Hopfield-style models: Dale’s Law, binary patterns, high firing rates, and global inhibition.  
- Casts memory storage as a control problem, optimizing synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron firing rates to create stable fixed points.  
- Applies robust control (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA) to ensure stability of stored patterns.  
- Results produce balanced excitation-inhibition and sparse connectivity after optimization.  
- Demonstrates robust recall under noisy initial conditions and ongoing noise.  
- Incorporates biological realism: Dale’s Law, realistic firing-rate dynamics, and emergent sparsity.  
- Paper is well-written with clear explanations and informative figures.  
- Evaluation is thorough: stability analysis, recall performance, synaptic weight distributions, and E/I balance.  
- Compares performance to an “ideal observer” benchmark (Amit & Felsen).  
- Links model behavior to brain data (stimulus-onset reduction in trial-to-trial variability).  
- Novel contribution: use of SSA/robust control in designing recurrent memory networks.  
- Weakness: optimization using BFGS is likely computationally expensive and may not scale well.  
- Weakness: key hyperparameters (ηs, ηF, ε) are chosen heuristically without systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: experiments limited to a fixed network size (n = 150); scalability not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: rate-based model omits spike-based dynamics, limiting conclusions about temporal coding and precise timing.  
- Weakness: fixed gain function g(vi) may be limiting; more realistic/adaptive gains not explored.  
- Overall: strong, significant contribution with convincing results; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance (rating 8.5/10).
