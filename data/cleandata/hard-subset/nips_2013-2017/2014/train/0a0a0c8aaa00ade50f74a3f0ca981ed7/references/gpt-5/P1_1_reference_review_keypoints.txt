- Proposes a control-theoretic method to embed multiple graded analog memory patterns as stable attractors in rate networks that respect Dale’s law, sparse synapses, low firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance.  
- Uses an unbounded threshold-quadratic nonlinearity and achieves stability through inhibitory balance rather than output saturation.  
- Encodes memories as fixed points by minimizing squared velocity at target states and enforces robust local stability using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA).  
- Enforces Dale’s law via a softplus parameterization of weights and includes L2 regularization and per-memory inhibitory steady-state variables.  
- Optimization performed with L-BFGS; demonstrations show recall robustness, emergent weight sparsity, tight E/I balance, and variability quenching.  
- Novelty lies in combining Dale-constrained non-saturating dynamics, SSA-based stability optimization, and graded balanced memories—importing SSA from robust control is fresh.  
- Empirical results: successful storage/recall for n=150, m=30 (capacity ≈0.2), robustness to cue and ongoing noise, and a reasonable “ideal observer” baseline.  
- Soundness: objective directly targets fixed-pointness and stability; SSA is appropriate and differentiable, and authors track true spectral abscissa empirically.  
- Caveat: dv/dt is nonzero at targets, so claims rely on inferred nearby fixed points; authors should solve for actual fixed points post-optimization.  
- Limitations: capacity and scaling (m vs n, coding level, E/I ratio) not systematically quantified.  
- Computational cost: SSA-based training may be expensive and runtime/computational complexity are not reported.  
- Stability basins are not characterized beyond recall success probabilities.  
- Role of per-attractor inhibitory steady states is unclear—need to clarify whether they are training artifacts or reached during recall.  
- No spiking-network validation; mapping to spiking dynamics is argued but not demonstrated.  
- Clarity: overall clear and implementable for experts, but some details are deferred to Supplements and some hyperparameters are heuristic.  
- Recommend adding a sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters and explicitly reporting nE/nI and the precise definition of “capacity.”  
- Originality: considered novel relative to Hopfield-style and prior balanced-memory work that focused on binary patterns or saturating nonlinearities.  
- Significance: promising approach if scalable; current demonstrations are modest in scale and need broader validation to increase impact.  
- Pros: addresses multiple biological constraints jointly, uses principled SSA objective, demonstrates robustness and variability quenching, and shows emergent sparsity.  
- Cons: lacks systematic scaling/capacity analysis, uses global nonlocal optimization and per-attractor variables (biological plausibility concerns), fixed points not exactly enforced, no spiking comparison, and unclear computational reliability.  
- Suggested improvements: capacity scaling study; compute nearest true fixed points and their SA; clarify/use of inhibitory steady states during recall; report wall-clock time and convergence stats.  
- Additional suggestions: include ablations without SSA or with global inhibition, small-scale spiking instantiation, and fix reference formatting/related-work citations.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept—novel and promising but needs stronger capacity/fixed-point verification and scalability evidence.
