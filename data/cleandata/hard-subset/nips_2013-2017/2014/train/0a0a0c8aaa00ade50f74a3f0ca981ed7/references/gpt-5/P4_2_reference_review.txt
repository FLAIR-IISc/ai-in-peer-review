Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable fixed points in a biologically constrained, recurrent rate network with distinct excitatory and inhibitory populations (Dale’s law), sparse recurrent connectivity, non-saturating rectified-quadratic gains, and low-rate activity stabilized by dynamic E/I balance. Memory storage is formulated as enforcing, for each target pattern, (i) the fixed-point condition (dv/dt ≈ 0 at the target state) and (ii) local asymptotic stability (negative spectral abscissa of the Jacobian at the target). Because the spectral abscissa is nonsmooth, the method minimizes the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable upper bound to enforce stability, alongside an L2 weight penalty. Dale’s law is enforced via a softplus reparameterization of weights. The inhibitory steady-state coordinates for each pattern are treated as free variables during training, so the method learns inhibitory setpoints jointly with the synapses.

Empirically, the network (n=150; 100 E, 50 I) stores m=30 patterns (capacity ~0.2), exhibits robust recall from noisy cues, and performs competitively with an “ideal observer” that selects the closest stored pattern to the cue. Learned synapses display a heavy-tailed, sparse distribution; pruning small-magnitude weights leaves most attractors intact. The authors show tight E/I balance across memories and robustness to ongoing noise, including stimulus-driven reductions in trial-to-trial variability, consistent with cortical data. The work builds on and addresses limitations of classical Hopfield networks (binary encodings, symmetric weights), graded autoassociators without explicit inhibition, and balanced/spiking models relying on saturation or global inhibition. It leverages robust control ideas (SSA) previously used in related stabilized network control (e.g., Hennequin et al., 2014), applying them here to graded attractor storage under Dale constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of storage as fixed-point plus local stability constraints is sound. The use of SSA is appropriate for gradient-based optimization; the paper correctly notes the trade-off in the smoothing parameter ε. The reparameterization enforces Dale’s law cleanly. However, the work provides only local stability guarantees at target states; basin sizes and global dynamics (e.g., spurious attractors/limit cycles) are not analyzed theoretically. Capacity and scaling are demonstrated only on a small network with m=30; no systematic scaling analysis is given. The pruning experiment is informative but limited.
- Empirical support: Recall performance curves, comparison to an ideal observer, noise robustness, E/I balance diagnostics, and variability reduction are convincing demonstrations on the chosen setup. Ablations (e.g., removing SSA, varying ε, or regularization strength) and complexity/runtime analysis are missing. It would be helpful to quantify how performance depends on nE, nI, and m, and to report distributions of basin sizes.
- Reproducibility: Key details (objective, parameterization, optimizer) are given, but some implementation specifics are deferred to a table/supplement. Complexity of SSA evaluation and gradient computation is not discussed; code availability is not mentioned.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The objective and constraints are explicit, and the role of SSA is explained. A few issues:
- References are inconsistently formatted and in some cases incomplete.
- The choice and scaling of ε are heuristic; a brief complexity and sensitivity discussion would help.
- The statement that L2 regularization “does not promote sparsity” is correct; a clearer causal argument for emergent sparsity (e.g., via stability constraints) and a control comparing different regularizers would improve clarity.
- It would help to emphasize that inhibitory steady-state coordinates are optimized per memory only during training; at test time they arise from the learned dynamics.

Originality
The application of SSA-based robust stability optimization to embed multiple graded attractors in Dale-constrained E/I networks is a novel combination. Prior work addressed graded patterns, Dale constraints, or E/I balance separately, often relying on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibition. This work contributes a general recipe that (i) enforces local stability explicitly and (ii) yields non-saturating, low-rate balanced recall. The conceptual link to robust control is a strength, though SSA has been used before in related contexts. A more comprehensive comparison to other general-purpose network training methods that stabilize target states (e.g., FORCE learning for continuous-time RNNs) would better position the contribution.

Significance
The framework is a useful step toward constructing biologically more realistic analog memory networks that avoid reliance on saturation and global inhibition. Demonstrating robust recall and E/I balance under noise is valuable. However, the current scale (n=150) and lack of capacity/scaling analysis limit the immediate impact. Providing evidence that the approach scales and clarifying computational costs would increase significance. Bridging to spiking implementations, as suggested, would further enhance relevance.

Pros
- Clear formulation of storage as fixed-point plus robust local stability under Dale’s law.
- Use of SSA enables efficient gradient-based stability optimization.
- Demonstrated robust recall with competitive performance to an ideal observer.
- Emergent sparse weight distributions and tight E/I balance without saturating nonlinearities.
- Noise robustness and variability quenching consistent with neurophysiology.

Cons
- No systematic capacity or scaling analysis; results limited to small networks and m=30.
- Only local stability is enforced; basin sizes and global dynamics are not quantified.
- Limited ablations (SSA necessity, ε sensitivity, effect of regularization); no complexity/runtime analysis.
- Comparative baselines to alternative training frameworks are missing.
- References need cleanup; some methodological details are deferred.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide scaling experiments: capacity versus n, nE/nI ratios, and m; report computational costs.
- Quantify basin sizes across memories; examine spurious attractors/limit cycles.
- Add ablations: remove SSA (fixed-point only), vary ε, L2 strength; compare L1 to probe sparsity origins.
- Compare to alternative training methods (e.g., FORCE/recursive least squares, Lyapunov-based designs) and to models with global inhibition.
- Explore transfer to spiking implementations using matched gains.
- Improve reference formatting and include missing related work.

Overall assessment
A solid and technically sound contribution that combines robust control tools with biologically constrained recurrent networks to realize analog attractor memories with balanced E/I dynamics. The approach is promising and clearly presented, but the paper would benefit from capacity/scaling analysis, ablations, and stronger comparisons. Arguments favoring acceptance are the principled stability optimization under Dale constraints and the robust, low-rate balanced recall; arguments against are limited scale and evaluation breadth.
