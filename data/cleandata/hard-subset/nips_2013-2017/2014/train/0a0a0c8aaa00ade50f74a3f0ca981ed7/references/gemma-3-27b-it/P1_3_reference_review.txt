## Peer Review: Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing recurrent neural networks capable of storing and recalling analog memories. The authors address limitations of classical models (Hopfield networks and their extensions) by incorporating biologically plausible features: separation of excitatory and inhibitory neurons (Dale’s Law), recurrent and sparse connectivity, graded activity levels, low firing rates, and a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition. They formulate memory storage as a control problem, optimizing network parameters – synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron firing rates – to create stable fixed points corresponding to desired memory patterns. Crucially, they employ robust control theory (specifically, Smoothed Spectral Abscissa minimization) to ensure stability, overcoming challenges posed by positive feedback loops. Results demonstrate successful storage of multiple graded memories, robust recall in the presence of noise, and a dynamically balanced E/I state.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively situates itself within the landscape of memory modeling. It acknowledges the limitations of the original Hopfield network (binary states, symmetry requirements) and subsequent attempts to address these (non-binary patterns, LIF neurons).  It distinguishes itself from recent work focusing on LIF neurons with saturating dynamics or spatially connected networks with specialized connectivity patterns. The authors build on recent advances in robust control theory (SSA minimization) applied to neural networks [13, 14], and importantly, extend this to a biologically realistic E-I network context. The work contrasts with spike-timing based memory networks [12] by explicitly including inhibitory units.  The authors clearly highlight the gap in existing literature regarding a general framework for stable, graded memory recall in realistic E-I networks.

**Strengths:**

* **Biologically Plausible Model:** The network architecture adheres to several key biological constraints (Dale’s Law, realistic firing rates, E/I balance), making it a more plausible model of cortical memory circuits.
* **Robustness:** The demonstrated robustness to noise in both the initial recall cue and ongoing network dynamics is a significant strength. This addresses a major limitation of many theoretical memory models.
* **Novel Control-Theoretic Approach:**  Framing memory storage as a control problem and leveraging SSA minimization is a creative and effective approach to achieving stable fixed points and overcoming issues with positive feedback.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methods, and results. The inclusion of Figure 1 to visually illustrate the analog memory patterns is helpful.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The authors provide a thorough evaluation, assessing both storage capacity, recall performance, sparsity of connections, and the dynamic E/I balance.
* **Good Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper is well-referenced and clearly positions itself within the broader field of memory research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Rate-Based Model:** While acknowledged, the reliance on a rate-based model limits the biological realism and applicability to spiking neural networks.  Further discussion on how the findings might translate to spiking systems would be valuable.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The heuristic choice of parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of parameter space, or a justification for the selected values, would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Capacity:** The reported capacity of 0.2 (30 memories in a 150-neuron network) is relatively low. While the authors demonstrate successful storage, scaling up the network to achieve higher capacity remains an open question.
* **Fixed Network Size:**  The study focuses on a fixed network size (n=150). Investigating how performance scales with network size would provide valuable insights.
* **Justification for Log-Normal Distribution:** The choice of a log-normal distribution for generating memory patterns is motivated by observed firing rate distributions, but a more rigorous justification for this choice, perhaps through comparison with other distributions, would be beneficial.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for constructing robust analog memory networks.
* The results demonstrate successful storage and recall of graded memories with a level of robustness not typically seen in existing models.
* The use of robust control theory represents a significant methodological advance.
* The biologically plausible constraints incorporated into the model enhance its relevance to understanding real neural circuits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The rate-based model and limited capacity are potential drawbacks.
* The parameter tuning feels somewhat ad-hoc.
* While the results are promising, the scalability of the approach remains an open question.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. While the rate-based model and relatively low capacity are weaknesses, the novel approach, demonstrated robustness, and theoretical soundness outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides a valuable framework for understanding how analog memories could be implemented in biologically plausible neural circuits.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting the paper pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically:

1.  **Expand the Discussion on Spiking Networks:** Briefly discuss how the optimized connectivity could be used in a spiking network and the potential challenges/modifications required.
2.  **Justify Parameter Choices:** Provide a more detailed justification for the selected parameter values (ηs, ηF, ε), or conduct a sensitivity analysis.
3.  **Address Scalability:**  Acknowledge the limited capacity and briefly discuss potential avenues for scaling up the network.
