- Proposes a control-theoretic framework that optimizes synaptic connectivity to store analog memory patterns as stable fixed points in a balanced E-I network obeying Dale’s law.  
- Uses the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control as a differentiable stability criterion to enforce fixed-point stability.  
- Demonstrates robust memory recall under noise, balanced E-I activity, and realistic firing rates; includes ablation studies (e.g., removing weak synapses).  
- Acknowledges limitations such as using a rate-based model instead of spiking networks and discusses storage-capacity trade-offs.  
- Technically sound: formalizes fixed-point and stability conditions and provides a clear optimization procedure.  
- Clever application of SSA solves a key technical challenge previous models faced with stability and differentiability.  
- Experiments are well-designed and reproducible, with informative figures and parameter details.  
- Ablation studies provide insights into network structure and the role of weak synapses.  
- Storage capacity reported is relatively low (≈0.2 for 30 memories with 150 neurons) compared to some binary models.  
- Does not analyze basin-of-attraction sizes thoroughly, limiting understanding of robustness to large perturbations.  
- Computational complexity and scalability of the optimization procedure are not discussed in detail.  
- Paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulation and motivating exposition.  
- Figures (especially 1–5) effectively aid understanding and the methods section supports reproducibility.  
- The link between SSA optimization, ε scaling, and biological plausibility needs clearer exposition.  
- Implementation details of the BFGS optimization and convergence behavior could be clarified for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel use of robust control to create stable analog attractors in biologically constrained E-I networks.  
- Simultaneously addresses multiple prior limitations (Dale’s law, graded activity, low firing rates, balance), marking a significant advancement.  
- Caveat: optimization-for-memory is not entirely new—paper should better emphasize how the analog setting fundamentally changes the problem.  
- Significance: fills a fundamental gap in modeling biologically realistic analog memory and suggests experimentally testable mechanisms (balanced E-I, sparse connectivity).  
- Framework is potentially generalizable to other problems requiring stable attractors and aligns with experimental observations about noise robustness and variability reduction.  
- Does not fully explore extensions to more complex memory tasks or biologically plausible learning rules.  
- Connection to spiking-network implementations is discussed but lacks concrete pathways or proposals for translation.  
- Overall assessment: strong, technically rigorous, original contribution that is well-written and merits acceptance despite noted limitations.  
- Recommendation: accept — novel method, solid experiments, and meaningful implications for theoretical and experimental neuroscience.  
- Arguments for acceptance: innovative robust-control integration, biologically realistic analog memory, robustness to noise, balanced E-I activity, and clear methodology with ablation validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modest storage capacity, reliance on rate-based modeling without direct spiking translation, and limited basin-of-attraction analysis.
