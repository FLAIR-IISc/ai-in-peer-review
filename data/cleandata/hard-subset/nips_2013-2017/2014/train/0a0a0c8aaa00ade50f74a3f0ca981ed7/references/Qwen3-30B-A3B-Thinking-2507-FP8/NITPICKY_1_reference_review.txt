

# Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing balanced E-I neural networks that can store and recall analog memory patterns. The authors address limitations of previous memory models by respecting Dale's law, using graded activity patterns, and ensuring dynamic excitation-inhibition balance. They optimize network parameters to satisfy fixed-point conditions and stability via the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA), enabling robust memory recall with low firing rates.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by formalizing memory storage as fixed-point conditions with stability constraints. The use of SSA for stability optimization is novel and well-justified. The experimental results demonstrate robust memory recall with balanced E-I inputs, sparse connectivity, and graded activity patterns - addressing key limitations of previous models. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate theoretical grounding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Equation Errors:** Equation (4) states "dv/dt|v=vµ = 0" but the derivative is with respect to time, not the state variable. This is technically incorrect and could confuse readers. The correct expression should be "dv/dt|t=t0 = 0" or "dv/dt|v=vµ = 0" (which is what they have, but needs clarification).

2. **Figure and Caption Inconsistencies:** Figure 1c labels the y-axis "frequency" while the caption states "firing rate [Hz]". This creates confusion between probability density and actual firing rates. Figure 1a's caption mentions "Hz exc. (prescribed distribution)" but the plot shows "exc. neurons" as the x-axis label, creating a mismatch between visual and textual information.

3. **Reference Formatting Issues:** The reference list contains numerous formatting errors. Many entries have " , , , " instead of proper citation details, and reference [1] is missing publication year. This suggests insufficient attention to detail in the final manuscript preparation.

4. **Vague Parameter Justification:** The paper states γ = 0.04 "such that g(vi) spans a few tens of Hz when vi spans a few tens of mV" but provides no quantitative support for this choice. The exact relationship between vi (mV) and firing rates (Hz) should be more precisely justified given the paper's emphasis on technical rigor.

5. **Missing Implementation Details:** The optimization procedure mentions "a variant of the low-storage BFGS algorithm" but doesn't specify which variant or why it was chosen over alternatives. The paper also doesn't provide computational complexity analysis or convergence guarantees for the optimization.

6. **Incomplete Comparison to Baselines:** The paper claims "remarkably, our network dynamics (continuous lines) and the ideal observer (dashed lines) have comparable performances" but doesn't quantify the performance difference or provide statistical significance testing. The comparison lacks proper statistical analysis.

7. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses "firing rate" and "activity" interchangeably in some places, while elsewhere it distinguishes between the two. This creates confusion about whether the model is truly rate-based or incorporates spike timing.

8. **Methodological Gap in Stability Analysis:** The paper states "the SSA depends on the Jacobian matrix elements {Jµij}, which in turn depend both on the connectivity parameters {βij} and on vµinh." However, it doesn't explicitly show how vµinh affects the Jacobian, which is critical for understanding the optimization process.

9. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of using rate-based models instead of spiking models, despite claiming applicability to "understanding spiking memory networks" in the discussion. The connection between rate and spiking models needs stronger justification.

10. **Formatting Inconsistency in Results:** The paper states "the network was initialized prior to learning with the baseline as the single global attractor" but doesn't provide evidence of this initialization in the results. The claim that "the network was initialized with the baseline as the single global attractor" isn't supported by the presented data.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound approach to constructing balanced E-I memory networks with analog patterns, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and lack of precise justification for key parameter choices undermine the paper's rigor. As a perfectionist reviewer who prioritizes meticulous attention to detail, I find these flaws sufficiently significant to warrant rejection, despite the paper's interesting conceptual contributions.

The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the field and makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on neural memory models. However, the technical and presentation issues are too numerous and significant to overlook in a NIPS submission, where precision and clarity are paramount. These issues suggest the paper may not have undergone sufficient peer review or editing before submission.

**Recommendation:** Reject

The paper's technical contributions are noteworthy, but the numerous minor flaws in methodology, notation, and presentation prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for precision and rigor. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission to a venue that values meticulous attention to detail.
