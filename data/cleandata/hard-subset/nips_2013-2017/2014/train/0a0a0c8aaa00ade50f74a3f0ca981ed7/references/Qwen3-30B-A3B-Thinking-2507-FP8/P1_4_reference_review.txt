

# Peer Review: "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in theoretical neuroscience: constructing biologically plausible neural network models that can store and recall analog (graded) memory patterns while respecting key biological constraints (Dale's law, balanced E/I dynamics, low firing rates). The authors propose a control-theoretic framework that directly optimizes network parameters to satisfy two conditions: (1) target activity patterns must be fixed points of the dynamics, and (2) these fixed points must be stable. They overcome the stability optimization challenge by using the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable surrogate for the spectral abscissa. The method successfully generates networks that store multiple analog patterns as stable fixed points with realistic firing rates and balanced excitation-inhibition dynamics, demonstrating robust memory recall even with noisy inputs.

This work builds upon and significantly extends previous memory models (e.g., Hopfield networks, LIF-based models) by addressing multiple limitations simultaneously: it respects Dale's law (unlike Hopfield), uses graded patterns (unlike binary models), avoids reliance on saturating transfer functions (unlike many LIF-based models), and achieves balanced E/I dynamics (unlike previous models with global inhibition). The paper provides a compelling contrast with prior work in its introduction, clearly articulating how their approach overcomes previous limitations.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous mathematical formulation. The authors carefully address both fixed-point conditions and stability constraints, providing a well-justified optimization framework. Their experimental validation is comprehensive, including detailed analysis of recall performance, robustness to noise, synaptic sparsity, and E/I balance. They demonstrate that their networks achieve biologically plausible low firing rates (5-10 Hz) without relying on saturation mechanisms, and the results consistently support their claims.

**Originality:** This is a genuinely novel contribution. The application of SSA for stability optimization in neural networks is innovative, and the authors successfully combine multiple biological constraints in a single framework. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work (Hopfield, binary models, spatially structured networks) and presents a significant advancement over previous attempts to model graded memory patterns in E-I networks.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Fig. 1 showing analog patterns, Fig. 5 demonstrating E/I balance). The connection between the mathematical framework and biological plausibility is consistently emphasized, making the paper accessible to both computational neuroscience and machine learning audiences.

**Significance:** The work provides a biologically grounded framework for understanding memory storage that could bridge the gap between theoretical models and empirical neuroscience. The demonstration of robust analog memory recall with balanced E/I dynamics aligns with experimental observations in cortical networks. This could open new research avenues for understanding memory mechanisms in the brain and inform the design of more biologically plausible artificial neural networks.

## Weaknesses

**Connection to Biological Learning Rules:** While the paper demonstrates the feasibility of the approach, it doesn't address how such a network could be learned through biologically plausible synaptic plasticity rules. The authors acknowledge this as a future direction but don't connect their optimization framework to existing plasticity mechanisms (e.g., inhibitory plasticity), which limits the immediate applicability to neuroscience.

**Scalability and Capacity:** The paper demonstrates a storage capacity of 0.2 (30 patterns in 150 neurons), which is lower than some binary memory models. The authors don't thoroughly explore how this capacity scales with network size or how it compares to theoretical limits for analog patterns. A more detailed analysis of capacity limits would strengthen the significance claim.

**Spiking Network Connection:** The rate-based model is a simplification, and while the authors briefly discuss the connection to spiking models, they don't provide evidence that their approach would transfer to spiking networks. The gain function they use is justified as physiologically plausible, but a more concrete demonstration of this would be valuable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network models of memory. The authors successfully address multiple longstanding limitations in the literature with a novel, technically sound approach. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and makes a compelling case for the biological plausibility of their framework.

The main limitations (connection to biological learning rules, capacity analysis, spiking network connection) are important but do not undermine the paper's core contributions. These are natural directions for future work, and the authors have appropriately identified them as such.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper represents a significant advance in the modeling of memory in neural networks that respects key biological constraints. It provides a novel framework that could influence both theoretical neuroscience and computational modeling, and the results are well-supported by rigorous analysis and experiments. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

## Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Successfully addresses multiple limitations of previous memory models simultaneously
- Novel application of SSA for stability optimization in neural networks
- Demonstrates biologically plausible graded memory recall with balanced E/I dynamics
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple metrics
- Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of biological learning rules for implementation
- Storage capacity analysis could be more thorough
- Connection to spiking networks is somewhat speculative

This paper is a valuable contribution that advances our understanding of how memory could be implemented in biologically plausible neural circuits. It should be accepted for publication at NeurIPS.
