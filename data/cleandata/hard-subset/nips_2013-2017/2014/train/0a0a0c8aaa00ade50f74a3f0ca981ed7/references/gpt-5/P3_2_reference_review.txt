Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable attractors in a recurrent rate-based network that enforces Dale’s law and achieves dynamic E/I balance. Neurons have a rectified-quadratic transfer function; synaptic weights are parameterized to be strictly excitatory or inhibitory via a softplus transform. Given a set of desired excitatory patterns, the authors optimize both the synaptic matrix and the inhibitory population’s steady-state activities per pattern by minimizing (i) a fixed-point residual (the norm of dv/dt at the target states), (ii) a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa (the smoothed spectral abscissa, SSA) to enforce local stability, and (iii) an L2 penalty on weights. They demonstrate recall robustness to noisy cues and ongoing noise, emergent sparsity of synaptic weights, tight E/I balance across memories, and reduced trial-to-trial variability following stimulus onset. Performance is compared to an “ideal observer” nearest-neighbor baseline and is similar at tested noise levels. The storage example uses n=150 (100 E, 50 I) and m=30 patterns.

Relation to prior work
This paper sits at the intersection of associative memory and balanced network theory. It goes beyond classical Hopfield models (binary units/weights and symmetry) and graded-response extensions (Treves & Rolls) by enforcing Dale’s law and maintaining low rates without relying on hard saturation. It is also related to balanced networks and the stabilized supralinear network (e.g., Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham; Ahmadian et al.) but focuses on storing arbitrary graded patterns rather than global inhibition or spatially-structured “bumps.” The use of SSA from robust control is novel in this context and builds on prior control-theoretic optimization of recurrent dynamics (e.g., Hennequin et al., 2014). As far as I am aware, this is among the first demonstrations of robust, low-rate, analog attractors in a Dale-compliant E/I network optimized directly for local stability across many target states.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casting memory embedding as a joint fixed-point and robust stability optimization problem using SSA is elegant and powerful.
- Biological realism: Dale’s law, sparse recurrent connectivity, low firing rates without saturation, dynamic E/I balance, and variability quenching are all well-aligned with cortical phenomenology.
- Empirical validation: recall accuracy versus cue noise compares favorably to an ideal-observer bound; recall persists under ongoing noise; inhibitory/excitatory currents are tightly balanced.
- Emergent sparsity: interestingly heavy-tailed weight distributions emerge without explicit L1 penalties; pruning tests suggest recall relies on a subset of strong synapses.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability and capacity: Results are shown for a single small network (n=150, m=30; “capacity” ~0.2). There is no systematic scaling analysis with n, sparsity, or pattern statistics, nor comparisons to known capacity results for analog memories. How does capacity scale with n, m/n, and choice of ε in SSA?
- Optimizing inhibitory steady states: Treating v_inh^μ as free per-memory variables improves feasibility but weakens biological plausibility. In recall, the inhibitory activities should emerge from the same W and inputs. Because dv/dt residuals are not exactly zero, it’s unclear if the learned v_inh^μ correspond to genuine fixed points or are acting as slack variables during training. Please quantify the mismatch (e.g., report dv/dt norms and SA at the converged fixed points found by simulation) and show that the simulated fixed point for each memory has SA<0.
- SSA vs SA guarantees: ε is chosen heuristically; the paper asserts SA decreases but is not always negative during training. Please include histograms of SA and SSA after training for all memories, and sensitivity to ε; otherwise robust stability is not fully guaranteed.
- Basins of attraction and spurious attractors: Local stability does not address basin size or the presence of unintended attractors. The recall metric is informative, but a more systematic analysis (e.g., retrieval radius per memory, rates of convergence, existence/number of spurious attractors) would strengthen the claims.
- Comparisons and ablations: Beyond the ideal-observer bound, comparisons to classical graded autoassociative networks, to networks with global inhibition, or to alternatives that stabilize low-rate attractors (e.g., via saturation) would contextualize the benefits of SSA-based optimization. Ablate L2 regularization and Dale’s law to quantify their roles. Provide pruning curves rather than a single threshold; report units for the 0.01 cutoff and its relation to the learned weight scale.
- Computational cost: The approach optimizes O(n^2 + n_I m) parameters and repeatedly evaluates Jacobians/eigenspectra for m memories; complexity likely scales as O(m n^3). Discussion of runtime and scaling to larger n is needed.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper references parameters in Table 1 and Supplementary Material not provided here. Code or detailed pseudo-code would help. Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted; please fix.
- Biological interpretation: While the resulting networks display E/I balance, the learning is global and nonlocal. It would be valuable to discuss whether inhibitory plasticity (e.g., Vogels et al.) or other local rules could approximate the SSA objective, and whether the emergent inhibitory patterns are interpretable (e.g., can I activity be predicted from E via W_EI).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated control-theoretic approach to a longstanding problem.
- Enforces key biological constraints and reproduces several cortical phenomena.
- Demonstrates robust analog recall with Dale’s law and low rates.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scale and capacity characterization; unclear generalization to larger systems.
- The use of per-memory inhibitory setpoints raises plausibility concerns unless shown to correspond to true fixed points with SA<0.
- Missing comparisons and ablations; pruning and stability guarantees need deeper quantification.

Quality: Good conceptual and methodological contribution; empirical support is promising but would benefit from broader evaluation.
Clarity: Generally clear; some missing details and references need attention.
Originality: High in the use of SSA for memory embedding in E/I networks.
Significance: Potentially impactful for computational neuroscience and control of RNNs, pending scalability and robustness demonstrations.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing the stability guarantees (SA after training), adding capacity/scaling and pruning analyses, and clarifying the role of optimized inhibitory steady states. Confidence: High.
