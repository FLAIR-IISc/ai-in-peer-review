Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct rate-based recurrent networks with separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations (obeying Dale’s law) that store multiple analog (graded) attractor memories. The network uses a rectified quadratic transfer function without saturation; stability is achieved via dynamic E/I balance rather than single-neuron saturation. The authors formalize memory storage as (i) making the target patterns fixed points (small dv/dt at the patterns) and (ii) enforcing local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target state. They jointly optimize all recurrent weights (respecting Dale’s law through a reparameterization) and, crucially, the inhibitory steady-state activities at each attractor (as “auxiliary” variables) using L-BFGS on a composite loss. They demonstrate robust recall of 30 analog patterns in a 150-neuron network (capacity ≈ 0.2), performance comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor “observer,” emergent sparsity of synaptic weights, tight E/I balance across attractors, robustness to ongoing noise, and stimulus-driven variability quenching reminiscent of cortical data. The paper positions itself against Hopfield-style binary attractors [Hopfield ’82], graded activity variants [Treves & Rolls], and more recent E/I and balanced-network models [Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham; Ahmadian et al.], arguing that prior models either relied on saturation, global inhibition, or effectively binary patterns.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Casts embedding analog attractors in biologically constrained E/I networks as a robust stability optimization problem, importing SSA from control theory. This is novel and broadly relevant to dynamics-first approaches in ML/neuroscience.
- Biological realism: Enforces Dale’s law, achieves low-rate, balanced operating regimes without saturating nonlinearities, and reproduces variability quenching on stimulus onset.
- Empirical performance: Robust recall with noise; performance comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor classifier; demonstration of E/I balance across attractors per neuron.
- Emergent sparsity: Despite L2 regularization (not L1), many weights become near-zero; pruning weak weights preserves most attractors, suggesting some robustness to synaptic perturbations.
- Clear objectives and diagnostics: Simultaneous tracking of dv/dt, SA, and SSA; explicit Jacobian-based stability analysis.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- Over-parameterization via auxiliary inhibitory variables: The optimization treats the inhibitory steady-state rates v_inh^μ for each memory as free variables (n_I × m additional degrees of freedom). While these are not clamped at test time, the large number of per-memory latent variables may make the feasibility of stability constraints easier and inflate apparent capacity. It would be important to constrain these auxiliary variables (e.g., require they be generated by the same network dynamics without per-memory freedom), or show that similar performance holds when v_inh^μ are not optimized independently.
- Limited capacity and scaling analysis: Only one working point (n=150, m=30) is presented. No systematic scaling with N, m/N, or with sparsity. Compared to classic and modern results (including analog patterns), the reported capacity is modest and lacks theory or empirical scaling curves.
- Computational practicality: SSA evaluation and Jacobian eigen-structure are O(n^3). No training time or convergence reliability is reported, nor evidence of robustness across random initializations. It is unclear how the method scales beyond a few hundred neurons.
- Structural sparsity vs functional small weights: The network starts dense; post-training many weights are small but not strictly zero. Biological claims about sparse connectivity would be stronger with hard structural sparsity constraints (fixed in-degree/out-degree) during optimization.
- Basin size: The method optimizes local stability (SSA) and attains small but non-zero dv/dt at targets (“slow points”), relying on the existence of nearby true fixed points. Basin sizes are not quantified; failure modes at higher noise are observed.
- Biological plausibility of large, canceling currents: The balanced regime entails large E and I inputs that finely cancel; magnitude distributions and sensitivity to weight perturbations (beyond pruning tiny weights) are not characterized.
- Comparisons: No quantitative comparison to other E/I balanced attractor constructions or recent robust attractor-learning methods; the nearest-neighbor “ideal observer” is an upper bound for decoding, not a model competitor.

Clarity
- Overall well written and well structured. However, important details are deferred to Supplementary Material (e.g., Table 1 values, mean-field initialization). Reference formatting is incomplete. Some choices (ε scaling for SSA, choice of ηs, ηF) are heuristic with limited justification. Reporting of τi, hi, and numerical tolerances would help reproducibility.

Originality
- High. Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to embed analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks appears novel and bridges control theory and computational neuroscience.

Significance
- Potentially significant for designing stable recurrent networks with analog memories under biological constraints. However, the absence of capacity scaling, the reliance on per-memory inhibitory latent variables, and unclear scalability reduce immediate impact for large-scale memory modeling or ML applications.

Questions for the authors
1. How does capacity scale with N and with the number of inhibitory neurons? Does optimizing v_inh^μ materially increase achievable m compared to fixing v_inh^μ (e.g., to baseline or to values determined by a single shared inhibitory subnetwork)?
2. Can you constrain the inhibitory auxiliaries to be generated by a learned function of excitatory activity (e.g., same W with no per-memory degrees of freedom) and retain performance?
3. What is training time and success rate across seeds? How does training behave for n≥1000?
4. How sensitive is recall to weight noise (e.g., Gaussian perturbations of trained weights, or 10–20% synapse deletions beyond clipping tiny weights)?
5. Can you demonstrate structural sparsity by enforcing a fixed connection probability during optimization?
6. Do your results generalize beyond log-normal patterns (e.g., correlated patterns, low-rank structure), and to partial cues?
7. Can you port the learned W to an LIF network and retain recall/stability, as claimed?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide capacity vs N curves, with and without optimizing v_inh^μ, and with structural sparsity constraints.
- Replace per-memory inhibitory auxiliaries with a restricted parameterization (e.g., a single set of inhibitory biases and recurrent weights) or a learned mapping from E to I to remove per-memory degrees of freedom.
- Report training complexity, convergence statistics, and ablations on ε, ηs, ηF. Include code.
- Quantify basin sizes and robustness to synaptic perturbations beyond pruning small weights.
- Add comparisons to alternative E/I attractor models and modern energy-based or control-theoretic approaches.
- Demonstrate transfer to spiking neurons with matched f–I curves.
- Clean up references and include missing details (Table 1, τi, hi).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled stability-optimization framework; strong conceptual bridge to robust control.
- First clear demonstration of analog attractors with Dale’s law, low rates, and balanced E/I without relying on saturation.
- Robust empirical phenomena (noise robustness, variability quenching), emergent sparsity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Over-parameterization via per-memory inhibitory variables; unclear necessity/biological plausibility.
- No capacity/scaling analysis; modest demonstrated capacity.
- Limited comparative evaluation and unclear computational scalability.
- Structural sparsity and robustness not rigorously established.

Recommendation: Weak reject (score: 5/10). The approach is promising and could be impactful with additional experiments addressing capacity/scaling, inhibitory parameterization, structural sparsity, and robustness. I encourage resubmission with these additions.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
