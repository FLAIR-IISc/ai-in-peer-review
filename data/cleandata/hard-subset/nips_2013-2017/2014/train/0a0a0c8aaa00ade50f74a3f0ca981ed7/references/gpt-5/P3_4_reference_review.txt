Summary
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a recurrent rate network that respects key biological constraints: explicit excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) segregation (Dale’s law), recurrent connectivity, non-saturating single-neuron gains, low firing rates, and dynamic E/I balance. The authors formulate storage as a constrained optimization: for each target pattern over excitatory neurons, they minimize (i) the norm of dv/dt at that point to enforce a fixed point (or near-fixed point), and (ii) the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian to enforce local asymptotic stability. Inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory are treated as free auxiliary variables during optimization. They use a log-normal distribution of target excitatory rates, a rectified-quadratic gain, and an L-BFGS solver to minimize a composite objective with an L2 weight penalty. They demonstrate: successful recall for m = 30 patterns in n = 150 neurons (capacity ~0.2), robustness to noisy initial cues and ongoing noise, tight E/I balance, emergent sparsity of synaptic weights without explicit L1 regularization, and stimulus-driven variability quenching reminiscent of cortical data. They argue this overcomes limitations of classical Hopfield-like models, graded memory models without explicit inhibition, and previous E/I LIF models relying on saturation or global inhibition.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent and the use of SSA is appropriate for differentiable stability optimization. The Dale-constrained parametrization is standard and sensible. Simulations support the claims of robust recall, E/I balance, and variability reduction.
- Caveats: (i) Only local stability is optimized. SSA<0 guarantees local asymptotic stability but says nothing about basin size or global dynamics; spurious attractors or limit cycles are not analyzed. (ii) The fixed-point condition is enforced via a penalty on dv/dt rather than as a hard constraint; the authors acknowledge residual drift and argue an exact fixed point exists nearby. A more principled equality-constrained approach (or an explicit Newton step to solve dv/dt=0 post-optimization) would strengthen the claim. (iii) Inhibitory steady-state rates are free per memory during training. While biologically plausible (I rates adapt across memories), this increases degrees of freedom and may inflate capacity; an ablation constraining I patterns (e.g., bounded range, distributional prior, or shared low-dimensional subspace) would clarify necessity. (iv) Capacity is assessed on a single scale (n=150, m=30). No scaling analysis vs. n, m, or comparison to analytical capacity bounds for graded memories is provided. (v) The pruning analysis uses a single threshold and changes the recall success criterion post hoc; a systematic sparsification curve would be more convincing. (vi) Computational complexity and convergence properties of the SSA-based training are not characterized; this matters for scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The modeling assumptions, optimization objective, and evaluation metrics are well explained. However:
- Some critical details are relegated to a missing Table/Supplement (e.g., learning rates, regularization weights, mean-field initialization values). Reproducibility would benefit from a complete parameter table and pseudo-code or code release.
- The choice and sensitivity of ε in SSA, and the gap between SA and SSA during/after training, should be reported quantitatively.
- Units and magnitudes for noise and inputs could be better contextualized.

Originality
- The combination is novel: embedding multiple graded attractors in a Dale-constrained rate network without saturating gains, with stability enforced via SSA. Prior graded memory models typically lack explicit inhibitory populations or rely on global inhibition; prior balanced E/I models with memory often use binary-like patterns or saturation. Using SSA from robust control to shape neural dynamics is a meaningful methodological contribution extending earlier uses for transient control.
- Related work is mostly acknowledged, though a deeper comparison to clustered balanced networks and manifold attractors would help situate the contribution.

Significance
- Scientifically relevant: provides a constructive recipe to build analog attractor networks with realistic E/I structure and balanced operation. The results on E/I balancing across memories and variability reduction are compelling and in line with cortical phenomenology.
- Potential impact: The approach could inform both theoretical neuroscience and the training of ML RNNs with prescribed fixed points and stability properties. However, significance would be stronger with capacity scaling results, constrained inhibitory degrees of freedom, and a demonstration that the learned connectivity transfers to spiking implementations.

Specific strengths
- Principled stability optimization using SSA with gradients.
- Dale-consistent parametrization and emergent E/I balance without saturating transfer.
- Compelling demonstrations: robust recall under substantial cue corruption and ongoing noise; variability quenching; emergent heavy-tailed weight distribution; limited dependence on many weak synapses.
- Clear conceptual separation: specify E patterns; let I adapt to ensure stability.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Basin size and global dynamics: quantify basins (e.g., success vs. cue noise across memories; energy-like Lyapunov analysis if available) and characterize spurious attractors.
- Capacity and scaling: study recall probability as a function of m/n for several n; analyze sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF; report compute time.
- Inhibitory degrees of freedom: constrain I patterns (range, distribution, low-dimensionality) or share inhibitory parameters across memories; report whether recall remains successful and how weight structure changes.
- Fixed-point enforcement: add an exact solve for dv/dt=0 after optimization and compare; or use an augmented Lagrangian.
- Pruning: provide performance vs. pruning curve and compare to L1 regularization; ensure a consistent success threshold.
- Robustness: test with different single-neuron nonlinearities (e.g., rectified power exponents, mild saturation) and with sparser initial connectivity.
- Clarity: include full parameter table, convergence criteria, and SA–SSA trajectories; fix incomplete citations.

Pros
- Novel and principled method; addresses key biological constraints.
- Strong qualitative and quantitative demonstrations of balanced analog memory recall.
- Bridges robust control and neural memory modeling.

Cons
- Limited scaling/capacity analysis; per-memory inhibitory freedom may overfit.
- Only local stability ensured; basins and global attractors not characterized.
- Some evaluations (pruning, thresholding) are ad hoc; reproducibility details missing.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a compelling and original framework with clear neuroscientific relevance and methodological novelty. Acceptance would be strengthened by additional analyses on scaling, inhibitory constraints, and basin characterization, many of which seem feasible within the current setup.
