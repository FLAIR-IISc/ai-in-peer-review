- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent networks that store/recall analog memories by optimizing synaptic weights and inhibitory firing rates to create stable fixed points using SSA.  
- Demonstrates a biologically plausible E-I network obeying Dale’s Law with emergent E-I balance and robust recall from noisy cues.  
- Positions the work relative to Hopfield and subsequent models, addressing binary/symmetry/capacity/saturation limitations by jointly optimizing stability and fixed points.  
- Key innovation: employing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) from robust control theory for stability optimization.  
- Strength: tackles the core problem of robust, high-capacity analog memory with a principled approach.  
- Strength: novel combination of control-theoretic optimization and biologically realistic E-I architecture.  
- Strength: biological plausibility via Dale’s Law, realistic firing-rate dynamics, and emergent balance.  
- Strength: strong empirical results showing storage/recall performance comparable to an ideal observer and robustness to noise.  
- Strength: thorough analysis of learned networks, including synaptic sparsity and excitation–inhibition relationships.  
- Strength: generally clear and well-organized writing that makes the methodology accessible.  
- Weakness: SSA-based optimization appears computationally expensive and scalability to larger networks (beyond n=150) is not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: heuristic parameter choices (ηs, ηF, ε) lack full justification and may require extensive tuning.  
- Weakness: limited exploration of how optimized parameters could be learned via biologically plausible local plasticity rules.  
- Weakness: rate-based simplification omits spiking dynamics, and bridging to spiking models needs more discussion.  
- Quality: technically sound and rigorous framework supported by simulations.  
- Clarity: generally clear, but control-theoretic components may challenge non-expert readers; an SSA appendix is recommended.  
- Originality: highly original combination of techniques that differentiates from prior work.  
- Significance: potentially significant implications for understanding biological memory systems and ML algorithms.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/principled approach, strong empirical results, biological plausibility, thorough analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational/scalability concerns, parameter sensitivity, limited discussion of learning rules.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution that outweighs limitations; suitable for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
