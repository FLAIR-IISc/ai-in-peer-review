Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an optimization framework to construct rate-based recurrent networks with separate excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations (obeying Dale’s law) that embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories as asymptotically stable fixed points. The key idea is to jointly optimize synaptic weights (via a Dale-preserving softplus reparameterization) and per-memory inhibitory steady-state rates to (i) make desired activity patterns fixed points and (ii) ensure local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target pattern. The network uses a rectified quadratic gain without saturation, with stability emerging through dynamic E/I balance. Experiments on a small network (n=150; about 100 E and 50 I) show recall performance comparable to an “ideal observer,” robustness to input and ongoing noise, sparse synaptic weight distributions, and E/I balancing across attractors.

This is positioned against Hopfield-type models (binary/symmetric, often violating Dale’s law), LIF models with global inhibition or relying on saturating nonlinearities, and spatial bump attractors. It draws on robust control (SSA) and echoes work on balanced networks and variability quenching. While there have been methods for training RNNs for desired fixed points and dynamics (e.g., spectral radius control, contraction analysis, strongly stable RNNs, FORCE learning), the combination of Dale-constrained optimization with SSA applied to graded attractors is a noteworthy angle.

Strengths
- Interesting and potentially impactful idea: recasting memory embedding as a constrained stability optimization problem that respects Dale’s law and achieves low-rate, E/I-balanced attractors.
- Use of SSA is technically appealing, providing differentiable stability surrogates and an explicit stability objective beyond heuristic spectral radius constraints.
- Demonstrations of recall robustness, variability quenching, and the emergence of sparse weight distributions are compelling.
- Empirical observation that only a subset of strong synapses are functionally critical is interesting and biologically relevant.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological and conceptual:
- Per-memory inhibitory steady states as free optimization variables: You optimize both W and {v_inh^μ} (n_I m extra degrees of freedom). This substantially relaxes the constraints and risks “cheating” by letting inhibitory activity be tuned per memory independently of the trained dynamics. While you argue I acts as auxiliary, at test time these I rates must arise from the same dynamics and connectivity. Please (i) demonstrate that the fixed points found when optimizing {v_inh^μ} coincide with the true fixed points under full dynamics, and (ii) quantify the performance/capacity without optimizing {v_inh^μ}, or when only W is learned. An ablation is essential.
- Fixed point condition not enforced: You minimize ||dv/dt|| at target states but accept nonzero residuals; SSA is then evaluated at points that are not fixed points. There is no guarantee SSA minimization at an approximate point yields a nearby stable fixed point of the actual dynamics. Provide a principled argument or proof (e.g., via the implicit function theorem, Lipschitz bounds on residuals vs. fixed point displacement), and quantify the distance between targets and true fixed points.
- Basin of attraction: Beyond a few σ-levels, basin sizes are not quantified. Please report basin volumes or success as a function of angular/Euclidean distance, and per-memory variability. The threshold for success (0.001; later relaxed to 0.08 after pruning) appears ad hoc and changes across experiments.
- Capacity and scaling: m/n=0.2 for n=150 is modest; no scaling analysis (m vs. n, dependence on n_E, n_I, sparsity) is provided. SSA’s ε is heuristically scaled; complexity likely scales poorly (eigs per pattern). Please provide computational complexity, wall-clock, and scaling/capacity trends.
- Transient amplification and non-normality: Ensuring negative SSA does not control transient growth; non-normal Jacobians can yield large transients even with α(J)<0. Given your emphasis on robust stability, quantify transient gains (e.g., H∞ norms, pseudospectra) and show they are bounded.
- Robustness: Tests add white noise to states, but no robustness to parameter perturbations (weight noise, heterogeneity in τ_i, h_i, or structured perturbations). A core claim is robust E/I balance; please test weight noise and parameter drift.
- Biological realism claims: The spiking generalization is speculative; no spike-based simulation with the learned W is shown. Balance is assessed via mean E vs I inputs, but synaptic delays, spike noise, and refractory effects are not addressed.

Technical clarity and completeness:
- The derivative of g at 0 is ill-defined; you effectively use g′(v)=2γv for v>0 and 0 otherwise. Patterns include many zeros (especially I with target 0). Please specify how g′(0) is handled in SSA/Jacobian gradients and whether subgradient issues affect optimization.
- Hyperparameters (η_s, η_F), τ_i, h_i are not specified (you refer to Table 1 and Supplementary, not provided). Results depend critically on these choices. Provide exact values and sensitivity analyses.
- Weight clipping experiment: The 0.01 threshold is arbitrary and fairly large; success threshold relaxed post hoc to 0.08. This weakens the conclusion that weak weights are dispensable. Please fix a single success criterion and include a systematic pruning curve.
- Reference implementation details are missing (optimizer settings, stopping criteria, initialization distributions, random seeds).

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous reference inconsistencies/incompleteness (missing years, volumes, pages; inconsistent capitalization). E.g., multiple entries lack years; Hopfield (1982/1984) not correctly formatted; several journal details are missing.
- Some notation is unclear or inconsistent: use of brackets b·c+ to denote positive part is not defined; vector superscripts v^μ vs subscripts; spacing/typos (e.g., “Hz m em or ie s” in Fig. 1 caption), “Jacobians-based”, “g−1(5Hz)” spacing, “vex c”.
- Figures and Table 1 are referenced but not present; crucial parameter choices are deferred to Supplementary Material.

Quality: The core idea is sound and results are promising, but key methodological choices (optimizing per-memory I states, approximate fixed points) and missing analyses (scaling, transient control, robustness) undermine the strength of the claims.

Clarity: Generally readable, but important details are missing, and notation/formatting issues are significant.

Originality: The SSA-based, Dale-constrained stability optimization for graded attractors appears novel and interesting.

Significance: Potentially high if validated at scale and with stricter constraints; current empirical scope and methodological looseness limit impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Remove {v_inh^μ} from the optimization or constrain them via the same dynamics; provide ablations.
- Prove or empirically quantify that minimizing SSA at near-fixed points yields nearby true stable fixed points; report distances.
- Provide capacity scaling and computational complexity; include larger n and m.
- Quantify transient amplification; report pseudospectra or H∞ norms.
- Robustness to parameter perturbations; sensitivity to ε, η_s, η_F.
- Use a fixed success criterion across experiments; quantify basin sizes.
- Clean up references and notation; include all missing hyperparameters and Supplementary essentials in the main text if possible.

Pros
- Novel, principled stability objective (SSA) for Dale-constrained analog attractors.
- Compelling demonstrations of E/I balance, variability quenching, and sparsification.
- Addresses important gap between abstract memory models and biologically plausible constraints.

Cons
- Over-reliance on per-memory inhibitory free variables.
- Fixed-point condition not enforced; theoretical guarantees missing.
- Limited scale/capacity analysis; robustness and transient control under-explored.
- Presentation and references need substantial polishing.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The contribution is promising, but the methodological issues (especially per-memory I optimization and lack of guarantees for approximate fixed points) and insufficient empirical breadth warrant a more rigorous and complete revision.
