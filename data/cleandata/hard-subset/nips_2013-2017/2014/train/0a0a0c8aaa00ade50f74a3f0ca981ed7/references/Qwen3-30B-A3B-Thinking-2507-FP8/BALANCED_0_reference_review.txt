

# Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

This paper proposes a novel control-theoretic framework for constructing biologically plausible memory networks that can store and recall analog (non-binary) activity patterns. The authors address a fundamental gap in theoretical neuroscience: most memory models either violate Dale's law, assume binary activity states, or fail to maintain realistic firing rates through balanced excitation and inhibition. The key insight is to optimize network parameters (synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity) to satisfy two conditions: 1) target patterns must be fixed points of the dynamics (dv/dt = 0), and 2) the dynamics must be stable around these fixed points (spectral abscissa < 0). The authors employ the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable stability measure to enable gradient-based optimization, resulting in networks that successfully store multiple analog memory patterns with realistic firing rates and balanced E/I dynamics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel approach to stability optimization:** The use of SSA as a differentiable stability measure is a significant technical contribution that overcomes a major barrier in previous work. This enables the construction of networks that simultaneously satisfy fixed point and stability conditions for analog patterns while adhering to Dale's law.

2. **Biologically plausible implementation:** The resulting networks exhibit key biological features missing from prior work: realistic firing rates (5-10 Hz), sparse connectivity (matching cortical data), and tight dynamic E/I balance. The sparsity emerges naturally from the optimization rather than being imposed by regularization.

3. **Comprehensive experimental validation:** The paper thoroughly demonstrates the network's capabilities across multiple dimensions: successful recall of analog patterns (Fig. 3), robustness to noise (Fig. 6), balanced E/I inputs (Fig. 5), and the functional importance of weak synapses (Fig. 4). The comparison to an ideal observer provides a meaningful performance benchmark.

4. **Clear theoretical foundation:** The paper effectively connects control theory concepts to neuroscience, providing a rigorous mathematical framework for memory storage that addresses specific limitations in prior work (cited in Section 1).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited biological translation:** While the paper claims the rate model is appropriate for cortical neurons, it doesn't sufficiently address how this would translate to spiking networks. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide concrete pathways for validation in spiking regimes, which is a significant gap given the spiking nature of biological neurons.

2. **Storage capacity and scalability:** The storage capacity of 0.2 (30 patterns with 150 neurons) is relatively modest compared to binary memory models. The paper doesn't explore how capacity scales with network size or how the optimization method would perform with larger networks, which is important for biological relevance.

3. **Learning rule gap:** The paper addresses the optimization problem from an "algorithmic" perspective but doesn't connect to how this could be implemented with local synaptic plasticity rules. The authors mention inhibitory plasticity as a potential mechanism but don't develop this idea further, leaving a critical gap between the optimization framework and biological implementation.

4. **Computational cost:** The optimization procedure using BFGS is computationally intensive (noted in the Supplementary Material), which limits the applicability to large-scale networks. The paper doesn't discuss potential approximations or alternative optimization methods that might improve scalability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to theoretical neuroscience by bridging a critical gap between memory models and biological constraints. The framework successfully demonstrates how analog memory storage can be achieved in biologically plausible networks with balanced E/I dynamics, addressing limitations that have persisted in the field for decades. The experimental validation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the network's capabilities.

However, the paper would benefit from more concrete discussion of how the rate-based model translates to spiking networks and how the optimization could be implemented with biologically plausible learning rules. The modest storage capacity also warrants further exploration, particularly in relation to the biological capacity of cortical networks.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical neuroscience of memory systems. The technical approach is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the framework addresses a genuine gap in the literature. While the biological translation and scalability concerns are valid limitations, they do not undermine the core scientific contribution. The paper advances the field by providing a new method for constructing realistic memory networks that could inspire future experimental work and theoretical developments.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the discussion of how the rate-based model could be validated in spiking networks, potentially by referencing recent work on rate-to-spiking conversions.

2. Include a brief analysis of how storage capacity scales with network size, or discuss the implications of the observed capacity for biological memory systems.

3. Develop the discussion of potential learning rules beyond the current speculation about inhibitory plasticity, perhaps by citing recent work on inhibitory plasticity in memory contexts.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations for larger networks, which would strengthen the paper's applicability to more realistic cortical models.

This paper represents an important step toward biologically plausible models of memory, and with the suggested clarifications, it would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
