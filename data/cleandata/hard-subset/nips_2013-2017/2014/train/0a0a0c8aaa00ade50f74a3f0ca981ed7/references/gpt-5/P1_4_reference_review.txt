Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to construct recurrent rate networks with explicit excitatory and inhibitory (E/I) populations that store multiple analog (graded) attractor memories. The model respects Dale’s law via a softplus reparameterization of weights, uses a rectified-quadratic gain (consistent with power-law neuronal f–I curves), maintains low average firing rates, and achieves dynamic E/I balance. The key technical idea is to optimize both (i) a fixed-point condition (minimizing the squared velocity ‖dv/dt‖2 at each desired state) and (ii) local stability (minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA) over synaptic weights and per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities. The authors show robust autoassociative recall from noisy cues, resilience to ongoing noise, a balanced-state mechanism, and sparsity-like weight distributions after training. They report storing 30 attractors in a 150-neuron network (capacity ≈0.2), compare recall to an ideal observer, and examine performance after clipping small weights.

This work connects to classic autoassociative memories (Hopfield), graded-response models and capacity analyses (Treves & Rolls), E/I balanced networks and stability (Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham), and spatial bump attractors. It explicitly addresses two limitations of much prior work: Dale-constrained connectivity and graded attractors without saturating single-neuron nonlinearities. The use of SSA from robust control (cf. Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.) for stability optimization is a notable methodological contribution in this context. Related machine learning/control approaches to stabilizing recurrent networks (e.g., FORCE learning; stabilization via Lyapunov constraints) could be cited and contrasted more explicitly.

Quality
- Soundness: The objective combines a fixed-point residual and a differentiable upper bound to the spectral abscissa. This is a reasonable surrogate for local asymptotic stability and is practically optimizable with L-BFGS. The use of Dale’s law via softplus is appropriate. The demonstrations (recall success, ideal-observer bound, balance, noise robustness, variability quenching) are coherent and consistent with the narrative.
- Caveats:
  - The fixed-point residuals remain nonzero; stability is certified at the candidate states, but they are “slow points.” The empirical observation that nearby exact fixed points exist is plausible but not guaranteed; a small proof-of-concept (e.g., Newton solve from the slow points) would strengthen claims.
  - Per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities vinhµ are optimized as free “auxiliary” variables. While the final recall simulations use only the dynamics with fixed W and constant h, optimizing over vinhµ substantially increases degrees of freedom and may inflate effective capacity/stability. It would be important to quantify how performance degrades when vinhµ are not optimized (or are regularized/penalized), and to clarify the biological interpretation.
  - Capacity: Only one network size is reported. No scaling analysis with N, E/I ratio, pattern statistics, or SSA smoothing parameter ε is provided. Given prior capacity results for graded memories, this is a significant gap.
  - “Sparsity”: The trained weight distributions are heavy-tailed with many small but nonzero entries. Structural sparsity (zero connectivity) is not enforced; the clipping experiment is informative but still leaves a dense graph. Claims about sparse connectivity should be tempered or supported with explicit sparsity constraints and analyses.
  - SSA parameterization: The heuristic scaling ε = 0.01·150/n is reasonable but unvalidated. Sensitivity analyses (ε vs convergence/stability/recall) would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The problem formulation, constraints (Dale’s law), objective, and optimization method are described succinctly. Figures (as described) appear to support claims. Some suggestions:
- Provide more detail on SSA computation and gradients, or a short appendix/pseudocode; specify exact ε used, line-search/termination criteria, and gradient checks.
- Explicitly state E/I population sizes and all simulation parameters in the main text (Table 1 is referenced but not visible here).
- Clarify the role of vinhµ in training vs recall, and whether any regularization is applied to these variables.
- Define the external inputs h and their magnitudes; indicate τi values.
- Justify the success thresholds (0.001 and 0.08 after clipping) and report robustness to threshold choices.

Originality
- Novel combination: Applying SSA-based robust stability optimization to build Dale-constrained, balanced, graded attractor networks is original and timely. Prior balanced memory models often yielded effectively binary patterns or relied on saturating f–I curves; here graded attractors are stabilized without saturation via dynamic E/I balance.
- Related work is mostly covered, though adding comparisons to alternative stabilization/training methods for recurrent nets (e.g., Sussillo & Abbott 2009, Lyapunov-based constraints, spectral regularization) would situate the contribution more broadly in the NeurIPS community.

Significance
- If scalable, the framework could become a useful tool to construct mechanistically plausible attractor circuits with controlled stability properties, and to probe hypotheses about E/I balance, noise robustness, and cortical variability quenching.
- The present empirical scope (N=150, m=30) is promising but limited. Demonstrating scaling, capacity dependence on pattern statistics, and ablations (without vinhµ optimization, with explicit sparsity) would substantially increase impact.

Pros
- Addresses core biological constraints (Dale’s law, low rates, balanced E/I) while achieving analog attractors.
- Principled stability objective via SSA; clear operationalization of fixed-point and stability criteria.
- Robust recall comparable to an ideal observer; resilience to ongoing noise; variability quenching aligns with cortical data.
- Insightful analysis of E/I balance across attractors; informative clipping experiment.

Cons
- Per-memory inhibitory degrees of freedom may overstate feasibility; need ablations/regularization.
- Lack of capacity and scaling analysis; single network size.
- No formal guarantee that slow points are near true stable fixed points; could be checked numerically.
- Structural sparsity not enforced; claims about sparse connectivity should be moderated.
- Limited comparisons to alternative training/stabilization methods.

Suggestions for improvement
1) Provide capacity scaling with N and m, and sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF, E/I ratio, and pattern variance/correlation. 2) Analyze performance without optimizing vinhµ, or add penalties tying vinhµ across memories; report realism of inhibitory rate distributions. 3) Solve for exact fixed points near the learned slow points to verify existence and stability ex post (e.g., Newton or root-finding on dv/dt=0, then SA). 4) Add experiments with explicit structural sparsity (L1 or hard masks) and report impact on recall and balance. 5) Compare to at least one alternative method (e.g., FORCE learning with Dale’s law) on the same tasks. 6) Release code or provide detailed pseudocode for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The approach is technically sound and novel, and the results are compelling for a proof-of-concept. However, the reliance on per-memory inhibitory variables, the absence of scaling/capacity analysis, and the limited sparsity claims should be addressed to maximize significance and ensure biological plausibility.
