## Review of "Analog Memories in a Balanced Rate-Based Network of E-I Neurons"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sophisticated approach to building biologically plausible memory networks. It tackles several key limitations of previous models, successfully integrating Dale’s law, balanced excitation and inhibition, and graded activity patterns into a robust memory recall framework. While the methodology is complex and potentially difficult to extend, the results are promising and the paper is of high quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The authors address the challenge of creating neural networks capable of storing and recalling analog memories – patterns of graded neuronal activity – in a biologically realistic manner. Existing models often fall short by relying on binary representations, unrealistic network constraints (e.g., symmetrical connectivity), or instability issues.  This work proposes a control-theoretic framework, explicitly optimizing network parameters (synaptic weights and inhibitory neuron activity) to ensure both fixed-point properties (memory storage) and dynamical stability. A key innovation is the use of the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) for robust stability optimization, combined with a re-parameterization to enforce Dale’s law. The authors demonstrate successful storage and recall of analog memories, exhibiting robustness to noise and dynamic balance of E/I activity.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper directly tackles a crucial question in computational neuroscience: how are memories represented and recalled in the brain in a manner consistent with its known biological constraints?
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of control theory, robust stability optimization (SSA), and the specific network parametrization (Dale’s Law, rate-based dynamics) is a genuinely novel and powerful approach.  Using SSA is a particularly clever move, allowing for gradient-based optimization of stability which is often intractable.
* **Biologically Realistic Constraints:** Explicitly incorporating Dale’s Law and balancing excitation and inhibition significantly enhances the biological plausibility of the model compared to many existing theoretical frameworks.
* **Robustness to Noise:** The demonstrated robustness of recall to noisy initial conditions and ongoing noise is a significant strength, suggesting a more realistic memory system than many prior models.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors present compelling simulations showcasing stable storage and recall, along with a detailed analysis of network dynamics and weight distributions. The comparison to an "ideal observer" is a valuable benchmark.
* **Clear and Thorough Presentation:**  Despite the complexity of the approach, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.  The inclusion of equations and figures is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Scalability:** The optimization procedure, while effective, appears computationally intensive.  The authors mention using BFGS, but the scalability of this approach to larger networks isn’t explicitly addressed.  This could be a barrier to applying the framework to more complex, brain-scale simulations.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The reliance on heuristically chosen parameters (ηs, ηF, ε) in the cost function is a potential weakness.  A more systematic exploration of these parameters, and a justification for the specific values chosen, would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Exploration of Neuron Model:** While the rate-based model is a reasonable simplification, the authors acknowledge its limitations. A brief discussion of how the results might change with a more biophysically realistic spiking neuron model would be helpful. They *do* mention this in the discussion, but a short experiment or analysis would be impactful.
* **Sparsity Explanation:** The observed synaptic sparsity is attributed to stability requirements, but the mechanism isn't fully elucidated. While plausible, a more detailed explanation of *why* stability favors sparse connectivity would be beneficial.




**Detailed Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 3, Line 17:** “dynamic feedback inhibition can stabilize relatively low firing rates…” –  Cite Ref [9] more specifically, as it provides a theoretical framework for this stabilization.
* **Page 4, Equation 3:** Clarify the units of the terms in the equation.
* **Page 5, Figure 1:**  Consider adding a schematic diagram illustrating the relationship between the excitatory and inhibitory populations within the network.
* **Page 6, Optimization:** The description of the optimization procedure could be more detailed. What specific stopping criteria were used? What was the typical runtime for optimization?
* **Page 7, Parameter Choice:** Justify the choice of ε=0.01 more thoroughly. How sensitive are the results to changes in ε?
* **Page 8, Figure 3:**  Provide confidence intervals or standard deviations for the recall performance data.
* **Page 9, Synaptic Sparsity:** Elaborate on why the L2 regularization doesn’t drive sparsity and what mechanisms are at play.
* **Page 10, Ongoing Noise:** The description of the noise model is somewhat vague. Specify the statistical properties of the noise (e.g., mean, variance).




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience.
* Addresses a core problem in memory research with a biologically plausible approach.
* Technically sound methodology and robust results.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Demonstrates robustness and dynamic balance, key features of biological memory systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity might limit scalability to larger networks.
* Parameter tuning relies on heuristics.
*  Limited exploration of spiking neuron model alternatives.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. The paper represents a significant advance in our understanding of how analog memories could be implemented in neural networks, and is a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference. Addressing the concerns regarding scalability, parameter tuning, and the neuron model will further strengthen the paper.
