- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple graded attractor memories in biologically constrained E/I recurrent networks.
- Network uses rate dynamics with rectified-quadratic transfer functions and enforces Dale’s law, sparse recurrence, low rates, and dynamic E/I balance.
- Optimizes synaptic weights (sign-constrained reparameterization) and per-memory inhibitory steady-state activities to make targets fixed points and ensure local stability.
- Uses a differentiable surrogate, the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA), to enforce local asymptotic stability.
- Fixed-point objective minimizes dv/dt at target states, producing “slow points” and empirically convergent dynamics.
- Demonstrates recall performance that tracks an ideal observer, robustness to cue and ongoing noise, balanced E/I inputs, emergent synaptic sparsity, and variability quenching.
- Novelty: combines Dale reparameterization with SSA-based stability optimization to stabilize graded memories without saturating nonlinearities.
- Soundness: SSA is an appropriate differentiable surrogate and ensures stability if α̃ε ≤ 0, but fixed-point satisfaction is only approximate in practice.
- Empirics: compelling demonstrations at n=150, m=30 (capacity ≈0.2) but lacks capacity scaling, basin-of-attraction, and computational complexity analyses.
- Assumptions: optimizes inhibitory steady-states per memory (inflates parameter count), keeps external inputs fixed, and relies on non-saturating nonlinearities under balanced operation.
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized but defers many hyperparameters and SSA implementation details to supplement.
- Reproducibility needs exact parameter settings, SSA/ε choice and sensitivity, optimization pseudocode, and code release.
- Strength: principled joint fixed-point and stability optimization under biologically grounded constraints.
- Strength: effective use of robust control tools (differentiable SSA) and solid empirical demonstrations of robustness and emergent sparsity.
- Weakness: no systematic analysis of capacity, basins, or scaling with N, E/I ratio, sparsity, or pattern statistics.
- Weakness: fixed-point satisfaction is approximate and lacks direct verification of true equilibria and spectral abscissa at converged points.
- Weakness: reliance on per-memory inhibitory variables and global optimization raises scalability and biological plausibility concerns.
- Weakness: limited analysis of failure modes (spurious attractors, interference) and incomplete references.
- Key questions for authors: capacity scaling, basin sizes, sensitivity to ε/regularizers, direct fixed-point verification and SA reporting, role of L2 regularizer in sparsity, robustness to heterogeneous τi/h and other nonlinearities, and transfer to spiking networks.
- Pros for acceptance: novel, principled approach linking control theory and biologically realistic memory networks with convincing small-scale results.
- Cons for acceptance: missing scaling/basin analyses, approximate fixed-point verification, heavy global optimization, and reproducibility gaps.
- Recommendation: weak accept; work is promising but would be strengthened by fixed-point checks, capacity/basin analyses, and improved implementation details.
