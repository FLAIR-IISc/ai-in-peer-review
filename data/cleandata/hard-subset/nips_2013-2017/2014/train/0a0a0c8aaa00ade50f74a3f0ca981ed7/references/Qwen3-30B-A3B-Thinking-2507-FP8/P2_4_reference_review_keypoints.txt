- Presents a framework to embed analog (graded) memory patterns as stable fixed points in a balanced E-I network while respecting Dale’s law and maintaining low firing rates.  
- Core technical innovation: applying robust control theory (Smoothed Spectral Abscissa) as a differentiable proxy to optimize fixed-point and dynamical stability.  
- Rigorous mathematical formulation with clear fixed-point conditions and stability criteria; SSA use is well-justified.  
- Comprehensive experiments testing noise robustness, synaptic sparseness, E-I balance, and validation against an ideal observer; methods are reproducible though optimization cost is not fully detailed.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations: networks exhibit “slow points” rather than exact fixed points and achieve modest storage capacity (~0.2).  
- Clear, well-organized writing and informative figures; could benefit from a brief glossary for control-theory terms for non-expert readers.  
- Original contribution: enables graded-pattern storage under biological constraints, differing from prior binary or saturating-transfer approaches.  
- High significance: connects control theory with neural computation and offers testable hypotheses about cortical memory and E-I balance.  
- Strengths: fills a biological-theoretical gap, novel control-theoretic optimization, validates key biological properties, demonstrates sparse connectivity, and compares to an ideal observer.  
- Weaknesses: computational scalability/optimization cost unclear, no direct comparison to other recent analog-memory models, speculative transition to spiking networks, and limited contextualization of capacity.  
- Recommendation: Accept — substantial, high-quality contribution relevant to computational neuroscience and machine learning.
