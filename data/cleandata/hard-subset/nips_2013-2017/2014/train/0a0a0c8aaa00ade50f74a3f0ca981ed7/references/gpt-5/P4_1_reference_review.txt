Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple graded (analog) attractor memories in a recurrent rate network with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations and Dale’s law. Neurons have rectified quadratic transfer functions without saturation; stability is achieved through a dynamic balance of excitation and inhibition rather than through saturating single-neuron nonlinearities. The authors formulate storage as a joint optimization problem: for each target memory, they (i) minimize the norm of the time derivative to make the pattern a fixed point (or a nearby “slow” point) and (ii) enforce local stability by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian, a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa. Synaptic weights are parameterized via a softplus map with fixed signs to satisfy Dale’s law. Inhibitory steady-state activities for each memory are treated as auxiliary variables during optimization, allowing the method to find stable excitatory–inhibitory operating points. The authors demonstrate recall from noisy cues, robustness to ongoing noise, tight E/I balance across memories, emergent sparsity of learned weights, and a modest capacity (30 memories in a 150-neuron network). They also show that many small weights can be pruned with minimal loss.

Relative to classic Hopfield-type models and graded-response variants, this work enforces Dale’s law, explicitly models inhibitory neurons, and supports analog memories with non-binary activity distributions. Compared to balanced-inhibition memory models (e.g., Roudi & Latham 2007) and clustered balanced networks (e.g., Litwin-Kumar & Doiron 2012), this study trains connectivity to stabilize arbitrary graded patterns rather than relying on special connectivity motifs or saturating transfer functions (cf. Amit & Brunel; Battaglia & Treves; Latham & Nirenberg). It builds on robust control concepts (SSA; Vanbiervliet et al.) and related stabilized supralinear network analyses (Ahmadian et al.), and connects to prior demonstrations that optimized E/I dynamics can stabilize low-rate regimes (e.g., Hennequin et al.). The approach also relates to RNN training methods that shape fixed points and dynamics, though many do not enforce Dale’s law or balanced regimes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of SSA to impose local stability is appropriate and addresses the non-differentiability of the spectral abscissa. The reparameterization enforces Dale’s law cleanly. The joint objective is reasonable. However:
  - The fixed-point condition is only approximately satisfied (dv/dt small but nonzero). The paper asserts nearby exact fixed points exist but does not provide a formal validation (e.g., via Newton iterations to find the true fixed point from each target or by reporting actual spectral abscissae at those fixed points).
  - The SSA bound depends on ε; the choice and scaling are heuristic. Sensitivity analyses are not reported.
  - Capacity and scaling are not systematically characterized; only a single setting (n=150, m=30) is shown.
  - The pruning analysis relaxes the success threshold substantially (from 0.001 to 0.08), making comparisons difficult; a principled evaluation (e.g., overlap measures, or re-centering distances relative to refound fixed points) would be stronger.
  - Computational complexity and convergence behavior (e.g., cost per iteration ∼ O(n^3 m) due to Jacobian eigendecompositions) are not discussed.

- Empirical support: Simulations show recall success probabilities close to an “ideal observer,” robustness to ongoing noise, balanced E/I inputs, and across-trial variability quenching after cue onset. These are compelling demonstrations, but broader evaluation is missing: basin sizes, spurious attractors, cross-pattern interference, and comparisons to alternative methods.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear and well organized, and the control-theoretic angle is well motivated. Figures (as described) seem informative. Useful details include weight parameterization, objective terms, and initialization. That said:
- Many implementation details are deferred (e.g., exact SSA/gradient computation, hyperparameter values, stopping criteria), and Table 1/Supplementary are referenced but not provided.
- Several references are incomplete and some bibliographic entries lack key details.
- It would help to explicitly report eigenvalue spectra before/after training and to verify fixed points numerically.

Originality
The main novelty is to combine SSA-based stability optimization with Dale-constrained rate networks to embed multiple analog attractors with graded firing rates and demonstrate balanced E/I stabilization across memories. While prior work has used robust control to shape network dynamics and achieve balanced regimes, applying SSA to construct multi-memory analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks appears to be a new and valuable contribution. The emphasis on arbitrary analog patterns (log-normal distributed), rather than binary or spatially structured bumps, is also distinctive.

Significance
Conceptually, the method provides a principled route to design biologically constrained associative memories that operate in balanced, low-rate regimes without requiring saturating neurons. This can be impactful for computational neuroscience and for machine learning settings where stable fixed points under constraints are needed. However, the current scope is limited by a lack of capacity/scaling analysis, sensitivity/ablation studies, and quantitative comparisons to strong baselines. As presented, the contribution is promising but preliminary.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear formulation of storage as fixed-point plus local stability with a differentiable stability surrogate (SSA).
- Enforces Dale’s law and produces balanced E/I regimes supporting low firing rates with analog patterns.
- Demonstrates robust recall from noisy cues and ongoing noise; shows variability quenching consistent with physiology.
- Emergent sparsity without L1; pruning analysis suggests many small weights are non-essential.
- Bridges robust control and biologically plausible memory networks.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Fixed points are not verified exactly; reliance on small dv/dt and SSA negativity is suggestive but not conclusive.
- Limited empirical scope: small networks, single capacity point, no scaling or basin-of-attraction characterization, and no comparison to Dale-constrained or alternative attractor-learning methods.
- Heuristic choices (ε scaling, thresholds) lack sensitivity analyses; pruning evaluation changes success criterion.
- Incomplete reporting (hyperparameters, computational cost, convergence) and incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Verify fixed points explicitly (e.g., Newton solver from each target) and report true spectral abscissae at those points.
- Provide capacity-versus-size curves, basin size estimates, and spurious attractor counts; study correlated patterns.
- Include ablations (with/without SSA term; alternative stability penalties) and sensitivity to ε, ηs, ηF.
- Compare to other attractor-learning approaches (e.g., networks trained without SSA, clustered balanced networks, Dale-constrained RNN training).
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock cost; provide code or detailed pseudocode.
- Use consistent success metrics when pruning (e.g., overlap to nearest fixed point) and analyze connectivity statistics more rigorously.
- Complete and correct references.
