Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an optimization-based framework to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a recurrent rate network with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations (Dale’s law enforced via a softplus reparameterization). The network uses a threshold-quadratic f–I curve (no saturation) and recurrent EI connectivity; external currents are memory-independent. For each target memory µ, the authors set a cost that (i) penalizes the norm of dv/dt at the target state (fixed-point matching) and (ii) enforces local stability using the smoothed spectral abscissa (SSA) as a differentiable upper bound on the spectral abscissa of the Jacobian. They optimize all synapses and, critically, allow inhibitory steady-state potentials vinh per memory as free variables. They demonstrate recall from noisy cues, robustness to ongoing noise, balanced E/I at recall fixed points, emergence of many small weights, and variability quenching upon stimulus onset. In a 150-neuron network they embed m=30 memories (capacity ~0.2 per neuron) and show recall performance comparable to an ideal nearest-neighbor “observer.”

This work connects classic associative memory models (Hopfield; Treves & Rolls; Amit & Brunel) with modern balanced EI dynamics (e.g., Latham & Nirenberg; Roudi & Latham; Ahmadian et al.), while bringing in robust control tools (SSA; see Vanbiervliet; Hennequin et al.) to directly stabilize desired attractors under Dale’s law. Related machine learning approaches for learning recurrent dynamics (e.g., FORCE learning; low-rank RNNs/manifolds) are not discussed but provide useful context for comparison.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A principled, control-theoretic formulation of embedding graded attractors in Dale-compliant networks, directly optimizing both fixed-point conditions and local stability.
- Biological relevance: Low-rate regimes stabilized without saturation via tight dynamic E/I balance; Dale’s law enforced; graded firing distributions reflect in vivo heavy tails.
- Empirical demonstrations: Recall robustness vs cue noise, variability quenching with ongoing noise, and EI input cancellation across attractors are convincing and align with known cortical phenomenology.
- Interesting observation: Many near-zero “silent” synapses emerge without explicit sparsity regularization; removal of very weak weights leaves most memories intact.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Fixed-point condition not enforced exactly: The method minimizes ‖dv/dt‖ at the target states but does not impose equality constraints, and reports non-zero residuals. Evaluating SSA at non-fixed points is not meaningful for stability of actual dynamics. While the authors argue nearby fixed points emerge, this should be demonstrated rigorously (e.g., root-finding from target states; reporting distances to the nearest true fixed point and its Jacobian spectrum).
- Capacity and scaling: A single example (n=150, m=30). No systematic scaling with N, E/I ratio, pattern statistics, or hyperparameters; no exploration of basins (e.g., critical cue noise vs m). This limits significance relative to classical capacity analyses for analog memories and more recent balanced networks. SSA ε-scaling is heuristic; its effect on capacity and stability is not quantified.
- Free inhibitory setpoints per memory: Treating vinh(µ) as per-memory free variables increases degrees of freedom and may inflate capacity and stability. In biology, these are determined by the same circuitry across memories. It would be important to show results when vinh(µ) are not free design variables (or are tied/regularized across memories), or by optimizing only W and letting vinh be whatever the network dynamics yields at the fixed points.
- Global vs local stability: SSA guarantees local linear stability at the designed points, but there is no global stability guarantee. The risk of runaway trajectories (given supralinear gains) or unintended high-rate attractors is not thoroughly assessed. A more extensive exploration of the state space (random initializations; distribution of attractors and their rates) would strengthen the claim.
- Comparisons: No direct comparisons to alternative training approaches (e.g., FORCE/full-FORCE; low-rank constructions for manifolds/fixed points; spectral radius/Lyapunov-regularized training). This makes it hard to assess advantages of SSA-based stabilization beyond conceptual appeal.
- Biological constraints beyond signs: Connectivity is dense a priori; sparsity “emerges,” but this could be largely due to L2 shrinkage and softplus parameterization. Quantitative comparison to cortical degree distributions and motifs (Song et al.; Lefort et al.) would be informative. Also, robustness to heterogeneity (time constants, external inputs) is not analyzed.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Key details (exact SSA definition, gradients, computational complexity per iteration, NLopt settings, and all hyperparameters) should be fully specified. References are incomplete. Some thresholds used to score recall (0.001 vs 0.08 after clipping) seem arbitrary; sensitivity analyses would help.

Quality
Technically competent and internally consistent, but key claims hinge on approximate fixed-point satisfaction and single-scale experiments. More rigorous validation of true fixed points and broader ablations are needed.

Clarity
Generally well written and well motivated. However, provide the exact SSA formulation used, gradient computations, and computational costs. Complete and standardize references.

Originality
Applying robust control (SSA) to embed analog attractors under Dale’s law is novel within this literature. However, the relation to adjacent RNN training methods in ML/neuro (FORCE; low-rank RNNs) should be discussed.

Significance
Potentially impactful for computational neuroscience by offering a general method to stabilize multiple analog memories in EI-balanced circuits. Current evaluation is limited in scale and comparisons; significance would increase with stronger capacity/scaling analyses and constraints on inhibitory degrees of freedom.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce dv/dt=0 exactly (equality-constrained optimization) or post hoc solve for nearby true fixed points; report distances and spectra.
- Systematically measure capacity vs N, m, pattern overlap, and ε; report basin sizes (e.g., σ at 0.5 success) and robustness to heterogeneity.
- Remove or regularize per-memory inhibitory targets; alternatively, learn a fixed inhibitory connectivity and show stability arises without per-memory vinh(µ) freedoms.
- Compare to baselines (FORCE/full-FORCE; low-rank constructions; spectral radius regularization) under Dale’s constraints.
- Provide more comprehensive connectivity statistics and rule out that sparsity is an artifact of L2 shrinkage.
- Clarify computational complexity and provide code for reproducibility.

Pros
- Strong, principled approach; biologically motivated constraints; compelling qualitative phenomena (balanced EI, variability quenching).
- Clear demonstration of robust recall for analog patterns.

Cons
- Approximate satisfaction of fixed points; limited capacity/scaling analysis; per-memory inhibitory freedoms; lack of comparisons.

Recommendation: Borderline reject (leaning to weak accept if the authors can address fixed-point exactness and provide at least a basic capacity/scaling analysis and an ablation removing per-memory inhibitory freedoms). Confidence: high.
