Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic approach to embedding multiple graded (“analog”) attractor memories in a biologically constrained rate-based recurrent network with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations obeying Dale’s law. Neuron outputs follow a threshold-quadratic non-saturating nonlinearity. The key technical idea is to jointly optimize synaptic weights (via a Dale-compliant softplus reparameterization) and the steady-state inhibitory activities for each memory so that (i) the desired states are fixed points (by minimizing the norm of dv/dt at each target) and (ii) those fixed points are locally stable (by minimizing the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa, SSA, of the Jacobian at each target). The authors show robust recall from noisy cues, ongoing-noise robustness with variability quenching, tight E/I balance at recall, and an emergent heavy-tailed distribution of learned weights. They demonstrate that pruning small weights minimally affects recall for most attractors. The approach is contrasted to classical Hopfield-type models (symmetry violations, binary memories), E/I models relying on saturating gains or global inhibition, spatial bump attractors, and timing-based models without inhibition. Use of SSA from robust control [Vanbiervliet 2009] is a novel ingredient; the work relates to balanced networks [Roudi & Latham], stabilized supralinear networks [Ahmadian et al.], and optimal control of balanced dynamics [Hennequin et al.].

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casts memory storage as a constrained stability optimization problem that explicitly enforces local asymptotic stability of multiple graded attractors under Dale’s law and non-saturating gains.
- Methodological innovation: practical use of SSA as a smooth surrogate to the spectral abscissa enables gradient-based training for stability, which is nontrivial in high dimensions.
- Biological plausibility: separate E/I populations, balanced operating regime with non-saturating transfer, realistic firing-rate distributions for memory patterns, and heavy-tailed synaptic strengths that align with cortical data.
- Empirical demonstrations: robust recall vs. an ideal-observer baseline, ongoing-noise robustness and variability quenching, and evidence that only a subset of strong synapses is functionally necessary.
- Clarity of objective: the cost function transparently combines fixed-point constraints, stability, and weight regularization.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Capacity and scaling: Results are shown for n=150 with m=30 (capacity ~0.2) but there is no systematic scaling analysis with network size, number of memories, or pattern statistics. Theoretical or empirical capacity scaling is critical for significance and comparison to prior art.
- Free inhibitory steady-states per memory: Treating v_inh^μ as free variables (n_I m additional parameters) greatly increases degrees of freedom and may inflate attainable capacity and stability. It is unclear to what extent the learned inhibitory patterns would emerge without explicitly optimizing them per attractor, and whether this choice biases the problem toward feasibility.
- Basin geometry: The approach optimizes local stability (SSA) and dv/dt at the target but provides limited analysis of basin sizes, spurious attractors, and global dynamics. Successful recall vs. σ probes this indirectly, but a more direct characterization is needed.
- Robustness and generalization: No tests of robustness to parameter perturbations (e.g., synaptic noise, dropout) beyond small-weight pruning; no sensitivity analysis for SSA smoothness ε, regularization weights, or initialization. It is unclear how often optimization converges and how sensitive outcomes are across runs.
- Biological constraints and sparsity: Sparsity is not explicitly enforced; the observed heavy tail could depend on L2 shrinkage and task. If true synaptic sparsity is a desideratum, an L1/structured penalty or explicit sparse parameterization would be more convincing. Connectivity remains dense before pruning.
- Comparisons: Aside from the ideal observer, there is no comparison to trained E/I networks that stabilize attractors by other means (e.g., Lyapunov-based constructions, low-rank RNNs, FORCE-trained rate networks adapted to Dale’s law), nor to prior balanced-memory models in terms of recall performance/capacity.
- Reproducibility and computational cost: The paper lacks complexity analysis (per-iteration cost dominated by m Jacobians and SSA) and guidance for scaling to larger networks or many memories.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of formulation and experiments shown; use of SSA is appropriate and well motivated. However, key claims about analog memory in balanced E/I networks would be much stronger with capacity scaling, basin analyses, and robustness results. The choice to optimize v_inh^μ per memory is a central modeling decision that should be more carefully justified and stress-tested.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The training objective and dynamics are well specified. Some important details are missing: precise computational cost, convergence statistics across random seeds, and sensitivity to ε and regularization. Figures appear informative but more quantitative summaries (e.g., distributions of SA/SSA across memories after training, basin radii) would help.

Originality
High in the specific combination of graded memories, Dale’s law, non-saturating gains, and robust stability optimization via SSA. The framing of memory embedding as stability control is fresh for this literature.

Significance
Potentially significant for computational neuroscience as a constructive recipe for building balanced, multi-attractor networks with graded activity. As presented, impact is limited by the absence of scaling laws, comparisons, and stronger robustness analyses.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide capacity scaling with N and m, including dependence on n_E/n_I and pattern statistics; compare to classical results for graded patterns.
- Quantify basin sizes (e.g., along random directions or worst-case perturbations) and report spurious attractors.
- Justify and test the role of optimizing v_inh^μ: ablation with fixed v_inh^μ (e.g., set by baseline or mean-field) or penalize deviations from baseline to assess necessity.
- Evaluate robustness to synaptic perturbations and noise beyond pruning small weights; test performance after adding Gaussian perturbations to trained weights.
- Include sensitivity analyses for ε and regularization weights; report convergence rates and failure cases.
- Compare to alternative training strategies (e.g., without SSA, or with Lyapunov constraints) and to related E/I memory models.
- Consider explicit sparsity constraints or structured connectivity to better match cortical microcircuits.
- Discuss computational complexity and practical limits for larger networks.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled method to synthesize balanced E/I networks with analog attractors.
- Strong qualitative alignment with cortical phenomena (E/I balance, variability quenching, heavy-tailed synapses).
- Technically interesting use of SSA for neural stability design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient capacity/scaling characterization; unclear generality.
- Key modeling choice (free v_inh^μ) may over-parameterize the problem.
- Limited robustness and basin analyses; few comparisons to alternatives.
- Reproducibility and scalability not established.

Overall: promising and creative contribution; with additional quantitative analyses addressing capacity, robustness, and the role of inhibitory degrees of freedom, this work would be significantly strengthened.
