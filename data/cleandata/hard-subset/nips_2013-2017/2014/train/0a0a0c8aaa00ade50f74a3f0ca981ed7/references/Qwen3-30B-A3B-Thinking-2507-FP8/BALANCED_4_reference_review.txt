

This paper proposes a control-theoretic framework for constructing balanced rate-based networks of excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) neurons that can store and recall analog memory patterns. The authors address key limitations in previous memory models: they respect Dale's law, use realistic graded activity patterns rather than binary states, maintain low firing rates through dynamic E-I balance, and avoid symmetrical connectivity requirements. Their key innovation is using Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) optimization to achieve stability around desired analog memory patterns, rather than relying on saturating neuron models or global inhibition.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to bridging theoretical memory models with biologically realistic neural circuits. Previous work either used binary patterns [1,2], lacked inhibitory neurons [3,4], or relied on saturating neuron models [5,6], while this work successfully demonstrates stable analog memory storage in a balanced E-I network.

2. The technical approach is sound and well-executed. The use of SSA optimization to address the stability problem is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate robust memory recall in the presence of noise.

3. The paper is clearly written with excellent figures that effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Fig. 1 showing analog patterns, Fig. 5 demonstrating E-I balance). The empirical distributions of firing rates (Fig. 1c) and the demonstration of balanced E-I inputs (Fig. 5) provide compelling evidence for the model's biological plausibility.

4. The findings have clear significance for neuroscience, showing how dynamic E-I balance can maintain low firing rates during memory recall without requiring saturating neuron models. The demonstration of robustness to noise (Fig. 3) and the observation of stimulus-induced variability reduction (Fig. 6) align with known neural phenomena.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better address computational scalability. While they use n=150 neurons, they don't discuss how the SSA optimization would scale to larger, more brain-like networks, which is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of their approach.

2. The transition from rate-based models to spiking networks remains superficial. The authors state that their rate model "could still be used in a spiking model" if the gain function matches, but provide no evidence or discussion of how well this would translate to spiking dynamics, which is a significant gap for a neuroscience-focused contribution.

3. The discussion of biological learning mechanisms is underdeveloped. While they briefly mention inhibitory plasticity as a promising candidate, they don't explore how local learning rules could achieve their stability objective, which is a critical gap for a neuroscience paper.

4. The paper doesn't adequately position itself against recent work on similar topics, such as the recent NIPS 2017 paper by [22] that also addressed variability reduction in multiple-attractor scenarios with graded patterns.

Pro/Con Arguments:
Pros: Strong theoretical framework addressing a fundamental limitation in memory models; clear demonstration of E-I balance; high-quality experimental results; significant contribution to neuroscience.

Cons: Limited discussion of scaling to larger networks; insufficient exploration of biological learning mechanisms; somewhat superficial treatment of the spiking network connection.

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of computational neuroscience, addressing a critical gap in how memory networks can be constructed to respect biological constraints. The framework provides a new pathway for understanding memory storage in the brain and opens promising avenues for future research. While the paper has some limitations regarding scaling and biological plausibility of learning, these are reasonable gaps for a first proof-of-concept paper, and the core contribution is significant.

Suggestions for Improvement:
1. Expand the discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the SSA optimization approach, including how it might be adapted for larger networks (e.g., with 1000+ neurons).

2. Provide more concrete discussion of how the optimized weights could be learned through biological mechanisms, perhaps by connecting to specific inhibitory plasticity rules.

3. Include a brief exploration of how the rate-based model might be translated to spiking networks, including potential limitations or necessary modifications.

4. Add a short comparison to recent related work (e.g., [22]) to better position their contribution within the current literature.

This paper represents an important step toward building biologically realistic models of memory storage. The control-theoretic approach to stability optimization is particularly innovative, and the demonstration of dynamic E-I balance during memory recall provides valuable insights into how the brain might maintain stable memory representations. With the suggested improvements, this work could become a valuable reference point for future research in computational neuroscience.
