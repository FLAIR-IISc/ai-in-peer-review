- Proposes a control-theoretic framework to train recurrent rate networks with separate excitatory/inhibitory populations (Dale’s law) to store multiple analog attractor memories.  
- Neurons use rectified quadratic gains without upper saturation; stability arises from dynamic E/I balance rather than saturation.  
- Main technical contribution: optimization objective that (i) minimizes dv/dt at target patterns and (ii) minimizes the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian to enforce local stability.  
- Network parameters include synaptic weights (Dale’s constraint via softplus) and per-memory inhibitory steady-state potentials treated as auxiliary training variables.  
- Demonstrates recall in n=150 storing m=30 analog patterns (capacity ≈ 0.2), robustness to cue noise and ongoing noise, and variability quenching matching cortical observations.  
- Learned synapses are sparse and heavy-tailed despite L2 regularization; many small weights can be clipped with minimal performance loss.  
- Shows tight E/I input balance across attractors and compares recall accuracy to an ideal-observer baseline with comparable performance.  
- Situates work relative to Hopfield networks, continuous attractors, spiking and balanced E/I networks, and prior stability-based RNN training (e.g., Hennequin et al.).  
- Novelty: jointly enforces Dale’s law, graded low-rate analog patterns, and SSA-based stability optimization without relying on saturating nonlinearities or global inhibition.  
- Strength: frames memory embedding as a differentiable stability optimization under biological constraints.  
- Strength: biologically plausible constraints (separate E/I, sparsity, low firing rates, dynamic balance) are incorporated and demonstrated.  
- Strength: empirical evaluation includes principled baselines, noise robustness, and qualitative cortical-like dynamics.  
- Strength: clear exposition and practical training details (reparameterization, ε-scaling, optimizer).  
- Weakness: limited capacity and scaling analysis—results shown only for n=150, m=30 with no systematic study of scaling or m/n dependence.  
- Weakness: objective minimizes dv/dt but does not guarantee exact fixed points; existence and distance to true fixed points are not formally proven.  
- Weakness: per-memory inhibitory auxiliary variables (vinh) raise biological plausibility and identifiability concerns; their role needs clarification or ablation.  
- Weakness: basin-of-attraction sizes are not quantified; local stability (SSA<0) does not imply large or functional basins.  
- Weakness: limited comparisons to alternative network constructions (e.g., continuous Hopfield with Dale’s constraint, global inhibition models, low-rank learned RNNs).  
- Weakness: sparsity claims need ablations varying regularization (ηF) or adding L1 to disentangle causes of pruning.  
- Weakness: clipping experiment uses altered success threshold, complicating interpretation; distribution of convergence distances should be reported.  
- Weakness: some reproducibility details (τi, SSA gradients, hyperparameters) are deferred; explicit gradients, pseudocode, or code would aid reproduction.  
- Key questions for authors: empirically locate true fixed points and report distances/stability margins.  
- Key questions for authors: how does capacity scale with network size and pattern variance?  
- Key questions for authors: what happens if vinh are not optimized per memory or are constrained/shared?  
- Key questions for authors: how sensitive are results to ε and weighting hyperparameters (ηs, ηF)?  
- Key questions for authors: can learned connectivity be transferred to a spiking LIF network to validate rate-to-spike mapping?  
- Recommendation: weak accept—original and promising but would benefit from capacity/scaling analysis, fixed-point verification, and additional ablations/comparisons.
