Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) memory patterns as stable fixed points in recurrent rate-based networks that respect key cortical constraints: Dale’s law (separate E and I populations with sign-constrained weights), sparse recurrent connectivity, graded firing rates at recall, and dynamic E/I balance. The network uses threshold-quadratic rate nonlinearity without high-rate saturation, so stability must arise from recurrent E/I interactions rather than neuronal saturation.

Memory storage is formalized by two conditions for each target pattern: (i) the pattern is a fixed point of the dynamics, and (ii) the fixed point is locally stable. The authors jointly optimize synaptic weights (reparameterized with a softplus to enforce sign constraints) and the inhibitory steady-state activity values at each attractor (“auxiliary” variables) to minimize a composite objective: squared drift at the target state plus the Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian as a differentiable surrogate for spectral abscissa, with L2 weight regularization. This directly targets both fixed-point placement and robust local stability using gradients (L-BFGS, NLopt).

Empirically, for n=150 neurons and m=30 memories (capacity 0.2), optimization yields: (a) negative SSA for all targets, (b) successful autoassociative recall from noisy initial cues with performance comparable to an “ideal observer” nearest-neighbor baseline, (c) robustness to ongoing dynamic noise, (d) tight E/I balance that tracks across attractors for each neuron, (e) emergent sparse weight distributions, with recall largely preserved after clipping small weights, and (f) stimulus-onset reduction of across-trial variability, consistent with cortical data.

The work connects to classical associative memory models (Hopfield 1982), non-binary graded-response analyses (e.g., Treves and colleagues), balanced network theory and stability in E/I cortical circuits, and recent use of SSA in robust control for neural dynamics optimization. It addresses limitations of prior models that relied on symmetric weights, effectively binary attractors, global inhibition, saturating neuronal transfer functions, or strong spatial constraints for bump attractors, and complements spike-timing-based graded memory work that lacked explicit inhibitory populations.

Strengths
- Technical soundness: The formulation cleanly separates fixed-point and stability requirements and uses SSA as a differentiable, conservative surrogate for spectral abscissa. The Dale-respecting reparameterization is standard and appropriate. Empirical tests assess recall basins (via noisy cues), robustness to ongoing noise, and E/I balance, aligning with neurophysiological expectations.
- Biological plausibility: Explicit E/I populations, sign-constrained weights, low firing rates without saturation, log-normal target rate distributions, and balanced inputs provide a coherent, mechanistically grounded network.
- Novelty: Applying robust control tools (SSA minimization) to construct analog attractors in Dale-constrained networks appears novel and pragmatic, overcoming challenges where direct spectral abscissa optimization is non-smooth.
- Informative analyses: Comparisons to an ideal observer, examination of sparsity and weight clipping, and variability quenching analyses are well chosen to demonstrate functional and mechanistic properties.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Scope of validation: Results are shown for a single network size (n=150) and capacity example (m=30). No systematic scaling analysis (capacity vs. N, basin sizes, sensitivity to pattern statistics) is provided. Including capacity scaling and robustness across seeds would strengthen claims.
- Reproducibility details: Several implementation details are deferred (e.g., choice of τi, exact gradient computation through SSA, Table 1 parameters, supplementary mean-field initialization). Providing full hyperparameters and code would improve reproducibility.
- Stability vs. exact fixed points: The method minimizes squared drift and SSA; learned states are reported as “slow points” close to fixed points. It would help to explicitly demonstrate that actual stable fixed points exist near targets (e.g., by fixed-point solvers or linearization checks at converged states).
- Biological learning plausibility: The framework is an offline, global optimization. Discussion notes the open question of local plasticity rules; adding even preliminary links (e.g., ablations demonstrating the role of inhibitory plasticity surrogates) would improve the narrative.

Clarity
- Overall writing is clear and well structured, but several references are incomplete, and there are formatting artifacts (figure labels, “Hz mem or ies”), and key parameter values reside in a missing Table 1. A brief algorithm box outlining the optimization loop, gradients, and stopping criteria would aid readers.
- The role of optimizing vinh per attractor: While valid as auxiliary variables to shape fixed points, a sentence clarifying that these are not free, memory-dependent inputs at recall but the inhibitory steady-state values implied by the learned connectivity would avoid misinterpretation.

Originality
- The main original contribution is importing SSA-based robust stability optimization into analog attractor embedding under Dale’s law, with consistent E/I balance across memories. Prior graded memory models or stabilized supralinear networks do not directly provide this joint construction with per-memory graded targets and explicit local stability control.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a constructive recipe for building mechanistic E/I memory circuits and as a normative target for deriving local learning rules. However, significance is currently limited by lack of scaling results, absence of comparison to alternative constructive methods (e.g., pseudoinverse learning, covariance-based rules), and no spiking implementation test. Extending to larger networks, spiking neurons with matched f–I curves, and benchmarking against established storage rules would increase impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros
- Clear, principled method to embed analog memories with explicit stability control.
- Respects key cortical constraints and demonstrates E/I balance, low rates, and variability quenching.
- Novel integration of robust control (SSA) with neural memory construction.
- Empirical demonstrations are coherent and support claims.

Cons
- Limited scale and breadth of experiments; no capacity scaling or basin quantification.
- No comparisons to standard associative memory training rules.
- Reproducibility gaps (missing parameters/code, incomplete references).
- The learning procedure is global; biological learning plausibility is only discussed, not instantiated.

Requested clarifications and experiments
- Provide complete hyperparameters (including τi, ηs, ηF, initial means/variances), optimization tolerances, and code.
- Capacity and scaling: vary N and m to quantify storage capacity and recall probability; report how ε scaling affects SSA-SA gap and success.
- Basin analysis: quantify basins (e.g., maximum σ for successful recall) across memories.
- Comparisons: evaluate performance vs. pseudoinverse/covariance rules adapted to Dale’s law (e.g., via nonnegative factorization or constrained optimization) and vs. networks without the SSA term.
- Structural constraints: test with enforced sparse connectivity (L1 or mask) to assess robustness.
- Spiking validation: instantiate a spiking network with matched f–I curves and test recall and balance.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a technically sound and novel constructive approach to embedding analog memories in balanced E/I networks using SSA-based stability optimization, with convincing small-scale demonstrations and biologically relevant properties. Strengthening empirical breadth, comparisons, and reproducibility would substantially enhance its significance.
