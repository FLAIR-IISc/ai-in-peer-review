Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a control-theoretic framework to embed multiple analog (graded) attractor memories in a recurrent rate-based network that obeys Dale’s law and exhibits dynamic E/I balance without relying on saturating single-neuron nonlinearities. The network uses a rectified quadratic gain function and optimizes synaptic weights (with a Dale-constrained reparameterization) by minimizing a composite objective: (i) small state velocity at each target pattern (approximate fixed point condition), (ii) negative Smoothed Spectral Abscissa (SSA) of the Jacobian at each target (local stability proxy), and (iii) L2 weight regularization. Inhibitory neuron steady-state activities in each attractor are treated as auxiliary variables during optimization. Empirically, the trained network robustly recalls noisy cues, balances excitation and inhibition across attractors, exhibits sparsity-like weight distributions, and shows variability quenching under stimulus—both consistent with cortical phenomenology. Performance approaches that of an “ideal observer” that selects the nearest stored pattern in cue space.

This work addresses limitations of classical Hopfield networks (symmetry; binary-like attractors), and of later E/I-balanced models where stability relies on saturation or global inhibition tuned to a single level of excitation. It connects to robust control (SSA) used previously for transient control in balanced networks, extending it to memory storage. It is complementary to spatially structured bump attractor models, and to work on balanced spiking networks and inhibitory plasticity, by providing a principled optimization framework for graded attractors under Dale constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent and well-motivated. Using SSA as a differentiable upper bound to the spectral abscissa is appropriate for gradient-based stability optimization. The rate model, Dale enforcement, and cost components are internally consistent.
- Empirical support: The paper provides simulations demonstrating recall success vs noise, robustness to ongoing noise, E/I balance at steady state across attractors, and near “ideal observer” performance. The emergence of many small weights and resilience to clipping most weak weights are interesting.
- Caveats:
  - Fixed points are approximated via small but nonzero dv/dt at targets; stability is certified via SSA at these approximate points. This leaves a gap: no formal guarantee of actual fixed points. The authors claim nearby exact fixed points exist empirically; it would strengthen the work to numerically locate them (e.g., Newton solve) post-training and verify α(J) < 0 there.
  - Capacity is only illustrated (n=150, m=30; capacity ~0.2). No systematic scaling with n, pattern statistics, or regularization is provided. This limits conclusions about practicality and generality.
  - Sensitivity to ε (SSA smoothing), ηs, and ηF is not explored; these choices could materially affect stability/recall and sparsity.
  - Treating inhibitory steady-state activities v_inh^μ as free variables in training increases degrees of freedom by nI m. While weights are shared across memories, this could make storage easier; it would help to quantify how much of the stability burden is carried by connectivity vs per-memory inhibitory states. An ablation constraining v_inh^μ (or showing the trained inhibitory states are indeed realized by the learned dynamics) would clarify.
  - Biological plausibility: the optimization is global and offline; the Discussion notes this, but no constructive path to local rules beyond citing inhibitory plasticity is given.
  - Evaluation thresholds (e.g., 0.001, then 0.08 after clipping) feel ad hoc; reporting basins of attraction (fraction σ leading to correct recall) and confusion matrices per attractor would be more robust.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The objective, constraints, and algorithmic components are described at a level that an expert could reproduce with some effort. However:
  - Provide more detail on gradient computation for SSA and dv/dt terms, step sizes/termination, and initialization distributions (some deferred to supplement).
  - Clarify units and scales (e.g., the ongoing noise amplitude).
  - References are inconsistently formatted and sometimes incomplete.
  - Explicitly demonstrate that after training, the network’s true fixed points (found numerically) closely match the target patterns, and report their Jacobian spectra.

Originality
- The combination of: (i) embedding arbitrary graded attractors, (ii) enforcing Dale’s law with separate E and I populations and non-saturating gains, and (iii) using SSA-based robust stability optimization, is novel within the memory literature. Prior balanced network and continuous attractor work did not provide this optimization-based recipe for arbitrary analog memories under these constraints.
- The idea that E/I balance can stabilize graded attractors without saturation is a useful conceptual contribution.

Significance
- If scalable, this could become a standard approach for synthesizing biologically constrained recurrent networks with desired attractor landscapes, and a bridge between control theory and neural memory models. The empirical observations (sparse-like weights, E/I balance across attractors, variability quenching) tie nicely to experiments.
- Current limitations (no capacity scaling, no formal fixed-point guarantees, and a global offline training rule) temper immediate impact but do not negate the contribution.

Pros
- Principled, differentiable stability objective via SSA; respects Dale’s law.
- Robust recall of analog patterns without saturating nonlinearities; low firing rates via dynamic E/I balance.
- Empirical performance close to an ideal observer; robustness to cue and ongoing noise.
- Emergent sparse-like synapses; many weak weights can be pruned with limited degradation.
- Links to experimentally observed variability quenching.

Cons
- No systematic capacity or scaling analysis; single setting demonstrated.
- No explicit verification of exact fixed points and their spectra post-training.
- Reliance on auxiliary per-memory inhibitory steady states during optimization; unclear necessity/impact.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization not explored.
- Learning rule is global and offline; biological plausibility of learning left open.

Suggestions for authors
- Numerically find exact fixed points post-training and report α(J) and basin sizes.
- Provide capacity vs n, m/n, and statistics of patterns; include ablations for ε, ηs, ηF.
- Quantify the role of v_inh^μ (e.g., train with constraints, or show that learned inhibitory states are recovered by dynamics).
- Compare against alternative training baselines (e.g., pseudo-inverse learning under Dale constraints, Lyapunov-constrained approaches).
- Explore transfer to spiking networks with matched gains.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel optimization framework for embedding analog memories in Dale-constrained, balanced networks and supports it with thoughtful experiments. Addressing the noted gaps would strengthen both the scientific claims and the impact.
