- Proposes Expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM): soft-label linear SVM using expected hinge loss under Bernoulli labels with Pr(Y=1)=g(u) (set to u).  
- Extends to latent-eSVM for weak supervision: unobserved u regularized toward a bounding-box-derived proxy via convex KL term and optimized by alternating minimization.  
- Introduces a spatial confidence map feature that aggregates segment confidences over windows and concatenates with RCNN features.  
- Applied to segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection.  
- Empirical results: e-SVM outperforms SVC/SVR on PASCAL VOC 2011 (TAPC/NDCG) and gives +1.2–1.5 AP over RCNN on VOC 2007 detection.  
- Formulation is sound and simple; known-u case is convex reweighting of hinge losses; latent extension is block-convex with clear alternating scheme.  
- New evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) are appropriate for segment scoring.  
- Experiments lack statistical analysis, ablation studies, and sensitivity analyses (choice of g(u), λR, initialization, proposal quality/number).  
- Limited experimental scope: only VOC 2007/2011, no cross-dataset validation, and no runtime/memory reporting.  
- Writing and derivation are clear overall, but reference formatting, hyperparameter selection, and implementation details (solver settings, convergence) are sparse.  
- Method is related to prior weighted/probabilistic/soft-label SVM work; core loss not fundamentally new but combination with KL regularizer and spatial map is novel.  
- Practical and easy to implement using off-the-shelf solvers; applicable beyond vision and yields consistent but modest gains.  
- Impact tempered by dependence on proposal quality and small absolute improvements.  
- Pros: simple principled extension to soft/latent labels; consistent improvements over baselines; sensible new evaluation for segments.  
- Cons: limited novelty relative to prior literature; insufficient empirical validation and small gains; reliance on proposal quality.  
- Suggestions: compare to probabilistic-label SVMs and MIL baselines; report sensitivity to g(u) and λR, provide error bars; evaluate on more datasets; analyze proposal-quality dependence and computational cost; consider logistic loss or calibrated g(u).
