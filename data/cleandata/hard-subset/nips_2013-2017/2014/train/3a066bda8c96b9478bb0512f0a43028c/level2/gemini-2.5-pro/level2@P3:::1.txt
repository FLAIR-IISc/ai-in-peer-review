**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for binary classification problems where training labels are not discrete {-1, +1} but are continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]). The core idea is to model the true binary label as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by the positiveness score. The e-SVM formulation then minimizes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent binary label. This approach elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM formulation that can be solved with standard solvers.

The work further extends this model to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised scenarios, such as object detection with bounding-box annotations, where the exact positiveness of sub-regions (e.g., segment proposals) is unknown. In this extension, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are optimized alternately with the SVM model parameters, guided by a regularization term that encourages them to remain close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The proposed method is validated on the tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. A key contribution is showing that segmentation-based features trained with the latent e-SVM can be integrated with the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector to achieve a significant performance improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Relationship to Prior Work**

The paper situates itself effectively within the context of weakly supervised learning and computer vision. It correctly distinguishes its problem setting from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), where bags are assumed to contain at least one fully positive instance. Here, all instances (segment proposals) can be partially positive. The work also draws a clear connection to, and distinction from, instance-weighted SVMs, noting that e-SVM effectively weights the positive and negative *labels* for each data point, rather than weighting the data point itself. In the context of computer vision, this work builds upon a line of research that uses segment proposals for semantic segmentation and object detection, but offers a more principled way to learn from the imperfect overlap of these proposals with ground truth, as compared to ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or direct regression with SVR.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the e-SVM as an expected loss is principled, and its reduction to a weighted SVM problem is a significant practical advantage. The alternating optimization proposed for the latent e-SVM is a standard and reasonable approach for this type of problem. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on challenging PASCAL VOC datasets. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification task is a notable contribution that allows for a direct and fair assessment of the core model, demonstrating a high degree of care in the evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, and the progression from the basic e-SVM to the latent e-SVM is logical and easy to follow. The description of how the learned model is integrated into an object detection pipeline via "spatial confidence map features" is also clear. The provided algorithm pseudo-code and figures aid in understanding the proposed framework.

**Originality:** The proposed expectation loss for handling continuous "positiveness" labels is a novel contribution. While related to weighted SVMs, the probabilistic motivation and its specific formulation are new. The extension to the latent e-SVM, where the positiveness itself is a latent variable, is also original and addresses a very practical form of weak supervision in computer vision. Combining these ideas to improve a very strong baseline like R-CNN represents a novel application and a non-trivial research achievement for its time.

**Significance:** The results presented are important. The problem of learning from imperfect or continuous-valued labels is of broad interest. The proposed method is general, simple, and effective. The demonstration that it can provide a significant 1.5% mAP boost to the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector on a standard benchmark is a strong indicator of its significance and potential for impact. Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas, both for object detection and for other problems involving weak supervision.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Quantitative Comparison in Segmentation:** In Section 5.2, the semantic segmentation performance is described as "comparable" to the SVR baseline. For completeness, it would be beneficial to include the specific performance numbers of the SVR baseline in a table alongside the e-SVM results, allowing for a more direct and quantitative comparison.

2.  **Latent Variable Regularization:** The choice of the KL-divergence-based regularizer for the latent positiveness `u` is well-motivated. However, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to the regularization parameter `λR` would be useful. This could be a short note in the experimental section or supplementary material.

3.  **Algorithm Details:** For reproducibility, Algorithm 1 could specify the convergence criterion used (e.g., change in loss function below a threshold, or a fixed number of iterations).

These are minor suggestions that could enhance an already high-quality submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and practical method (e-SVM) for an important problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   The work is technically sound and the claims are convincingly supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   The results show a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art in object detection on a competitive benchmark, demonstrating clear impact.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   This reviewer finds no major flaws in the work that would warrant rejection. The minor points for improvement listed above do not detract from the overall contribution.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and impactful contribution to the field. It is well-executed, thoroughly evaluated, and addresses a significant problem with a novel and elegant solution.
