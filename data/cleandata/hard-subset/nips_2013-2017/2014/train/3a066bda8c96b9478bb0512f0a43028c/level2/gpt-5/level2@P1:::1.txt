Summary
The paper introduces expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple extension of linear SVM for training with “soft” labels in [0,1] that represent the “positiveness” of each example (e.g., IoU to ground truth). The method treats the binary label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability g(u) (taken to be u), and minimizes the expectation of the hinge loss, yielding for each training instance a convex combination of positive and negative hinge losses: g(u)·max(0,1−w^T x) + (1−g(u))·max(0,1+w^T x). The authors also propose a latent e-SVM for weak supervision when u is unobserved (only boxes available): u is optimized jointly with w under a convex regularizer R(u) given by the cross-entropy between the initialized u_bb (IoU with the box) and u. They alternate between convex optimization in w and in u.

Empirically, e-SVM is evaluated on segment proposal scoring for semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC 2011), and coupled to RCNN for object detection (PASCAL VOC 2007) via a “spatial confidence map” feature derived from segment confidences. The method outperforms SVC and SVR on two new segment-scoring metrics (TAPC and NDCG), achieves comparable semantic segmentation accuracy to SVR, and improves RCNN mAP by about 1.5 points after bounding-box regression.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy-to-implement objective that uses standard linear SVM solvers (per-instance mixing of positive/negative hinge losses).
- Clean extension to a weakly-supervised setting with a convex u-update, anchored by a principled cross-entropy regularizer.
- Practical visual pipeline: the spatial confidence map is a reasonable way to inject segment-level evidence into window-based detection systems.
- Empirical gains on RCNN on VOC 2007 (≈+1.5 mAP) are non-trivial for that era and suggest the approach provides complementary information.
- The work addresses a relevant and common setting in vision where only bounding boxes are available; the formulation is broadly applicable beyond the specific tasks considered.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears modest. Using expected hinge loss w.r.t. probabilistic/soft labels is a fairly direct idea; closely related formulations exist in learning with uncertain labels, soft labels, and label proportions. The paper’s distinction from weighted SVM is correct but partial; the proposed loss can be seen as weighting the positive/negative hinge parts per instance, which is known in variants of SVMs with probabilistic labels and partial labels. Related literature is not adequately discussed (e.g., learning with soft/uncertain labels, learning from label proportions, partial/ambiguous labels, learning-to-rank for IoU prediction).
- The latent extension may be overly reliant on the initialization u_bb and the strength of the KL regularization; if λ_R is large, u may remain close to u_bb (which can be poorly correlated with true IoU), limiting the benefit of learning. There is no sensitivity analysis for λ_R or ablation of R(u).
- Comparisons are limited. For segment scoring, baselines like logistic regression on soft labels, ordinal/ranking SVM, or probabilistic regression calibrated to [0,1] would be informative. For detection, it would help to compare alternative ways to integrate segment cues (e.g., simple pooling of segment confidences without e-SVM, or rankers), and report per-class effects more thoroughly.
- Theoretical analysis is light: no generalization guarantees, calibration discussion (why g(u)=u is appropriate), or convergence properties of the alternating scheme beyond convexity of subproblems.
- Some practical details are missing: how the expected hinge loss is implemented with standard solvers (e.g., duplicate examples with weights g(u) and 1−g(u)), optimization details for the u-update (constraints, step size/solver), runtimes, and memory.

Quality
- The formulation is technically sound, and the objective for fixed u is convex; the u-update is convex with the proposed R(u). Alternating minimization is standard, though global optimality is not guaranteed.
- Experiments are reasonable and suggest effectiveness on detection, though the segmentation gains over SVR are small and the detection comparison space is narrow. Additional ablations (role of g(.), λ_R, number of segments, grid size m, CPMC vs superpixels) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Claims about “state-of-the-art” detection should be carefully scoped to the specific baseline and protocol; they appear to be relative to RCNN at that time.

Clarity
- Overall well-organized and readable. The core idea is clearly presented. A few notational slips aside, the method can be understood. Please add implementation details for how the expected loss is handled in LibLinear, constraints on u∈(0,1), and stopping criteria. The new metrics (TAPC, NDCG) are sensible but should be described more precisely (e.g., threshold grid, averaging protocol).

Originality
- Moderate. The specific combination—expected hinge with soft labels plus a KL-regularized latent extension tailored to bounding box supervision and a practical confidence-map integration into RCNN—is a useful package. However, the expected-hinge formulation and learning with soft/uncertain labels are not new; related works should be cited and contrasted more thoroughly.

Significance
- The weakly supervised setting is important, and the method’s simplicity is appealing. The detection improvements over a strong baseline indicate potential utility. Broader impact may be limited by modest novelty and relatively small gains, but the approach could be adopted by practitioners given its ease of use.

Questions and suggestions
- Why fix g(u)=u? Did you try calibrated mappings (e.g., sigmoid/temperature scaling) learned on a validation set?
- Please detail the u-update (closed-form per instance? projected gradient with box constraints u∈(0,1)?). Provide sensitivity to λ_R.
- Compare against soft-label logistic regression and learning-to-rank baselines for segment IoU prediction.
- Provide ablations for the spatial confidence map: effect of m, pooling type, segment generator, and using raw IoU (where available) vs learned e-SVM confidences.
- Analyze failure modes: when does u_bb mislead the latent optimization? Can additional priors (e.g., spatial coherence across segments of the same image) help?
- Report training time and memory; the method scales with number of segments and classes.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant weakly supervised scenario with a simple, general method.
- Empirically improves a strong detector.
- Easy to implement and likely useful to others.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to prior work on soft/uncertain labels and latent SVMs.
- Restricted baselines and ablations; theoretical justification minimal.
- Some reliance on heuristic choices (g(u)=u, KL anchor to u_bb) not fully validated.

Overall, I find the idea practical and the detection gains encouraging, but I encourage the authors to strengthen the positioning w.r.t. related work on soft labels, broaden the comparisons, and add sensitivity/ablation studies to solidify the contribution.
