

The paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with "positiveness" values (continuous values in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The method extends standard SVMs by treating the label as a random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness value, leading to an expectation-based loss function. The paper demonstrates applications to semantic segmentation and object detection, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

The work builds on the growing need for weakly supervised learning in computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. It relates to previous work on multiple instance learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs (WSVMs), but clearly distinguishes itself by addressing a middle ground between strong and weak supervision where segment proposals have partial overlap with ground truth (0 < IoU < 1), which MIL cannot handle. The paper also connects to recent advances in segmentation and detection, particularly the use of segment proposals and CNN features.

Strengths include a clear problem formulation, well-motivated mathematical derivation of the e-SVM loss function, and thorough experimental validation across multiple computer vision tasks. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification is particularly valuable, providing a more direct performance measure than previous approaches. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across different threshold values and annotation scenarios, with a notable 1.5% average precision gain on object detection. The implementation details are sufficiently clear to reproduce, and the method's flexibility to use various segment proposal detectors and feature extractors is well-documented.

The main weaknesses include an insufficient justification for the significance of the 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007, especially considering the field has since progressed beyond RCNN with faster detectors. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but does not compare against more recent methods like Fast R-CNN or Faster R-CNN, which would better contextualize the contribution. Additionally, the weak supervision extension (latent e-SVM) is not clearly justified as necessary when bounding box IoU could serve as a proxy for positiveness, and the paper does not demonstrate scenarios where the latent extension provides significant advantages over simpler approaches.

The regularization term for the latent variables (Equation 3) is described as "strong" but lacks theoretical or empirical justification for why this specific form is superior to alternatives. The paper also doesn't explore potential limitations, such as performance degradation when the positiveness distribution is highly skewed or when the bounding box IoU is a poor proxy for true segment quality.

For improvement, the authors should:
1. Compare against more recent detection methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN) to better establish the significance of their improvement.
2. Provide more detailed analysis of when the latent e-SVM extension is truly beneficial versus using bounding box IoU directly.
3. Include a discussion of potential limitations and failure cases.
4. Clarify the theoretical justification for the specific regularization term used in the latent extension.
5. More thoroughly justify the significance of the 1.5% improvement in the context of the field's progress.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the problem of weakly supervised learning in computer vision, with a clear algorithmic formulation and compelling empirical results on standard benchmarks. While the improvements are modest, the method is well-motivated and addresses a practical problem. The work advances the state of the art in the specific context of using segment proposals for object detection and semantic segmentation under weak supervision, with potential to reduce annotation costs for large-scale vision tasks. The paper is well-written and the experiments are appropriately designed, though the significance claim could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to contemporary methods.
