- Introduces e-SVM: an SVM variant that uses expected hinge loss over latent Bernoulli labels with known positiveness scores in [0,1].
- e-SVM yields a weighted loss where each sample contributes to both positive and negative margins and is solvable with standard SVM solvers.
- Extends to latent e-SVM for weak supervision by jointly optimizing latent positiveness scores and classifier weights via alternating minimization with a KL regularizer.
- Applies method to learn segment classifiers from bounding-box supervision and produces a spatial confidence map feature.
- Combining the spatial confidence feature with R-CNN features yields a 1.5% mAP gain and a new state-of-the-art on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Proposes appropriate intermediate evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for segment proposal classification.
- Technically sound and well-founded; experimental evaluation, especially detection results, is strong and convincing.
- Lacks theoretical analysis of the alternating minimization—no convergence guarantee or monotonicity proof provided.
- Paper is well-written with clear motivation and easy-to-follow e-SVM derivation.
- Needs clearer justification for using KL-divergence regularizer over simpler alternatives (e.g., L2).
- The construction of the spatial confidence map feature is described densely and would benefit from a step-by-step explanation for reproducibility.
- Semantic segmentation results are only described as "comparable" and should be shown in a table alongside baselines for direct comparison.
- Original contribution: novel treatment of soft labels in SVM (weighted contributions to both slacks) and a creative latent-variable extension for weak supervision.
- Significant practical impact: enables learning from weak (bounding-box) supervision and complements deep detectors, with potential applicability beyond vision.
- Acceptance arguments: principled novel method, effective weakly supervised extension, strong state-of-the-art empirical gains, and high practical significance.
- Rejection arguments: missing formal convergence analysis and a few presentation/reproducibility issues.
- Overall conclusion: strong, novel, and impactful paper whose strengths outweigh minor weaknesses.
