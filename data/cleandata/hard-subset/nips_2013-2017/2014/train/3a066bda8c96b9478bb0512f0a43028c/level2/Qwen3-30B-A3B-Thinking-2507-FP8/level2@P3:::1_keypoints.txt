- Proposes Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), which treats labels as Bernoulli random variables with given "positiveness" in [0,1] and optimizes an expectation-based SVM loss.
- Targets weakly supervised learning where examples have continuous positiveness values (e.g., segment IoU), filling a gap between fully supervised and MIL settings.
- Demonstrates applications to semantic segmentation and object detection using segment proposals and CNN features.
- Shows consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC, including a reported ~1.5% mAP gain for detection.
- Introduces new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification, argued to be more direct than prior measures.
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, well-motivated mathematical derivation, reproducible implementation details, flexible to different proposal detectors and features, and thorough experiments.
- Distinguishes itself from MIL and WSVM by explicitly modeling partial overlap (0 < IoU < 1) between proposals and ground truth.
- Weakness: the reported 1.5% AP improvement lacks contextual justification given advances beyond R-CNN (no comparison to Fast/Faster R-CNN).
- Weakness: the latent e-SVM extension is not clearly justified or demonstrated to outperform simpler IoU-based proxies.
- Weakness: the regularization term for latent variables (Eq. 3) is described as "strong" but lacks theoretical or empirical justification versus alternatives.
- Weakness: no analysis of failure modes or limitations, e.g., performance under highly skewed positiveness distributions or when IoU poorly reflects quality.
- Recommended improvements: compare to more recent detectors (Fast/Faster R-CNN), analyze when latent e-SVM helps versus IoU, discuss limitations and failure cases, justify the chosen regularizer, and better contextualize the significance of the reported gains.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written work addressing a practical weak-supervision problem with modest but consistent empirical gains, though significance claims should be strengthened with broader comparisons and analyses.
