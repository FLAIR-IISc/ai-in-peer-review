### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning scenarios where training labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded [0, 1] interval. This problem is highly relevant in computer vision, where the quality of a segment proposal is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea of e-SVM is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is determined by the known positiveness score. The objective function then becomes the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this latent label distribution. This results in an intuitive weighted loss where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative sides of the margin, and can be optimized using standard SVM solvers.

The paper further extends this model to a weakly supervised setting, called latent e-SVM, for cases where the true positiveness is unknown (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). Here, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are optimized jointly with the classifier weights via an alternating minimization scheme. A KL-divergence based regularization term is used to constrain the latent positiveness to remain close to an initial estimate derived from the weak labels.

The method is validated on several computer vision tasks. The primary contribution is demonstrated in object detection, where the latent e-SVM is used to learn a segment classifier from bounding-box supervision. The output of this classifier is then used to generate a "spatial confidence map feature" which, when concatenated with features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, yields a significant performance improvement and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a well-founded piece of work. The formulation of the e-SVM as an expected loss is a principled and clean extension of the standard SVM framework. The experimental evaluation is strong, particularly the object detection results. Showing a clear and significant improvement (1.5% mAP) over a powerful contemporary baseline like R-CNN provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors are also careful to propose new and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for the intermediate task of segment proposal classification, which is a commendable contribution.

A minor weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis for the latent e-SVM optimization. The alternating minimization procedure in Algorithm 1 is intuitive, but the paper does not discuss its convergence properties. It would strengthen the work to include a brief analysis showing that the objective function decreases monotonically and converges to a stationary point.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and the core concepts are communicated effectively. The motivation for the problem is clear, and the derivation of the e-SVM model is easy to follow.

A few suggestions for improvement:
- The rationale for choosing the KL-divergence regularizer (Equation 3) for the latent e-SVM could be better explained. A sentence or two justifying this choice over simpler alternatives (e.g., L2 norm) would be helpful.
- The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 is a bit dense. A clearer, step-by-step description of its construction would enhance reproducibility.
- In Section 5.2, the semantic segmentation results are described as "comparable" to the state-of-the-art. It would be more convincing to present these results in a table alongside the baseline numbers for a direct comparison.

**Originality**

The work is original. While it is related to weighted SVMs, the e-SVM formulation—where a single data point contributes weighted costs to both positive and negative slack variables based on its positiveness—is a novel approach to incorporating soft labels. The extension to a latent variable model for weak supervision is also a creative and effective contribution. The paper successfully situates itself in the context of prior work, clearly differentiating its problem setting from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and highlighting its place between fully supervised and weakly supervised paradigms. The combination of weakly supervised segmentation cues with a strong detection pipeline to improve performance is also a novel and impactful idea.

**Significance**

The significance of this paper is high. The problem of learning from weak supervision is of immense practical importance, as generating precise, pixel-level labels is a major bottleneck in building large-scale vision systems. The latent e-SVM provides an effective and practical method for leveraging more readily available bounding box annotations.

The most significant contribution is the demonstration that these weakly learned segmentation cues can provide complementary information that improves even state-of-the-art deep learning-based object detectors. This result is important as it suggests that combining different model types and sources of supervision can lead to further advances in challenging vision tasks. The e-SVM framework is general and could likely be applied to other domains outside of computer vision where supervision is noisy or expressed as a degree of confidence. The strong results are likely to encourage further research into hybrid models and learning from weak supervision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and elegant method (e-SVM) for learning from continuous "positiveness" labels.
*   Effectively extends the method to the challenging and highly relevant weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM).
*   Demonstrates a significant, state-of-the-art result on the competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark by improving a very strong baseline.
*   The problem and solution are of high practical significance for the machine learning and computer vision communities.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The optimization for the latent e-SVM lacks a formal convergence analysis.
*   Minor aspects of the presentation could be clarified to improve reproducibility and impact.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel and useful contribution, backed by excellent experimental results. The strengths significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses.
