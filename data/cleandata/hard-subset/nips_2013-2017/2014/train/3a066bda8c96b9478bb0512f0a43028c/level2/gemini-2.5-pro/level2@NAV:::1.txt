This paper presents a novel algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where labels are continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded interval (e.g., IoU) rather than binary. The key idea is to model the traditional SVM hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, whose Bernoulli probability is a function of the known positiveness score. This formulation is cleverly extended to a latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., with only bounding-box supervision), treating it as a latent variable to be optimized jointly with the classifier. This work provides a principled alternative to common heuristics like thresholding for Support Vector Classification (SVC) or directly applying Support Vector Regression (SVR).

The paper's strengths are numerous.
**Pro-acceptance:**
*   **Originality & Quality:** The e-SVM formulation is an elegant and novel extension of the max-margin principle. The technical approach is sound, and the extension to the latent variable case for weak supervision is well-motivated. Claims are well-supported by rigorous experiments on PASCAL VOC datasets.
*   **Significance:** The problem of learning from weaker supervision is highly relevant. The paper demonstrates significant empirical gains, not only outperforming strong baselines like SVR and various SVC configurations on the segment classification sub-task but also achieving a notable 1.5% average precision improvement on top of the very strong R-CNN baseline for object detection on VOC 2007. This shows clear practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, logically progressing from the basic model to the latent extension and its applications. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the specific sub-problem is a thoughtful addition.

**Con-acceptance (Minor Weaknesses):**
*   The discussion on the optimization of the latent e-SVM could be expanded. For example, an analysis of the sensitivity to initialization or the convergence properties of the alternating optimization would strengthen the paper.
*   While the choice of KL-divergence for the regularization term R(u) is reasonable, a brief discussion or ablation study comparing it to other potential regularizers could provide additional insight.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and original contribution. It tackles an important problem with a technically sound and elegant solution, backed by strong experimental validation. The work advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
