This paper proposes the Expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a soft-label variant of linear SVM that replaces binary labels with a “positiveness” value u ∈ [0,1] and optimizes the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label model with Pr(Y=1)=g(u) (set to u in experiments). The method extends naturally to a weakly supervised, latent-eSVM setting where u is unobserved and regularized toward a bounding-box-derived proxy via a convex KL term; alternating minimization over w and u uses standard SVM solvers. The authors apply e-SVM to segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection, introducing a spatial confidence map feature that aggregates segment confidences over candidate windows and concatenates them with RCNN features. On PASCAL VOC 2011 (segments) e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR under TAPC/NDCG; on VOC 2007 detection it yields +1.2–1.5 AP over RCNN.

Quality: The formulation is sound and simple; when u is known, the objective is a convex reweighting of hinge losses; the latent-u extension is block-convex with a clear alternating scheme. The new evaluation criteria (TAPC, NDCG) make sense for segment scoring. However, experimental support lacks statistical analysis, ablations, and sensitivity studies (choice of g(u), λR, initialization, number/quality of proposals). Reporting is limited to VOC 2007/2011 with no cross-dataset validation, and runtime/memory are not discussed.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, with a clear derivation and algorithm. Some reference formatting is rough, and hyperparameter selection and implementation details are sparse (e.g., solver settings, convergence criteria).

Originality: Modeling soft labels via expected margin losses is related to weighted/label-uncertain SVMs and soft-label learning; the core loss is not fundamentally new but its combination with a KL regularizer for weak supervision and the spatial confidence map feature is novel and practically motivated. Related work on SVMs with probabilistic/uncertain labels should be discussed more comprehensively.

Significance: The method is practical (uses off-the-shelf solvers), applicable beyond vision, and delivers consistent, though modest, gains on a strong detector. Its utility will scale with the availability of weak annotations. Dependence on proposal quality and the small absolute improvements temper impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled extension of SVM to soft/latent labels; easy to implement.
- Consistent improvements over SVC/SVR and a measurable boost to RCNN.
- New, sensible evaluation for segment scoring.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to prior soft/weighted/uncertain-label SVM literature.
- Insufficient ablations, sensitivity, and statistical significance.
- Modest gains and reliance on proposal quality.

Suggestions: Compare against probabilistic-label SVMs and MIL baselines; report sensitivity to g(u) and λR; provide error bars; evaluate on additional datasets; analyze proposal-quality dependence and computational cost; consider logistic loss or calibrated g(u). Overall, a solid, useful contribution that would benefit from stronger empirical validation.
