This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for problems with continuous “positiveness” values representing the degree of affiliation to a positive class, rather than binary labels. The work extends to weakly supervised scenarios, specifically segment proposal classification, and demonstrates strong performance on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection. 

The core idea – adapting SVMs to handle continuous confidence scores – is a reasonable and practical approach to weakly supervised learning. The motivation stemming from the expense of fully labeled datasets is well-established and relevant to the NIPS audience. The proposed algorithm is presented with a clear mathematical formulation, and its extension to the latent variable case for weak supervision is thoughtfully considered. The experimental validation across multiple tasks – segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection – demonstrates the versatility of the method. The comparisons against SVC and SVR are relevant and show consistent improvements. The inclusion of a spatial confidence map feature for object detection, coupled with an existing CNN-based detector, appears to be a simple yet effective augmentation.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the paper mentions related work on weakly supervised learning and weighted SVMs, the connections could be made more explicit and detailed. For instance, the discussion of Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) feels brief, and a more thorough comparison of e-SVM to existing MIL approaches (even a justification for why MIL isn't directly applicable) would be beneficial.  Similarly, the nuances between e-SVM and weighted SVMs could be expanded. The claim that the loss function difference leads to advantages without further elaboration feels weak. A more detailed explanation of *why* e-SVM's weighting scheme is particularly suitable for this problem would be helpful.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, lacks a more nuanced analysis of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM. The impact of different regularization parameters, and a justification for the chosen parameter setting, is missing. The chosen regularization term (KL divergence) is presented without a comprehensive discussion of alternatives and why this specific choice was preferred.  Furthermore, the improvement of 1.5% on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection, while state-of-the-art, is presented without a thorough discussion of statistical significance.  A statistical test comparing the results would provide more confidence in the reported gains.  The paper also mentions the potential to utilize ImageNet data but doesn't explore it experimentally.

Regarding clarity, the notation could be made more consistent. For example, the definition of *ui* varies slightly throughout the paper.  The writing is generally good, but some sentences are overly complex and could be simplified for better readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to handling continuous confidence scores in SVM learning.
*   Strong experimental results across multiple tasks, including state-of-the-art object detection.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and extension to weakly supervised settings.
*   Practical relevance to the problem of expensive data labeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion and comparison to related work (particularly MIL and weighted SVMs).
*   Insufficient analysis of the regularization term and its impact.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing for performance improvements.
*   Minor clarity issues in notation and writing.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is a practical and effective approach to handling continuous confidence scores, and the experimental results are compelling.  Addressing the weaknesses regarding related work discussion, regularization analysis, and statistical significance would significantly strengthen the paper.  This reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of related work, a more thorough analysis of the regularization term, and the inclusion of statistical significance tests would address the key concerns.
