

This paper proposes the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with a "positiveness" value (a continuous value in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors frame this as a general problem where the label is a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the positiveness value. They extend this to a latent e-SVM version for weak supervision where positiveness is unobserved. The method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with claims of state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a clear mathematical formulation. The e-SVM model elegantly addresses the problem of continuous label values by treating the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the positiveness value. The derivation of the expectation loss function is mathematically rigorous, and the extension to latent variables for weak supervision is well-motivated. The authors provide a thorough comparison to SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset, demonstrating consistent improvements in both TAPC and NDCG metrics. The application to object detection using RCNN as a base detector shows a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007, which is a meaningful result for a relatively simple feature fusion approach.

The paper's strengths include a clear problem statement, sound mathematical formulation, comprehensive experimental evaluation, and practical application to important computer vision tasks. The authors demonstrate how their method can work with both strong supervision (pixel-level annotations) and weak supervision (bounding box annotations), which is particularly valuable for large-scale datasets where pixel-level annotations are prohibitively expensive.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is modest (1.5% AP improvement over RCNN), especially when compared to more recent advances in object detection (e.g., Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN). The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with these methods, as the 1.5% improvement is smaller than many recent advances in the field. Second, the paper claims generality to "other classification problems" but fails to provide concrete examples beyond computer vision, which weakens the perceived broader contribution. Third, the paper does not thoroughly explore the relationship to weighted SVMs (WSVMs), which are mentioned in the related work but not fully compared. The authors state that their method differs from WSVMs in "weighting labels instead of data points," but this distinction requires more detailed explanation to demonstrate why WSVMs cannot be directly applied to this problem.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points. Specifically, the authors should: (1) provide a more detailed comparison with recent object detection methods beyond RCNN; (2) include a concrete example of their method applied to a non-computer vision problem to demonstrate broader applicability; (3) provide a more thorough analysis of the relationship between their method and weighted SVMs; and (4) discuss the computational complexity of the latent e-SVM training compared to standard SVMs.

The quality of the paper is good, with clear theoretical foundations and well-executed experiments. The clarity is generally good, though some technical details about the regularization term (Equation 3) could be more thoroughly explained. The originality is moderate - the formulation is novel for this specific problem setting, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from related work on weighted SVMs and weakly supervised learning. The significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains compared to the state of the art, though the problem addressed is clearly important for reducing annotation costs in computer vision.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound approach to a relevant problem in weakly supervised learning. The method is well-motivated, the mathematical formulation is clear, and the experimental results support the claims. However, the contribution's significance would be substantially enhanced by addressing the limitations noted above, particularly by demonstrating more substantial improvements over recent state-of-the-art methods and providing a clearer distinction from related work on weighted SVMs. With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
