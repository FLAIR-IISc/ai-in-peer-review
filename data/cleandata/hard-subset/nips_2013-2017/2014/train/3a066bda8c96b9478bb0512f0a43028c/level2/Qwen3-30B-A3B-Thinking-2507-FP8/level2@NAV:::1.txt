

This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors propose e-SVM as a novel extension to standard SVMs that directly handles this continuous supervision setting, and extend it to a latent form for weak supervision scenarios where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available). The paper demonstrates the method on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

The main strengths of the paper include a clear and well-motivated problem formulation, a technically sound algorithmic contribution with a well-justified loss function, and comprehensive experimental validation across multiple computer vision tasks. The authors introduce two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that better assess segment proposal classification performance than previous approaches. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection compared to RCNN.

The paper's quality is strong, with well-supported claims through statistically significant experiments. The clarity is good, with clear mathematical formulations and pseudocode for the optimization algorithm. The novelty lies in the expectation loss formulation that properly handles continuous supervision without requiring thresholding heuristics, distinguishing it from prior work like MIL and weighted SVMs. The significance is high, as weakly supervised learning addresses a major bottleneck in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are prohibitively expensive.

However, the paper has some limitations. The claim of "state-of-the-art" object detection performance requires more context, as the comparison is only against RCNN and not more recent detectors. The regularization term's impact on performance could be better analyzed, and the paper doesn't explore how the method compares to more recent weakly supervised learning approaches. The extension to weak supervision is promising but could be more thoroughly validated with additional datasets.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem in computer vision with a technically sound and practically useful approach. The paper's clear presentation, comprehensive experiments, and meaningful improvement over baselines make it a valuable addition to the literature. With minor clarifications about the comparison to recent detectors and the regularization analysis, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
