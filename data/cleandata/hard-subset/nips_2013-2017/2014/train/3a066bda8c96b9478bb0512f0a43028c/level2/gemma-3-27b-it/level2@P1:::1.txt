This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for problems where training data is weakly supervised, specifically when only a “positiveness” value representing the affiliation to the positive class is available instead of a binary label. The core idea is to model the positiveness as a probability and formulate a loss function that weights examples by this probability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in computer vision – learning from weakly labeled data. The introduction clearly outlines the challenges of obtaining large, fully annotated datasets and positions e-SVM as a solution. The connection to existing methods like SVC and SVR is also well explained, highlighting the limitations of these approaches in the context of continuous positiveness values. The extension to handle the weakly supervised case by treating positiveness as latent variables is a significant contribution. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering a variety of tasks and demonstrating the performance gains of e-SVM over existing methods. The inclusion of both strong and weak supervision scenarios, and the clear presentation of results in tables and figures, enhances the clarity and impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM algorithm offers a novel approach to learning with continuous confidence values, effectively bridging the gap between standard binary classification and regression.
*   **Practicality:** The method addresses a very practical problem in computer vision – leveraging weakly labeled data to reduce annotation costs.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements in segment classification, semantic segmentation, and particularly object detection (state-of-the-art on PASCAL VOC 2007).
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and experimental setup.
*   **Generality:** The authors emphasize the generality of the approach, noting its compatibility with different segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Latent Variable Optimization:** While the latent e-SVM is a key contribution, the discussion of the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the choice of regularization term (Equation 3) feels somewhat brief. More details on the practical considerations of implementing and tuning this algorithm would be helpful. Specifically, the sensitivity to the regularization parameter and potential issues with convergence should be addressed.
*   **Comparison to More Recent Object Detection Methods:** While the state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is impressive, the comparison is against RCNN [14], which is now somewhat dated. Including comparisons to more recent detectors like Faster R-CNN or YOLO would provide a more comprehensive benchmark and demonstrate the continued relevance of the approach.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study investigating the contribution of different components of the system (e.g., the spatial confidence map features) would provide further insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Repetitive Explanations:** Some explanations, particularly concerning the motivation and problem setup, are repeated several times throughout the paper. This redundancy could be reduced to improve conciseness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (e-SVM) that addresses a significant challenge in computer vision – learning from weakly labeled data.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on a variety of tasks.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to understand.
*   The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion of the latent variable optimization and the choice of regularization could be more detailed.
*   The comparison to object detection methods is somewhat dated.
*   The absence of an ablation study limits the ability to fully assess the contribution of individual components.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to provide more detail on the latent variable optimization, include comparisons to more recent object detection methods, and consider adding an ablation study.
