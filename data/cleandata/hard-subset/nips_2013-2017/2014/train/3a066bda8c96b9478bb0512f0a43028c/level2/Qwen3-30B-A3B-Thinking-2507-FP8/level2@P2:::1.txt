

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are annotated with continuous "positiveness" values (between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their method can handle both strong supervision (when positiveness is observed) and weak supervision (when positiveness is unobserved, treated as latent variables). They validate the approach on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear motivation for weakly supervised learning in computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive but bounding box annotations are more readily available. The e-SVM formulation is a reasonable extension of standard SVMs, treating the label as a random variable whose success probability is determined by the positiveness value. The authors provide a novel evaluation framework (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification, which is a significant contribution to the evaluation of such models.

However, the paper has several critical weaknesses that undermine its main claims. First, the connection to prior work is superficial. While the authors briefly discuss Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs, they fail to adequately explain why existing MIL approaches don't apply to their problem setting. The key distinction they claim (that "most proposals will contain both foreground and background pixels") is not sufficiently argued - MIL methods like the one in [26] explicitly handle partial labels, which seems directly relevant. The authors also don't cite recent weakly supervised learning methods that directly address similar problems (e.g., [19, 20, 22]).

Second, the theoretical contribution is minimal. The e-SVM is essentially a weighted SVM with weights derived from the positiveness values. The authors don't provide theoretical justification for why this weighting scheme is optimal or how it differs fundamentally from existing weighted SVM approaches. The latent extension (Section 3.2) is presented as novel, but the optimization procedure resembles standard EM for latent SVMs, which has been well-studied.

Third, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient comparison to relevant baselines. For object detection, the authors compare to RCNN but don't include comparisons to other weakly supervised object detection methods that might be more directly comparable. Their claim of "state-of-the-art" performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is misleading without context - how does their method compare to other weakly supervised approaches? The 1.5% improvement over RCNN is modest, especially considering the complexity of the proposed approach.

The paper also has several technical weaknesses. The regularization term for the latent variables (Equation 3) is chosen without theoretical justification, and the authors don't explore alternatives. The claim that "the performance of our e-SVM is much better than SVC" is not substantiated with statistical significance tests. The experiments don't discuss limitations of the approach or failure cases, which would strengthen the paper.

The writing is generally clear, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a more nuanced presentation of the results. The authors should explicitly address why their approach is necessary given existing methods for handling continuous supervision signals.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a reasonable algorithmic extension, the contributions are incremental rather than novel. The lack of sufficient theoretical justification, superficial connection to prior work, and inadequate experimental comparison to relevant baselines weaken the paper's claims. The method is interesting but not sufficiently novel or impactful for NIPS.

Recommendation: Weak Reject. The paper has some interesting ideas but falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The authors should substantially revise the paper to better position their work in the context of existing literature, provide stronger theoretical justification, and conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing to relevant baselines.
