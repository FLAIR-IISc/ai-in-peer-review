This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) that can handle the situation where only the "positiveness" of each training sample is available, instead of a binary label. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm, including the formulation of the expectation loss SVM model and the optimization algorithm used to train the model. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a clear comparison to other methods and a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is that it addresses a common problem in computer vision, where only weakly labeled data is available. The authors show that their algorithm can effectively handle this type of data and achieve state-of-the-art performance on several tasks.

The paper also has some weaknesses. One of the main limitations is that the authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of their algorithm. For example, they do not provide any guarantees on the convergence of the optimization algorithm or the generalization performance of the learned model.

Another limitation is that the authors do not compare their algorithm to other methods that can handle weakly labeled data, such as multiple instance learning (MIL) or weighted SVMs. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and contribution of the proposed approach.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a new algorithm that is different from existing methods. However, the idea of using a weighted loss function to handle weakly labeled data is not new, and the authors could have provided more context and comparison to other methods that use similar ideas.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a novel algorithm that achieves state-of-the-art performance on several tasks. However, it could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of the algorithm and a comparison to other methods that can handle weakly labeled data.
