This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically when only a “positiveness” score (between 0 and 1) is available instead of a binary label. The core idea is to modify the SVM loss function to weight examples by their positiveness value, effectively treating the binary label as a latent variable in the weakly supervised setting. The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in weakly supervised learning, drawing connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. The authors appropriately acknowledge related works, although the discussion of weighted SVMs could be more nuanced regarding the differences in weighting schemes. The framing of the problem – learning from continuous “positiveness” scores – is well-motivated, and the problem setting is common in computer vision tasks where obtaining precise labels is expensive (e.g., bounding box annotations vs. pixel-level segmentation).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM algorithm offers a relatively novel approach to addressing the weakly supervised learning problem with continuous confidence scores.  The modification to the standard SVM loss function is conceptually simple yet effective.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results, especially the state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007, are compelling. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing to SVC and SVR) provide convincing evidence that e-SVM outperforms existing methods.  The consistent improvements across different tasks (segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection) suggest the method’s generality.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:**  The authors clearly define the problem and its motivations.  The explanation of the “positiveness” concept is intuitive and the connection to IoU is well-defined.
* **Practical Algorithm:**  The algorithm is presented in a way that suggests it’s easily implementable and can leverage existing SVM solvers. The iterative optimization procedure for the latent variable case is clearly described.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on multiple datasets and tasks, showcasing its broad applicability.  The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification is a welcome addition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While relevant work is cited, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related approaches for handling continuous labels or uncertainty in the loss function.  For example, approaches based on noise modeling or label smoothing could be discussed.  The difference between the weighting in e-SVM and weighted SVMs (as noted in the paper) is somewhat superficial; a deeper dive into the theoretical underpinnings of each could be beneficial.
* **Regularization Sensitivity:** The latent e-SVM model relies on a regularization term to prevent the optimization from getting stuck. The paper mentions this is important but provides limited insight into *why* certain regularization terms work better than others or how to tune the regularization parameter. More analysis of the regularization term's impact would strengthen the paper.
* **Clarity of Spatial Confidence Map Feature:** While the idea of using spatial confidence maps for object detection is sound, the explanation of how they are created and integrated with RCNN features could be more detailed. The statement "we encode it by additive kernels approximation methods" is a bit vague; a more detailed description or a reference to the relevant work would be helpful.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper would be strengthened by some error analysis.  Where does e-SVM struggle?  Are there specific types of segments or objects where it performs poorly?  Understanding the limitations of the method is crucial.
* **Figure 1 is low resolution:** This makes it difficult to understand the flow of the algorithm.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for a challenging problem in computer vision.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the method's practical value.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.
* The method has the potential to be widely used in applications where obtaining fully labeled data is difficult.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work and a more in-depth analysis of the regularization term.
* The explanation of the spatial confidence map feature could be more detailed.
* The absence of error analysis limits the understanding of the method's limitations.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should expand the related work discussion, provide more details on the regularization term and spatial confidence map feature, and include some error analysis.
