**Summary**

The authors present a novel learning algorithm called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that addresses the problem of learning from weakly supervised data where only the "positiveness" of each training sample is available, rather than a binary label. The e-SVM algorithm is applied to segment proposal classification tasks and can be extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision, where only bounding box annotations are available. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the e-SVM algorithm on two major tasks in computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Originality**

While the e-SVM algorithm presents a new approach to handling weakly supervised data, its algorithmic novelty is limited. The weighted SVM is a well-known concept, and the authors do not cite pertinent previous work, such as [Suykens et al., 2002] and [Yang et al., 2005]. The "latent e-SVM" proposed by the authors is a weighted version of a transductive SVM, similar to [Vapnik, 1998]. However, the authors' contribution lies in applying these concepts to the specific problem of learning from weakly supervised data in computer vision.

**Significance**

The e-SVM algorithm's ability to outperform recent CNN results on object detection is a significant achievement and of great interest to the community. The algorithm's performance on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks demonstrates its potential for real-world applications.

**Quality**

The paper's quality is affected by the lack of crucial references to previous work. The object detection framework involves a significant amount of engineering, but the authors could provide more details on the feature engineering process. Additionally, the authors should report the results of using regularized logistic regression as a baseline to support the use of the sum-constrained weighted SVM. The choice of regularization coefficients λW and λR could also be better explained.

**Clarity**

The title and abstract do not accurately summarize the core contribution of the paper. The abstract implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, which is not true. The paper spends too much space on well-known algorithms and passes quickly over the feature engineering details. For a NIPS audience, the paper could benefit from a clearer and more concise presentation of the e-SVM algorithm and its contributions.
