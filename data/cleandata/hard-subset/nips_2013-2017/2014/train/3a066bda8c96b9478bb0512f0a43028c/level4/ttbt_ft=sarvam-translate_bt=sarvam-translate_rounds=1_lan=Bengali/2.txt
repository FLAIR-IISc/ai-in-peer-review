This paper proposes a modification of the SVM learning algorithm that works with weakly supervised data. Specifically, a counter-example weight (between 0 and 1) is assigned to each training example. Then, the SVM's loss function is modified to account for this weight. The method is then extended to a case where the counter-example weight is not observed (i.e., it is a latent variable).

The method is well-described, and experimental results indicate that using such a method may be more valuable than judging each example with equal weight for semantic segmentation and object recognition tasks. However, I do not see any difference between the proposed method and the existing "example-dependent cost," which is widely known and used in the machine learning community. For example, SVMlight provides such a process.

When the counter-example weight is not observed, the extension is interesting but simple. The problem in this case is non-convex, and no theoretical analysis is provided. In fact, a strong regularization term is used that indicates that the counter-example weights do not deviate too much from their initial values. Can the authors comment on this?

Small comment: On L050's claim that "it is not even a standard regression problem, because the positivity belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]." Logistic regression is very standard and limited to [0, 1].
