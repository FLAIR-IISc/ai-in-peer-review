

This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for problems where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" scores (ranging from 0 to 1) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a principled framework that can handle both observed positiveness (strong supervision) and unobserved positiveness (weak supervision) scenarios, with applications to semantic segmentation and object detection. The work addresses an important gap in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain, but bounding box annotations are readily available.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending the standard SVM framework to incorporate continuous confidence values rather than binary labels. This differs from traditional multiple instance learning (MIL) approaches, which assume at least one completely positive instance exists within a positive bag. The authors correctly note that MIL cannot handle their problem setting since most segment proposals contain both foreground and background pixels. The e-SVM model treats the label as a random variable with success probability determined by the positiveness score, leading to a novel expectation-based loss function. The latent extension of the model for weak supervision settings is particularly well-motivated and practical.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple computer vision tasks. For semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC 2011, the e-SVM achieves state-of-the-art performance with pixel-level annotations (36.8% accuracy) and competitive results with bounding box annotations (27.7%). More significantly, when applied to object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007, the method improves the RCNN baseline by 1.5% average precision (reaching 60.0% mAP), with particularly notable gains for challenging categories like "potted plant" (3.65% improvement). The experimental design is thorough, with the authors introducing two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically tailored for the segment classification task.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The e-SVM formulation is well-justified, and the latent model extension is logically derived. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and thorough ablation studies. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the performance could be further improved with additional data from ImageNet.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem setting is well-motivated, the methodology is clearly explained with appropriate mathematical notation, and the results are presented in a logical manner. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing the framework and Figure 2 describing the spatial confidence map feature) are helpful for understanding the approach. The writing is concise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning fundamentals.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the weakly supervised learning literature. While the authors note connections to weighted SVMs, they clearly distinguish their approach by focusing on label weighting rather than data point weighting. The core innovation lies in the expectation-based loss function that naturally handles continuous positiveness values. The paper thoroughly discusses related work, particularly highlighting how their approach differs from MIL methods and previous weakly supervised segmentation approaches. The authors correctly acknowledge that their work is a novel application of a modified SVM framework to a specific problem setting rather than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough, which is appropriate for the NIPS scope.

The significance of the work is evident from its practical applications and strong results. The authors demonstrate that their method can effectively leverage bounding box annotations (which are abundant in large-scale datasets like ImageNet) to reduce the need for expensive pixel-level annotations. This addresses a critical bottleneck in computer vision where manual annotation costs limit dataset scale. The 1.5% improvement on the challenging PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task represents a meaningful advance in the field, particularly given that the baseline (RCNN) was already state-of-the-art.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed in revision. The theoretical justification for setting the Bernoulli success probability equal to the positiveness score (µi = ui) is somewhat brief. While the authors note this is a simplification for the experiments, a more detailed discussion of when this assumption holds and potential alternatives would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Additionally, the paper could better articulate how the proposed method relates to other approaches that use continuous confidence values, such as those in the recent work on "confidence-aware" learning.

The authors have successfully demonstrated that their e-SVM framework provides a principled solution to the weakly supervised learning problem in computer vision. The method is general enough to be applied to various segmentation and detection tasks, and the experimental results clearly show its advantages over existing approaches. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, with clear theoretical grounding and compelling empirical validation.

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The e-SVM approach provides a novel and practical solution to a common problem, with clear advantages demonstrated across multiple tasks. The work is clearly written, well-executed, and addresses an important problem in the field. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
