This paper introduces an adaptation of the Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning. The core contribution is the assignment of a per-instance weight, valued between 0 and 1, to each training example, which is then incorporated into the SVM loss function. The authors further extend this framework to handle scenarios where these weights are unobserved and treated as latent variables.

The methodology is clearly articulated, and the empirical evaluation suggests its potential utility for tasks like semantic segmentation and object detection when compared to a uniform weighting scheme. Nevertheless, the novelty of this approach is questionable, as it appears functionally identical to the well-established concept of "example-dependent costs," a common feature in the machine learning literature and implemented in standard toolkits like SVMlight. The authors should clarify the distinction between their work and this existing practice.

The extension of the method to treat weights as latent variables is interesting, though the formulation is straightforward. This approach results in a non-convex optimization problem, for which the paper provides no theoretical analysis or guarantees. The reliance on a strong regularization term suggests a need to constrain the learned weights close to their initial values, likely to manage the non-convexity. We ask the authors to comment on the motivation for this design choice and the absence of a theoretical treatment.

Minor points:
*   The assertion on L050 that the problem is "not a standard regression problem" because the output is bounded in [0, 1] is incorrect. Logistic regression is a standard model that produces outputs in this exact interval.
*   There is a typographical error in the abstract; "continues" should be corrected to "continuous."
