This paper presents a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) that is designed to handle continuous attributes of positive instances in a learning problem. The authors apply this model to learn visual segmentation classifiers under weak supervision, where only the "positiveness" of each training sample is available, rather than a binary label. This is a common scenario in computer vision, where manually labeling large datasets is expensive and impractical.

The e-SVM algorithm is an extension of the classical SVC models, where the label of each segment is treated as an unobserved random variable. The authors assume that the label follows a Bernoulli distribution, and the probability of the label being positive is a function of the positiveness of the segment. The loss function of the e-SVM is the expectation over the random variables, which is equivalent to "weighting" each sample with a function of its positiveness.

The authors evaluate the performance of the e-SVM algorithm on the segment proposal classification task, using two new evaluation criteria: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). They compare the results to two common methods, SVC and SVR, and show that the e-SVM algorithm consistently outperforms them.

The authors also apply the e-SVM algorithm to two major tasks in computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection. For semantic segmentation, they use the PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset and achieve comparable results to the state-of-the-art segment confidence prediction model. For object detection, they combine the e-SVM algorithm with the state-of-the-art object detection system, Regions with CNN features (RCNN), and achieve a non-trivial improvement on detection performance.

The paper is well-motivated, and the presentation is clear and easy to follow. The experiments are interesting and well-executed, and the results are promising. The technical content is an extension of the standard multiple instance learning framework, and represents a valid contribution for NIPS.
