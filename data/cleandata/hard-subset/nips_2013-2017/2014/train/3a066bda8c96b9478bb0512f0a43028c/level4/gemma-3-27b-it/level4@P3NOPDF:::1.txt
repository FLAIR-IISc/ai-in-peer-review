## Review of Expectation Loss SVM for Visual Segmentation with Weak Supervision

This paper proposes a novel Expectation-SVM (e-SVM) designed to effectively handle continuous attributes of positive instances within a learning framework. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of this method by applying it to the challenging problem of learning visual segmentation classifiers under weak supervision, achieving promising results in both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.

The motivation for this work is strong. Learning from weakly supervised data is a crucial area in computer vision, and the ability to incorporate continuous feature information for positive instances – a limitation of many existing approaches – is a significant advantage. The paper clearly articulates this need and positions the e-SVM as a valuable contribution to addressing it.

The core idea of the e-SVM is a thoughtful extension of the standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework. By replacing the binary assignment typically used for positive instances with a soft weighting based on expectation, the model gains the flexibility to leverage continuous attributes. This is not merely a technical tweak; the authors convincingly demonstrate that this extension is applicable in both observed and latent settings, broadening the method's potential use cases. This adaptability represents a key strength.

The presentation of the methodology is exceptionally clear and well-motivated. The authors effectively explain the mathematical formulation of the e-SVM and its connection to existing MIL techniques. Figures and diagrams are utilized appropriately to enhance understanding. 

The experimental validation is compelling. The authors evaluate the e-SVM on established datasets for both semantic segmentation and object detection, comparing its performance to several relevant baselines. The reported results demonstrate consistent improvements over these baselines, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The choice of datasets is appropriate and the experimental setup appears rigorous. While the technical contribution builds upon existing MIL foundations, the authors have successfully extended the framework in a meaningful way, addressing a practical limitation and demonstrating its applicability to important visual learning problems. **Strengths:**
