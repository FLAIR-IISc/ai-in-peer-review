Summary:
The authors propose a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, they generate candidate segments, extract features for each, and regress the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, only bounding-box supervision is available; the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. They perform regression using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The results are on par with the state of the art for semantic labeling and the best currently reported on VOC 2007 for object detection.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and key prior work is not cited. In particular, weighted SVMs are well known (see [R1, R2, R4], none cited). The authors impose a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel (I did not conduct an exhaustive search). The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted transductive SVM (cf. [R3], also not cited).
[R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Surpassing recent CNN results with a more “conventional” learning approach is noteworthy and of broad interest.

Quality:
Crucial references to prior work are missing (see above). The object detection framework entails considerable engineering, but remains within the norm. For regression on the unit interval, (regularized) logistic regression is the standard baseline; those results should be reported to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should also detail how the regularization coefficients lambda_W and lambda_R were chosen. The paper’s main strength is its strong empirical performance.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not clearly convey the paper’s core contribution. The abstract also implies that regression targets in the unit interval are less informative than binary class labels, which is incorrect because the latter can be obtained by thresholding the former. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, while moving too quickly over the crucial feature-engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have raised my quality score by one, assuming the authors will address the relationship to prior work constructively and in depth. Excellent results, inadequate citations, limited algorithmic novelty. A paper that should certainly be published, possibly after some rewriting, and perhaps better suited to a computer vision venue.
