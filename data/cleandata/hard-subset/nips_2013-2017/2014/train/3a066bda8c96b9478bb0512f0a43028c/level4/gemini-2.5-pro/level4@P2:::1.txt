### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for classification problems where training examples are labeled with a continuous "positiveness" score (a value in [0, 1]) rather than a binary label. The core idea is to formulate the SVM loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the probability of the positive class is a function of the known positiveness score. The authors show this formulation is equivalent to a weighted SVM that can be solved with standard solvers.

Crucially, the authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available for a segmentation task). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and inferred through an alternating optimization procedure. The method is validated on the tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. Experiments demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines for segment proposal classification. When integrated with the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector, the proposed method achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. As datasets grow, the cost of obtaining dense, pixel-level annotations becomes prohibitive. Methods that can effectively leverage cheaper forms of annotation, such as bounding boxes, are of significant value to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The demonstrated ability to improve a powerful, state-of-the-art object detector underscores the practical impact of this work.

2.  **Methodological Clarity:** The proposed e-SVM model is elegant, well-motivated, and clearly explained. The connection to classical SVMs is made explicit, and the extension from the fully supervised to the weakly supervised (latent) case is logical and easy to follow. The paper provides a clear justification for its approach over more naive alternatives like thresholding for SVC or direct application of SVR.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation (Quality):** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors not only apply their method to challenging, large-scale vision tasks but also conduct a principled comparison for the core sub-problem of segment proposal classification. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for this specific task is a thoughtful contribution that allows for a more direct and fair comparison between models. The experiments convincingly show the superiority of e-SVM over SVC and SVR in both the fully and weakly supervised settings. Achieving a 1.5% average precision improvement over the very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant result.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Originality:** While the application is novel and the results are significant, the core technical formulation of the e-SVM can be seen as an incremental extension of instance-weighted SVMs. The main novelty lies in the problem framing—interpreting the weights as probabilities derived from a continuous "positiveness" label—and the successful application of this idea in a latent variable model for large-scale vision tasks. This is a valid and useful contribution, but the underlying machine learning machinery is not fundamentally new.

2.  **Discussion of Model Choices:** The paper makes several simplifying assumptions that prove effective, but a deeper discussion would strengthen the work. For example, the probability `μi` is set directly to the positiveness score `ui` (i.e., `g(ui) = ui`). It would be interesting to know if the authors experimented with other monotonic functions and how sensitive the results are to this choice. Similarly, for the latent e-SVM, the KL-divergence regularizer `R(u)` is introduced to prevent `u` from collapsing to binary values. A brief discussion of alternative regularizers (e.g., a simple L2 penalty on `u - ubb`) and their potential effects would be valuable.

3.  **Optimization Details:** The paper presents an alternating optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) for the latent e-SVM. For reproducibility and a more complete understanding, it would be helpful to include more details about its practical behavior. For instance, how sensitive is the algorithm to initialization? How many iterations does it typically take to converge?

**Summary of Arguments for and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in machine learning and computer vision: learning with weak supervision.
*   The proposed e-SVM model is intuitive, well-formulated, and effectively bridges the gap between binary classification and regression for labels in a bounded interval.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over relevant baselines on standard, challenging benchmarks.
*   The work achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task, highlighting its real-world utility.
*   The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The core technical novelty of the e-SVM model itself is somewhat limited, as it is closely related to existing weighted SVM formulations.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid and useful contribution. The authors propose a simple yet effective solution to an important problem. The motivation is clear, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results are strong and significant. Despite the incremental nature of the core model, its novel application and demonstrated impact on challenging vision tasks make it a valuable addition to the field. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, and I recommend its acceptance.
