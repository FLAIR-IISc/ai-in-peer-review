The paper proposes a modification to the SVM learning algorithm that deals with weakly supervised data. Specifically, a per-example weight (between 0 and 1) is assigned to each training example, and the loss function of the SVM is modified to incorporate this weight. The method is then extended to the case when the per-example weight is not observed, i.e., is a latent variable.

The method is well described, and the experimental results indicate that, for the semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, using such an approach may be beneficial over treating each example with equal weight. However, I fail to see the distinction between the proposed method and existing "example-dependent costs," which are widely known and used in the machine learning community. SVMlight, for example, provides such a mechanism.

The extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is interesting but straightforward. In this case, the problem becomes non-convex, and no theoretical analysis is provided. Notably, a strong regularization term is used, suggesting that the per-example weights do not deviate far from their initial values. I would appreciate it if the authors could comment on this.

Minor comment: The claim on L050 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is not accurate. Logistic regression is a standard approach, and it is indeed bounded to [0, 1].

Additionally, there is a minor spelling mistake in the abstract: "continues" should be replaced with "continuous".

Overall, the paper proposes a modified SVM learning algorithm in which the loss function is modified by a per-example weight. However, example-dependent costs are already widely used in machine learning, which raises questions about the novelty of the proposed approach.
