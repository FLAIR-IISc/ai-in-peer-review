The authors propose an expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) capable of handling continuous attributes of positive instances in a learning problem, and apply this model to learn visual segmentation classifiers under weak supervision. The methodology is validated for both semantic segmentation and object detection, achieving promising results. This paper addresses an important problem in visual learning – learning with weak supervision – and the presentation is clear, well-motivated, and the experiments are both interesting and well-executed. While the technical content extends the standard multiple instance learning (MIL) framework by replacing a binary weighting of positives with a soft weighting, its applicability to both observed and latent settings represents a valid contribution for NIPS. 

Specifically, the e-SVM allows for a more nuanced representation of positive instances than traditional MIL approaches, which typically treat all positive examples as equally representative. This is particularly beneficial when dealing with continuous-valued features where the degree to which an instance embodies the positive class may vary. The authors effectively leverage this capability to improve performance in weakly supervised visual segmentation.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on challenging semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. The results are competitive with, and in some cases surpass, existing methods designed for weak supervision. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation, including a clear comparison to relevant baselines. 

However, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the e-SVM, especially concerning the expectation calculation, would be valuable. While the paper reports reasonable running times, a theoretical analysis of the scaling behavior with respect to data size and feature dimensionality would strengthen the work. Furthermore, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of kernel and expectation estimation techniques could provide further insights. 

Despite these minor points, the paper presents a significant advance in weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM offers a principled and effective way to incorporate continuous attribute information into the MIL framework. The strong empirical results, combined with the clear presentation, make this a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
