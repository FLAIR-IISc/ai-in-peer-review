The authors present a novel learning algorithm, called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to handle situations where training data labels are continuous values within a bounded interval. The proposed e-SVM model is applied to segment proposal classification tasks and can be extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision, where only bounding box annotations are available. The authors evaluate their method on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The e-SVM model is related to previous work on weakly supervised learning and weighted SVMs. However, the authors do not provide a comprehensive review of pertinent previous work, including the weighted SVM, which is well-known in the literature. The "latent e-SVM" proposed by the authors is a weighted version of a transductive SVM. The authors should provide more details on the connections to previous work and how their approach differs from existing methods.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to outperform recent CNN results using a "conventional" learning approach. The object detection framework involves a significant amount of engineering, but the authors should provide more details on how they selected the regularization coefficients. The main strength of the paper is its good empirical results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the e-SVM model.

The quality of the paper is affected by the lack of crucial references to previous work. The authors should provide a more comprehensive review of the literature and compare their approach to existing methods. Additionally, the authors should report the results of a baseline method, such as regularized logistic regression, to support the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM.

The clarity of the paper is good, but the title and abstract do not accurately summarize the core contribution of the paper. The abstract insinuates that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, which is not true. The paper spends too much space on well-known algorithms and passes quickly over the feature engineering details.

However, it lacks crucial references to previous work and could benefit from a more comprehensive review of the literature. With some rewriting and additional details, the paper could be suitable for publication, possibly at a computer vision conference.
