This paper proposes a modification to SVM training for weakly supervised data by assigning each example a weight in [0, 1] and incorporating these weights directly into the SVM loss. The approach is further extended to the setting where the per-example weights are latent (unobserved).

Strengths:
- The method is clearly described.
- Experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest potential benefits over uniform weighting, indicating that example-specific weighting can be valuable in practice.

Major concerns:
- Novelty relative to existing work is unclear. The proposed approach appears closely related to example-dependent (or cost-sensitive) SVM formulations that are well known and widely used. For instance, SVMlight and other toolkits already support per-example costs or weights within the loss. Please clarify the substantive differences—conceptual, algorithmic, or theoretical—between your method and standard example-dependent cost formulations. A direct empirical comparison to such baselines would also help establish the contribution.
- The extension with unobserved per-example weights is interesting but seems straightforward. In this formulation, the optimization becomes non-convex, yet no theoretical analysis (e.g., guarantees on convergence, identifiability, or conditions under which the procedure improves over fixed weights) is provided. Moreover, the strong regularization on the weights appears designed to keep them close to their initial values, which raises concerns about how much effective flexibility the latent weights actually provide. Please comment on:
  - Why such strong regularization is necessary and how its strength is selected.
  - Sensitivity to initialization and regularization strength.
  - Whether the method meaningfully departs from a fixed-weight baseline under realistic settings.
  - Any convergence properties or practical diagnostics you can offer.

Questions for the authors:
- In what precise way does your loss or training procedure differ from assigning per-example costs in standard SVM implementations? Are there constraints, parameterizations, or update rules that are novel beyond existing cost-sensitive SVMs?
- How does your method compare empirically to off-the-shelf per-example weighting (e.g., SVMlight with instance-specific costs) on the same tasks and datasets?
- Can you provide ablations isolating the effect of the latent-weight component versus fixed weights, and quantify the benefits beyond initialization?

Minor comments:
- Line L050: The statement that “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]” is inaccurate. Logistic regression is a standard model whose predicted probabilities lie in [0, 1].
- Abstract: Replace “continues” with “continuous.”

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a clear presentation and promising empirical results indicating that per-example weighting can be beneficial for weakly supervised tasks. However, the contribution appears very close to established example-dependent cost formulations already prevalent in the literature and supported by standard tools. The latent-weight extension, while potentially useful, lacks theoretical backing and seems heavily regularized toward initial values. Clarifying the novelty, providing stronger empirical comparisons against standard cost-sensitive SVMs, and offering analysis or diagnostics for the non-convex extension would substantially strengthen the paper.
