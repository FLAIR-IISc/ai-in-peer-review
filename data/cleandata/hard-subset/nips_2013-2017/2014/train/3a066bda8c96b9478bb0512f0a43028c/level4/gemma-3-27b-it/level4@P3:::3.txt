The authors present a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for problems with weakly supervised data where only a confidence value representing “positiveness” towards a binary label is available. The method extends the standard SVM framework to handle continuous target values and addresses both supervised and weakly supervised scenarios. The approach is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

This paper tackles the important problem of learning from weakly supervised data, a crucial area for scaling machine learning to large datasets. The core idea of adapting the SVM framework to directly handle continuous confidence values is reasonable and addresses a limitation of standard binary classification and regression approaches. The experimental results, particularly the strong performance in object detection, are noteworthy. However, the paper lacks sufficient engagement with relevant prior work, presenting the method as more novel than it is. Several related techniques, particularly in weighted SVMs and transductive learning, are not adequately acknowledged or discussed.

**Originality:** The algorithmic novelty is incremental. While the proposed e-SVM formulation is a reasonable extension, it builds heavily on established concepts. The authors do not cite key prior work in weighted SVMs (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014) or transductive SVMs (e.g., Vapnik, 1998), which are closely related. The latent e-SVM, used in the weakly supervised setting, is effectively a weighted transductive SVM. The addition of a sum constraint on the weights may represent a minor contribution, but its impact requires more thorough analysis and comparison to existing techniques. A more comprehensive literature review is essential to accurately position the work within the field.

**Significance:** The reported performance improvements, especially in object detection, are significant. Outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a more conventional approach is a valuable contribution, suggesting the potential for competitive performance without the computational demands of deep learning. The method’s applicability to scenarios with varying levels of supervision – from fully annotated data to weakly labeled datasets – enhances its practicality and potential impact.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach, with a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined experimental setup. However, the lack of citations to related work undermines the claim of novelty and makes it difficult to assess the true contribution. The choice of a weighted SVM framework, while reasonable, should be justified by a comparison to alternative regression methods like regularized logistic regression, which is a common baseline for predicting values in the unit interval. Furthermore, the paper lacks detail regarding the selection of regularization parameters (λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>), which are crucial for the performance of SVMs. The experimental results are strong, but a more rigorous ablation study could further validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the title and abstract do not accurately reflect the core contribution. The abstract’s implication that continuous confidence values are less informative than binary labels is misleading. The introduction spends considerable space describing variations of well-known algorithms while glossing over the essential details of feature engineering. More emphasis should be placed on the specifics of the image features used and how they are integrated with the e-SVM framework. The notation and mathematical derivations are clear and easy to follow.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results, particularly achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance.
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning – learning from weakly supervised data.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-defined method.
*   Demonstrates the applicability of the approach to multiple computer vision tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient engagement with related work, particularly in weighted SVMs and transductive learning.
*   Incremental novelty – the method builds heavily on existing techniques.
*   Lack of a comparison to standard regression baselines (e.g., regularized logistic regression).
*   Limited discussion of hyperparameter selection.
*   The title and abstract are not fully representative of the paper's contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution with promising results. However, the lack of thorough comparison to related work and the limited discussion of key implementation details are significant weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  **Expand the related work section:** Thoroughly discuss and cite relevant papers on weighted SVMs, transductive learning, and weakly supervised learning.
2.  **Compare to regression baselines:** Include a comparison to regularized logistic regression as a baseline for the regression task.
3.  **Provide details on hyperparameter selection:** Explain how the regularization parameters (λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>) were chosen and consider performing a sensitivity analysis.
4.  **Revise the title and abstract:** Ensure they accurately reflect the core contribution of the paper.
5.  **Clarify feature engineering:** Provide more details on the image features used and how they are integrated into the e-SVM framework.

Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact within the research community.
