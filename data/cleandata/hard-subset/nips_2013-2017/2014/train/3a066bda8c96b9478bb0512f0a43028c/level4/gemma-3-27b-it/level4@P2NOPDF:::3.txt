## Review: Semantic Labeling and Object Detection via Regression

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the approach generates candidate segments, extracts features from them, and then employs regression to predict the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between each candidate and the corresponding pixel-accurate ground truth. In the object detection task, weaker bounding box labels are used, treating the precise spatial extent of the object as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM (“latent e-SVM”) for object detection. The results achieve state-of-the-art performance on object detection on VOC 2007 and are competitive with current methods for semantic labeling.

**Originality:**

The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited. A significant concern is the omission of citations to relevant prior work. For instance, weighted SVMs (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]) are not acknowledged, despite being well-established techniques. While the authors’ imposition of a sum constraint on the weights may be a novel contribution (though not exhaustively investigated by this reviewer), the proposed “latent e-SVM” appears to be a weighted variant of existing transductive SVM approaches (e.g., [R3]). 

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Significance:**

Achieving performance that surpasses recent Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) results using a more conventional learning approach is a noteworthy accomplishment and should be of considerable interest to the community.

**Quality:**

The paper suffers from crucial omissions of references to relevant prior work, as highlighted above. The object detection framework appears well-engineered, though not exceptionally so. The authors employ a sum-constrained weighted SVM for regression on the unit interval, but a comparison to (regularized) logistic regression as a baseline would better justify this design choice. Further clarification is needed regarding the selection of regularization coefficients, λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>. The primary strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not effectively convey the core contributions of the paper. The abstract’s suggestion that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships is inaccurate, as the latter can be trivially derived from the former through thresholding. Given the NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space to minor variations of known algorithms (up to page 4) while providing insufficient detail regarding the crucial aspect of feature engineering (lines 254ff). 

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 19: “continues” should be “continuous”
*   Line 94: “boxes” should be “box”
*   Line 98: “AP” is used without prior definition.
*   Line 350: “SVM” should be “SVC”
*   Line 350: “with every different values” should be “for all threshold values”

**Overall Assessment:**

I have slightly increased my quality score, anticipating that the authors will proactively address the missing references and provide a comprehensive discussion of related work. The paper presents strong results but is hampered by a lack of novelty and insufficient acknowledgement of existing literature. While suitable for publication, particularly with revisions, this work might also be well-received at a computer vision conference.
