**Summary:**

The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. The first is accomplished by creating potential segments and extracting features for them; and then a regression is performed based on the intersection-over-union overlap ratio between a potential segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. In the case of object detection, only weak labels are obtained in terms of the bounding box, and specific spatial extent is used as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted adaptive SVM. The results are equivalent to state-of-the-art methods in semantic labeling, and are currently the best known for object detection in VOC 2007.

**Basicness:**

The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors unfortunately do not mention relevant previous work. In particular, weighted SVMs are well-known (e.g., [R1,R2,R4] below, not cited). The authors impose a sum constraint on the weights, which is probably new (I have not searched the extensive literature here). The "latent e-SVM" that the authors propose is a weighted version of an adaptive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

*   [R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
*   [R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
*   [R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Importance:**

Exceeding recent CNN results through "conventional" learning methods is an achievement and a matter of great interest to the community.

**Quality:**

Important references to previous work are absent (see above). Several engineering aspects are involved in the object detection framework, but that is not out of the ordinary. The default method for regression in unit intervals is (regularized) logistic regression, so these results should be reported as a baseline in support of using sum-constrained weighted SVMs. The authors should provide more detailed information on how they selected the regularization coefficients \lambdaW and \lambdaR. The main strength of this paper is good empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not adequately summarize the main contribution of the paper. The abstract also suggests that regression targets from unit intervals are somehow less informative than only binary class membership. This is not true, because the latter can always be obtained from the former through thresholding. For NIPS readers, the paper spends quite a bit of space on minor variations of well-known algorithms (up to page 4) and goes a bit fast on detailed descriptions of important feature engineering (starting from line 254).
