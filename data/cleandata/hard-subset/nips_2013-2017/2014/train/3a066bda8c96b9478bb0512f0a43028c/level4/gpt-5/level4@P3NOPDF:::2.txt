Summary
The paper proposes an SVM variant that incorporates per-example weights (in [0,1]) into the loss, with an extension where these weights are treated as latent variables. The approach is motivated by weak supervision scenarios where examples may have varying reliability. The method is clearly described, and experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest potential benefits relative to uniform weighting.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and straightforward integration of instance weights into an SVM objective.
- Practical motivation: weak supervision and heterogeneous label quality are common in vision tasks.
- Experimental results on two relevant benchmarks indicate that instance weighting can improve performance.
- The latent-weight extension is pragmatic and likely useful in settings where reliabilities are not given.

Concerns and novelty
- The core idea appears to duplicate well-known instance-dependent cost/weighting schemes. Cost-sensitive learning and per-example weighting are long-established (e.g., instance weights in SVM toolkits such as SVMlight). The paper should clearly differentiate its contribution from standard sample weighting in SVMs:
  - Is the loss modification any different from assigning per-example slack penalties or importance weights?
  - If the distinction is only in using continuous weights in [0,1], this still aligns with standard instance weighting.
  - Please position the work against classical cost-sensitive SVMs, importance weighting, and robust SVM variants that reweight or downweight noisy examples.
- The latent-weight extension, while useful, seems conceptually straightforward: alternate between updating weights and the classifier. This leads to a non-convex objective without theoretical guarantees. The paper currently provides no analysis (e.g., stationary point guarantees, convergence of alternating optimization, or generalization bounds).
- A strong regularization term keeps latent weights near their initialization, which suggests limited flexibility. This raises questions about whether the gains are due to careful initialization rather than meaningful learning of reliabilities.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please provide a precise statement of how your weighted loss differs from standard per-example weighting/cost-sensitive SVMs. If equivalent, the contribution should emphasize empirical insights or specific algorithmic/practical innovations (e.g., how weights are estimated, regularized, or constrained).
- For the latent-weight model:
  - What optimization scheme is used (e.g., alternating minimization, EM-like updates)? What stopping criteria?
  - Are there any convergence guarantees (even to a local minimum/stationary point)?
  - How sensitive are results to the initialization of weights and to the strength/form of the regularizer that keeps weights near their initial values?
  - Do learned weights deviate meaningfully from initialization in practice? Please report distributions of learned weights and correlations with proxy measures of label quality.
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Include baselines using standard per-example weights (e.g., from confidence scores), as implemented in existing SVM packages, to directly compare against your formulation.
  - Compare to simple heuristics (e.g., clipping/extreme downweighting of low-confidence examples) to show the added value of your objective and regularization.
  - Provide ablations on the regularization of weights (strength, form), and on constraints (e.g., [0,1] vs. unbounded positive weights).
- Relation to weak supervision literature:
  - How does this approach compare to Multiple Instance Learning and other weakly supervised methods that model instance reliability? A brief empirical or conceptual comparison would help clarify the niche your method fills.
- Practical aspects:
  - Report training time and scalability relative to a standard SVM.
  - Clarify how hyperparameters (regularization on w and on the weights) were selected and whether validation uses any additional supervision.

Clarity and presentation
- The method is generally well explained. However, the connection to existing instance-weighted learning needs a dedicated related-work discussion. Without it, the novelty and positioning are unclear.
- Please provide a compact mathematical formulation that makes equivalence (or difference) to standard instance weighting explicit (e.g., rewriting the loss with per-example slack penalties).

Minor comments
- Line 50 claim: “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0,1].” Logistic regression is a standard approach producing outputs in [0,1]; please revise this statement or clarify what is meant (e.g., constraints on latent reliabilities vs. probabilistic outputs).
- Abstract: “continues” → “continuous.”
- Please standardize terminology: “weight,” “cost,” and “reliability” are often used interchangeably; define each precisely to avoid confusion.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important practical issue and presents promising results. However, the core modeling idea appears very close to established sample-weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs, and the latent-weight extension, while useful, is a straightforward non-convex augmentation without theoretical support. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should (1) explicitly position the method with respect to standard instance weighting and demonstrate what is novel; (2) provide stronger empirical comparisons and ablations, particularly against off-the-shelf per-example weighted SVMs; (3) analyze or at least thoroughly assess the optimization behavior and sensitivity of the latent-weight variant. With these clarifications and additional experiments, the paper would make a clearer and more compelling case for its contributions.
