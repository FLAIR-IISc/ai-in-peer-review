The authors present a learning framework for semantic labeling and object detection that leverages weakly supervised data. The approach generates candidate segments and extracts features, then regresses on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. When only bounding box annotations are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a latent variable extension, respectively. The method achieves competitive results in semantic labeling and surpasses state-of-the-art performance in object detection on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

This work builds upon existing techniques in weakly supervised learning and SVMs. While the core idea of weighting samples based on their “positiveness” isn't entirely novel, the specific formulation and application to segment proposal confidence prediction are valuable contributions. However, the paper lacks sufficient acknowledgment of closely related prior work. For example, weighted SVMs are a well-established technique [Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014], and the latent variable approach bears resemblance to transductive SVMs [Vapnik, 1998] – these are not cited. The imposition of a sum constraint on the weights may represent a novel aspect, although a more thorough literature review is needed to confirm this.

The significance of this work lies in its ability to achieve strong performance, even outperforming recent CNN-based approaches in object detection, using a more traditional machine learning framework. This is a noteworthy accomplishment and potentially offers a computationally more efficient alternative for certain applications. The demonstration of effectiveness in both semantic segmentation and object detection showcases the versatility of the proposed method.

The technical quality of the paper is generally sound. The experimental setup appears well-designed, and the results are presented clearly. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to baseline regression methods specifically designed for the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression. Providing details on the selection of regularization coefficients (λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>) would strengthen the reproducibility of the results. Crucially, the lack of citations to relevant prior work is a significant weakness. The object detection framework incorporates standard engineering practices, but a more detailed explanation of the feature engineering process (lines 254ff) would be beneficial.

The paper’s clarity suffers from a somewhat misleading title and abstract, which don’t adequately highlight the core contribution. The abstract’s claim that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary labels is inaccurate, as binary labels are simply a thresholded version of the former. The paper dedicates excessive space to incremental variations of established algorithms (up to page 4), while quickly glossing over crucial details of feature engineering. Minor textual issues include "continues" instead of "continuous" (line 19), "boxes" instead of "box" (line 94), and an undefined abbreviation “AP” (line 98). “SVM” is inconsistently used and at times should be “SVC” (line 350). The phrasing "with every different values" should be "for all threshold values" (line 350).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness in both semantic segmentation and object detection.
*   The method addresses a practical problem: learning from weakly supervised data.
*   Potential for computational efficiency compared to deep learning approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Significant omission of relevant prior work, particularly regarding weighted SVMs and transductive learning.
*   Limited algorithmic novelty; the core idea builds heavily on existing techniques.
*   Lack of comparison to appropriate baseline regression methods.
*   Clarity issues in the title, abstract, and overall presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, particularly for segment-based prediction tasks. The strong empirical results are compelling, but the lack of proper citation and discussion of related work, and the limited algorithmic novelty, are significant concerns. I believe the paper is worthy of publication, contingent on the authors addressing the cited weaknesses through a substantial revision that thoroughly contextualizes their work within the existing literature. A computer vision-focused conference might be a more suitable venue if these revisions are not possible.
