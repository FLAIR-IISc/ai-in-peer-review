Summary:
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is accomplished by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio between each candidate segment and its pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker bounding box labels are available, the precise spatial extent of the object is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. Results achieve state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and the best currently known results for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors unfortunately do not cite relevant prior work. Specifically, the weighted SVM approach is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4] below, which are not cited). While the authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel (though I haven’t conducted an exhaustive literature search), the “latent e-SVM” they propose is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], also not cited). 
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Outperforming recent Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) results with a “conventional” learning approach is a significant achievement and of considerable interest to the community.

Quality:
Crucially, several references to prior work are missing (see above). While the object detection framework requires considerable engineering effort, this is not beyond the typical scope of the field. Regularized logistic regression is the standard method for regression on the unit interval, and these results should be included as a baseline to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide more details regarding their selection of the regularization coefficients, λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>. The paper’s main strength lies in its strong empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the paper’s core contribution. The abstract also suggests that regression targets from the unit interval are somehow less informative than binary class memberships, which is incorrect, as the latter can always be obtained from the former by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates too much space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, while covering the crucial feature engineering details (lines 254ff) rather briefly.

Overall Assessment:
I have increased my quality score by one point, anticipating that the authors will address the connections to prior work constructively and provide extensive commentary on it. The paper presents excellent results, but suffers from deficient referencing and limited algorithmic novelty. It is a paper that should certainly be published, potentially after some revisions, and may be particularly well-suited for a computer vision conference.
