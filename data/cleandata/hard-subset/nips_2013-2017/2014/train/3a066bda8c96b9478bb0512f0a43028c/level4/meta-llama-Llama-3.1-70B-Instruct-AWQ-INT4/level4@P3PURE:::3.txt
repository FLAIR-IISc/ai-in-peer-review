Summary:
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. This is achieved by generating candidate segments and extracting features for these, followed by regression on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. In the case of object detection, only weaker labels in terms of bounding boxes are available, and the precise spatial extent is used as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The results are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and represent the best currently known for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
While the authors' approach yields impressive results, the algorithmic novelty is limited. Unfortunately, they fail to cite pertinent previous work, which is essential for contextualizing their contributions. Specifically, the weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1,R2,R4] below, not cited). The authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel, although a thorough literature search would be required to confirm this. The proposed "latent e-SVM" is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
The fact that the authors' "conventional" learning approach outperforms recent CNN results is a significant achievement and of great interest to the community.

Quality:
A major concern is the omission of crucial references to previous work (see above). While the object detection framework involves a substantial amount of engineering, it is not unusually complex. Notably, the default method for regression on the unit interval is (regularized) logistic regression, which should be reported as a baseline to support the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide more details on how they selected the regularization coefficients λW and λR. The primary strength of the paper lies in its good empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not adequately summarize the core contribution of the paper. Furthermore, the abstract implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, which is not accurate, as the latter can always be obtained from the former by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, while glossing over the essential feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

Minor comments:

Line 98: The abbreviation "AP" is not introduced.
Line 350: "SVM" should be "SVC", and "with every different values" should be "for all threshold values".

I have increased my quality score by one, assuming the authors will not be defensive about the relation to previous work, but rather provide an extensive commentary on it and connections to it. The paper presents great results, but is deficient in references to earlier work and lacks significant algorithmic novelty. It is a paper that should be published, possibly after some rewriting, and possibly more suited to a computer vision conference.
