Review- Summary:
The paper introduces a pipeline for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, it generates candidate segments, extracts features on these regions, and then regresses to the Intersection-over-Union between a candidate and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, only bounding-box supervision is used, with the exact object extent treated as a latent variable. Regression is carried out with a weighted SVM for labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for detection. The semantic labeling results match the state of the art, and the detection results are currently the strongest on VOC 2007.

Originality:
The algorithmic contributions are modest, and key prior art is not cited. In particular, weighted SVMs are established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not referenced). The imposed sum constraint on weights might be new (I did not perform an exhaustive search). The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted transductive SVM (cf. [R3], also not cited).
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Beating recent CNN baselines with a more “traditional” learning setup is impressive and of significant interest to the community.

Quality:
Important references are missing (see above). The detection system involves substantial engineering, but nothing unusual for the area. Since the natural baseline for regression on [0,1] is (regularized) logistic regression, those results should be reported to justify the sum-constrained weighted SVM. Please also detail how the regularization parameters λW and λR were selected. The paper’s main strength lies in its strong empirical performance.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not clearly convey the central contribution. The abstract further suggests that targets on the unit interval are less informative than binary labels, which is incorrect—binary labels can always be obtained by thresholding the continuous targets. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (through page 4) to minor tweaks of well-known methods, while skimming over the crucial feature-engineering aspects (lines 254ff).

I have increased my quality score by one, contingent on the authors engaging constructively with prior work and clearly articulating connections. Excellent results, weak referencing, limited novelty. This should be published after some revision, potentially more suited to a computer vision venue.
