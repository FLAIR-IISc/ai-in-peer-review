The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), that deals with weakly supervised data. Specifically, a per-example weight (between 0 and 1) is assigned to each training example, which represents the "positiveness" of the example belonging to the positive class. The method is then extended to the case when the per-example weight is not observed (i.e., is a latent variable).

The authors provide a clear description of the method and demonstrate its effectiveness on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. The experimental results indicate that the proposed approach can outperform existing methods, such as Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR), in certain scenarios.

However, the reviewer questions the novelty of the proposed method, as example-dependent costs are already widely used in the machine learning community. The authors should clarify how their approach differs from existing methods, such as those implemented in SVMlight.

The extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is an interesting aspect of the paper. However, the problem becomes non-convex, and the authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the method's behavior. The use of a strong regularization term suggests that the per-example weights do not deviate far from their initial values. The authors should comment on this aspect and provide more insights into the method's behavior.

Minor comments include the correction of a spelling mistake in the abstract ("continues" -> "continuous") and the clarification of the statement on line 50, which claims that the problem is not a standard regression problem due to the bounded interval [0, 1]. Logistic regression is a standard approach that deals with bounded intervals.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm that deals with weakly supervised data.
* The authors provide a clear description of the method and demonstrate its effectiveness on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.
* The experimental results indicate that the proposed approach can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios.

Weaknesses:

* The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as example-dependent costs are already widely used in the machine learning community.
* The extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is non-convex, and the authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the method's behavior.
* Minor errors and clarifications are needed.
