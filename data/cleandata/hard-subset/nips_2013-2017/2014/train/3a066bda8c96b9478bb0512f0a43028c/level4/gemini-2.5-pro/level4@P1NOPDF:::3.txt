### Summary

The authors present a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features for them, and then performs regression on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between each candidate and the pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker bounding box labels are available, the model treats the precise spatial extent of the object as a latent variable. The regression tasks are handled using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The reported results are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and establish a new state-of-the-art performance for object detection on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### Originality

The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited. A significant concern is the lack of citation to relevant prior work. For instance, the weighted SVM is a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]), yet these foundational papers are not cited. The proposed "latent e-SVM" appears to be a weighted variant of a transductive SVM, a concept introduced in earlier work [R3] that also goes unreferenced. The authors do introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be a novel contribution, although this is not thoroughly contextualized within the existing literature.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

### Significance

The paper's primary significance lies in its impressive empirical results. Achieving performance that surpasses recent deep learning-based methods using a more classical machine learning framework is a notable accomplishment and will be of considerable interest to the community.

### Quality

The quality of the paper is diminished by its failure to cite key prior work, as noted above. While the object detection framework involves significant feature engineering, this level of complexity is not unusual for methods in this area.

The experimental evaluation could be strengthened. To justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM for regression on the unit interval, the authors should report results for a standard baseline, such as regularized logistic regression. Furthermore, the paper lacks important implementation details, specifically regarding how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were selected. The main strength of the paper remains its strong empirical results.

### Clarity

The paper's clarity could be improved. The title and abstract do not effectively convey the paper's central contributions. The abstract also makes a questionable claim, suggesting that regression targets on the unit interval are less informative than binary labels. This is incorrect, as binary labels can be derived from continuous values via thresholding.

The paper's exposition seems unbalanced for a NIPS audience. It dedicates excessive space (through page 4) to describing minor variations of well-known algorithms, while the crucial details of the feature engineering pipeline (lines 254ff) are covered too briefly.
