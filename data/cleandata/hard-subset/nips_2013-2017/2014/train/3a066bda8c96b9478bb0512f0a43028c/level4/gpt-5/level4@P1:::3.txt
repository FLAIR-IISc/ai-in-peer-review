Summary:
The paper presents a framework for semantic segmentation and object detection built around an “expectation loss SVM” (e-SVM). For segmentation, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features for each, and learns to predict the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap with pixel-accurate ground truth. For detection, where only bounding-box labels are available, the precise segment-level “positiveness” is treated as a latent variable. The approach is instantiated as a weighted SVM for the fully supervised case and as a weighted, latent (transductive-style) SVM when only bounding boxes are provided. Empirically, results are competitive with the state of the art for semantic segmentation and achieve the best reported performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 detection when combined with RCNN.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty appears limited, and key prior work is not cited. Weighted SVMs are well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]; not cited). The paper imposes a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel, though I did not perform an exhaustive literature search. The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted variant of a transductive SVM (cf. [R3]; not cited). The authors should clearly position their model relative to these lines of work and discuss similarities/differences, including the distinction they draw between weighting labels and weighting examples.

[R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
The empirical results are strong. Improving over recent CNN-based detectors with a relatively simple, “conventional” addition is noteworthy and of broad interest. The framework’s ability to leverage weak supervision is practical given the scarcity of pixel-level labels.

Quality:
- Missing references to foundational and closely related work (see above) need to be addressed.
- The detection pipeline entails substantial engineering, but remains within the norm for modern systems and is clearly useful.
- For targets in [0, 1], (regularized) logistic regression is a natural baseline. Including logistic regression baselines (and discussing calibration) would strengthen the case for the proposed sum-constrained weighted SVM objective.
- Please provide details on hyperparameter selection, particularly how λW and λR are chosen (e.g., cross-validation protocol, search ranges, sensitivity).
- The main strength of the paper is the quality of the empirical results; the methodology would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to established alternatives and ablations (e.g., effect of the KL regularizer, initialization ubb, and convergence behavior).

Clarity:
- The title and abstract do not succinctly convey the core contribution. Consider emphasizing: (i) learning with continuous “positiveness” labels; (ii) a latent extension for weak supervision; and (iii) integration with a state-of-the-art detector yielding consistent gains.
- The abstract suggests that continuous labels in [0, 1] are somehow less informative than binary labels. This is not accurate: binary labels can be derived by thresholding continuous scores, whereas the reverse is not true; please rephrase.
- For a NIPS audience, the paper spends too much space (up to page 4) on minor variations of well-known formulations, while important feature-engineering and integration details (lines 254ff) are treated briefly. Consider tightening the methodological exposition and allocating more space to design choices, ablations, and error analysis.
- The description of the relationship to instance-weighted SVMs and to latent/transductive SVMs should be made explicit in the main text, with appropriate citations.

Minor comments:

- Line 98: Introduce “AP” (Average Precision) before use

Recommendation:
I have increased my quality score assuming the authors will address the relationship to prior work comprehensively and revise the presentation accordingly. Strong empirical results, but with insufficient references and limited algorithmic novelty. I recommend publication after revision; the work may also be a good fit for a computer vision venue.
