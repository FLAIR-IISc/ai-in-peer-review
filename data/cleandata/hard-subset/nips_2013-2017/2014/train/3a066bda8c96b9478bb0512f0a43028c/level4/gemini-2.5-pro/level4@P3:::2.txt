This review evaluates the paper "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm".

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for binary classification problems where training labels are not discrete {-1, +1} values, but rather continuous "positiveness" scores within the interval [0, 1]. This scenario is common in computer vision, where an object proposal's quality is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth annotation.

The core idea is to reframe the standard SVM hinge loss. For each training example, the true binary label is treated as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of the label being positive is given by its "positiveness" score. The e-SVM objective function then minimizes the *expectation* of the hinge loss with respect to this distribution. This formulation results in each training example contributing weighted loss terms for both the positive and negative classes.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM parameters using an iterative, alternating procedure.

The proposed method is evaluated extensively on computer vision tasks. For segment proposal classification, e-SVM is shown to outperform standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines using newly proposed evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The method is then applied to semantic segmentation and object detection, where it demonstrates strong performance, notably achieving a state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark by improving upon the powerful R-CNN framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Relevance and Significance:** The problem of learning from continuous-valued or "soft" labels is highly practical and important. In many domains, particularly computer vision, obtaining precise binary labels is expensive and often impractical, whereas measures of "correctness" like IoU are naturally available. This work directly addresses a significant challenge in leveraging such weakly supervised data.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The e-SVM demonstrates clear improvements over sensible baselines (SVC with various thresholds, SVR) on the segment classification task. More importantly, the application to object detection shows a non-trivial improvement (+1.5 AP) over the state-of-the-art R-CNN method on a standard benchmark. This demonstrates the practical value and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The formulation of the expected loss is elegant and well-motivated. The extension to the latent variable case is a logical and powerful addition, allowing the method to be applied in settings with even weaker supervision. The introduction of specific evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment-wise prediction task is also a useful contribution.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are described clearly, making the work easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern is the distinction between the proposed e-SVM and existing weighted SVM (WSVM) formulations, which allow for instance-dependent costs. The authors briefly discuss this in Section 2, claiming that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points." While the resulting loss function (`u_i * l_i^+ + (1-u_i) * l_i^-`) is indeed different from the standard WSVM loss (`C_i * l_i`), it is not entirely clear how significant this distinction is in practice. A standard WSVM could be trained with instance weights `C_i` set as a function of the positiveness `u_i`. An experimental comparison against such a baseline would be crucial to firmly establish the novelty and superiority of the e-SVM formulation over existing, widely-available techniques (e.g., as implemented in SVMlight or LibLinear).

2.  **Analysis of the Latent Model:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM is non-convex, and the paper relies on an alternating minimization scheme. While this is a standard approach, the paper lacks any theoretical discussion of the optimization, such as convergence properties. Furthermore, the model uses a strong KL-divergence based regularization term to keep the learned latent positiveness values `u` close to their initial estimates `u_bb`. This raises a question: how much do these latent variables actually change from their initialization during training? It would strengthen the paper to include an analysis of the sensitivity to the regularization parameter `λ_R` and a characterization of the learned `u` values, to demonstrate that the latent aspect of the model is indeed learning something meaningful beyond the initial guess.

**Minor Comments**

*   **Abstract:** There is a spelling mistake: "continues" should be "continuous".
*   **Introduction (Page 2, Line 50):** The claim that this is "not a standard regression problem since the value of positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" could be clarified. While standard SVR may not be ideal, other regression methods like logistic regression or beta regression are specifically designed for targets in a bounded interval and are very much standard in machine learning. Rewording this might avoid potential confusion.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated method and demonstrates impressive empirical results. The primary weakness lies in the positioning of the work relative to standard weighted SVMs. While the formulation is novel, its practical advantage over a carefully configured WSVM baseline is not experimentally demonstrated. Nevertheless, the strong performance, particularly the state-of-the-art results in object detection, suggests that the proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field.
