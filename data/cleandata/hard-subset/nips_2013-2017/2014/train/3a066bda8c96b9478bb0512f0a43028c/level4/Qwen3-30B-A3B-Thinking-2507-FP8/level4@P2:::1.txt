

The paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data, where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (quantifying affiliation with the positive class) rather than binary labels. The authors demonstrate how this approach can be applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning in computer vision. Previous work in this area has largely focused on multiple instance learning (MIL), which assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag - a problematic assumption when dealing with segment proposals that typically contain both foreground and background pixels. The authors correctly identify this limitation and propose e-SVM as a more appropriate framework. The method is technically sound, with a well-motivated extension from standard SVMs that handles continuous positiveness values through an expectation-based loss function. This approach elegantly handles both observed positiveness (strong supervision) and latent positiveness (weak supervision) scenarios, making it particularly valuable for practical applications where pixel-level annotations are scarce.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation (with both pixel-level and bounding-box annotations) and PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (using RCNN as a base detector). The results consistently show improvements over SVC and SVR baselines, with a 1.5% average improvement in per-class average precision for object detection. The authors introduce two novel evaluation metrics (Threshold Average Precision Curve and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) that provide more direct assessment of segment classification performance than standard task-specific metrics. The ablation studies and comparisons with multiple SVC thresholds demonstrate careful analysis of the method's behavior.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The problem statement is compelling, addressing a genuinely important practical challenge in computer vision where detailed pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. The methodology is clearly explained, with well-structured sections that guide the reader through the formulation, extension to weak supervision, and application to specific computer vision tasks. The experiments are comprehensive and provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The authors also properly contextualize their work within related literature, distinguishing their approach from MIL and weighted SVMs.

Some minor weaknesses should be noted. The theoretical analysis of the latent e-SVM optimization process could be strengthened - while the iterative algorithm is well-described, a more detailed discussion of convergence properties would be valuable. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007, the significance of the 1.5% AP improvement could be better contextualized against recent advances in object detection (e.g., how this compares to other recent improvements in the field).

The paper's contribution is significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. The e-SVM framework provides a principled approach to handling continuous positiveness values, which is a more natural representation of many real-world labeling scenarios than binary labels. The method is general enough to be applied to various segmentation and detection tasks, and the authors demonstrate this versatility effectively. The work represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods for weakly supervised learning in computer vision, with clear potential for broader application beyond the specific domains tested.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental design is appropriate, and the results are robust across multiple evaluation metrics and tasks. The clarity of presentation is excellent, with well-organized sections and helpful illustrations (Figures 1 and 2). The originality is strong, as the specific formulation of expectation loss for continuous positiveness values represents a novel contribution distinct from prior work on MIL and weighted SVMs. The significance of the work is high, addressing a practical bottleneck in computer vision (the cost of detailed annotations) while delivering state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks.

The authors have made a strong contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality scientific contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with meaningful results. While there are minor opportunities for theoretical depth and contextualization, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The work represents a clear and valuable advance in weakly supervised learning for computer vision, with strong theoretical foundations, thorough experimental validation, and significant practical implications. The paper is well-suited for the NIPS audience, addressing a problem of broad interest while making a technically sound contribution.
