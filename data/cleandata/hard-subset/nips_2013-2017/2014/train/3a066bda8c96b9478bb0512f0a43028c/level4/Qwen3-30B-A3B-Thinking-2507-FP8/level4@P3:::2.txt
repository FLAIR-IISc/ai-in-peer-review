

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors extend standard SVM by incorporating these positiveness values into the loss function, treating each example as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness. They further extend the method to handle cases where positiveness values are unobserved (latent variables), using an iterative optimization approach that alternates between optimizing the SVM parameters and the latent positiveness values.

The work is well-motivated by the practical challenge of reducing annotation effort in computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive but bounding-box annotations are more readily available. The authors demonstrate their method on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over standard SVM and SVR baselines. The paper is clearly written, with a well-structured presentation of the algorithm, experiments, and results.

The main strength of this work is its practical application to important computer vision tasks. The authors demonstrate that e-SVM achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 (60.0 AP), a 1.5% improvement over the baseline RCNN method. The semantic segmentation results are also compelling, showing that the method can effectively learn from bounding-box annotations rather than requiring pixel-level ground truth. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and comprehensive evaluation using both TAPC and NDCG metrics.

However, the paper's main contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to existing literature. The core idea of incorporating example-dependent weights into SVM is well-established in machine learning, with implementations available in standard libraries like SVMlight. The authors acknowledge weighted SVM (WSVM) methods in Section 2 but fail to clearly distinguish their approach from these existing techniques. The distinction they make between "weighting labels" versus "weighting data points" is not sufficiently justified or explained in the context of standard weighted SVM literature.

The extension to the latent case (where positiveness values are unobserved) is interesting but straightforward. The optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) is a standard alternating minimization approach, and the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of this non-convex optimization problem. The regularization term (Equation 3) is used without sufficient justification—it is described as "strong" but the authors don't explain why this particular regularization is necessary or optimal. The claim that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is incorrect, as logistic regression is specifically designed for [0, 1] bounded outputs, and the authors should acknowledge this in their related work.

The paper contains a minor error in the abstract ("continues" instead of "continuous") and a few other minor grammatical issues that should be corrected. The connection to previous work could be strengthened by more thoroughly comparing with weighted SVM methods (e.g., [23, 27]) and clarifying how the authors' approach differs from them.

The significance of the work is moderate. While the method provides a practical solution to weakly supervised learning in computer vision with clear benefits on standard benchmarks, the core technical contribution is incremental. The approach does not fundamentally advance the theoretical understanding of weakly supervised learning but rather applies an existing technique to a new application domain with promising results.

In summary, the paper makes a practical contribution to computer vision by demonstrating how a standard SVM extension can effectively handle weakly supervised learning scenarios. The experimental results are strong, but the novelty claim is overstated. The method could be of interest to practitioners in computer vision, but the technical contribution to machine learning is limited.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The method is well-motivated and addresses a practical problem in computer vision
- The experimental results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines
- The approach is general and could be applied to other domains beyond computer vision
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

Con arguments against acceptance:
- The core idea (weighted SVM) is not novel and is already implemented in standard machine learning libraries
- The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from existing weighted SVM methods
- The theoretical foundation is weak, with no analysis of the latent model's convergence
- The claim about logistic regression not being suitable for [0,1] bounded values is incorrect

Overall, the paper presents a useful practical approach that could benefit computer vision researchers, but its technical contribution to machine learning is limited. The authors would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing weighted SVM literature and a clearer articulation of the novel aspects of their approach.
