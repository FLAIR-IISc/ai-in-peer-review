This work presents a variant of SVM tailored to weak supervision. Each training example is assigned a “positiveness” score in [0,1], and the SVM loss is reformulated to take this score into account. The approach is further extended to the setting where these per-example scores are unobserved and treated as latent variables.

The exposition is clear, and the experiments indicate potential benefits on semantic segmentation and object detection compared to treating all examples equally. However, it is not evident how the proposed method differs from well-known “example-dependent cost” schemes that are widely used in the ML community; for instance, SVMlight already supports such mechanisms.

The extension to unobserved per-example weights is interesting yet fairly straightforward. In this case the objective is non-convex and no theoretical analysis is provided. In practice, a strong regularizer is employed, which appears to keep the latent weights close to their initialization. Could the authors discuss this choice and its consequences?

Minor: The claim on L050 that “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]” is debatable—logistic regression is a standard method whose outputs lie in [0,1].

Minor typo: In the abstract, “continues” should be “continuous.”

In sum, the paper’s main idea is to reweight the SVM loss by per-instance factors, but example-dependent costs are already commonplace in machine learning.
