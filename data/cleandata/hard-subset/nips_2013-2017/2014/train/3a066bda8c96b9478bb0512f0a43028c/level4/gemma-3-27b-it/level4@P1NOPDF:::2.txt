The paper proposes a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data. Specifically, the authors assign a per-example weight—a value between 0 and 1—to each training instance and modify the SVM loss function to incorporate these weights. The method is then extended to scenarios where these per-example weights are not directly observed, but rather treated as latent variables.

The method is clearly described, and the experimental results suggest that this approach can offer advantages over standard SVM training (where all examples are weighted equally) for tasks such as semantic segmentation and object detection. However, I question the novelty of the proposed method, as it appears closely related to existing techniques involving example-dependent costs, which are well-established and widely used in the machine learning community. For instance, SVMlight provides functionality for specifying such costs.

The extension to latent per-example weights is an interesting contribution, though it appears relatively straightforward. Importantly, this extension introduces a non-convexity to the problem, and the paper lacks theoretical analysis addressing this. The authors employ a strong regularization term, which seems to constrain the per-example weights to remain close to their initial values. Could the authors please comment on the necessity of this strong regularization and its potential impact on the learned weights?

A minor point: the claim on line 50 that the problem “is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]” is inaccurate. Logistic regression, a standard regression technique, also produces outputs bounded between 0 and 1. 

There is also a minor spelling error in the abstract: “continues” should be “continuous.”

In summary, the paper presents a modified SVM learning algorithm incorporating per-example weights into the loss function. However, the core idea of example-dependent costs is already prevalent in machine learning.
