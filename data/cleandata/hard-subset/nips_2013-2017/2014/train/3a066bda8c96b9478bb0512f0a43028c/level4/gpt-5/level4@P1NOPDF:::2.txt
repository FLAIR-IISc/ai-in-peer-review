This paper proposes a modification to SVM training for weakly supervised data by assigning a per-example weight in [0, 1] to each training instance and incorporating this weight into the SVM loss. The approach is further extended to the setting where these per-example weights are unobserved (treated as latent variables).

The method is clearly described, and the experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest that weighting examples can provide benefits over uniform weighting.

However, I struggle to see the novelty relative to existing “example-dependent costs,” which are well known and widely used in the machine learning community. For instance, SVMlight already supports per-example cost weighting. The paper should make the distinction explicit: what is fundamentally new beyond established cost-sensitive SVM formulations and their standard implementations?

The extension to latent per-example weights is interesting but appears straightforward. In this case the objective is non-convex, and the paper provides no theoretical analysis. Moreover, the use of strong regularization seems intended to keep the learned per-example weights close to their initial values, which raises questions about how much is actually being learned from the data versus imposed by the prior. Can the authors comment on this design choice and its implications?

Minor comments:
- Line 050: The claim that “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]” is not accurate. Logistic regression is a standard method whose outputs are bounded in [0, 1].
- Abstract: “continues” should be “continuous.”

In summary, the paper presents a modified SVM objective with per-example weights, including a latent-weight extension. While the empirical results are promising, the contribution appears close to existing example-dependent cost frameworks, and the novelty and theoretical support—particularly for the non-convex latent case—should be clarified.
