Summary
The paper proposes a unified scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features, and regresses the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between each segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding-box annotations are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. The regression is implemented via a weighted SVM for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for detection. Empirically, the approach achieves results on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and the best reported performance on VOC 2007 object detection at the time of submission.

Originality
Algorithmic novelty is limited. Weighted SVMs are well-known (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], none cited), and the proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited). The sum constraint on the weights may be novel, though I did not conduct an exhaustive search and the paper should more thoroughly situate this choice relative to prior work.

References the paper should consider:
- [R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
- [R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
- [R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
- [R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance
Beating contemporary CNN-based results with a comparatively conventional learning pipeline is a noteworthy result of clear interest to the community. The empirical gains on VOC 2007 are particularly compelling.

Quality
The empirical section is strong, but several aspects require strengthening:
- Related work: The paper omits key references on weighted SVMs and (weighted) transductive SVMs. These connections should be acknowledged and discussed, including how the proposed sum-constraint departs from or extends prior formulations.
- Baselines for regression on [0, 1]: Regularized logistic regression (or a calibrated probabilistic classifier with a monotonic link) is a standard choice for targets in the unit interval. A logistic (or probit) regression baseline should be included to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM for IoU prediction. Reporting comparisons with squared-loss regression (ridge/L2-regularized) would also help isolate the contribution of the margin-based loss.
- Hyperparameter selection: Please provide details on how λW and λR were chosen (e.g., cross-validation protocol, search ranges, held-out validation split, and whether the same values are reused across classes/datasets). A sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in robustness.
- Ablations: Consider ablations isolating (i) the effect of the sum constraint on the weights, (ii) the impact of the latent-variable treatment versus simpler heuristics for selecting segments within bounding boxes, and (iii) per-feature family contributions to performance for transparency.
- Engineering complexity: The detection framework involves substantial engineering, though it is within the norm for high-performing pipelines. A modular breakdown and clear guidance on which components are most critical would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
- Title and abstract: They do not clearly foreground the core contribution (weighted SVM regression for IoU with a sum constraint; weighted transductive SVM with latent extents for detection). Please revise to highlight these points succinctly.
- Informational content of targets: The abstract suggests that unit-interval regression targets are “less informative” than binary labels. This is not accurate: binary labels can be derived by thresholding the continuous target, while the converse is not true. Please correct this claim.
- Exposition balance: For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (up to page 4) to small variations of well-known algorithms, while providing too little detail on the crucial feature engineering and implementation specifics (lines 254ff). Consider moving routine derivations to an appendix and expanding the feature/architecture details, training schedules, and computational costs in the main text.
- Notation and terminology: Ensure consistent use of SVM/SVC terminology and define all abbreviations at first use (e.g., AP).

Minor comments
- Line 19: “continues” → “continuous”
- Line 94: “boxes” → “box”
- Line 98: Define AP (Average Precision) at first use.
- Line 350: “SVM” → “SVC”
- Line 350: “with every different values” → “for all threshold values”

Strengths
- Strong empirical results, including state-of-the-art detection performance on VOC 2007.
- Conceptually simple pipeline that leverages well-understood tools effectively.
- Sensible use of continuous IoU targets that aligns training loss with evaluation metrics.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient engagement with prior work; key references and attributions are missing.
- Limited algorithmic novelty; primary contribution is the careful engineering and weighting scheme.
- Missing standard baselines and incomplete hyperparameter reporting hinder interpretability of gains.

Recommendations for revision
- Add and discuss the missing references [R1–R4], clarifying what is new (if anything) in the sum-constrained weighting and how it relates to transductive/latent SVM literature.
- Include logistic and/or probit regression baselines, and report ridge regression as an additional point of comparison.
- Detail hyperparameter selection and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Expand the feature engineering and implementation details; consider moving standard derivations to supplementary material.
- Revise the title and abstract to accurately reflect the contributions and correct the claim about target informativeness.

Overall assessment
Great results, deficient references to earlier work, and limited algorithmic novelty. With a stronger related work section, clearer positioning relative to weighted and transductive SVM literature, and the addition of standard baselines, this will be a solid and impactful paper. I recommend acceptance after revision; if venue fit is a concern, this would also be very suitable for a computer vision conference.
