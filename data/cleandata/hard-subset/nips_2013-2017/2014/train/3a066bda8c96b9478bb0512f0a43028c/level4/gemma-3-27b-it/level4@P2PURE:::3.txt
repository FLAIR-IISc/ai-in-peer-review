Summary:
The authors present a method for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is achieved by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between each candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding box labels are available, the precise spatial extent of the object is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The results achieve state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and currently represent the best known performance for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited, and the authors fail to cite relevant prior work. Specifically, the use of a weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4] – not cited). While the authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel, a comprehensive literature search was not conducted to confirm this. Furthermore, the proposed “latent e-SVM” is effectively a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3] – not cited).
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Achieving performance that surpasses recent CNN results with a more conventional learning approach is a noteworthy accomplishment and of significant interest to the research community.

Quality:
The paper suffers from a lack of crucial references to previous work (as detailed above). The object detection framework involves a substantial amount of engineering effort, but remains within the scope of standard practice. Given that regularized logistic regression is the standard approach for regression on the unit interval, these results should be presented as a baseline to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide further detail regarding the selection of the regularization coefficients λW and λR. The paper’s primary strength lies in its strong empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not accurately reflect the paper’s core contributions. The abstract incorrectly suggests that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships; the latter can always be derived from the former through thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, while comparatively glossing over critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have increased my quality score by one, contingent on the authors responding positively to feedback regarding related work and providing an extensive discussion of these connections. The results are strong, but the paper’s novelty is limited and its references are deficient. This is a paper that warrants publication, potentially with revisions, and could also be a strong fit for a computer vision conference.
