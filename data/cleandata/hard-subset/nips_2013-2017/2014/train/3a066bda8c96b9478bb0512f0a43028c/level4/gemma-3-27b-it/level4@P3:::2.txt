This paper proposes the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification to the standard SVM algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where training labels are represented as a continuous “positiveness” value between 0 and 1, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to weight the SVM loss function by this positiveness value, effectively treating it as a confidence measure. The algorithm is further extended to handle cases where the positiveness is unobserved, treating it as a latent variable and employing an iterative optimization procedure. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in weakly supervised learning and SVM modifications. The introduction correctly identifies the need for algorithms capable of leveraging weakly labeled data, contrasting it with the expense of full annotation, a common theme in recent NIPS publications (e.g., [1, 2]). The approach of using a continuous confidence measure is related to previous work using weighted SVMs [3] and regression-based approaches [4], although the authors frame it within a novel loss function specifically designed for this scenario. The application to segment proposal classification connects to advancements in object detection pipelines that rely on generating a large set of candidate regions [5].

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Weakly supervised learning is an increasingly important area, given the challenges of obtaining fully labeled datasets.
*   **Novel loss function:** The proposed expectation loss function is a reasonable approach to incorporating continuous confidence values into the SVM framework.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The authors demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark datasets, including PASCAL VOC for object detection.
*   **Clear explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the algorithm and experimental setup clearly.
*   **Handles latent variables:** Extending the method to the case of unobserved positiveness values expands its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited novelty:** The core idea of weighting examples based on confidence is not entirely new. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing weighted SVM approaches. The authors should more explicitly discuss how e-SVM differs from techniques like SVMlight which natively support instance weighting.
*   **Lack of theoretical analysis:** The latent variable extension introduces a non-convex optimization problem, and the paper lacks theoretical guarantees or analysis of its convergence properties. The reliance on a strong regularization term feels ad-hoc and motivates further investigation.
*   **Minor technical inaccuracies:** The claim that logistic regression is “not a standard regression problem” is incorrect. Logistic regression is a standard approach for binary classification with a bounded output.
*   **Limited ablation study:** While the experimental results are strong, a more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different regularization parameters and feature choices would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Overstated Generality:** While the authors claim their approach is general, the heavy reliance on segment proposals and IoU as a measure of positiveness makes it less adaptable to other problem domains.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods in certain scenarios.
*   The proposed approach is well-motivated and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea lacks significant novelty, given the existing literature on weighted SVMs.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited, particularly regarding the non-convex optimization problem introduced by the latent variable extension.
*   The paper does not fully address the relationship to closely related existing work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable, albeit incremental, contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While the core idea of weighting examples by confidence is not entirely novel, the specific formulation of the expectation loss function and its extension to latent variables are interesting. The strong experimental results support the effectiveness of the approach. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and limited discussion of related work raise concerns.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept**. The paper is technically sound and presents valuable empirical results. However, to strengthen the contribution, the authors should:

1.  More explicitly differentiate e-SVM from existing weighted SVM techniques.
2.  Provide some theoretical justification or analysis for the latent variable extension.
3.  Address the minor technical inaccuracies in the text.
4.  Consider a more thorough ablation study.
