Summary
The paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “positiveness” values—continuous targets in [0,1] that quantify how strongly a segment belongs to the positive class. With pixel-level annotations, the positiveness is observed (e.g., IoU with ground truth); with only bounding-box annotations, the positiveness is treated as a latent variable and learned via a regularized alternating optimization (latent e-SVM). The method is evaluated in two settings: (i) semantic segmentation, where segment proposals (e.g., CPMC) are scored using e-SVM and then assembled into label maps; and (ii) object detection, where the authors build class-specific spatial confidence maps from segment scores and concatenate these features with RCNN descriptors, yielding gains on PASCAL VOC 2007. Results are comparable to state of the art for semantic segmentation and improve RCNN object detection performance on VOC 2007.

Originality
The algorithmic novelty is limited. The proposed e-SVM is closely related to instance-weighted SVMs, which are well known (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]; not cited). The latent e-SVM resembles a weighted variant of transductive or latent SVM formulations (e.g., Vapnik [R3]; not cited). The authors introduce a sum constraint on weights, which may be novel, though this claim requires a clearer positioning in the literature. More thorough discussion and citations are needed to situate the work relative to weighted SVMs, latent SVMs, and transductive SVMs.

[R1] Suykens et al., Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] Yang et al., IJCNN, 2005
[R3] Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] Lapin et al., Neural Networks, 2014

Significance
Beating strong CNN-based baselines in object detection using a comparatively conventional approach is noteworthy and of clear interest. The demonstrated 1–1.5 AP point improvements over RCNN on VOC 2007 suggest the proposed segment-derived features are complementary and practically useful.

Quality
The empirical results are strong and the engineering appears sound and within the norm for the area. However:
- Important related work is not cited (see above), weakening the positioning.
- For targets in [0,1], regularized logistic regression is a natural baseline; including it would better justify the specialized e-SVM loss and the sum-constraint weighting.
- The paper should explain how regularization parameters (lambda_w and lambda_R) were selected and report sensitivity analyses.
- The latent formulation relies on a regularizer pulling latent positiveness toward IoU-with-box initialization; ablations quantifying its impact (versus no regularization) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The title and abstract underemphasize the key contribution: a loss that takes expectations over binary labels with class probabilities tied to “positiveness,” and its latent extension for weak supervision. The abstract also implies that continuous targets are less informative than binary labels; in fact, binary labels are derivable by thresholding continuous scores. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space to small variations of standard SVM formulations (up to about page 4), while skimming over crucial details of feature construction and integration (e.g., lines 254ff). More clarity there would improve reproducibility.

Pros
- Strong empirical gains in object detection; competitive segmentation results.
- Simple, general approach that can plug into existing pipelines.
- Clear motivation for handling graded “positiveness” and weak supervision.

Cons
- Limited algorithmic novelty; missing key citations.
- Incomplete baseline comparisons (no logistic regression on [0,1]).
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and feature engineering.

Minor comments
- Line 19: “continues” → “continuous”
- Line 94: “boxes” → “box”
- Line 98: Define AP (Average Precision) on first use.
- Line 350: “SVM” → “SVC”
- Line 350: “with every different values” → “for all threshold values”

Overall evaluation
Excellent empirical results with a practical and broadly applicable framework, but with limited novelty and incomplete discussion of related work. I recommend acceptance, contingent on a more thorough treatment of prior art, clearer presentation of the core contribution, inclusion of a logistic-regression baseline, and added details on regularization and features. The paper could also be a strong fit for a computer vision venue after revisions.
