### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for classification problems where training labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" values in the range [0, 1]. The authors apply this framework to computer vision tasks, particularly segment proposal classification. The core idea is to model the binary label of a training example as an unobserved random variable whose expectation is given by its "positiveness" score (e.g., Intersection over Union with ground truth). Minimizing the expected hinge loss results in a formulation that is equivalent to an instance-weighted SVM.

The paper addresses two scenarios:
1.  **Strong Supervision:** When pixel-level annotations are available, the positiveness of each segment proposal is known, and the e-SVM is trained directly.
2.  **Weak Supervision:** When only bounding box annotations are available, the true positiveness is unknown and is treated as a latent variable. The resulting "latent e-SVM" is optimized iteratively, alternating between updating the model weights and the latent positiveness values. This approach is conceptually similar to a transductive SVM.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this framework on semantic segmentation and object detection. By combining features derived from their method with the R-CNN pipeline, they achieve state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark, outperforming the R-CNN baseline.

### **Strengths (Pros)**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its impressive performance on a competitive benchmark. Achieving a 1.5% average precision improvement over the very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant and noteworthy accomplishment. This demonstrates the practical value of the proposed system.
2.  **Effective Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a principled way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision (like IoU scores) into a max-margin framework. This is a common and important problem in computer vision, and the proposed method is elegant and effective.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The proposed framework is general. It can be integrated with different segment proposal methods, feature extractors, and base detectors, making the ideas potentially useful to a wide range of researchers in computer vision.

### **Weaknesses (Cons)**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty and Missing Citations:** The main weakness is the paper's positioning with respect to existing work in machine learning. The proposed e-SVM appears to be a specific instance of a well-established technique: the weighted SVM. Similarly, the latent e-SVM for the weakly supervised case is conceptually very similar to transductive SVMs. The paper currently lacks a discussion of these connections and fails to cite key foundational work in these areas (see references below). This gives the impression that the methods are entirely novel, which is not the case.

    *   [R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
    *   [R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
    *   [R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
    *   [R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

**Originality:**
The core algorithmic ideas of weighted SVMs and transductive learning are not new. The paper's novelty lies in the application and successful engineering of these ideas for weakly supervised vision tasks. To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly acknowledge and discuss the relationship between their proposed e-SVM and the existing weighted SVM literature [R1, R2, R4], as well as the connection between the latent e-SVM and transductive SVMs [R3]. Clarifying how their formulation relates to, or differs from, these established methods is crucial for properly contextualizing the contribution for the NIPS audience.

**Quality:**
The empirical results are strong and compelling. However, the scientific contribution could be strengthened in the following ways:
*   **Baselines:** The standard method for regression on the unit interval is typically (regularized) logistic regression. To better justify the use of the proposed SVM-based formulation, results from this baseline should be included in the comparison in Section 5.1.
*   **Reproducibility:** The paper would be more complete if it provided details on how the regularization hyperparameters λ_w and λ_R were selected (e.g., via cross-validation).
*   **Engineering vs. Algorithm:** The success in object detection appears to rely heavily on a well-engineered system (feature generation, spatial pooling, integration with R-CNN). While this is a valid contribution, the paper should be careful to disentangle the gains from the feature engineering versus the learning algorithm itself.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity could be improved:
*   **Title and Abstract:** The title and abstract could be revised to better reflect the paper's core contribution, which is a highly effective application of weighted/transductive SVM principles to computer vision, rather than the invention of a fundamentally new learning algorithm.
*   **Abstract's Phrasing:** The abstract insinuates that continuous labels from a bounded interval are a weaker form of supervision than binary labels. This is misleading, as binary labels can always be obtained from continuous "positiveness" scores via thresholding. This statement should be rephrased.
*   **Paper Structure:** For the NIPS audience, the paper spends considerable space (up to page 4) on derivations of SVM variants that will be familiar to many readers. This space could be more effectively used to elaborate on the crucial feature engineering details (e.g., the spatial confidence map, Section 4.2), which are key to the method's success but are described somewhat briefly.

**Significance:**
Despite the limited algorithmic novelty, the paper's significance is high due to its state-of-the-art empirical results. It provides a valuable demonstration that carefully engineered features combined with well-established learning paradigms can still advance the state of the art, even in an area increasingly dominated by end-to-end deep learning approaches. The work is likely to be influential for practitioners and researchers in object detection and semantic segmentation.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents an impressive system that achieves excellent results on challenging computer vision tasks. The primary concern is the lack of connection to and citation of prior, fundamental work on weighted and transductive SVMs. If the authors revise the paper to properly contextualize their work within the existing machine learning literature, this would be a strong contribution. The empirical results are compelling enough that the paper warrants publication.
