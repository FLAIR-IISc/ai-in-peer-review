This paper introduces a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised problems where training examples are associated with a continuous "positiveness" score in [0, 1] rather than a binary label. The core idea is to reformulate the SVM hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the expectation is taken with respect to the known positiveness score. This formulation is then extended to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved and must be inferred during training, such as learning from bounding-box annotations instead of pixel-level segmentations. The authors position this work as an alternative to standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) with a heuristic threshold or Support Vector Regression (SVR). The method is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using the PASCAL VOC datasets, where it is shown to outperform SVC and SVR on a segment classification task and improve state-of-the-art object detection results when combined with R-CNN.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem: how to effectively learn from supervision that is weaker than binary labels but richer than no labels at all. This is particularly salient in computer vision, where precise pixel-level annotation is expensive, while bounding boxes or other weak labels are more readily available.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification task is a useful contribution.
*   The results are strong, particularly in the object detection application. Achieving a 1.5% average AP improvement over a very strong baseline like R-CNN on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset is a significant and convincing result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary weakness concerns the originality of the basic e-SVM formulation (Section 3.1). The proposed method of weighting the positive and negative hinge loss terms for each example based on its "positiveness" score appears functionally equivalent to established techniques for instance-weighted or cost-sensitive SVMs. Many standard SVM solvers (e.g., LibLinear, cited by the authors) support instance-dependent costs, which can implement this formulation directly. While the authors briefly mention weighted SVMs in Section 2, their claimed distinction—that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points"—is not sufficiently clear, as the resulting objective function still assigns a unique cost profile to each data point. A more detailed comparison and clearer articulation of the novelty relative to the extensive literature on cost-sensitive learning is required.
*   The analysis of the latent e-SVM model (Section 3.2) is somewhat limited. The proposed alternating optimization procedure addresses a non-convex problem, yet there is no theoretical discussion of its properties, convergence, or potential to get stuck in poor local minima. The inclusion of a strong regularization term (Eq. 3) suggests that the learned latent positiveness `u` is heavily constrained to remain close to its initialization `u_bb`. This raises the question of how much is truly being "learned" versus simply refining an initial estimate. An analysis of the sensitivity to this regularization and initialization would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance on important computer vision tasks, including achieving state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark.
*   The problem of learning from continuous-valued weak labels is well-motivated and of practical importance to the NIPS community.
*   The framework is shown to be effective in both the fully-observed ("positiveness" is known) and latent ("positiveness" is inferred) settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty of the core e-SVM method is questionable, as it appears to be a re-framing of well-known cost-sensitive SVMs. The paper fails to adequately position its contribution with respect to this prior art.
*   The theoretical treatment of the non-convex latent variable model is sparse, lacking analysis of convergence or the nature of the solutions found by the optimization algorithm.

**Minor Comments:**
*   The claim in the introduction that SVR is not a standard regression problem "since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is slightly misleading. Many standard regression models, such as logistic regression, are designed specifically for bounded outputs.
*   There is a minor typo in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".
