

Summary:  
The paper proposes a unified framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, it generates candidate segments, extracts features, and regresses on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between segments and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, with only bounding box labels available, it treats precise spatial extent as a latent variable and employs weighted SVMs for regression. Results match state-of-the-art for semantic labeling and achieve the best reported performance on VOC 2007 for object detection.  

Originality:  
The algorithmic novelty is limited. Crucially, the paper fails to cite key prior work on weighted SVMs (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014) and the latent SVM framework (Vapnik, 1998). While the sum constraint on weights may be novel, the core approach—weighted SVMs and latent e-SVM (a weighted transductive SVM)—is not. The omission of these references significantly undermines the contribution claim.  

Significance:  
The strong empirical results—outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a non-deep learning approach—represent a notable achievement and would interest the broader community. This demonstrates the viability of conventional learning techniques in a domain dominated by deep learning.  

Quality:  
The paper omits key prior work (see Originality section), which is a critical flaw. The object detection pipeline involves standard engineering but no major innovations. The authors should report regularized logistic regression (the standard baseline for unit-interval regression) to justify their choice of sum-constrained weighted SVM. Additionally, details on hyperparameter selection (e.g., regularization coefficients $\lambda_W$ and $\lambda_R$) are insufficient. Despite these issues, the empirical results are compelling and well-validated.  

Clarity:  
The title and abstract misrepresent the contribution. The abstract erroneously implies that regression targets (IoU scores) are less informative than binary labels, which is incorrect—the latter can always be derived from the former via thresholding. The paper also overemphasizes minor algorithmic variations (up to page 4) while underplaying critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff), which is inappropriate for a NIPS audience.  

Minor Comments:  
- Line 19: "continues" → "continuous"  
- Line 94: "boxes" → "box" (singular)  
- Line 98: "AP" introduced without definition (add "Average Precision")  
- Line 350: "SVM" → "SVC" (consistent with scikit-learn terminology)  
- Line 350: "with every different values" → "for all threshold values"  

Overall, the paper presents strong empirical results but suffers from inadequate citations and insufficient methodological transparency. The core technical contributions are not novel, and the claims about the approach’s novelty are overstated. However, the results are significant for their domain. I am willing to raise my quality score to "Accept" if the authors thoroughly address the citation gaps and clarify the methodological choices (e.g., hyperparameters, baseline comparisons) in revision. The work would be better suited for a computer vision venue like CVPR, but given its empirical strength and relevance to learning theory, it merits publication at NIPS after revision.
