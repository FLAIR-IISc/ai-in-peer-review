Summary:
The paper presents a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For labeling, it generates candidate segments, extracts features, and regresses the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between each candidate and pixel-accurate ground truth. For detection, where only bounding-box annotations are available, the precise object extent is treated as a latent variable. The authors use a weighted SVM for regression in the labeling setting and a weighted transductive SVM for detection. Results match the state of the art for semantic labeling and achieve the best known performance on VOC 2007 detection.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and key prior work is not cited. Weighted SVMs are well established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], all missing). The introduced sum constraint on the weights may be new, though I did not conduct an exhaustive search. The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (cf. [R3], also not cited).

[R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Surpassing recent CNN-based results with a more conventional learning pipeline is notable and of clear interest to the community.

Quality:
Important references to prior work are missing (see above). The object detection pipeline requires substantial engineering, but remains within the norm for the area. Since the target lies in [0,1], (regularized) logistic regression is the standard baseline; it should be included to justify the advantage of the sum-constrained weighted SVM. More detail is needed on how the regularization parameters lambda_W and lambda_R were selected. The principal strength of the paper is its strong empirical performance.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not clearly convey the paper’s central contributions. The abstract also implies that unit-interval regression targets are less informative than binary labels, which is incorrect: binary labels can be obtained by thresholding continuous targets. For a NeurIPS audience, the paper spends too much space (up to page 4) on minor variations of well-known algorithms and too little on the crucial feature engineering details (from line 254 onward).

I have raised my quality score by one on the assumption that the authors will thoroughly acknowledge and discuss related work and its connections to their approach. In summary: excellent results, insufficient referencing, and limited algorithmic novelty. This is a publishable paper after revisions, potentially more appropriate for a computer vision venue.
