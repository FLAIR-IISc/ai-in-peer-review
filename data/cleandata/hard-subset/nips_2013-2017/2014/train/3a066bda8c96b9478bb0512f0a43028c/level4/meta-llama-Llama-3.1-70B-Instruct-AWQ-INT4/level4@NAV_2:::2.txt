The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that can effectively handle weakly supervised data. Specifically, the algorithm deals with situations where only the "positiveness" of a binary label is available, represented as a continuous value within a bounded interval. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The main idea behind the e-SVM algorithm is to modify the loss function of the traditional SVM to take into account the "positiveness" of each training example. This is achieved by treating the label of each segment as an unobserved random variable and assuming that it follows a Bernoulli distribution. The probability of the label being positive is denoted as µi, which is a function of the positiveness ui. The loss function of the e-SVM is then defined as the expectation over the random variables Yi.

The authors also propose a latent e-SVM model to handle the situation where the positiveness of each segment proposal is unobserved. In this case, the positiveness ui is treated as a latent variable that is determined by minimizing a loss function that includes a regularization term.

The experimental results indicate that the e-SVM algorithm outperforms alternative approaches such as Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) on the segment proposal classification task. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Strengths of the paper include the clear and concise description of the e-SVM algorithm, as well as the thorough experimental evaluation of the approach. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of related work and the connection to instance-weighted SVMs in the literature.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is that the difference between the proposed method and existing "example-dependent costs" is not clearly explained. The authors may benefit from providing a more detailed comparison with existing approaches and highlighting the unique contributions of their work.

Minor comments include a spelling mistake in the abstract ("continues" should be "continuous") and the claim on page 5 that logistic regression is not a standard approach for dealing with bounded intervals [0, 1]. Logistic regression is indeed a standard approach for dealing with binary classification problems, and its output is bounded to [0, 1].

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm that can effectively handle weakly supervised data.
* The experimental results indicate that the e-SVM algorithm outperforms alternative approaches on the segment proposal classification task.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The difference between the proposed method and existing "example-dependent costs" is not clearly explained.
* The extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is interesting but straightforward, and no theoretical analysis is provided.
* A strong regularization term is used in the latent e-SVM model, suggesting that the per-example weights do not deviate far from their initial values.
