**Summary:**

The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. The first candidate is completed to generate segments and extract features for them; and then regressive on the intersection-over-union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-precise ground truth. In the case of object detection, only weak labels are available in terms of bounding boxes, and precise spatial boundaries are used as latent variables. Regression is performed using weighted SVMs and weighted Transductive SVMs. The results are comparable to the state of the art in semantic labeling, and currently best known for object detection on VOC 2007.

**Novelty:**

The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors unfortunately do not cite relevant previous work. In particular, weighted SVMs are well known (e.g., not quoted below [R1,R2,R4]). The authors imposed a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel (I did not conduct a comprehensive literature search here). The "secret e-SVM" proposed by the authors is a weighted version of the Transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not quoted).

*   [R1] Suenkens et al. Weighted Least Square Support Vector Machines: Robustness and Rare Precision, Neurocomputing, 2002
*   [R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machines for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
*   [R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
*   [R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVMs + and Weighted SVMs, Neural Networks, 2014

**Importance:**

The recent CNN results are an achievement of performing better than a "traditional" learning approach and are of great interest to the community.

**Quality:**

Important references to previous work are missing (see above). The object detection framework involves considerable engineering, but it is not trivial. The default method for regression at unit intervals (regularized) is logistic regression, so these results should be reported as a baseline to support the use of a sum-weighted SVM. The authors should provide more information on how they chose the regularization coefficients \lambdaW and \lambdaR. The paper's main strength is the good empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not well summarize the paper's original contribution. The abstract also indicates that the regression target from unit intervals is somehow less informative than only binary class membership. This is not true, as the latter can always be obtained by thresholding from the former. For the audience of the NIPS, the paper spends too much space on minor changes in well-known algorithms (up to page 4 and including), and all the important feature engineering details (lines 254ff) are glossed over somewhat quickly.

Great results, insufficient reference to previous work, little algorithmic novelty. A paper that should definitely be published, possibly after some rewriting, and possibly at a computer vision conference.
