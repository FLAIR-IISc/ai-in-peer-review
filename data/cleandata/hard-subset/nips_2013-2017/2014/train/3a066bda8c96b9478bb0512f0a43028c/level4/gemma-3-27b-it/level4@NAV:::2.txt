This paper introduces a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data. The core idea involves assigning a weight – ranging from 0 to 1 – to each training example, and incorporating this weight into the SVM’s loss function. This approach is further extended to scenarios where these per-example weights are not directly observed, but rather treated as latent variables.

The method is clearly presented, and the experimental results on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest potential benefits over standard SVM training with uniform example weighting. However, the novelty of this approach is unclear, as it bears a strong resemblance to existing techniques employing example-dependent costs, which are well-established within the machine learning field – for example, as implemented in SVMlight. 

The extension to latent per-example weights is a worthwhile exploration, though relatively straightforward. Notably, this extension introduces non-convexity, and the paper lacks accompanying theoretical analysis. The strong regularization employed suggests a prior belief that the learned weights remain close to their initial values. Further discussion from the authors on this point would be valuable.

Finally, a minor clarification is needed on line L050, where the authors state the problem is “not a standard regression problem.” Logistic regression is, in fact, a standard regression method with outputs bounded between 0 and 1. Additionally, there is a minor typographical error in the abstract – “continues” should be “continuous.”

In summary, while the paper presents a logically sound approach, its contribution feels incremental given the existing literature on example-dependent costs in SVMs.
