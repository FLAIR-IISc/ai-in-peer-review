Summary:
The authors present a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for tasks involving weakly supervised data where training labels are continuous values representing the degree of “positiveness” for a binary classification. The method extends the standard SVM formulation to handle these continuous labels, and can be further adapted to a latent variable setting when these positiveness values are unobserved, such as when only bounding box annotations are available. The algorithm is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Originality:
While the core idea of weighting samples based on a continuous “positiveness” value is sensible, the algorithmic novelty is incremental. The authors do not adequately cite related work, particularly in the area of weighted SVMs and transductive learning. For instance, weighted SVMs are a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4] below), and the latent e-SVM bears a strong resemblance to transductive SVM approaches [R3]. The sum constraint on the weights, while potentially a useful addition, requires further justification and comparison to existing regularization techniques.
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Achieving competitive results, and surpassing state-of-the-art performance in object detection using a method distinct from recent deep learning approaches, is a significant contribution. The ability to effectively leverage weakly supervised data is crucial for scaling machine learning to larger, less laboriously annotated datasets.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound and the experimental evaluation is thorough. However, the omission of key references weakens the contribution. The comparison to standard regression techniques (e.g., regularized logistic regression) as a baseline for the unit interval regression task is necessary to justify the use of the weighted SVM. A more detailed discussion of the regularization coefficient selection (λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>) would be beneficial. The strength of the paper lies in its solid empirical results and demonstration of effectiveness on challenging computer vision tasks.

Clarity:
The title and abstract could be more descriptive of the core contribution. The abstract’s statement regarding the informativeness of continuous labels versus binary labels is misleading; binary labels can trivially be obtained through thresholding. The paper spends considerable space discussing minor variations of established algorithms, while details about crucial aspects of feature engineering are presented too briefly (lines 254ff). For a NIPS audience, a more focused discussion of the algorithmic contributions and their theoretical underpinnings would be valuable.

Overall Assessment:
This is a strong paper with promising results. The main weakness is the lack of thorough engagement with related work. Addressing the cited omissions and providing a more concise and focused presentation would significantly strengthen the paper. I’ve slightly increased my quality score, anticipating the authors will address the references constructively. The results are excellent, the algorithmic novelty is limited, and the paper is well-suited for publication, potentially with some revisions, and possibly at a computer vision-focused conference.
