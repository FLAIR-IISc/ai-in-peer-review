Summary:
The authors propose a scheme for both semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is achieved by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. In contrast, object detection relies on weaker labels in the form of bounding boxes, with the precise spatial extent treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The results are comparable to the state of the art in semantic labeling and surpass the current best results for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited, and unfortunately, the authors fail to cite relevant previous work. Specifically, the weighted SVM is a well-established concept (e.g., [R1,R2,R4] below, not cited). The authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be a novel contribution (although a thorough literature search was not conducted to confirm this). The proposed "latent e-SVM" is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
The fact that a "conventional" learning approach outperforms recent CNN results is a notable achievement and of great interest to the community.

Quality:
Crucial references to previous work are missing (see above). While the object detection framework involves some engineering effort, it is not unusually complex. However, the authors should report the results of using (regularized) logistic regression as a baseline for regression on the unit interval to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. Furthermore, the authors should provide more details on how they selected the regularization coefficients λW and λR. The primary strength of the paper lies in its good empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not accurately summarize the core contribution of the paper. The abstract also implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, which is not true since the latter can be obtained from the former by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates too much space (up to page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms and glosses over the important feature engineering details (lines 254ff) too quickly.

Given the paper's great results, despite its deficiencies in referencing earlier work and limited algorithmic novelty, I have increased my quality score by one, assuming the authors will provide a thorough discussion on the relation to previous work and connections to it. This paper should be published, possibly after some rewriting, and possibly in a computer vision conference.
