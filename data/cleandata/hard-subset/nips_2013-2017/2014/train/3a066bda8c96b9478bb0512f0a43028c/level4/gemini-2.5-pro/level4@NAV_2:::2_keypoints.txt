- Introduces e-SVM: reformulates hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label for continuous "positiveness" scores, and extends to latent e-SVM when scores are unobserved.  
- Positioned as an alternative to thresholded SVC and SVR, validated on PASCAL VOC for segmentation and detection, improving segment classification and boosting R-CNN by ~1.5% AP.  
- Strength: tackles a practical weakly supervised learning problem relevant to computer vision where fine labels are costly.  
- Strength: thorough experiments and useful new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains, including state-of-the-art object detection improvements on a competitive benchmark.  
- Weakness: core e-SVM formulation appears functionally equivalent to instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs supported by standard solvers (e.g., LibLinear); novelty is unclear.  
- Weakness: claimed distinction of “weighting labels instead of data points” is not clearly justified; needs clearer comparison to cost-sensitive literature.  
- Weakness: latent e-SVM optimization is non-convex but lacks theoretical analysis (convergence, local minima) and sensitivity study.  
- Weakness: strong regularization bias on latent positiveness u raises concerns that the model largely refines rather than learns u; initialization sensitivity should be analyzed.  
- For acceptance: well-motivated problem, strong empirical results, effective in both observed and latent settings.  
- Against acceptance: novelty questionable due to overlap with cost-sensitive SVMs; insufficient theoretical treatment of the non-convex latent model.  
- Minor: misleading statement that SVR is non-standard because outputs lie in [0,1]; many regressors handle bounded outputs (e.g., logistic regression).  
- Minor: typo in abstract—“continues” should be “continuous.”
