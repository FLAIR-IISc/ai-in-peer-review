This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel method for learning from data where labels are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" score within a bounded interval, such as [0, 1]. The core idea is to model the binary label of each training example as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of its known "positiveness" score (e.g., the Intersection over Union ratio). This formulation results in an objective function that takes the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss, which can be elegantly solved using standard weighted SVM solvers.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting with the latent e-SVM. In this scenario, the "positiveness" scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available) and are treated as latent variables to be inferred during training. This approach provides a principled alternative to common heuristics like thresholding continuous scores to create binary labels for Support Vector Classification (SVC), or using standard Support Vector Regression (SVR), which is not specifically designed for bounded outputs.

The method is validated on the challenging computer vision tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, features derived from the latent e-SVM are used to augment the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, achieving a new state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Strengths**

*   **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. As datasets continue to grow, methods that can effectively leverage cheaper and more readily available annotations like bounding boxes, rather than expensive pixel-level labels, are of increasing importance to the community.
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed e-SVM formulation is clear, well-motivated, and provides an elegant solution for incorporating continuous-valued supervision. The extension to the latent e-SVM for weak supervision is a natural and effective generalization, making the framework broadly applicable.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate the superiority of e-SVM over standard SVC and SVR on the segment proposal classification task. Commendably, they introduce two new and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for this specific problem, which is a valuable contribution in itself. The application to both semantic segmentation and object detection successfully showcases the versatility of the method.
*   **State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper demonstrates significant empirical results, particularly in object detection. Improving upon the very strong R-CNN baseline on the competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark is a non-trivial achievement and highlights the practical value of the proposed segmentation-based features.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and the core ideas are presented in a way that is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality in Context:** While the formulation is novel in this specific application, the core technical idea can be viewed as a specific instance of a weighted SVM, where the weights on the positive and negative slack variables for each example are derived from its "positiveness" score. The latent version also has conceptual similarities to latent SVMs and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), although it generalizes the binary instance assumption in standard MIL to a continuous one. A more explicit discussion of these connections would help to position the work more precisely within the existing literature.

**Summary**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and effective method for learning from continuous-valued weak labels. The approach is technically sound, clearly presented, and backed by compelling experimental results that advance the state-of-the-art in a challenging domain. Although the technical novelty can be seen as an elegant extension of existing frameworks, its specific formulation and successful application represent a valid and valuable contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
