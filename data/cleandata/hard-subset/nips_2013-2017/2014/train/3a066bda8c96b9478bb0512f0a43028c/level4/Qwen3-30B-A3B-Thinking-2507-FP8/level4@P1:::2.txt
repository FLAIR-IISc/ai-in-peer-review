

The paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where training examples are assigned continuous "positiveness" values (ranging between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The core idea is to modify the standard SVM loss function by incorporating these per-example weights, and extend the approach to cases where the positiveness values are unobserved (treated as latent variables). The authors demonstrate applications to semantic segmentation and object detection, claiming state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation behind e-SVM, particularly highlighting the limitations of existing approaches like Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) when applied to problems with bounded continuous labels. The authors correctly identify that binarizing positiveness values for SVC requires arbitrary thresholds, while standard SVR does not properly handle the bounded nature of the target variable (which is confined to [0, 1]). The e-SVM framework elegantly addresses these issues by incorporating the positiveness values directly into the loss function.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with the authors demonstrating performance improvements over SVC and SVR on the segment proposal classification task using two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The application to semantic segmentation and object detection is well-motivated and shows meaningful gains (1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007), particularly on categories where the base RCNN system struggled. The ablation studies comparing different threshold values for SVC and the effectiveness of the latent e-SVM extension are convincing.

However, the paper's key contribution appears to be an application of well-known techniques rather than a novel theoretical advance. The core idea of incorporating instance-dependent weights into SVM loss functions is not new; it has been widely used in the machine learning community for decades. For instance, SVMlight and LIBLINEAR have supported instance-dependent costs for many years, which is precisely what the e-SVM framework implements. The paper would have been stronger with a more thorough discussion of this connection and a clearer articulation of what makes this specific implementation novel compared to existing approaches.

The extension to handle unobserved positiveness values (the latent e-SVM) is interesting but appears straightforward. The authors use a simple iterative optimization approach with a strong KL-divergence regularization term that forces the estimated positiveness values to remain close to their initial bounding-box-based estimates. While this is a reasonable practical approach, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the convergence properties or optimality guarantees for this non-convex optimization problem. The authors acknowledge the non-convex nature but do not provide sufficient justification for the regularization choice or discuss potential pitfalls of the approach.

The claim on line 50 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is misleading. Logistic regression is a standard method for bounded continuous outputs, and the authors' approach is conceptually similar to weighted logistic regression. The paper would benefit from a more precise comparison with these established methods.

The paper contains a minor error in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous." The writing is generally clear, though some technical terms could be more precisely defined (e.g., "positiveness" is used throughout without a formal mathematical definition).

Overall, the paper presents a useful practical algorithm for a specific weakly supervised learning scenario with demonstrated empirical success in computer vision applications. However, the contribution lacks sufficient theoretical novelty to justify publication at NIPS. The core idea is an application of standard weighted SVM techniques, and the extension to latent variables is a straightforward adaptation of existing methods. While the experimental results are compelling, the paper would need to better establish how this approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon established techniques to meet NIPS' standards for originality.

Strengths:
- Clear problem statement and motivation
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple computer vision tasks
- Demonstrated practical improvements over baseline methods
- Well-structured methodology and evaluation

Weaknesses:
- Lack of novelty compared to existing weighted SVM approaches
- Insufficient theoretical analysis of the latent extension
- Misleading claim about the bounded nature of the problem
- Inadequate discussion of connections to existing literature on instance-dependent costs

The work is technically sound and shows practical value, but the novelty is limited. I recommend rejection based on insufficient originality, though the approach may be suitable for a more application-focused venue.
