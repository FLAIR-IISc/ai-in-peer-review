

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised settings where training examples are assigned "positiveness" values (continuous measures of affiliation with the positive class) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a modified SVM loss function that incorporates these continuous weights, and extend the approach to handle cases where positiveness values are unobserved (latent variables) through an iterative optimization procedure. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with results showing improvements over standard SVM classification (SVC) and regression (SVR) approaches.

The paper builds upon recent NIPS work in weakly supervised learning, particularly addressing a gap between fully supervised and multiple instance learning (MIL) settings. Unlike MIL approaches that assume at least one "completely" positive instance per bag, the authors' framework handles the common scenario where bounding box annotations (with partial overlap) are available but pixel-level labels are not. The work relates to previous NIPS papers on weakly supervised learning [2, 7, 20, 22, 26] but addresses a distinct problem setting where the "positiveness" of each segment proposal can be quantified, rather than relying on bag-level labels.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical justification. The e-SVM formulation properly accounts for the continuous nature of the positiveness values through an expectation loss function. The experimental validation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple computer vision tasks. The authors provide detailed ablation studies and carefully compare against relevant baselines.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is compelling, the problem formulation is precise, and the experimental methodology is adequately described. The extension to latent variables is logically developed and the optimization algorithm is clearly presented. The use of IoU as a natural measure of positiveness is particularly well-justified.

3. **Originality:** While the core idea of weighted SVMs has appeared in the literature [17, 23, 27], the authors make a clear distinction between weighting examples (as in standard weighted SVMs) and weighting labels (as in e-SVM), which leads to a fundamentally different loss function. The specific application to segment proposal classification with bounded positiveness values represents a novel contribution to the weakly supervised learning literature. The connection to computer vision tasks is well-motivated and demonstrates practical significance.

4. **Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in computer vision: reducing annotation effort while maintaining performance. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve state-of-the-art results on object detection (PASCAL VOC 2007) using only bounding box annotations, which are more readily available than pixel-level annotations. The method's flexibility to work with various segment proposal detectors and feature representations enhances its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical Depth:** The paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of the latent e-SVM extension. While the authors note the non-convex nature of the latent case, they do not provide convergence guarantees or discuss the implications of the strong regularization term (which essentially forces the learned positiveness values to remain close to their initial estimates). A more rigorous theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Comparison to Weighted SVMs:** The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing weighted SVM methods. The distinction between weighting examples versus weighting labels is made, but the authors do not adequately address why standard weighted SVM solvers (like those in SVMlight) cannot directly handle their problem. The paper claims their approach is "readily extendable" to weak supervision, but this advantage isn't clearly demonstrated in comparison to existing weighted SVM frameworks that could potentially handle similar scenarios.

3. **Experimental Details:** The paper could provide more details about the implementation of the spatial confidence map feature in object detection. The specific parameters (e.g., m=5 for spatial bins) and their sensitivity to performance are not thoroughly analyzed. Additionally, while the authors note improvements over RCNN, they don't compare against other state-of-the-art detection systems beyond RCNN.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pros for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision with practical implications for reducing annotation costs.
- Demonstrates significant improvements over standard SVM approaches on multiple tasks.
- Well-structured paper with clear theoretical motivation and thorough experimental validation.
- Makes a meaningful distinction from existing weighted SVM approaches that is well-justified.

**Cons for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is limited, particularly regarding the latent extension.
- The distinction from existing weighted SVM methods requires more careful elaboration.
- Some experimental details could be more thoroughly presented.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a relevant problem in computer vision with a well-motivated approach. The paper makes a clear distinction between standard weighted SVMs and their expectation loss formulation, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of their method. The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical analysis for the latent case and insufficient differentiation from existing weighted SVM frameworks. However, the practical significance and thorough experimental validation outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with the following suggestions for the authors:

1. Expand the theoretical discussion of the latent e-SVM formulation, particularly addressing the non-convexity and the impact of the regularization term.
2. Provide a more detailed comparison with existing weighted SVM methods to clarify the novelty of their approach.
3. Include additional experimental details about the spatial confidence map implementation and parameter sensitivity.

The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria for NIPS, and while the originality is somewhat limited by the connection to weighted SVMs, the specific application to segment proposal classification with bounded positiveness values represents a meaningful contribution to the field.
