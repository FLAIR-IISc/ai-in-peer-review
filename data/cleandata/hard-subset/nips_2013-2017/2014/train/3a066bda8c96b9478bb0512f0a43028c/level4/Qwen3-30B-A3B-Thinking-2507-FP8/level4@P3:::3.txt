

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap ratios) rather than binary labels. The authors extend their approach to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the exact positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., bounding box annotations only). The method is applied to two core computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For semantic segmentation, the authors demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive results using either pixel-level or bounding-box annotations. For object detection, they integrate their spatial confidence map features with RCNN, achieving a 1.5% average precision improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper builds on the growing need for weakly supervised learning methods as pixel-level annotations become increasingly impractical for large-scale datasets. While the problem setting (learning from continuous label values) is well-motivated, the technical approach is closely related to existing weighted SVM literature. The authors' e-SVM can be viewed as a weighted SVM where weights are derived from the positiveness values (which themselves are constrained to sum to 1), and the latent e-SVM extends this to a transductive setting. Crucially, the authors fail to cite foundational weighted SVM work (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014), which directly addresses the core technical contribution. The connection to transductive SVMs (Vapnik, 1998) is also not properly acknowledged.

**Strengths:**
- The empirical results are strong, particularly for object detection where the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 with a simple integration into RCNN.
- The paper demonstrates clear practical value by showing how the approach can leverage existing bounding box annotations (which are abundant in large datasets like ImageNet) without requiring expensive pixel-level labels.
- The framework is general and can be applied to various segmentation methods and feature extraction schemes.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Originality:** The core algorithmic contribution is limited. The weighted SVM approach is well-established, and the authors' modification (constraining weights to sum to 1) appears to be a minor variation rather than a novel contribution. The failure to properly contextualize the work within existing weighted SVM literature is a significant oversight.
- **Quality:** The paper does not adequately compare against the default method for regression on the unit interval (regularized logistic regression), which would strengthen the claim that their weighted SVM approach is superior. The paper also lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameter selection (e.g., λ_W and λ_R).
- **Clarity:** The title and abstract do not accurately capture the paper's contribution. The abstract incorrectly implies that continuous labels are somehow more informative than binary labels, when in fact binary labels can be derived from continuous ones via thresholding. The paper spends excessive time on algorithmic details that are minor variations of known methods while omitting crucial details about feature engineering (lines 254ff).
- **Significance:** While the results are impressive, the paper does not sufficiently articulate why this approach is a significant advance over recent deep learning methods (e.g., RCNN itself). The 1.5% improvement on VOC 2007 is notable but should be placed in context with other recent advances.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pros:*
- Strong empirical results on two important computer vision tasks
- Clear practical motivation for weakly supervised learning
- General framework applicable to various segmentation and detection methods
- Demonstrates how to effectively leverage bounding box annotations without pixel-level labels

*Cons:*
- Insufficient citation of foundational weighted SVM literature
- Limited algorithmic novelty compared to existing methods
- Overstated significance without proper comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches
- Poorly articulated contribution that conflates existing methods with a minor variation

This paper presents an interesting application of weighted SVMs to weakly supervised learning in computer vision, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the lack of proper contextualization within the existing weighted SVM literature significantly undermines the perceived novelty. The authors should revise the paper to carefully compare their approach to relevant prior work (particularly weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs) and more clearly articulate their specific contributions. The current version risks being perceived as a minor extension of existing techniques rather than a novel contribution to the field.

The quality of the empirical results is high, but the paper would benefit from more detailed methodological descriptions and a more balanced comparison to the appropriate baselines. Given the strong results and clear practical value, this paper has potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but only after addressing the critical literature gap and clarifying the technical contribution.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors will substantially revise the paper to properly contextualize their work within the existing literature on weighted SVMs and transductive learning. The current version lacks the necessary scholarly rigor to be accepted in its present form, but with appropriate revisions, it could make a meaningful contribution to the field.
