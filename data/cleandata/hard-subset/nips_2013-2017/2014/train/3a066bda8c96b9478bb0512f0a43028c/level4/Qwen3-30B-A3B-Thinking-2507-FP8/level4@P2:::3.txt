

This paper introduces an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) framework for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with "positiveness" values (continuous measures of class affiliation between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors demonstrate applications in semantic segmentation and object detection, where the method outperforms SVC and SVR baselines, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection.

The core contribution is a novel SVM formulation that treats segment labels as random variables following a Bernoulli distribution parameterized by the observed positiveness. The standard e-SVM handles cases where positiveness is directly available (e.g., pixel-level annotations), while a latent extension treats positiveness as a hidden variable when only bounding box annotations are provided. The approach is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

**Originality** is limited. The authors fail to properly contextualize their work within existing literature on weighted SVMs and transductive learning. The "weighted SVM" approach is well-established in the literature (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014), and the authors' modification (imposing a sum constraint on weights) appears to be a minor variant rather than a significant innovation. The latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted version of transductive SVM (Vapnik, 1998), yet the authors do not cite these foundational works. This omission significantly weakens the paper's claim of novelty. While the specific application to computer vision tasks with bounding box annotations is useful, the algorithmic contribution is not sufficiently original for NIPS.

**Quality** is generally strong, with thorough experiments demonstrating consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines across multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The object detection results are particularly compelling, showing a 1.5% average AP improvement over RCNN on PASCAL VOC 2007. However, the paper lacks crucial baselines: logistic regression (the standard approach for unit-interval regression) should have been included as a comparison point. The authors also fail to adequately justify their choice of regularization parameters λw and λR, making it difficult to assess the robustness of their results.

**Significance** is high, as the paper demonstrates that a relatively simple learning framework can achieve state-of-the-art results on object detection without relying on deep learning architectures. This is particularly valuable for applications where pixel-level annotations are prohibitively expensive to obtain. The approach provides a practical solution for leveraging the abundance of bounding box annotations already available in large datasets like ImageNet.

**Clarity** is moderate. The title and abstract do not accurately reflect the paper's core contribution, mistakenly suggesting that unit-interval regression is fundamentally different from binary classification (when thresholding can easily convert continuous predictions to binary labels). The paper spends excessive time detailing minor algorithmic variations while insufficiently explaining the critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff) that are essential for replicating the results. The technical explanation of the latent e-SVM optimization is clear, but the connection to prior work is poorly established.

The paper contains several minor errors: "continues" should be "continuous" (line 19), "boxes" should be "box" (line 94), "AP" is introduced without definition (line 98), "SVM" should be "SVC" (line 350), and "with every different values" should be "for all threshold values" (line 350).

Overall, this is a well-executed application of a standard machine learning technique to important computer vision problems. While the empirical results are strong and the approach is practically valuable, the paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel for NIPS. The failure to properly contextualize the work within existing literature on weighted SVMs and transductive learning significantly undermines the paper's scientific contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these gaps, including a thorough comparison to logistic regression and a comprehensive discussion of related work.

The paper would be better suited for a computer vision conference (e.g., CVPR or ICCV) where such applications are more common. For NIPS, the algorithmic novelty is insufficient to justify acceptance, though the results are compelling. With significant revision to properly position the work within the existing literature and address the missing baselines, the paper could be a strong candidate for a vision conference.
