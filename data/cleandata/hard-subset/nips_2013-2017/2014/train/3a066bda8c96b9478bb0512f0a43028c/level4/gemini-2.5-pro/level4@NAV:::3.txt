**Summary:**
The paper proposes a framework for object detection and semantic labeling. For semantic labeling, the approach generates candidate segments, extracts features from them, and then performs regression on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap with the ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding box labels are available, the model treats the precise object extent as a latent variable. The regression tasks are handled by a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The reported results are competitive with the state of the art for semantic labeling and establish a new benchmark for object detection on the VOC 2007 dataset.

**Originality:**
The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited, and the manuscript fails to acknowledge several pertinent prior works. Specifically, the weighted SVM is a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], none of which are cited). While the authors impose a sum constraint on the weights, which could be a novel contribution, this is not thoroughly explored. Furthermore, the proposed "latent e-SVM" appears to be a weighted variant of the standard transductive SVM, a concept introduced in earlier literature (e.g., [R3], also not cited).

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Significance:**
The paper's primary significance lies in its empirical results. Achieving performance that surpasses recent deep learning (CNN) approaches using a more "conventional" machine learning method is a notable achievement and of considerable interest to the research community.

**Quality:**
The main strength of the paper is its strong empirical performance. However, there are several weaknesses. Crucial references to foundational work on weighted and transductive SVMs are missing, as noted above. The object detection pipeline involves significant engineering, though it is within the expected norms for the field. To properly justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM, the authors should include a comparison against a standard baseline for regression on the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression. Additionally, the manuscript should provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter selection process for the regularization coefficients λW and λR.

**Clarity:**
The title and abstract do not effectively convey the paper's central contribution. The abstract also incorrectly suggests that continuous regression targets (from the unit interval) are less informative than binary class labels, when the latter can be trivially derived from the former via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (through page 4) to describing minor variations of well-known algorithms, while the critical details of the feature engineering (lines 254ff) are covered too briefly.

My quality score has been increased on the condition that the authors thoroughly address the relationship to prior work in a constructive manner rather than a defensive one. In summary, the paper presents impressive empirical results, which are its primary strength. However, this is offset by limited algorithmic novelty and a significant omission of relevant literature. The work is certainly worthy of publication, pending substantial revisions. The authors might also consider that a computer vision venue could be a more suitable forum for this contribution.
