

Review Summary:  
The authors propose a method for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling involves generating candidate segments, extracting features, and regressing the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between candidates and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, which relies on weaker bounding box labels, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression employs a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. Results match state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and achieve the best reported scores for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.  

Originality:  
Algorithmic novelty is limited. The authors omit critical prior work: the weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not cited), and the proposed "latent e-SVM" is a weighted transductive SVM variant (e.g., [R3], not cited). The sum constraint on weights may represent a minor novelty, though an exhaustive literature review was not conducted.  

Significance:  
Outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a conventional machine learning approach is noteworthy and holds significant interest for the community.  

Quality:  
Key references are missing (see Originality). While the object detection framework involves standard engineering, the manuscript lacks baseline comparisons against regularized logistic regression (the standard approach for unit-interval regression), which should be included to justify the sum-constrained weighted SVM. The rationale for regularization parameters \(\lambda_W\) and \(\lambda_R\) requires elaboration. The paper’s strongest aspect is its empirical results.  

Clarity:  
The title and abstract inadequately convey the core contribution. The abstract incorrectly suggests unit-interval regression targets are less informative than binary class labels—a misconception, as binary labels can always be derived via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to page 4) to minor algorithmic variations of established methods, while underdeveloping critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have raised my quality score by one point, assuming the authors will address the literature gaps and contextualize their work relative to prior contributions. The results are strong, but the paper requires revisions to clarify connections to existing methods. It merits publication after substantial rewriting, likely at a computer vision conference.
