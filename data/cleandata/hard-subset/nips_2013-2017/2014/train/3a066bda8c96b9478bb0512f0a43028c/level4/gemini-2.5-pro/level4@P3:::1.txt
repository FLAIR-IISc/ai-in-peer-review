**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for binary classification problems where training labels are not discrete {-1, +1} but are continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval [0, 1]. This scenario is common in computer vision, for instance, when the quality of a segment proposal is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground-truth object mask. The authors formulate a loss function that is the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss, where the label is treated as a Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is the "positiveness" score. This effectively weights the positive and negative hinge loss terms for each example.

The paper presents two variants of the model. The basic e-SVM addresses the case where positiveness scores are known (e.g., from pixel-level annotations). More significantly, the paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings where only bounding box annotations are available. In this latent model, the positiveness scores are treated as unobserved variables and are learned jointly with the classifier weights through an alternating optimization procedure, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate (IoU with the bounding box).

The authors validate their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. They first show on a segment proposal classification task that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines, using novel and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). They then demonstrate that their method, when integrated into a state-of-the-art object detection pipeline (R-CNN), yields a significant performance improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, achieving a new state-of-the-art result at the time of submission.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. The cost and difficulty of obtaining dense, pixel-level annotations for large-scale datasets is a major bottleneck in computer vision. The proposed method provides an elegant way to leverage more readily available bounding box annotations, which is a significant contribution.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The formulation of the e-SVM is a natural and intuitive extension of the classical SVM. The progression from the fully supervised model to the more complex latent variable model for the weakly supervised case is logical and well-explained. The overall framework is presented coherently.

3.  **Technical Quality and Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   A key strength is the introduction of two direct performance evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification task. This is a valuable contribution, as it allows for a more principled evaluation than relying solely on downstream task performance.
    *   The experiments clearly demonstrate the superiority of e-SVM over sensible baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR) in both the fully and weakly supervised settings.
    *   Most importantly, the authors show the practical utility of their method by improving upon the then state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector. The proposed spatial confidence map feature is a simple yet effective way to incorporate segmentation cues, leading to a non-trivial 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007. This convincingly demonstrates the significance and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Originality:** While the application and formulation are novel and effective, the core technical idea can be viewed as an incremental advance. The e-SVM loss is essentially a re-weighting of the hinge loss for each sample, which connects it closely to the existing literature on weighted SVMs. The paper does a good job of distinguishing its approach (weighting labels vs. data points), but the underlying mechanism is not a major departure from established concepts. Similarly, the latent extension has conceptual similarities to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), albeit adapted for continuous labels. The primary novelty lies in the specific formulation and its successful application to this important problem.

2.  **Latent Variable Optimization:** The alternating optimization scheme for the latent e-SVM is practical but is only guaranteed to converge to a local minimum. The authors rightly note that the regularization term is crucial to prevent trivial solutions. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final model to the initialization of the latent variables (`u`) would be beneficial. The paper states that IoU with the bounding box is used for initialization, which is a sensible choice, but it would be interesting to know how robust the method is to this initial guess.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The latent e-SVM model introduces a regularization parameter, `λR`, that balances the expected loss term with the regularization on the latent positiveness values. The paper does not include an analysis of how performance is affected by the choice of this parameter. An ablation study or a discussion on the sensitivity to `λR` would strengthen the paper's claims and provide better guidance for practitioners wishing to apply the method.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a well-executed paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective solution to an important and practical problem in machine learning and computer vision. The motivation is strong, the proposed method is clearly explained, and the experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate a significant performance improvement on a challenging benchmark. While the theoretical novelty is perhaps modest, the paper's strength lies in its clear formulation and impactful application. The work represents a solid contribution that is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working in weakly supervised learning and computer vision. The strengths clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses.
