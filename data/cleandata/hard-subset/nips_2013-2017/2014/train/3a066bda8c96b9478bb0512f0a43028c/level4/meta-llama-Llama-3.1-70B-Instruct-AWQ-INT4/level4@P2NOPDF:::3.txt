Summary:
The authors propose a method for semantic labeling and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art results in semantic labeling and the best known results in object detection on VOC 2007. The approach involves generating candidate segments, extracting features, and regressing on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between candidate segments and pixel-accurate ground truth for semantic labeling. For object detection, where only weaker labels in the form of bounding boxes are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. The authors employ a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM for regression.

Originality:
While the results are impressive, the algorithmic novelty of the approach is limited. Unfortunately, the authors fail to cite relevant previous work, including the use of weighted SVMs (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]). The introduction of a sum constraint on the weights may be a novel contribution, although a thorough literature search is needed to confirm this. The proposed "latent e-SVM" is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3]).

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
The fact that the authors' "conventional" learning approach outperforms recent CNN results is a significant achievement and of great interest to the community.

Quality:
The paper lacks crucial references to previous work, which is a significant drawback. While the object detection framework involves some engineering, it is not particularly innovative. The authors should provide more details on their choice of regularization coefficients λW and λR. Additionally, they should report the results of using (regularized) logistic regression as a baseline for regression on the unit interval. The paper's main strength lies in its good empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not accurately summarize the paper's core contribution. The abstract also implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, which is not the case. For a NIPS audience, the paper spends too much space on well-known algorithms and not enough on the important feature engineering details.

Minor comments:
- Line 19: "continues" should be "continuous"
- Line 94: "boxes" should be "box"
- Line 98: AP is not defined
- Line 350: "SVM" should be "SVC", and "with every different values" should be "for all threshold values"

Overall, this is a paper that should be published, possibly after some rewriting, and potentially in a computer vision conference. The authors' results are impressive, but they need to address the deficiencies in referencing previous work and provide more clarity on their approach.
