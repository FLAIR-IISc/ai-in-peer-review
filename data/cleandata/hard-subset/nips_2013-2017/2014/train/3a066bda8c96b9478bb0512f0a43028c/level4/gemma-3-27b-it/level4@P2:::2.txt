This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data where, instead of binary labels, training samples are associated with a “positiveness” value representing confidence in belonging to the positive class. The core idea is to incorporate this positiveness value as a weight within the SVM loss function, and to extend this approach to scenarios where the positiveness is unobserved and must be inferred as a latent variable. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of e-SVM on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper clearly articulates the motivation for developing an algorithm capable of handling weakly supervised data, particularly in computer vision where large, fully annotated datasets are expensive to obtain. The extension to latent variable estimation is a logical progression and addresses a practical challenge in scenarios with only weak supervision (e.g., bounding box annotations). The experimental results presented are compelling and demonstrate improvements over existing methods like SVC and SVR on the tested tasks. The inclusion of both strong and weak supervision scenarios strengthens the evaluation.

However, the paper's originality is somewhat diminished by its close relationship to existing weighted SVM techniques. While the authors present their approach as novel, it shares significant overlap with the well-established concept of instance-dependent costs in SVM learning (e.g., as implemented in SVMlight [Yang et al., IJCNN, 200?]). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of these connections, acknowledging the existing literature and explicitly highlighting the specific contributions of e-SVM beyond simply re-framing the problem as a weighted SVM. 

The latent variable extension, while practical, lacks theoretical justification. The use of a strong regularization term to constrain the inferred positiveness values suggests potential issues with non-convexity and local optima. A discussion of the theoretical properties of this optimization problem, or a more detailed analysis of the impact of the regularization strength, would strengthen the paper.  The authors should address whether the choice of regularization term is critical and whether other regularization strategies were considered.

A minor technical point: the statement regarding logistic regression being “not a standard regression problem” is inaccurate. Logistic regression *is* a standard approach for binary classification with outputs bounded between 0 and 1. Also, the abstract contains a minor typographical error (“continues” should be “continuous”).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem: learning from weakly supervised data.
*   Presents a conceptually clear and well-described algorithm.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on standard benchmarks (semantic segmentation, object detection), achieving state-of-the-art results in object detection.
*   Provides a practical extension for handling latent variable estimation in the weak supervision setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks significant novelty: closely related to existing weighted SVM techniques.
*   Limited theoretical analysis, particularly for the latent variable extension.
*   Minor technical inaccuracies (e.g., regarding logistic regression).



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the application to segment-based prediction and the extension to latent variable estimation are well-executed and lead to promising results. However, the lack of a detailed discussion regarding related work on weighted SVMs and the absence of theoretical analysis for the latent variable case detract from the paper's overall impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough discussion of related work and a more detailed analysis of the latent variable optimization.
