

The authors introduce an Expectation SVM (E-SVM) framework designed to handle continuous-valued attributes of positive instances in weakly supervised learning tasks, specifically applied to visual segmentation classifiers. This work addresses a significant challenge in visual learning under weak supervision, where standard multiple-instance learning (MIL) frameworks typically treat positive instances as binary (present/absent) rather than accounting for their continuous relevance. The E-SVM extends MIL by introducing a soft weighting mechanism for positive examples, enabling more nuanced modeling of instance-level attributes. Crucially, the approach is applicable in both observed and latent settings, broadening its utility beyond prior methods.  

The methodology is rigorously validated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against established baselines. Experimental results are well-executed, with thorough ablation studies and clear comparisons that substantiate the method’s effectiveness. The paper’s motivation is compelling, as it directly tackles the limitations of existing weakly supervised approaches in handling graded instance relevance—a common scenario in real-world visual data. The presentation is exceptionally clear, with a logical flow from problem formulation to technical derivation and empirical evaluation. Key contributions are concisely articulated, and the writing avoids unnecessary jargon, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience.  

While the core idea builds upon MIL extensions, the authors’ adaptation to continuous attributes and its dual applicability to observed/latent settings constitute a meaningful technical contribution. The solution is not merely incremental but provides a principled pathway to leverage richer supervision signals in weakly labeled data, which is increasingly relevant as weakly supervised learning gains traction in computer vision. The empirical results further strengthen the case, showing consistent gains across diverse benchmarks without overfitting or excessive computational overhead.  

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear problem statement, elegant technical extension, and robust validation. The authors avoid overstating claims, and the limitations (e.g., sensitivity to attribute quality) are appropriately acknowledged. For a venue like NIPS, which prioritizes theoretical novelty and practical impact, this work delivers both: it refines a foundational framework (MIL) with a simple yet effective modification, yielding tangible improvements in downstream tasks. The results for semantic segmentation and object detection are particularly convincing, as they demonstrate cross-task applicability without requiring task-specific tuning.  

In summary, this is a strong submission that advances weakly supervised visual learning through a well-motivated, technically sound approach. The E-SVM framework is a valuable addition to the literature, offering a practical tool for scenarios where positive instance relevance is not binary. The paper’s clarity, thorough experiments, and relevance to core NIPS themes (scalable learning, structured prediction) make it a worthy contribution. I recommend acceptance.
