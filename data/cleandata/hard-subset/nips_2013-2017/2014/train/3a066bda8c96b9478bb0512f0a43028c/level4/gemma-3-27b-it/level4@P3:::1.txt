This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for scenarios where training data is labeled with a continuous “positiveness” value rather than a binary label. The authors effectively address the challenge of learning from weakly supervised data, specifically in the context of visual segmentation, and demonstrate strong performance on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The motivation for this work is well-articulated. The increasing impracticality of obtaining large, fully-annotated datasets for computer vision tasks necessitates the development of algorithms capable of leveraging weakly supervised data, such as bounding box annotations instead of pixel-level segmentations. The e-SVM algorithm is presented as a principled approach to handling this type of supervision, offering an alternative to ad-hoc thresholding methods used with standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) or the limitations of applying Support Vector Regression (SVR) to a non-regression problem.  The extension to a latent variable model for truly weakly supervised scenarios (where positiveness is unobserved) is a significant contribution.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction provides a concise overview of the problem and the proposed solution, while subsequent sections detail the model formulation, experimental setup, and results. The use of equations is appropriate and contributes to the technical clarity of the description. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 for the latent e-SVM optimization is particularly helpful. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing e-SVM against relevant baselines (SVC and SVR) and demonstrating improvements on both semantic segmentation and object detection. The introduction of novel evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification is also a valuable addition.

The originality of this work lies in the formulation of the expectation loss, which effectively weights examples based on their “positiveness,” allowing the algorithm to learn from continuous, rather than binary, supervision signals.  While building upon the foundations of SVMs and weakly supervised learning, the e-SVM represents a significant refinement and generalization of existing techniques. The authors adequately reference related work in weakly supervised learning (e.g., Multiple Instance Learning) and weighted SVMs, demonstrating awareness of the broader research landscape.  The ability to seamlessly transition between observed and latent positiveness values provides a practical advantage.

The significance of this work is substantial. The demonstrated improvements in object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 are noteworthy. More broadly, the e-SVM algorithm provides a valuable tool for tackling a wide range of machine learning problems where data is inherently weakly supervised, or where obtaining precise labels is costly or impractical. The generality of the approach, as highlighted by the authors – its compatibility with different segment proposal detectors, feature representations, and base detectors – further enhances its potential impact.  The method has the potential to reduce annotation costs and improve the scalability of computer vision systems. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in computer vision – learning from weak supervision.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound algorithm (e-SVM) with clear theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on established benchmarks (PASCAL VOC) achieving state-of-the-art results in object detection.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.
*   Provides a useful generalization of standard SVM techniques.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvement over existing methods, while significant, might not be *revolutionary*.
*   The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, is primarily focused on PASCAL VOC.  Evaluation on other datasets would strengthen the claims of generality.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a solid technical contribution, addresses an important problem, and is supported by compelling experimental results. The paper meets the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. While further evaluation on more diverse datasets would be beneficial, the current work provides a strong foundation for future research in weakly supervised learning.
