**Summary:**

The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. The first candidate is accomplished by generating segments and extracting features for them; and then performing regression on the intersection-over-union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-precise ground truth. In the case of object detection, only weak labels are available in the context of bounding boxes, and precise spatial boundaries are used as latent variables. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted Transductive SVM, respectively. The results are comparable to the state of the art in semantic labeling, and currently the best known for object detection on VOC 2007.

**Novelty:**

The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors unfortunately do not cite relevant previous work. In particular, the weighted SVM is well known (e.g., not cited below [R1,R2,R4]). The authors have imposed a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel (I did not conduct a comprehensive literature search here). The "secret e-SVM" proposed by the authors is a weighted version of a Transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

*   [R1] Suenkens et al. Weighted Least Square Support Vector Machines: Robustness and Rare Precision, Neurocomputing, 2002
*   [R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machines for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
*   [R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
*   [R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Importance:**

The recent CNN results are an achievement of performing better than a "traditional" learning approach and are of great interest to the community.

**Quality:**

Significant references to previous work are missing (see above). The object detection framework involves considerable engineering, but it is not out of the ordinary. The default method for regression at unit intervals (regularized) is logistic regression, so these results should be reported as a baseline to support the use of a sum-weighted SVM. The authors should provide more information on how they chose the regularization coefficients \lambdaW and \lambdaR. The paper's main strength is good empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not well summarize the paper's original contributions. The abstract also indicates that the regression target from unit intervals is somehow less informative than only binary class membership. This is not true, as the latter can always be obtained by thresholding from the former. For the audience of the NIPS, the paper spends too much space on minor changes to well-known algorithms (up to and including page 4), and passes over all important feature engineering details (lines 254ff) somewhat quickly.
