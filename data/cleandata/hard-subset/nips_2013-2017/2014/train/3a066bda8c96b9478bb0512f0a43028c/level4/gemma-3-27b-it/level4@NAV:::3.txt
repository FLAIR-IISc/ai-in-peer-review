The authors introduce a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is achieved through candidate segment generation, feature extraction, and regression of Intersection-over-Union overlap with ground truth pixel labels. For object detection, bounding box labels are treated as weak supervision, with precise spatial extent modeled as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM. The reported results achieve state-of-the-art performance on semantic labeling and the best currently known results on VOC 2007 for object detection.

The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the paper lacks citations to relevant prior work. Specifically, the use of a weighted SVM is established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not cited), though the authors’ imposition of a sum constraint on the weights may represent a novel contribution. The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted variant of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited). 
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

The achievement of competitive performance, and even surpassing recent CNN results, using a conventional learning approach is a significant contribution.

The paper suffers from a notable omission of crucial references to prior work (as detailed above). The object detection framework requires considerable engineering effort, but remains within typical boundaries for this task. While the authors employ a sum-constrained weighted SVM for regression on the unit interval, the standard approach of (regularized) logistic regression should be included as a baseline for comparison. Further clarification is needed regarding the selection of regularization coefficients \(\lambda_W\) and \(\lambda_R\). The paper’s strength lies primarily in its strong empirical results.

The title and abstract do not effectively convey the core contribution of the work. The abstract also incorrectly suggests that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships, as the latter can be readily obtained from the former through thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space to minor variations of established algorithms (up to page 4), while glossing over important details regarding feature engineering (lines 254ff).
