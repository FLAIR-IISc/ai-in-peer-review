Summary:
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, they generate candidate segments, extract features for these segments, and regress the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between each candidate and the pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, only weaker bounding-box labels are available, and the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. Results are on par with the state of the art for semantic labeling and achieve the best known performance for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and the paper unfortunately omits key related work. In particular, weighted SVMs are well known (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], none cited). The authors impose a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel (I did not conduct an exhaustive search). The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

[R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Outperforming recent CNN results with a conventional learning approach is impressive and of clear interest to the community.

Quality:
Crucial references to prior work are missing (see above). The detection framework involves substantial engineering, though within community norms. Because the regression target lies on the unit interval, (regularized) logistic regression is the natural default; this baseline should be reported to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should also provide more detail on how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were selected. The main strength of the paper is its strong empirical performance.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not succinctly capture the paper’s core contribution. The abstract also suggests that unit-interval regression targets are less informative than binary class labels. This is not correct, since binary labels can be obtained from the former by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (through page 4) to small variations of well-known algorithms, while moving too quickly over the crucial feature-engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have increased my quality score by one on the assumption that the authors will not be defensive about the relation to prior work but will instead discuss it thoroughly and make the relevant connections. Excellent results, inadequate referencing, limited algorithmic novelty. This paper should certainly be published, ideally after some rewriting, and possibly at a computer vision conference.
