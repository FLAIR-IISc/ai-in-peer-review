This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for problems where training labels are not binary but are continuous values representing a degree of "positiveness," such as the Intersection over Union (IoU) of a segment proposal with a ground truth object. The core idea is to formulate the SVM loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the probability of being positive is a function of the observed continuous positiveness score. The authors extend this model to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings, such as learning from bounding-box annotations, where the positiveness of each proposal is unobserved and treated as a latent variable to be optimized. The paper situates this work relative to standard Support Vector Classification (SVC), Support Vector Regression (SVR), and weighted SVMs, arguing that the proposed formulation is better suited to the problem structure. The method is validated on the tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC datasets, where it is shown to improve upon baselines and contributes to state-of-the-art object detection performance when combined with the R-CNN framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. As datasets grow, methods that can effectively leverage cheaper annotations like bounding boxes instead of pixel-perfect segmentations are increasingly important. The motivation for a method that directly handles continuous positiveness values, rather than thresholding for SVC or fitting a regression for SVR, is clear and compelling.
*   **Quality of Contribution:** The proposed e-SVM model is an elegant and principled approach to the problem. The extension to the latent e-SVM for handling unobserved positiveness is a natural and powerful generalization. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against appropriate baselines (SVC, SVR) and demonstrating the model's effectiveness. The introduction of new evaluation criteria for segment proposal classification (TAPC and NDCG) is a thoughtful contribution that allows for a more direct assessment of the model's performance on its primary task.
*   **Experimental Results:** The results are strong. The e-SVM consistently outperforms SVC and SVR on the segment classification task under the proposed new metrics. More importantly, the method demonstrates practical utility by improving the performance of a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN) on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark, showcasing its ability to provide complementary segmentation-based cues.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed models are explained logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality in Context:** While the specific formulation is novel, the core concepts are closely related to existing ideas. The e-SVM can be viewed as a specific, probabilistically-motivated form of an instance-weighted SVM, a well-established area. Similarly, the iterative optimization for the latent e-SVM is a standard alternating optimization approach, reminiscent of methods used for latent SVMs. A more detailed comparison of the final objective function to that of various weighted SVMs would help to clarify the distinction more formally.
*   **Latent Model Optimization:** The paper proposes an iterative algorithm for the latent e-SVM but does not discuss its convergence properties. While such alternating minimization schemes are common, a brief discussion of potential local minima or conditions for convergence would strengthen the technical presentation.
*   **Limited Scope of Baselines in Applications:** For the object detection task, the improvement is shown over one specific, though state-of-the-art, system (R-CNN). While convincing, demonstrating that the spatial confidence map feature provides a general benefit to other families of detectors would further underscore the significance of the approach.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. It presents a novel and well-motivated model, backs it up with solid experimental results on important computer vision tasks, and demonstrates a clear performance improvement on a competitive benchmark. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-suited for the conference.
