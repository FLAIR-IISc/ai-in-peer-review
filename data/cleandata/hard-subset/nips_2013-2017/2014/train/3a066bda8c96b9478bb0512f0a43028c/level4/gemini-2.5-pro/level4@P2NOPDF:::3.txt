### **Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features, and then regresses on their Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap with pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding box labels are available, the object's precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. The regression tasks are addressed using a weighted SVM and a novel weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The method achieves performance on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and reports the best-known results for object detection on the VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Originality**

The paper's primary weakness lies in its limited algorithmic novelty and a significant omission of relevant prior work. The core learning methods, particularly the weighted SVM, are well-established techniques in the literature [R1, R2, R4], yet these foundational papers are not cited. Similarly, the proposed "latent e-SVM" appears to be a weighted variant of a transductive SVM, a concept explored in works such as Vapnik's "Statistical Learning Theory" [R3], which also goes unreferenced. While the introduction of a sum constraint on the weights may represent a novel contribution, its originality is not thoroughly established by the authors.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

### **Significance**

The significance of this work is substantial. Achieving results that outperform recent, powerful CNN-based approaches using a more "conventional" learning framework is a notable accomplishment. These findings are of considerable interest to the community, as they highlight the continued potential of feature-engineering and structured prediction models.

### **Quality**

The primary strength of the paper is its impressive empirical results. However, the quality of the technical presentation and experimental evaluation could be improved in several areas.

1.  **Missing References:** As noted under Originality, the paper is missing crucial citations to foundational work on weighted and transductive SVMs. This needs to be rectified.
2.  **Missing Baselines:** The experimental evaluation, while strong, lacks a necessary baseline. For a regression task on the unit interval, regularized logistic regression is a standard approach. Its performance should be reported to properly justify the use of the more complex sum-constrained weighted SVM.
3.  **Lack of Detail:** The paper provides insufficient detail on the hyperparameter selection process, specifically how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were chosen.
4.  **Engineering Complexity:** The object detection framework involves significant engineering, though this is not atypical for the domain.

### **Clarity**

The paper's clarity could be significantly enhanced. The title and abstract do not effectively communicate the core contributions. The abstract also contains a misleading statement suggesting that regression targets on the unit interval are less informative than binary labels; this is incorrect, as binary labels can be trivially derived from continuous values by thresholding.

More critically, the paper's structure seems misaligned with a NIPS audience. A substantial portion of the manuscript (through page 4) is dedicated to describing minor variations of well-known learning algorithms. In contrast, the crucial details of the feature engineering (lines 254ff)—which are likely a primary driver of the excellent results—are covered too briefly.

### **Minor Comments**

*   Line 19: "continues" should be "continuous".
*   Line 94: "boxes" should be "box".
*   Line 98: The abbreviation "AP" is used without prior definition.
*   Line 350: "SVM" should likely be "SVC" if referring to classification.
*   Line 350: "with every different values" should be rephrased to "for all threshold values".

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a method that achieves outstanding empirical results, particularly in object detection. Its main weaknesses are a lack of algorithmic novelty and a failure to properly situate the work within the existing literature by citing foundational papers. The excellent performance, however, makes a strong case for publication. Acceptance is recommended on the condition that the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to address the missing citations, clarify the relationship to prior work, provide stronger experimental baselines, and rebalance the presentation to focus more on the feature engineering and less on standard learning algorithms. Given its strong empirical focus and engineering contributions, this work might also be a very strong candidate for a top-tier computer vision conference.
