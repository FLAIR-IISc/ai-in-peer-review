

This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where training samples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors demonstrate that their method can effectively handle both strong supervision (where positiveness values are observed) and weak supervision (where positiveness is latent, as in bounding box annotations), and apply it to two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The paper builds upon previous work in weakly supervised learning, particularly addressing limitations in standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) approaches for problems with continuous label values. The authors correctly note that SVC requires ad-hoc thresholding to binarize continuous positiveness values, while SVR is not well-suited for bounded regression targets. Their e-SVM model elegantly handles this by treating labels as random variables with success probabilities derived from the positiveness values. The latent extension of the model for weak supervision settings is particularly valuable, as it allows handling cases where only bounding box annotations are available (common in large-scale datasets like ImageNet) without requiring pixel-level annotations.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by presenting a unified framework that addresses the continuous label problem in a theoretically grounded way. While the authors acknowledge connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs (WSVMs), they correctly argue that their problem setting differs significantly from MIL (which assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag) and that their approach to weighting differs from WSVMs (which weight data points rather than labels). The authors provide a clear theoretical derivation of their loss function and optimization procedure, showing how it can be efficiently solved using standard SVM solvers.

In terms of quality, the paper presents strong empirical results across multiple tasks. The e-SVM model achieves state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (improving AP by 1.5% over RCNN) and demonstrates competitive results on semantic segmentation using both pixel-level and bounding box annotations. The experimental design is well-justified, with appropriate baselines (SVC with multiple thresholds and SVR) and thorough evaluation using both TAPC and NDCG metrics specifically designed for the segment classification task. The ablation studies on different threshold values for SVC provide valuable insights into why the authors' approach outperforms alternatives.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem statement is well-motivated, the technical contributions are clearly articulated, and the experimental results are presented in a logical and transparent manner. The figures illustrating the framework and spatial confidence map feature are particularly helpful for understanding the method's application to object detection. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics like the use of CPMC segments and the spatial confidence map feature construction.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by addressing a specific gap in weakly supervised learning where standard approaches fail due to the bounded continuous nature of the labels. While the problem setting shares some similarities with MIL and WSVM, the authors clearly differentiate their approach and demonstrate its advantages through rigorous experiments. The paper's contribution is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS work, as evidenced by the absence of comparable methods in recent proceedings.

The significance of this work is substantial. As the authors note, obtaining pixel-level annotations for large-scale datasets is prohibitively expensive, while bounding box annotations are widely available. Their method enables the effective use of these more accessible annotations, potentially reducing the annotation burden for many computer vision tasks. The demonstrated improvements on object detection (a core computer vision problem) and semantic segmentation (a major challenge in the field) suggest this approach could have meaningful impact on both research and practical applications.

The paper does have a few minor weaknesses. The connection to existing work on weakly supervised learning could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting with recent methods like the weakly-supervised dual clustering approach (Liu et al., 2013) and Multi-Image Models (Vezhnevets et al., 2006). Additionally, while the authors mention that the method can use "any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers," more specific discussion of how the method might interact with different feature representations would be valuable. The regularization term in the latent model is well-justified, but a more thorough analysis of its sensitivity to hyperparameters would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem in computer vision with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful advance over existing methods for weakly supervised learning. The results are significant and the method is general enough to be applied to other classification problems with continuous label values.

In summary, the paper's strengths include:
- Clear problem formulation addressing a real-world limitation in computer vision
- Theoretical grounding of the e-SVM model
- Effective handling of both strong and weak supervision settings
- Strong empirical results on multiple computer vision tasks
- Clear presentation and sufficient detail for reproducibility

The minor weaknesses include:
- Limited discussion of how the method might interact with different feature representations
- Limited comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond SVC and SVR

Given the paper's technical quality, clear presentation, and significance to the field of computer vision, I recommend acceptance. The work represents a valuable contribution to the literature on weakly supervised learning and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches.
