The authors propose an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) designed to handle continuous-valued “positiveness” indicators for training data, specifically addressing scenarios with weak supervision in visual learning. The core idea is to extend the standard SVM framework to accommodate a continuous measure of affiliation to the positive class, rather than relying on binary labels. This is applied to the problem of learning visual segmentation classifiers under weak supervision, and the methodology is validated through experiments in both semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating promising results.

The paper addresses an important problem: learning with weakly labeled data is crucial for scaling visual learning to large datasets where detailed manual annotation is impractical. The proposed approach is well-motivated, building upon the limitations of applying standard classification and regression techniques (SVC and SVR) to this problem. The presentation is clear and well-structured, effectively communicating the technical details of the e-SVM algorithm and its latent variable extension. The experimental evaluation is thorough, utilizing standard datasets (PASCAL VOC) and comparing against relevant baselines. The state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant result.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a critical area of research, and this work offers a valuable contribution.
*   **Clear and Well-Motivated:** The problem statement and the motivation for the proposed approach are clearly articulated. The connection to existing limitations of SVC and SVR is well explained.
*   **Solid Technical Foundation:** The e-SVM formulation appears technically sound, extending the standard SVM framework in a principled manner.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.
*   **Strong Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance is a noteworthy accomplishment.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While technically sound and practically effective, the core idea builds upon established concepts like SVMs and multiple instance learning. The novelty lies in the specific adaptation to continuous positiveness values and the latent variable extension, which feels somewhat incremental. It would be helpful to explicitly contrast the method with recent work in weakly-supervised learning using consistency regularization or other alternative approaches (e.g., [19, 26]).
*   **Regularization Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the latent e-SVM model relies on the choice of the regularization parameter for the latent variable *u*. The paper mentions this is important to avoid local minima, but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to this parameter and guidelines for its selection would be valuable.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of different components (e.g., the specific choice of regularization term, the spatial confidence map features) would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem with a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on standard benchmarks.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental.
*   The sensitivity to the regularization parameter in the latent variable setting is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While the novelty is not groundbreaking, the proposed e-SVM algorithm is a practical and effective solution to a challenging problem. The strong experimental results and clear presentation make this a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance.
