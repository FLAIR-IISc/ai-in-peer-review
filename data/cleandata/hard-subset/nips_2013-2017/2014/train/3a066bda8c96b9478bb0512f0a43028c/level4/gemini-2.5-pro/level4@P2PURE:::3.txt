**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features for them, and then performs regression on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio with the pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding box labels are available, the object's precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. The regression tasks are addressed using a weighted SVM for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for object detection. The reported results are competitive with the state of the art in semantic labeling and achieve state-of-the-art performance for object detection on the VOC 2007 dataset.

**Originality**

The algorithmic novelty of this work is limited, and the paper fails to cite several key pieces of prior work. Specifically, the weighted SVM is a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]), yet these references are omitted. The authors' introduction of a sum constraint on the weights may represent a novel contribution, though a more extensive literature review would be needed to confirm this. The proposed "latent e-SVM" appears to be a weighted variant of a transductive SVM, a concept introduced in earlier work (e.g., [R3]) that also goes uncited.

**Significance**

The paper's primary significance lies in its impressive empirical results. Outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a more "conventional" learning approach is a notable achievement and will be of significant interest to the community.

**Quality**

The paper's quality is hampered by the omission of crucial references to previous work. The object detection framework involves significant engineering, though this level of complexity is not unusual for such systems. To justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM, the authors should have reported baseline results using a standard method for regression on the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression. The paper should also provide more detail on the selection process for the regularization coefficients λW and λR. The main strength of the paper is its strong empirical performance.

**Clarity**

The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the paper's core contributions. Furthermore, the abstract incorrectly suggests that continuous regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class labels. This is misleading, as binary labels can be derived from continuous scores via thresholding. The paper's exposition seems imbalanced for a NIPS audience; it dedicates excessive space (through page 4) to describing minor variations of well-established algorithms, while glossing over the crucial feature engineering details (lines 254ff), which are likely key to the method's success.

*   Line 350: "SVM" should likely be "SVC" (Support Vector Classification).

---
This paper presents excellent results, but its contributions are weakened by limited algorithmic novelty and deficient referencing of prior work. I have increased my quality score on the assumption that the authors will address these omissions thoroughly by providing an extensive discussion of the connections to existing weighted and transductive SVMs. While the paper's strong empirical performance certainly merits publication, its focus on system engineering over novel machine learning principles might make it a better fit for a computer vision conference.
