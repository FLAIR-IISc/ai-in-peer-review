The paper proposes a modification to the SVM learning algorithm to handle weakly supervised data. Specifically, a per-example weight (between 0 and 1) is assigned to each training example, and the SVM loss function is modified to incorporate this weight. The method is then extended to the case where the per-example weight is unobserved, becoming a latent variable.

The method is well described, and the experimental results suggest that this approach may offer advantages over treating all examples with equal weight, particularly for semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. However, the novelty relative to existing methods employing “example-dependent costs” is not clearly established. While the authors acknowledge weighted SVMs, they do not adequately differentiate their approach. Many machine learning libraries, such as SVMlight, already provide mechanisms for implementing such costs.

The extension to the latent variable case, where the per-example weight is unobserved, is interesting but appears straightforward. The resulting optimization problem is non-convex, and the paper lacks theoretical analysis of its properties. The use of a strong regularization term, preventing the per-example weights from deviating significantly from their initial values, suggests a reliance on this initialization. It would be helpful if the authors could provide further justification for the choice of regularization and discuss the sensitivity of the results to its parameters. 

Minor comment: The claim on page 5 that “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]” is inaccurate. Logistic regression is a standard regression technique designed for bounded output values between 0 and 1. 

Minor typographical error in the abstract: “continues” should be “continuous.”

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method demonstrates empirical improvements over standard SVM and SVR approaches on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection.
*   The extension to handle unobserved “positiveness” values (latent variable case) provides a practical approach for weakly supervised learning.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting a coherent framework for expectation loss SVM.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea of weighting examples based on confidence is not novel and overlaps with existing weighted SVM techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited, particularly regarding the non-convex optimization problem in the latent variable case.
*   The connection to related work on weighted SVMs could be more thoroughly discussed, highlighting the specific contributions of this work.
