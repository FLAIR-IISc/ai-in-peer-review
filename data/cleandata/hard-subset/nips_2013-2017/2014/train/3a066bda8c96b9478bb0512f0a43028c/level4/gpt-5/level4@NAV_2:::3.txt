Summary:
The paper proposes a framework for semantic segmentation and object detection. For segmentation, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features for each, and predicts the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between a segment and pixel-level ground truth. For detection, where only bounding-box labels are available, the fine-grained spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Learning is carried out with a weighted SVM and its latent variant (a weighted transductive-style SVM) for the two settings, respectively. Empirically, the approach matches the state of the art on semantic segmentation and achieves the best reported performance on VOC 2007 detection.

Originality:
The methodological novelty appears modest, and several key precedents are not cited. Weighted SVMs are well established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], none cited). The paper’s construction effectively assigns complementary weights to the positive and negative hinge losses that sum to one (through a Bernoulli success probability), which may be a novel twist, though I did not exhaustively verify this. The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted analogue of a transductive SVM (cf. [R3], not cited).
[R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Beating recent CNN-based systems with a more “classical” learning recipe is noteworthy and of broad interest.

Quality:
Important related work is missing (see above). The detection pipeline includes substantial engineering, but this is typical for the area. Since the targets lie in [0,1], (regularized) logistic regression is a natural baseline and should be reported to justify the proposed sum-constrained weighted SVM formulation. Please also clarify how the regularization parameters λW and λR were selected. The chief strength of the paper is the strong empirical performance.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not clearly convey the core contribution. The abstract also implies that continuous labels in [0,1] carry less information than binary labels, which is not accurate since binary labels can always be derived by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (up to about page 4) to minor variations of known methods, while moving too quickly over the crucial feature-engineering aspects (lines 254ff).

This is a paper that merits publication, ideally after revisions, and might also fit well at a computer vision venue.
