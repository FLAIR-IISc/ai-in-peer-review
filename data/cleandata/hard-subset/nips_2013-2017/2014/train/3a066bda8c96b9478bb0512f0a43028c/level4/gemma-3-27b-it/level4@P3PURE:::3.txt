Summary:
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is accomplished by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker bounding box labels are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. Results achieve state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and currently the best known results for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
The algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors unfortunately do not cite pertinent previous work. Specifically, the use of a weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4] below, not cited). While the authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights – which may represent a novel contribution (though I did not conduct an exhaustive literature search) – the proposed "latent e-SVM" is fundamentally a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited). 
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Outperforming recent CNN results with a “conventional” learning approach is a significant achievement and of considerable interest to the community.

Quality:
Crucially, the paper omits references to prior work (see above). The object detection framework involves a substantial amount of engineering, but remains within typical boundaries for this task. Regularized logistic regression is the standard approach for regression on the unit interval; therefore, results from this baseline should be included to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide more detail regarding their selection of the regularization coefficients λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>. The primary strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the core contributions of the paper. The abstract also implies that regression targets from the unit interval are somehow less informative than binary class memberships, which is incorrect as the latter can always be obtained from the former via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, while relatively quickly covering the essential feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

Overall Assessment:
I have increased my quality score by one point, anticipating that the authors will address the connection to previous work thoroughly and constructively, rather than defensively. The paper presents strong results, suffers from insufficient referencing of earlier work, and demonstrates limited algorithmic novelty. It is a paper that merits publication, potentially with revisions, and may be particularly well-suited for a computer vision conference.
