

Summary:  
The authors propose a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, the approach generates candidate segments, extracts features, and regresses on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio between each candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only bounding box labels are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression employs a weighted SVM for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for object detection. Results match state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and achieve the best reported results for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.  

Originality:  
Algorithmic novelty is limited. Crucially, the authors omit critical prior work. The weighted SVM (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]) is well-established, and the authors’ sum constraint on weights may be novel (though no extensive literature search was conducted here). The proposed "latent e-SVM" is a weighted variant of transductive SVM (e.g., [R3]), yet [R3] is not cited.  

[R1] Suykens et al., *Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation*, Neurocomputing, 2002  
[R2] Yang et al., *Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification*, IJCNN, 2005  
[R3] Vapnik, *Statistical Learning Theory*, 1998  
[R4] Lapin et al., *Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM*, Neural Networks, 2014  

Significance:  
Outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a conventional learning approach represents a significant contribution, offering valuable insights for the community.  

Quality:  
Key references are missing (see Originality section). The object detection framework involves standard engineering practices. The default method for regression on the unit interval is (regularized) logistic regression; these results should be reported as a baseline to justify the use of the sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide greater detail on how regularization coefficients \(\lambda_W\) and \(\lambda_R\) were selected. The paper’s primary strength lies in its strong empirical results.  

Clarity:  
The title and abstract inadequately summarize the core contribution. The abstract incorrectly implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class labels, which is false—the latter can always be derived from the former via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to page 4) to minor variations of established algorithms, while glossing over critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

Overall, I have increased my quality score by one, assuming the authors will address the missing references and algorithmic connections rather than being defensive. The results are strong, but the paper requires revisions to acknowledge prior work. It merits publication after revisions, potentially at a computer vision conference.
