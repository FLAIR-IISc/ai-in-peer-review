

Review: The paper introduces a modified SVM learning algorithm incorporating per-example weights (within [0,1]) into the loss function for weakly supervised learning. The approach extends to scenarios where these weights are latent variables, treated as optimization parameters. The experimental evaluation on semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrates empirical gains over uniform weighting, suggesting potential utility for these tasks.

The core contribution, however, lacks sufficient novelty. The proposed weight-modified SVM loss is fundamentally equivalent to the well-established concept of "example-dependent costs," extensively documented in the machine learning literature and implemented in standard tools like SVMlight. SVMlight explicitly supports per-example cost weights (e.g., via the `--weight` option), enabling the same weighting mechanism described here. The paper fails to adequately distinguish its formulation from this existing paradigm, presenting it as a novel technique rather than a reimplementation of a standard feature. This undermines the primary claim of the work and necessitates a clearer justification of how the proposed method advances beyond current practice.

The extension to latent weights is conceptually straightforward but suffers from significant theoretical gaps. The formulation becomes non-convex when weights are latent, yet the paper provides no theoretical analysis of convergence, optimality, or solution properties. Instead, it relies on strong regularization to constrain weights near initial values (e.g., the term `λ∥w - w₀∥²`). The justification for this regularization strategy is insufficient; it implicitly assumes weights remain close to their initialization without empirical or theoretical support. The authors should clarify: (1) Why this regularization is necessary and appropriate for the latent weight setting, (2) Whether alternative regularization or optimization strategies (e.g., alternating minimization with convergence guarantees) could be employed, and (3) How the choice of `w₀` (initial weights) impacts results. Without this, the approach appears ad hoc.

The claim on line 50 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is inaccurate. Logistic regression is a standard, well-established method for bounded [0,1] outputs and is routinely used for such problems. This mischaracterization weakens the paper’s technical precision.

Minor issues include a typo in the abstract ("continues" → "continuous") and a grammatical error in line 50 ("value is using" → "value in using"). These should be corrected.

In summary, while the empirical results on segmentation and detection are promising, the paper’s core contribution is not novel—it replicates a standard SVM feature (example-dependent costs) without sufficient differentiation from existing tools like SVMlight. The latent weight extension lacks theoretical grounding and relies on unexplained regularization. The minor technical inaccuracies further diminish the paper’s rigor. Given the high bar for NIPS, the work does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant acceptance in its current form. The authors should either (1) substantially reframe the contribution to highlight a *new* mechanism beyond standard example-dependent costs, or (2) provide rigorous theoretical analysis for the latent weight case and a detailed comparison against SVMlight’s implementation. Without these, the paper’s novelty is questionable.
