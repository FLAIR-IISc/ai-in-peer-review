

This paper proposes an SVM modification for weakly supervised learning, introducing a weight in [0,1] per training example that scales the loss function. The authors extend this to cases where weights are latent variables (unobserved). While experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest potential benefits over uniform weighting, the core contribution appears to be an application of established example-dependent cost techniques—a standard feature in machine learning toolkits like SVMlight. The authors do not clearly distinguish their approach from this well-known paradigm.

The extension to latent weights is straightforward but non-convex, yet the paper provides no theoretical analysis of this optimization challenge. The heavy regularization used (to keep weights near initial values) suggests the method relies on strong prior assumptions rather than principled handling of uncertainty. The authors should clarify whether the regularization is necessary for convergence or merely a practical heuristic, and whether alternative optimization strategies could be explored.

A minor but notable error appears on line 50: the claim that "it is not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0,1]" contradicts standard practice. Logistic regression is a canonical example of a [0,1]-bounded model, making this assertion misleading. The abstract also contains a spelling error: "continues" should be "continuous."

The paper’s technical contribution lacks novelty relative to existing SVM implementations with example-dependent costs. While the empirical results demonstrate utility for specific tasks, the method does not advance beyond known techniques. The authors should explicitly address prior work (e.g., SVMlight’s implementation of example-dependent costs) and clarify how their formulation differs conceptually or practically. Without this, the work risks being perceived as a reimplementation rather than a meaningful extension. The latent weight approach, though simple, would benefit from deeper theoretical grounding or a clearer justification for the chosen regularization strategy. Overall, the experimental validation is promising but insufficient to justify the paper’s novelty claim.
