

This paper introduces an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel extension to multiple instance learning (MIL) that accommodates continuous supervision signals for positive instances. The authors address a critical challenge in visual learning: leveraging weak supervision where labels are imprecise or incomplete, such as in image-level annotations for pixel-wise segmentation. The core innovation lies in replacing standard binary weighting of positive bags with a continuous expectation-based loss, enabling the model to handle soft supervision signals. This approach is rigorously validated across semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline MIL methods.

The problem formulation is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The authors convincingly argue that real-world weak supervision often provides *continuous* rather than binary cues (e.g., confidence scores for object presence), and that existing MIL frameworks fail to exploit this information. The e-SVM framework elegantly bridges this gap by incorporating expectation maximization into the SVM optimization, allowing the model to learn from partial or noisy supervision. Crucially, the method operates effectively in both *observed* (where supervision is directly provided) and *latent* (where supervision is inferred) settings—a distinction that broadens its applicability to diverse weakly supervised scenarios.

The technical contribution is sound and well-executed. The extension from binary to soft weighting is non-trivial, requiring careful derivation of the expectation loss function and its integration into the SVM dual problem. The authors provide clear theoretical justification for the approach, including convergence guarantees and complexity analysis. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough, and the model’s flexibility is demonstrated through its seamless adaptation to both segmentation (using pixel-level features) and detection (using region proposals) pipelines. Experiments on standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC, MS COCO) show consistent gains over strong baselines, with particularly compelling results in scenarios with sparse or noisy supervision. For instance, on VOC 2012 segmentation, the e-SVM achieves a 3.2% mIoU improvement over standard MIL methods under weak supervision, without requiring additional training data or complex post-processing.

The presentation is exceptionally clear. The paper is structured logically: problem statement → method → theory → experiments → discussion. Figures effectively illustrate the soft supervision mechanism and segmentation results, while the ablation studies convincingly isolate the impact of the expectation loss component. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon, making the technical contribution accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The authors also thoughtfully address limitations (e.g., computational overhead in latent settings) and suggest promising future directions, such as extending to video or multi-modal learning.

While the e-SVM builds upon MIL foundations, its extension to continuous supervision signals—especially in the latent setting—is a meaningful advance. It avoids the "binary bottleneck" of traditional MIL without introducing excessive complexity, making it both theoretically principled and practically deployable. The validation across two distinct vision tasks (segmentation and detection) strengthens the claim of generalizability, and the results are statistically significant. The paper thus makes a solid contribution to the growing literature on weakly supervised learning, offering a practical and effective tool for practitioners.

In summary, this work addresses an important, underexplored aspect of weak supervision with a technically sound and well-executed solution. The method is clearly presented, thoroughly validated, and demonstrates tangible improvements over existing approaches. It represents a valuable addition to NIPS’ program on machine learning for vision, particularly for its focus on leveraging more nuanced supervision signals. The reviewers recommend acceptance.
