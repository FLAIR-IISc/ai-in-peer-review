This paper proposes a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) framework to better handle weakly supervised data. The core idea is to introduce a continuous weight for each training example, bounded between 0 and 1, which modulates its contribution to the SVM loss function. The paper first presents a formulation for the case where these weights are observed and then extends this to a latent variable model where the weights are unobserved and learned jointly with the classifier parameters.

The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed method is described clearly. The experimental evaluation on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks suggests that the proposed weighting scheme can offer performance benefits over a standard SVM where all examples are weighted equally.

Despite these positive aspects, there are several significant concerns regarding the work's contribution and analysis.

My primary concern with this work lies in its novelty, particularly for the observed weights case. The idea of assigning a per-example cost or weight to the loss term in an SVM is a well-established technique in the machine learning community, often referred to as cost-sensitive learning or example-dependent costs. For instance, widely used implementations such as SVMlight have long provided functionality for example-dependent cost factors, which serve an almost identical purpose to the formulation presented here. The authors should clearly articulate how their formulation for the observed weight case differs from, and improves upon, these standard cost-sensitive SVMs. Without this distinction, this part of the contribution appears to be an application of a known technique rather than a novel method.

The extension to the latent weight case is an interesting direction, but the treatment is somewhat straightforward and lacks theoretical depth. The proposed alternating optimization scheme is a standard approach for such non-convex problems. However, the paper provides no theoretical analysis regarding the convergence of this procedure or the quality of the resulting local minimum. Furthermore, the use of a strong regularization term on the weights suggests that the optimization may be constrained to prevent the learned weights from deviating significantly from their initial values. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the results to this regularization parameter and clarify how much the final weights actually differ from their initialization? A more thorough analysis is needed to understand the behavior and limitations of this non-convex optimization.

**Minor Points:**

*   The claim on L050 that the problem is "not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is inaccurate. Standard and widely-used models like logistic regression are specifically designed for outputs in this range. This statement should be revised.
*   There is a minor spelling mistake in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

In summary, while the paper addresses an important problem and shows some promising empirical results, its main contribution in the observed-weights case appears to overlap significantly with existing, well-known methods for cost-sensitive learning. The latent-variable extension, while interesting, is presented without sufficient theoretical analysis, leaving open questions about its stability and behavior. The authors need to better position their work relative to prior art and provide a deeper analysis of the non-convex optimization problem.
