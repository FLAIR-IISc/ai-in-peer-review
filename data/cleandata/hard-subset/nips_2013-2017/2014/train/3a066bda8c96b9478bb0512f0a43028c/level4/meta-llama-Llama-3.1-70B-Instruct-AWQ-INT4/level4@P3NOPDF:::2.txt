The paper proposes a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) learning algorithm, specifically designed to handle weakly supervised data. This is achieved by assigning a per-example weight, ranging from 0 to 1, to each training example. The loss function of the SVM is then modified to incorporate this weight, allowing for a more nuanced approach to learning from the data.

The method is clearly described, and the experimental results suggest that using per-example weights can be beneficial for tasks such as semantic segmentation and object detection, particularly when compared to treating each example with equal weight. However, it is unclear how the proposed method differs from existing approaches that utilize "example dependent costs," which are widely recognized and employed in the machine learning community. For instance, SVMlight provides a mechanism for incorporating example-dependent costs into the learning process.

The extension of the method to cases where the per-example weight is not observed, and is instead treated as a latent variable, is an interesting contribution. However, this extension results in a non-convex problem, and the authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the resulting optimization problem. Notably, a strong regularization term is used, which suggests that the per-example weights are not allowed to deviate significantly from their initial values. It would be beneficial for the authors to provide further insight into this aspect of their approach.

On a minor note, the authors claim on line 50 that the problem is not a standard regression problem due to the bounded interval [0, 1]. However, logistic regression is a well-established and widely used method that is indeed bounded to the interval [0, 1]. Additionally, there is a minor spelling mistake in the abstract, where "continues" should be replaced with "continuous."

In summary, while the paper proposes a modified SVM learning algorithm that incorporates per-example weights, it is unclear how this approach differs from existing methods that utilize example-dependent costs. The extension to the case where per-example weights are not observed is an interesting contribution, but further analysis and insight into the optimization problem would be beneficial.
