Summary
The paper proposes an “expectation loss SVM” (e-SVM) for learning with weak or partial supervision. Instead of binary labels, each example has a “positiveness” score ui in [0,1] (e.g., IoU between a segment proposal and ground truth). The method treats the label Yi as Bernoulli with success probability µi = g(ui) (set to ui in experiments) and minimizes the expected hinge loss: each example contributes a convex combination of positive and negative hinge losses, weighted by µi and 1−µi, respectively. This yields a formulation solvable by standard linear SVM solvers.

The model is extended to the weakly supervised case where ui is unobserved. The authors treat ui as latent variables initialized from IoU with bounding boxes (ubb) and regularize them via a convex cross-entropy term R(u) = ∑i DKL[Bern(ubb_i) || Bern(ui)], then alternate between optimizing w and u.

Experiments evaluate segment proposal confidence prediction with new criteria (TAPC and NDCG), semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC 2011, and object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007. For detection, a spatial confidence map is built from segment scores and concatenated with RCNN features, yielding 1.2–1.5 AP improvement on average.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions e-SVM relative to SVC, SVR, MIL, and instance-weighted SVM (WSVM). The closest prior art appears to be example-dependent costs/weights (available in e.g., SVMlight) and SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels. The authors argue e-SVM “weights labels instead of data points,” but the practical effect is that each example’s hinge loss is weighted by µi and 1−µi for the two classes; this looks very similar to instance-dependent weighting and soft-label SVM formulations. A clear, formal comparison and discussion of equivalences/differences to WSVM and soft-label SVMs would strengthen the positioning.

Quality
- The convex expected-loss formulation (with observed ui) is straightforward and can be handled by standard solvers. The latent extension leads to a non-convex objective; the paper uses alternating minimization with a strong convex regularizer that keeps u close to ubb. No convergence guarantees or theoretical analysis are provided.
- The new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) are sensible for segment confidence prediction, though precise definitions and protocol details should be clearly stated.
- Empirically, e-SVM outperforms thresholded SVC and is slightly better than SVR on the segment confidence task. For semantic segmentation, performance is comparable to SVR. For detection, concatenating spatial confidence maps with RCNN features yields a modest but consistent improvement (≈1.5 AP after bounding box regression).

Clarity
The method is generally well described. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent use of vectors and augmented bias), and explicit definitions of TAPC/NDCG and training/testing protocols would help reproducibility. The term “positiveness” is used throughout; “class membership probability” or “soft label” may read more naturally. Minor typos noted below.

Originality
The observed-label formulation reduces to an expected hinge loss with sample-specific mixing weights; this is very close to existing instance-weighted and soft-label SVM variants. The latent extension, regularized toward bbox-derived IoU via a convex KL term, is reasonable but also fairly straightforward. The spatial confidence map feature for detection integration is simple and practical.

Significance
The approach is practical for scenarios with soft labels or weak supervision and integrates easily with existing SVM tooling. The empirical gains for detection are modest but potentially useful. However, given the apparent overlap with established example-dependent cost/soft-label SVM methods, the conceptual novelty seems limited unless clearer distinctions are demonstrated.

Specific comments and questions
- Relation to example-dependent costs: Please clarify whether your expected hinge loss with µi and 1−µi is equivalent to training with per-example costs (e.g., class-conditional costs or soft-label SVM). If not equivalent, provide a formal comparison and empirical ablation showing conditions where e-SVM differs materially from WSVM/SVMlight-style weighting and from probabilistic-label SVMs.
- Latent e-SVM: The strong KL regularization suggests u will not deviate far from initialization ubb. Can you report sensitivity to λR, initialization choice, and the number of alternations? How much do the learned ui move from ubb in practice? What is the impact on performance if λR is reduced (or removed)?
- Choice of g(ui): You set g(ui)=ui. Did you try other monotonic mappings (e.g., temperature scaling) and assess robustness?
- Baselines: Since the target is in [0,1], logistic regression or probabilistic SVM with calibrated probabilities seems a natural baseline. Can you compare against these, in addition to SVC/SVR?
- Detection: Provide per-class gains and ablations isolating the contribution of the spatial confidence map (e.g., RCNN alone vs. RCNN + e-SVM features vs. using SVR features). Any sensitivity to m (number of spatial bins) or pooling choices?
- Evaluation details: Please fully define TAPC and NDCG computation (threshold grid, class averaging, tie handling) and report variance or confidence intervals if possible.

Pros
- Clear, simple formulation that leverages standard SVM solvers.
- Practical latent extension for weak supervision with a convex regularizer.
- Sensible evaluation for segment confidence prediction; consistent improvements over SVC/SVR there.
- Easy integration with RCNN yields measurable AP gains.

Cons
- Conceptual novelty over existing instance-weighted/soft-label SVMs is unclear.
- Latent optimization is non-convex, with no analysis; heavy regularization may limit learning from weak labels.
- Missing comparisons to natural probabilistic baselines (e.g., logistic regression).
- Detection gains are modest and may depend on feature concatenation details.

Minor comments
- L050: “it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1].” Logistic regression is a standard approach for bounded [0,1] outputs; please clarify the intended contrast with SVR.
- Abstract: “continues” → “continuous.”
- Please standardize “e-SVM” vs. “eSVM” and fix minor typos/formatting in equations and references.

Overall assessment
A useful and practical treatment of soft/weak labels with solid empirical work, but the contribution appears incremental relative to established example-dependent cost and soft-label SVM methods. Clarifying the theoretical and empirical distinctions, and strengthening baselines/ablations, would significantly improve the paper.
