Summary
The paper introduces the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for settings where each training instance has a “positiveness” score in [0,1] rather than a binary label. The method replaces the standard hinge loss with its expectation under a Bernoulli label whose success probability is a function g(u) of the positiveness u (set to g(u)=u in experiments). This yields an objective that effectively weights the positive and negative hinge losses for each example by u and 1−u, respectively, and can be optimized with standard linear SVM solvers. The authors also present a latent variant for weak supervision, where u is unobserved (e.g., when only bounding boxes are available) and is optimized jointly with the classifier under a convex regularizer encouraging proximity to an initialization (e.g., IoU with bounding boxes). They evaluate on segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection, reporting consistent improvements over SVC/SVR for segment scoring and a ~1.5 AP gain when augmenting R-CNN features with an e-SVM–based spatial confidence map.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is closely related to instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs and to approaches that handle soft/probabilistic labels by weighting hinge losses. Many SVM toolkits (e.g., SVMlight) support example-dependent costs. The paper briefly contrasts with weighted SVMs, claiming e-SVM “weights labels instead of data points,” but the practical distinction and conditions under which e-SVM is or is not equivalent to instance-weighted or probabilistic-label SVMs are not clearly articulated.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: exploiting continuous overlap/IoU targets avoids heuristic binarization.
- Simple, practical objective that can be solved with off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers.
- Empirical gains on segment proposal scoring (via TAPC and NDCG) and downstream tasks.
- Weakly supervised extension with a sensible regularizer anchored at bounding-box–derived IoU.
- The spatial confidence map feature for object detection is simple and effective, yielding measurable improvements when combined with R-CNN.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty appears limited relative to existing example-dependent costs and soft/probabilistic-label SVM formulations. Please clarify the theoretical difference and, if different, provide conditions where e-SVM cannot be reduced to per-example cost weighting.
- The latent variant is non-convex; no convergence guarantees or analysis are provided. In practice, a strong regularizer keeps u close to its initialization, suggesting limited deviation from initial IoUs. How sensitive are results to the regularization strength and initialization? Do u-values meaningfully change?
- The choice g(u)=u is reasonable but unexamined. How do alternative link functions (e.g., calibrated mappings) affect performance?
- Comparisons focus on SVC and SVR. It would strengthen the case to include baselines with cost-sensitive/instance-weighted SVMs configured to mirror the e-SVM weighting, and to compare against logistic regression with probabilistic labels or calibrated SVMs.
- Some terminology and claims could be tightened (see minor comments).

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound and easy to implement. The latent optimization lacks theoretical analysis and ablations (e.g., sensitivity to g, λR) that would deepen understanding.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable. The distinction from weighted SVMs needs clearer exposition; minor wording/typos exist.
- Originality: Incremental. The expected hinge-loss view is neat, but similar ideas (instance weighting/soft labels) are established.
- Significance: Moderate. The reported gains are consistent and the approach is practical, but the conceptual contribution over known methods seems small.

Pros
- Addresses a common weakness in binarizing continuous supervision.
- Simple, general, and compatible with standard solvers and pipelines.
- Demonstrates improvements on recognized benchmarks and integrates cleanly with R-CNN.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to example-dependent costs/soft-label SVMs.
- Non-convex latent variant lacks analysis; relies on strong regularization.
- Missing comparisons to the most directly related baselines.

Requests for clarification
- Precisely formalize how e-SVM differs from instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs and from SVMs with probabilistic/soft labels. If equivalent under reparameterization, please acknowledge; if not, show why.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for g(u), λR, and initialization of u; report how much u moves during training.
- Add baselines that use per-example costs matching u and 1−u in a standard SVM, and logistic regression with probabilistic targets.

Minor comments
- The claim that this is “not a standard regression problem since the positiveness is in [0,1]” is misleading; logistic regression is standard for targets/probabilities in [0,1].
- Typo: “continues” → “continuous.”
- Consider using “soft label,” “probabilistic label,” or “affinity” instead of “positiveness.”

Overall, the paper presents a practical and useful formulation with solid empirical results, but it needs a clearer positioning relative to existing cost-sensitive and soft-label SVM literature and a deeper analysis of the latent variant to substantiate originality and robustness.
