

Summary:  
The authors propose a method for semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is achieved by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and regressing on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between each candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, only bounding box labels are available, with precise spatial extent treated as a latent variable. Regression employs a weighted SVM for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for object detection. Results match state-of-the-art performance in semantic labeling and achieve the best reported performance for object detection on VOC 2007.  

Originality:  
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors omit key prior work. The weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not cited). The authors impose a sum constraint on weights, which may be novel (though no exhaustive literature search was conducted). The proposed "latent e-SVM" is a weighted variant of transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).  

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, *Neurocomputing*, 2002  
[R2] X. Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, *IJCNN*, 2005  
[R3] V. Vapnik, *Statistical Learning Theory*, 1998  
[R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, *Neural Networks*, 2014  

Significance:  
Outperforming recent CNN-based results with a conventional learning approach is significant and of substantial interest to the community.  

Quality:  
Critical prior work is omitted (see above). The object detection framework involves standard engineering. The default regression method for the unit interval is (regularized) logistic regression; these results should be reported as a baseline to justify the sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should clarify how regularization parameters $\lambda_W$ and $\lambda_R$ were selected. The paper’s main strength is its strong empirical results.  

Clarity:  
The title and abstract poorly capture the core contribution. The abstract incorrectly implies that regression targets on the unit interval are less informative than binary class labels; this is untrue, as binary labels can always be derived via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to page 4) to minor variations of established algorithms, while skipping over critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

The results are strong, but the paper suffers from deficient references and minimal algorithmic novelty. It merits publication after revision, potentially at a computer vision conference.
