Summary:
The authors present a scheme for learning from weakly supervised data using the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm. The e-SVM model is designed to handle situations where the labels of training data are continuous values with a bounded interval. It can be applied to segment proposal classification tasks and can be easily extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision, where only bounding box annotations are available. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the e-SVM algorithm on two major tasks in computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection. They achieve state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Originality:
While the authors propose a novel learning algorithm, the algorithmic novelty is limited. The weighted SVM is well-known, and the authors do not cite pertinent previous work. The "latent e-SVM" proposed by the authors is a weighted version of a transductive SVM. However, the authors put a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel. The authors should comment extensively on the connections to previous work and provide a more thorough literature review.

Significance:
The authors' results are of great interest to the community, as they outperform recent CNN results using a "conventional" learning approach. The e-SVM algorithm's ability to handle weakly supervised data makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

Quality:
The paper is missing crucial references to previous work. The object detection framework involves a significant amount of engineering, but not outside the norm. The authors should report the results of regularized logistic regression as a baseline to support the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. Additionally, the authors should provide more details on how they picked regularization coefficients λW and λR. The main strength of the paper is the good empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not summarize the core contribution of the paper well. The abstract insinuates that regression targets from the unit interval are somehow less informative than mere binary class memberships, which is not true. The paper spends too much space on small variations of well-known algorithms and passes somewhat quickly over the feature engineering details. The authors should revise the paper to better summarize the core contribution and provide more details on the feature engineering.
