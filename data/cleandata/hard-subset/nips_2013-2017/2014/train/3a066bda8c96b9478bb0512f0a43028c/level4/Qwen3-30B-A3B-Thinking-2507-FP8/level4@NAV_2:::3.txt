

The paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where training samples are annotated with continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels. The method extends to weak supervision via latent variables (e.g., bounding box annotations) and demonstrates state-of-the-art object detection performance (60.0 mAP) on PASCAL VOC 2007, outperforming RCNN by 1.5 points. It also achieves competitive results on semantic segmentation using pixel-level or bounding-box annotations.  

**Originality**: The core weighted SVM framework is well-established in literature (e.g., Lapin et al. 2014, Suykens et al. 2002), and the authors fail to cite these works. While the sum-constrained weight formulation (∑w_i = 1) may offer a minor technical distinction, the "latent e-SVM" is a standard weighted transductive SVM (Vapnik 1998), not a novel contribution. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work on weighted SVMs or weakly supervised segmentation (e.g., Müller & Behnke 2012, Vezhnevets et al. 2008).  

**Quality**: The empirical results are strong and well-motivated, but critical omissions undermine rigor. The authors omit logistic regression (the standard approach for [0,1] regression tasks) as a baseline, which is essential for justifying the weighted SVM choice. Hyperparameters λ_W and λ_R lack justification, and the regularization term R(u) (Eq. 3) is not compared to alternatives. The method’s engineering details (e.g., feature extraction, spatial confidence map construction) are underdeveloped relative to algorithmic exposition.  

**Clarity**: The title and abstract misrepresent the contribution by implying continuous labels are inherently more informative than binary labels (which can be derived via thresholding). The paper spends excessive space (up to p. 4) on algorithmic variations while neglecting crucial feature engineering details (lines 254–260). Key terms (e.g., "AP" on line 98) are undefined.  

**Significance**: The achievement of SOTA object detection performance (60.0 mAP) using a conventional learning approach—without relying on deep learning—holds significant practical value for reducing annotation costs in large-scale vision tasks. This contribution is compelling, especially given the growing scale of datasets like ImageNet.  

**Strengths**:  
- Empirical validation across segmentation and detection tasks with strong results (SOTA detection on VOC 2007).  
- Clear extension to weak supervision via latent variables (bounding box annotations).  
- Practical applicability to real-world datasets with limited annotations.  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Critical prior work on weighted SVMs (Lapin et al. 2014, Suykens et al. 2002) is omitted.  
- No comparison to logistic regression (standard baseline for [0,1] targets).  
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and feature engineering.  
- Overemphasis on algorithmic exposition at the expense of technical depth.  

**Recommendation**: The paper’s empirical significance is strong, but the lack of engagement with foundational work and methodological gaps in the quality section warrant major revisions. Acceptance is justified only if the authors thoroughly address the cited literature, add logistic regression as a baseline, and clarify feature engineering and hyperparameter choices. The work is valuable for its practical impact but requires stronger technical grounding to meet NIPS standards.
