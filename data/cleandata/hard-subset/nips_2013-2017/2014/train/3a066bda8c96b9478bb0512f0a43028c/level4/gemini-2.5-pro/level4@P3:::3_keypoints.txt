- Paper proposes e-SVM: an expectation-loss SVM for training with continuous "positiveness" scores in [0,1], modeling loss as expectation over a latent binary label.  
- Extends to latent e-SVM for weak supervision by treating positiveness scores as latent variables optimized jointly with model parameters.  
- Applied to semantic segmentation and object detection, using segment classifiers to produce spatial confidence maps that augment R-CNN features.  
- Empirically strong: achieves a 1.5% mAP improvement over R-CNN on PASCAL VOC 2007, showing segmentation-based confidence features are complementary to CNN features.  
- Practical problem formulation: addresses learning from weak/continuous supervision (e.g., IoU scores or bounding boxes), which is valuable for large-scale vision tasks.  
- Introduces two new evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification (TAPC and NDCG) and shows e-SVM outperforms SVC/SVR baselines on these.  
- Limited algorithmic novelty: e-SVM is essentially a weighted SVM and latent e-SVM resembles a weighted transductive SVM; paper understates prior connections.  
- Missing crucial references and discussion linking to weighted SVM and transductive SVM literature (e.g., Suykens et al. 2002; Yang et al. 2005; Vapnik 1998; Lapin et al. 2014).  
- Insufficient baselines: omits standard and strong baselines for [0,1] regression such as regularized logistic regression.  
- Clarity and positioning issues: title/abstract overemphasize a novel algorithm while main contribution is an effective feature-engineering application; exposition disproportionately details known SVM derivations.  
- Reproducibility concerns: lacks details on selection of regularization parameters (λW, λR) and other implementation specifics.  
- Important method details (e.g., spatial confidence map generation) are underdescribed relative to their centrality to results.  
- Minor editorial errors and ambiguous captions/phrasing (several typos, unclear use of "AP", possible Figure 3 caption inconsistency).  
- Significance is high due to strong empirical gains, but acceptance should be contingent on reframing contributions, adding comparisons to suggested baselines, and thoroughly citing/prior-work alignment.
