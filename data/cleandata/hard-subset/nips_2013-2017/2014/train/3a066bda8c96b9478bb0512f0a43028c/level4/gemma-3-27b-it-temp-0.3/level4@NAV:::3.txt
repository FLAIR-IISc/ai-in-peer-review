## Review:

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a scheme for both semantic labeling and object detection. Semantic labeling is addressed by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then performing regression to predict the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between candidate segments and ground truth pixel labels. For object detection, the approach leverages weaker bounding box labels, treating the precise spatial extent as a latent variable. Regression is implemented using a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The reported results achieve performance competitive with the state-of-the-art in semantic labeling, and represent the best currently published performance on the VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Originality:**

The algorithmic novelty presented is limited, and the manuscript unfortunately lacks citations to relevant prior work. Specifically, the use of a weighted SVM is a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not cited). While the authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights – which may represent a novel contribution (though not exhaustively investigated) – the proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted variant of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited). 

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Significance:**

The achievement of superior performance to recent Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) results using a “conventional” learning approach is a noteworthy result and of considerable interest to the research community.

**Quality:**

A significant weakness of the paper is the omission of crucial references to existing work (as detailed above). The object detection framework requires substantial implementation effort, but falls within the typical scope of the field. Given that (regularized) logistic regression is the standard approach for regression on the unit interval, results using this method should be included as a baseline to justify the adoption of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide further details regarding the selection of regularization coefficients λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>. The primary strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not effectively convey the core contributions of the work. The abstract also incorrectly implies that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships; the latter can always be derived from the former through thresholding. For a NeurIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to page 4) to minor variations of established algorithms, while comparatively glossing over the critical details of feature engineering (lines 254ff).

**Minor comments:**

Line 19: “continues” should be “continuous”
Line 94: “boxes” should be “box”
Line 98: “AP” – Abbreviation not introduced
Line 350: “SVM” should be “SVC”
Line 350: “with every different values” should be “for all threshold values”



I have increased my quality assessment, anticipating that the authors will address the relationship to prior work constructively and expand upon it. Overall, this is a strong paper with excellent results, but suffers from insufficient referencing and limited algorithmic novelty. It is suitable for publication, potentially with revisions, and may be particularly well-suited for a computer vision conference.
