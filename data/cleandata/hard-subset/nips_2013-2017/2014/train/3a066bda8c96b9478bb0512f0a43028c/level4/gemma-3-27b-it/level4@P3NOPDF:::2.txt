This paper proposes a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) learning algorithm tailored for weakly supervised data. The core idea is to incorporate per-example weights – values between 0 and 1 – into the SVM loss function, allowing the algorithm to prioritize more reliable training instances. The method is further extended to a scenario where these per-example weights are not directly observed, becoming latent variables inferred during learning.

The paper is clearly written and the proposed method is well-described. The experimental results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks demonstrate that incorporating these weights can yield performance improvements compared to standard SVM learning with uniform weighting. However, a significant concern is the novelty of this approach. The concept of assigning example-dependent costs or weights is well-established in the machine learning community. Existing tools like SVMlight explicitly support this functionality, and the proposed weighted SVM appears largely equivalent to utilizing these existing features. A clearer differentiation from prior work is crucial.

The extension to latent per-example weights is an interesting contribution. However, this introduces non-convexity into the optimization problem, and the paper lacks any theoretical analysis regarding convergence or optimality. The strong regularization employed – effectively constraining the per-example weights to remain close to their initial values – suggests a reliance on prior knowledge to guide the learning process and avoid getting stuck in poor local optima. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the implications of this strong regularization, perhaps analyzing the sensitivity of the results to the regularization strength. Further, the authors should comment on why standard techniques for non-convex optimization were not explored or deemed insufficient in this context.

I have a few specific comments. On line 50, the authors state that the problem “is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1].” This statement is inaccurate; logistic regression is a standard regression technique specifically designed for predicting values within the [0, 1] interval. Furthermore, there is a minor typographical error in the abstract where “continues” should be “continuous”.

In summary, while the paper presents a functional extension to the SVM algorithm and demonstrates empirical improvements, its core contribution – weighted SVM learning – lacks clear novelty given existing approaches. The extension to latent weights introduces complexity but lacks theoretical grounding. Addressing the questions of novelty and theoretical justification, along with clarifying the role of the strong regularization, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend further review contingent on the authors providing a more compelling argument for the distinctiveness of their approach and a deeper analysis of the latent weight formulation.
