

The paper introduces a modified SVM learning framework incorporating per-example weights (in [0,1]) to handle weakly supervised data, extending naturally to cases where weights are latent variables. The method is clearly presented, and experimental results on semantic segmentation and object detection suggest potential benefits over uniform weighting. However, the core contribution appears largely subsumed by established "example-dependent cost" mechanisms already implemented in standard SVM solvers like SVMlight, raising significant novelty concerns. The authors should explicitly address how their formulation differs from these well-known approaches and justify its necessity.

The extension to latent weights is conceptually straightforward but lacks theoretical grounding. The non-convex nature of the latent-weight optimization is acknowledged, yet no analysis of convergence, optimality guarantees, or theoretical properties is provided. The reliance on strong L2 regularization to constrain weight deviations from initial values (e.g., near 1) suggests the solution is effectively a perturbation of standard SVM, but this critical assumption requires justification. The authors should clarify: (1) whether the regularization is essential for tractability or reflects a modeling choice, and (2) how this approach compares theoretically to alternatives like expectation-maximization for latent weights.

Minor issues require correction:  
- Line 50's claim that "it is not a standard regression problem since positivity belongs to [0,1]" is incorrect. Logistic regression is a standard bounded [0,1] model, making this assertion misleading.  
- The abstract contains a spelling error: "continues" should be "continuous."

While the empirical results are promising for specific tasks, the paper's primary contribution—weighting examples via modified SVM loss—lacks sufficient differentiation from existing practice. The experimental validation should explicitly benchmark against SVMlight's built-in example-dependent costs to demonstrate added value. Without this, the work risks being perceived as a reimplementation of known techniques rather than a novel advancement. The authors must strengthen the novelty argument and address the theoretical gaps in the latent-weight formulation to justify publication.
