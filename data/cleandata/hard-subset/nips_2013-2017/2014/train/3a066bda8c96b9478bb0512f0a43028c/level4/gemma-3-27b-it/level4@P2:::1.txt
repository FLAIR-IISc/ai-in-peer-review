This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed to handle scenarios where training data is labeled with continuous “positiveness” values rather than binary labels. The authors effectively apply this approach to visual segmentation tasks, particularly under weak supervision, and demonstrate promising results in both semantic segmentation and object detection.

The paper addresses a significant challenge in computer vision: learning from weakly labeled data. Manually annotating large datasets with pixel-level precision is costly, making algorithms capable of leveraging weaker forms of supervision (e.g., bounding boxes) highly valuable. The e-SVM algorithm is well-motivated, clearly presented, and tackles this problem with a sound theoretical foundation. The experimental validation is thorough, showcasing the method’s effectiveness on standard benchmarks like PASCAL VOC. 

The core idea of the e-SVM—extending the standard SVM framework to accommodate continuous positiveness scores—is a logical and valuable contribution. While conceptually related to multiple instance learning, the e-SVM distinguishes itself by handling a soft weighting of positives rather than relying on a strict binary classification. The ability to operate effectively in both fully-supervised (positiveness observed) and weakly-supervised (positiveness latent) settings is a notable strength. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from related approaches like SVC and SVR, and convincingly demonstrate its superiority through comprehensive experiments. Achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a particularly strong result.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a critical area of research in computer vision.
*   **Clear and well-motivated:** The paper provides a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and its advantages.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the e-SVM on several benchmarks, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance.
*   **Theoretical soundness:** The algorithm builds upon a solid foundation in SVM theory and provides a principled approach to handling continuous labels.
*   **Generality:** The method’s ability to work with different segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers increases its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the method is sound and effective, the core innovation (extending SVM to continuous labels) is relatively incremental. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this differs significantly from existing weighted SVM techniques ([23, 27]).
*   **Regularization Details:** The regularization term used in the latent e-SVM model (Equation 3) could benefit from more justification and exploration of alternative regularization strategies. The authors mention it prevents sticking but don’t provide empirical evidence.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly in the latent variable setting. It would be useful to compare its efficiency to other weakly supervised methods.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in computer vision.
*   The proposed method is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results.
*   The e-SVM algorithm represents a valid and potentially useful contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning.
*   The presentation is clear and concise, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The core innovation is relatively incremental, and the paper could benefit from a more in-depth comparison to related work, particularly weighted SVMs.
*   The regularization term in the latent variable setting lacks detailed justification.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the contribution is not groundbreaking, it is a solid and well-executed piece of work that addresses an important problem and offers a practical solution. The clear presentation, thorough experiments, and promising results make it a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a future revision would further strengthen the paper.
