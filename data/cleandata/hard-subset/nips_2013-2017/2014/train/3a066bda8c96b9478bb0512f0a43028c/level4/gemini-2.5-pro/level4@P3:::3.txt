### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for binary classification problems where training labels are not strictly binary (-1/+1) but are instead represented by a continuous "positiveness" score within the [0, 1] interval. This scenario is common in computer vision, where, for instance, the quality of a segment proposal can be measured by its Intersection-over-Union (IoU) with a ground-truth object mask.

The core idea of e-SVM is to formulate the SVM loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the probability of the label being positive is a function of the known "positiveness" score. This results in a weighted SVM formulation where each training example contributes to both the positive and negative class hinge loss terms, weighted by its positiveness score.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting with the latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM model parameters, akin to a transductive SVM.

The proposed method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, the e-SVM is used to train segment classifiers that produce a "spatial confidence map." This map is used as a new feature and concatenated with features from the R-CNN model. The combined approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark, outperforming the strong R-CNN baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **Significant Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its impressive performance on a challenging and standard benchmark. Achieving a 1.5% absolute improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) over the state-of-the-art R-CNN model on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a substantial contribution and demonstrates the practical value of the proposed approach. This suggests that the segmentation-based confidence features provide valuable information that is complementary to the R-CNN features.

2.  **Practical Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem. In many large-scale vision tasks, obtaining perfect, pixel-accurate labels is prohibitively expensive. The ability to learn effectively from weaker forms of supervision, such as bounding boxes or continuous quality scores of proposals, is highly valuable.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation of the Core Task:** The authors introduce two new and sensible evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification task, providing a direct and task-agnostic way to measure performance. The experiments convincingly show that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines in this context.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Originality and Missing References:** The core algorithmic ideas are not new, and the paper fails to cite or discuss crucial prior work. The proposed e-SVM is a specific instance of a weighted SVM, a well-established technique. Similarly, the latent e-SVM, which optimizes over latent labels, is conceptually a weighted version of a transductive SVM. The lack of discussion on this front misrepresents the paper's novelty.

2.  **Insufficient Baselines:** For the core problem of regressing a value on the unit interval [0, 1], a standard and strong baseline would be (regularized) logistic regression. This comparison is missing, making it harder to assess whether the specific e-SVM formulation is superior to other standard approaches for this type of target variable.

3.  **Lack of Clarity in Positioning and Exposition:** The title, abstract, and introduction frame the work as a novel learning algorithm. However, the main contribution appears to be a clever and effective engineering application of existing machine learning concepts to create powerful features for object detection. The paper spends significant space detailing minor variations of well-known SVMs, which a NIPS audience would be familiar with, but is comparatively brief on the feature engineering details that are critical to the method's success.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its formulation and the empirical results are very strong. However, the quality is diminished by the missing citations to fundamental work and the absence of a key baseline (logistic regression). Furthermore, details regarding the selection of regularization parameters λW and λR are not provided, which hinders reproducibility. The main strength lies in the excellent empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper could be significantly improved in clarity.
*   The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the core contribution. They emphasize a general learning algorithm, whereas the most significant result comes from its specific application as a feature generator for object detection.
*   The abstract misleadingly insinuates that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary labels. This is incorrect, as a binary label can always be derived from a continuous one via thresholding.
*   For a machine learning audience, too much space is dedicated to deriving the weighted SVM formulation (Sections 3.1 and 3.2), while the novel and critical aspects, such as the spatial confidence map feature generation (Section 4.2), are covered relatively quickly.

**Originality:** The algorithmic novelty is limited. The paper's contribution lies in the novel application of these ideas rather than the invention of a new algorithm. The failure to cite and position the work relative to the existing literature on weighted and transductive SVMs is a major weakness. The following references are highly relevant and should have been discussed:
*   [R1] Suykens et al. "Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation," Neurocomputing, 2002.
*   [R2] X. Yang et al. "Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification," IJCNN, 2005.
*   [R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory," 1998 (for transductive SVMs).
*   [R4] M. Lapin et al. "Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM," Neural Networks, 2014.

**Significance:** The significance of the paper is high due to its state-of-the-art results. Outperforming a strong CNN-based method like R-CNN using features derived from a more "conventional" learning approach is an important finding that will be of great interest to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The results demonstrate that careful feature engineering based on segmentation cues can still provide substantial gains, even in the era of deep learning.

### Minor Comments
*   Line 19: "continues" -> "continuous"
*   Line 94: "bounding boxes annotations" -> "bounding box annotations"
*   Line 98: The abbreviation "AP" (Average Precision) should be introduced on its first use.
*   Figure 3 caption: The SVC results are compared against models "trained with... weakly-labelled bounding-box annotation". Is this a typo for SVC? The text implies SVC is trained by thresholding the IoU, which is not available in the weakly-labeled case. Please clarify.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a well-engineered system that achieves excellent results on a competitive task. The empirical contribution is significant. However, the paper suffers from a serious lack of engagement with prior literature, which overstates its algorithmic novelty. While the results are compelling, the paper would need substantial revision to be acceptable.

My recommendation leans towards acceptance, contingent on the authors thoroughly addressing the issues raised, especially regarding the relationship to prior work. The authors should reframe the paper to accurately reflect its contribution as a successful application and extension of known techniques that leads to state-of-the-art performance, rather than as the invention of a fundamentally new algorithm. A detailed discussion comparing e-SVM to weighted SVMs and latent e-SVM to transductive SVMs, along with the inclusion of the suggested baselines, would greatly strengthen the paper.
