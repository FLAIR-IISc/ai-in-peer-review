Summary: 
The authors propose a framework for both semantic labeling and object detection tasks. The semantic labeling is achieved by generating candidate segments, extracting features from these segments, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and the pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker labels in the form of bounding boxes are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. The authors employ a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM for regression in these tasks, respectively. The results obtained are comparable to the state of the art in semantic labeling and surpass the current best results for object detection on the VOC 2007 dataset.

Originality:
While the proposed approach yields impressive results, the algorithmic novelty is limited. Unfortunately, the authors fail to acknowledge pertinent previous work in the field. Specifically, the concept of weighted SVM is well-established (e.g., [R1,R2,R4]), and the authors do not provide citations for these works. The introduction of a sum constraint on the weights may be a novel contribution, although a thorough literature search is required to confirm this. Furthermore, the proposed "latent e-SVM" is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3]), which is not cited.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
The fact that the authors' "conventional" learning approach outperforms recent CNN-based results is a notable achievement and of significant interest to the community.

Quality:
A major concern is the lack of crucial references to previous work, which undermines the paper's credibility. The object detection framework, while involving some engineering efforts, is not overly complex. However, the authors should provide a baseline comparison using the default method for regression on the unit interval, namely (regularized) logistic regression, to justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. Furthermore, the authors should provide more details on the selection process for the regularization coefficients λW and λR. The paper's main strength lies in its impressive empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract fail to accurately summarize the paper's core contribution. The abstract also contains a misleading statement, suggesting that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships. However, the latter can be obtained from the former through thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes excessive space to explaining minor variations of well-known algorithms (up to page 4) and glosses over the critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

Minor comments:
Line 19: "continues" should be replaced with "continuous."
Line 94: "boxes" should be replaced with "box."
Line 98: The abbreviation "AP" is not introduced.
Line 350: "SVM" should be replaced with "SVC," and "with every different values" should be rephrased as "for all threshold values."

Given the paper's strong results and assuming the authors will address the deficiencies in referencing previous work, I have increased the quality score by one. The paper presents great results, but is lacking in references to earlier work and algorithmic novelty. It is a paper that deserves publication, possibly after some rewriting, and perhaps in a computer vision conference.
