Summary: 
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. The approach involves generating candidate segments and extracting features for these; then, regression is performed on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. In cases where only weaker labels, such as bounding boxes, are available, the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. Results are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling, and the authors achieve best-known performance for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and the authors do not cite pertinent previous work. In particular, the weighted SVM is a well-established technique (e.g., [R1, R2, R4] below, not cited). The authors introduce a sum constraint on the weights, which may be novel, though an extensive literature search was not conducted to confirm this. The “latent e-SVM” proposed by the authors is a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited). 
[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:
Outperforming recent CNN results with a "conventional" learning approach is a significant achievement and of great interest to the community. 

Quality:
Crucial references to previous work are missing (see above). The object detection framework involves a reasonable amount of engineering, consistent with work in the field. The default method for regression on the unit interval is (regularized) logistic regression; reporting results using this baseline would strengthen the justification for employing a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should provide more details regarding the selection of regularization coefficients λw and λr. The main strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not adequately summarize the core contribution of the paper. The abstract also suggests that regression targets from the unit interval are somehow less informative than binary class memberships, which is inaccurate, as the latter can always be obtained from the former through thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms while discussing feature engineering details (lines 254ff) somewhat quickly.

Arguments pro acceptance:
* Strong empirical performance, outperforming state-of-the-art methods on object detection.
* Clear presentation of the method and experimental setup.
* Demonstrates the effectiveness of weakly supervised learning approaches.

Arguments con acceptance:
* Lack of thorough citation of relevant previous work.
* Limited algorithmic novelty.
* The paper would benefit from a more concise presentation of background material.
