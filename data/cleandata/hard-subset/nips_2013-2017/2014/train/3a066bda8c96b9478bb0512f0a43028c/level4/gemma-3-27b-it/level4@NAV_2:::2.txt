The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to address learning from weakly supervised data where training labels are “positiveness” values rather than binary classifications. The core idea involves weighting each training example within the SVM loss function by its positiveness value, effectively treating it as a confidence score. This is further extended to a latent variable setting where the positiveness is unobserved, requiring iterative optimization. The algorithm is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance, particularly in object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.

This work builds upon the established need for learning algorithms capable of leveraging weakly labeled data, a critical challenge given the expense of fully annotated datasets [6]. The proposed e-SVM offers a direct approach to incorporating confidence information inherent in weakly supervised signals, contrasting with ad-hoc binarization heuristics applied to standard SVMs [18] or the use of SVR, which may not be ideally suited for outputs bounded within [0,1] [4, 18]. The extension to the latent variable setting is a practical consideration for scenarios where positiveness values are unavailable, mirroring real-world data acquisition challenges.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear motivation, well-defined algorithm, and strong empirical validation on benchmark datasets. The consistent outperformance of e-SVM compared to SVC and SVR in segment proposal classification, coupled with state-of-the-art object detection results, demonstrates the practical efficacy of the approach. The presented experimental framework appears rigorous, and the inclusion of results under both strong and weak supervision is valuable. The method’s generality – applicable to various segment proposal detectors, image features, and base detectors – is also a notable strength.

However, the paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its relationship to existing weighted SVM methods [23, 27]. While the authors acknowledge these methods, the unique contribution of weighting *labels* rather than data points could be more explicitly articulated and differentiated. The regularization strategy employed in the latent variable setting is crucial for convergence, and a deeper theoretical justification or sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter would be beneficial. Further, while the claim regarding logistic regression being non-standard for bounded outputs is acknowledged as a minor correction, a clearer discussion of why standard regression approaches are less suitable, beyond just the bounded interval, would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated strong performance on benchmark tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results in object detection.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem – learning from weakly supervised data.
*   Clearly defined algorithm with a straightforward extension to handle latent variables.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in the core idea, as it shares similarities with existing weighted SVM approaches.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the latent variable setting, relying heavily on empirical observation and regularization.
*   A more detailed comparison to related work on weighted SVMs and discussion of their differences would strengthen the contribution.
