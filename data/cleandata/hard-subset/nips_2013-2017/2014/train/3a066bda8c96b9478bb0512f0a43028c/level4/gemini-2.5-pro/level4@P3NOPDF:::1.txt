This paper proposes an expectation loss support vector machine (e-SVM) designed to handle positive instances with continuous attributes in a weakly supervised learning setting. The authors apply this model to the challenging problem of learning visual segmentation classifiers using only image-level labels, effectively treating object locations and bounding boxes as latent variables. The methodology is validated on standard benchmarks for both semantic segmentation and object detection, where it achieves promising results.

The paper effectively addresses the significant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision, which is crucial for reducing the reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations in computer vision. The motivation for the e-SVM framework is well-articulated, and the overall presentation is clear and easy to follow. The experiments are interesting, relevant, and well-executed, providing compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.

From a technical standpoint, the e-SVM can be viewed as an elegant extension of the standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework. Where traditional MIL often assumes a binary selection of the "true" positive instance within a bag, this work introduces a more flexible soft weighting. This is accomplished by framing the objective as an expectation over the loss across all potential positive instances, which is a natural and effective way to model the continuous attributes (e.g., location, scale) of objects. A notable strength of this formulation is its applicability to both the observed setting, where attributes are known, and the more complex latent setting, where they must be inferred during training.

While the technical contribution builds upon established ideas, its generalization of the MIL framework to incorporate an expected loss over continuous variables is a solid and valuable contribution. The successful application to weakly supervised segmentation and detection demonstrates its practical utility. In summary, this is a well-motivated paper with a clear presentation and strong empirical results, making it a valid and interesting contribution for the NIPS conference.
