This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, termed the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning problems where labels are not binary but are instead represented by a continuous "positiveness" score within the range [0, 1]. The core idea is to reframe the SVM hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the probability of the label being positive is given by the observed positiveness score. This formulation effectively weights the positive and negative hinge loss components for each training example based on its positiveness.

The authors extend this model to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness scores are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available instead of pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are learned jointly with the classifier's parameters via an alternating optimization procedure. The proposed methods are evaluated extensively on the computer vision tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. The experiments demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines and that integrating features from the latent e-SVM improves the performance of a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN) on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### Strengths

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is described clearly. The motivation for handling continuous-valued "positiveness" labels is well-argued.
*   **Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is a significant strength. The experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection are thorough and demonstrate that the proposed approach has practical value. Achieving a 1.5% average AP improvement over a very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a notable result.
*   **Problem Formulation:** The problem of learning from continuous confidence scores instead of hard binary labels is relevant and important, particularly in computer vision, where precise annotations are expensive to obtain.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Originality and Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern is the novelty of the proposed e-SVM model. The concept of applying instance-dependent costs to the SVM loss function is well-established in the machine learning literature and is a standard feature in many SVM solvers (e.g., SVMlight). While the paper positions the method as weighting labels rather than data points, the resulting objective function is closely related to existing weighted SVM formulations. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion clarifying the fundamental differences between e-SVM and standard instance-weighted SVMs. What is the specific advantage of decomposing the cost for each example into separate weights for the positive and negative loss terms, as opposed to applying a single, example-dependent cost C_i to the standard hinge loss?

2.  **Analysis of the Latent Model:** The extension to the latent e-SVM is interesting but appears to be a relatively straightforward application of alternating optimization for a non-convex problem. The paper provides no theoretical analysis regarding the convergence of this procedure or the properties of its local minima. Furthermore, the optimization relies on a strong regularization term to prevent the latent positiveness values `u` from deviating far from their initial estimates `u_bb`. This raises a question about how much is truly being learned. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the model to the regularization parameter `lambda_R`? It would be valuable to see an analysis of how much the learned `u` values differ from their initializations, to better understand whether the model is simply refining initial guesses or discovering genuinely new latent structure.

3.  **Minor Points:**
    *   On line 50 of the introduction, the paper claims that this is "not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]". This statement is questionable. Standard techniques like logistic regression are explicitly designed to model probabilities in the [0, 1] interval and are widely used.
    *   There is a minor typo in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

### Summary

In summary, this paper presents a well-described modification of the SVM algorithm for weakly supervised learning, supported by strong empirical results on challenging computer vision tasks. The performance gains, particularly in object detection, are impressive. However, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing concerns about the novelty of the core formulation relative to established instance-weighted SVMs and by providing a more rigorous analysis of the non-convex latent variable model. Clarification on these points would help to better situate the paper's contribution within the existing literature.
