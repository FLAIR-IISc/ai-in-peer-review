

The authors introduce an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where each training sample is associated with a continuous "positiveness" value (representing the degree of affiliation to the positive class) rather than a binary label. This work extends beyond standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) frameworks, which typically assume at least one "completely positive" instance in a positive bag, by effectively handling cases where proposals contain both foreground and background pixels (0 < positiveness < 1). The e-SVM model handles both observed positiveness (strong supervision) and unobserved positiveness (weak supervision) by treating positiveness as latent variables. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of e-SVM for segment proposal classification and applies it to two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. On PASCAL VOC 2007, the authors achieve state-of-the-art object detection performance with a 1.5% improvement in average precision over the baseline RCNN method.

The paper excels in multiple dimensions. Quality is strong: the technical formulation is sound, with a clear derivation of the expectation loss function, and the experimental evaluation is thorough with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that directly measure the confidence prediction task. Clarity is excellent, with a well-organized presentation that makes the technical content accessible, including detailed explanations of the latent e-SVM extension. Originality is well-established through the novel application of continuous positiveness values in a learning framework, which represents a meaningful extension of existing weighted SVM approaches (distinguishing between label weighting versus data point weighting). Significance is compelling, as the problem of learning under weak supervision is increasingly important with the growing size of image datasets, and the results demonstrate tangible improvements over state-of-the-art methods in both semantic segmentation and object detection, with the method achieving state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed comparison with related weighted SVM approaches, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability. Additionally, while the authors mention using bounding box annotations from large datasets like ImageNet, a more concrete discussion of how this would be implemented in practice would further enhance the significance of the work. The authors also briefly mention potential applications beyond computer vision, but a more detailed discussion of these broader implications would strengthen the paper's impact.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses an important problem in computer vision with a technically sound approach. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission, with clear significance, good quality, strong clarity, and meaningful originality. I recommend acceptance.
