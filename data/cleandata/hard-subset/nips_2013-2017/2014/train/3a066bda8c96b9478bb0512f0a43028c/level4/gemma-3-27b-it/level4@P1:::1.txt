The authors propose an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed to handle continuous values representing the confidence of an instance belonging to the positive class. This is particularly relevant in scenarios with weak supervision, where precise labels are unavailable. They apply the method to learn visual segmentation classifiers and validate it on both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving promising results. The paper addresses an important problem in visual learning – leveraging weakly supervised data – and the presentation is clear and well-motivated. The experiments are interesting and well-executed. While the technical contribution builds upon the standard multiple instance learning framework by introducing a continuous weighting of positives instead of a binary assignment, its applicability to both observed and latent settings represents a valid contribution to NIPS.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), tailored for problems where training data is associated with a “positiveness” value—a continuous measure of the likelihood of belonging to the positive class—rather than a binary label. This approach is especially useful in weakly supervised learning scenarios. The e-SVM is demonstrated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, showing state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection dataset. The method extends naturally to handle latent positiveness values when only weak supervision (e.g., bounding boxes) is available.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is crucial for scaling computer vision applications to large datasets where manual annotation is prohibitive.
*   **Clear and Well-Motivated:** The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivates the proposed approach.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper presents thorough experiments on established datasets (PASCAL VOC) and tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of e-SVM.
*   **State-of-the-Art Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance is a significant accomplishment.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The extension of SVMs to handle continuous positiveness values appears well-reasoned and mathematically sound.
*   **Flexibility:** The method's adaptability to both observed and latent positiveness values, as well as its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors and image features, increases its general applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The core concept of weighting samples based on confidence is not entirely new (see weighted SVMs [23, 27]). The main contribution lies in the specific application to this "positiveness" setting and the formulation as an expectation loss.
*   **Regularization Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the latent e-SVM model relies on the regularization term for the positiveness values. The choice of this regularization term and its associated parameters might be sensitive and require careful tuning. More discussion on the sensitivity of this parameter would be helpful.
*   **Complexity of Spatial Confidence Map:** While effective, the method for incorporating spatial confidence maps in the object detection task seems somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed analysis of the impact of the bin size (m=5) and the pooling method could strengthen the argument.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work on confidence estimation in object detection. For example, methods that directly predict confidence scores alongside bounding box coordinates could be discussed and compared.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in computer vision.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The ability to handle weak supervision makes the approach practical for real-world applications.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is not radically novel and builds upon existing work in weighted SVMs and multiple instance learning.
*   Some aspects of the method (e.g., spatial confidence map construction) appear somewhat heuristic.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While the core idea is not entirely groundbreaking, the specific formulation, the thorough experimental validation, and the state-of-the-art results justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper is well-written, and the authors have clearly demonstrated a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the regularization parameter sensitivity and clarifying the spatial confidence map construction).
