

Summary:  
The authors propose a framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, they generate candidate segments, extract features, and regress the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between each segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection—where only bounding box labels are available—they treat precise spatial extent as a latent variable and use a weighted SVM and weighted transductive SVM for regression, respectively. Results match state-of-the-art performance on semantic labeling and set a new benchmark for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.  

Originality:  
Algorithmic novelty is limited. Crucially, the authors omit key prior work. The weighted SVM they employ is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], not cited). Their sum constraint on weights may be novel (though I did not conduct an exhaustive literature search). The "latent e-SVM" is merely a weighted variant of transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).  

[R1] Suykens et al., *Weighted least squares support vector machines*, Neurocomputing, 2002  
[R2] Yang et al., *Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification*, IJCNN, 2005  
[R3] Vapnik, *Statistical Learning Theory*, 1998  
[R4] Lapin et al., *Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM*, Neural Networks, 2014  

Significance:  
Outperforming recent CNN-based methods with a conventional approach is a notable achievement and holds significant interest for the community.  

Quality:  
Key references are omitted (see above). While the object detection framework involves standard engineering, the authors should report baseline results using regularized logistic regression (the standard approach for unit-interval regression) to justify their sum-constrained weighted SVM. They also need to clarify how they selected regularization parameters $\lambda_W$ and $\lambda_R$. The paper’s main strength lies in its strong empirical results.  

Clarity:  
The title and abstract misrepresent the core contribution. The abstract incorrectly suggests that regression targets on the unit interval are less informative than binary class labels—a claim that is false, as binary labels can always be derived from regression outputs via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space (up to page 4) to minor variations of established algorithms while skipping over critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have increased my quality score by one point, assuming the authors will address the omitted references and clarify connections to prior work. The empirical results are strong, but the insufficient citations and minimal algorithmic novelty require revision. This paper merits publication after thorough rewriting, though it may be better suited for a computer vision venue.
