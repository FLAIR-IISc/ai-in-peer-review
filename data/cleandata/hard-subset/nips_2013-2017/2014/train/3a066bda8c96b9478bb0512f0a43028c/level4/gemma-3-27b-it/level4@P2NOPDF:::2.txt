This paper introduces a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data. The core idea is to incorporate per-example weights – values between 0 and 1 – into the SVM loss function, effectively modulating the contribution of each training instance. The authors further extend this approach to handle scenarios where these weights are not directly observed, treating them instead as latent variables.

The paper is clearly written and the proposed method is well-explained. Empirical results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks suggest potential benefits from using this weighted approach compared to standard SVM training with uniform example weighting. However, a significant concern is the novelty of the proposed method. The concept of weighting examples based on their perceived reliability or cost is not new; equivalent techniques, often termed “example-dependent costs,” are already well-established and readily available in machine learning packages such as SVMlight. The authors should better articulate the specific advantages of their formulation compared to these existing methods.

The extension to latent per-example weights is a potentially interesting direction, but the resulting optimization problem is non-convex. The paper lacks theoretical analysis regarding convergence or optimality in this case, and the strong regularization employed – which appears to constrain the learned weights close to their initial values – raises questions about the method’s flexibility and potential to truly discover informative weights. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the rationale for this strong regularization and its impact on the learned solution. A discussion of alternative regularization strategies, or an analysis of sensitivity to the regularization parameter, would strengthen the paper.

Several minor points require attention. On line 50, the statement that the problem "is also not a standard regression problem" because the output is bounded between 0 and 1 is inaccurate. Logistic regression, a standard regression technique, inherently produces outputs within the [0, 1] interval. Additionally, a minor typographical error exists in the abstract where “continues” should be “continuous.” 

In summary, while the paper presents a well-described method and demonstrates promising empirical results, the lack of clear differentiation from existing techniques and the absence of theoretical justification for the latent variable extension diminish its overall contribution. Addressing these points would significantly improve the paper’s impact.
