This paper presents a modification to the Support Vector Machine (SVM) framework designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios. The core idea is to introduce a continuous, per-example weight (bounded between 0 and 1) that modulates the contribution of each training example's loss to the overall objective function. The authors first consider the case where these weights are known and then extend the model to a latent variable setting where the weights are learned jointly with the SVM parameters.

The paper is clearly written, and the proposed method is described in sufficient detail. The empirical evaluation, conducted on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrates that the proposed weighted approach can outperform a standard SVM that treats all examples equally.

Despite these strengths, there are several significant concerns that should be addressed:

1.  **Novelty of the Core Contribution:** My primary concern is the novelty of the proposed method when the weights are known. The idea of re-weighting the loss for individual examples is conceptually equivalent to the well-established technique of using "instance-dependent" or "example-dependent costs" in SVMs. This functionality has been a standard feature in popular SVM implementations, such as SVMlight, for many years. The authors should explicitly differentiate their contribution from this existing body of work and clarify what is fundamentally new about their formulation in this supervised case.

2.  **Analysis of the Latent Variable Model:** The extension where weights are treated as latent variables is interesting. However, the resulting optimization problem is non-convex, and the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of its properties (e.g., convergence guarantees, characterization of local minima). The authors employ a strong regularization term on the weights, which penalizes deviation from their initial values. This practical solution raises a question about the extent to which the weights are truly being *learned* versus being only slightly perturbed from their initialization. The authors should provide more analysis or discussion on the behavior of the optimization in this non-convex setting and the impact of this regularization.

**Minor Comments:**

*   On L050, the authors claim their problem is "not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]." This statement is potentially misleading, as standard and widely used models like logistic regression or beta regression are specifically designed for targets within this interval.
*   There is a minor typo in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".
