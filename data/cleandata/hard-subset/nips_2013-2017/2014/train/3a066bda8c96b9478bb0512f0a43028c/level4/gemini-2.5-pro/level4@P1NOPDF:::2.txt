This paper proposes a modification to the SVM learning algorithm to handle weakly supervised data. The core idea is to assign a continuous weight (between 0 and 1) to each training example, which then modulates its contribution to the SVM loss function. This method is further extended to a latent variable setting where these per-example weights are unobserved and learned jointly with the classifier.

The method is well-described, and the experimental results on semantic segmentation and object detection indicate that this approach can offer advantages over treating each example with equal weight. However, the distinction between the proposed method and existing techniques for "example-dependent costs," which are widely known and used in the machine learning community, is not clear. Standard SVM implementations, such as SVMlight, have long provided a mechanism for assigning different costs to individual examples. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could clarify the novelty of their approach in light of these established methods.

The extension to the case where the per-example weights are treated as unobserved latent variables is an interesting direction, though the formulation appears straightforward. This approach results in a non-convex optimization problem, for which the paper provides no theoretical analysis of convergence or solution quality. Notably, a strong regularization term is used, which suggests that the learned weights are constrained to not deviate far from their initial values. This raises questions about how much the weights are truly being learned from the data versus being determined by their initialization and the regularizer. The authors are encouraged to comment on the sensitivity to this regularization term and the challenges posed by the non-convexity of the problem.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The claim on line 50 that modeling the weights is "not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" seems inaccurate. Logistic regression, for example, is a very standard method whose output is bounded in the [0, 1] interval.
*   There is a minor spelling mistake in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous."
