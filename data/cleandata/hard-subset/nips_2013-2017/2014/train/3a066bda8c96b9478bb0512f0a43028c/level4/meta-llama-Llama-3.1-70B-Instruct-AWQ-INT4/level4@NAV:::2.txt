The paper introduces a novel modification to the SVM learning algorithm, designed to accommodate weakly supervised data. This approach involves assigning a per-example weight, ranging from 0 to 1, to each training example, which is then incorporated into the SVM loss function. The authors further extend this method to scenarios where the per-example weight is unobserved, treating it as a latent variable.

The proposed method is clearly described, and experimental results suggest its potential benefits over traditional approaches that assign equal weights to all examples, particularly in semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. However, the distinction between this method and existing "example-dependent costs" is not immediately apparent, as these are well-established and widely utilized in the machine learning community. SVMlight, for instance, offers a similar mechanism.

The extension to unobserved per-example weights is intriguing, albeit straightforward. Notably, this formulation yields a non-convex problem, and the authors do not provide a theoretical analysis. The use of a strong regularization term implies that the per-example weights remain relatively close to their initial values. It would be beneficial for the authors to provide insight into this aspect.

Additionally, the authors claim on Line 50 that the problem is not a standard regression problem due to the bounded interval [0, 1] of the positiveness. However, logistic regression is a standard approach that is indeed bounded to [0, 1].

Minor corrections include a spelling error in the abstract ("continues" instead of "continuous").

In summary, while the paper proposes a modified SVM learning algorithm with per-example weights, it bears a strong resemblance to existing example-dependent costs, which are widely used in machine learning.
