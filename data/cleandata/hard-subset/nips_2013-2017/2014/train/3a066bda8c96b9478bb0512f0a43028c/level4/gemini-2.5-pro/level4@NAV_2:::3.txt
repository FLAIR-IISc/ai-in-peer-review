**Summary**

This paper introduces a framework for semantic segmentation and object detection, centered around a novel expectation loss SVM (e-SVM). For semantic segmentation with pixel-level ground truth, the method generates segment proposals and uses the e-SVM to regress on their Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap. For object detection, where only bounding box annotations are available, the IoU is treated as a latent variable, and a latent e-SVM is employed. These models correspond to a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The method achieves competitive performance in semantic segmentation and state-of-the-art results for object detection on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Originality**

The algorithmic contributions of this paper are limited. The proposed "e-SVM" is a variant of the well-established weighted SVM (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]), and the "latent e-SVM" is effectively a weighted transductive SVM (e.g., [R3]). Critically, the paper fails to cite this body of pertinent previous work. The novelty appears to be confined to minor variations of existing techniques.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

**Significance**

The paper's primary significance lies in its empirical results. Surpassing the performance of recent, powerful CNN-based approaches on a standard object detection benchmark using a more "classical" machine learning framework is a notable achievement that will be of considerable interest to the research community.

**Quality**

The paper's main strength is its strong empirical performance. However, there are issues with the technical presentation. The omission of key citations to prior work on weighted and transductive SVMs is a major weakness. While the object detection pipeline involves significant engineering, this is typical for the field. To better justify the proposed model, the authors should include a comparison against a standard baseline for regression on the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression. Furthermore, the manuscript lacks details on the selection of the regularization hyperparameters λ_w and λ_R.

**Clarity**

The paper's clarity could be significantly improved. The title and abstract fail to accurately convey the paper's core contributions. The abstract makes a questionable claim that continuous labels in [0, 1] are somehow more difficult to handle than binary labels, which is incorrect as binary labels can be derived from continuous ones via thresholding. For a machine learning audience like NIPS, the paper dedicates excessive space to describing minor variations of standard SVMs, while providing insufficient detail on the critical feature engineering aspects (e.g., the spatial confidence map feature) that are likely key to the method's success.

**Minor Comments**
*   Abstract, Line 3: "continues value" should be "continuous value".
*   Section 3.1, paragraph 1: The paper states that binarizing the positiveness degrades performance and cites [18], but this point is not strongly supported or elaborated upon.
*   Section 4.2: The description of the spatial confidence map feature is important but somewhat brief. More detail on the choice of M=m x m bins and the pooling strategy would be beneficial.
*   Section 5.1.2: The table comparing TAPC and NDCG results is not numbered and lacks a proper caption, making it difficult to reference.
*   Section 5.1.2: The text refers to "figure 3 (a) and (b)" but no figures are included in the manuscript provided for review.

**Recommendation**

In summary, this paper presents impressive empirical results but suffers from a lack of algorithmic novelty and a failure to properly situate the work within the existing literature. The core methods are reformulations of well-known weighted and transductive SVMs, and the omission of citations to this prior work is a serious flaw. While the paper's strong results warrant publication, this should be conditional on a major revision that thoroughly discusses the connections to previous methods and addresses the clarity issues. Given the focus on application and feature engineering, the work might also be a strong fit for a dedicated computer vision venue.
