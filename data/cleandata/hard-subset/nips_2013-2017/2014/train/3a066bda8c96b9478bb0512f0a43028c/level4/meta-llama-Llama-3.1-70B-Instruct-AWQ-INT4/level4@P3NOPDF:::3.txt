Summary:

This paper presents a scheme for both semantic labeling and object detection. The authors accomplish semantic labeling by generating candidate segments, extracting features, and then regressing on the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker labels in the form of bounding boxes are available, the precise spatial extent is used as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM for object detection. The results are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and currently the best known for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:

While the paper presents a comprehensive approach to both semantic labeling and object detection, its algorithmic novelty is somewhat limited. Unfortunately, the authors fail to cite pertinent previous work, which is a significant oversight. Specifically, the concept of weighted SVMs is well-established (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]), and the authors do not provide sufficient recognition of this existing body of work. The addition of a sum constraint on the weights may be a novel contribution, although a thorough literature search would be necessary to confirm this. Furthermore, the "latent e-SVM" proposed by the authors is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3]), which is not a new concept. The authors should provide more context and acknowledge the existing research in this area.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

Significance:

The fact that the authors' "conventional" learning approach outperforms recent CNN results is a significant achievement and of great interest to the community. This demonstrates that traditional methods can still be highly effective in certain tasks, even in the face of deep learning advancements.

Quality:

The paper's quality is somewhat marred by the lack of crucial references to previous work. The object detection framework, while involving some engineering, does not present any groundbreaking new ideas. The choice of regression method on the unit interval is also not particularly innovative, as logistic regression is a common default approach. The authors should provide a baseline comparison to support their use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM. Additionally, more details on the selection of regularization coefficients λW and λR would be beneficial. The paper's main strength lies in its good empirical results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Clarity:

The title and abstract do not accurately summarize the core contribution of the paper. The abstract also contains a misleading statement regarding the informativeness of regression targets from the unit interval compared to binary class memberships. This is not accurate, as binary class memberships can always be obtained from regression targets through thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper spends too much space on well-known algorithms and not enough on the critical feature engineering details. The authors should consider revising the paper to better reflect its contributions and provide more clarity on the key aspects of their approach.

Overall, this is a paper that should be published, possibly after some rewriting, and potentially in a computer vision conference. The authors' results are impressive, but the lack of references to earlier work and limited algorithmic novelty are notable drawbacks.
