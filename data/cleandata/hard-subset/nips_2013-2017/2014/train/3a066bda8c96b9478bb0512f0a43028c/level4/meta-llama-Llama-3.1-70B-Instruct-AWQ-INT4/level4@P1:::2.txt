The paper proposes a modification to the SVM learning algorithm that deals with weakly supervised data. Specifically, a per-example weight (between 0 and 1) is assigned to each training example. Then the loss function of the SVM is modified to take into account this weight. The method is then extended to the case when the per-example weight is not observed (i.e., is a latent variable).

The method is well described and the experimental results indicate that, for the semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, there may be value in using such an approach over treating each example with equal weight. However, I fail to see the difference between the proposed method and existing "example dependent costs", which are widely known and used in the machine learning community. SVMlight, for example, provides such a mechanism.

The extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is interesting but straightforward. In this case the problem is non-convex and no theoretical analysis is given. Indeed a strong regularization term is used suggesting that the per-example weights do not deviate far from their initial values. Can the authors comment?

Minor comment: The claim on L050 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is not entirely accurate. Logistic regression is very much standard and is bounded to [0, 1].

Minor spelling mistake in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

In terms of the quality of the paper, I would say that it is technically sound but lacks novelty. The proposed method is well described and the experimental results are convincing. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and the similarity to existing methods are drawbacks.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well written and easy to follow. However, some of the sentences are quite long and convoluted, which can make them difficult to understand.

In terms of originality, I would say that the paper is not particularly original. The proposed method is similar to existing methods and the extension to the case when the per-example weight is not observed is straightforward.

In terms of significance, I would say that the paper is moderately significant. The experimental results indicate that the proposed method can be useful for certain tasks, but the lack of novelty and theoretical analysis reduces the significance of the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a method that can be useful for certain tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:

Overall, I would say that the paper is a solid but not outstanding contribution to the field. It is well written and easy to follow, but it lacks novelty and theoretical analysis.
