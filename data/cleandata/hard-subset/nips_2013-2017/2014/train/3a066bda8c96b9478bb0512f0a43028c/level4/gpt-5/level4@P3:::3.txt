Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning with “positiveness” scores in [0,1] instead of binary labels. The key idea is to treat the label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as Bernoulli with success probability µi = g(ui) (set to ui in experiments), and to minimize the expected hinge loss EYi[max(0, 1−Yi w⊤xi)], yielding a weighted sum of positive and negative hinge losses per example. This can be optimized with standard linear SVM solvers. The authors also introduce a latent variant for weak supervision, where the positiveness ui is unobserved and regularized toward the IoU with the ground-truth bounding box ubb via a convex KL-based penalty. They alternate between optimizing w and u. The method is applied to (i) segment proposal confidence prediction for semantic segmentation and (ii) object detection by converting segment confidences to spatial confidence map features and concatenating them with RCNN features. The paper also proposes task-agnostic evaluation criteria for segment confidence prediction (TAPC and NDCG). Experiments on PASCAL VOC show that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on proposal scoring, achieves competitive semantic segmentation performance, and improves RCNN detection AP on VOC 2007 by about 1.5 points with simple feature concatenation.

The formulation is closely related to weighted SVMs and to learning with soft/probabilistic labels, where examples contribute to both positive and negative margins with instance-dependent weights. The latent extension connects to transductive/latent SVM formulations and weak supervision. However, several pertinent references are missing or under-emphasized (see below).

Strengths
- Clear, practical formulation that reduces to expected hinge loss with soft labels and can be solved by off-the-shelf linear SVM software.
- Straightforward latent extension for weak supervision with a convex regularizer; alternating optimization is simple and scalable.
- Strong empirical results: consistent gains over SVC/SVR on proposal scoring; competitive segmentation; nontrivial gains on VOC 2007 detection by augmenting RCNN.
- The spatial confidence map is a simple, effective way to inject segmentation cues into window-based detectors.
- Introduction of TAPC and the use of NDCG to evaluate proposal scoring are sensible and task-agnostic.

Weaknesses
- Limited algorithmic novelty relative to prior work on weighted/soft-label SVMs and latent/transductive SVMs; differences are not clearly delineated.
- Missing baselines: logistic regression (Bernoulli cross-entropy) or other proper-scoring-rule regressions on [0,1] (e.g., calibrated logistic, beta regression), and ranking SVM for NDCG.
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection (λw, λR), initialization, convergence, and sensitivity; no ablation on the KL regularizer.
- The engineering choices in object detection (binning m, pooling type, use of different segment proposals) are not ablated; unclear how robust the gains are.
- Writing and references need polishing; some typos and unclear phrasing.

Quality
- Soundness: The expected hinge objective with soft labels is well-defined and convex in w for fixed u; the latent variant alternates between two convex subproblems when using a convex R(u). The KL regularizer is reasonable and prevents degenerate 0/1 solutions.
- Empirical support: Results are compelling, particularly the detection gains. To strengthen the case, please:
  - Add logistic regression (with L2) on [0,1] targets using cross-entropy with µi=ui, and a calibrated variant (e.g., Platt scaling on SVC outputs) as baselines; report calibration (ECE/reliability plots) if claiming probabilistic interpretability.
  - Include RankSVM for the ranking objective and discuss how TAPC/NDCG correlate with downstream segmentation performance.
  - Provide details and sensitivity analyses for λw and λR selection, initialization ubb, stopping criteria, and the number of alternations; include wall-clock comparisons versus SVC/SVR.
  - For detection, give per-class AP changes, significance where feasible, and ablations over m, pooling type, and segment proposals (CPMC vs superpixels). Clarify training/validation splits and whether hyperparameters are tuned on val only.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but could be tighter. The title/abstract should emphasize the “expected hinge loss with soft labels” and the latent extension for weak supervision, rather than implying that [0,1] targets are inherently problematic.
- Move some algorithmic variations to an appendix and expand the section on feature engineering and integration with detectors; Figure 2 should be captioned more explicitly.
- Notation: define all variables once (µi, ui, g(·), λw, λR) and be consistent (SVM vs SVC).
- Minor edits:
  - Line 19: “continues” → “continuous”
  - Line 94: “boxes” → “box”
  - Line 98: introduce “AP” on first use
  - Line 350: “SVM” → “SVC”
  - Line 350: “with every different values” → “for all threshold values”
- References: ensure completeness and correctness (several entries have missing years/pages). Please add or discuss closely related work:
  - Weighted/soft-label SVMs: Suykens et al., Neurocomputing 2002; Yang et al., IJCNN 2005; Lapin et al., Neural Networks 2014 (already cited as [17]).
  - Transductive/latent SVMs and learning with latent variables (e.g., Vapnik 1998; Felzenszwalb et al. for latent SVM in detection).
  - Proper probabilistic baselines on [0,1] (logistic regression).

Originality
- The expected hinge with soft labels and instance-dependent weighting is known; the paper’s contribution is a clean instantiation for segment scoring with a practical latent extension and a KL regularizer toward bounding-box IoU. The sum constraint/normalization of weights and the specific regularizer may be incremental novelties; please clarify what is new relative to prior weighted SVM formulations and to latent/transductive SVMs.

Significance
- The empirical improvements on a strong detector underscore the utility of integrating segmentation-derived soft cues, which is of interest to both vision and weak supervision communities. The formulation is general and could transfer to other domains where soft labels are available. The impact would be enhanced by stronger baselines and ablations, and by clearer positioning amid related work.

Pros
- Simple, scalable method; uses standard tooling.
- Solid gains in detection; consistent advantages over SVC/SVR for proposal scoring.
- Sensible weak-supervision treatment via latent u with convex regularization.
- General framework applicable beyond vision.

Cons
- Limited novelty; missing or under-emphasized related work.
- Missing logistic/ranking baselines and calibration analyses.
- Sparse details on hyperparameters, convergence, and ablations.
- Presentation and references need polishing.

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The empirical results are strong and the method is practical, but the paper should (i) better situate itself with respect to weighted/soft-label and latent/transductive SVM literature, (ii) add proper probabilistic and ranking baselines, and (iii) provide more detail and ablation on the latent optimization and detection integration. With these revisions, this would be a valuable contribution; it may also be a strong fit for a computer vision venue.
