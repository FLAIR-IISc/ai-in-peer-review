### **Summary**

This paper presents a learning framework for semantic segmentation and object detection, called the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM). The core idea is to handle training data where labels are not binary but are instead continuous "positiveness" values on the interval [0, 1] (specifically, the Intersection-over-Union overlap ratio). For semantic segmentation, where pixel-level ground truth is available, the method regresses on these positiveness values using a weighted SVM. For object detection, where only weaker bounding box labels are provided, the exact positiveness is treated as a latent variable, and the model is trained using a formulation akin to a weighted transductive SVM. The method achieves results that are on par with the state-of-the-art in semantic segmentation and, notably, sets a new state-of-the-art for object detection on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, outperforming recent deep learning approaches.

### **Originality**

The paper's algorithmic novelty is limited, and it unfortunately fails to cite or discuss highly relevant prior work.
*   The proposed e-SVM is a form of weighted SVM, a well-established concept in the literature (e.g., [R1, R2, R4]). This connection is not acknowledged. While the authors' specific formulation (e.g., the sum constraint on weights) may have novel aspects, the paper should be framed in the context of this existing work.
*   The "latent e-SVM" for the weakly supervised case appears to be a weighted version of a transductive SVM (see, e.g., [R3]), another critical link to existing work that is overlooked.

[R1] Suykens et al. Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002
[R2] X.Yang et al. Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005
[R3] V. Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998
[R4] M.Lapin et al, Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014

### **Significance**

The paper's primary significance lies in its strong empirical results. Outperforming recent, powerful CNN-based methods on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark with a more "classical" learning framework is an impressive achievement and will be of considerable interest to the community.

### **Quality**

The main strength of the paper is its strong empirical performance. However, the quality is diminished by several factors:
*   As noted above, crucial references to prior work on weighted and transductive SVMs are missing.
*   To better justify the use of the proposed weighted SVM, the authors should compare it against standard baselines for regression on the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression.
*   The paper would be strengthened by providing more details on the hyperparameter selection process, specifically for the regularization coefficients λ_w and λ_R.
*   The object detection framework involves significant engineering, which, while not unusual for this application, should be clearly presented as such.

### **Clarity**

The paper's clarity could be improved in several areas:
*   The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the paper's core contribution, which is primarily a successful application and engineering effort rather than a new fundamental algorithm.
*   The abstract makes the confusing claim that continuous regression targets on the unit interval are somehow less informative than binary class memberships. This is incorrect, as binary labels can be derived from continuous ones by simple thresholding.
*   For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates considerable space (through page 4) to describing what are ultimately minor variations of well-known algorithms. In contrast, the crucial feature engineering details (e.g., the spatial confidence map in Section 4.2), which are likely key to the method's success, are covered too briefly.

### **Minor Comments**
*   Line 19: "continues" should be "continuous".
*   Line 94: "bounding boxes annotations" should be "bounding box annotations".
*   Line 98: The abbreviation "AP" (Average Precision) should be defined on its first use.
*   In Figure 3 and Section 5.1.2, the labels "SVC-0.0", "SVC-0.2", etc., should be more clearly explained. For example, the caption should state that these refer to SVC models trained by thresholding the positiveness value at 0.0, 0.2, etc.

### **Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents an effective system that achieves excellent results on challenging computer vision benchmarks. However, the contributions are diminished by limited algorithmic novelty and a significant failure to acknowledge and discuss closely related prior work on weighted and transductive SVMs. While the strong empirical results make a compelling case for publication, the paper would require substantial revision to address these major issues. Given its focus on application-specific engineering, the work might be a better fit for a top-tier computer vision conference.
