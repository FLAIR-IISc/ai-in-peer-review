### **Summary**

This paper introduces a unified framework for semantic labeling and object detection, framing both tasks as a regression problem on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) ratio. For semantic labeling, the method generates candidate segments, extracts features for them, and then regresses the IoU overlap between each candidate and the pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, where only weaker bounding box labels are available, the precise object segment is treated as a latent variable. The regression is performed using a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) for the fully supervised case and a weighted transductive SVM for the latent variable case. The proposed method achieves results that are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and establishes a new state of the art for object detection on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its impressive empirical performance. The method achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the challenging PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark. Significantly, it surpasses recent methods based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) using a more "classical" learning approach based on engineered features and SVMs. This achievement is noteworthy and of considerable interest to the community, as it provides a valuable data point in the ongoing comparison between deep learning and feature-based methods.

### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite the strong results, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty and Missing Citations:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of acknowledgment of highly relevant prior work. The proposed learning algorithms appear to be variations of well-established techniques, but key foundational papers are not cited.
    *   The weighted SVM is a known technique (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014). While the sum constraint on the weights might be novel, this is not discussed in the context of previous work.
    *   The proposed "latent e-SVM" is effectively a weighted version of a transductive SVM, a concept introduced by Vapnik (1998).
    A thorough literature review is needed to properly contextualize the contributions and clearly delineate what is novel versus what is an application of existing methods.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Baselines:** The choice of a sum-constrained weighted SVM for regression on the unit interval [0, 1] is not sufficiently motivated. A standard and simpler baseline for this task would be regularized logistic regression. To justify the use of a more complex model, the authors should report comparative results against such a baseline.

3.  **Lack of Methodological Details:** The paper omits crucial details about hyperparameter selection. Specifically, it is unclear how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were chosen. This information is essential for reproducibility.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The title and abstract do not effectively summarize the paper's core contribution, which seems to be the regression-based framework itself rather than the specific SVM variants.
    *   The abstract incorrectly insinuates that continuous regression targets (from the unit interval) are somehow less informative than binary class labels. This is logically flawed, as a binary label can always be derived from a continuous value by thresholding, making the continuous target strictly more informative.
    *   For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates too much space (nearly four pages) to describing minor variations of well-known SVM algorithms. Conversely, the critical feature engineering details, which are likely key to the method's success, are covered too briefly (lines 254ff).

### **Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents excellent empirical results that advance the state of the art. However, its contribution is significantly undermined by limited algorithmic novelty and a lack of attribution to prior work. While the results are strong enough to warrant publication, the paper requires substantial revision to address the issues raised, particularly concerning the literature review and experimental comparisons.

I have based my score on the assumption that the authors will constructively address these points by thoroughly discussing the connections to previous work. The paper is certainly of interest, but given its focus on a specific application with significant feature engineering, it might be an even stronger candidate for a top-tier computer vision conference.
