Summary:
The authors present a scheme for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, they generate candidate segments, extract features for these segments, and then regress the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between a candidate segment and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, only weaker labels in the form of bounding boxes are available, and the precise spatial extent is treated as a latent variable. Regression is performed using a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM, respectively. The results are on par with the state of the art in semantic labeling and are the best currently reported for object detection on VOC 2007.

Originality:
Algorithmic novelty is limited, and the paper omits pertinent prior work. In particular, the weighted SVM is well known (e.g., [R1, R2, R4], none cited). The sum constraint the authors impose on the weights may be novel (I did not conduct an extensive literature search to confirm). The proposed “latent e-SVM” is essentially a weighted version of a transductive SVM (e.g., [R3], not cited).

References:
- [R1] Suykens et al., Weighted least squares support vector machines: robustness and sparse approximation, Neurocomputing, 2002.
- [R2] X. Yang et al., Weighted Support Vector Machine for Data Classification, IJCNN, 2005.
- [R3] V. Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory, 1998.
- [R4] M. Lapin et al., Learning Using Privileged Information: SVM+ and Weighted SVM, Neural Networks, 2014.

Significance:
Outperforming recent CNN results with a “conventional” learning approach is a notable achievement and of broad interest to the community.

Quality:
Crucial references to previous work are missing (see above). The object detection framework involves substantial engineering, but nothing beyond the norm. The default method for regression on the unit interval is (regularized) logistic regression, so these results should be reported as a baseline to justify using a sum-constrained weighted SVM. The authors should also provide more details on how they selected the regularization coefficients \lambdaW and \lambdaR. The main strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

Clarity:
The title and abstract do not clearly summarize the core contribution. The abstract also suggests that regression targets on the unit interval are somehow less informative than binary class memberships. This is not correct, since binary labels can always be obtained from real-valued targets by thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper devotes too much space (up to and including page 4) to minor variations of well-known algorithms, and moves too quickly through the crucial feature-engineering details (lines 254ff).

I have increased my quality score by one, assuming the authors will not be defensive about the relation to previous work but will instead discuss it extensively and articulate clear connections. Great results, deficient references to earlier work, and limited algorithmic novelty. This is a paper that should certainly be published, possibly after some rewriting, and perhaps better suited to a computer vision venue.
