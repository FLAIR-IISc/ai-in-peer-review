

The paper introduces a novel SVM-based learning algorithm designed for scenarios where training examples are associated with continuous "positiveness" values (ranging from 0 to 1) rather than binary labels. The core contribution is modifying the SVM loss function to incorporate per-example weights corresponding to these positiveness values, and extending the approach to handle cases where the positiveness values are unobserved (treated as latent variables). The method is thoroughly described and evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with experiments showing consistent improvements over standard SVM classification (SVC) and regression (SVR) approaches.

While the experimental results demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method for computer vision applications, the primary technical contribution appears to be a special case of existing machine learning techniques. The core idea of incorporating example-dependent weights into the SVM loss function is well-established in the literature and implemented in standard SVM solvers like SVMlight. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from this existing body of work, particularly in relation to weighted SVM methods that have been extensively studied in the machine learning community.

The extension to the weakly supervised setting (where positiveness values are latent) is conceptually straightforward but lacks theoretical analysis. The optimization process involves a non-convex problem that requires strong regularization to prevent the latent weights from deviating significantly from their initial values (derived from bounding box overlaps). The paper does not provide justification for why this particular regularization approach is necessary or optimal, nor does it address the theoretical implications of the non-convex optimization.

Minor technical point: The claim on line 50 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is inaccurate. Logistic regression is specifically designed for bounded outputs in [0, 1] and is a standard approach for such problems.

Additionally, the abstract contains a minor error: "continues" should be "continuous."
