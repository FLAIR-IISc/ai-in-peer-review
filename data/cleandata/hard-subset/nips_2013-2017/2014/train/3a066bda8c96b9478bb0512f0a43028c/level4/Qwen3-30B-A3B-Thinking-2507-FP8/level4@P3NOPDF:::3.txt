

**Summary:**  
The authors propose a unified framework for semantic labeling and object detection. For semantic labeling, they generate candidate segments, extract features, and regress on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap between candidates and pixel-accurate ground truth. For object detection, with only bounding box labels available, they treat precise spatial extent as a latent variable and employ a weighted SVM and weighted transductive SVM (latent e-SVM) for regression. Results achieve state-of-the-art performance for object detection on VOC 2007 and are competitive with the best methods for semantic labeling.  

**Originality:**  
The algorithmic contributions are limited. Crucially, the weighted SVM (used for regression) is well-established (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Lapin et al., 2014), yet the authors omit key citations. While the sum constraint on weights may be novel, this requires verification. The proposed "latent e-SVM" is a weighted variant of transductive SVM (Vapnik, 1998), which is also uncredited. The paper fails to contextualize its method within existing literature on weighted SVMs and latent variable learning, undermining its novelty claim.  

**Significance:**  
The work demonstrates that a non-deep learning approach can outperform recent CNN-based methods for object detection—a noteworthy result for the community. However, this significance is partially offset by the lack of novelty in the core algorithm.  

**Quality:**  
The empirical results are strong and well-validated. However, the paper lacks critical baseline comparisons: (1) The default regression method for unit-interval targets (regularized logistic regression) should be reported as a baseline to justify the use of the sum-constrained weighted SVM; (2) Hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficients \(\lambda_W\), \(\lambda_R\)) are not sufficiently justified. The object detection pipeline involves standard engineering but no substantial innovation.  

**Clarity:**  
The title and abstract misrepresent the core contribution. The abstract incorrectly implies that regression targets (IoU) are "less informative" than binary class labels, which is false—the latter can always be derived via thresholding. The paper also overemphasizes minor algorithmic variations (e.g., Sections 3–4, up to p. 4) while underreporting critical feature engineering details (lines 254ff). For a NIPS audience, this imbalance detracts from the paper’s impact.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents strong empirical results that merit attention, particularly for object detection. However, the omission of foundational citations (weighted SVM, transductive SVM) and insufficient novelty in the core method are significant weaknesses. The authors must substantially revise the related work section to acknowledge prior contributions and clarify their technical distinctions. If these issues are addressed, the paper’s empirical merits would support publication.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept (after major revision).** The results are compelling, but the paper requires rigorous revision to contextualize its contributions within existing literature. The current version risks misleading readers about the novelty of its approach. If the authors fully address the cited references and clarify the relationship between their "latent e-SVM" and prior work (e.g., Vapnik’s TSVM, Lapin et al.’s weighted SVM), this would become a strong contribution. The current lack of citations is a critical flaw that must be resolved.  

*Note: The paper’s focus on non-CNN methods in 2014 was timely, but its impact is diminished by the failure to properly situate its approach within the broader literature on weighted learning and latent variables.*
