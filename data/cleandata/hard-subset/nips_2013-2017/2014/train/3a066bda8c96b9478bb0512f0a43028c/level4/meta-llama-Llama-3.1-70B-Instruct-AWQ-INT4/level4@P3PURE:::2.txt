The paper proposes a modification to the SVM learning algorithm to accommodate weakly supervised data. Specifically, a per-example weight, ranging from 0 to 1, is assigned to each training example, and the loss function of the SVM is subsequently modified to incorporate this weight. The method is then extended to the scenario where the per-example weight is not observed, treating it as a latent variable.

The method is well-described, and the experimental results suggest that, for semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, this approach may offer advantages over treating each example with equal weight. However, the distinction between the proposed method and existing "example-dependent costs" is unclear, as these are widely recognized and utilized in the machine learning community. SVMlight, for instance, provides such a mechanism.

The extension to the case where the per-example weight is not observed is intriguing but straightforward. In this scenario, the problem becomes non-convex, and no theoretical analysis is provided. Notably, a strong regularization term is employed, implying that the per-example weights do not deviate significantly from their initial values. The authors are encouraged to provide further insight into this aspect.

On a minor note, the claim on line 50 that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]" is not entirely accurate. Logistic regression is, in fact, a standard approach, and its output is bounded to the interval [0, 1].

Additionally, there is a minor spelling mistake in the abstract, where "continues" should be replaced with "continuous".

Overall, the paper proposes a modified SVM learning algorithm incorporating per-example weights into the loss function. However, the novelty of this approach is diminished by the existence of widely used example-dependent costs in machine learning.
