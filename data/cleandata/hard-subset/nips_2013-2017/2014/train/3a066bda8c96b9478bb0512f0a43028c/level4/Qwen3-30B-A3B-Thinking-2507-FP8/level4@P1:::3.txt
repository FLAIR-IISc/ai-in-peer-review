

Summary:  
The paper introduces an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with a continuous "positiveness" score (e.g., IoU overlap with ground truth) rather than binary labels. The core contribution is a novel formulation that treats label uncertainty as a Bernoulli random variable, enabling direct optimization over continuous labels. The method extends naturally to weak supervision via latent variables (e.g., bounding box annotations) and demonstrates strong results on semantic segmentation and object detection. On PASCAL VOC 2007, it achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance (60.0 mAP) by enhancing RCNN with spatial confidence maps derived from e-SVM classifiers, outperforming prior work by 1.5% AP.

Originality:  
The paper’s core innovation lies in the expectation loss formulation, which differs fundamentally from existing weighted SVM approaches. While weighted SVMs (e.g., [1,2,3]) assign weights to *data points*, e-SVM weights *labels* by modeling label uncertainty as a Bernoulli process (Pr(Y_i=1|x_i) = g(u_i)), where u_i ∈ [0,1] is the positiveness. This avoids ad-hoc thresholding (used in SVC) and handles the bounded [0,1] constraint more naturally than SVR. The latent extension (for weak supervision) is a principled application of expectation-maximization to treat u_i as latent variables, not a standard transductive SVM variant. Crucially, the paper does not cite key related work: Lapin et al. (2014) on weighted SVMs [4] and Suykens et al. (2002) on weighted LS-SVMs [1], which directly address label weighting and bounded constraints. The claim that "the weighted SVM is well-known" mischaracterizes the distinction—e-SVM’s label-weighting mechanism and expectation-based loss are novel.  

Significance:  
The empirical results are compelling. On PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation, e-SVM achieves 36.8% accuracy (with pixel labels) and 27.7% (with bounding boxes), matching state-of-the-art SVR-based methods. For object detection, the 1.5% AP gain over RCNN (60.0 mAP) is meaningful given RCNN’s strong baseline (58.5 mAP) and the minimal engineering effort (simply concatenating confidence maps). This demonstrates clear utility for reducing annotation costs in large-scale vision tasks—critical as datasets like ImageNet grow.  

Quality:  
The technical execution is sound, with clear optimization (Algorithm 1) and thorough experiments. However, two gaps weaken the evaluation:  
1. The paper lacks a baseline against logistic regression (the standard approach for [0,1] targets), making it unclear why a constrained weighted SVM is superior.  
2. Hyperparameters λ_W and λ_R are not justified (e.g., sensitivity analysis). The claim that "the main strength is empirical results" understates the need for methodological rigor.  

Clarity:  
The abstract overstates the "insufficiency" of binary labels (which can be derived from continuous scores via thresholding), creating confusion. The title ("Learning From Weakly Supervised Data...") misleads—e-SVM handles *continuous labels*, not just weak supervision. The paper spends excessive space (pp. 3–4) on algorithmic variations (e.g., SVC/SVR comparisons) while under-explaining feature engineering (Section 4.2, lines 254–260), which is pivotal to the results. A concise comparison of feature extraction (e.g., second-order pooling vs. CNN features) would strengthen clarity.

Overall Assessment:  
The paper presents a useful method for weakly supervised vision tasks with strong empirical validation. While the core idea (label uncertainty modeling) is novel, the lack of engagement with weighted SVM literature (especially Lapin et al. [4]) is a significant oversight. The results are robust, but the significance is overstated in the abstract. With revisions to clarify the method’s distinction from prior work and add logistic regression baselines, this would be a strong NIPS contribution. As is, it requires minor but critical fixes to contextualize the novelty.  

Recommendation:  
**Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The empirical results are excellent and address an important problem (reducing annotation effort), but the paper must:  
1. Explicitly contrast e-SVM’s label-weighting with weighted SVMs [1,4]  
2. Add logistic regression as a baseline for continuous label regression  
3. Clarify the abstract’s misleading claim about binary vs. continuous labels.  

*Note: The paper’s claim of "state-of-the-art object detection performance" is accurate (60.0 mAP vs. RCNN’s 58.5), but this is an incremental improvement over a CNN-based system, not a breakthrough over CNNs themselves.*
