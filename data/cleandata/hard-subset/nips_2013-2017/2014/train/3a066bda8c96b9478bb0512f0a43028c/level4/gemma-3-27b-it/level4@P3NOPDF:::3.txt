## Review of NIPS_2013-2017/2014 Submission

**Summary:**

This paper presents a framework for semantic labeling and object detection, leveraging regression-based approaches. For semantic labeling, the authors generate candidate segments, extract features, and regress directly on the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap ratio with pixel-accurate ground truth. In the object detection setting, where only bounding box labels are available, the precise spatial extent of objects is treated as a latent variable. Regression is then performed using a weighted Support Vector Machine (SVM) for semantic labeling and a weighted transductive SVM (termed “latent e-SVM”) for object detection. The results demonstrate performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods for semantic labeling and achieve the best currently reported performance on the VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Originality:**

The algorithmic novelty of this work is somewhat limited. The use of weighted SVMs is well-established in the literature (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2005; Lapin et al., 2014), yet these related works are not cited. The authors introduce a sum constraint on the SVM weights, which may represent a novel contribution, though a more exhaustive literature search would strengthen this claim. Similarly, the proposed "latent e-SVM" is fundamentally a weighted instantiation of a transductive SVM, a technique also previously explored (e.g., Vapnik, 1998). A more thorough engagement with existing literature, particularly regarding weighted SVMs and transductive learning, is crucial.

**Significance:**

Achieving performance comparable to, and in the case of object detection surpassing, recent Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) results with a “conventional” learning approach is a noteworthy accomplishment and will be of considerable interest to the research community. This demonstrates the continued viability of feature engineering and regression-based methods in computer vision tasks.

**Quality:**

The paper suffers from significant omissions in its citation of relevant prior work, as detailed above. While the object detection framework appears to be well-engineered, the choice of a sum-constrained weighted SVM for regression on the unit interval requires justification. Regularized logistic regression represents a natural baseline for this type of regression problem, and its inclusion in the experimental evaluation would strengthen the claims made regarding the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the authors should provide more detail regarding the selection of the regularization coefficients, λ<sub>W</sub> and λ<sub>R</sub>, and the sensitivity of the results to their values. The primary strength of the paper lies in its strong empirical results.

**Clarity:**

The title and abstract do not accurately reflect the core contribution of the paper. The abstract’s assertion that regression targets from the unit interval are less informative than binary class memberships is inaccurate; binary labels are trivially obtainable from the continuous regression output via thresholding. For a NIPS audience, the paper dedicates excessive space to relatively minor variations of established algorithms (up to and including page 4) while providing insufficient detail on the crucial aspect of feature engineering (lines 254ff). Expanding on the feature selection process and providing a more concise presentation of the algorithmic variations would improve clarity.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 19: “continues” should be “continuous”
*   Line 94: “boxes” should be “box”
*   Line 98: "AP" should be defined upon first use.
*   Line 350: “SVM” should be “SVC” (or clarify if referring to the general SVM concept)
*   Line 350: “with every different values” should be “for all threshold values”

**Overall Assessment:**

I have increased my initial quality score, anticipating that the authors will address the cited omissions regarding related work constructively and provide further connections to existing research. The empirical results are strong, but the paper is currently held back by a lack of engagement with the existing literature and a presentation that prioritizes minor algorithmic variations over important details like feature engineering. Despite these shortcomings, the paper represents a solid contribution and is worthy of publication, potentially benefiting from further refinement or consideration for a computer vision-focused conference.
