Summary:
The paper proposes an expectation-loss SVM (E-SVM) that accommodates continuous attributes for positive instances, thereby extending multiple instance learning (MIL) beyond binary labels. The framework supports both observed and latent settings, and is applied to weakly supervised visual learning tasks, specifically semantic segmentation and object detection. Experiments demonstrate promising performance, and the approach is clearly motivated and presented.

Strengths:
- Clearly defined problem and strong motivation: handling continuous, soft attributes of positives is important for weakly supervised learning, where precise instance-level labels are often unavailable.
- Methodological contribution: replacing binary instance indicators with expected (soft) weights provides a principled generalization of MIL that applies both when continuous attributes are observed and when they must be inferred as latent variables.
- Breadth of application: the method is validated on two central tasks—semantic segmentation and object detection—showcasing versatility.
- Empirical results: experiments appear carefully designed and substantiate the utility of the approach under weak supervision.
- Clarity: the presentation is generally clear, making the intuition behind the method accessible.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Incremental novelty: conceptually, the method can be viewed as a relatively straightforward extension of MIL via soft weighting of positive instances. The paper would benefit from sharper theoretical or algorithmic insights beyond this substitution to strengthen the perceived novelty.
- Optimization details: for the observed case, is the objective convex? For the latent case, what is the optimization strategy (e.g., EM-like alternating updates, CCCP, or other)? Convergence guarantees, initialization sensitivity, and complexity relative to MIL-SVM or latent SVM are not fully clear from the draft.
- Calibration and robustness: the effectiveness of soft weights likely depends on their calibration and noise. It is unclear how sensitive performance is to mis-specified or noisy continuous attributes, and whether the method includes mechanisms (e.g., regularization or constraints) to prevent over-reliance on uncertain weights.
- Empirical analysis depth: while the results are promising, the draft does not specify whether there are ablations isolating the contribution of expectation-based weighting, comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., latent SVM, MIL-SVM, recent weakly supervised segmentation/detection methods), or statistical significance analyses.

Questions for the authors:
1. Objective and properties: Please provide the explicit E-SVM objective, including how expectations enter the loss or margin constraints, and discuss convexity in the observed setting. For the latent setting, what guarantees (if any) exist for monotonic improvement or convergence to a stationary point?
2. Weight construction: How are the continuous attributes (soft weights) obtained in practice for each task under weak supervision? Are they normalized or calibrated, and do you model uncertainty in these weights?
3. Regularization: Do you regularize or constrain the weights (e.g., sum-to-one per bag, bounds, entropy penalties)? How does this affect performance?
4. Complexity: What is the computational cost compared to standard SVM, MIL-SVM, and latent SVM in terms of training time and memory? Can the method scale to large datasets and high-dimensional features?
5. Sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to hyperparameters governing the expectation loss and regularization? Please include sensitivity plots or a brief analysis.
6. Failure modes: In which regimes do soft weights hurt performance (e.g., when weights are highly noisy or biased)? Any diagnostics to detect such cases?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Strengthen the theoretical section by highlighting conditions under which the expected-loss formulation yields tighter bounds or better generalization than binary MIL, and clarify links to existing frameworks (e.g., risk minimization with soft labels, label-proportion learning).
- Provide a detailed optimization description, including initialization, update rules, and convergence behavior for the latent case. If using an alternating approach, specify subproblem convexity and stopping criteria.
- Expand empirical comparisons to include:
  - MIL-SVM and latent SVM baselines implemented under comparable conditions.
  - Recent weakly supervised segmentation and detection methods.
  - Ablations: (i) binary vs. expectation-weighted instances; (ii) observed vs. latent variants; (iii) effect of weight calibration and noise.
  - Statistical significance testing and reporting of variance across runs.
- Clarify how continuous attributes are derived in weak supervision (e.g., from image-level labels, region proposals, objectness/saliency scores, or rough proportion labels), and whether the approach requires any task-specific heuristics.
- Include qualitative visualizations (e.g., heatmaps of learned soft weights, segmentation overlays) to illustrate how expectation weighting influences predictions.
- Discuss broader applicability beyond vision (e.g., other MIL domains), and outline limitations and future directions (e.g., end-to-end integration with deep features or joint learning of weights and classifiers).

Minor comments:
- Use consistent terminology and capitalization (e.g., “expectation-loss SVM,” “E-SVM”).
- Define all acronyms at first use and ensure notation is consistent across observed and latent formulations.
- If possible, release code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
This paper addresses an important problem in weakly supervised visual learning and presents a conceptually clean and practically useful extension of MIL through an expectation-based SVM formulation. While the core idea is somewhat incremental, the applicability to both observed and latent settings, together with strong empirical results on segmentation and detection, make it a solid and relevant contribution. Clarifying the optimization, deepening the comparative and ablation studies, and discussing robustness would considerably strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the above concerns.
