This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are associated with a continuous "positiveness" score (a value in [0, 1]) rather than a binary label. The core idea is to model the true, unobserved binary label of each example as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its known "positiveness" score. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expected* hinge loss over these random labels. This formulation results in a weighted SVM problem where each example contributes to both the positive and negative class costs, weighted by its positiveness score. The authors also present a latent e-SVM extension for scenarios where the positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available), treating them as latent variables to be optimized alternately with the model parameters.

The proposed method is evaluated on the tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection using the PASCAL VOC datasets. Experimentally, the e-SVM is shown to outperform standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) for the task of segment proposal classification. When combined with the R-CNN object detector, the features generated using the latent e-SVM lead to a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 detection benchmark.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. Reducing the reliance on expensive, pixel-perfect annotations is a critical challenge in computer vision and other fields, making this work highly relevant to the NIPS community.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a clear and significant performance improvement on a competitive, standard benchmark. Achieving a 1.5% absolute improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) over the then state-of-the-art R-CNN on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a strong result and a compelling argument for the method's utility.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is described clearly. The motivation is easy to follow, and the mathematical formulation is straightforward.

4.  **Intuitive Formulation:** The core concept of treating labels as random variables based on a "positiveness" score is an elegant and intuitive way to frame the problem, providing a principled alternative to ad-hoc thresholding for binarizing labels.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern is the novelty of the proposed e-SVM formulation relative to existing weighted SVMs. The authors correctly state that their method is different from standard instance-weighted SVMs (WSVMs). However, the formulation in Equation 1, which weights the positive and negative hinge loss terms separately (`l+i * g(ui) + l-i * [1 - g(ui)]`), appears to be functionally equivalent to an SVM with per-example costs for false positives and false negatives. Many standard SVM solvers (e.g., LibLinear, cited by the authors) allow for setting different C values for the positive and negative classes (`-w1` and `-w2` parameters), and this can be extended to a per-example basis. Could the authors please clarify the fundamental difference between e-SVM and an SVM where each example `i` is given a positive class weight proportional to `g(ui)` and a negative class weight proportional to `1 - g(ui)`? A more detailed discussion and comparison with this well-established baseline would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

2.  **Technical Depth of the Latent Model:** The extension to the latent e-SVM is a natural step, but the treatment feels somewhat superficial. The optimization problem becomes non-convex, yet there is no theoretical analysis of the alternating optimization procedure (e.g., convergence properties). Furthermore, the regularization term `R(u)` is a strong KL-divergence penalty that heavily discourages the learned positiveness scores `u` from deviating far from their initial estimates `ubb`. This raises the question of how much is truly being "learned" in the latent variable update step versus simply performing a minor refinement of the initial guess. Can the authors provide more insight or analysis on the sensitivity of the model to the regularization parameter `λR` and demonstrate that the latent variables `u` converge to meaningful values that are substantially different from their initializations?

3.  **Minor Comments:**
    *   In the introduction (page 2), the authors state: "it is also not a standard regression problem since the value of positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]". While this is a fair critique of standard SVR, it overlooks other common regression models, such as logistic regression, which are explicitly designed to predict values within this range and are very much "standard".
    *   There is a minor typo in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and pragmatically successful approach to learning from weakly labeled data. The strong experimental results are a major plus. However, the claims of novelty would be more convincing with a clearer differentiation from established per-example, per-class weighting schemes in SVMs, and the technical contribution of the latent model could be strengthened with deeper analysis.
