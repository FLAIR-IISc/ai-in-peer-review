- Proposes e-SVM to learn from continuous "positiveness" labels using an expectation loss solvable by standard SVMs.  
- Extends to latent e-SVM for unobserved positiveness (e.g., bounding boxes) via alternating optimization.  
- Method is technically sound and mathematically well-posed, with appropriate experiments.  
- Theoretical justification for the expectation loss is insufficient.  
- Expectation loss is mathematically equivalent to a weighted SVM (weights = positiveness); paper lacks direct comparison to weighted SVM methods [23,27].  
- The paper’s distinction between "weighting labels" and "weighting data points" is confusing and not well-motivated.  
- Latent e-SVM relies on regularization to prevent trivial u (0/1) solutions but lacks rigorous justification and ablation studies on regularization choices.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; experimental setup for segmentation and detection is well-described.  
- Needs clearer justification of using IoU as the positiveness measure for segments.  
- Connection to other weakly supervised methods (e.g., MIL) is mentioned but not thoroughly compared, and continuous-supervision methods [19,20] are under-discussed.  
- Novelty is limited because the core idea closely resembles existing weighted SVM approaches; applications to segmentation/detection are not novel.  
- Empirical improvement is modest (≈1.5% over RCNN) and comparisons to more recent detectors (Fast/Faster R-CNN) are missing.  
- Claim of state-of-the-art detection is overstated given limited baselines and modest gains.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement, sound SVM-based formulation, practical applications, and useful new evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG).  
- Weaknesses: weak theoretical grounding for expectation loss, inadequate comparisons to related weighted SVM and weakly supervised work, modest empirical gains, and missing ablation/regularization analysis.  
- Recommendation: weak accept, conditional on adding direct comparisons to weighted SVM, broader related-work discussion, regularization ablations, and comparisons to newer detection methods.
