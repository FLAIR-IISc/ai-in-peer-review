Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a max-margin method for learning from “soft” labels in [0,1] that encode the “positiveness” of an example (e.g., IoU between a proposal and a ground-truth instance). The core idea is to treat the binary label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as Bernoulli with success probability µi = g(ui), and minimize the expectation of the hinge loss under this Bernoulli, plus an L2 regularizer. The resulting objective per example is a convex combination of positive and negative hinge losses: µi max(0,1−w⊤xi) + (1−µi) max(0,1+w⊤xi). The method is implemented with off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers by an equivalent reweighting/duplication trick. The latent e-SVM extends to weak supervision where ui is unobserved (only bounding boxes available) by alternating minimization over w and u with a convex KL/CE regularizer that keeps u close to an initialization from bbox IoU.

Empirically, e-SVM is evaluated for segment proposal scoring on VOC2011 using new direct metrics (mean AP over thresholds, and NDCG), performs better than SVC and slightly better than SVR, yields comparable semantic segmentation accuracy to SVR, and improves RCNN detection mAP on VOC2007 by ~1.5 points when concatenating a spatial confidence-map feature to RCNN features.

This work connects to a line of methods that learn from uncertain/soft labels and instance-weighted SVMs. The expected hinge loss with soft labels is closely related to “fuzzy SVMs” and probabilistic-label SVMs (e.g., membership-weighted SVMs; Lin & Wang 2002; Wu et al. 2005), and also to per-instance cost-sensitive/weighted SVMs (e.g., WSVM). The latent extension is in the spirit of latent SVM (Felzenszwalb et al.) with a convex regularizer to avoid hard 0/1 u. The application to detection builds on segment proposals (CPMC), second-order pooling, and RCNN.

Quality
- Positives: The expected-hinge formulation is simple, convex in w for known µi, and easily implementable. The latent extension is a reasonable alternating scheme with a convex regularizer; the insight that λR=0 collapses u to {0,1} and behaves like a latent SVM is useful. The spatial confidence map feature is a pragmatic way to integrate segment-level confidences into window-based detectors. Experiments are reasonably thorough on VOC segmentation/detection, and comparisons against SVC/SVR are appropriate baselines for segment scoring.
- Concerns:
  - The novelty of the core formulation appears limited: e-SVM is effectively a per-example, label-dependent weighted hinge loss, equivalent to duplicating each example with labels ±1 and weights µi and 1−µi. This is very close to fuzzy/probabilistic-label SVMs and weighted SVM variants; the paper should position itself more clearly versus this prior work and demonstrate what is genuinely new (beyond the particular application and the latent u with KL regularization).
  - Theoretical analysis is minimal. There is no consistency or risk analysis for the expected hinge with µi∈[0,1] (e.g., does it estimate class-probabilities up to a link? how sensitive is performance to miscalibrated µi?). For the latent model, no convergence guarantees (beyond monotonic decrease) or properties of solutions are discussed.
  - Optimization details for u are missing. With g(u)=u, the per-example subproblem is strictly convex; a closed-form or efficient update (and its complexity) should be provided. How is λR selected, and how sensitive are results to it?
  - Implementation claim that “any standard SVM solver” can be used may be misleading: many solvers support class weights but not per-example weights; the duplication/weighting trick should be spelled out (and whether liblinear was modified).
  - Empirical evidence: while TAPC/NDCG show improvements over SVR/SVC for proposal scoring, the downstream semantic segmentation accuracy is only “comparable” to SVR; more analysis on why the better proposal ranking does not translate more strongly to pixel accuracy would strengthen the case. For detection, a +1.2 to +1.5 mAP boost is promising, but error bars and ablations (effect of m, pooling choice, choice of g, different proposal generators, and contribution per class) are needed to assess robustness. It is unclear whether the RCNN baseline exactly matches the published one (training data splits, bbox regression variant).
  - Several presentation issues (typos in equations, incomplete references, and unclear constant factors in the regularizer/weight of ||w||^2) reduce clarity.

Clarity
The paper is mostly readable, but:
- Fix Eq. (1) formatting: clearly specify the regularization as (λ/2)||w||^2 (or similar), avoid “λw·1/wTw”. Define g(u) once and justify choosing g(u)=u.
- Provide the explicit equivalence to an SVM with duplicated samples and weights, and the exact solver settings (liblinear options).
- Specify the u-update derivation (e.g., derivative set to zero yields a scalar equation with a unique solution in (0,1); provide the solver).
- Complete and clean up references; several entries are malformed.

Originality
The latent-u with KL regularization and its application to segment scoring and integration with RCNN are the more original aspects. The core expected-hinge loss with soft labels is, in my view, not new and overlaps with fuzzy/probabilistic-label SVMs and per-instance weighted SVMs. The paper should cite and compare to: fuzzy SVMs (e.g., Lin & Wang, TNN 2002), SVMs with probabilistic labels/soft labels (e.g., Wu, Lin “Probability estimates for multi-class classification by pairwise coupling”, and related), cost-sensitive/instance-weighted SVMs, and learning from label proportions/soft supervision. A logistic regression or squared-loss baseline trained to predict ui should also be included.

Significance
The idea is simple and practical, and the observed detection gains suggest utility. If better positioned relative to prior work and backed by more thorough analysis/ablations, the contribution could be of interest to the community working on weak supervision and proposal scoring. As-is, the novelty seems modest and the empirical gains, while non-trivial, are incremental.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, convex formulation for soft labels that practitioners can adopt.
- Sensible latent extension with a convex regularizer; practical training scheme.
- New direct evaluation metrics for proposal scoring and solid baselines.
- Demonstrated improvement when coupled with a strong detector (RCNN).

Cons for acceptance
- Core formulation overlaps with existing fuzzy/probabilistic/weighted SVM literature; insufficient positioning and missing comparisons.
- Limited theory; no analysis of calibration, robustness to noisy µ, or convergence properties.
- Incomplete optimization and implementation details (u-update, solver).
- Modest impact on semantic segmentation; detection gains need stronger ablation and reproducibility details.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add and discuss baselines: logistic regression (cross-entropy to ui), squared loss regression on ui, ranking SVM/RankNet for proposal ranking; report TAPC/NDCG and downstream task performance.
- Provide ablations on g(u) (identity vs. nonlinear mappings), λR sensitivity, grid size m, pooling type, and different proposal methods (CPMC vs. superpixels).
- Clarify training details for RCNN integration and ensure a fair, documented baseline.
- Tighten the theoretical section: show convexity properties, give the u-update derivation, and discuss calibration/consistency.
- Clean up equations and references.

Overall recommendation: Leaning to reject in current form due to limited novelty relative to prior soft-label SVMs and missing analysis/details, but the idea is practical and the empirical improvements are encouraging; with stronger positioning, comparisons, and clarity, this could be a useful contribution.
