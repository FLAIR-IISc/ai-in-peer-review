## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for scenarios with continuous “positiveness” values instead of binary labels, common in weakly supervised learning. The core idea is to treat the positiveness as a probabilistic label and incorporate it directly into the SVM loss function. The authors extend this to a latent variable setting for cases where positiveness is unobserved, and demonstrate its effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection.

The work builds upon existing literature in weakly supervised learning (MIL, MIM) and weighted SVMs, correctly identifying the limitations of standard SVC and SVR approaches when applied to this problem. The proposed e-SVM addresses these limitations by directly modeling the continuous positiveness value.  The extension to the latent variable case using a regularization term is a sensible addition to handle the bounding-box-only annotation setting.  The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons with baselines and state-of-the-art methods across multiple tasks. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification is a positive contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation addresses a specific gap in existing SVM approaches for weakly supervised learning.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant result. The consistent improvements across tasks demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed method.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated, and the extension to the latent variable case is well-justified.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  Experiments are thorough and demonstrate the benefits of the method under varying levels of supervision.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the core concepts clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares against several baselines, a more detailed ablation study on the impact of different components of e-SVM (e.g., the regularization term in the latent setting) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter settings. 
* **Computational Complexity:** The authors don't provide any discussion on the computational cost of their method compared to standard SVMs, particularly for large datasets.
* **Redundancy in Description:** The lengthy description of related work and existing methods feels somewhat excessive, particularly regarding established techniques like MIL.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The proposed method is novel and addresses a relevant problem in weakly supervised learning.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques.
* The theoretical formulation is sound and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed ablation studies and analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity weakens the conclusions.
* The computational complexity aspect needs addressing.

**Overall:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through a more detailed ablation study and a discussion of computational cost, would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
