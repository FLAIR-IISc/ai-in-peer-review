

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training samples are associated with "positiveness" values (continuous values in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors address two scenarios: (1) strong supervision where positiveness values are observed (e.g., pixel-level annotations), and (2) weak supervision where positiveness values are latent (e.g., only bounding box annotations available). The e-SVM model treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the positiveness value, and formulates an expectation loss function. The latent extension handles unobserved positiveness by iteratively optimizing over both model parameters and latent variables. The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

The paper positions itself between standard binary classification (SVC) and multiple instance learning (MIL), addressing a problem setting where standard approaches are inadequate. It relates to weighted SVMs (WSVMs), distinguishing itself by weighting labels rather than data points.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical formulation. The e-SVM model is well-motivated and the latent extension is logically developed. The authors provide a detailed optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) for the latent case. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR), and the use of two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification is well-justified. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines across both strong and weak supervision settings. However, the paper does not thoroughly address the theoretical properties of the algorithm (e.g., convergence guarantees for the latent case) or provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the expectation loss versus alternative weighting schemes.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, mathematical formulation, and experimental setup are presented with appropriate detail. The connection between the learning problem and computer vision applications is well-explained. The authors make a good effort to clarify the distinction between their approach and related methods (MIL and WSVMs). The use of figures (Figure 1, Figure 2) to illustrate the framework and spatial confidence map feature is helpful. Some technical details (e.g., the specific choice of regularization term R(u) in Equation 3) could be better motivated, but the paper generally provides sufficient information for reproducibility.

## Originality
The paper addresses a specific weak supervision scenario (continuous positiveness values in [0,1]) that is distinct from standard binary classification, MIL, and standard weighted SVM approaches. The key insight of modeling the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness value is novel. The authors correctly identify the gap between their problem setting and existing approaches, noting that MIL cannot be applied because "most proposals will contain both foreground and background pixels." While the expectation loss formulation is related to weighted SVMs, the paper makes a clear distinction between weighting labels versus weighting data points. The extension to handle latent positiveness through an EM-like optimization is a useful contribution. Compared to recent NIPS papers (e.g., those on weakly supervised learning), this work presents a distinct approach to a specific weak supervision problem.

## Significance
The problem addressed (learning from weak supervision with continuous label values) is significant for reducing labeling costs in computer vision, which is a major practical challenge. The results demonstrate meaningful improvements (1.5% average AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection) over strong baselines. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance for object detection is well-supported by the experimental results. The method's flexibility to work with various segment proposal detectors and features, as well as its applicability to other domains beyond computer vision, enhances its significance. However, the modest performance gains (1.5% AP) compared to other recent advances in object detection may limit the perceived impact.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem formulation addressing a practical weak supervision scenario
- Well-motivated mathematical approach with a sound extension to latent variables
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple tasks (segmentation, detection)
- Consistent performance improvements over relevant baselines
- Good organization and clear presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- The distinction between e-SVM and weighted SVMs is not fully justified (the expectation loss appears equivalent to a weighted SVM with weights proportional to positiveness)
- The regularization term for latent variables (Equation 3) lacks sufficient theoretical motivation
- The paper does not demonstrate the method on non-computer vision tasks to prove general applicability
- The claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is modest (1.5% AP improvement) compared to other recent advances
- Limited theoretical analysis of the algorithm's properties

## Recommendation
This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a specific weak supervision problem with clear applications in computer vision. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are well-conducted, and the results demonstrate meaningful improvements over baselines. While the performance gains are modest and the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the work addresses an important practical problem in computer vision and makes a clear contribution to the literature on weakly supervised learning. The paper is well-written and meets the quality standards expected for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better clarify the relationship with weighted SVMs and to provide more justification for the regularization term.
