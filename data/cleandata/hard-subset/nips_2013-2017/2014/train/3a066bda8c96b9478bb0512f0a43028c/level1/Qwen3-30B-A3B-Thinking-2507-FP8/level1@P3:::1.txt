

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are labeled with "positiveness" values (continuous values in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors frame this as a general problem in computer vision where bounding box annotations are available but pixel-level annotations are not. The e-SVM formulation treats the label as a Bernoulli random variable parameterized by the positiveness value, leading to an expectation loss that can be solved with standard SVM solvers. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., only bounding boxes available), using an alternating optimization approach. They apply the method to semantic segmentation and object detection, claiming state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with appropriate experimental validation. The e-SVM formulation is mathematically well-posed, and the extension to the latent case using alternating optimization is standard but appropriate. However, the theoretical justification for the expectation loss formulation is insufficient. The paper claims their approach is superior to SVC and SVR, but the expectation loss formulation (Equation 1) is mathematically equivalent to a weighted SVM with weights = positiveness, which should have been directly compared to existing weighted SVM approaches [23, 27]. The paper's distinction between "weighting labels" and "weighting data points" is confusing, as both are standard weighted SVM formulations. The latent e-SVM's reliance on regularization (Equation 3) to prevent u from becoming 0/1 is noted but not rigorously justified, and the paper lacks ablation studies showing the impact of different regularization terms.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the methodology. The experimental setup for semantic segmentation and object detection is well-described. However, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the positiveness value relates to the underlying problem. The paper states "the positiveness of each segment Si is set as the IoU ratio," but doesn't sufficiently justify why IoU is the appropriate measure for positiveness in this context. The connection to existing weakly supervised learning methods like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is mentioned but not thoroughly explored - the authors correctly note that MIL doesn't apply to their setting, but don't provide a detailed comparison with other weakly supervised approaches that handle continuous supervision [19, 20].

**Originality:** The core idea of using continuous "positiveness" values with an SVM-like formulation has some novelty, but the paper fails to sufficiently distinguish itself from existing weighted SVM approaches. The authors cite weighted SVMs [23, 27] but don't provide a clear theoretical or empirical comparison to these methods, which would have strengthened the novelty claim. The application to semantic segmentation and object detection is not novel - the use of segment proposals and confidence scores has been explored in previous work [5, 14]. The paper's main contribution appears to be the specific formulation of the expectation loss, but this contribution is somewhat obscured by the lack of clear comparison to related weighted SVM methods.

**Significance:** The problem of weakly supervised learning is important, particularly for large-scale vision datasets. The modest 1.5% improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (over RCNN) is non-trivial, but the paper doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods. The method's generality to other domains is noted, but the paper doesn't provide convincing evidence that this approach would scale to much larger datasets or outperform modern deep learning approaches for weakly supervised learning. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art object detection performance" is somewhat overstated, as the improvement over RCNN is modest (1.5%) and the paper doesn't compare to more recent detectors like Fast R-CNN or Faster R-CNN.

**Strengths:** The paper clearly articulates a useful problem setting (weak supervision with continuous positiveness values), provides a theoretically sound solution that extends standard SVMs, and demonstrates practical application to important computer vision tasks. The two new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification are well-motivated and useful.

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the expectation loss formulation, fails to properly compare to existing weighted SVM approaches, provides only modest improvements over baselines, and doesn't adequately distinguish itself from recent weakly supervised learning work. The latent e-SVM's reliance on regularization is not thoroughly justified, and the paper would benefit from ablation studies showing the contribution of different components.

**Recommendation:** I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound approach, but the novelty is limited by insufficient comparison to existing weighted SVM methods, and the empirical improvements are modest. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. Direct comparison to weighted SVMs with weights = positiveness (which is equivalent to their e-SVM)
2. More thorough discussion of related weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL
3. Ablation studies showing the impact of different regularization terms in the latent case
4. Comparison to more recent object detection methods (e.g., Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN)

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, but the lack of clear differentiation from existing weighted SVM approaches and the modest empirical gains prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The method could be valuable for specific applications where continuous supervision is available, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish this.
