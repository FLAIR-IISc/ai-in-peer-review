This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from data where labels are not binary but are instead represented by a continuous "positiveness" value in the interval [0, 1]. This is a common scenario in computer vision, where, for instance, the quality of a segment proposal can be measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object mask. The core idea is to treat the binary label of a training example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, with the success probability given by its known positiveness score. The SVM loss is then formulated as the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegant formulation results in a weighted SVM problem where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative class losses, weighted by its positiveness `u` and `1-u` respectively.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness values `u_i` are themselves unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available). In this case, the `u_i` are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM model parameters `w` via an alternating optimization scheme, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate. This work connects to previous literature on weighted SVMs and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), but carves out a distinct and practical problem space between fully supervised pixel-level annotation and image-level weak supervision, which is highly relevant for large-scale vision tasks.

The method is validated extensively on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection using the PASCAL VOC datasets. Notably, when combined with the R-CNN framework, the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem formulation is both novel and highly significant. Learning from continuous-valued "soft" labels is a practical and important problem that is often handled with ad-hoc heuristics (like thresholding for SVC) or models not designed for bounded outputs (like SVR). The proposed expectation loss is a principled and elegant way to incorporate this information directly into the max-margin framework. The successful application to learning from bounding-box-level supervision is a major contribution, as this type of annotation is far more scalable than pixel-level segmentation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the e-SVM loss is clear, and its reduction to a weighted SVM problem that can be solved by standard solvers is a significant practical advantage. The extension to the latent e-SVM with an alternating optimization strategy is a standard and well-justified approach for this type of problem. The experimental validation is strong, using standard, challenging benchmarks (PASCAL VOC) and comparing against appropriate baselines (SVC, SVR). The introduction of new, sensible metrics like TAPC and NDCG for the segment classification task is also a welcome contribution.

3.  **Experimental Results:** The results are impressive. The e-SVM consistently outperforms SVC and SVR on the direct task of segment classification under both strong and weak supervision. More importantly, the authors demonstrate that their learned segmentation cues can provide a substantial boost (1.5% mean AP) to a very strong, state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN) on PASCAL VOC 2007. This demonstrates the practical utility and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Optimization:** In Section 3.2, the optimization of the latent variables `u` could be described in more detail. The paper states `u(new) ← argmin_u L(w(new),u)`. Given the form of the loss function and the KL-divergence regularizer, it seems likely that a closed-form solution for `u_i` exists when `w` is fixed. Explicitly providing this update rule would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the paper.

2.  **Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The paper mentions the connection to weighted SVMs but could elaborate further. The e-SVM loss function seems equivalent to duplicating each data point `x_i`, creating a positive instance `(x_i, +1)` with weight `u_i` and a negative instance `(x_i, -1)` with weight `1-u_i`. Clarifying if this is indeed the case would help readers place the work in context and simplify implementation. While the motivation is novel, a deeper discussion of the final objective function's relationship to prior work in cost-sensitive or weighted classification would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Choice of `g(u)`:** The authors choose `g(u_i) = u_i` for simplicity. While this is a reasonable starting point, the performance of the model might be sensitive to this choice. A brief discussion or ablation study on other monotonic functions mapping [0,1] to [0,1] (e.g., sigmoidal functions, power functions) would provide insight into the model's robustness and potentially lead to further performance gains.

4.  **Generality:** The paper claims the approach is general, but the experiments are focused entirely on computer vision tasks where IoU serves as a natural measure of "positiveness." To better support the claim of generality, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss how this framework could be applied to other domains where such continuous labels might arise (e.g., sentiment analysis with scores from 0 to 1, or probabilistic labels from human annotators).

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and elegant loss function for an important and practical weakly supervised learning problem.
*   The method is technically sound and can be implemented using standard SVM solvers, enhancing its accessibility.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the highly competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark by improving upon a very strong baseline (R-CNN).
*   Experiments are thorough, well-designed, and provide convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness over relevant alternatives.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   Some technical details, particularly regarding the optimization of latent variables in the latent e-SVM, are underspecified.
*   The relationship to and distinction from existing weighted SVM formulations could be made clearer.
*   The sensitivity to certain design choices (e.g., the function `g(u)`) is not explored.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed in a final version. The proposed e-SVM framework is a valuable tool for learning from the kind of noisy, continuous-valued supervision that is increasingly common in large-scale machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
