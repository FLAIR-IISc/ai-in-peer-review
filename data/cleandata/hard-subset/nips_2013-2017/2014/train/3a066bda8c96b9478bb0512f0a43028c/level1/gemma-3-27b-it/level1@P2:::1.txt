This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically where only a “positiveness” value (between 0 and 1) representing the affiliation to the positive class is available, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to modify the SVM loss function to weight examples by this positiveness value. The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper builds upon prior work in weakly supervised learning, particularly multiple instance learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. It correctly identifies the limitations of applying standard SVC and SVR directly to this problem – binarization issues with SVC and the non-binary nature of the target variable for SVR. The work also references relevant segmentation and object detection techniques like CPMC, R-CNN, and selective search. It positions itself well within the existing literature, acknowledging both the challenges and the opportunities presented by weakly supervised learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM algorithm presents a reasonable and potentially effective approach to handling continuous-valued labels in a classification context, a scenario common in weakly supervised settings. The modification of the SVM loss function is conceptually sound and logically justified.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are strong, particularly the state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007. The consistent improvements over SVR and SVC in segment proposal classification are also encouraging. The ablation studies and comparisons with existing methods are reasonably thorough.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the generality of their approach, highlighting its ability to work with different segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers, making it broadly applicable.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of learning from "positiveness" values and explains why standard approaches are insufficient.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM algorithm seems correct, and the iterative optimization procedure for the latent variable extension is clearly described.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the algorithm is intuitively appealing, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical understanding of why e-SVM performs better than SVR or SVC. A more rigorous analysis of the loss function and its properties could strengthen the work.
* **Regularization Details:** The choice of the regularization term for the latent e-SVM model (based on KL divergence) seems somewhat arbitrary. The justification for this specific regularization is weak, and an exploration of alternative regularization strategies would be beneficial. The sensitivity of performance to the regularization parameter is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn’t provide sufficient details about the hyperparameter tuning process (e.g., how the λ parameter in the loss function was chosen). This lack of detail makes it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the robustness of the algorithm.
* **Comparison Depth:** While comparisons to SVR and SVC are provided, they are relatively limited. A comparison to more recent and advanced weakly supervised learning techniques (beyond those explicitly mentioned in the related work) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation. For example, exploring methods that directly model segment proposals or use more sophisticated confidence estimation techniques.
* **Clarity of Figure 2:** Figure 2, illustrating the spatial confidence map feature, is a bit difficult to understand. A clearer visual representation would improve comprehension.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results, particularly state-of-the-art object detection performance.
* Novel and conceptually sound algorithm for a challenging problem.
* Clear presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
* Good positioning within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical analysis.
* Weak justification for the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model.
* Lack of detail regarding hyperparameter tuning.
* Limited comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** with minor revisions. The e-SVM algorithm presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The empirical results are compelling, and the method addresses a practical problem. While the theoretical analysis is limited and some details regarding regularization and hyperparameter tuning are missing, these issues do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend accepting this paper contingent on the authors providing more details on hyperparameter selection, a more thorough justification for the regularization term, and potentially exploring a broader range of comparison methods. Specifically, including a brief discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential avenues for future research would be beneficial.
