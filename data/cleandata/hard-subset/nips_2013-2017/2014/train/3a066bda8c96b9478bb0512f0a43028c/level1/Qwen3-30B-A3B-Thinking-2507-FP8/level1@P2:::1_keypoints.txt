- Introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for weak supervision with continuous "positiveness" labels.  
- Models labels as Bernoulli random variables with success probability equal to positiveness.  
- e-SVM weights labels in SVC; latent e-SVM handles unobserved positiveness (e.g., bounding boxes).  
- Regularization in latent e-SVM designed to prevent degenerate solutions.  
- Applied to semantic segmentation and object detection with state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007.  
- Technical formulation is sound and well-motivated (Equation 1) with solid theoretical foundations.  
- Latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1) is carefully designed but may be hard to follow for some readers.  
- Experimental design is comprehensive, using appropriate baselines (SVC, SVR) and new metrics (TAPC, NDCG).  
- Results convincingly show e-SVM outperforming baselines on VOC 2011 segmentation and VOC 2007 detection.  
- Paper lacks deeper analysis comparing continuous-label approach to regression-based methods like SVR.  
- Writing and organization are clear; mathematical formulations are precise.  
- Needs more intuitive justification for the Bernoulli assumption µi = g(ui).  
- Novel contribution to weakly supervised learning; treats labels as random variables rather than weighting data points.  
- Differentiates from weighted SVMs and MIL, but could contrast more with recent weakly supervised methods.  
- Significant practical impact for vision tasks by reducing annotation costs and improving detection AP (+1.5 over RCNN).  
- Scalability to much larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and comparisons to post-2013 methods are not addressed.  
- Strengths: novel continuous-supervision formulation, solid theory, strong empirical results, latent-variable extension, new evaluation metrics.  
- Weaknesses: limited recent comparisons, insufficient justification of Bernoulli model, regularization term not fully evaluated, latent optimization complexity not fully addressed, literature connection could be broader.  
- Recommendation: paper is a valuable contribution and recommended for acceptance.  
- Pro acceptance: strong theory, clear formulation, significant benchmark gains; Con acceptance: limited recent comparisons and some theoretical/scale concerns.
