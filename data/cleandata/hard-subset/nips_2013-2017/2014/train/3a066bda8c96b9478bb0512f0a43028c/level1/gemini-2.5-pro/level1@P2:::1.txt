### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, termed the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning problems where labels are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" score within the range [0, 1]. This setting is highly relevant in computer vision, where, for instance, the quality of an object segment proposal is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground-truth annotation. The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its known positiveness score. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this latent label distribution.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown and only weaker labels, such as bounding boxes, are available. In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are estimated jointly with the classifier weights using an alternating optimization procedure, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The paper situates this work relative to common alternatives like Support Vector Classification (SVC), which requires ad-hoc thresholding of the positiveness scores, and Support Vector Regression (SVR), which treats the problem as standard regression without considering the bounded nature of the label. The proposed method is empirically validated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. Notably, by integrating features from the latent e-SVM into the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, the authors achieve a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed e-SVM formulation is sound, well-motivated, and provides an elegant way to incorporate soft labels into the max-margin framework. The optimization problem for the basic e-SVM can be solved with standard solvers, which is a significant practical advantage. The extension to the latent e-SVM is a logical and powerful step for handling weak supervision, and the proposed alternating optimization with a KL-divergence based regularizer is a reasonable approach to solve the non-convex problem.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
1.  The authors rightly identify that standard metrics are ill-suited for the segment classification sub-task and propose two new, sensible evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG).
2.  The comparison against strong baselines (SVC with multiple thresholds and SVR) on this sub-task clearly demonstrates the superiority of the e-SVM formulation.
3.  The application to semantic segmentation and object detection shows the practical utility of the learned models. The claims are well-supported by the results, particularly the improvement over the R-CNN baseline on VOC 2007, which is a significant achievement. The authors are honest about their method being a component that improves an existing system rather than a complete, novel detection pipeline.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for addressing weakly supervised learning with "positiveness" scores is well-established in the introduction. The mathematical formulation of both the e-SVM and the latent e-SVM is presented precisely in Section 3. The use of figures and an algorithm box helps in understanding the overall framework and optimization process. The experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail for the reader to understand what was done and appreciate the significance of the outcomes.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide more detail on the optimization of `u` in Algorithm 1 (line 4). While the paper states the objective is convex in `u` when `w` is fixed, it is not immediately obvious if a closed-form solution exists or what numerical method was used. Adding this detail would enhance reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core contribution—the expectation loss formulation for SVMs—is novel. While it bears a conceptual relationship to weighted SVMs (WSVMs), the authors correctly distinguish their approach: e-SVM effectively weights the positive and negative class losses for a *single* data point, whereas WSVMs assign a single weight to the entire loss of a data point. This is a subtle but important distinction that is tailored specifically to the problem of learning from positiveness scores.

The application to object detection, while building heavily on the existing R-CNN framework, introduces a novel "spatial confidence map" feature. This is a creative and effective way to integrate segmentation-based cues into a bounding-box-based detector. The introduction and justification of new evaluation criteria (TAPC, NDCG) for the segment classification task is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are highly significant.
1.  It addresses a practical and increasingly important problem: learning from supervision that is cheaper to obtain than full, pixel-perfect labels. The proposed method provides a principled way to leverage such weak labels.
2.  The demonstrated performance improvement on the challenging PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark is a strong testament to the method's effectiveness. Achieving a 1.5% mAP improvement over a very strong baseline like R-CNN is a non-trivial result that would likely be of great interest to the research community and practitioners.
3.  The e-SVM algorithm is general and could potentially be applied to other domains beyond computer vision where supervision might come in the form of confidence scores or probabilities rather than hard labels. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by future research in weakly supervised learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Introduces a novel, elegant, and principled e-SVM formulation for learning from continuous "positiveness" labels.
*   **Pro:** The latent e-SVM provides an effective method for learning from weak supervision (bounding boxes), which is a very important problem.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are strong, including a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method is general and has high potential for impact in other areas of machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con (Minor):** The paper lacks theoretical analysis, such as convergence guarantees for the latent e-SVM's alternating optimization.
*   **Con (Minor):** Some implementation details (e.g., the specific solver for the latent variable `u` update) could be clearer to facilitate full reproducibility.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning with compelling empirical validation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
