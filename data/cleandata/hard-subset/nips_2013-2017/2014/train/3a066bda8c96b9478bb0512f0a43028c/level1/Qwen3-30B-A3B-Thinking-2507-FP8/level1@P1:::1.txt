

# Peer Review

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm, a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where training samples are provided with continuous "positiveness" values (measuring how strongly a sample belongs to the positive class) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a theoretically sound framework that treats labels as random variables with Bernoulli distributions, where the success probability is a function of the observed positiveness. This approach extends to weak supervision scenarios by treating positiveness as latent variables. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper is well-positioned within the context of weakly supervised learning. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs (WSVMs), noting that MIL assumes at least one "completely" positive instance in a positive bag (which doesn't hold for segment proposals with partial overlaps), and WSVMs weight examples rather than labels. The paper references relevant work including recent NIPS papers like [19] on weakly-supervised dual clustering and [17] on learning with privileged information, but could better contextualize the contribution within the broader NIPS weakly supervised learning literature from 2013-2014.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical formulation of e-SVM is technically sound, with clear derivation of the loss function and convex optimization properties. The latent e-SVM extension for weak supervision is well-motivated and properly justified. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification that better measure performance on the continuous target problem. The results on segment proposal classification (outperforming SVC and SVR) and object detection (1.5% improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 over RCNN) are convincing and well-supported. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for regularization in the latent case to prevent convergence to local minima.

The clarity of the paper is generally good. The organization is logical, with clear sections on motivation, related work, methodology, and experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. Figures 1 and 2 effectively illustrate the framework and spatial confidence map feature. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including details about CPMC segment proposals, feature extraction, and evaluation criteria. One minor weakness is that the connection between the theoretical formulation and the implementation details (e.g., the specific regularization term in Equation 3) could be made more explicit.

The originality of the contribution is notable. While there are related works on weakly supervised learning, the e-SVM's specific formulation that weights labels rather than examples to handle continuous confidence values represents a meaningful advancement. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous methods, particularly in addressing the specific problem of segment proposals with partial overlaps. The application to computer vision tasks is well-motivated and demonstrates the approach's generality.

The significance of the work is high. The paper addresses a practical problem in computer vision: reducing the need for expensive pixel-level annotations by leveraging bounding box annotations. The method's flexibility (applicable to any segment proposal detector, image features, or classifier) and its ability to improve state-of-the-art object detection performance (60.0% average AP on PASCAL VOC 2007) demonstrate clear practical value. The results on semantic segmentation (comparable to SVR, the state-of-the-art for this task) further support the approach's effectiveness.

Potential improvements could include:
1. More explicit comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond SVC and SVR (e.g., deep learning approaches for weakly supervised object detection)
2. Further justification for the specific regularization term used in Equation 3, perhaps with ablation studies on alternative regularizers
3. Better contextualization of the contribution within the NIPS weakly supervised learning literature from 2013-2014

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to weakly supervised learning. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experiments are well-designed and convincing, and the results demonstrate clear practical impact. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a novel approach that addresses an important problem in computer vision. The e-SVM method is a valuable addition to the weakly supervised learning toolkit.

Pro acceptance arguments:
- Novel formulation addressing a specific gap in weakly supervised learning
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear optimization properties
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple tasks
- Significant improvements over baselines (1.5% AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007)
- Practical applicability to reducing annotation costs

Con acceptance arguments:
- Limited comparison to very recent weakly supervised learning methods (2013-2014)
- Some aspects of the regularization term could be better justified
- Could better connect to recent NIPS papers on weakly supervised learning

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art. I recommend acceptance.
