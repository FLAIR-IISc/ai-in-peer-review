The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for problems where only a “positiveness” value, rather than a binary label, is available for each training sample. The algorithm is presented as a generalization of SVMs, handling continuous confidence scores in a more principled way than simply thresholding. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in weakly supervised learning and SVMs. The motivation – reducing the reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations – is well-established (e.g., [6] and the introduction acknowledges this). The use of segment proposals (e.g., CPMC [4]) as a basis for learning is also common (e.g., [5]). The paper positions itself relative to existing approaches like SVC and SVR, noting their limitations when dealing with continuous confidence values. Related work in weighted SVMs [23, 27] and multiple instance learning [7, 2] are also discussed, appropriately differentiating the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation appears to be a reasonable extension of standard SVMs to handle continuous confidence scores. The idea of weighting samples based on their “positiveness” is intuitive and potentially powerful.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling, particularly the state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007. The consistent improvements over SVC and SVR in segment proposal classification are also significant.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the algorithm’s flexibility – it can work with various segment proposal detectors, image features, and classifiers.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of the e-SVM model and the latent extension is clear and concise.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on multiple tasks and datasets, demonstrating its versatility. Introduction of TAPC and NDCG as segment proposal evaluation metrics is a good contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors explain the loss function, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the properties of e-SVM (e.g., convergence guarantees, generalization bounds) would strengthen the paper.  Is the proposed loss function convex? This should be explicitly stated.
* **Regularization in Latent e-SVM:** The choice of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model (Equation 3) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough justification or comparison with alternative regularization strategies would be beneficial. The sensitivity of performance to the regularization parameter is not discussed.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper does not provide sufficient details about hyperparameter tuning. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results and reproduce them.
* **Comparison to More Recent Object Detection Methods:** While achieving SOTA on VOC 2007 is impressive, the comparison is to R-CNN [14]. More recent and powerful object detection frameworks (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO, SSD) should be considered for a more current benchmark.
* **Implementation Details:** The lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The work addresses a practical problem in computer vision – reducing the need for expensive annotations.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The regularization strategy in the latent e-SVM model could be better justified.
* Lack of detail on hyperparameter tuning and code availability hinder reproducibility.
* Comparison to more recent object detection methods would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a reasonable and effective approach for handling continuous confidence scores, and the experimental results are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding theoretical analysis and reproducibility, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting the paper with the following revisions:

1.  **Clarify the convexity of the loss function.**
2.  **Provide a more thorough justification for the choice of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model, and discuss the sensitivity of performance to its parameter.**
3.  **Include more details about hyperparameter tuning.**
4.  **Consider comparing the method to more recent object detection frameworks.**
5.  **Ideally, release the code to facilitate reproducibility.**
