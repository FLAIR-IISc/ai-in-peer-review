**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that can handle situations where only continuous values of "positiveness" are available for binary labels, rather than explicit binary labels. The algorithm is designed to work with weakly supervised data, where the exact positiveness value of each training example is unobserved. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on segment proposal classification tasks under both strong and weak supervision, as well as on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. The algorithm achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper relates to previous work on weakly supervised learning, multiple instance learning, and weighted SVMs. However, the authors argue that their problem setting is distinct from previous work, as it involves continuous values of positiveness rather than binary labels or image-level labeling. The authors also discuss the connection to instance-weighted SVMs in the literature, highlighting the difference between their approach and previous work.

**Technical Soundness**

The paper appears to be technically sound, with a clear and well-motivated formulation of the e-SVM algorithm. The authors provide a thorough derivation of the loss function and optimization procedure, and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on several datasets. The use of a latent variable to model the unobserved positiveness values is a novel and effective approach to handling weak supervision.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clear, with a logical structure and concise language. The authors provide sufficient background and context for non-experts to understand the problem and approach. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the figures and tables are helpful in illustrating the concepts and results.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel and original approach to handling weakly supervised data, particularly in the context of computer vision tasks. The use of a continuous value of positiveness rather than binary labels is a distinctive aspect of the paper, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on several datasets.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for computer vision and machine learning research, particularly in the context of weakly supervised learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets, including the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, and achieve state-of-the-art object detection performance. The paper also highlights the potential for using weakly supervised data to reduce the amount of labeling required for computer vision tasks.
