Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “positiveness” scores u ∈ [0,1] instead of binary labels. It models the label Y ∈ {−1,+1} as Bernoulli with mean µ = g(u) (set to u in experiments), and minimizes the expectation of hinge loss over this label distribution, yielding a per-example loss g(u)·max(0,1−w·x) + (1−g(u))·max(0,1+w·x) plus ℓ2 regularization. The method is extended to a weakly supervised scenario (latent e-SVM), where u is unobserved and regularized via a KL term toward a proxy ubb derived from IoU with ground-truth bounding boxes; alternating minimization over w and u is used. Applications are shown to segment proposal scoring (using CPMC segments) and to downstream semantic segmentation and object detection (augmenting R-CNN with pooled “spatial confidence map” features). On VOC11 segmentation, e-SVM outperforms SVC and slightly outperforms SVR on proposed TAPC and NDCG metrics; on VOC07 detection, concatenating the proposed features to R-CNN yields about +1.2 to +1.5 mAP.

The idea of training with soft labels is closely related to fuzzy/soft-label SVMs and cost-sensitive hinge losses (e.g., Lin & Wang 2002 Fuzzy SVM; class-dependent hinge losses; label-noise/uncertain-label SVMs), and to posterior regularization for latent variables (Ganchev et al. 2010). The latent formulation is conceptually related to latent SVM (Felzenszwalb et al.) but uses a convex regularizer anchoring u to ubb. In computer vision, there is extensive work on weakly supervised segmentation (e.g., Vezhnevets et al. 2012; Liu et al. 2013; MIL-based models) and on integrating segmentation cues into detection.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive loss: expected hinge under a Bernoulli label leads to a convex objective in w with standard SVM machinery, and a straightforward latent extension.
- Addresses a practically important supervision regime (IoU-like soft targets or bounding-box-only annotations) common in segmentation/detection pipelines.
- Spatial confidence map feature offers a sensible way to integrate segment cues with window-based detectors; the empirical +1–1.5 mAP on VOC07 is practically meaningful for a strong baseline (R-CNN).
- Introduces task-agnostic evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for segment proposal scoring.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: the expected hinge with a soft label is mathematically equivalent to a label-dependent, “soft” class-weighted hinge loss; closely related formulations exist (fuzzy/soft-label SVMs, uncertain-label SVMs, cost-sensitive SVMs). The paper’s positioning versus this literature is insufficient; the claimed distinction from weighted SVMs (“weights labels instead of data points”) is largely notational—one can implement this by duplicating examples with different signs and weights.
- Latent e-SVM lacks theoretical treatment: no discussion of identifiability, convergence guarantees beyond alternating convex steps, or sensitivity to λR. The update for u (with g(u)=u) admits a per-sample convex problem; a closed-form or efficient numerical update is not derived or described. Without the KL regularizer the problem collapses to 0/1; with it, the solution will hinge on λR, yet hyperparameter selection is not detailed.
- Choice of g(u)=u is ad hoc. Since IoU is not a calibrated probability of positiveness, exploring monotone transforms (e.g., logistic, learned isotonic calibration) would be natural. No ablation on g is provided.
- Baselines are narrow. For regression to [0,1], logistic regression trained with soft targets or bounded regression losses could be competitive; Ranking-based baselines (e.g., RankSVM) are especially relevant given NDCG. For weakly supervised segmentation, comparisons to contemporary MIL/weakly supervised segmentation methods are missing. For detection, comparisons to alternative ways of injecting segment cues (e.g., DPM+segmentation, parts+seg) would strengthen claims.
- Experimental gains on segment scoring are modest (slightly better than SVR), and the semantic segmentation numbers are comparable rather than clearly superior. The detection improvement, while useful, comes from feature concatenation rather than an end-to-end integration.
- Clarity/organization issues: notation in Eq. (1) is error-prone (duplicated N/N, inconsistent bolding), and the connection to standard SVM solvers is not fully explained (e.g., how per-example positive/negative weights are implemented in liblinear). Algorithm 1 omits stopping criteria and the precise solver for u. Several references are incomplete or malformed, and some reported R-CNN baselines (e.g., 58.5 “with” bbox regression) do not seem to match the original paper; please reconcile.
- Reproducibility gaps: key hyperparameters (λw, λR), optimization details for u, runtime and computational overhead (especially the dense spatial pooling) are not reported; TAPC/NDCG definitions would benefit from exact threshold grids and normalization choices.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of proposing a convex expected-hinge loss and an alternating latent extension; however, important baselines and ablations are missing, and the latent optimization is under-specified. Empirical evidence is mixed: small but consistent improvements over SVR for segment scoring, and a modest yet nontrivial boost to detection.

Clarity
Readable overall, but the notation, algorithmic details, and references need polishing. Providing a derivation for the u-update, a precise description of how to use standard SVM solvers, and more rigorous definitions for the proposed metrics would improve clarity.

Originality
The core loss is a straightforward instance of training with soft labels via expected surrogate loss; related ideas have appeared in fuzzy/uncertain-label/cost-sensitive SVMs and posterior regularization. The spatial confidence map feature is a reasonable engineering contribution, but similar pooling strategies are common.

Significance
Given the pervasiveness of weak supervision in vision, the approach is relevant. The detection improvement is likely to interest practitioners circa 2014. That said, the conceptual contribution is incremental and would benefit from stronger empirical validation and positioning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position e-SVM against prior soft-label/fuzzy/uncertain-label SVMs and posterior-regularization methods; clarify mathematical equivalences/differences.
- Provide the per-sample closed-form or efficient Newton update for u, discuss convergence, and include sensitivity plots for λR and for choices of g(u).
- Add baselines: logistic regression with soft targets, RankSVM, isotonic calibration; and, for weak supervision, comparisons to MIL/weakly supervised segmentation methods.
- Report more implementation details and ablations: effect of m, pooling types, segment generator (CPMC vs superpixels), and computational cost.
- Clarify and correct references and R-CNN baseline numbers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant weak-supervision setting with a simple, generalizable loss.
- Provides consistent improvements over common baselines and a tangible mAP gain on VOC07 when combined with a strong detector.
- Easy to implement and potentially useful beyond vision.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing soft-label/cost-sensitive SVM literature; insufficient related-work coverage.
- Under-specified latent optimization and lack of theoretical analysis.
- Modest gains on segment scoring; limited baselines and ablations reduce confidence that the method is the right tool versus simpler alternatives.
