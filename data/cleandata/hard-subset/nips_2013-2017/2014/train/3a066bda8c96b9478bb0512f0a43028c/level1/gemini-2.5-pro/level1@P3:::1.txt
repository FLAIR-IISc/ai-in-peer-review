### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel SVM variant, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning problems where training labels are not binary but are instead continuous "positiveness" values in a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]). This situation is common in computer vision, where an object proposal's quality is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea is to model the true, unobserved binary label as a Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is the given positiveness value. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this label distribution. This formulation elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM-like problem that can be solved efficiently with standard solvers.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness values themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness values are treated as latent variables and inferred jointly with the classifier weights via an alternating optimization procedure, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate.

The method is validated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. The most significant result is its application to object detection, where features derived from the latent e-SVM, when combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector [14], achieve a new state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The e-SVM formulation is sound, well-motivated, and its connection to the standard SVM hinge loss is clear. The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors introduce two new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment classification task, which seem appropriate for evaluating ranked predictions against a continuous ground truth. The empirical results convincingly show that e-SVM outperforms sensible baselines like thresholded SVC and SVR. Most impressively, the method demonstrates a non-trivial improvement of 1.5 mAP over the very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007, which is a significant result and a strong validation of the approach.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical formulation of both the basic and latent e-SVM models is presented logically. Figure 1 provides a helpful overview of the framework.

However, a few areas could be improved for clarity:
1.  The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 could be more detailed. While the text describes the process of pooling confidence scores into spatial bins, a more explicit description or a clearer diagram illustrating the feature construction for a single candidate window would be beneficial.
2.  The use of "additive kernels approximation mapping" [25] is mentioned as a final step in feature creation. Its importance is not entirely clear. An ablation study showing the performance with and without this mapping would help the reader understand if this is a critical component or a minor engineering choice.

#### **Originality**
The core contribution, the e-SVM formulation, is novel. While it bears resemblance to instance-weighted SVMs (WSVMs), the authors correctly distinguish their approach by highlighting that e-SVM weights the positive and negative loss components for each example separately, based on the positiveness score `u_i`. This is a principled and original way to incorporate continuous supervision directly into the max-margin framework. Previous work often resorts to heuristics like thresholding the continuous score to create binary labels or treating it as a standard regression problem (SVR), both of which are shown to be suboptimal here. The application of this idea to build complementary features for a CNN-based detector is also a novel and effective combination of techniques.

#### **Significance**
The significance of this work is high. It addresses a practical and common problem in machine learning and computer vision: how to learn from supervision that is weaker than perfect binary labels but stronger than, for example, image-level labels. The e-SVM provides an elegant, effective, and easy-to-implement solution. The fact that it can be solved with standard linear SVM solvers is a major advantage that will facilitate its adoption.

The most significant impact is the demonstrated improvement on object detection. At a time when methods like R-CNN [14] have substantially advanced the state of the art, showing a further clear improvement is a notable achievement. It demonstrates that combining segmentation-based cues (learned via e-SVM) with powerful region-based deep features can push performance boundaries, likely inspiring further research into hybrid detection models.

### **Areas for Improvement**
While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to strengthen it further:

*   **Justification of Design Choices:** The latent e-SVM model involves several design choices that are presented without extensive justification. For example, the function `g(u)` is set to the identity, `g(u_i) = u_i`. While simple, it would be useful to discuss if other mappings were considered. More importantly, the regularization term `R(u)` is based on KL-divergence. A brief justification for this choice over simpler alternatives, like a squared L2-norm `||u - u_bb||^2`, would be valuable. Does the KL-divergence offer specific advantages, for instance, in terms of its gradients or convexity properties?
*   **Latent Variable Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent model is a standard approach but is prone to local minima. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the initialization of the latent variables `u`. Are the results stable?
*   **Generalizability:** All experiments are within computer vision. While the method is presented as general, its applicability would be even more convincing with a small-scale experiment on a non-vision dataset where continuous or probabilistic labels are available.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The e-SVM is a simple, principled, and novel method for learning from continuous "positiveness" labels.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform strong baselines (SVC, SVR) and, most importantly, improves upon the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector on a major benchmark.
3.  **High Practical Significance:** The problem setting is common, and the proposed solution is both effective and easy to implement using existing tools, increasing its potential for broad impact.
4.  **Clear Extension to Weak Supervision:** The latent e-SVM provides a practical way to apply the method when only bounding-box supervision is available.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Justification for Some Design Choices:** The rationale behind the specific form of the latent variable regularizer is not fully explored.
2.  **Incremental Nature of the Detection Result:** While achieving SOTA, the improvement in object detection comes from adding a new feature to a very powerful existing system rather than proposing a fundamentally new detection paradigm. (Note: This is a weak argument against acceptance, as such improvements are valuable contributions.)

In summary, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated learning algorithm and demonstrates its effectiveness in a compelling way on a challenging and important task. I recommend acceptance.
