This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for scenarios where training data is weakly supervised, specifically when only a “positiveness” score (between 0 and 1) representing the degree of affiliation to the positive class is available instead of a binary label. The authors extend this to segment classification under weak supervision, demonstrating its effectiveness in segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. They achieve state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The core idea – weighting examples in an SVM based on their “positiveness” rather than binarizing them – is intuitively sound and addresses a clear limitation of directly applying standard SVMs or SVRs to this type of problem. The paper builds upon existing work in weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs, and clearly articulates how e-SVM differs from these approaches. The authors correctly identify that their problem setting falls between standard MIL and fully supervised learning, and propose a method tailored to this scenario. The extension to a latent variable model for fully weak supervision is also a logical and well-presented extension. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a sensible and novel extension to traditional SVMs, specifically designed to handle continuous confidence values instead of hard binary labels.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The loss function is clearly defined and the connection to standard SVM solvers is advantageous.  The iterative optimization procedure for the latent variable model is well-described.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. Achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection is a significant achievement. The inclusion of comparisons against SVC and SVR establishes a strong baseline.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the model formulation is presented logically.
* **Generality:** The authors explicitly state the method’s flexibility with respect to segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors, which enhances its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Latent Variable Model Regularization:** While the paper acknowledges the importance of the regularization term in the latent variable model, the justification for the specific choice of the Kullback-Leibler divergence-based regularization (Equation 3) is somewhat lacking. A more detailed discussion of why this particular regularization is suitable, or a comparison to alternative regularization schemes, would be beneficial.  The potential for other divergence measures or constraints could be explored.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λR in the latent variable model, the threshold used for initializing the latent variables). How robust are the results to different hyperparameter settings?
* **Comparison to More Recent Object Detection Methods:** While achieving state-of-the-art results with RCNN is commendable for 2013, the field of object detection has rapidly advanced since then (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO, SSD).  A brief discussion of how e-SVM might perform in conjunction with these newer detectors, or a comparison against them, would strengthen the paper’s relevance.
* **Tuning of CPMC segment proposals:** The performance can be easily affected by the number or the quality of the CPMC segment proposals. It would be useful to show the effect of different parameter settings of the CPMC on the performance. 
* **NDCG Explanation:** While introducing NDCG as a novel evaluation metric, the paper does not fully explain its relevance in this specific context. A more detailed justification for its use would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The e-SVM algorithm offers a principled and effective approach to weakly supervised learning, addressing a practical limitation of standard machine learning techniques.
* The comprehensive experimental results demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness across multiple computer vision tasks.
* Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset (PASCAL VOC 2007) is a significant contribution.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the technical details of the proposed method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
* The comparison to more recent object detection methods is missing.
* The explanation of the latent variable model regularization is somewhat superficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated extension to traditional SVMs, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and including a discussion of how the method might perform with more recent object detection architectures.  A more detailed justification for the choice of regularization in the latent variable model would also be beneficial.
