

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised settings where training data is labeled with continuous "positiveness" values (between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors formulate the problem by treating the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness value. The e-SVM model extends standard Support Vector Classification by weighting samples according to their positiveness, while the latent e-SVM version handles cases where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The paper applies this method to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical foundations. The e-SVM formulation (Equation 1) is well-motivated and logically derived from the problem setting. The latent e-SVM extension (Equation 2) is carefully designed with a proper regularization term (Equation 3) to prevent degenerate solutions. The experimental design is comprehensive, with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that directly measure the quality of the continuous confidence prediction. The results on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and 2007 object detection are convincing, with e-SVM outperforming baselines consistently. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of why the continuous label approach is superior to SVR (which also handles continuous targets) beyond the presented results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical formulations are precise, though the latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with alternating optimization techniques. The connection between the technical formulation and practical applications (semantic segmentation and object detection) is well-explained. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) is particularly valuable for the problem domain. However, the paper could improve by providing more intuitive explanations for why the Bernoulli distribution assumption (µi = g(ui)) is appropriate, and why this approach differs fundamentally from regression-based methods.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the weakly supervised learning literature by addressing a specific problem setting that differs from standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and standard binary classification. The e-SVM formulation is novel in its treatment of labels as random variables with probability equal to the continuous label. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from related work on weighted SVMs (Section 2), noting that e-SVM weights labels rather than data points. The application of this method to semantic segmentation and object detection with continuous supervision is also novel, though the core technique could have broader applicability. While the paper acknowledges related work (e.g., [5, 18] for segmentation), it could strengthen its originality claim by more thoroughly contrasting with recent weakly supervised methods like [19, 20, 22].

**Significance:** The problem addressed (weakly supervised learning with continuous labels) is highly significant for computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. The paper demonstrates practical impact by achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (60.0 AP, +1.5 over RCNN), which is a meaningful improvement in the field. The method's flexibility (applicable to any segment proposal detector, image features, and classifiers) enhances its potential impact. The approach could substantially reduce labeling costs for large-scale datasets, which is increasingly important as datasets grow in size. However, the paper could strengthen the significance argument by discussing how this approach scales to much larger datasets (like ImageNet) and how it compares to more recent weakly supervised methods published after 2013.

**Strengths:**
- Novel formulation of the learning problem with continuous supervision
- Solid theoretical foundation with proper mathematical derivation
- Strong experimental results on two important computer vision tasks
- Clear extension to weak supervision with latent variables
- Introduction of appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for the problem

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with more recent weakly supervised methods beyond 2013
- Insufficient justification for the Bernoulli distribution assumption
- The regularization term in the latent e-SVM (Equation 3) is not thoroughly evaluated
- Does not fully address the computational complexity of the latent optimization
- The connection to existing weakly supervised learning literature could be more comprehensive

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to weakly supervised learning with a well-motivated approach that addresses a significant practical problem in computer vision. The technical quality is strong, the experimental results are convincing, and the method has clear potential for broader application. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing some of the weaknesses noted above, it represents a significant improvement over existing approaches for this specific problem setting. The results on object detection (1.5% AP improvement) are particularly compelling given the high baseline of RCNN at the time.

**Pro Acceptance:** Strong theoretical foundation, clear problem formulation, significant experimental results on standard benchmarks, practical relevance to computer vision.

**Con Acceptance:** Limited comparison with more recent weakly supervised methods, some theoretical aspects could be better justified, and the significance could be more fully demonstrated by discussing scalability to larger datasets.

Overall, this paper meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It presents a novel solution to a well-motivated problem with strong experimental validation, making it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
