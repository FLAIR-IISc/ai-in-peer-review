This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from data with continuous-valued "positiveness" labels in the [0, 1] interval, rather than standard binary labels. This problem is common in computer vision, where the quality of a segment proposal is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea is to treat the true binary label as a latent random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, where the success probability is the known positiveness value. The e-SVM loss is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this distribution. This approach extends prior work on SVMs by providing a principled way to handle non-binary supervision, differing from Support Vector Regression (SVR) by constraining the target to a bounded interval and from weighted SVMs by weighting the positive and negative loss components for each sample. The authors further propose a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings (e.g., only bounding boxes are available), where the positiveness itself is treated as a latent variable optimized iteratively with the model weights.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the expectation loss is a novel and elegant way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision into the max-margin framework. The problem it addresses is highly significant, as obtaining fine-grained, pixel-level annotations is a major bottleneck in scaling up datasets. Demonstrating a clear improvement (+1.5% mAP) on a strong, contemporary baseline like R-CNN for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007 underscores the practical importance of this work.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations are clear, and the extension to the latent variable model with a KL-divergence regularizer is well-justified to prevent trivial solutions. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including the introduction of new and appropriate metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for the segment classification sub-task, and validation on multiple large-scale vision tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The performance gains over SVR in the fully supervised setting appear modest (described as "comparable" for segmentation and "slightly better" for classification). A more detailed analysis of why e-SVM is theoretically or practically superior to a well-tuned SVR would strengthen the paper.
*   The contribution in the object detection task relies on using the e-SVM output as an additional feature for an existing system (R-CNN). While effective, this is a less integrated application of the core model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Proposes a novel, well-motivated loss function for an important and practical problem in weakly supervised learning.
2.  Provides strong empirical evidence of its utility by improving a state-of-the-art object detection model.
3.  The method is general and could be impactful beyond the specific computer vision applications explored.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The performance improvement over the SVR baseline in fully supervised tasks is not substantial, which slightly tempers the impact of the core e-SVM model.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, novel, and significant contribution with solid theoretical and empirical support. The strengths strongly outweigh the minor weaknesses.
