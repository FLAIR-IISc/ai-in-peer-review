

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples are annotated with continuous "positiveness" values (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors present a novel extension to standard SVMs that treats the label as a random variable with probability determined by the positiveness value. The method handles both strong supervision (positiveness observed) and weak supervision (positiveness latent, inferred during training). The approach is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical foundation for the e-SVM formulation and a well-designed experimental evaluation on PASCAL VOC datasets. The authors provide thorough comparisons using appropriate metrics (TAPC, NDCG) and show state-of-the-art results on object detection (60.0 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007). The method's ability to handle weak supervision with bounding box annotations is particularly valuable for reducing annotation costs in computer vision.

Strengths include a clear problem formulation, solid empirical results, and practical significance for real-world applications. The paper is well-organized and clearly written with helpful figures. The connection to related work on weakly supervised learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs is generally good, though the theoretical connection to weighted SVMs could be more detailed.

Weaknesses include limited theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the latent e-SVM extension and somewhat superficial discussion of how the method relates to existing weighted SVM approaches. While the results are promising, the paper could better discuss the limitations of the approach and potential failure cases.

The work is highly significant as it addresses a practical bottleneck in computer vision (annotation burden) with a generalizable framework that could benefit other domains. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and achieves demonstrable improvements on standard benchmarks. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical discussion.
