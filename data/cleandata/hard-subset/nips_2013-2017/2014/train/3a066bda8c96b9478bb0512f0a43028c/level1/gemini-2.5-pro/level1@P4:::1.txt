### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, termed the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning problems where training labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" scores within the interval [0, 1]. This scenario is common in computer vision, for example, where a segment proposal's quality is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground-truth object. The proposed e-SVM model treats the binary label for each sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, with its parameter determined by the known positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this latent label. This results in a convex objective that can be solved using standard SVM solvers.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting, creating the latent e-SVM, for cases where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding-box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this formulation, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are optimized jointly with the model parameters using an alternating optimization procedure. A regularization term is introduced to keep the inferred positiveness scores close to an initial estimate derived from the weak labels (e.g., IoU with a bounding box).

The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, introducing two new metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and demonstrating superior performance against standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR). The paper then applies the method to semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, the learned e-SVM model is used to generate a "spatial confidence map feature," which, when combined with the features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, improves performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, achieving a new state-of-the-art result at the time of publication.

### Qualitative Evaluation

**Quality: Is the paper technically sound?**

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the e-SVM loss as an expectation of the hinge loss is mathematically well-founded and leads to a convex optimization problem. The extension to the latent e-SVM model uses a standard and appropriate alternating minimization scheme to solve the bi-convex problem. The introduction of a KL-divergence-based regularizer to constrain the latent variables is a sensible choice that prevents trivial solutions. The experimental methodology is solid, particularly in the object detection task where the proposed method is integrated with a strong, state-of-the-art baseline (R-CNN) and demonstrates a clear and significant improvement. The claims are well-supported by the empirical results presented.

**Clarity: Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for addressing learning with "positiveness" scores is clearly articulated, and the distinction from standard classification (SVC) and regression (SVR) is well explained. The mathematical derivations for the e-SVM and latent e-SVM models are presented clearly. The overall framework and its application to vision tasks are easy to follow.

However, there are minor areas where clarity could be improved for reproducibility:
-   The optimization step for the latent variables `u` in Algorithm 1 (line 4) is not explicitly detailed. While the objective function is provided, the closed-form solution or optimization procedure for `u` is not shown.
-   The construction of the final feature for the object detection task, including the application of "additive kernels approximation mapping," could be described in greater detail.

**Originality: Are the problems or approaches new?**

The core contribution, the expectation loss SVM, is a novel formulation. While weighted SVMs exist, the authors correctly point out that e-SVM differs by effectively assigning separate weights to the positive and negative loss components for each sample, which is a direct and principled consequence of the expectation-based derivation. This approach provides a new way to incorporate soft or continuous labels directly into a max-margin framework. Applying this framework to the problem of learning from segment proposal IoUs under both strong and weak (bounding box) supervision is also a novel application. The method for generating and integrating the spatial confidence map feature into a modern object detector is a valuable and original engineering contribution. The paper adequately references and distinguishes its work from related approaches in weakly supervised learning, such as Multiple Instance Learning.

**Significance: Are the results important?**

The results presented in the paper are significant.
1.  The e-SVM provides a general and effective tool for a class of problems common not only in computer vision but potentially in other fields where supervision signals are non-binary.
2.  The demonstration of state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark is a strong indicator of the method's practical impact. Improving upon a very strong baseline like R-CNN, even by 1.5% mAP, is a non-trivial achievement and shows that incorporating segmentation-based cues via the proposed method provides complementary information to powerful deep learning features.
3.  The work highlights the value of learning from weak supervision, which is critical for scaling up learning algorithms to large datasets where detailed annotation is infeasible. The latent e-SVM offers a practical solution for leveraging widely available bounding-box annotations.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Formulation:** The e-SVM model is an elegant, well-motivated, and novel contribution to the SVM literature for handling continuous-valued supervision signals.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant improvement over the state-of-the-art on a standard and challenging object detection benchmark (PASCAL VOC 2007).
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem of learning from weak supervision (e.g., bounding boxes) is of high practical importance, and the paper presents an effective solution.
*   **Generality:** The proposed method is general and could be applied to other proposal-based vision tasks or even problems outside of computer vision.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some implementation details concerning the optimization of latent variables and feature construction are not fully specified, which could hinder exact reproducibility.
*   **Weaker Segmentation Results:** The semantic segmentation results are presented as "comparable" to the baseline without providing a direct quantitative comparison, making this part of the evaluation less compelling than the object detection results.
*   **Novelty of Evaluation Metrics:** The primary evaluation of the core segment classification model relies on two newly proposed metrics (TAPC and NDCG). While they appear reasonable, their validation against more established metrics, if available, would strengthen the claims.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly the novelty of the e-SVM formulation and the significant object detection results, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses.
