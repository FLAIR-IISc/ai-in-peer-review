**Summary**

This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel formulation for learning from data where supervision is provided not as a binary label, but as a continuous "positiveness" score within the range [0, 1]. The core idea is to frame the hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the expectation is weighted by the given positiveness score. This approach effectively assigns a cost to each training example for both the positive and negative classes, proportional to its positiveness and negativeness (1 - positiveness), respectively. The authors extend this model to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings (e.g., using bounding box annotations) where the positiveness scores are unobserved and are treated as latent variables, optimized via an alternating procedure. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using the PASCAL VOC dataset. The experiments demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) for segment proposal classification. Furthermore, when used to generate features for an object detector, the method provides a significant performance boost over the state-of-the-art R-CNN system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weak supervision. As datasets grow, the cost of obtaining fine-grained, pixel-level annotations becomes prohibitive. The proposed framework, which can learn from continuous confidence scores or weaker forms of annotation like bounding boxes, is highly relevant to the NIPS community and computer vision practitioners.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The proposed evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification (TAPC and NDCG) are well-motivated. The e-SVM consistently outperforms strong baselines like SVC and SVR in this task. More impressively, the features derived from the latent e-SVM lead to a 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 detection when combined with the R-CNN, which was the state-of-the-art at the time. This demonstrates that the method provides complementary information and has significant practical value.

3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is clearly explained. A key advantage of the e-SVM is its simplicity; the basic model can be solved using any standard SVM solver that supports instance-level weights, making it easy to implement and deploy.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The primary concern with this paper is the novelty of the proposed e-SVM formulation. The loss function in Equation 1 is: `sum_i [ g(u_i) * max(0, 1 - w^T x_i) + (1 - g(u_i)) * max(0, 1 + w^T x_i) ]`. This is mathematically equivalent to training a standard SVM on a duplicated dataset where each original example `x_i` contributes two examples: a positive-labeled example `(x_i, +1)` with a misclassification cost of `g(u_i)`, and a negative-labeled example `(x_i, -1)` with a misclassification cost of `(1 - g(u_i))`. The concept of instance-dependent costs has been a standard feature in SVM implementations (e.g., SVMlight, LibSVM) for many years and is often referred to as weighted SVM (WSVM). While the authors mention WSVMs, their argument that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points" seems to be a superficial distinction. The paper would be much stronger if it explicitly acknowledged this connection and framed its contribution more as a novel and effective application of instance-weighting for this specific problem, rather than as a fundamentally new SVM algorithm.

2.  **Analysis of the Latent Model:** The extension to the latent e-SVM is interesting but lacks technical depth. The optimization procedure alternates between solving for the model weights `w` and the latent positiveness scores `u`. This is a form of coordinate descent on a non-convex objective function. The paper provides no discussion of the optimization's properties, such as guarantees of convergence or the quality of the local minimum found.

3.  **Effect of Regularization:** In the latent model, a strong KL-divergence based regularization term is used to keep the learned latent positiveness `u_i` close to its initialization `u_bbi`. This raises the question of how much the latent variables are actually being "learned" versus simply being anchored to their initial state. A discussion on the sensitivity of the model to the regularization parameter `lambda_R` and an analysis of how much the `u_i` values change during training would be valuable to understand the dynamics of the latent model.

4.  **Flawed Motivation:** In the introduction, the authors state that SVR is not ideal because "the value of positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]." This is not a valid reason to dismiss standard regression techniques. Many widely-used regression models, such as logistic regression, are specifically designed to predict values within a bounded interval. This weakens the motivation for the proposed method over SVR.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a significant and timely problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   The experimental results are strong, showing clear improvements over relevant baselines and advancing the state-of-the-art on a competitive object detection benchmark.
*   The proposed method is simple, effective, and of practical use to the community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The primary technical contribution, the e-SVM, appears to be a re-formulation of the well-established instance-weighted SVM, raising significant concerns about novelty.
*   The analysis of the latent variable model is superficial, lacking discussion of the non-convex optimization or the impact of the strong regularization.

**Minor Comments:**
*   Abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

In conclusion, while the paper demonstrates impressive empirical results on important problems, the lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the core method is a major issue. I would be willing to reconsider my evaluation if the authors can provide a more compelling argument for how their method fundamentally differs from, or significantly advances, prior work on instance-weighted SVMs.
