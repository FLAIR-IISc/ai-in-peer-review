This paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a variant of hinge-loss minimization designed for settings where each example has a “positiveness” score u ∈ [0,1] instead of a binary label. The key idea is to take the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label model with Pr(Y=+1|x)=g(u) (set to u in experiments), yielding an objective where each example contributes to both positive and negative hinge terms with weights g(u) and 1−g(u), respectively. The method extends naturally to weak supervision by treating u as latent and introducing a convex regularizer that keeps u close to an initial estimate (IoU with the ground-truth bounding box), optimized by alternating minimization. The approach is instantiated for segment proposal classification and then used to build spatial confidence map features that are concatenated with RCNN features for object detection. The authors also propose two task-agnostic evaluation metrics for segment scoring (TAPC and NDCG). Empirically, e-SVM outperforms SVC with thresholding and slightly improves over SVR for segment scoring; for semantic segmentation it is comparable to SVR; for VOC07 detection, adding their feature to RCNN yields +1.2 to +1.5 mAP.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself between fully supervised segmentation and MIL-style weak supervision, arguing that standard MIL assumptions (at least one “pure” positive instance per bag) do not hold for segment proposals. It also discusses weighted SVMs (WSVM) and latent SVMs. However, the proposed expected hinge loss with soft labels is very close to “soft-label/fuzzy” SVMs and instance-dependent cost-weighting (e.g., SVMs with example-dependent costs), and can be implemented by duplicating each example with labels +1 and −1 and weights g(u) and 1−g(u). The latent extension resembles latent SVM with an additional convex regularizer on u. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning relative to soft-label SVMs, WSVM, and probabilistic label SVM variants, and a more precise explanation of what is genuinely new.

Strengths:
- Simple, general objective that can be solved with off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers.
- Clean extension to weak supervision with a convex regularizer on latent u and an intuitive alternating scheme.
- Practical impact: nontrivial performance gains for object detection when combined with RCNN (+1.5 mAP on VOC07).
- Sensible segment-scoring metrics (TAPC/NDCG) that decouple evaluation from downstream tasks.
- The spatial confidence map feature is a practical way to aggregate segment confidences into window-level descriptors.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is limited. The basic e-SVM is essentially an expected hinge loss under soft labels, closely related to fuzzy/soft-label and instance-weighted SVMs. The connection to existing example-dependent cost frameworks (e.g., SVMlight/LibLinear with per-example weights) and to duplicating samples is not fully articulated.
- The latent optimization is non-convex; no analysis of convergence properties, conditions, or sensitivity is provided.
- The regularizer on u appears strong; empirically, do learned u deviate meaningfully from initialization? Without ablations, it is unclear whether gains come primarily from simply using u_bb as fixed weights.
- Claim that a bounded target in [0,1] is “not standard regression” is inaccurate; logistic/Poisson/Beta regression and probabilistic regression are standard. A cross-entropy loss with soft labels is a natural baseline that is not evaluated.
- Some clarity issues: small typos (e.g., “continues”→“continuous”), inconsistent notation in the objective, and incomplete references.

Quality: Experiments are sound at a high level, with fair comparisons to SVC/SVR on segment scoring and a strong application to detection. However, important ablations are missing: (i) fixing u=ubb (no latent update); (ii) varying λR to show the effect of regularization strength; (iii) testing alternative g(u) (e.g., calibrated/nonlinear mappings); (iv) sensitivity to initialization and number/quality of proposals. Comparisons to probabilistic/logistic models with soft labels would strengthen the case.

Clarity: Generally readable, with a clear description of the objective and training algorithm. The presentation of the loss could be streamlined, and the connection to existing SVM variants clarified. The new evaluation metrics are useful but should be specified more precisely (threshold grid for TAPC, NDCG gain definition, and whether per-class or pooled).

Originality: Moderate. The expected hinge loss with soft labels and the latent extension with a convex proximity term are incremental relative to fuzzy/weighted SVMs and latent SVMs. The spatial confidence map feature is a practical engineering contribution.

Significance: Moderate. The approach is easy to adopt and yields measurable gains in a prominent application (VOC07 detection with RCNN). As a general recipe for leveraging weak supervision via soft-label hinge loss, it could be used by practitioners. However, the theoretical and algorithmic novelty is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify equivalence/relationships to WSVM, fuzzy/soft-label SVMs, and show how e-SVM differs beyond implementation convenience.
- Add baselines with logistic regression (cross-entropy) using soft labels u and with example-weighted SVM (using u as weights) to test whether the “two-sided” weighting is essential.
- Provide ablations on λR, initialization, and alternative g(u); report statistics on how much u moves from u_bb.
- Discuss convergence behavior (monotonicity of the alternating scheme, stopping criteria) and runtime.
- Improve writing/typos and unify notation in the objective.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general technique with practical benefits and easy adoption.
- Solid empirical gains in object detection; consistent improvements over thresholds/SVC/SVR for segment scoring.
- Useful evaluation tooling (TAPC/NDCG) and a neat feature construction for detection.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to existing soft/weighted SVM formulations and latent SVM; theoretical contribution is thin.
- Missing key baselines (soft-label logistic) and ablations to isolate where the gains come from.
- Some claims (re regression) are inaccurate; clarity and positioning need work.

Overall, this is a useful, well-executed application of soft-label hinge-loss training with a weakly supervised extension. Its practical value is evident, but the paper should better acknowledge and differentiate from prior art and include stronger analysis and ablations.
