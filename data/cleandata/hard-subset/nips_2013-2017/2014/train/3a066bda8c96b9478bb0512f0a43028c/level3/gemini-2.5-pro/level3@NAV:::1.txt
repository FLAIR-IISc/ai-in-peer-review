This paper introduces the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel formulation for learning classifiers from data with continuous-valued "positiveness" labels, as opposed to traditional binary ones. The core idea is to treat the standard hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the expectation is weighted by the observed positiveness score (e.g., IoU overlap). This approach can be seen as an elegant extension of both weighted SVMs and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), effectively replacing the binary concept of an instance's presence in a positive bag with a soft, continuous affiliation. The authors apply this framework to the important domain of visual learning under weak supervision, training segment classifiers using only bounding-box annotations by treating the positiveness scores as latent variables.

The paper has several significant strengths.
*   **Significance:** It addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of reducing the reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations in computer vision. Learning effectively from weaker supervision is a critical area of research, and this work makes a solid contribution.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound, and the experiments are comprehensive and well-executed. The authors validate e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection, showing it outperforms sensible baselines like SVC and SVR. Crucially, they demonstrate that augmenting a state-of-the-art detector (R-CNN) with features from their latent e-SVM yields a non-trivial performance boost on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-motivated, and logically organized, making the proposed method and its evaluation easy to follow.

In terms of originality, the e-SVM is a novel and well-motivated extension of familiar techniques rather than a completely new paradigm. The formulation to handle both observed and latent positiveness scores is elegant and represents a valid contribution to the field. The paper has no major weaknesses; while the contribution is more incremental than revolutionary, it presents a complete, impactful, and well-validated piece of work. The approach is generalizable and likely to be adopted or built upon by other researchers working on weakly supervised learning. Given its clarity, strong empirical results, and importance to the NIPS community, this paper makes a fine contribution.
