

This paper proposes an "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples have continuous "positiveness" values in [0,1] rather than binary labels. The authors extend this to a latent-weight version for weak supervision scenarios where exact positiveness values are unobserved. The method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with experiments showing improvements over standard SVM classification and regression approaches.

The paper is well-written and clearly describes the method and its applications. The experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection (particularly the 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007) demonstrate practical benefits of the approach. The authors provide sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

However, the novelty claim is problematic. The core idea of weighting examples by their positiveness values is equivalent to existing "example-dependent costs" in SVM literature (e.g., SVMlight), and the paper fails to clearly distinguish this work from prior contributions. The latent-weight extension, while useful, appears straightforward without theoretical justification for the non-convex optimization approach. The heavy regularization used (with weights staying close to initial values) should be explicitly discussed, as it suggests the learned weights don't significantly deviate from the initial IoU-based estimates.

A notable error appears in the abstract ("continues" should be "continuous") and in the incorrect claim that [0,1] bounded values make it "non-standard regression" (logistic regression is standard and bounded to [0,1]). The related work section inadequately addresses existing weighted SVM approaches, making it difficult to assess the paper's contribution.

While the method is technically sound and produces good empirical results, the unclear novelty and theoretical gaps undermine the contribution. The significance is limited to specific computer vision applications without broader implications for machine learning. The paper would benefit from clearer differentiation from prior work, theoretical analysis of the optimization, and addressing the regularization concerns. I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and theoretical grounding.
