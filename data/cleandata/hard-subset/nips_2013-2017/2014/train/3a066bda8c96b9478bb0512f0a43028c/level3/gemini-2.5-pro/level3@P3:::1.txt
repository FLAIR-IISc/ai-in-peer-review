This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), for classification problems where training labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]). The core idea is to model the binary label of each instance as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its known positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random label. This formulation elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM where each sample contributes a soft weight to both the positive and negative classification costs.

The authors extend this model to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings, where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available for a segmentation task). In this case, the model treats the positiveness scores as latent variables and optimizes them jointly with the classifier weights using an alternating optimization scheme. This approach is a sensible extension of multiple-instance learning (MIL), effectively replacing the assumption of a binary positive instance in a bag with a continuous, soft-weighted one.

The proposed method is validated on the tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, the learned e-SVM classifiers are used to generate spatial confidence map features, which are then combined with features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, leading to improved performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance & Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weak supervision, which is crucial for scaling up visual learning systems while mitigating the high cost of detailed annotation. The formulation of learning from continuous "positiveness" labels (like IoU) is a natural and well-motivated fit for many computer vision tasks, moving beyond the often-forced binarization of such scores.
*   **Clarity:** The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The motivation for e-SVM over alternatives like thresholded SVC or SVR is compelling. The progression from the basic e-SVM to the latent e-SVM for weak supervision is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Quality & Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors introduce two new and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification sub-task, which is a valuable contribution in its own right. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms strong baselines (SVC, SVR) in both the fully supervised and weakly supervised settings. The successful application to object detection, showing a clear performance gain over the very strong R-CNN baseline, highlights the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** The technical contribution, while elegant, is an incremental extension of existing ideas. The e-SVM can be viewed as a specific instance of a weighted SVM, and the latent e-SVM is conceptually similar to latent SVMs and other alternating optimization schemes used in weakly supervised learning. The primary novelty lies in the specific formulation of the expectation loss and its direct application to handle continuous soft labels, rather than a fundamentally new learning paradigm.
*   **Latent Variable Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is guaranteed only to find a local minimum. While the authors correctly note that regularization on the latent variables `u` is crucial to prevent trivial solutions, a deeper analysis of the optimization landscape, sensitivity to initialization, or convergence properties would have strengthened the paper.
*   **Feature Integration:** The method of combining the learned segmentation cues with the R-CNN detector via simple feature concatenation is straightforward but somewhat ad-hoc. While this serves as a proof-of-concept, exploring more principled or tightly coupled integration methods could be a direction for future work.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses an important and highly relevant problem in weakly supervised learning.
    *   The proposed e-SVM is a simple, elegant, and principled solution for handling continuous-valued "positiveness" labels.
    *   The model can be solved using standard SVM solvers, making it practical and easy to implement.
    *   The experimental validation is strong, with convincing results on segment classification, semantic segmentation, and a notable improvement on a state-of-the-art object detection system.
    *   The paper is very well-written and easy to understand.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The core technical idea is an incremental extension of weighted/latent SVMs, representing a novel combination of familiar techniques rather than a breakthrough.

**Conclusion:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed e-SVM provides a clean and effective way to handle a common type of weak supervision in computer vision, where the quality of a positive example is a continuous variable. The clarity of the presentation and the strength of the experimental results, particularly the improvement on a very competitive object detection baseline, make a compelling case for the method's utility. While the core technical novelty is incremental, the problem formulation is insightful and the overall work represents a valid and useful NIPS contribution that others are likely to build upon. I recommend acceptance.
