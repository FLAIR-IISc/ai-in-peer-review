Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “expectation loss SVM” (e-SVM), a modification of the hinge loss where each training example contributes to both positive and negative hinge losses, weighted by a per-example “positiveness” ui ∈ [0,1] (in the experiments, ui is IoU). Concretely, the loss for example i is g(ui)·max(0,1−w⊤xi) + (1−g(ui))·max(0,1+w⊤xi), with g(ui)=ui in practice. The method is then extended to a weakly supervised scenario where ui is unobserved and treated as a latent variable, initialized from bounding-box IoU (ubb) and regularized via a convex KL term that strongly penalizes deviation from ubb. Alternating minimization is used: standard linear SVM on w given u, and convex updates of u given w.

The approach is evaluated on segment proposal scoring and semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC 2011, and as an additional cue for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007 by concatenating class-specific spatial confidence maps derived from e-SVM with RCNN features. Results show e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on proposal scoring and yields +1.2–1.5 mAP when combined with RCNN.

The idea closely relates to instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs (e.g., SVMlight’s example-dependent costs), fuzzy SVMs (membership weights in (0,1]), and soft-label/ambiguous-label SVM formulations where labels are probabilistic. The latent extension resembles EM-like alternating optimization used in latent SVMs and weak supervision (MIL variants), augmented with a strong entropy-style regularizer to stay near ubb.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem setting; the loss is well described and easy to implement with off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers.
- Empirical section is thorough for CV applications, with both strong- and weak-supervision settings. The spatial confidence map is a practical way to inject segment cues into detector pipelines.
- Consistent improvements over SVC/SVR for segment scoring; non-trivial gains on VOC07 detection when concatenated with RCNN features.
- The latent formulation is intuitive and convex in each block, and the chosen KL regularizer is sensible and pragmatic.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: The basic e-SVM objective is effectively an instance-weighted, example-dependent cost SVM with two label replicas per example: one positive weighted by g(ui) and one negative weighted by 1−g(ui). This can be implemented directly with existing weighted/cost-sensitive SVM solvers (e.g., SVMlight’s example-dependent costs, Liblinear with per-example Cs, fuzzy SVM). The paper claims to “weight labels instead of data points,” but mathematically the two are equivalent in standard SVM implementations. The paper should position itself relative to weighted SVM, fuzzy SVM, and soft-label SVMs, and clearly articulate what is new.
- Latent extension: The alternating procedure is non-convex; no theoretical analysis (e.g., monotonic decrease, convergence to stationary points) or identifiability discussion is provided. Given the very strong regularizer that anchors u to ubb, it is unclear whether the learned u deviates meaningfully from initialization. This undermines the claim that estimating latent u is crucial.
- Missing baselines/ablations: (i) Fix u=ubb (no latent updates); (ii) instance-weighted SVM (or fuzzy SVM) using the same u; (iii) logistic regression trained on soft labels ui (a standard choice for [0,1]-valued targets). These are necessary to support the claimed advantages. The current SVC/SVR comparisons are weak for establishing novelty and superiority.
- Incorrect statement: The paper states that regression is “not standard” because the target is bounded in [0,1]. Logistic regression (and general probabilistic linear models) handles [0,1] targets naturally; even SVR can be used with bounded targets. Please correct.
- Methodological clarity: Optimization details for updating u (closed-form vs. gradient-based) are not described; complexity and runtime are not reported. For detection, ensure fairness when comparing to RCNN: same training splits, data augmentation, and whether the additive kernel mapping alone changes baselines.
- Writing/typos: “continues” → “continuous” (abstract, intro). Several reference entries are incomplete.

Quality
- The empirical results are promising and generally sound, but the absence of comparisons to the most directly related baselines (weighted/fuzzy SVM, soft-label logistic regression) weakens the evidence. The latent optimization is reasonable but lacks analysis, and its actual contribution may be small under heavy regularization.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Please add: (i) an explicit equivalence to instance-weighted SVM or a careful distinction if you believe it is different; (ii) details for the u-update step; (iii) sensitivity plots for λR and the degree of deviation of u from ubb (histograms, mean absolute deviation per class).

Originality
- Limited. The core supervised objective is a special case of example-dependent cost/fuzzy SVM or soft-label SVM. The latent extension with KL regularization is a straightforward application of block coordinate descent with strong anchoring. The spatial confidence map feature is a practical engineering contribution but not conceptually novel.

Significance
- For practitioners, the recipe is useful: turn soft IoU signals into per-example weights and integrate segment cues for detection. The detection gains suggest potential impact. However, without stronger novelty or deeper analysis, the scientific contribution is modest.

Requested revisions and suggestions
- Situate e-SVM among: instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVM (e.g., SVMlight example-dependent costs), fuzzy SVM (membership weights), and soft-label/probabilistic-label SVMs; add appropriate citations and, if distinct, prove the distinction formally.
- Add baselines: weighted/fuzzy SVM, soft-label logistic regression, and u=ubb (no latent update). Report sensitivity to λR and show how much u moves from ubb.
- Provide optimization details for u; discuss convergence behavior and initialization sensitivity.
- For detection, isolate contributions: show results with spatial confidence maps derived from SVR or weighted SVM to ensure gains are due to the loss, not just the feature.
- Correct the regression claim and typos; complete references.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical method; easy to adopt with standard solvers.
- Solid empirical gains on well-known CV tasks.
- Weak supervision framing resonates with current large-scale annotation constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty over existing weighted/fuzzy/soft-label SVM formulations is unclear.
- Latent extension lacks theoretical and empirical evidence of learning meaningful u beyond initialization.
- Missing key baselines and ablations.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The paper would be considerably strengthened by positioning relative to weighted/fuzzy/soft-label SVMs, adding the missing baselines, and analyzing the latent weights’ behavior under regularization. If these issues are addressed, the work could be a useful contribution for weakly supervised learning in vision.
