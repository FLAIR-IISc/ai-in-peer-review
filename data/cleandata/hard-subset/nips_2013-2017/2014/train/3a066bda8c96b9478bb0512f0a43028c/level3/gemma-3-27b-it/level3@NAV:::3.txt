## Review of “Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm”

This paper proposes a novel Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for semantic labeling and object detection using weakly supervised data, specifically bounding box annotations. The core idea is to address the continuous nature of “positiveness” (e.g., IoU) by weighting the SVM loss function based on this value, and extending this to a latent variable setting when the positiveness is unobserved. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The approach builds on established techniques like SVMs and segment proposals but attempts to bridge the gap between fully supervised and weakly supervised learning by directly modeling the confidence of segment proposals. While the idea of weighting samples based on their “correctness” is intuitive, the algorithmic novelty is limited. A significant weakness is the insufficient acknowledgement of prior work. The authors fail to cite crucial literature on weighted SVMs (e.g., Suykens et al., Yang et al., Lapin et al., Vapnik) and transductive SVMs, particularly those that address instance weighting. The imposed sum-constraint on weights, while potentially novel, hasn't been thoroughly investigated or justified. The proposed latent e-SVM appears largely equivalent to a weighted transductive SVM, lacking sufficient differentiation. 

Despite these shortcomings, the work demonstrates practical significance. Achieving state-of-the-art object detection with a conventional approach (rather than relying on deep learning) is a notable contribution and will likely be of interest to the community. The experimental results are compelling, showcasing performance gains in both semantic segmentation and object detection. However, the authors should justify the choice of sum-constrained weighted SVM over standard regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression, providing baseline comparisons. Further detail on the selection of regularization coefficients (λW and λR) is also needed.

The paper suffers from clarity issues. The title and abstract overstate the contribution and incorrectly imply that unit-interval targets are less informative than binary labels. The exposition is uneven, spending excessive space on minor algorithmic variations while lacking detail on critical feature engineering choices. Several minor typographical errors were observed.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong empirical results, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset. Potential for impact in weakly supervised learning.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited algorithmic novelty, critical omissions in related work, clarity issues, and insufficient justification for methodological choices.

**Recommendation:** Reject with request for major revisions. The authors *must* address the missing literature, clarify the novelty of their approach, and improve the clarity and justification of their methods. This work may be more suitable for a computer vision-focused venue. The quality score is contingent on addressing the prior-work deficiencies.
