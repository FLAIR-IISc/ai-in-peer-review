

# Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where each training example has a "positiveness" value in [0,1] rather than a binary label. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for cases where the positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The method treats the positiveness as the probability of a positive label and formulates a loss function that weights examples based on this value. The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, claiming state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper relates to previous work on weighted SVMs (WSVMs) but does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution. The core idea of weighting examples based on instance-specific costs is well-established in the SVM literature (e.g., SVMlight [27] allows example-dependent costs, and the paper's approach is mathematically equivalent to these methods). The authors cite weighted SVMs in Section 2 but fail to clarify how their approach differs from these existing methods. The claim that "positiveness in [0,1] makes it non-standard regression" is incorrect, as logistic regression is a standard regression method with outputs bounded to [0,1], and the authors' approach is essentially a form of weighted regression.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper is well-written with clear experimental results demonstrating that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR in both strong and weak supervision scenarios for semantic segmentation and object detection.
2. The application to object detection is practical and shows meaningful improvements (1.5% average AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007).
3. The experiments are thorough and cover two major computer vision tasks with appropriate baselines.
4. The latent extension to handle unobserved positiveness is conceptually straightforward and useful for real-world applications where pixel-level annotations are unavailable.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty concern:** The method appears to be a straightforward application of existing weighted SVM techniques, with no clear distinction from prior work. The authors do not adequately address how their approach differs from standard example-dependent cost methods.
2. **Lack of theoretical analysis:** The latent e-SVM involves non-convex optimization, but the paper provides no theoretical analysis of convergence or optimality guarantees.
3. **Heavy regularization:** The paper uses strong regularization on the latent weights (Equation 3), suggesting the learned weights stay close to the initial bounding box IoU values. This important detail is not discussed, and the authors should address why the weights don't diverge from the initial values.
4. **Incorrect claim:** The paper incorrectly states that "positiveness in [0,1] makes it non-standard regression" when logistic regression is a standard bounded regression method.
5. **Minor typo:** The abstract contains a typo ("continues" should be "continuous").

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-executed experiments, but the theoretical foundation is weak. The experimental results support the claims about performance improvements, but the methodological contribution is questionable due to the lack of novelty.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to experiments. The description of the latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1) is clear. However, the incorrect regression claim and lack of discussion about the heavy regularization reduce the paper's technical accuracy.

**Originality:** The core idea is not novel, as the method is equivalent to standard example-dependent cost approaches in SVMs. The paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from prior work, which is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission.

**Significance:** The application to computer vision tasks is relevant and useful, particularly for reducing annotation requirements. The modest improvements in object detection (1.5% AP gain) are meaningful for practitioners. However, the significance is limited by the lack of novelty in the core method.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a practical approach to weakly supervised learning with demonstrated benefits in two important computer vision tasks.
- The experiments are thorough and well-designed, showing consistent improvements over baselines.
- The latent extension is useful for real-world applications where pixel-level annotations are unavailable.

*Con:*
- The core method is not novel, as it is equivalent to existing weighted SVM techniques.
- The paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from prior work.
- The heavy regularization used is not discussed, and the weights' behavior is not analyzed.
- The incorrect claim about regression undermines the paper's technical credibility.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a useful application of existing techniques to computer vision tasks, the lack of novelty in the core method is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission. The authors have not sufficiently demonstrated how their approach differs from standard weighted SVM methods. The paper would benefit from a thorough literature review clarifying the relationship to prior work on example-dependent costs, a discussion of the regularization's impact on weight learning, and correction of the incorrect regression claim. Without these improvements, the contribution does not meet the novelty threshold expected at NIPS.
