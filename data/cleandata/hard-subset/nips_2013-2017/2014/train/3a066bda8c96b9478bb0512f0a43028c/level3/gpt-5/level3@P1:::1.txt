This paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple, general formulation for learning when each training instance has a continuous “positiveness” score u ∈ [0,1] rather than a binary label. The core idea is to model the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability g(u) (taken as u in experiments) and minimize the expected hinge loss: a convex weighted combination of positive and negative hinge losses where weights are g(u) and 1−g(u), respectively. This yields a standard linear SVM objective with instance-dependent label weights and can be solved with off-the-shelf solvers (e.g., LibLinear). The authors further extend the method to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where u is unobserved and regularized towards a proxy ubb derived from bounding-box IoU via a convex KL (cross-entropy) penalty. Alternating minimization over w and u yields a biconvex optimization scheme.

The paper situates e-SVM relative to SVC (which requires thresholding u to form binary labels) and SVR (which regresses IoU despite its bounded nature), and contrasts with WSVMs that weight examples rather than labels. Compared to MIL/latent SVM approaches for weak supervision, the paper targets a different regime where instances are rarely purely positive and soft overlap is informative; the latent extension regularizes soft labels rather than enforcing at-least-one constraints. On vision benchmarks, e-SVM is used to learn segment confidence predictors with CPMC segments and second-order pooling features, and to augment RCNN for object detection via a spatial confidence map feature derived from segment scores. The method improves TAPC/NDCG against SVC/SVR on segment ranking, achieves competitive semantic segmentation, and yields +1.2 to +1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007 when concatenated with RCNN features.

Quality: The formulation is sound. In the observed case, the loss is convex and compatible with standard solvers; in the latent case, each subproblem is convex (linear in u plus convex KL) and the alternating scheme is reasonable, though only local optimality can be guaranteed. The experimental protocol (VOC’11 segmentation; VOC’07 detection with RCNN) is standard and results are persuasive. The introduction of task-agnostic segment-ranking metrics (TAPC and NDCG) is helpful. Some analysis is missing: sensitivity to g(·), λR, and initialization; convergence behavior; and run-time/memory comparisons to SVR/SVC.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The distinction between weighting labels and weighting instances is well articulated, and the latent regularizer is motivated and described with pseudocode. A few references appear malformed; tightening the related work around “probabilistic/soft-label SVMs” and learning with uncertain labels would further clarify novelty.

Originality: The expected-hinge-loss with soft labels is a natural and, to my knowledge in this exact SVM form, underexplored idea, though it is closely related to prior work on soft/probabilistic labels in large-margin learning and to WSVMs. The latent extension with a convex KL prior towards bounding-box-derived overlaps is a neat and practical twist distinct from MIL. The spatial confidence map feature is a straightforward but effective integration into RCNN.

Significance: The ability to train from soft and weak supervision is important for scaling vision systems. Improvements over SVR/SVC on segment ranking and the +1.5 mAP gain on VOC’07 (from a lightweight, general plug-in feature) suggest the approach will be useful. The gains on semantic segmentation are modest but competitive; the detection results are the stronger evidence of impact.

Strengths
- Simple, convex, and easily implemented formulation for soft labels; works with standard SVM solvers.
- Clean latent-variable extension with convex regularization and stable alternating optimization.
- Solid empirical validation on two major tasks; consistent improvements over baselines; non-trivial boost to a strong detector (RCNN).
- Introduction of task-agnostic metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for segment confidence prediction.
- Clear presentation and practical details (features, proposals, pooling, m×m spatial bins) enabling reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- g(u) is fixed to identity; consider learning/calibrating g (e.g., monotone link learned on validation) and report sensitivity.
- Limited analysis of hyperparameters (λ, λR, m, number/type of proposals), initialization (ubb), and convergence; include ablations and convergence plots.
- No comparison to prior “soft/probabilistic label SVM” or “learning with uncertain labels” literature; discuss conceptual/technical differences and add citations.
- The KL prior may anchor u too strongly to ubb; explore alternative convex priors (e.g., quadratic, Huber) and compare.
- Report per-class detection gains and error analysis (e.g., bounding box localization vs classification) to understand where the feature helps.
- Provide training time/memory overhead relative to SVR/SVC; clarify how dual hinge terms are implemented in LibLinear.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of g and λR? Did you try non-linear g (e.g., temperature-scaled) or learned calibration?
- Does the latent e-SVM ever push u away from ubb substantially, and in what visual scenarios?
- How many alternating iterations are typically needed, and do you observe oscillations?
- Would using other proposal methods (e.g., Selective Search/MCG/SLIC superpixels) change conclusions?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, common setting (soft/weak supervision) with a principled, simple method.
- Demonstrates clear empirical gains on strong baselines with minimal integration complexity.
- Method is general and likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is incremental relative to existing soft-label/weighted SVM ideas; theoretical analysis is light.
- Some empirical gains (segmentation) are modest, and ablations/sensitivity analyses are limited.

Overall: A solid, practically useful contribution with clear exposition and convincing detection improvements. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions to strengthen positioning and analysis.
