This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for binary classification problems where training labels are not discrete {-1, +1} but are continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]). This scenario is common in computer vision, where an object proposal's quality is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth bounding box.

The core idea is to model the unobserved binary label of a training example as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of its known "positiveness" value. The e-SVM loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random label. This elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM variant, where each sample contributes weighted positive and negative loss terms, and can be solved using standard SVM solvers.

The paper presents two versions of the model. The basic e-SVM handles the case where positiveness values are observed (e.g., from pixel-level annotations). More importantly, the authors propose a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings (e.g., only bounding box annotations) where the positiveness values are treated as latent variables. These are inferred via an iterative optimization procedure that alternates between updating the SVM weights and the latent positiveness values, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate. The method is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating superior performance over standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Regression (SVR) baselines and achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection by augmenting the R-CNN framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses the crucial and highly practical problem of learning from weak supervision. As datasets grow, the cost of detailed, pixel-level annotation becomes a major bottleneck. Methods that can effectively leverage cheaper forms of annotation, like bounding boxes, are of significant value to the community. This work provides a principled way to do so.

2.  **Originality:** The proposed e-SVM formulation is novel and well-motivated. It provides a more natural way to handle continuous-valued supervision than the common ad-hoc approaches of either thresholding the values to create binary labels for SVC or treating them as targets for SVR. While related to weighted SVMs, the specific formulation as an expectation over a latent binary label is a creative and elegant contribution. The extension to the latent variable model for the weakly supervised case is a key strength, moving beyond the typical Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework.

3.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The authors rightly point out the lack of standard evaluation metrics for the intermediate task of segment proposal classification and propose two new, sensible criteria: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). This is a valuable contribution in itself.
    *   The experiments clearly demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms strong baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR) on this task.
    *   The application to object detection is particularly compelling. Improving the performance of a very strong, state-of-the-art model like R-CNN by 1.5% AP on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a non-trivial achievement and underscores the practical utility of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is well-established, and the mathematical formulation of the e-SVM and its latent extension is described with sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Optimization and Convergence:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) alternates between two convex subproblems, but the overall objective function L(w, u) is non-convex. As the authors note, this can lead to local minima. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to the initialization of the latent variables `u` and its empirical convergence behavior.

2.  **Model Choices:** The model makes two simplifying assumptions that could be explored further. First, the probability of a positive label is set directly to the positiveness value (`µ_i = u_i`). A discussion on the sensitivity to this choice or the potential benefits of learning a more complex mapping `g(·)` could be beneficial. Second, the regularization term `R(u)` is based on KL divergence. While this is a reasonable choice, a brief justification for this over other potential regularizers (e.g., a squared L2 distance from the initial estimate `u_bb`) would be helpful.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The iterative training procedure for the latent e-SVM involves solving an SVM in each outer loop, which could be computationally intensive. A comment on the training time compared to the baseline methods would provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a novel, principled, and effective method for learning from data with continuous-valued ("soft") labels. The e-SVM formulation is elegant, and its extension to a latent variable model for weakly supervised settings is particularly significant. The strengths of this work—its relevance, originality, and strong empirical validation on challenging computer vision tasks—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The paper is a solid contribution to both machine learning and its application in computer vision and is well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
