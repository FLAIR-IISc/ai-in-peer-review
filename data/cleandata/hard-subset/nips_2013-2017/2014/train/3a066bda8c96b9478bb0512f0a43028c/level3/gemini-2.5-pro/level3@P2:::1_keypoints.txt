- Introduces expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for weakly supervised learning where labels are continuous "positiveness" scores (e.g., IoU in vision).
- Models binary labels as latent Bernoulli variables with known success probabilities and uses expected hinge loss, yielding weighted positive/negative loss terms solvable by standard SVMs.
- Extends to latent e-SVM when positiveness scores are unknown, jointly inferring scores and classifier via alternating optimization with a KL-divergence based regularizer to avoid trivial solutions.
- Applied to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC, showing improvements over SVC and SVR for segment proposal classification.
- Incorporating latent e-SVM features into R-CNN significantly improves object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Differentiates from weighted SVMs by weighting both positive and negative loss terms per sample and from MIL by handling partially correct instances without requiring a perfectly positive instance.
- Strength: tackles a practically important weak supervision problem, enabling learning from cheaper annotations like bounding boxes.
- Strength: technically sound, principled probabilistic formulation and reasonable alternating optimization for the latent case.
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with concise mathematical formulations and clear distinctions from related work.
- Strength: thorough experimental validation, including two new metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and strong empirical gains over baselines and prior state-of-the-art.
- Weakness: novelty seen as a combination of existing techniques (SVM, latent variables, alternating optimization) rather than a wholly new theory.
- Weakness: alternating optimization is non-convex and has no guarantees for global convergence; limited discussion of optimization landscape and initialization sensitivity.
- Weakness: regularizer choice (cross-entropy R(u)) is heuristic; could use more formal motivation or comparison to alternatives (e.g., L2 on u - u_bb).
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, practical algorithm with strong empirical results, clear presentation, and potential to inspire further weakly supervised work.
- Arguments against acceptance: perceived incremental novelty and lack of theoretical guarantees for the latent optimization.
