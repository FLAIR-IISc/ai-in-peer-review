This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that can effectively learn from weakly supervised data where only the "positiveness" of each training sample is available, rather than a binary label. The algorithm is an extension of the classical Support Vector Classification (SVC) models and can be readily extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision where the exact positiveness value of each training example is unobserved.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, formulation, and experimental results of the proposed algorithm. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work and clearly highlight the differences between their approach and existing methods. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm on various tasks, including semantic segmentation and object detection.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the proposed method. The idea of weighting each example in the loss function is not new and has been explored in existing work on weighted SVMs. The authors claim that their method is different from existing weighted SVMs because it weights labels instead of data points, but this distinction is not entirely clear. Furthermore, the latent extension of the algorithm, which is used to handle the weak supervision situation, is straightforward and does not seem to offer any significant theoretical or practical advantages over existing methods.

Another concern is the lack of theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. The authors do not provide any theoretical guarantees or bounds on the performance of the algorithm, which makes it difficult to understand its limitations and potential pitfalls. Additionally, the optimization algorithm used to train the latent e-SVM model is non-convex, and the authors do not provide any analysis of its convergence properties.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its components, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner. However, there are some minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel learning algorithm that can effectively learn from weakly supervised data. While there are some concerns regarding the novelty and theoretical analysis of the proposed method, the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness on various tasks. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The proposed algorithm is effective in learning from weakly supervised data.
* The experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on various tasks, including semantic segmentation and object detection.
