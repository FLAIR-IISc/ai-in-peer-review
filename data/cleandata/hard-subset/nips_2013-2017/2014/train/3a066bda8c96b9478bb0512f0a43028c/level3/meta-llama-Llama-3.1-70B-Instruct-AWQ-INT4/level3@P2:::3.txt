**Paper Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm**

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), that can handle weakly supervised data where only a continuous value representing the "positiveness" of a binary label is available. The e-SVM algorithm is designed to work with segment proposal classification tasks and can be extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The authors build upon previous work on weakly supervised learning, multiple instance learning, and weighted SVMs. However, they fail to cite several pertinent prior works, including Suykens et al., Yang et al., and Lapin et al. The proposed latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, which is not sufficiently differentiated from prior art.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel approach to handling weakly supervised data, which is a common problem in computer vision.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.
* The paper achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Weaknesses:

* The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, and many pertinent prior works are not cited.
* The imposed sum constraint on weights may be novel, but it is not thoroughly investigated.
* The paper spends excessive space on small algorithmic variations and too little on critical feature-engineering details.
* The title and abstract do not clearly convey the core contribution and make a misleading claim about unit-interval targets being less informative than binary labels.

**Quality**

The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough. However, the authors fail to provide crucial references, and the object detection system involves substantial engineering. The paper could benefit from more detail on how regularization coefficients (λW and λR) were chosen.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-organized, but the title and abstract could be improved to better convey the core contribution. The authors could also provide more detail on the critical feature-engineering details.

**Originality**

The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, and many pertinent prior works are not cited. However, the paper presents a novel approach to handling weakly supervised data, which is a common problem in computer vision.

**Significance**

The paper achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, which is a notable and high community interest result.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

Pro:

* The paper presents a novel approach to handling weakly supervised data, which is a common problem in computer vision.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.
* The paper achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

Con:

* The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, and many pertinent prior works are not cited.
* The imposed sum constraint on weights may be novel, but it is not thoroughly investigated.
* The paper spends excessive space on small algorithmic variations and too little on critical feature-engineering details.

**Recommendation**

I recommend publishing the paper after rewriting and addressing the prior-work relations. The paper may fit better at a computer-vision venue.
