### **Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for classification problems where training labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" values within the interval [0, 1]. The authors apply this method to computer vision tasks, specifically semantic segmentation and object detection. For this application, candidate object segments are generated, and their positiveness is defined by their Intersection over Union (IoU) with ground-truth annotations. The e-SVM is formulated to regress these IoU values. The paper further extends this to a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised scenarios where only bounding-box supervision is available, treating the precise IoU values as unobserved latent variables to be inferred during training. The core learning algorithms are variants of a weighted SVM and a weighted transductive SVM. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by integrating it with a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN) and achieving a new state-of-the-art result on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its empirical results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a highly competitive benchmark like PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant accomplishment. The fact that this improvement comes from augmenting a powerful deep learning-based system (R-CNN) with features learned via a more conventional SVM-based approach is particularly noteworthy. This suggests that sophisticated feature engineering and classical learning models can still provide complementary information to deep networks, which is a result of high interest to the machine learning and computer vision communities. The problem formulation itself, addressing learning from continuous-valued labels and weakly supervised data (bounding boxes), is well-motivated and highly relevant for practical, large-scale vision applications.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the strong results, the paper has several major weaknesses concerning originality, clarity, and scholarly quality.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, and this is exacerbated by a failure to cite a large body of highly relevant prior work.
    *   The proposed e-SVM is, in essence, an instance-weighted SVM. The loss function weights the positive and negative hinge-loss terms for each sample based on its "positiveness" value `u_i`. There is extensive literature on weighted SVMs for handling class imbalance, outliers, or incorporating prior knowledge, which is not referenced (e.g., Suykens et al., *Neurocomputing 2002*; Yang et al., *IJCNN 2007*).
    *   The latent e-SVM, where the positiveness `u` is treated as a latent variable, is functionally equivalent to a weighted transductive SVM (TSVM). The algorithm iteratively estimates the labels (here, the continuous weights `u_i`) for the "unlabeled" (weakly supervised) data and retrains the classifier. Foundational work on transductive inference (e.g., Vapnik, *1998*) and related methods for learning with label proportions or privileged information (e.g., Lapin et al., *NN 2014*) are not discussed, making the contribution appear more novel than it is.
    *   The authors claim the novelty lies in weighting labels instead of data points, but this results in a formulation that can be mapped directly to existing instance-weighted SVM frameworks. The imposed sum-constraint on the weights may be a novel contribution, but this is not clearly articulated or investigated. The paper needs to be thoroughly rewritten to correctly position its contributions within the context of this prior art.

2.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper's presentation obscures its core ideas and is misleading in places.
    *   The title and abstract do not clearly convey that the proposed method is a variant of weighted/transductive SVMs. Furthermore, the abstract's framing of [0, 1] interval targets as being somehow less informative than binary labels is confusing; a continuous value in [0, 1] objectively contains more information than a binary label.
    *   The paper dedicates excessive space to describing small algorithmic variations while providing insufficient detail on the components that are likely critical to its success, namely the feature engineering. The "spatial confidence map feature" is central to the object detection results but is described too briefly, making it difficult for a reader to fully understand and reproduce the work.

3.  **Experimental Methodology and Quality:**
    *   The experiments lack important baselines. To justify the use of the proposed e-SVM for what is effectively a unit-interval regression task, the authors should compare it against standard methods for this problem, such as regularized logistic regression or beta regression. The comparison is limited to SVR and a naively thresholded SVC.
    *   Crucial details regarding hyperparameter selection are omitted. The paper does not explain how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were chosen. This information is essential for reproducibility and for understanding the sensitivity of the model to these parameters.
    *   The overall quality is diminished by the significant omission of crucial references, which is a serious scholarly issue.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Prior Work:** The authors must thoroughly review and incorporate literature on weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs. The introduction and related work sections should be rewritten to frame the e-SVM as a specific application and potential extension of these well-established techniques, rather than an entirely new algorithm.
*   **Clarity:** Revise the title and abstract to be more precise. Rebalance the paper to provide more detail on the feature representation and integration with the R-CNN pipeline, as this is a key part of the empirical contribution.
*   **Baselines:** To strengthen the justification for e-SVM, please add experimental comparisons to standard regression models suitable for targets in [0, 1].
*   **Reproducibility:** Please provide details on how λW and λR were set (e.g., cross-validation on a held-out set).
*   **Minor Comments:**
    *   Line 19: "positiveness" is an awkward term. Consider "confidence score," "label proportion," or "regression target."
    *   Line 94: Notation `λw · 1/wTw` is unusual. Standard notation is `(λ/2) ||w||^2`.
    *   Line 98: Notation `N/N` is redundant.
    *   Line 350: "boundary regularization" should be "bounding-box regression."
    *   The phrasing "couple our appearance cues" and "simple concatenating" could be more formal.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents an important empirical result but is undermined by a flawed presentation of its novelty and a failure to engage with relevant prior work. The strong performance on VOC 2007 suggests the paper has merit and is likely to be of interest. However, the issues with originality and scholarship are too significant for acceptance in its current form.

I would be willing to raise my score if the authors undertake a major revision to address the relationship to prior work in weighted and transductive SVMs. The paper must be reframed to accurately reflect its contribution. Given its strong application focus, the work might also be a better fit for a premier computer vision venue. As it stands, its contribution to the core machine learning literature is not clearly differentiated from existing methods.
