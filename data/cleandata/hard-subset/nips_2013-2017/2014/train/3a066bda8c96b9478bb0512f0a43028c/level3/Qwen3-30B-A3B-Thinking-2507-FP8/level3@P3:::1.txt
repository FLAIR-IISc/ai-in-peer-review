

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces an expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) framework designed for learning from continuous "positiveness" labels rather than binary labels. The authors address an important problem in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain, but bounding box annotations are more readily available. The key insight is that segment proposals can be assigned continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU with ground truth), and the e-SVM model can leverage these values directly without requiring ad-hoc binarization thresholds.

The paper makes a clear connection to related work. It properly situates itself between standard SVM (which requires binary labels) and multiple instance learning (MIL), which assumes at least one "completely" positive instance per bag. The authors correctly argue that their setting is distinct from MIL because most segment proposals contain both foreground and background pixels, making the "completely positive instance" assumption invalid. The paper also relates to weighted SVMs, but notes that e-SVM weights labels rather than data points, leading to a fundamentally different formulation.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and elegantly presented. The e-SVM model formulates the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability derived from the observed positiveness. The resulting loss function is the expectation over this random variable, which can be efficiently optimized using standard SVM solvers. The latent extension for weak supervision scenarios is particularly well-executed, using an iterative optimization approach with a regularization term based on KL divergence to prevent the solution from collapsing to 0 or 1.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over SVC and SVR on segment proposal classification using both new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and standard segmentation metrics. The application to semantic segmentation shows promising results even with bounding box annotations, and the object detection results are particularly impressive, achieving a 1.5% average precision improvement over the state-of-the-art RCNN method on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The motivation is strong, the problem statement is precise, and the technical presentation is accessible. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including algorithm pseudocode and implementation specifics.

Strengths:
- Addresses a genuinely important problem in computer vision (reducing annotation costs)
- Novel approach that properly bridges the gap between strong and weak supervision
- Theoretical formulation is elegant and well-justified
- Experimental results are comprehensive and consistently show improvements over baselines
- Clear connection to related work with proper differentiation
- Well-written with good organization and sufficient detail

Weaknesses:
- The regularization term design (Equation 3) could be better motivated theoretically; the authors acknowledge it's a heuristic but don't explore alternatives
- The paper doesn't fully address how the method scales to very large datasets or complex scenes with many overlapping objects
- While the object detection results are impressive, the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis on which specific object categories benefit most from the proposed method

Quality: The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations and strong experimental validation. The authors are careful to compare against appropriate baselines and acknowledge the limitations of their approach. The results are well-supported by multiple metrics and experiments.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, motivation, and technical contributions are presented clearly, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Originality: The formulation of the expectation loss SVM represents a novel extension of SVM theory for continuous label problems. While related to weighted SVMs and MIL, the specific problem setting and solution are distinct and well-justified.

Significance: The problem addressed is highly relevant to computer vision, where large-scale datasets with pixel-level annotations are prohibitively expensive to obtain. The demonstrated improvements on standard benchmarks and the ability to work with bounding box annotations (which are widely available) make this work significant for both practitioners and researchers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant problem with practical impact
- Presents a clear, novel technical contribution
- Shows strong empirical results across multiple tasks
- Well-written and clearly motivated
- Represents a meaningful extension of SVM theory

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical analysis of the optimization convergence properties
- The regularization term could be better justified
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limits of the approach in more complex scenarios

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The e-SVM framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods on standard benchmarks. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, with the minor weaknesses not detracting from the overall significance and novelty of the work. I recommend acceptance.
