This paper proposes the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification to standard SVMs designed for problems with continuous “positiveness” values instead of binary labels, and extends this to a latent variable setting for weakly supervised learning. The core idea is to weight examples in the loss function by their positiveness, reflecting confidence in their label. Experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrate performance gains over standard SVM and SVR approaches.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. The experimental setup is reasonable and the results are presented comprehensively. The problem of learning with noisy or imprecise labels is a crucial one in computer vision, and the proposed approach addresses it in a conceptually sound manner. The extension to latent variables is also a valuable contribution, making the method applicable to weakly supervised scenarios, which are increasingly important due to the cost of full annotation.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of novelty. The core idea of weighting examples based on their confidence (or “positiveness” in this context) is not new. Methods like SVMlight and other approaches employing example-dependent costs have been around for a considerable time. The paper does not adequately differentiate itself from this prior work, and the claim of novelty is questionable. While the authors attempt to justify their approach by framing it in terms of continuous positiveness values, this argument is weak, as correctly pointed out in the rebuttal – standard regression techniques (like logistic regression) also operate on bounded outputs. The latent variable extension, while interesting, is a fairly straightforward application of existing techniques for handling hidden variables within an SVM framework. Furthermore, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the optimization process, particularly concerning the non-convexity introduced by the latent variable extension. 

The heavy regularization used in the latent variable setting is concerning. The authors acknowledge that learned weights tend to stay close to their initial values, suggesting that the optimization may be getting stuck in local optima or is heavily constrained. This observation should be discussed more thoroughly – what is the impact of this regularization on the learned model’s capacity? Why is such strong regularization necessary?

Finally, there is a minor typo in the abstract: “continues” should be “continuous.”

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Addresses a relevant problem (learning with noisy/imprecise labels).
*   Conceptually sound approach based on weighting examples.
*   Extension to latent variables for weak supervision.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation.
*   Demonstrated performance gains on standard datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea is not original.
*   Insufficient discussion of related work on example-dependent costs.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis, particularly regarding non-convexity.
*   Heavy regularization raises concerns about optimization and model capacity.
*   The claim regarding “non-standard regression” is incorrect.
*   Minor typo in the abstract.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a functional and well-implemented algorithm that demonstrably improves performance on several tasks.
*   The extension to latent variables provides a practical solution for weakly supervised learning.
*   The experimental results are convincing and well-supported.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of significant novelty is a major concern.
*   The absence of theoretical analysis limits our understanding of the method's properties.
*   The heavy regularization raises questions about the robustness and generalization ability of the learned models.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and the experiments are well-executed, the lack of novelty and theoretical analysis are significant drawbacks. The paper feels more like an engineering application of existing ideas than a fundamental research contribution. Addressing the novelty concerns by thoroughly discussing related work and providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. Alternatively, focusing on the specific advantages of the e-SVM approach in a niche application, and providing a deeper analysis of those benefits, could justify acceptance.
