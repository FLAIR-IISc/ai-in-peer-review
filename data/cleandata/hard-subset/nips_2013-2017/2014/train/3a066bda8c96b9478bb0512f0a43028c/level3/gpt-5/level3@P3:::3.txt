Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “positiveness” scores in [0,1] (e.g., IoU to ground-truth segments). The basic model minimizes the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label whose success probability is g(u), with g(u)=u in experiments, so each sample contributes to both positive and negative hinge losses weighted by u and 1−u. A latent e-SVM extends this to weak supervision where the true positiveness is unobserved; the method alternates between optimizing w (a standard linear SVM subproblem) and u (regularized to stay close to an initialization derived from box IoU via a convex KL/entropy penalty). The approach is instantiated for segment-proposal scoring, semantic labeling from CPMC segments, and object detection, where segment confidences are pooled into a class-specific spatial confidence map feature and concatenated with RCNN features. The method improves segment scoring versus SVC and SVR, yields competitive semantic segmentation, and improves RCNN on PASCAL VOC 2007 detection by 1.5 mAP, matching or exceeding state-of-the-art at the time.

Relation to prior work
The problem sits between fully supervised segmentation and typical weak supervision/MIL for image labels. The basic e-SVM is related to instance-weighted, cost-sensitive SVMs and to bounded/probability-target regression. The latent e-SVM closely resembles transductive/semi-supervised SVMs with latent labels and regularization toward initial labels/scores. The paper does not adequately position itself with respect to this literature and omits several standard references (see below).

Strengths
- Addresses practically important settings (IoU-valued targets; bounding-box supervision) with a simple, efficient formulation solvable by off-the-shelf SVM solvers.
- Clear, useful instantiation to semantic labeling and to object detection via a well-designed spatial confidence map feature.
- Strong empirical results: consistent gains over SVC and SVR for segment scoring; non-trivial, state-of-the-art improvements on VOC 2007 detection when combined with RCNN, which is of broad interest (conventional cues complementing CNNs).
- The latent formulation is convex in each block and uses a principled, convex KL regularizer to stabilize optimization.

Weaknesses
- Originality is limited: the basic e-SVM is a reweighting of hinge losses by soft labels; the latent version is essentially a weighted transductive/semi-supervised SVM with an entropy/KL regularizer. The paper does not clearly differentiate from this prior art.
- Missing or insufficiently discussed related work: weighted SVMs and transductive/S3VM literature. Please add and discuss:
  • Vapnik’s transductive inference; Joachims (1999) Transductive SVM; Bennett & Demiriz (1999) Semi-supervised SVM; Sindhwani & Keerthi (2006) large-scale S3VM; Collobert et al. (2006) large-scale TSVM and variants.
  • Cost-sensitive/instance-weighted SVMs beyond the few cited, including common sample-weighted hinge-loss formulations; connections to SVM+ (Lapin et al.) should be clarified as conceptually distinct.
  • If the method enforces a sum-constraint on weights (as implied in parts of the text), discuss its relationship to normalized sample-weight SVMs and to probability calibration.
- The claim (title/abstract) that unit-interval targets are “less informative” than binary labels is misleading; in many settings they are strictly more informative. The paper should rephrase and clarify what supervision signal is available and why hinge-loss-with-soft-labels is preferable.
- Methodology/baselines: there is no comparison to standard probabilistic baselines for [0,1]-valued targets and ranking, e.g., regularized logistic regression on IoU (with or without logit transform), isotonic regression, ordinal regression, or Beta regression; nor to probability-calibrated SVMs (Platt scaling) used directly for TAPC/NDCG. These are necessary to justify the specific choice of a sum-constrained weighted SVM objective.
- Limited transparency on hyperparameters and optimization: selection of λW and λR, convergence criteria, number of alternations, sensitivity analyses, and details of the u-update are missing.
- Exposition focuses on small algorithmic variations while omitting critical engineering choices (features for segments, pooling choices, calibration, proposal generation, and ablations for the spatial confidence map), which makes reproduction harder.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the loss is well-defined and experiments are solid and relevant. However, the omission of crucial related work and missing baselines undermines the strength of the claims about novelty and necessity of the specific objective. The object detection system involves considerable engineering, but that is common for this domain and well within norms.

Clarity
Readable overall, but the title/abstract should better reflect the core insight (hinge-loss with soft labels; latent transductive extension) and avoid misleading statements about supervision. Please also improve organization to foreground the key modeling idea, then the practical feature-engineering that drives the detection gains. Add implementation details sufficient for reproduction (feature dimensions, pooling parameters, m, encoding settings, training schedule, runtime).

Originality
Moderate. The weighting of hinge losses by soft labels and the latent alternating optimization with an entropy regularizer are close to known WSVM/TSVM ideas. A possible novelty is the specific sum-constraint on weights (if indeed enforced); if so, highlight, justify, and evaluate it. The spatial confidence map feature is a useful engineering contribution but conceptually incremental.

Significance
High practical interest. Demonstrating gains over contemporaneous CNN detectors using complementary, conventional cues will attract the community. The generality of the formulation (drop-in with standard SVM solvers) also aids adoption.

Requests for additional experiments/analyses
- Add baselines: regularized logistic regression (bounded target or logit of IoU), isotonic regression, ordinal regression; calibrated SVM probabilities; and report TAPC/NDCG and downstream task impact.
- Provide ablations for the detection system: effect of m×m grid size, pooling type, feature encoding, and contribution per class; effect of using CPMC vs. superpixels; with/without KL regularizer (λR=0) to demonstrate avoidance of poor local minima.
- Hyperparameters: detail how λW and λR were selected (data splits, search ranges, criteria), and provide sensitivity plots.
- Optimization details: initialization, update rules for u (closed-form vs. numerical), convergence thresholds, and number of alternations.
- Compare to standard transductive/semi-supervised SVM variants on the same tasks, to establish the distinctiveness and utility of latent e-SVM.

Minor comments
- Fix typos/wording: e.g., “continues”→“continuous” (around line 19); “segmentic segmentation”→“semantic segmentation”; clarify notation around Eq. (1) and (2); ensure consistent use of Bern(ui); tighten wording in lines 94, 98, 350; define TAPC on first use; proofread references (several incomplete citation entries).
- Clarify whether g(u)=u is fixed or learned/calibrated; discuss alternatives (e.g., monotone link functions).

Recommendation
Accept after revision. I raised my quality score conditional on the authors adding the missing prior-work discussion (especially TSVM/WSVM), correcting clarity issues, and reporting the requested baselines and hyperparameter details. The paper would also be a natural fit for a computer-vision venue, but the core learning formulation and the weakly supervised setting are of broad NIPS interest.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method; strong empirical results (state-of-the-art on VOC 2007 detection).
- Practical impact; complements CNNs with interpretable cues.
- Efficient optimization with standard solvers.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited algorithmic novelty; insufficient differentiation from WSVM/TSVM literature.
- Missing baselines and some experimental/implementation details.
