This paper proposes a method for learning from weakly supervised data, where labels are continuous "positiveness" scores in [0, 1] (e.g., Intersection-over-Union). The proposed "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM) is an SVM variant where each training example contributes to both positive and negative class costs, weighted by its positiveness score `u_i` and `1-u_i` respectively. For weakly supervised settings where `u_i` is unknown (e.g., only bounding boxes are given for segmentation), a latent e-SVM is introduced, which iteratively optimizes the model weights and the latent positiveness values. The authors apply this framework to semantic segmentation and object detection. By combining features from their method with the R-CNN detector, they achieve state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its impressive empirical results. The method, when combined with R-CNN features, achieves a new state-of-the-art on the highly competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task. Improving upon a very strong baseline like R-CNN is a significant achievement and will be of considerable interest to both the computer vision and machine learning communities.
*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the practical and important problem of learning from supervision that is neither fully binary nor a standard regression target. Framing the IoU overlap as a "positiveness" score and designing a learning algorithm for it is a well-motivated approach for tasks where precise pixel-level labels are prohibitively expensive to obtain.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The algorithmic novelty of the paper is very limited, and it fails to acknowledge a large body of highly relevant prior work.
    *   The proposed e-SVM appears to be a direct application of instance-weighted SVMs, where the hinge loss for each example `x_i` is split into two weighted components. The literature on weighted SVMs is extensive (e.g., Suykens et al., Neurocomputing 2002; Yang et al., IJCNN 2007), yet it is not discussed or cited. The authors should clarify what, if anything, distinguishes their formulation from these established methods. The sum constraint on weights might be a novel element, but this is not sufficiently highlighted or justified.
    *   The latent e-SVM is effectively a form of transductive SVM (TSVM) or semi-supervised SVM, where the labels (here, the continuous positiveness `u_i`) of some data points are inferred during training. The alternating optimization scheme is standard for such problems (Vapnik, "Statistical Learning Theory", 1998). The connection to this entire line of work is missing.
    *   The idea of learning with "privileged information" (e.g., bounding boxes) also has precedent in methods like SVM+ (Lapin et al., Neural Networks 2014), which should be discussed.
    *   This lack of context and citation significantly misrepresents the contribution of the paper. The core learning algorithms are presented as novel inventions when they are, at best, minor variations on well-known techniques.

*   **Quality:**
    *   The experimental methodology for justifying the e-SVM model is weak. The authors compare against SVC and SVR but fail to include standard baselines for regression on the unit interval, such as regularized logistic regression or beta regression. Without these comparisons, the claim that the proposed loss function is superior for this task is not well-supported.
    *   The paper lacks crucial details about hyperparameter selection. It is unclear how the regularization coefficients λW and λR were chosen. This is critical for reproducibility and for understanding the sensitivity of the model.
    *   While the engineering effort for the object detection system is substantial, its description is insufficient. The key to the performance improvement might lie more in the "spatial confidence map feature" and other implementation details than in the e-SVM formulation itself.

*   **Clarity:**
    *   The title and abstract are misleading. They suggest a general learning algorithm, but the paper's contribution is almost entirely empirical and application-specific. The abstract's claim that this is not a standard regression problem because the target is bounded in [0, 1] is incorrect; this is a common regression setting.
    *   The exposition is poorly balanced. Too much space is dedicated to the simple algorithmic formulations, which, as noted above, are not particularly novel. In contrast, critical details about the feature engineering, which are likely responsible for the strong results, are glossed over. For a reader to reproduce the results, a much more detailed description of the spatial confidence map feature generation is necessary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper reports state-of-the-art results on a major, competitive benchmark (PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection).
2.  It addresses an important practical problem: learning effectively from weak, continuous-valued supervision like IoU scores.
3.  The proposed spatial confidence map feature appears to be an effective way to integrate segmentation cues into a region-based object detector.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The core algorithms (e-SVM, latent e-SVM) are presented as novel but are essentially re-formulations of existing weighted SVM and transductive SVM methods.
2.  The paper fails to cite or discuss decades of relevant and foundational machine learning literature, which is a serious scholarly omission.
3.  The experimental validation of the core algorithm is weak due to missing standard baselines.
4.  The paper is poorly written, with a misleading title/abstract and an unbalanced focus that obscures the most important technical details.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The paper requires a major revision to accurately position its contribution. The authors must thoroughly review the literature on weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs and rewrite Sections 2 and 3 to frame their work as an application or adaptation of these techniques, clearly stating what is new.
*   The title should be changed to better reflect the contribution, which is primarily in applying these learning techniques to computer vision tasks and introducing an effective feature representation.
*   Please add experiments comparing e-SVM to standard methods for unit-interval regression, like logistic regression.
*   Provide a detailed description of how all hyperparameters (λW, λR, m for spatial pooling, etc.) were set.
*   Expand the description of the spatial confidence map feature, as this appears to be a key component of your system's success.
*   Minor fixes:
    *   Line 19: "continues" -> "continuous"
    *   Line 94: Notation `λw · 1 wTw` is unconventional. Please use standard notation like `(λ/2) ||w||^2`.
    *   Line 98: `l+i` and `l-i` should be defined with `w^T x_i` as per the text, not `w^T xi`. The notation for augmented weights and features is a bit confusing.
    *   Line 350: "datesets" -> "datasets"

In its current form, the paper is not acceptable due to the major issues with originality and missing citations. However, the empirical results are very strong. I would be willing to increase my score if the authors undertake a substantial revision to address the relationship to prior work honestly and comprehensively. Given its strong empirical focus and vision-specific engineering, the paper might also be a better fit for a top-tier computer vision conference.
