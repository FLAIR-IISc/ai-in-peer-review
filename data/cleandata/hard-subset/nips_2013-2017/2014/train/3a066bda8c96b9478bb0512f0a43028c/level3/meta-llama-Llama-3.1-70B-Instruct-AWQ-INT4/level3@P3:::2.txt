This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) that can handle situations where only the "positiveness" of a binary label is available, rather than the binary label itself. The "positiveness" is a continuous value whose range is a bounded interval, and it quantifies the affiliation of each training data to the positive class. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major tasks of computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The main idea of the paper is to modify the traditional SVM loss function to incorporate the "positiveness" of each example. This is achieved by treating the label of each example as an unobserved random variable and assuming that it follows a Bernoulli distribution. The probability of the label being positive is denoted as µ, which is a function of the positiveness. The authors show that their approach can be easily extended to the situation where only bounding box annotations are available, by treating the positiveness as a latent variable.

The authors evaluate their approach on the PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset and compare it to two common methods: Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR). They introduce two new evaluation criteria for the segment-wise target class prediction task: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). The results show that their approach outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios.

The authors also apply their approach to the task of object detection and show that it can improve the performance of the state-of-the-art object detection system, Regions with CNN features (RCNN). They propose a new spatial confidence map feature and demonstrate that it can be used to improve the detection performance.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors propose a novel approach to handle situations where only the "positiveness" of a binary label is available.
* The approach is well-described, and the experiments show its benefits over equal-weight training.
* The authors evaluate their approach on two major tasks of computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

* The method appears equivalent to existing "example-dependent costs" (e.g., SVMlight), and the novelty over prior work is unclear.
* The latent-weight extension is interesting but straightforward, and the optimization is non-convex, with no theoretical analysis provided.
* Heavy regularization is used, suggesting that the learned weights stay close to their initial values, and the authors should comment on this.
* The claim that positivity in [0,1] makes it non-standard regression is incorrect (e.g., logistic regression is standard and bounded to [0,1]).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the novelty of the approach is unclear, and the authors should provide more discussion on how their approach differs from prior work.
