The paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that can handle weakly supervised data where the labels are continuous values within a bounded interval. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two major tasks in computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The main idea of e-SVM is to weight each example in the loss function based on its positiveness, which is a continuous value between 0 and 1. This approach is extended to handle latent or unobserved per-example weights. The authors show that their method outperforms traditional Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) methods on the segment proposal classification task.

The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate the benefits of their approach on two challenging tasks in computer vision, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the method. The idea of weighting each example in the loss function is not new and has been explored in previous work, such as example-dependent costs in SVMlight. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of how their method differs from existing approaches and what specific contributions they make to the field.

The latent-weight extension is an interesting aspect of the paper, but the optimization process is non-convex, and no theoretical analysis is provided. The authors should discuss the implications of this and provide more insights into the optimization process.

Furthermore, the authors use heavy regularization in their experiments, which suggests that the learned weights stay close to the initial values. The authors should comment on this and provide more insights into the effect of regularization on the performance of their method.

Additionally, the authors make an incorrect claim that the positivity in [0,1] makes it a non-standard regression problem. Logistic regression, for example, is a standard regression method that is bounded to [0,1].

In conclusion, while the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the method and the lack of theoretical analysis. The authors should address these concerns and provide more insights into their approach.
