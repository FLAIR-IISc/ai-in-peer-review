This paper proposes a learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), for problems where training labels are continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval, such as an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) score. The core idea is to frame the problem as a weighted SVM where weights are derived from the positiveness score. The authors extend this to a weakly supervised setting with a latent e-SVM, where positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., given only bounding-box supervision) and are treated as latent variables. This latent model closely resembles a transductive SVM. The method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection, achieving impressive state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark by augmenting a strong CNN-based detector.

The primary strength of this paper is its significant empirical contribution. Achieving results that surpass contemporary deep learning methods on a challenging benchmark using a more conventional, SVM-based framework is a notable achievement and of high interest to the community. The problem formulation for learning from continuous IoU scores is also well-motivated for weakly supervised vision tasks.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses. The most critical is its originality and relationship to prior work. The proposed e-SVM and latent e-SVM are essentially reformulations of weighted SVM and transductive SVM, respectively. The authors fail to cite or discuss a large body of relevant literature (e.g., Suykens et al., Vapnik, Lapin et al.), which significantly undermines the claims of algorithmic novelty.

From a quality perspective, the experimental methodology is incomplete. The authors should include standard baselines, such as regularized logistic regression, to justify their specific SVM formulation for unit-interval regression. Furthermore, crucial details regarding the selection of regularization hyperparameters are missing, which harms reproducibility.

The clarity of the paper is also lacking. The title and abstract do not clearly convey the core contribution and make a questionable claim that unit-interval targets are less informative than binarized labels. The paper dedicates excessive space to minor algorithmic variations while providing insufficient detail on the feature engineering pipeline, which is likely a key factor in its success.

In summary, while the strong empirical results are compelling, the paper in its current form is not acceptable due to its failure to properly contextualize the work within existing literature. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors undertake a major revision to thoroughly address the relationship to prior work on weighted and transductive SVMs, improve the clarity of their contribution, and include the necessary experimental baselines. The paper's heavy engineering focus might also make it a better fit for a computer vision venue.
