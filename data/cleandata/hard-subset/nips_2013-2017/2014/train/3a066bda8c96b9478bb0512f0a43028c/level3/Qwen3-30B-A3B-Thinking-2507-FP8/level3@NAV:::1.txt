

This paper introduces expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for problems where training examples have continuous "positiveness" values (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors demonstrate how e-SVM extends traditional SVM approaches by incorporating these continuous values through soft weighting, and successfully apply it to visual segmentation under weak supervision (where only bounding box annotations are available). The method represents a meaningful extension of multiple instance learning (MIL) by replacing binary positives with soft weights, handling both observed and latent positiveness settings.

The paper excels in all four NIPS criteria. Quality is strong: the technical content is sound with well-executed experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection, showing clear improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations, including the trade-offs between different evaluation metrics. Clarity is excellent, with a well-structured presentation that clearly motivates the problem and explains the methodology. Originality is significant: while related to weighted SVMs, e-SVM's specific formulation for continuous positiveness values represents a distinct contribution that addresses a gap in weakly supervised learning. The significance is high, as weakly supervised learning is increasingly important for large-scale computer vision applications where pixel-level annotations are prohibitively expensive.

The main limitation is that the technical extension from standard SVM to e-SVM is conceptually straightforward, potentially making the novelty seem incremental to some reviewers. However, the paper effectively demonstrates the practical value of this extension through compelling results on standard benchmarks.

The method's flexibility is demonstrated by its successful application to both semantic segmentation (achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2011) and object detection (improving RCNN by 1.5% AP on PASCAL VOC 2007). The experimental design is robust, with appropriate baselines and multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that directly assess the segment classification task.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a clear and valuable advancement in weakly supervised learning. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with practical significance. I recommend acceptance as a strong NIPS contribution.
