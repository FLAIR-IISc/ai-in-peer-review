

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples have "positiveness" values (continuous measures of affiliation to positive class) rather than binary labels. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for handling cases where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The method is applied to segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, with claimed state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The core contribution is a modified SVM formulation that treats label probabilities as functions of observed positiveness values. The loss function incorporates expectation over the random label variable, effectively weighting examples based on their positiveness. The authors argue this approach better handles the bounded [0,1] interval of positiveness compared to standard SVC (which requires thresholding) or SVR (which doesn't account for boundedness).

The paper demonstrates the method on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and VOC 2007 detection tasks, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. For object detection, the authors combine their spatial confidence map features with RCNN, achieving 60.0% average AP (1.5% improvement over the original RCNN).

The work has several strengths. The empirical results are compelling, with consistent improvements across multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) and tasks. The application to object detection is particularly valuable, as it shows how weak supervision can be leveraged to improve state-of-the-art systems with minimal modifications. The paper's framework is general and could be applied to other weakly supervised learning problems beyond computer vision.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. Most critically, the authors fail to properly cite and differentiate their work from established weighted SVM and transductive SVM literature. The latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM (as noted by the authors themselves in Section 2), but they fail to cite key references including Suykens et al. (2004) on weighted SVMs, Lapin et al. (2014) on learning with privileged information, and Yang et al. (2007) on weighted SVMs. This omission makes the claimed novelty difficult to justify.

The paper also makes misleading claims in the title and abstract about unit-interval targets being less informative than binary labels, when the authors' approach actually requires the continuous target values. The justification for using their sum-constrained weighted SVM over standard regression approaches (like regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression) is insufficiently supported. The paper lacks a proper comparison to these more direct baselines.

The methodological details are incomplete. The authors don't explain how they selected the regularization coefficients (λW and λR), which are crucial for the model's performance. The paper spends excessive space on minor algorithmic variations while omitting critical feature engineering details that would help reproduce their results.

The significance of the work is somewhat diminished by the fact that the object detection improvements are achieved using a conventional approach (concatenating features with RCNN) rather than a novel detection architecture. While the 1.5% improvement is notable, the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this conventional approach with weak supervision beats the state-of-the-art CNN-based detection system.

The clarity of the paper is hampered by the misleading title/abstract and the lack of proper contextualization within existing literature. The paper would benefit from a more precise description of how the authors' method differs from and improves upon the transductive SVM framework.

In summary, the paper presents a useful algorithmic approach with strong empirical results, but the lack of proper contextualization within the weighted SVM literature significantly weakens its contribution. The claimed novelty is questionable given the close relationship to existing work. While the empirical results are strong, the paper would need substantial revision to properly establish its novelty and place within the literature.

Quality: The experimental results are solid, but the theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by proper citations and comparisons to relevant literature.

Clarity: The paper's title and abstract misrepresent the contribution, and the exposition is unnecessarily convoluted in places.

Originality: The core algorithmic approach is not novel, as it closely follows existing weighted SVM and transductive SVM work that the authors fail to cite.

Significance: The results are significant for weakly supervised learning, but the methodological novelty is limited, making the significance somewhat overstated.

The paper has merit but requires substantial revision to properly position itself within the literature. The authors should address the missing references, clarify the relationship to existing weighted SVM work, and provide a more convincing justification for their approach over standard alternatives. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. As it stands, the paper's claims of novelty are not well-supported, and it would be better suited for a computer vision venue where the specific applications are more central to the community's interests. I recommend rejection with the understanding that a substantially revised version addressing these concerns could be reconsidered.
