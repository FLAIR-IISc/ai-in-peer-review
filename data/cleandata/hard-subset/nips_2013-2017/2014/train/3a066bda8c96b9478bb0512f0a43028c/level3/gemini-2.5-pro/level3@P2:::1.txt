This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) designed for weakly supervised learning problems where the training labels are not binary but are instead continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]). This scenario is common in computer vision, where an object proposal's quality can be measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth annotation. The proposed e-SVM formulates the learning problem by treating the binary label of each example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, with the success probability determined by its known "positiveness" score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random variable. This elegant formulation results in an objective where each training sample contributes weighted loss terms for both the positive and negative classes, which can be solved using standard SVM solvers.

The paper further extends this model to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the "positiveness" score itself is unknown and only weaker supervision (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level masks) is available. In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and inferred jointly with the classifier parameters using an alternating optimization procedure. The authors apply their method to the challenging computer vision tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection on the PASCAL VOC datasets. The results show that e-SVM outperforms standard baselines like Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) for segment proposal classification. Furthermore, by integrating features from the latent e-SVM, the authors demonstrate a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detection method.

This work relates to previous efforts in weighted SVMs and multiple-instance learning (MIL). However, it differs from standard weighted SVMs, which typically assign a single weight to each data point, by instead weighting the positive and negative loss terms for each sample. It also addresses a different problem than classical MIL, which assumes at least one perfectly positive instance in a positive bag, whereas the e-SVM framework naturally handles cases where all instances are only partially correct.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a very important and practical problem in modern machine learning and computer vision: how to effectively learn from weak supervision. As datasets scale, the cost of detailed annotation (e.g., pixel-level segmentation) becomes prohibitive. The proposed framework provides a principled way to leverage cheaper forms of annotation like bounding boxes, where the notion of a "partially correct" positive example is inherent.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed e-SVM model is a technically sound and elegant extension of the max-margin principle. The probabilistic interpretation of the label as a random variable is well-founded. The extension to the latent e-SVM for the weakly supervised case is a logical next step, and the use of an alternating optimization scheme is appropriate for this formulation. The introduction of a KL-divergence based regularizer to prevent trivial solutions in the latent case is a sensible design choice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The mathematical formulations are presented concisely, and the distinctions from related work (SVC, SVR, WSVM) are clearly articulated. The overall framework and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-executed on standard, challenging benchmarks. The authors make a strong case for their method by:
    1.  Introducing two new and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification task, allowing for a direct and fair comparison.
    2.  Showing clear improvements over relevant baselines (SVC, SVR) in both the fully-supervised and weakly-supervised settings.
    3.  Demonstrating significant practical impact by improving the state-of-the-art object detection performance of the R-CNN model on PASCAL VOC 2007, which is a non-trivial achievement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the final formulation is novel, the core components (SVM, latent variables, alternating optimization) are well-established. The contribution can be seen as a novel and effective combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new theoretical concept. However, this combination is non-obvious and highly effective for the target problem.
*   **Optimization Guarantees:** The alternating optimization procedure for the latent e-SVM is not guaranteed to converge to a global optimum. This is a common issue for non-convex problems involving latent variables. While the authors mention that their regularization term helps avoid poor local minima, a more in-depth discussion of the optimization landscape or the sensitivity to initialization would strengthen the paper.
*   **Regularization Term:** The choice of the cross-entropy based regularization term `R(u)` is justified heuristically by its convexity and its effect of keeping the latent `u` close to an initial estimate. While it works well in practice, the paper could benefit from a more formal motivation for this specific choice or a comparison with other potential regularizers (e.g., an L2 penalty on `u - u_bb`).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel, principled, and practical algorithm (e-SVM) for the important problem of learning from continuous-valued or weak supervision.
2.  The method is technically sound and is presented with excellent clarity.
3.  The experimental results are strong, demonstrating clear superiority over relevant baselines and achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging object detection task.
4.  The contribution is general enough that it could inspire further work on weakly supervised learning in other domains beyond computer vision.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The technical novelty could be viewed as incremental, as it combines several existing machine learning concepts.
2.  The optimization for the latent variable model is non-convex and lacks theoretical guarantees for global convergence.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM method is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically powerful. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem addressed and the strong experimental results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper represents a solid NIPS contribution.
