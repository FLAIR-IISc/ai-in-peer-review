

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where each training sample is associated with a "positiveness" value (a continuous measure of affiliation to the positive class) rather than a binary label. The authors present both a basic e-SVM model for when positiveness values are observed and a latent e-SVM model for cases where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). They apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper builds upon the growing interest in weakly supervised learning for computer vision tasks, particularly in the context of reducing annotation costs. However, it fails to adequately situate its work within the broader machine learning literature on weighted SVMs and transductive learning. The core idea of using continuous labels (positiveness values) to weight SVM loss functions has been explored in prior work, notably in weighted SVMs (Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2008) and in learning with privileged information (Lapin et al., 2014). The authors' "sum constraint" on weights is presented as novel but appears to be a specific implementation of existing weighted SVM approaches rather than a fundamental theoretical contribution.

**Strengths:**

*Significance*: The paper demonstrates practical utility of the approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (60.0 AP vs. RCNN's 58.5), which is notable given that it uses conventional features rather than deep learning. The application to semantic segmentation with bounding box annotations is also valuable for reducing annotation costs.

*Quality*: The experimental results are well-designed and show consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines across multiple metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The method is technically sound, and the implementation details for the spatial confidence map feature are clear and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

*Originality*: The core contribution is significantly less novel than claimed. The latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, and the paper fails to properly cite and differentiate from relevant prior work in the machine learning literature (Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2008; Lapin et al., 2014). The title and abstract misleadingly claim that "unit-interval targets are less informative than binary labels," while the paper actually shows the opposite (continuous labels provide more information).

*Clarity*: The paper's focus on minor algorithmic variations (e.g., the sum constraint) at the expense of critical feature engineering details is problematic. The title and abstract fail to clearly convey the core contribution, and the paper contains several notational inconsistencies (lines 19, 94, 98, 350).

*Quality*: The paper lacks crucial comparisons to standard approaches for unit-interval regression (e.g., regularized logistic regression), which would strengthen the argument for using the sum-constrained weighted SVM. Hyperparameter selection (λW and λR) is not adequately justified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Demonstrates practical value by achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection
- Provides a useful framework for weakly supervised learning in computer vision
- Experiments are well-designed and clearly presented
- The spatial confidence map feature is a clever engineering solution

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Core algorithm is not sufficiently novel compared to existing weighted SVM literature
- Fails to properly cite and differentiate from relevant prior work
- Misleading claims about the information content of continuous labels
- Insufficient justification for methodological choices (hyperparameters, baseline comparisons)
- Poorly focused exposition with too much space on minor algorithmic variations

**Recommendation:** The paper has significant practical value and demonstrates strong empirical results, but its theoretical contribution is limited due to insufficient differentiation from prior work. The authors should substantially revise the paper to properly contextualize their work within the weighted SVM literature, clarify the relationship to existing methods (particularly weighted transductive SVMs), and provide stronger justification for their methodological choices. The current version does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in a way that would warrant publication at NIPS.

I recommend *conditional acceptance* if the authors address the above concerns, particularly by:
1. Adding comprehensive citations to relevant weighted SVM and transductive learning literature
2. Clarifying the relationship between e-SVM and existing weighted SVM approaches
3. Providing proper justification for hyperparameter selection
4. Including comparisons to standard unit-interval regression baselines
5. Correcting misleading claims in the abstract and title

The paper's practical contributions to computer vision are valuable, but they would be better suited for a computer vision venue (e.g., CVPR or ICCV) where the focus on applications is more appropriate than at NIPS, which emphasizes theoretical and algorithmic advances.
