This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for scenarios where training data isn’t labeled with binary classifications, but rather with a continuous measure of “positiveness” representing the degree to which a sample belongs to the positive class. The authors address a critical challenge in computer vision: leveraging weakly supervised data, specifically moving beyond expensive pixel-level annotations to utilize more readily available information like bounding boxes. The core idea is to treat the “positiveness” as a weight within an SVM framework, effectively creating a soft assignment instead of a hard binary classification. This allows the algorithm to function effectively even when the ground truth is not precisely known, extending naturally to situations where the positiveness is latent and needs to be inferred.

The paper clearly situates itself within the landscape of weakly supervised learning, referencing related work on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. It distinguishes itself from prior work by explicitly addressing the continuous nature of the confidence measure, which MIL doesn’t accommodate and standard SVMs require ad-hoc binarization of. The connection to weighted SVMs is also acknowledged, but the authors highlight the difference in weighting – applying weights to labels rather than data points, which offers computational advantages through compatibility with standard SVM solvers.  The problem is well-motivated, pointing to the impracticality of large-scale pixel-level annotation and the abundance of weakly labeled datasets like ImageNet.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM algorithm presents a novel approach to handling continuous confidence measures in SVMs, offering a more nuanced solution than simple thresholding or regression-based methods.
*   **Practicality:** The method directly addresses the need for learning algorithms that can function with weakly supervised data, a common scenario in many real-world computer vision applications.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate competitive and often superior performance on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, including achieving state-of-the-art object detection results on PASCAL VOC 2007.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the loss function is mathematically sound, and the extension to the latent variable setting is well-motivated and clearly explained.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible and easy to follow. The explanation of the algorithm and the experimental setup is thorough.
*   **Generality:** The authors emphasize that the method is flexible and can be used with various segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors, increasing its potential applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Regularization:** While the paper mentions regularization for the latent variable setting, the choice of the specific KL-divergence based regularization could benefit from further justification or a more thorough analysis of its impact. Alternative regularization strategies could be explored.
*   **Dependence on Segment Proposals:** The performance of e-SVM is inherently tied to the quality of the segment proposals. The paper doesn’t deeply investigate the sensitivity of the method to different proposal generation techniques beyond using CPMC.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Although leveraging existing SVM solvers is a strength, the extension to the latent variable setting adds complexity. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost, particularly for large datasets, would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to Recent Advances:** While the paper references relevant work, it might benefit from a more in-depth comparison to more recent advances in weakly supervised learning, particularly those that leverage deep learning architectures beyond simply concatenating features.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm that addresses an important problem in computer vision.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
*   The approach has the potential to be broadly applicable to various computer vision tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The dependence on segment proposals and lack of sensitivity analysis regarding different proposal methods.
*   The somewhat limited discussion regarding the regularization choice in the latent variable setting.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong submission that makes a significant contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm offers a principled and effective way to handle continuous confidence measures, and the experimental results are highly encouraging. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them considerably. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2014.
