This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification to the standard Support Vector Machine designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where, instead of binary labels, training data is associated with a “positiveness” value indicating the degree of affiliation to the positive class. The authors extend this to a latent variable setting when the positiveness is unobserved, and demonstrate its effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in weakly supervised learning, acknowledging the limitations of relying on large, fully annotated datasets. The introduction positions the problem within the context of reducing labeling costs, referencing prior work on ImageNet and bounding box annotations as a more practical alternative to pixel-level segmentation. The authors also relate their approach to existing methods like Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR), framing e-SVM as a solution to the drawbacks of these alternatives when applied to this specific problem.  The connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs are also appropriately discussed.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of learning from “positiveness” values and articulates the motivation behind it.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on segment proposal classification, and achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. The performance gain in object detection, specifically, is noteworthy.
* **Practicality:** The method is readily adaptable to different segment proposal detectors, feature representations, and classifiers, increasing its general applicability. The ability to seamlessly handle both fully supervised and weakly supervised scenarios is a significant advantage.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of weighting examples based on confidence or “positiveness” is not entirely novel.  The method bears a strong resemblance to existing techniques involving instance-dependent costs in SVM learning, such as those found in SVMlight. The paper does not adequately differentiate itself from these established methods, and a more thorough discussion of related work in this area is needed.
* **Optimization Concerns:** The latent e-SVM extension involves a non-convex optimization problem. While the authors provide an iterative algorithm, there is no theoretical analysis of its convergence or guarantees of finding a good local optimum. The heavy reliance on regularization, as evidenced by the fact that learned weights remain close to their initial values, suggests potential instability or sensitivity to parameter settings.  The authors should provide a more in-depth discussion of this.
* **Inaccurate Claim Regarding Regression:** The statement that using a positiveness value in the range [0,1] makes the problem non-standard for regression is incorrect.  Logistic regression, for example, is a standard regression technique for predicting probabilities within this interval. This undermines the authors' justification for their approach.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the paper shows improvements over baselines, it would be beneficial to include ablation studies to dissect the contribution of different components of the method. For instance, analyzing the impact of the specific regularization term used in the latent e-SVM could provide valuable insights.
* **Typo:**  A minor error exists in the abstract, where "continues" should be "continuous".

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a practical and effective method for learning from weakly supervised data.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements on challenging computer vision tasks.
* The method is well-motivated and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core novelty of the approach is questionable, given its similarity to existing techniques.
* The optimization aspects of the latent e-SVM are not thoroughly addressed.
* The inaccurate claim regarding regression raises concerns about the authors’ understanding of related work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, but its novelty is limited. While the empirical results are promising, the lack of a strong differentiation from existing methods and the unanswered questions surrounding the optimization process weaken the overall impact of the work. A major revision addressing the novelty concerns, providing a more detailed analysis of the optimization, and correcting the inaccurate claim would significantly strengthen the paper. Currently, I recommend **borderline reject** with a strong encouragement for revision.
