

**Review**

The paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for situations where training examples have continuous "positiveness" scores (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a principled approach to handle this by treating the label as a random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness value. The model can be extended to handle weak supervision where the positiveness is unobserved (treated as latent variable), making it applicable to scenarios with bounding box annotations rather than pixel-level annotations. The authors validate their approach on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The work builds on multiple instance learning (MIL), which typically assumes at least one positive instance per bag but cannot handle partial positives, and weighted SVMs, which weight data points rather than labels. The key difference in e-SVM is that it weights the labels (not the data points), allowing for a more natural handling of continuous positiveness values. This addresses a gap between standard binary classification and MIL, where many proposals contain partial positive information (e.g., IoU scores).

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The technical content is sound with well-motivated theoretical development. The e-SVM formulation extends standard SVMs in a principled way, and the latent extension for weak supervision is well-justified. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically developed for segment classification. The results consistently demonstrate improvement over SVC and SVR across both strong and weak supervision settings.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation, model description, and experimental setup are clear and accessible. The introduction of new evaluation metrics is particularly helpful, and the application to semantic segmentation and object detection is well-explained with sufficient detail on integration with existing pipelines.

3. **Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by addressing the specific problem of learning from continuous positiveness values rather than binary labels. While the problem of weak supervision has been explored in MIL, this work provides a more natural and effective solution for cases where partial positive information is available. The extension to handle unobserved positiveness as a latent variable is a clever and practical approach.

4. **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. The method's ability to work with bounding box annotations (which are more readily available) has significant practical value. The demonstrated state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection show meaningful improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparison to More Recent Work:** While the paper compares to SVC and SVR, it does not compare to more recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL, such as those using deep learning for weakly supervised segmentation. A comparison to recent works like those using deep learning for weakly supervised segmentation would strengthen the evaluation.

2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the latent e-SVM optimization compared to standard SVM training. While the algorithm is presented as "effortlessly solvable by any standard SVM solver," the iterative optimization process might add significant overhead, especially for large datasets.

3. **Theoretical Justification:** The choice of Bernoulli distribution and regularization term is pragmatically justified, but the theoretical justification for these specific choices could be strengthened. The paper states the regularization term is "strong," but doesn't provide theoretical analysis of how this affects generalization.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically valuable contribution to weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM framework is a natural extension of existing SVM approaches that effectively handles the problem of weak supervision with continuous positiveness values.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in computer vision with practical applications
- Well-executed experiments with clear results
- Novel approach that extends beyond existing MIL and weighted SVM methods
- Clear writing and organization
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a major benchmark (PASCAL VOC 2007)

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods
- No detailed discussion of computational complexity
- Theoretical justification for model choices could be strengthened

The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field. The e-SVM framework is a natural extension of existing SVM approaches that effectively handles the problem of weak supervision with continuous positiveness values. While there are some areas for improvement in the theoretical justification and comparison to more recent methods, these do not detract significantly from the paper's overall quality and contribution. Given the significance of the problem addressed and the solid experimental results, I recommend acceptance. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel machine learning method with strong practical applications in computer vision.
