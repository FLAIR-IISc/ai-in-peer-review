- Introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) to handle continuous "positiveness" labels in [0,1] instead of binary labels.  
- Motivated by computer vision tasks where pixel-level labels are costly but bounding-box overlap yields continuous scores.  
- Models true label as Bernoulli with success probability equal to observed positiveness, yielding an expected SVM loss that weights examples by their continuous label.  
- Connects to MIL and weighted SVMs, and explains why MIL and standard SVC/SVR are unsuitable for this problem.  
- Avoids arbitrary thresholding required by SVC and the mismatch of SVR for bounded [0,1] labels.  
- e-SVM formulation is principled and can be solved with standard SVM solvers, a practical advantage.  
- Extends to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision when positiveness values are unobserved, using an iterative optimization procedure.  
- Includes a regularization term in latent training to reduce local-minima problems.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC 2011), outperforming SVC and SVR.  
- Proposes two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) tailored to segment classification for more direct assessment.  
- Integrates e-SVM into RCNN for object detection using spatial confidence map features, improving AP by 1.5% on PASCAL VOC 2007.  
- Shows particularly large gains on difficult categories (e.g., "potted plant") with only PASCAL data and no extra context.  
- Strong experimental design with appropriate baselines and comprehensive evaluation across tasks.  
- Writing is clear, well-organized, and accessible to the NIPS audience.  
- Original contribution: weights labels (not examples) via expectation-loss SVM, conceptually novel relative to weighted SVMs.  
- Significant for weakly supervised learning in vision and broadly applicable to other visual tasks.  
- Main weakness: limited analysis of computational complexity for latent e-SVM training.  
- Suggests additional validation on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to assess scalability; overall recommendation: accept.
