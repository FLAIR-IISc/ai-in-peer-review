**Paper Review**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) that can handle weakly supervised data where only the "positiveness" of each training sample is available, instead of a binary label. The e-SVM algorithm can be extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision, where the exact positiveness value of each training example is unobserved. The authors evaluate their method on two major tasks of computer vision: semantic segmentation and object detection, and achieve state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Relation to Prior Work**

While the paper presents a new algorithm, it does not thoroughly discuss the relationship to prior work in the field of weighted SVM and transductive SVM. The authors should provide a more comprehensive review of the literature, including works by Suykens et al., Yang et al., Vapnik, and Lapin et al. The proposed latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, and the authors should clearly differentiate their work from prior art.

**Originality**

The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, as it builds upon existing ideas in the field of weighted SVM and transductive SVM. However, the application of the e-SVM algorithm to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks is novel and of high community interest.

**Quality**

The paper is well-written, but there are some issues with clarity and focus. The title and abstract do not clearly convey the core contribution of the paper, and the authors make a misleading claim about unit-interval targets being less informative than binary labels. Additionally, the paper spends excessive space on small algorithmic variations and too little on critical feature-engineering details.

**Baselines and Methodology**

The authors should report standard baselines, such as regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression, to justify using sum-constrained weighted SVM. Furthermore, more detail is needed on how regularization coefficients (λW and λR) were chosen.

**Significance**

The paper presents strong results, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset. Beating recent CNN results with a conventional approach is notable and of high community interest.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some minor issues with notation and typography. The authors should address these issues to improve the clarity of the paper.

**Recommendation**

I recommend publishing the paper after rewriting and addressing the issues mentioned above. The paper may fit better at a computer-vision venue, where the application of the e-SVM algorithm to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks is more relevant.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents strong results, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.
* The application of the e-SVM algorithm to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks is novel and of high community interest.
* The paper is generally well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and methodology.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper does not thoroughly discuss the relationship to prior work in the field of weighted SVM and transductive SVM.
* The algorithmic novelty of the paper is limited, and the authors should clearly differentiate their work from prior art.
* The paper spends excessive space on small algorithmic variations and too little on critical feature-engineering details.
