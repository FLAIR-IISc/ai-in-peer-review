

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for problems where training data has continuous "positiveness" values (in [0,1]) rather than binary labels. The authors address an important problem in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain, but bounding box annotations (which provide only a continuous measure of object overlap) are more readily available. The e-SVM extends standard SVMs by modeling the label as a random variable with success probability determined by the observed positiveness value, leading to a loss function that weights each example based on its continuous label.

The paper makes a clear connection to related work, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. The authors correctly identify that MIL cannot solve their problem since it assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag, which is not the case when dealing with partially overlapping segments. The paper also distinguishes itself from standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR), which are not designed for this specific problem of continuous label values in [0,1]. The authors note that SVC requires arbitrary thresholding to binarize the positiveness values, while SVR treats the problem as regression, which is not ideal for bounded continuous values.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and clearly presented. The e-SVM formulation in Section 3.1 provides a principled approach to handling continuous label values by taking the expectation of the standard SVM loss over the Bernoulli-distributed label. The authors show this formulation can be solved using standard SVM solvers, which is a significant practical advantage. The extension to the latent case (Section 3.2) for weak supervision, where positiveness values are unobserved, is particularly valuable. The authors propose a simple iterative optimization procedure that converges to a reasonable solution, with a carefully designed regularization term to prevent the algorithm from getting stuck in local minima.

The paper demonstrates the utility of e-SVM through extensive experiments on two important computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For semantic segmentation, the authors show that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on the PASCAL VOC 2011 dataset under both pixel-level and bounding-box annotation settings. The authors introduce two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically designed for this segment classification task, which provide more direct and unbiased performance assessment than previous task-specific metrics.

For object detection, the authors integrate e-SVM with the state-of-the-art RCNN framework, generating spatial confidence map features that capture segment-level confidence. This simple integration leads to a 1.5% average improvement in AP on PASCAL VOC 2007, with particularly significant gains for difficult categories like "potted plant." The results are impressive given the simplicity of the approach and the fact that they use only the PASCAL VOC 2007 annotations without additional data or context.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The problem addressed is important and timely, as weakly supervised learning is increasingly crucial for large-scale vision applications. The technical contribution is well-motivated and represents a meaningful extension of existing SVM techniques. The experimental design is thorough, with appropriate baselines and evaluation metrics. The writing is clear, well-organized, and accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors demonstrate the approach on two major computer vision tasks, showing significant improvements over previous methods in both cases.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't thoroughly address the computational complexity of the latent e-SVM training procedure, though the authors note it can be solved with standard SVM solvers. Additionally, while the paper shows strong results on PASCAL VOC, it would be valuable to see the approach tested on a larger dataset like ImageNet to better assess scalability.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical formulation is clear, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of e-SVM over alternative approaches. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses, noting that their method is slightly better than SVR but significantly better than SVC.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem statement, motivation, and technical contributions are all presented logically. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) is particularly helpful for readers to understand the evaluation of segment classification.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by extending SVMs to handle continuous label values in [0,1] through the expectation loss formulation. While related to weighted SVMs, the key distinction is weighting labels rather than data points, which is a significant conceptual difference. The authors correctly position their work relative to MIL and other related approaches.

Significance: The problem addressed is highly significant for computer vision, where weak supervision is increasingly important for large-scale applications. The results on semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrate clear practical impact, with state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007. The method is general enough to be applied to many other visual learning tasks.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Addresses an important problem in computer vision (weakly supervised learning) with significant practical implications
- Novel technical contribution that extends SVMs to handle continuous label values in a principled way
- Clear, well-written paper with thorough experiments across multiple computer vision tasks
- Demonstrates significant improvements over previous methods on standard benchmarks
- The method is simple to implement and can be easily integrated with existing systems

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited to computer vision applications (though the method is general)
- The computational complexity of the latent e-SVM training could be more thoroughly analyzed
- The paper doesn't explore the relationship with other weakly supervised learning approaches in depth

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a meaningful advance in weakly supervised learning for computer vision. The technical contribution is sound, the experiments are well-executed, and the results demonstrate significant improvements over previous approaches. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important problem in the field. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to NIPS and should be accepted.
