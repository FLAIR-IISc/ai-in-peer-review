## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for scenarios with continuous “positiveness” values instead of binary labels. The authors effectively extend the SVM framework to handle this, and demonstrate its applicability to weakly-supervised visual segmentation, achieving promising results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, even reaching state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The core idea of weighting samples based on their positiveness is a logical and well-motivated extension of multiple instance learning and weighted SVMs. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard SVC and SVR approaches in this context, and convincingly demonstrates the benefits of e-SVM. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and motivates the need for algorithms capable of leveraging weakly labeled data, a crucial area in computer vision given the cost of full annotation. The presentation is clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to method description and experimental validation. 

A key strength lies in the thorough experimental evaluation. The authors validate e-SVM under both strong and weak supervision, providing a comprehensive assessment of its capabilities. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification is a valuable contribution, offering a more nuanced assessment than traditional metrics. Furthermore, the combination of e-SVM with a state-of-the-art object detector (RCNN) and the resulting performance improvement demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

However, while the paper is strong, some areas could be improved. The connection to existing weighted SVM literature could be more thoroughly explored, particularly regarding the differences in weighting schemes and potential advantages of the proposed approach. The regularization term in the latent e-SVM model, while justified, could benefit from a more detailed discussion of its impact on performance and sensitivity to hyperparameter settings. Finally, while the results are impressive, a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of different components (e.g., the spatial confidence map feature) would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relevant problem in weakly-supervised learning.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (e-SVM).
*   Demonstrates strong experimental results, including state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The connection to existing weighted SVM literature could be more thoroughly discussed.
*   A more detailed analysis of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model would be beneficial.
*   An ablation study could further strengthen the conclusions.

**Overall:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a significant contribution to the field of weakly-supervised learning, offering a novel and effective algorithm with strong empirical validation. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the overall quality and potential impact of the work. This paper is a valid NIPS contribution and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the machine learning and computer vision communities.
