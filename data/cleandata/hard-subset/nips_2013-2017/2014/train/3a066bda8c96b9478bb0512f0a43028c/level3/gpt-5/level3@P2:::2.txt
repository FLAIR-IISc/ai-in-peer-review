Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification of the linear SVM for cases where training supervision comes as a “positiveness” score in [0,1] rather than a binary label. For a sample xi with “positiveness” ui, the method treats the unknown label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as Bernoulli with Pr(Y=+1|xi)=g(ui) (set to ui in experiments) and minimizes the expected hinge loss EY[max(0,1−Y w^T x)] plus an L2 penalty. This yields a weighted combination of positive and negative hinge losses for each example, with weights ui and 1−ui. The paper further introduces a latent e-SVM variant for weak supervision where ui is unobserved (e.g., only bounding boxes available). It alternates between optimizing w (a standard linear SVM with example-dependent weights) and ui, regularized to stay close to initial estimates from box IoU via a convex cross-entropy/KL term. Applications are shown to segment proposal scoring (with new evaluation metrics TAPC and NDCG), semantic segmentation (using CPMC and second-order pooling features), and object detection by concatenating a spatial confidence map feature derived from the e-SVM with RCNN features, yielding 1.2–1.5 mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The formulation is closely related to several established ideas: instance-weighted SVMs and example-dependent misclassification costs (e.g., SVMlight), SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels (expected hinge loss), and latent SVMs (Felzenszwalb et al.) for alternating optimization over latent variables. The weak-supervision setting sits between MIL/bag-label supervision and full pixel supervision, leveraging bounding-box IoU to initialize latent “positiveness” per segment.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive formulation; can be implemented with standard linear SVM solvers by using per-example (and per-class) weights.
- Practical weakly supervised extension with a convex KL regularizer; easy to plug into existing pipelines.
- Solid empirical work on segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection; improvements over SVC/SVR baselines and a consistent mAP lift over RCNN on VOC 2007.
- The spatial confidence map feature is a practical way to integrate segment-based cues with window-based detectors.
- New task-appropriate evaluation measures (TAPC, NDCG) for segment scoring.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. The expected hinge-loss with probabilistic/soft labels is known; and instance-dependent costs are widely supported. The distinction “weights labels rather than data points” seems semantic—the loss is equivalent to weighting two class-specific hinge terms per example (or duplicating examples with fractional weights).
- The latent extension is straightforward alternating minimization; no theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence properties beyond convex subproblems, conditions on identifiability, or generalization with uncertain labels).
- Heavy reliance on a strong KL regularizer around the box-IoU initialization raises the question of how much the latent ui actually moves; lack of analysis/ablation on λR and sensitivity to initialization.
- The claim that [0,1]-valued targets are “not standard regression” is inaccurate; logistic regression and other bounded-regression models are standard. Comparisons omit strong probabilistic baselines (logistic regression on segments with calibration), as well as SVMs with soft labels/uncertain labels.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete (hyperparameters for λw, λR, stopping criteria, exact solver settings). No statistical significance or variance reporting for the detection gains.
- Some citation/formatting issues and minor typos.

Quality
- Method is technically sound as an engineering contribution; the optimization for w and u is clear and uses convex subproblems. However, theoretical support (beyond convexity of substeps) is lacking, and the connection to existing formulations is underdeveloped.
- Empirical evaluation is broad and shows consistent gains, but baselines could be stronger (logistic regression with/without calibration; soft-label SVM; isotonic regression; Beta or bounded regression). Ablations on g(u), λR, initialization ubb, and the contribution of the latent update vs. fixed ubb are missing.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with a straightforward exposition of the objective and alternating optimization. The relationship to WSVM/soft-label SVM should be clarified explicitly, ideally with an equivalence proof or constructive reduction. Minor typos and some broken references should be fixed. Please specify all hyperparameters and convergence criteria.

Originality
- Modest. The expected hinge-loss with probabilistic labels and the alternating optimization with a convex prior are natural extensions of known ideas. The spatial confidence map feature is practical but incremental.

Significance
- The improvements on VOC 2007 detection are useful in practice, and the approach may be adopted in pipelines that have segment proposals plus weak supervision. However, given the limited novelty and missing comparisons to closer baselines, the broader impact is moderate.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify novelty relative to: (i) instance-weighted SVMs and example-dependent costs; (ii) SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels (expected hinge or duplicating samples with fractional weights); (iii) latent SVMs. A small theoretical note showing equivalence of your expected hinge to a weighted SVM objective (or to duplicating examples) would strengthen the contribution and demystify solver compatibility.
- Please correct the statement about regression. Bounded targets are routinely handled (e.g., logistic/Bernoulli likelihood, Beta regression, SVR with clipping, or regression followed by calibration). Include logistic regression and calibrated probabilistic baselines.
- Analyze the role of the KL regularizer: report how far ui moves from ubb during training (e.g., average KL, L1/L2 drift), and performance vs. λR. Include an ablation comparing latent e-SVM to a fixed-u variant using only ubb.
- Explore other g(u) mappings (identity vs. monotone transforms) and report sensitivity.
- For detection, control for feature dimensionality: compare against concatenating random features of the same size, or against spatial pooling of simpler segment scores (e.g., using ubb only). Report per-class gains with variance or cross-val splits to assess significance.
- Provide full training details (C values, λw, λR, number of alternations, stopping criteria) to aid reproducibility.
- Broaden related work to include uncertain/soft-label SVM, learning with label noise, and probabilistic label SVMs; clarify differences.

Minor issues
- Abstract: “continues” → “continuous.”
- Broken/duplicated citations and missing years should be fixed; ensure all references are complete.
- Algorithm 1 formatting; explain stopping criterion.
- Briefly justify TAPC and NDCG choices and include confidence intervals.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, practical method with consistent empirical gains; easy to integrate into existing pipelines.
- Addresses an important weakly supervised setting with bounding-box-only annotations.
- Clear exposition and new evaluation measures for segment scoring.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited conceptual novelty; close to known instance-weighted/soft-label SVM and latent SVM.
- Missing stronger baselines and theoretical grounding; unclear how much the latent variables contribute beyond the initialization.
- Reproducibility and statistical rigor could be improved.

Overall: A solid engineering paper with practical value, but originality and analysis are not yet at NeurIPS bar. Strengthening the positioning versus existing soft-label/weighted SVM literature, adding stronger baselines and ablations, and analyzing the latent component would substantially improve the paper.
