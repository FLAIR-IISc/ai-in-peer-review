Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a soft-label max-margin formulation that directly models “positiveness” as a continuous attribute in [0,1]. Rather than thresholding IoU to cast training as binary classification or treating it as standard regression, the method assumes a Bernoulli label with success probability µi=g(ui) (instantiated as µi=ui) and minimizes the expected hinge loss: each example contributes both positive and negative hinge terms weighted by ui and 1−ui, respectively. This yields a simple optimization that can be solved with standard linear SVM solvers (by interpreting each example as two weighted pseudo-instances). The framework extends naturally to weak supervision by treating ui as latent and adding a convex regularizer that keeps u close to an initial estimate derived from bounding-box overlaps, using a KL divergence between Bernoulli(ubb) and Bernoulli(u). Alternating minimization over w and u provides a practical procedure.

The approach is applied to segment proposal confidence prediction and then instantiated for semantic segmentation (ranking and selecting CPMC segments) and object detection (constructing class-specific spatial confidence maps from segment scores and concatenating with RCNN features). The paper also introduces two segment-level evaluation criteria—TAPC (thresholded AP averaged over IoU thresholds) and NDCG—to more directly assess segment scoring. On PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and VOC 2007 detection, e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR for segment scoring, yields competitive segmentation accuracy, and improves RCNN mAP by 1.2–1.5 points, achieving state-of-the-art on VOC 2007 at the time.

Relation to prior work
The work sits between strongly supervised segment classification and MIL-style weak supervision. Unlike classical MIL (bag-level labels with at least one positive instance), this setting admits fractional positiveness and does not assume “pure” positive instances exist among proposals. Compared to weighted SVMs, e-SVM weights labels rather than samples, effectively distributing each example’s contribution across positive and negative hinge losses. The latent extension resembles latent SVM but avoids degeneracy by keeping u continuous and regularized. The paper compares against SVC and SVR commonly used in segment scoring pipelines and builds on proposal methods like CPMC and RCNN for downstream tasks.

Quality
– Technical soundness: The expected hinge loss is a coherent surrogate for learning with soft labels; the latent variant’s alternating minimization with a convex KL prior is reasonable and avoids trivial 0/1 solutions. The connection to off-the-shelf SVM solvers is practical.
– Empirical support: Results on VOC11/07 are thorough: segment-scoring metrics (TAPC/NDCG) under strong and weak supervision, downstream semantic segmentation accuracy, and additive gains for detection with RCNN. The improvements over SVC/SVR are consistent; the +1.5 mAP on VOC07 is meaningful given the strength of RCNN.
– Limitations: The choice g(u)=u is ad hoc; no calibration study is provided. The regularizer assumes positiveness wrt the true mask is close to IoU with the bounding box; this may be dataset- and category-dependent. Convergence behavior and sensitivity to λR, initialization ubb, and the number of alternations are not deeply analyzed. There is no comparison to “probabilistic-label SVMs” or soft-label/label-distribution learning that also optimize expected margins.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-motivated, with intuitive diagrams and an accessible derivation of the loss. The latent optimization and the spatial confidence map feature are explained crisply. Some notation could be tidied (the regularization term on w and constants), and a short recipe for implementing e-SVM with standard solvers (e.g., splitting each instance into a positive and negative weighted copy) would aid reproducibility. Reporting runtime and memory overhead for per-pixel max over segments would also help.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is a clean, SVM-compatible expected hinge loss for soft labels, and its latent extension with a KL prior tailored to bounding-box supervision. While learning from soft labels has antecedents, applying this expected-margin formulation to segment scoring and demonstrating a robust latent version is a useful contribution. The spatial confidence map feature is a simple but effective integration with detection.

Significance
High for weakly supervised visual learning. The method addresses a practically important regime (bounding-box-only supervision), plugs into existing pipelines, and yields measurable gains on strong baselines. The formulation is general and could be adopted beyond vision wherever continuous “positiveness” arises.

Strengths
– Clear, principled extension of SVM to soft labels with a simple solver pathway.
– Latent variant for weak supervision with a convex, interpretable regularizer.
– Careful evaluation, including new segment-scoring metrics, under both observed and latent settings.
– Demonstrated utility in two major tasks; non-trivial mAP gains on RCNN.
– Well-written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and suggestions
– Justify or learn the link g(·); explore calibration (e.g., Platt scaling) or alternative monotone mappings.
– Analyze sensitivity to λR and initialization ubb; consider annealing λR or early stopping to avoid over-regularization.
– Compare against prior soft-label or probabilistic-label SVMs, and label-proportions/partial-label methods; clarify distinctions.
– Discuss computational costs (training/inference), especially the pixel-level max pooling across segments and the added detection feature.
– Explore other priors on u that encode spatial/shape biases (e.g., central bias within boxes, boundary penalties) rather than only proximity to ubb.
– Provide per-class breakdowns and failure cases to understand when e-SVM helps most.

Questions for the authors
– How sensitive are results to g(u)? Did you try nonlinear mappings or learning g from data?
– What are the convergence criteria and typical number of alternations for the latent model?
– Does the method remain beneficial with other proposal generators (e.g., MCG, Selective Search) and modern features?
– Can you clarify the exact reduction to a standard SVM solver (weighting scheme or duplicated instances)?

Pros for acceptance
– Addresses an important weak-supervision problem with a principled, practical method.
– Solid empirical gains on widely used datasets and baselines.
– Clear exposition; easy to reproduce and build upon.
– General tool likely to be adopted in related applications.

Cons for acceptance
– Theoretical novelty is modest; connections to existing soft-label SVM literature could be deepened.
– Some design choices (g(u), KL prior) are heuristic with limited ablation.
– Limited analysis of optimization behavior and computational cost.

Overall recommendation
A well-executed, clearly presented contribution that extends max-margin learning to continuous positiveness and demonstrates impact on semantic segmentation and object detection under weak supervision. I recommend acceptance.
