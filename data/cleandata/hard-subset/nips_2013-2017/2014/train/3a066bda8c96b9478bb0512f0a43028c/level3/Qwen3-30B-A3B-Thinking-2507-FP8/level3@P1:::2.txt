

This paper proposes an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where each training example has a continuous "positiveness" value in [0,1] rather than a binary label. The authors extend their approach to handle latent positiveness values when only bounding-box annotations are available. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The main contribution is a modification of the SVM loss function that weights examples by their continuous positiveness values. This is framed as a novel approach to weakly supervised learning, particularly for segment proposal classification where the ground-truth overlap (IoU) between proposals and object instances serves as the positiveness measure. The paper also introduces a latent e-SVM extension for cases where positiveness is unobserved, using an iterative optimization approach to estimate these values.

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and VOC 2007 object detection datasets. For segmentation, e-SVM achieves 36.8% accuracy with pixel-level labels and 27.7% with bounding-box annotations. For object detection, when combined with RCNN, it achieves 60.0% average AP, a 1.5% improvement over the baseline. The authors introduce two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) to better assess segment confidence prediction performance.

The paper is well-written with clear descriptions of the method and experiments. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines across multiple evaluation metrics. The applications to semantic segmentation and object detection are well-motivated, and the authors effectively show how their method can be integrated into existing computer vision pipelines.

However, the paper's novelty claim is problematic. The core idea of weighting examples by continuous values is not new; it's equivalent to standard weighted SVM approaches (e.g., as implemented in SVMlight). The authors cite weighted SVMs but don't adequately distinguish their contribution from existing work. The connection to the "weighted SVM" literature in Section 2 is superficial, and the paper fails to demonstrate how their formulation differs from or improves upon established weighted SVM methods. This is a significant concern for a NIPS paper, which requires substantial novelty.

The latent e-SVM extension is conceptually straightforward, but the optimization procedure is non-convex (as the authors acknowledge in Section 3.2). The paper does not provide any theoretical analysis of convergence or optimality for this iterative procedure. Instead, they rely on heuristic regularization that forces weights to stay close to initial IoU estimates. The authors should explicitly address why their approach is superior to simply using the initial IoU values as weights, which would be a simpler and more transparent alternative.

A critical error appears in Section 1, where the authors claim that "it is also not a standard regression problem since the value of positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]." This is incorrect—logistic regression is a standard method for bounded [0,1] outputs, and the authors' proposed approach is essentially a regression method with a specific loss function. This mischaracterization undermines their rationale for developing a new algorithm.

The paper also contains minor issues: "continues" instead of "continuous" in the abstract, and the heavy regularization term (Equation 3) is not adequately justified. The authors should explain why the weights must stay so close to the initial values, and whether this limitation affects the method's general applicability.

In terms of significance, the problem of weakly supervised learning is important, particularly for large-scale datasets where pixel-level annotations are expensive. However, the paper's contribution to this problem is marginal given the existing literature on weighted SVMs and weakly supervised learning (e.g., MIL, multiple instance learning). The paper's results on PASCAL VOC are competitive but do not demonstrate a fundamental advance over existing techniques.

Quality is good in terms of experimental design, but the technical contribution lacks depth. The method is well-executed but not novel. The clarity is excellent, with clear descriptions of the method and experiments. The originality is the paper's weakest aspect, as the core idea is not sufficiently distinct from prior work. The significance is moderate, as the method provides incremental improvements over existing approaches without addressing the fundamental limitations of weak supervision.

Pro: Well-executed experiments showing consistent improvements over baselines; clear presentation; practical applications to computer vision tasks.
Con: Insufficient novelty compared to existing weighted SVM work; incorrect technical claims about regression; non-convex optimization without theoretical analysis; heavy regularization without justification.

The paper would be better suited for a computer vision venue like CVPR than NIPS, as it primarily addresses a specific problem in computer vision with a method that is not substantially different from existing machine learning techniques. While the results are promising, the paper fails to make a compelling case for its novelty as a machine learning contribution. The authors should revise to more clearly distinguish their method from existing weighted SVM approaches and address the theoretical shortcomings.
