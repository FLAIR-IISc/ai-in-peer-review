This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for weakly supervised learning problems where training data is labeled with a “positiveness” value rather than a binary label. The core idea involves formulating the problem as a weighted SVM, where weights are assigned based on this positiveness, and extending it to a latent variable setting when positiveness is unobserved. The authors demonstrate competitive results, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

While the experimental results are strong, particularly the object detection performance, the paper suffers from several shortcomings related to originality, clarity, and completeness. The algorithmic novelty of e-SVM appears limited; it builds upon existing SVM formulations with a weighting scheme related to the observed “positiveness” and extends this via a latent variable approach. However, a substantial body of prior work on weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs is not cited, including key contributions from Suykens et al., Yang et al., Vapnik, and Lapin et al. The latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, and this connection isn’t sufficiently acknowledged or differentiated.

The title and abstract are misleading. The claim that continuous “positiveness” targets are less informative than binary labels is not well-justified and creates a false premise. The core contribution is not about the *informativeness* of continuous targets, but about how to effectively *learn* from them using a modified SVM approach. 

The paper’s exposition is uneven. While space is dedicated to small algorithmic variations, critical details regarding feature engineering are glossed over. Understanding the feature extraction process is crucial for reproducing and building upon this work, yet it receives comparatively little attention. More detail is needed on how the regularization coefficients (λW and λR) were chosen, as the impact of these hyperparameters is significant. The imposed sum-constraint on weights, though potentially novel, was not thoroughly investigated by the reviewer.

The methodology section would benefit from reporting standard baselines. For instance, including results from regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression would provide a stronger justification for the choice of a sum-constrained weighted SVM.  Minor typographical and notation fixes are needed (lines 19, 94, 98, 350, and several wording adjustments).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art object detection on VOC 2007 is a significant achievement, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is an increasingly important area, especially given the cost of large-scale, fully annotated datasets.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The method could be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with weakly labeled data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Originality:** The algorithmic novelty is incremental, and the paper fails to adequately address related work.
*   **Missing Citations:** The omission of key literature on weighted and transductive SVMs raises concerns about the completeness of the work and its proper positioning within the field.
*   **Clarity Issues:** The title and abstract are misleading, and the exposition could be more balanced.
*   **Methodological Gaps:** The lack of standard baselines weakens the justification for the chosen approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach with promising empirical results. However, its originality is limited, and crucial related work is missing. The clarity and completeness of the presentation could also be improved. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend publication *after major revision*. The authors must thoroughly address the prior-work deficiencies, clarify the core contribution, and provide a more comprehensive methodological evaluation. Given the focus on computer vision tasks and the experimental validation on datasets like PASCAL VOC, this paper may be a better fit for a computer vision venue than NIPS. My quality score is contingent upon the authors adequately addressing the concerns outlined in this review.
