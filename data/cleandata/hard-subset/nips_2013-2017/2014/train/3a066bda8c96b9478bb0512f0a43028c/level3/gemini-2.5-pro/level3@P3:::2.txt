### **Summary**

This paper proposes the "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM), a modification of the standard SVM framework for binary classification problems where training labels are not discrete {-1, +1} but are continuous "positiveness" values in the range [0, 1]. The core idea is to model the label for each example as a Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being positive is given by its "positiveness" score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this distribution. This formulation results in a convex optimization problem that can be solved with standard SVM solvers, effectively weighting the positive and negative hinge loss terms for each training example based on its positiveness score.

The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting by treating the positiveness scores as unobserved latent variables. This latent e-SVM is optimized using an alternating minimization scheme, where an SVM is trained with fixed weights, and then the weights are updated with a fixed model. The experiments apply e-SVM to semantic segmentation and object detection, using Intersection-over-Union (IoU) as the positiveness score. The results show that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines and improves the performance of a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN) on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its formulation and the experiments are well-executed. The empirical results are strong, showing clear benefits on challenging computer vision tasks. The applications in semantic segmentation and object detection are relevant and the improvements shown are non-trivial. The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC, NDCG) for the segment-wise prediction task is a thoughtful contribution.

However, the technical depth is limited. The optimization for the latent e-SVM is a standard alternating minimization approach for a non-convex problem, but it lacks any theoretical analysis regarding convergence properties or the quality of the local optima found. This is a significant omission for a NIPS paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The method is described clearly, and the connection between the expectation loss and the final weighted SVM objective is easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are also presented effectively. One point of confusion is the authors' attempt to distinguish their method from weighted SVMs, which is not clearly articulated (see Originality). A minor typo exists in the abstract ("continues" should be "continuous").

**Originality:** This is the major weakness of the paper. The core formulation of the basic e-SVM appears to be equivalent to existing instance-weighted or cost-sensitive SVMs. The objective in Equation 1, where each example `i` contributes `g(u_i) * l_i^+ + (1 - g(u_i)) * l_i^-` to the loss, is a form of an SVM with example-dependent costs. This functionality has been available in standard solvers like SVMlight and LibSVM for many years. The authors briefly discuss weighted SVMs in Section 2 but claim their method is different because it "weights labels instead of data points." This distinction is unclear and appears to be more semantic than functional; the resulting optimization problem is the same. The paper fails to clearly position its core contribution with respect to this extensive prior work.

The latent e-SVM extension is a more novel contribution in this specific context. However, combining a latent variable model with an SVM and optimizing via alternating minimization is a well-established pattern in machine learning (e.g., Latent SVMs). While the application is interesting, the methodological leap is straightforward.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem: learning from soft or weak labels, which are increasingly common. The strong empirical results on top-tier vision tasks demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed framing. If the novelty issues were resolved, the method could be a useful tool for practitioners. The improvement over a state-of-the-art detector like R-CNN is a significant result.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

1.  **Novelty over Instance-Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern is the novelty of the basic e-SVM model. Can the authors clarify how their formulation `L(w) = ... + sum_i [ g(u_i) * max(0, 1-w^T x_i) + (1-g(u_i)) * max(0, 1+w^T x_i) ]` is fundamentally different from a standard weighted SVM where the cost factor `C_i` for each example `i` is set differently for the positive and negative classes? It seems this is just a specific scheme for setting those per-example costs.

2.  **Analysis of the Latent Model:** In the latent e-SVM, a strong KL-divergence regularizer `R(u)` is used to keep the learned weights `u` close to the initial estimates `u_bb`. This suggests that the latent variables may not deviate much from their initialization. It would strengthen the paper significantly to include an analysis of how much the `u_i` values actually change during optimization. If the change is minimal, the benefit of the latent variable optimization over simply using the initial `u_bb` values is questionable.

3.  **Incorrect Claim about Regression:** In the introduction, the authors state that SVR is not ideal because the problem is "not a standard regression problem since the value of positiveness belongs to a bounded interval [0, 1]". This claim is incorrect. Many standard regression frameworks are designed for or can easily handle bounded outputs. For example, logistic regression predicts probabilities in [0, 1], and Beta regression is specifically designed for targets in (0, 1). This statement should be revised.

### **Arguments for/against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem of learning with continuous-valued labels.
*   The method is intuitive, simple, and can be implemented with standard tools.
*   Strong and compelling experimental results on challenging PASCAL VOC benchmarks, including an improvement over the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Major concern about the originality of the core e-SVM method, which appears to be a re-formulation of well-known instance-weighted SVMs. The paper does not adequately compare against or differentiate from this prior work.
*   The technical contribution of the latent variable model is incremental, and the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the proposed non-convex optimization procedure.
*   The paper contains some incorrect claims regarding the relationship of the problem to standard regression techniques.
