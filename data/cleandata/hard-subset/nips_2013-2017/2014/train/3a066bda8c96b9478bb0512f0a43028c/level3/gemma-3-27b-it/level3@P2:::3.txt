This paper proposes the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for problems where training data is labeled with a “positiveness” value representing the degree of affiliation to the positive class, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to frame this as a weighted SVM problem, where the weights correspond to the positiveness values. The algorithm is extended to a latent variable setting for weakly supervised learning, where positiveness is unobserved. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper tackles a relevant problem – learning from weakly supervised data – which is increasingly important in computer vision due to the high cost of detailed annotation. The approach of adapting SVMs to handle continuous labels is a natural one, and the experimental results, particularly the object detection performance, are impressive. The ability to handle both strongly and weakly supervised scenarios is a significant advantage.

However, despite the promising results, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily relating to originality and a lack of thorough engagement with prior work. The algorithmic novelty is limited; the proposed e-SVM is closely related to existing weighted SVM and transductive SVM methods, and crucial references in these areas are missing. Specifically, the paper fails to cite fundamental work by Suykens et al. (2002) on weighted least squares SVMs, Yang et al. (2004) on weighted SVMs, and Vapnik (1998) relating to SVM foundations, alongside more recent works utilizing privileged information (Lapin et al., 2010). The latent e-SVM formulation is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, a connection that isn’t adequately discussed or differentiated. 

While the sum constraint on weights may be a novel element, the reviewer acknowledges not fully investigating this aspect. However, the core contribution is not substantially different from previously published work. The authors should rigorously demonstrate the advantage of their specific formulation compared to existing methods.

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks comparison to standard baselines. For the continuous label regression problem, a regularized logistic regression model would provide a strong and easily implemented baseline for comparison, justifying the use of the more complex sum-constrained weighted SVM. Furthermore, the paper provides insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection, particularly for the regularization coefficients (λW and λR). How were these values chosen, and what sensitivity analysis was performed?

The clarity of the paper could be improved. The title and abstract do not accurately reflect the core contribution and misleadingly suggest that unit-interval targets are inherently less informative than binary labels. The exposition is also somewhat unbalanced, dedicating excessive space to minor algorithmic variations while neglecting crucial details regarding feature engineering. For example, the specific features used for semantic segmentation and object detection are not described in sufficient detail.

Minor issues include typographical errors (lines 19, 94, 98, 350) and some awkward wording.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong experimental results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.
*   The algorithm addresses a relevant and important problem in computer vision.
*   The method is versatile and can handle both strongly and weakly supervised learning scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited algorithmic novelty; the method is closely related to existing weighted and transductive SVMs, with critical prior work missing from the literature review.
*   Lack of comparison to strong baselines, hindering a proper evaluation of the proposed method.
*   Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and feature engineering.
*   Clarity issues with the title, abstract, and overall exposition.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a reasonably sound and potentially useful approach to learning from weakly supervised data. However, the lack of novelty and the omission of relevant prior work significantly detract from its overall quality.  The paper's quality score is contingent on the authors addressing the prior-work relations through a substantial revision. 

**Recommendation:**

Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper, in its current form, is not suitable for NIPS. However, with a thorough revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the comprehensive inclusion of relevant prior work and a justification for the algorithmic choices, the paper could become a valuable contribution. The work may be a better fit for a computer vision-focused venue.
