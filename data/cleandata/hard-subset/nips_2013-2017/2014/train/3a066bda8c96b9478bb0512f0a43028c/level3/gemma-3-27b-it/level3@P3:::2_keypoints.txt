- Proposes Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that weights examples by continuous “positiveness” and extends it to a latent-variable setting for weak supervision.
- Experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrate performance gains over standard SVM and SVR baselines.
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a comprehensive experimental setup and results.
- Addresses the important problem of learning with noisy/imprecise labels; latent-variable extension is practically useful.
- Core idea of weighting examples by confidence lacks novelty—similar example-dependent cost methods exist (e.g., SVMlight).
- Paper does not adequately differentiate itself from prior work on example-dependent costs and related regression approaches.
- The claim of “non-standard regression” is incorrect—standard regression methods can operate on bounded/continuous outputs.
- The latent-variable extension is a straightforward application and introduces non-convexity without novel methodological contributions.
- Lacks theoretical analysis of the optimization process, particularly regarding non-convexity, convergence, or guarantees.
- Heavy regularization in the latent setting is troubling; learned weights remain close to initial values, suggesting local optima or overly constrained learning.
- The impact of strong regularization on model capacity and why such regularization is necessary is not discussed.
- Implementation and experiments are solid and convincingly show empirical improvements.
- Arguments for acceptance: functional algorithm, practical latent-variable extension, and convincing empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, missing theoretical analysis, and concerns about robustness/generalization due to regularization.
- Minor typo in abstract: “continues” should be “continuous.”
- Overall assessment: weak reject — technically sound and well-executed but insufficient novelty and theoretical contribution.
