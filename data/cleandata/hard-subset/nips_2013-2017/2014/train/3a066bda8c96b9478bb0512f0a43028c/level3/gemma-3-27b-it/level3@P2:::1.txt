This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where, instead of binary labels, training data is associated with a continuous “positiveness” value indicating the degree of affiliation to the positive class. The core idea is to treat this positiveness as a weight in the SVM loss function, effectively performing soft weighting of instances. The authors demonstrate the applicability of e-SVM to segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results, particularly in object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The work builds upon a substantial body of related research in weakly supervised learning, particularly multiple instance learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. The authors correctly position their approach between the constraints of MIL and traditional supervised learning, noting that their setting doesn't satisfy the MIL assumption of at least one positive instance within a bag. They also acknowledge and differentiate their approach from standard weighted SVMs, highlighting the key distinction of weighting *labels* rather than data points. This distinction is crucial for their specific problem formulation. The paper references relevant work from NIPS (e.g., Vezhnevets et al. [26], Liu et al. [19]) and other venues, demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM algorithm presents a well-motivated and novel approach to handling continuous confidence values in weakly supervised learning. The concept of weighting labels instead of instances is a subtle but impactful difference from existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the loss function is clearly presented and appears theoretically sound, building upon the well-established SVM framework. The latent variable extension is also logically presented and addresses a common practical scenario.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of e-SVM across a range of tasks, from segment proposal classification to semantic segmentation and object detection. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant accomplishment.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The use of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1) is helpful in understanding the algorithm's workflow.
*   **Generality:** The authors emphasize the generality of their approach, highlighting its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors, image features, and base detectors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study. For example, exploring different regularization terms for the latent e-SVM (Equation 3) or investigating the sensitivity of the method to the choice of segment proposal algorithm would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Comparison Depth:** Although comparisons to SVC and SVR are provided, a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for weakly supervised learning (beyond simply citing them in the related work) could further solidify the contribution.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't delve into the details of hyperparameter tuning for e-SVM. Providing some insight into the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings would be valuable.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** A discussion of failure cases and potential limitations of the method would enhance the paper's objectivity and provide guidance for future research.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in visual learning: leveraging weakly supervised data.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM across multiple tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.
*   The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The ablation study could be more comprehensive.
*   The comparison to related work could be more in-depth.
*   The discussion of limitations and failure cases is lacking.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is a novel and effective approach to handling continuous confidence values, and the experimental results are promising. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly the inclusion of a more detailed ablation study and a more thorough comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods. The paper is a valid NIPS submission that should be of interest to a broad audience.
