This paper proposes “Expectation-loss SVM” (e-SVM) for learning from weak supervision when each training sample has a continuous “positiveness” score in [0,1] rather than a binary label. The core idea is to replace the hinge loss on a hard label by the expectation of the hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with mean equal to the given positiveness (µi = g(ui), instantiated as g(u)=u). This yields a standard linear SVM with per-example label-weighted loss terms. The method extends to the case where positiveness is unobserved (only bounding boxes available) by treating ui as latent variables, regularized to stay close to a bounding-box-derived proxy ubb via a convex KL term. The authors apply e-SVM to segment proposal confidence prediction (training on pixel-level or only bounding-box labels), and use the learned per-class segment confidences to form spatial confidence map features that are concatenated with RCNN features for detection. Experiments on PASCAL VOC 2011 (segmentation) and VOC 2007 (detection) show that e-SVM outperforms SVC/SVR on segment confidence prediction using two proposed metrics (TAPC and NDCG), yields competitive semantic segmentation accuracy, and improves RCNN detection by 1.2–1.5 mAP.

Relation to prior work: The formulation sits between weighted SVMs and latent/weakly supervised learning. Weighted SVMs (e.g., Suykens et al. 2002; Lapin et al. 2014; Yang et al. 2007) weight instance losses, whereas e-SVM interprets a soft label and weights the positive/negative hinge terms according to a Bernoulli mean. The latent extension resembles latent SVM (Felzenszwalb et al.) and transductive/semi-supervised SVMs (e.g., Joachims), with a convex regularizer to avoid degenerate 0/1 ui. The paper reviews some WSVM works, but the connection to transductive/latent SVMs and soft-label SVMs is only briefly mentioned and could be much clearer. The CV literature on MIL for segmentation is discussed; the paper correctly argues that MIL assumptions are violated when no proposal is fully positive.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple objective that reduces to standard SVM solvers; easy to implement and scale.
- Useful latent extension with convex KL regularization toward ubb; practical for bounding-box-only settings.
- Strong empirical significance: non-trivial gains over SVC/SVR for proposal confidence prediction; +1.2–1.5 mAP over RCNN on VOC07 with straightforward feature concatenation.
- Generality: agnostic to proposal generators and base detectors; the spatial confidence map feature is simple and effective.
- New, task-agnostic evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for proposal-confidence prediction are sensible.

Weaknesses:
- Originality is moderate. The basic e-SVM is essentially a soft-label hinge-loss SVM, and the latent variant is close to a weighted transductive/latent SVM with a specific convex prior; the novelty lies more in packaging and application than in fundamentally new learning principles.
- Prior work coverage is incomplete and the positioning is not sufficiently differentiated. The paper should explicitly relate to: transductive/semi-supervised SVMs (Joachims), latent SVM (Felzenszwalb et al.), soft-label/uncertain-label SVMs, learning from label proportions, and proper scoring-rule-based regression/classification for [0,1] targets.
- Baselines are limited. A strong and standard baseline for unit-interval targets is regularized logistic regression with cross-entropy treating ui as soft labels (or Beta regression), possibly with calibration. This is needed to justify hinge vs. log loss. Also include e.g., squared loss regression with clipping and isotonic regression as ranking baselines.
- Methodological details are thin. The paper needs to specify how λW (weight decay) and λR (KL regularizer) are chosen (search ranges, validation protocol, per-class vs. shared), convergence criteria for alternating optimization, initialization sensitivity (u=ubb vs. alternatives), and runtime.
- Focus/exposition: Disproportionate space is devoted to small objective variants, while the feature-engineering that drives the detection gains (spatial binning, pooling, kernel maps, concatenation with RCNN) is underdescribed. Reproducibility would benefit from more detail and ablations (e.g., effect of m×m, pooling choice, g(u)=u vs. other g).
- Clarity issues: The title/abstract should more directly emphasize “soft-label SVM with latent positiveness under weak supervision.” Avoid statements implying [0,1] targets are less informative than binary labels; they are more informative if measured reliably.
- Minor: Some typos/wording and notation inconsistencies.

Quality:
- Technically sound; the objectives are convex in each block and solvable with standard tools. The KL regularizer is appropriate and well-motivated.
- Claims are supported empirically, but missing baselines and hyperparameter details reduce interpretability. An ablation on the regularizer (KL vs. L2, varying λR), and on the choice of g(u), would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, but needs clearer positioning w.r.t. transductive/latent/soft-label SVM lines, more implementation details, and better organization of the detection integration.

Originality:
- Incremental. The combination of expected hinge with a convex prior for latent [0,1] labels and its application to segment confidence/detection is useful but not a large conceptual leap.

Significance:
- High practical impact. Improving a then-state-of-the-art detector with a simple, principled weak-supervision component is valuable and likely to influence practice.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: (a) logistic regression with cross-entropy on soft labels ui, (b) squared loss regression, (c) isotonic regression (ranking), and report calibration (ECE/Brier). Justify hinge vs. log loss.
- Expand related work to cover: transductive SVM, latent SVM, soft/uncertain-label SVMs, label-proportions learning, and weakly supervised segmentation beyond MIL. Clarify how e-SVM differs and why your choices matter (expected hinge, KL prior).
- Detail hyperparameter selection (λW, λR), convergence, sensitivity, and computation time. Provide ablations on g(u), λR, initialization, and spatial feature design.
- Report detection comparisons under identical RCNN settings and include per-class improvements; consider significance testing or multiple runs.
- If a sum-constraint on positiveness/weights over proposals is used anywhere, explicitly state it, motivate it, and ablate it.
- Clarify and tighten the abstract/title to emphasize the core contribution; avoid potentially misleading claims about information content.

Minor comments (non-exhaustive):
- Fix typographical errors: “continues” → “continuous”, “segmentic” → “semantic”, “It will lead” → “This leads”, missing articles and punctuation in several references; ensure consistent notation for λ, g(u), and boldface vectors.
- Define all acronyms on first use (e.g., TAPC), and ensure Algorithm 1 variables match the text.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general method; easy to adopt.
- Clear empirical gains on a prominent benchmark; community relevance.
- Solid weakly supervised formulation with a principled regularizer.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty; incomplete positioning vs. established SVM variants.
- Missing strong baselines and key methodological details.
- Clarity and exposition need revision for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The empirical significance and practicality outweigh the incremental originality, provided the paper is revised to better situate itself in prior work, add standard baselines, and clarify methodology. This work would also be well received at a computer-vision venue, but it is within NIPS scope given its learning contributions.
