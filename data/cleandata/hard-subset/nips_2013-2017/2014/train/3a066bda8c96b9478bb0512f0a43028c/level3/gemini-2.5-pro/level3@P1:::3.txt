This paper proposes a method for learning from weakly supervised data, framed in the context of computer vision tasks like semantic segmentation and object detection. The core idea is to treat the label of a training example (e.g., a segment proposal) not as a binary {-1, +1} value, but as a continuous "positiveness" score in [0, 1], typically corresponding to the Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object.

The authors formulate this as an "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM), where the binary label is a latent random variable whose expectation is the given positiveness score. This results in a weighted SVM formulation where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative slack terms, with weights determined by its positiveness `u_i` and `1-u_i`, respectively. For weakly supervised scenarios where only bounding boxes are available, the positiveness scores `u_i` are unknown. The paper proposes a latent e-SVM that treats these scores as latent variables and optimizes them iteratively, akin to a transductive SVM (TSVM).

The method is applied to semantic segmentation and, more significantly, to object detection. By generating a "spatial confidence map" from segment scores, the authors create a new feature that, when combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, improves performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark, achieving a new state-of-the-art result.

### Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Significance of Results:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is its empirical results. Improving upon the strong R-CNN baseline on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task is a significant achievement. This demonstrates that combining segmentation cues and well-designed features with deep learning models can yield substantial gains, which is a result of high interest to the community.

2.  **Practical Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from supervision that is weaker than pixel-level annotations but stronger than image-level labels (i.e., bounding boxes). This is a highly relevant setting, as bounding box annotations are far more common and cheaper to obtain than full segmentations.

3.  **Clever System Design:** The proposed "spatial confidence map feature" is an effective way to integrate segment-based classification scores into a window-based object detection pipeline. This is a non-trivial engineering contribution that successfully bridges two different object representations.

### Weaknesses / Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Originality and Missing References:** The primary weakness is the paper's positioning with respect to prior work.
    *   The basic e-SVM model is a form of weighted SVM. While the paper mentions this connection, it fails to cite or discuss a large body of relevant work on weighted SVMs (e.g., Suykens et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2007) and learning with label proportions. The specific formulation, where a single sample `i` has weights `u_i` and `1-u_i` for the positive and negative class constraints, might be novel, but this needs to be explicitly stated and justified against simpler weighting schemes.
    *   The latent e-SVM is conceptually very similar to Transductive SVMs (TSVMs) (Vapnik, 1998), which iteratively infer labels for unlabeled data. Here, the latent positiveness values `u_i` are analogous to the labels being inferred. This connection is not discussed.
    *   The work of Lapin et al. (Neural Networks, 2014) on SVM+ and weighted SVMs, which also deals with alternative supervision, is highly relevant and should be discussed.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for the Model:** The paper does not provide strong justification for the proposed e-SVM formulation over simpler alternatives. For a problem of regressing a target value in the [0, 1] interval, standard methods like regularized logistic regression or beta regression would be natural baselines. The paper only compares against SVR and thresholded SVC, which is not sufficient to establish the superiority of the specific sum-constrained weighted SVM loss function used.

3.  **Clarity and Exposition:**
    *   The title and abstract could be more precise. The contribution is not a general algorithm for learning from any "positiveness" score, but a specific weighted SVM formulation for IoU-like scores, which is then extended to a latent setting.
    *   The paper dedicates significant space to the SVM formulation but provides relatively little detail on the feature engineering (e.g., the second-order pooling features) and the spatial confidence map, which are likely critical to the impressive performance. More detail on these aspects would improve reproducibility and help the reader understand the source of the gains.
    *   Details on hyperparameter selection are missing. It is unclear how the regularization parameters `λw` and `λR` were chosen, which is important for reproducibility.

### Overall Recommendation:

This paper presents a well-engineered system that achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark, which is a significant contribution. However, the work suffers from a major weakness in its failure to properly situate its core algorithmic ideas within the existing literature on weighted and transductive SVMs. The novelty of the proposed method is therefore unclear.

I would be willing to raise my score if the authors thoroughly revise the paper to:
1.  Add a comprehensive discussion of related work on weighted SVMs and TSVMs, clearly articulating what is novel about their specific formulation.
2.  Provide stronger experimental justification for their model by comparing against other standard methods for regression on the [0, 1] interval.
3.  Improve the clarity of the exposition, particularly regarding the feature engineering and hyperparameter tuning.

As it stands, the paper feels more like a strong computer vision application paper than a foundational machine learning contribution. While the results are impressive, the lack of proper context regarding prior machine learning work is a serious flaw. I recommend acceptance, but only on the condition of a major revision to address these points.
