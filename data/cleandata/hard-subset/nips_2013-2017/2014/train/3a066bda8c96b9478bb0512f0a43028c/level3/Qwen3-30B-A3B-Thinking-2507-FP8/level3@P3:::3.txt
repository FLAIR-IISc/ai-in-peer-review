

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) approach for learning with continuous "positiveness" labels (values in [0,1]), with applications to semantic segmentation and object detection under weak supervision (bounding box annotations). The core idea involves treating the continuous label as a probability and incorporating it into the SVM loss function, with a latent extension for when the exact positiveness is unobserved.

## Summary and Significance
The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection (60.0% AP, 1.5% improvement over RCNN). The significance of beating recent CNN results with a conventional approach is notable, as it demonstrates the value of careful feature engineering and loss function design even in the era of deep learning. The method's applicability to semantic segmentation with both strong and weak supervision is also valuable.

## Strengths
- The empirical results are compelling, particularly the object detection improvement over RCNN.
- The latent e-SVM extension for weak supervision is well-motivated for practical computer vision applications where pixel-level annotations are expensive.
- The paper provides a clear framework for applying the method to both segmentation and detection tasks.

## Weaknesses

### Originality and Related Work
The paper's main contribution is limited in algorithmic novelty. The e-SVM model is essentially a weighted SVM with a specific weight function (g(ui)=ui), and the latent extension is a weighted transductive SVM. Crucially, the paper fails to cite key literature on weighted SVMs (Suykens et al., Lapin et al., Yang et al., Vapnik) and transductive SVMs, making it difficult to assess how this work differs from prior art. The claim that "unit-interval targets are less informative than binary labels" in the abstract is misleading, as the paper itself demonstrates that using the continuous label improves performance over binary classification.

### Methodology and Justification
The paper does not adequately justify why a sum-constrained weighted SVM (with the sum constraint on weights being a key feature) is superior to standard approaches like regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression. The authors should have compared against these standard baselines to justify their approach. The hyperparameters (λW and λR) are not properly explained, and the paper lacks sufficient detail on how they were selected.

### Clarity and Exposition
The title and abstract do not clearly convey the core contribution. The paper spends excessive time on minor algorithmic variations while neglecting critical details about feature engineering and implementation choices. The paper's claim about the novelty of the sum-constraint on weights is unsubstantiated, as the constraint appears to be a standard technique in weighted SVMs.

### Technical Accuracy
The latent e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM, but the paper does not sufficiently differentiate it from prior work. The paper's discussion of how the method handles the "weak supervision" setting is also not precise - it's not clear why MIL methods don't apply here, as the paper claims.

### Quality and Presentation
The paper is missing crucial references to foundational work in weighted SVMs and transductive learning. There are several typographical and notation errors (lines 19, 94, 98, 350), and the paper's focus on small algorithmic variations over critical implementation details is problematic.

## Recommendation
The paper has significant empirical merit and addresses an important problem in computer vision, but its theoretical contribution is limited and it fails to properly situate itself in the literature. The core idea is not novel enough for NIPS, and the paper would be better suited for a computer vision venue like CVPR or ICCV where the applied context is more relevant.

I recommend **conditional acceptance** if the authors address the following:
1. Properly cite and differentiate from key weighted SVM and transductive SVM literature
2. Justify the sum-constrained weighted SVM approach with comparisons to standard baselines
3. Provide more detail on hyperparameter selection
4. Clarify the relationship between latent e-SVM and existing transductive learning methods
5. Fix the misleading claims about unit-interval targets in the abstract

The paper has strong empirical results that would be valuable to the computer vision community, but it requires significant revision to properly acknowledge prior work and clarify its theoretical contribution before being suitable for NIPS.
