This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for tasks where training examples are labeled with a continuous "positiveness" value between 0 and 1, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to modify the standard SVM hinge loss by weighting each example's positive and negative contributions according to this positiveness score. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM where the positiveness weights are treated as unobserved variables, allowing the model to be trained with weaker supervision, such as bounding box annotations. This work relates to the broader literature on weakly supervised learning and instance-weighted SVMs.

The paper's main strength is its clear presentation and strong empirical evaluation. The experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection are thorough and convincingly demonstrate that the proposed e-SVM framework outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines in both strongly and weakly supervised settings. The application to challenging computer vision tasks and the demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection highlight the practical significance of the approach.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its contribution. The most significant concern is its originality. The proposed method of applying per-example weights to the SVM loss function appears functionally equivalent to existing SVMs with "example-dependent costs," a technique that has been known and implemented for many years (e.g., in SVMlight). The paper fails to adequately position itself with respect to this extensive prior work, making its core novelty unclear.

Furthermore, while the latent-weight extension is interesting, its optimization is non-convex, and the paper provides no theoretical analysis regarding convergence guarantees. The heavy regularization used on the latent weights raises questions about how much they are actually "learned" versus simply staying close to their initial values; an analysis of this would be beneficial. Finally, the paper makes an incorrect claim in the introduction that regression on a bounded [0,1] interval is "non-standard," overlooking common models like logistic regression.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a well-executed empirical study, its primary contribution appears to be a re-application of a known technique. The significant concerns about novelty and the lack of theoretical analysis for the latent model are major limitations.
