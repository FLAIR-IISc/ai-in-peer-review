- Problem: learning binary classifiers from continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU) common in computer vision.
- Method: model the unobserved binary label as Bernoulli with success probability from the positiveness value and minimize the expected SVM hinge loss (e-SVM).
- Formulation: e-SVM reduces to a weighted SVM where each sample contributes weighted positive and negative loss terms, solvable with standard SVM solvers.
- Variants: basic e-SVM for observed positiveness and latent e-SVM for weak supervision that alternates between updating SVM weights and latent positiveness u with regularization toward an initial estimate.
- Experiments: evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection, outperforming SVC/SVR baselines and improving R-CNN by 1.5% AP on PASCAL VOC 2007, achieving state-of-the-art detection.
- Evaluation metrics: introduces Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) for segment proposal classification.
- Significance: addresses a practical weak supervision challenge and leverages cheaper annotations like bounding boxes effectively.
- Originality: novel, well-motivated expectation-over-labels formulation that is more natural than thresholding or SVR and goes beyond typical MIL approaches.
- Quality: technically sound with thorough and convincing experiments.
- Clarity: well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.
- Weakness — optimization/convergence: latent e-SVM objective is non-convex; needs discussion of sensitivity to initialization and empirical convergence behavior.
- Weakness — model choices: sets µ_i = u_i and uses a KL regularizer; should justify these choices and consider alternatives (e.g., learnable mapping g(·) or L2 regularizer).
- Weakness — computational cost: iterative training requires solving an SVM each outer loop; report and discuss training time compared to baselines.
- Conclusion: solid, principled, and effective contribution with minor issues; recommendation for acceptance.
