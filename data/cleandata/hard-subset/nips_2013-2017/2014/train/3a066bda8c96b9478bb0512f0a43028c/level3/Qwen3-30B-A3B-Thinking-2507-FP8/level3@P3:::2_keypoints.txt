- Paper proposes e-SVM: SVM with example-specific "positiveness" in [0,1], and a latent e-SVM when positiveness is unobserved.
- Method weights examples by their positiveness and applies to semantic segmentation and object detection, claiming SOTA on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Core contribution insufficiently distinguished from prior weighted SVM / example-dependent cost methods (e.g., SVMlight); mathematical equivalence suspected.
- Claim that "positiveness in [0,1] makes it non-standard regression" is incorrect; bounded-output regression (e.g., logistic regression) is standard.
- Strength: Well-written with clear experimental results showing e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR in strong and weak supervision.
- Strength: Practical application to object detection with ~1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Strength: Thorough experiments covering two major vision tasks with appropriate baselines.
- Strength: Latent extension is conceptually straightforward and useful when pixel-level annotations are unavailable.
- Weakness: Novelty concern — approach appears to be a straightforward application of existing weighted SVM techniques without clear differentiation.
- Weakness: No theoretical analysis of the non-convex latent e-SVM optimization (no convergence or optimality guarantees).
- Weakness: Heavy regularization on latent weights implies learned weights remain near initial IoU values; this is neither analyzed nor justified.
- Weakness: Incorrect technical claim about regression undermines credibility.
- Weakness: Minor typo in abstract ("continues" → "continuous").
- Quality: Technically sound experiments support performance claims, but theoretical foundation is weak.
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and Algorithm 1 is clear, but lacks discussion on regularization and contains the incorrect regression claim.
- Originality: Core idea lacks novelty and fails to clearly articulate differences from prior example-dependent cost SVM work.
- Significance: Relevant and useful application to reduce annotation needs; gains are modest but meaningful for practitioners.
- Pro-accept: Practical approach with demonstrated benefits and thorough experiments; useful latent extension.
- Con-reject: Insufficient novelty, inadequate comparison to prior weighted-SVM literature, unexplained regularization, and incorrect claims.
- Recommendation: Reject — needs clearer literature comparison, analysis of regularization effects, correction of the regression claim, and articulation of novelty to meet NIPS standards.
