

This paper proposes an "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples have continuous "positiveness" values in [0,1] rather than binary labels. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision scenarios (e.g., bounding box annotations instead of pixel-level labels) and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection.

The paper has notable strengths: the approach is conceptually simple yet effective, achieving SOTA results on VOC 2007 (60.0 mAP), and the problem of learning from bounded interval labels is practically important given the prevalence of weakly labeled datasets. The application to semantic segmentation and object detection is well-motivated and demonstrates practical utility.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. The core contribution is not sufficiently novel: the proposed e-SVM is essentially a weighted transductive SVM that is not clearly differentiated from prior work (Suykens et al., Vapnik, Lapin et al.). Crucially, the paper fails to cite foundational work on weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs, making the novelty claim unsubstantiated. The sum-constraint on weights is claimed as novel but lacks thorough justification or comparison to existing methods.

The paper also has serious quality issues: the title and abstract misleadingly claim unit-interval targets are "less informative than binary labels," which is not supported by evidence. The methodological section lacks crucial details about hyperparameter selection (λW, λR), and the paper spends excessive space on minor algorithmic variations while omitting critical feature engineering details. Baselines like regularized logistic regression for unit-interval regression are not reported, undermining the justification for using the sum-constrained weighted SVM.

While the results are compelling, the paper requires substantial revision to properly situate the work in the literature, clarify contributions, and provide necessary methodological details. The significance of beating CNN results with a conventional approach is high, but this is overshadowed by the lack of proper scholarly context. I recommend acceptance conditional on addressing all these issues, particularly the extensive missing references and clarification of the relationship to weighted SVM literature. The paper would likely fit better at a computer vision venue than a general AI conference.
