- Proposes "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) for learning from continuous [0,1] "positiveness" labels and a latent e-SVM when positiveness is unobserved.
- Applies method to semantic segmentation and object detection, claiming state-of-the-art on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Builds on existing weakly supervised learning and weighted SVM literature; core idea is weighting examples by positiveness.
- Incorrectly claims [0,1] outputs are "non-standard regression" despite existing methods (e.g., logistic regression) handling bounded outputs.
- The example-weighting idea appears equivalent to established example-dependent cost/weighted SVM approaches (e.g., SVMlight).
- Latent extension is straightforward and similar to standard latent SVMs; optimization (Algorithm 1) is non-convex and lacks theoretical justification.
- Paper is well-written and organized with a clear algorithm description.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation (TAPC and NDCG).
- Object detection shows only a modest 1.5% AP improvement over the R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007.
- Heavy regularization in the latent version effectively keeps learned weights close to initial IoU, limiting actual learning beyond bounding-box annotations.
- Claims of "outperforming alternative approaches" are overstated given modest gains and incomplete comparisons to recent weakly supervised methods.
- Contribution is incremental; significance is limited despite practical utility for reducing labeling effort.
- Strengths: clear motivation, thorough experiments, consistent baseline improvements, and a useful latent extension description.
- Weaknesses: insufficient differentiation from prior weighted SVM work, incorrect technical claim about bounded outputs, no theoretical analysis of latent optimization, heavy regularization, and modest empirical gains.
- Arguments for acceptance: well-motivated practical method, thorough experiments, general applicability, and a useful latent extension.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient novelty vs. weighted SVM literature, weak theoretical foundation, modest improvements, and incorrect technical claims.
- Overall recommendation: reject due to insufficient novelty and theoretical justification, though the paper has some practical value and could be improved with stronger positioning against prior work.
