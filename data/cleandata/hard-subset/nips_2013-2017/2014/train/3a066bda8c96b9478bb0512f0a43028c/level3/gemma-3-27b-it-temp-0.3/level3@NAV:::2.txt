## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes a modification to Support Vector Machines (SVMs) – the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) – designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data is labeled with a “positiveness” value rather than a binary label. The core idea is to weight each example in the loss function by this positiveness, and extend this concept to latent, unobserved weights. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over standard SVM and SVR approaches.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. The experimental setup is comprehensive, and the results presented demonstrate a consistent performance gain across different tasks and annotation settings. The extension to latent weights is a logical progression and addresses a practical challenge in weakly supervised learning. The inclusion of comparisons to both SVC and SVR provides a good baseline for evaluating the proposed method. 

However, a significant concern is the novelty of the approach. The method appears remarkably similar to existing techniques for example-dependent costs, such as those implemented in SVMlight and other weighted SVM implementations. While the authors frame it as an “expectation loss,” the underlying principle of weighting examples based on their confidence or relevance is not new. The latent variable extension is interesting, but its implementation seems relatively straightforward, and lacks any theoretical analysis regarding convergence or optimality. The heavy regularization used in the latent variable case suggests the learned weights remain close to their initial values, a point the authors should address with further discussion. 

Furthermore, the authors make an incorrect claim stating that a bounded output range [0,1] makes this a non-standard regression problem. Logistic regression, a standard and widely used technique, also outputs values within this range. A minor typo exists in the abstract ("continues" should be "continuous").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated empirical improvements on multiple tasks.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in weakly supervised learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea closely resembles existing weighted SVM techniques.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the latent variable extension.
*   Heavy regularization raises questions about the learning process.
*   Incorrect claim regarding regression problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a well-executed empirical study and addresses a practical problem, the lack of significant novelty and theoretical grounding weakens its contribution. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to the authors to more thoroughly investigate and contrast their method with existing work on example-dependent costs and to provide a more rigorous analysis of the latent variable extension.
