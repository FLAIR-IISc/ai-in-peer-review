- Proposes expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from continuous "positiveness" labels in [0,1], and a latent e-SVM for unobserved positiveness from bounding boxes.  
- Core contribution: modifying SVM loss to weight examples by continuous positiveness, applied to segment proposal classification using IoU as positiveness.  
- Extension: iterative latent-variable optimization to estimate positiveness when only bounding-box annotations exist.  
- Evaluated on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and VOC 2007 detection; segmentation: 36.8% (pixel labels) and 27.7% (boxes); detection: RCNN+e-SVM = 60.0% AP (+1.5%).  
- Introduces two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment confidence prediction.  
- Strengths: well-written, clear method description, thorough experiments, consistent improvements over SVC/SVR baselines, practical integration into vision pipelines.  
- Major novelty concern: weighting by continuous values is effectively standard weighted SVM (e.g., SVMlight); authors do not sufficiently distinguish their method from existing weighted SVM literature.  
- Section 2’s connection to weighted SVMs is superficial and the paper fails to show substantive differences or advantages over established weighted-SVM methods.  
- Latent e-SVM optimization is non-convex with no theoretical convergence or optimality analysis; relies on heuristic regularization to keep weights near initial IoU.  
- Authors should justify why iterative estimation is better than simply using initial IoU weights as a simpler baseline.  
- Incorrect claim in Section 1 that bounded [0,1] outputs are not a standard regression problem—logistic/regression methods handle bounded outputs; this mischaracterization weakens motivation.  
- Minor issues: typo ("continues" vs "continuous") and insufficient justification for the heavy regularization term (Eq. 3).  
- Significance: weakly supervised learning is important, but the paper’s contribution is marginal given prior weighted-SVM and weak supervision work (e.g., MIL); results are incremental.  
- Overall assessment: good experimental quality and clarity, but limited technical novelty and theoretical depth; better suited for a computer-vision venue (e.g., CVPR) unless authors more clearly differentiate from weighted SVM and address theoretical shortcomings.
