## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes a modification to Support Vector Machines (SVMs) designed for scenarios where training data is labeled with a “positiveness” value (between 0 and 1) rather than a hard binary label. The core idea is to weight each example in the loss function by this positiveness value, and extend this to handle cases where even the positiveness is unobserved (treated as a latent variable). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) through experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection.

The work is well-described and the experimental setup is clear. The authors appropriately motivate the problem of learning from weakly supervised data, highlighting the cost of fully annotated datasets. The extension to latent weights is a reasonable approach to handling scenarios with incomplete information, and the experimental results demonstrate a clear benefit over standard SVM and SVR baselines, particularly in the weakly supervised setting. The inclusion of both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks provides a good breadth of evaluation.

However, the major novelty of this work is questionable. The core concept of weighting examples in an SVM based on their confidence or a related metric is not new.  Methods like SVMlight have long incorporated example-dependent costs, and the proposed weighting scheme appears closely related to these existing approaches. The paper doesn't adequately differentiate itself from this prior art.  The latent-weight extension, while interesting, is a fairly straightforward application of standard latent variable techniques and lacks theoretical justification regarding its convergence or optimality. The authors heavily rely on regularization, and the fact that learned weights remain close to initialization suggests the optimization may be stuck in a suboptimal region; a discussion of this would be valuable. Furthermore, the claim that using a [0,1] bounded positivity makes the problem "non-standard regression" is incorrect – logistic regression, for example, is a standard regression technique with outputs bounded to [0,1].  Finally, a minor correction: “continues” should be “continuous” in the abstract.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated empirical improvements over strong baselines in relevant tasks.
*   Clear presentation and well-structured experiments.
*   Addresses a practical problem – learning from weakly supervised data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; the core idea overlaps with existing methods (e.g., example-dependent costs in SVMlight).
*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the latent-weight extension.
*   Heavy reliance on regularization with insufficient discussion of its impact.
*   Incorrect statement about regression problems.



**Overall, I lean towards rejection.** While the paper presents a functional algorithm and validates it empirically, the lack of significant novelty and theoretical grounding, combined with the minor inaccuracies, prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors should address the novelty concerns by more thoroughly contrasting their work with prior art and providing a more in-depth analysis of the optimization process.
