This paper proposes a novel Expectation-Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed to address weakly supervised learning problems where training data is characterized by continuous “positiveness” values instead of binary labels. The core idea is to extend the standard SVM by weighting samples based on their positiveness, allowing the model to effectively leverage information from partially correct segment proposals. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving competitive results, including state-of-the-art object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The paper builds upon previous work in weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), and weighted SVMs. It correctly identifies the limitations of directly applying standard SVC and SVR to this problem, where positiveness values are continuous and bounded. Unlike MIL, which assumes at least one positive instance within a bag, e-SVM gracefully handles cases where segment proposals are only partially correct.  The work also acknowledges and differentiates itself from weighted SVMs by emphasizing the weighting of *labels* rather than data points, leading to a unique loss function. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature, including work by Carreira et al. on segmentation with region proposals, and Girshick et al. on R-CNN.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a crucial area in computer vision due to the high cost of obtaining fully annotated datasets. This work directly tackles this problem with a well-motivated approach.
* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation, extending the standard SVM to incorporate continuous positiveness weights, is a novel and effective approach. The latent e-SVM for handling unobserved positiveness is a valuable extension.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the proposed method is clearly articulated, and the mathematical formulation is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM across multiple tasks and settings (strong vs. weak supervision). Achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance is a significant accomplishment.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The paper provides a good comparison to relevant baselines (SVC and SVR) and demonstrates the advantages of e-SVM.
* **Practicality:** The method is applicable to various segment proposal detectors, image features, and classifiers, making it a versatile tool for weakly supervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Regularization in Latent e-SVM:** While the rationale for the regularization term in the latent e-SVM is explained (preventing u from becoming 0 or 1), a more detailed justification for the specific choice of the KL-divergence based regularization could be beneficial. Exploring alternative regularization techniques might also be valuable.
* **Spatial Confidence Map Details:** The description of the spatial confidence map feature for object detection, while conceptually clear, lacks detail regarding the impact of the bin size (m=5). A brief ablation study or discussion on the sensitivity of performance to this parameter would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successes but provides limited insight into potential failure cases or limitations of the approach. Discussing scenarios where e-SVM might struggle could strengthen the analysis.
* **Connection to Existing Regression Techniques:** While the authors correctly point out that standard SVR isn't ideal, a more nuanced discussion regarding how e-SVM differs from other regression techniques designed for bounded outputs (e.g., sigmoid-based regression) could be included.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel algorithm for weakly supervised learning.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and thoroughly evaluated.
* The contribution is significant and aligns well with the topics of interest at NIPS.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* While the regularization choice in the latent e-SVM is reasonable, its justification could be strengthened.
* The discussion of the spatial confidence map feature could benefit from more details and analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in computer vision with a well-designed and effectively validated algorithm. The e-SVM formulation is novel and offers a significant improvement over existing methods for weakly supervised learning.  The clear presentation and comprehensive experiments further solidify the paper's contribution.  I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and recommend acceptance.
