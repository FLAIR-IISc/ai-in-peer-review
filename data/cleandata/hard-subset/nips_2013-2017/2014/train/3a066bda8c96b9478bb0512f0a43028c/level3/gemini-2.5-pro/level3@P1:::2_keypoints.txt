- Proposes e-SVM: replace SVM hinge loss with its expectation over a latent Bernoulli label whose success probability is the example's known "positiveness" in [0,1].  
- Extends to latent e-SVM when positiveness values are unobserved, alternatingly optimizing latent u and model parameters.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on semantic segmentation and object detection, improving R-CNN by ~1.5% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.  

Strengths:
- Addresses an important, practical weakly supervised learning problem (continuous positiveness / bounding-box supervision).  
- Strong empirical results with thorough experiments and meaningful gains over strong baselines.  
- Clear, well-written presentation with an intuitive formulation linking positiveness to expected loss.  
- Creative use of segment-level spatial confidence maps to enhance window-based object detectors.  

Weaknesses:
- Major novelty concern: e-SVM appears mathematically equivalent to instance-weighted SVMs (example-dependent costs), a well-known and implemented technique.  
- Latent e-SVM uses straightforward alternating minimization for a non-convex objective with no theoretical analysis or convergence guarantees.  
- Heavy KL regularization on latent u raises questions about how much is actually learned versus staying near initialization (e.g., bbox overlap).  
- Incorrect motivation claim that SVR cannot handle bounded [0,1] outputs; standard regression approaches (e.g., logistic) can handle this.  

Detailed comments:
- Empirical work is high quality, but technical contribution is weakened by lack of discussion of weighted-SVM prior work and theoretical analysis of the latent optimization.  
- Paper is clear and well-explained; minor typo noted in abstract ("continues" -> "continuous").  
- Originality is questionable; the method needs careful positioning relative to instance-dependent cost SVM literature.  
- Significance is primarily empirical (practical value to vision practitioners) but limited for core ML novelty.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Tackles a relevant weak supervision problem and yields practical improvements.  
- Presents compelling, state-of-the-art empirical gains on a challenging detection benchmark.  
- Intuitive, well-explained approach that is effective in practice.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Core formulation likely equivalent to existing weighted SVM approaches, undermining novelty.  
- Latent model extension lacks theoretical justification and analysis.  
- Insufficient treatment of related work on instance-weighted SVMs and prior methods.  

Conclusion:
- Empirically strong and practically useful but methodologically borderline; authors must address equivalence to instance-weighted SVMs and analyze the latent optimization to be a clear contribution.
