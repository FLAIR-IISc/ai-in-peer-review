

# Review for NIPS 2014

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples have "positiveness" values (continuous values in [0,1]) instead of binary labels. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations available). The method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with claims of state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection.

The paper builds upon existing work in weakly supervised learning and weighted SVMs. The authors correctly note that standard SVM classification (SVC) requires thresholding the positiveness values, while standard SVM regression (SVR) is not ideal for bounded [0,1] outputs. However, the claim that the bounded [0,1] range makes it "non-standard regression" is incorrect; logistic regression and other standard methods already handle bounded outputs.

Regarding novelty, the core idea of weighting examples based on their positiveness values appears equivalent to existing "example-dependent cost" methods (e.g., SVMlight), which have been well-established in the literature for several years. The authors fail to sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work, particularly from weighted SVMs where examples are assigned different misclassification costs. The latent extension is straightforward and resembles standard latent SVM approaches, though the optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) is non-convex without theoretical justification.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a detailed description of the algorithm and thorough experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR on the PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset using both TAPC and NDCG metrics. For object detection, they achieve a modest 1.5% improvement in average precision on PASCAL VOC 2007 compared to the RCNN baseline.

The quality of the work is technically sound in terms of experimental evaluation, though the theoretical foundation is weak. The authors use heavy regularization in the latent version (equation 3), which effectively constrains the learned weights to stay close to the initial IoU values (as noted in their algorithm description). This should be explicitly acknowledged as a limitation, as it suggests the method may not be learning much beyond the initial bounding box annotations. The claim that the method "outperforms alternative approaches" is somewhat overstated given the modest gains and the lack of comparison to more recent weakly supervised methods.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution. While the approach could be useful for reducing labeling effort in computer vision tasks, the improvements are marginal (1.5% AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007), and the method is not substantially different from existing weighted SVM techniques. The authors' claim of "state-of-the-art" performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 is accurate but not particularly impressive given the current state of the art.

**Strengths:**
- Well-structured paper with clear motivation and problem setting
- Thorough experimental evaluation on two important computer vision tasks
- Demonstrated consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines
- Clear description of the latent extension for weak supervision
- Appropriate use of standard evaluation metrics for the tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of sufficient differentiation from existing weighted SVM approaches
- Incorrect claim about the non-standard nature of [0,1] bounded outputs
- No theoretical analysis of the latent optimization procedure
- Heavy regularization suggests limited learning beyond initial bounding box annotations
- Modest performance gains that don't fully justify the claimed significance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The method is well-motivated for practical weak supervision scenarios
- The experiments are thorough and well-designed
- The approach is general and could be applied to other domains
- The latent extension is a useful contribution for weakly supervised learning

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The core technical contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to existing weighted SVM literature
- The theoretical foundation is weak and lacks proper justification
- The performance gains are modest and don't represent a significant advance
- The paper makes incorrect technical claims that undermine its credibility

Overall, this paper presents a practical approach to weakly supervised learning with some empirical benefits, but the contribution appears incremental rather than novel. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their theoretical foundation and clarify how their method differs from existing weighted SVM techniques to justify publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a more careful comparison to prior work on weighted SVMs and a clearer discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the heavy regularization used in the latent version.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and theoretical foundation, though the paper has some practical value for computer vision practitioners working with weakly labeled data. The authors should revise to better position their work in the context of existing weighted SVM literature before resubmission.
