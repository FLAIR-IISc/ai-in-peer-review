This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for problems where training data is labeled with a “positiveness” value representing the degree of belonging to a class, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to treat this positiveness as an expectation and formulate a weighted SVM to learn from it. The algorithm is extended to a latent variable setting for weak supervision, specifically when only bounding-box annotations are available, and demonstrated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, achieving state-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The work builds upon the growing need for algorithms capable of leveraging weakly supervised data, motivated by the expense of large-scale, fully annotated datasets.  The authors correctly identify the limitations of directly applying standard binary classification (SVC) or regression (SVR) techniques to this problem, arguing that binarizing the positiveness or treating it as a standard regression target is suboptimal.  The e-SVM aims to address this by weighting the loss function based on the provided “positiveness” value.  This is a relevant direction for research given the prevalence of imperfect labels in real-world applications, particularly in computer vision.

The strengths of the paper lie in its practical application and empirical results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007, even with a modest improvement, is a significant contribution. The demonstration of the method’s effectiveness under both strong and weak supervision conditions, and its adaptability to different tasks (segmentation and detection), highlights its versatility. The clear presentation of experimental setups and results, with comparisons to SVC and SVR, provides a solid basis for evaluating the proposed approach.

However, the paper suffers from a significant omission of relevant prior work. The authors fail to adequately acknowledge and cite the extensive literature on weighted SVMs and, critically, transductive SVMs.  Methods like those by Suykens et al. (2002) and Yang et al. (2009) directly address learning with instance-dependent weights, and the latent e-SVM formulation closely resembles weighted transductive SVMs.  This omission undermines the claim of novelty. Furthermore, works on Privileged Information by Lapin et al. also leverage similar weighting schemes. The proposed "sum constraint" on weights, while potentially a useful detail, also warrants more thorough comparison to existing literature on constrained optimization in SVMs.  

The algorithmic novelty is therefore limited. While the application to segment proposal classification and the specific weighting scheme might be a reasonable combination, it doesn't represent a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough. The latent e-SVM, in particular, feels more like a specific instantiation of a well-established technique (weighted transductive SVM) rather than a novel contribution in itself.

The significance of the work is considerable.  The ability to leverage weakly supervised data for tasks like object detection is highly valuable, and the reported performance gains demonstrate the potential of the e-SVM approach. The fact that it surpasses recent CNN-based methods with a comparatively conventional approach is a notable result that will likely attract attention from the computer vision community.

In terms of quality, the paper is generally technically sound.  The description of the algorithm is clear, and the experimental setup is well-defined. However, the lack of thorough engagement with prior work raises concerns about the completeness of the technical justification.  The object detection experiments involve substantial engineering and integration of existing methods, which is acceptable, but could benefit from more detailed explanation of implementation choices. The authors should also justify the use of a sum-constrained weighted SVM by comparing its performance to standard regularized logistic regression, which is a natural baseline for unit-interval regression.  More details on the choice of regularization coefficients (λW and λR) would also be beneficial.

The clarity of the paper is hindered by a somewhat misleading title and abstract. The claim that the “positiveness” value provides more information than a binary label is not convincingly justified, and the abstract doesn’t clearly articulate the core contribution.  The exposition could also be improved by focusing less on minor algorithmic variations and providing more details on the crucial feature engineering aspects of the experiments.  For example, the description of the spatial confidence map feature in object detection is rather terse. There are also some minor typographical issues (e.g., lines 19, 94, 98, 350) and inconsistencies in notation that should be addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art object detection performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness under both strong and weak supervision.
*   Versatility across different computer vision tasks.
*   Clear experimental setup and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Significant omission of relevant prior work on weighted SVMs and transductive SVMs.
*   Limited algorithmic novelty.
*   Lack of comparison to standard baselines (e.g., regularized logistic regression).
*   Issues with clarity of title, abstract, and exposition.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with major revisions.  The paper presents a practical and potentially valuable contribution to weakly supervised learning. However, the authors *must* address the significant omission of prior work, particularly regarding weighted and transductive SVMs.  A thorough rewriting of the introduction and related work sections is necessary to accurately position the e-SVM within the existing literature. The paper might be a better fit for a computer vision-focused venue. Conditional on addressing these concerns, I believe this work could be a valuable addition to the field.
