This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm called expectation loss SVM (e-SVM) that is designed to handle problems where only continuous "positiveness" values are available instead of binary labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm on segment proposal classification tasks under both strong and weak supervision, as well as on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection.

The main idea of e-SVM is to treat the label of each segment as an unobserved random variable and use the expectation over this random variable in the loss function. This approach allows the algorithm to handle continuous positiveness values in a principled way. The authors also extend their algorithm to the latent setting, where the positiveness values are unobserved, and demonstrate its effectiveness on object detection tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their ideas. The experiments are interesting and well-executed, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The technical content is an extension of multiple instance learning, and the method applies in both observed and latent settings.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to handling continuous positiveness values, its effectiveness on various computer vision tasks, and its clear and well-motivated presentation. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their algorithm and its relationship to existing work in the field.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithm. While they mention that their algorithm can be solved using standard SVM solvers, they do not provide any information on the actual running time of their algorithm on large datasets.

Another potential weakness is that the authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art algorithms on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. While they mention that their algorithm outperforms SVC and SVR on segment proposal classification tasks, they do not provide a detailed comparison with other algorithms on the same tasks.
