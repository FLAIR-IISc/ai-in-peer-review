### Summary

This paper proposes a novel modification to the Support Vector Machine framework, called the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning problems where training labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" values in the interval [0, 1]. The core idea is to treat the true binary label of each example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, with the success probability given by its known positiveness value. The SVM hinge loss is then replaced by its expectation over this latent label. This formulation effectively weights the positive and negative hinge loss components for each training example according to its positiveness.

The authors extend this model to a fully latent version (latent e-SVM) for scenarios where the positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available instead of pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness values are treated as latent variables and are optimized alternately with the SVM model parameters. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. Notably, by integrating features derived from the latent e-SVM, the authors show a significant performance improvement over the then state-of-the-art R-CNN object detection method on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem. Learning from weak supervision, such as bounding boxes or continuous scores (like Intersection-over-Union), is critical for scaling machine learning models to large datasets where detailed, pixel-perfect annotation is prohibitively expensive.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The proposed e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines on a newly proposed segment classification benchmark. More importantly, the method demonstrates a non-trivial improvement (1.5% mAP) on top of a very strong and state-of-the-art baseline (R-CNN) for object detection. This is a significant result that clearly shows the practical value of the proposed approach.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the proposed e-SVM formulation is described in an intuitive and easy-to-follow manner. The connection between the observed "positiveness" and the expected loss is elegant.
4.  **Novel Application and Features:** The idea of creating a spatial confidence map from segment-level predictions to augment window-based object detectors is a creative and effective way to bridge the gap between segmentation and detection paradigms.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Major Novelty Concerns:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its originality. The proposed e-SVM formulation appears to be mathematically equivalent to existing instance-weighted SVMs, also known as SVMs with example-dependent costs. The e-SVM loss for a single example `i` is `g(ui) * max(0, 1-wTxi) + (1-g(ui)) * max(0, 1+wTxi)`. This is equivalent to training a standard SVM on two copies of the data point `xi`: one with label `y=+1` and cost `C_i+ = g(ui)`, and another with label `y=-1` and cost `C_i- = (1-g(ui))`. Setting different costs for positive and negative examples, and making these costs instance-dependent, is a well-established technique (e.g., for handling class imbalance or instance importance) and is implemented in standard solvers like SVMlight and LibSVM. The paper does not adequately discuss or differentiate its contribution from this large body of prior work on weighted SVMs.
2.  **Latent Model Analysis:** While the latent e-SVM extension is interesting, the optimization procedure is a straightforward alternating minimization scheme for a non-convex objective function. The paper provides no theoretical analysis of this procedure, such as convergence properties or guarantees.
3.  **Role of Regularization in Latent Model:** The latent model relies on a strong KL-divergence regularization term to keep the learned latent variables `u` close to their initializations based on bounding box overlap (`ubb`). This raises the question of how much is truly being "learned" in the latent variable update step. The heavy regularization may prevent the model from deviating significantly from the initial estimate, potentially limiting the effectiveness of the latent learning process. It would be beneficial if the authors could provide an analysis of how much the `u` variables change during training.
4.  **Incorrect Motivation:** The paper claims that Support Vector Regression (SVR) is "not a standard regression problem" because the positiveness value is bounded in [0, 1]. This claim is incorrect. Many standard regression models handle bounded outputs, with logistic regression being a prime example for the [0, 1] interval. While SVR's standard loss is unbounded, framing the problem correctly is not a fundamental barrier. This weakens the motivation for the proposed method over regression-based approaches.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The empirical work is of high quality. The experiments are well-designed and the results convincingly support the claim that the method is effective in practice. However, the technical quality is diminished by the lack of discussion on the connection to weighted SVMs and the absence of theoretical analysis for the non-convex latent model optimization.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clear and well-written. The method is explained logically and the experimental setup is detailed. A minor typo was noted in the abstract: "continues" should be "continuous".

**Originality:** This is the most significant concern. As detailed in the weaknesses, the core e-SVM model appears to be a re-branding of a known technique. While the application to weakly supervised segment classification is valuable, the novelty of the underlying machine learning method is questionable. The paper needs to position itself much more carefully with respect to prior work on instance-dependent costs in SVMs.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is primarily empirical. It provides a practical and effective tool for leveraging weak, continuous-valued labels in a max-margin framework. The improvement shown on a top-tier object detection system is significant and likely to be of interest to practitioners and researchers in computer vision. However, the limited methodological novelty may reduce its impact on the core machine learning community.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   Presents strong, state-of-the-art empirical results on a challenging object detection benchmark.
*   The approach is intuitive, well-explained, and shown to be effective.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Significant concerns regarding the methodological novelty. The core e-SVM formulation is likely equivalent to previously proposed weighted SVMs.
*   The latent model is a straightforward extension with no accompanying theoretical analysis of its non-convex optimization.
*   The paper fails to properly situate its contribution within the context of existing literature on instance-weighted classification.

In conclusion, while the paper presents strong and significant empirical results on an important problem, the major concerns about its methodological originality make it a borderline case. The authors must address the relationship between e-SVM and instance-weighted SVMs for this work to be considered a clear contribution to the NIPS community.
