

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data in computer vision tasks. The authors address a significant problem: the high cost of pixel-level annotations in segmentation and detection tasks. The core idea is to treat segment proposals' "positiveness" (e.g., IoU with ground truth) as a continuous label rather than binarizing it. The e-SVM model uses the expectation of the SVM loss over a Bernoulli-distributed label, effectively weighting examples by their positiveness. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision where positiveness is unobserved, using a KL divergence regularization.

The work builds on standard SVM formulations and relates to weighted SVMs (WSVMs) and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), but the authors correctly distinguish their approach as distinct from MIL (which assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag) and WSVMs (which weight examples, not labels).

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses a genuinely important problem in computer vision - the expense of pixel-level annotations. The proposed solution has clear practical value, especially given the prevalence of bounding box annotations in large datasets like ImageNet.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The e-SVM formulation is logically derived from standard SVM principles. The expectation loss formulation elegantly avoids the thresholding problem inherent in SVC, and the latent extension for weak supervision is a natural and well-motivated extension.

3. **Implementation Simplicity**: A major strength is that e-SVM can be implemented using standard SVM solvers (like LibLinear), making it highly practical for adoption. This is a significant advantage over methods requiring specialized solvers.

4. **Clear Experimental Design**: The paper evaluates the approach on two major computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR), providing a good test of the method's utility.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Weak Supervision Methods**: The paper only compares to SVC and SVR, but doesn't compare to more recent weakly supervised learning methods. For instance, the paper could have compared to recent work on weakly supervised semantic segmentation like [19] or [20] that use different weak supervision strategies. This weakens the significance claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

2. **Modest Performance Gains**: The reported improvements (1.5% AP gain on PASCAL VOC) are modest, especially given the current state of the art. While this is non-trivial, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these gains, making it difficult to assess their true importance.

3. **Inadequate Justification of Key Design Choices**: The regularization term (Equation 3) is justified as a KL divergence between Bernoulli distributions, but the paper doesn't explain why this specific regularization is better than alternatives (e.g., L2 regularization on u). The authors claim it's "strong" but don't demonstrate how this strength translates to better performance.

4. **Thresholding Issue in SVC Comparison**: The comparison with SVC uses different thresholds (SVC-0.0, SVC-0.2, etc.), which is problematic. The authors claim e-SVM avoids the thresholding problem, but they don't show how their method compares to the *best possible* SVC with an optimized threshold. This undermines their main motivation.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show how much each component (e.g., the expectation loss vs. the latent extension) contributes to the final performance.

## Technical and Methodological Concerns

The paper claims that "the positiveness of each segment proposal is set as the Intersection over Union (IoU) overlap ratio," but doesn't clarify whether this is the IoU with the ground truth object or with the bounding box. This distinction is crucial for understanding the weak supervision setting.

The latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1) states that "when w is fixed, L(w,u) is also a convex function if R(u) is a convex function with respect to u." However, the specific regularization term (Equation 3) is convex, but the paper doesn't explain how the optimization proceeds when R(u) is added. The convergence behavior of this alternating optimization is also not discussed.

## Significance Assessment

The paper makes a modest contribution to a relevant problem, but the significance is limited by the incremental nature of the method and the modest performance gains. The authors claim their method "can be easily extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision," but they only demonstrate it on two specific tasks. The paper would have been stronger with evidence that this approach generalizes to other weakly supervised learning problems.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practical approach to weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The e-SVM formulation addresses a genuine problem (the thresholding issue in SVC), and the method is straightforward to implement. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, the performance gains are modest, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from other weakly supervised learning methods.

The paper is technically sound but makes only an incremental contribution to the field. The significance would be stronger with more thorough comparisons to state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods, statistical significance testing, and a more convincing demonstration of the advantages over baselines.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper addresses an important problem with a reasonable method, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully support the significance claims. With additional experiments comparing to recent weakly supervised learning methods and statistical significance testing, this could be a strong paper. As it stands, it's a solid but incremental contribution that would be appropriate for NIPS with some revisions.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. Compare to recent weakly supervised learning methods (e.g., [19], [20]) beyond SVC and SVR
2. Provide statistical significance testing for the reported performance gains
3. Justify the regularization term more thoroughly and compare with alternative regularizers
4. Clarify the relationship between the ground truth used for positiveness (pixel-level vs. bounding box)
5. Include ablation studies to show the contribution of each component of the method

The paper has merit and is well-motivated, but needs stronger experimental validation to support its significance claims.
