

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed to handle learning from data with continuous labels (e.g., IoU overlap ratios) rather than binary labels. The authors position their approach as a solution to weakly supervised learning scenarios where pixel-level annotations are unavailable, using bounding box annotations instead. The core idea is to treat the label of each segment as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, with the success probability directly related to the observed "positiveness" (e.g., IoU). The authors present a basic e-SVM model for when positiveness is observed and extend it to a latent version for weak supervision scenarios where positiveness is unobserved.

The paper makes a clear distinction between their approach and existing methods like SVC (which requires thresholding continuous labels) and SVR (which is designed for regression but not well-suited for bounded continuous values). The authors validate their method on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using PASCAL VOC datasets, reporting modest improvements over SVC and SVR.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a novel formulation of the loss function that directly incorporates continuous labels without requiring ad-hoc thresholding.
2. The approach is theoretically well-motivated and cleanly extends to weak supervision via latent variables.
3. The experimental setup is appropriate for the problem, with careful comparisons against SVC and SVR.
4. The method is general enough to be applied to various computer vision tasks without requiring domain-specific modifications.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The core novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking. While the formulation is novel, it doesn't fundamentally change the approach to weakly supervised learning. The problem setting is actually closer to semi-supervised learning with continuous labels than to true weak supervision (where labels are only available at the image level).
2. The performance gains are modest (1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007), which is not sufficient to be considered a significant advancement at NeurIPS. Previous works have achieved similar improvements with more complex methods.
3. The paper doesn't adequately address the distinction between weak supervision (where labels are only available at the image level) and their continuous label approach, which is a critical limitation for the weak supervision claim.
4. There's insufficient theoretical justification for why the expectation loss formulation should outperform existing approaches, beyond empirical results.
5. The experiments don't explore the limits of the method or when it might fail, which is crucial for a paper claiming to offer a new direction.

**Novelty Assessment:**
While the expectation loss formulation is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work on continuous label learning. The problem they address (handling continuous labels in SVMs) has been explored in other contexts, and their solution is a straightforward extension of existing ideas. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent work on regression with SVMs, which the authors only briefly mention.

**Significance Assessment:**
The problem of weakly supervised learning is important, but the paper's contribution doesn't significantly advance the state of the art. The 1.5% AP improvement on object detection is non-trivial but not transformative, especially when considering the simplicity of the approach (just concatenating features with an existing detector). For a conference like NeurIPS, we typically expect more substantial improvements or fundamentally new approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound method that addresses a real problem in computer vision. The e-SVM formulation is well-motivated and the experiments are appropriately designed. However, the novelty is incremental rather than bold, and the performance gains are modest. While the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, it doesn't offer a significant new direction or fundamentally change how we approach weakly supervised learning. The paper would be better suited for a more application-focused venue rather than NeurIPS, where we prioritize novel theoretical insights and substantial methodological advances.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper is well-written and makes a reasonable contribution, it doesn't meet the high bar for novelty and significance expected at NeurIPS. The approach is a reasonable extension of existing ideas but doesn't provide a fundamentally new direction or sufficiently compelling evidence of a significant improvement. The authors should consider expanding the theoretical justification, exploring more challenging weak supervision settings, or demonstrating larger performance gains to strengthen their case for publication at NeurIPS.
