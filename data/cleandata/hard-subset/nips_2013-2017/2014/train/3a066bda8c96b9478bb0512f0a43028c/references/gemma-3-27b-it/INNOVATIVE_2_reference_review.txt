This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach to learning from weakly supervised data, specifically focusing on scenarios where only partial correctness (“positiveness”) of label proposals is available, rather than definitive binary labels. The core idea – weighting samples by their estimated correctness – is conceptually elegant and addresses a common limitation of applying standard SVMs to tasks like segmentation and object detection with weak supervision. The authors convincingly argue the limitations of direct application of SVC and SVR to this problem and propose a principled alternative. The extension to a latent variable model to handle truly weak supervision (bounding box annotations only) is a valuable addition.

The paper’s strength lies in its novelty. While weighted SVMs exist (acknowledged by the authors), the specific weighting scheme based on *expected* label correctness, and its seamless integration into the SVM framework, is a clear contribution. The application to both semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods (SVC, SVR, and a baseline RCNN model) further strengthens the argument for the e-SVM’s effectiveness. The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics specifically tailored for segment proposal confidence prediction is also commendable. 

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The experimental validation, while positive, feels somewhat…contained. The improvements, particularly the 1.5% AP gain in object detection, are modest considering the novelty of the approach. The justification for the chosen regularization term in the latent e-SVM (using KL divergence) feels a bit hand-wavy; a more thorough exploration of different regularization strategies might have been beneficial. Further, the comparison to other weakly-supervised learning techniques like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) feels cursory, given the authors acknowledge this work operates in an intermediate space. The paper doesn't deeply engage with *why* e-SVM performs better; is it the weighting scheme, the ability to handle continuous positiveness values, or something else?  More ablative studies could have addressed this. The presentation, while generally clear, could benefit from more visually compelling illustrations of the e-SVM framework (beyond Figure 1) and the confidence map feature in object detection.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM provides a novel and principled approach to weakly supervised learning, addressing a critical challenge in computer vision.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the expectation loss and its integration with SVMs are well-justified.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on multiple tasks and datasets, consistently outperforming baselines.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to effectively utilize weakly labeled data has significant implications for scaling up computer vision applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Modest Gains:** The performance improvements are incremental, particularly in object detection.
*   **Limited Ablation:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the factors contributing to e-SVM’s performance.
*   **Cursory Comparison:** The comparison with related work, particularly MIL, is insufficient.
*   **KL Divergence justification**: The choice of regularization term lacks strong justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor methodological shortcomings and incremental gains, the e-SVM presents a genuinely novel and promising approach to weakly supervised learning. The authors have identified a crucial problem, proposed an elegant solution, and provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness. I prioritize novelty in my reviews, and the e-SVM delivers on that front. The paper isn’t perfect, but the potential impact of this work outweighs its weaknesses. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above in the rebuttal phase, specifically offering further insight into the key factors driving the performance gains and engaging more deeply with the broader context of weakly-supervised learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
