

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data, where training labels are continuous "positiveness" scores (e.g., IoU) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their method outperforms standard SVC and SVR on segment proposal confidence prediction, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

## Quality Issues

The paper contains numerous technical and formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The mathematical formulation in Section 3.1 (Equation 1) is poorly typeset: "λw · 1 wTw" should be "λ||w||^2" and "N N∑ i=1" should be "1/N ∑_{i=1}^N". This formatting inconsistency appears throughout the paper, making the technical content difficult to follow. 

The paper states "u will become 0 or 1 in the optimization step" when λR=0, but this is not necessarily true for the linear function in u. The claim that "u will become 0 or 1" is overly strong and technically inaccurate for a linear optimization problem with bounded variables.

The terminology "positiveness" is non-standard and confusing. The authors use this term to describe the continuous label (IoU), but "positiveness" is not a standard machine learning term. This should be replaced with "overlap score" or "confidence score" to align with standard literature.

The paper claims "the average AP is 58.5" for RCNN on PASCAL VOC 2007, but the standard metric is mAP (mean Average Precision), not "average AP." This terminology confusion is problematic for a computer vision paper.

## Clarity and Consistency Issues

The paper uses inconsistent capitalization: "CPMC" vs. "Cpmc" in the text. The references also show inconsistent formatting (e.g., "TPAMI" vs. "IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence").

The paper states "IoU overlap ratio" repeatedly, but IoU is already a ratio, making this redundant. The authors should simply use "IoU" consistently.

The regularization term in Equation 3 is defined as R(u) = -1/N Σ[ubbi · log(ui) + (1-ubbi) · log(1-ui)], but the paper doesn't justify why this specific regularization is superior to alternatives. The claim that "this regularization term is a strong regularization term" is unsubstantiated.

## Methodological Concerns

The paper claims e-SVM is "different from those of WSVMs" but the connection to weighted SVMs is tenuous. The authors state "it weights labels instead of data points," but their formulation is functionally equivalent to a weighted SVM where the weight is g(ui). The distinction is not clearly justified.

The paper states "it can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver," but Algorithm 1 describes an iterative procedure that requires alternating optimization between w and u, which is not standard for SVM solvers. This claim is misleading.

The paper claims to "extend to the weakly supervised case by treating the positiveness as latent variables," but bounding box annotations don't provide direct information about the true IoU, making the latent variable approach questionable. The paper doesn't adequately address how the initialization ubb (IoU between segment and bounding box) relates to the true IoU.

## Experimental Issues

The paper claims "the accuracy w.r.t. these two settings are 36.8% and 27.7% respectively" for semantic segmentation, but accuracy is not the standard metric for segmentation. The paper should report mIoU (mean Intersection over Union), which is the standard evaluation metric for segmentation.

The paper states "our method can improve the average AP by 1.5 on average" for object detection, but doesn't provide the actual numbers or statistical significance. The paper also doesn't clarify whether these improvements are statistically significant.

The paper cites "SVC-0.0" as a baseline, but the notation is confusing - is this a threshold of 0.0 or a specific method? The paper should clarify.

## References

The paper cites "Weighted SVM (WSVM) methods [23, 27, ?]" with a question mark, indicating an incomplete reference. This is a serious formatting error that undermines the paper's credibility.

## Summary

While the paper addresses an interesting problem in weakly supervised learning, the numerous technical, formatting, and terminological inconsistencies significantly undermine its quality. The mathematical formulations are poorly presented, the terminology is non-standard, and the experimental results lack sufficient detail and clarity. The claimed novelty over existing weighted SVM approaches is not sufficiently justified.

The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues, particularly the mathematical formulation and terminology. As currently written, the paper fails to meet the technical standards expected at NIPS due to its numerous minor but significant flaws.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper's technical shortcomings and formatting inconsistencies outweigh its potential contribution to the field. The authors need to carefully revise the mathematical formulations, standardize their terminology, and provide clearer experimental results before resubmission.
