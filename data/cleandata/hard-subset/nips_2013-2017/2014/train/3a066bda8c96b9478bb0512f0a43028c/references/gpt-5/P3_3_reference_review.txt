Summary
The paper introduces expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “soft” labels (positiveness in [0,1], instantiated as IoU of segment proposals with ground-truth). The core idea is to minimize the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label Yi with success probability µi=g(ui), with g(ui)=ui in experiments. This yields a convex objective in w when ui is known and a block-wise convex objective when ui is treated as latent under weak supervision. For weak supervision, the authors regularize ui toward the IoU with the ground-truth bounding box via a KL divergence between Bernoullis, and alternate between optimizing w and u. They apply the method to segment ranking/selection for semantic segmentation and, more substantially, to object detection by building a per-class spatial “confidence map” feature from segment scores and concatenating it to RCNN features, reporting ~1.2–1.5 mAP gains on VOC07. They also propose two segment-ranking evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) and show e-SVM beats SVC and slightly outperforms SVR.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: expected hinge with soft labels is natural, convex in w, and easily integrates different proposal generators/features. The latent extension is a straightforward alternating scheme with a convex-in-u regularizer.
- Practical utility: the spatial confidence map is a reasonable way to inject segmentation cues into window-based detectors and appears to yield consistent AP gains on a strong baseline (RCNN).
- Empirical signal: on segment-ranking metrics (TAPC, NDCG), e-SVM improves over SVC baselines and slightly over SVR; on VOC07 detection, reported +1.5 mAP after bbox regression is non-trivial at the time.
- The paper addresses a relevant setting between full pixel supervision and image-level weak supervision (bounding boxes), and the approach should transfer beyond vision when only “soft labels” are available.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty/positioning: The core objective (expected hinge under soft/probabilistic labels) is not new. It is closely related to soft-label SVMs, fuzzy SVMs, SVMs with uncertain/probabilistic labels, and partial/ambiguous label learning. The paper cites weighted SVMs but omits several directly relevant lines of work (e.g., fuzzy SVM; SVM with probabilistic labels; ambiguous label SVMs; soft-label risk minimization). The contribution appears incremental in formulation and relies on application framing and engineering (spatial confidence map).
- Solver claim and implementation details: The loss L(w)=∑[g(ui)·max(0,1−w⋅xi)+(1−g(ui))·max(0,1+w⋅xi)] is not the standard SVM objective. Saying it “can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver (e.g., LibLinear)” is misleading unless one explains the reduction (e.g., duplicate each xi as a positive and negative example with sample weights g(ui) and 1−g(ui) respectively, or use a solver supporting per-example weights). This reduction should be made explicit and evaluated for numerical stability and efficiency.
- Latent optimization: Important details are missing:
  - Exact update for ui: with g(ui)=ui and KL regularizer, each ui has a 1D convex objective. Is there a closed form? If using numeric updates, what algorithm, step sizes, and stopping conditions?
  - Sensitivity to λR and to the choice of g(·). Why g(ui)=ui versus a calibrated mapping (e.g., sigmoid or isotonic)?
  - Convergence behavior: only a high-level comment about avoiding 0/1 degeneracy. Any guarantees of convergence to a stationary point? Empirical convergence plots?
  - The informativeness of the prior ubb (IoU with box) is questionable for thin/irregular objects; an analysis of correlation between ubb and true IoU would be helpful.
- Experimental rigor and fairness:
  - Semantic segmentation: numbers are only “comparable” to SVR, not clearly better; no ablations (e.g., with/without latent, effect of λR, g(·), proposal type), and limited comparison to contemporary methods that learn from boxes.
  - Detection: The gain could come from the new feature more than from e-SVM per se. A critical baseline is missing: use the same spatial confidence map derived from SVR-trained segment scores. Without it, the relative contribution of e-SVM vs. feature engineering is unclear.
  - RCNN integration details are underspecified (exact training split, non-max suppression protocol, whether your “modified” bbox regression is also applied to the RCNN-only baseline, hyperparameters). The “augmentation” of conv5 with additive kernels requires precise specification for reproducibility.
  - Computational cost: Computing per-pixel max over many segments and per-window m×m pooling is expensive; no runtime or memory analysis is provided.
- Evaluation measures: TAPC is sensible but under-specified (range/spacing of thresholds, averaging protocol). NDCG requires a gain definition; details are missing. Curves/tables are only partially presented in text.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos, incomplete/inconsistent references (e.g., question marks, missing years/authors), and figures are not shown. The writing needs substantial polishing; some claims are vague or oversold.

Relation to prior work
Beyond weighted SVMs, the most relevant threads include: SVMs with probabilistic labels/soft labels, fuzzy SVMs (membership-based), ambiguous/partial label learning, robust SVMs under label noise, and MIL variants that allow soft instance scores or regression targets. Several works (e.g., Cour et al., NIPS 2011 on learning from ambiguously labeled images; fuzzy SVM literature; label-noise robust hinge losses) should be discussed to clarify what is new (label-wise weighting of hinge vs instance-wise cost; use of KL prior from boxes; specific application pipeline).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, broadly applicable formulation; easy to implement via a reduction.
- Empirical improvements on a widely used benchmark and strong baseline.
- Addresses a timely weak-supervision setting; the spatial confidence map idea is practical.

Cons:
- Limited methodological novelty; incomplete positioning vs. soft-label/fuzzy SVM literature.
- Missing key baselines and ablations; reproducibility details lacking.
- Clarity and referencing issues; some claims about solvers are imprecise.

Recommendations and questions for authors
- Clarify the reduction to standard SVM solvers (data duplication with per-example weights) and provide implementation details.
- Provide the exact u-update (closed form or numerical), convergence criteria, and sensitivity analyses for λR and g(·).
- Add the critical baseline: spatial confidence map built from SVR (and SVC) segment scores; report ablations on m, pooling, proposal type.
- Report runtime/memory and convergence behavior.
- Expand and correct related work to include soft/probabilistic label SVMs, fuzzy SVMs, ambiguous/partial label learning, and weakly supervised segmentation from boxes.
- Improve clarity, fix references, and include full quantitative tables/plots.

Score: Weak reject. The idea is useful and the empirical gains encouraging, but the novelty is modest and the paper lacks rigor and clarity needed for NIPS. With stronger positioning, thorough baselines/ablations, and clearer optimization details, this could be a solid contribution.
