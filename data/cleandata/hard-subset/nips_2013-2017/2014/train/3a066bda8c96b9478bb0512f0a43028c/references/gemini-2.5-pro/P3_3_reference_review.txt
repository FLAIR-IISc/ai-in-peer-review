**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the Expectation-Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" values within [0, 1]. The core idea is to model the binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is the given positiveness score (e.g., IoU). The loss function then becomes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegant formulation effectively weights a single data point's contribution to both the positive and negative class loss, proportional to its positiveness `u` and `1-u`, respectively. The authors extend this to a fully latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness is unknown (e.g., only bounding box supervision is available), treating `u` as a latent variable to be optimized alternately with the model weights. The method is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection, where it is shown to outperform standard SVC and SVR baselines on a segment classification sub-task and, most notably, improves the performance of the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector on PASCAL VOC 2007 by a significant margin.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of the e-SVM is simple, intuitive, and theoretically sound. Framing the continuous label problem through an expected hinge loss over a latent binary variable is a clever way to bridge the gap between classification and regression within the max-margin framework. It provides a more principled approach than arbitrarily thresholding the continuous labels for SVC or using SVR, which is not designed for a bounded [0, 1] target range.

2.  **Practical Relevance and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem: learning from weaker forms of supervision like bounding boxes, which are far cheaper to acquire than pixel-level masks. The most significant result is the 1.5% absolute mAP improvement over the R-CNN detector on PASCAL VOC 2007. Given that R-CNN was the state-of-the-art at the time, this is a non-trivial and convincing demonstration of the method's utility. This result alone makes the paper a strong candidate for acceptance.

3.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The authors introduce two new evaluation metrics for the segment proposal classification task: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). This is a commendable contribution in itself, as it allows for a more direct and nuanced evaluation of the core learning algorithm, independent of its downstream application. The experiments using these metrics clearly demonstrate the superiority of e-SVM over sensible baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR).

4.  **Flexibility and Generality:** The framework is general. The latent e-SVM can be initialized with any rough estimate of "positiveness" (here, IoU with a bounding box), and the resulting classifier can be integrated into various systems. The proposed "spatial confidence map feature" is a simple yet effective way to incorporate segment-level predictions into a region-based object detector.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Novelty in Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The paper acknowledges the connection to weighted SVMs (WSVM) but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the distinction. The final loss function in Equation 1 can be rewritten as a sum over samples of `g(ui) * l+i + (1-g(ui)) * l-i`. This is functionally equivalent to duplicating each sample `(xi, ui)`, creating a positive instance `(xi, +1)` with weight `g(ui)` and a negative instance `(xi, -1)` with weight `1-g(ui)`. While the *conceptual* framing as an expectation loss is novel, the resulting optimization problem is very close to existing instance-weighting or cost-sensitive SVM schemes. A deeper comparison to prior work in this area (beyond the cited WSVM papers) would strengthen the paper's claims of originality.

2.  **Latent Variable Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is a standard approach for non-convex problems but lacks theoretical guarantees of convergence to a global optimum. The quality of the solution likely depends heavily on the initialization (`u(cur) <- ubb`). A discussion of this sensitivity and the robustness of the algorithm would be valuable. Furthermore, the KL-divergence regularizer is well-motivated, but its strength is controlled by a hyperparameter `λR`, which is not discussed. How was this parameter set, and how sensitive are the results to its value?

3.  **Clarity on Experimental Details:**
    *   The semantic segmentation results are described as "comparable" to the SVR-based state-of-the-art. This is vague. Providing the exact numbers for both methods in a table would allow readers to make their own judgment. "Comparable" can often hide a small but consistent decrease in performance.
    *   In the object detection experiment, it is unclear how much of the 1.5% mAP gain is attributable to the core e-SVM learning versus the specific design of the "spatial confidence map feature". An ablation study that replaces the e-SVM scores with, for example, SVR-based scores in the confidence map feature would isolate the contribution of the proposed learning algorithm.

4.  **Limited Scope of `g(u)`:** The paper introduces a general function `g(ui)` to map positiveness `ui` to the Bernoulli probability, but immediately simplifies it to `g(ui) = ui`. This simplification is pragmatic but leaves unexplored questions. What is the impact of this choice? Would a non-linear mapping (e.g., a sigmoid-like function) that more strongly pushes values near 0 and 1 to the extremes be more effective? Some justification for this linear choice would be helpful.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated, simple, and effective method for an important problem in computer vision and machine learning. The strengths, particularly the impressive empirical improvement on a state-of-the-art object detector and the principled formulation, clearly outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily centered on the need for deeper discussion of related work and more detailed experimental analysis, which could be addressed in a final version. The work is technically sound, original in its formulation, clearly presented, and demonstrates significant practical impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant "expectation loss" formulation for handling continuous [0, 1] labels.
*   Addresses the highly practical problem of learning from weak supervision (bounding boxes).
*   Demonstrates a significant, state-of-the-art performance improvement on a challenging object detection benchmark.
*   Introduces useful and direct evaluation metrics for the segment classification sub-problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty compared to various forms of weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs could be debated and requires a more thorough discussion.
*   The optimization for the latent model is heuristic, and its sensitivities are not discussed.
*   Some experimental details and ablations are missing, making it slightly difficult to pinpoint the exact source of performance gains.

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field.
