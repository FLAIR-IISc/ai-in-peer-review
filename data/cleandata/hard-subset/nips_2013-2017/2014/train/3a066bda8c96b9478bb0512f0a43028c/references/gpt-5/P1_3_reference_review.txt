Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “soft” labels in [0,1], where the “positiveness” u (e.g., IoU with ground-truth) substitutes for a hard {−1,+1} label. The core idea is to minimize the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with success probability µ = g(u) (instantiated as g(u)=u). This yields a per-example loss of µ·hinge(+1) + (1−µ)·hinge(−1). The authors note it can be solved by standard linear SVM solvers. For weakly supervised settings where u is unobserved (only boxes are given), they introduce a latent e-SVM: alternate minimizing over the classifier w and latent u, regularizing u via a convex cross-entropy KL term around an initialization ubb (IoU between segment and bounding box). They apply e-SVM to (i) segment proposal scoring for semantic segmentation, and (ii) to object detection by constructing per-class spatial confidence maps from segment scores and concatenating them with R-CNN features. They report modest gains over SVC/SVR in segment scoring and +1.2–1.5 mAP over R-CNN on VOC07.

This sits within a large body of work on weak supervision (MIL, MIM, segmentation from image-level labels) and on weighted/robust SVMs. The formulation is close to SVMs with probabilistic/soft labels and to duplicating each example with opposite labels and instance-dependent weights; the latent extension resembles latent SVMs with a convex regularizer on latent “soft labels.”

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective: expected hinge loss with soft labels in [0,1]; easily implemented via standard solvers by reweighting.
- The latent variant is clean and convex in each block (w and u); the KL regularizer prevents hard assignment degeneracy and encourages stability.
- Empirical evidence that the soft-label formulation performs at least as well as, and sometimes better than, SVC/SVR baselines for segment scoring under both strong and weak supervision (TAPC and NDCG).
- Practical contribution: a straightforward way to exploit segment confidences in object detection by spatial pooling, yielding reproducible, non-trivial mAP gains when combined with R-CNN.
- The paper emphasizes generality: the approach is agnostic to the proposal generator, features, and base detector.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Theory: The expected-hinge formulation is essentially a soft-label/“probabilistic label” SVM. The paper does not relate to or differentiate from prior formulations (e.g., SVMs with uncertain or probabilistic labels, label smoothing for SVMs, partial/ambiguous labels SVMs). A simple equivalence proof to a standard WSVM on a duplicated dataset (one +1 example with weight µ and one −1 with weight 1−µ) would clarify why standard solvers suffice and what is truly new.
- Latent optimization: The u-update is presented at a high level. Given f(u)=u l+ + (1−u) l− + λR[−ubb log u − (1−ubb) log(1−u)], the authors could derive the per-sample optimality condition and show existence/uniqueness and provide a closed-form or efficient 1D solver. Convergence properties (biconvex alternating minimization) and sensitivity to initialization/λR are not analyzed.
- Empirics: Results are promising but under-specified. Key details (λw, λR, number of alternations, g(u) choices beyond identity, runtime, convergence criteria, ablations on m×m bins or pooling schemes) are missing. For segment scoring, earlier text claims “slightly better than SVR,” later “comparable”—please reconcile and provide per-class or per-threshold statistics. For detection, the referenced table is not included; stronger analysis (per-class gains, with/without KL regularizer, using superpixels vs CPMC) would help.
- Baselines: For segment scoring framed as ranking, a RankSVM/learning-to-rank baseline is natural. For classification with soft labels, logistic regression with cross-entropy weighted by u (or with label smoothing) is also relevant. For weak supervision, comparisons to MIL variants that can handle partial overlap would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- The presentation is understandable but contains many typos, reference issues, and occasional inconsistencies (broken citations, figure/table omissions, formatting). Algorithm 1 lacks details for line 4 (how u is optimized in practice).
- The claim “weights labels instead of data points” is somewhat misleading; an explicit equivalence to instance-weighted SVM on duplicated samples would make the contribution clearer.
- The definition and motivation of TAPC are fine, but experimental protocol (threshold grid, averaging) should be precisely specified.

Originality
- The expected hinge loss with µ∈[0,1] is a known idea in soft/probabilistic label SVMs; the paper does not cite or contrast with that literature. The specific latent extension with a KL prior around ubb is a modest twist on latent SVM-style alternating optimization. The spatial confidence map feature is a practical engineering contribution, but conceptually close to standard spatial pooling of per-pixel/region scores. Overall, novelty is incremental.

Significance
- Practically, the method is simple and appealing, and the reported +1–1.5 mAP on VOC07 when plugged into R-CNN indicates potential utility. However, the gains are modest, and the study is limited to one dataset and one detector. The general formulation could be widely applicable if better positioned and evaluated against stronger baselines.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a derivation for the u-update and describe the solver you use (per-instance closed-form? Newton? Bounds handling?). How many alternations do you run and how sensitive are results to λR?
2. Can you formalize the equivalence to a standard WSVM on a duplicated dataset and clarify what is new beyond that equivalence?
3. Did you try g(u) other than identity (e.g., calibrated probabilities, monotone transforms)? Any calibration of IoU to probability?
4. How does e-SVM compare to RankSVM for the TAPC/NDCG metrics? To logistic regression with soft labels?
5. Please include full detection tables, per-class gains, and ablations on m and pooling.

Minor comments
- Numerous citation and formatting issues (e.g., missing years, authors, ? in refs). Please proofread.
- Figure/table references (Fig. 1–3, Table 1) are missing from the submission text.
- The statement about “not requiring a specific solver” would benefit from a brief recipe (how to construct the weighted dataset for LibLinear).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general formulation; easy to implement.
- Addresses an important problem: learning from weak/soft supervision for segments.
- Consistent improvements over SVC/SVR for segment scoring and modest but tangible detection gains.
- Latent extension is practical and convex in blocks.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing soft/probabilistic-label SVMs and latent SVM variants; related work coverage is incomplete.
- Incomplete experimental details, missing tables/figures, and limited ablations/baselines.
- Theoretical development is light; key algorithmic steps (u-update) are not specified.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is useful and the engineering is promising, but the paper needs stronger positioning w.r.t. prior soft-label SVM work, more thorough experiments and ablations, and clearer algorithmic details to meet NeurIPS standards. With these addressed, it could be a solid contribution.
