- Introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for weak supervision with continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU scores).  
- Models labels as Bernoulli random variables with success probability proportional to positiveness, extending to latent positiveness for weak supervision.  
- Relates to MIL and weighted SVMs but claims a distinct setting (weights on labels vs. instances) and different assumptions than standard MIL.  
- Technical formulation is sound: clear derivation of the expectation loss and a convex optimization procedure for the latent case.  
- Optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward and correctly handles the convex problem.  
- Experimental setup is appropriate, comparing to SVC and SVR and using novel metrics TAPC and NDCG.  
- Results: e-SVM consistently outperforms baselines on segment proposal confidence prediction.  
- Semantic segmentation results are comparable to state-of-the-art (36.8% with pixel-level annotations).  
- Object detection (with RCNN) shows a modest average AP improvement of 1.5%.  
- Paper does not thoroughly explore why e-SVM outperforms SVR despite both being margin-based methods.  
- Comparisons to more recent object detection methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN) are missing, weakening significance claims.  
- Clarity issues: insufficiently precise distinction from weighted SVMs and lack of detail on integrating spatial confidence with RCNN.  
- Implementation detail missing: how regularization strength λR is chosen in practice.  
- Originality: the expectation loss formulation is novel and well-motivated, though probabilistic label ideas are not entirely new.  
- Paper insufficiently distinguishes itself from related work (weighted SVMs, learning with noisy labels) and applications are standard.  
- Significance: addresses a relevant weakly supervised learning problem; modest gains can be meaningful but need stronger comparative evidence.  
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, solid mathematical derivation, well-designed experiments, novel metrics, and relevant applications.  
- Weaknesses: modest performance gains, limited comparisons to recent methods, insufficient relation to related work, shallow discussion of limitations/failure cases, and lack of analysis on SVR advantage.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, asking for stronger comparisons to recent detectors, clearer distinction from weighted SVMs, fuller discussion of limitations, and insight into why e-SVM beats SVR.
