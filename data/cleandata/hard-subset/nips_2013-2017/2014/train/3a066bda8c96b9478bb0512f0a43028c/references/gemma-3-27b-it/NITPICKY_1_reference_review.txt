This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically focusing on scenarios where only partial labels (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level segmentations) are available. The core idea is to treat the “positiveness” of segment proposals as a continuous value rather than forcing a binary classification, and to formulate a loss function that explicitly models this expectation. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving competitive results compared to existing methods like SVC and SVR.

While the proposed method presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem, and demonstrates promising experimental results, numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the submission. The presentation suffers from a lack of rigor in several areas, and the attention to detail is surprisingly lacking given the meticulous nature of the proposed algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Weakly supervised learning is crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets where detailed annotations are expensive.
*   **Novel formulation:** The expectation loss formulation and its extension to the latent variable setting are conceptually sound and offer a potential improvement over standard SVM approaches.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on the chosen tasks, suggesting its practical effectiveness.
*   **Generality:** The authors highlight the method's flexibility in terms of segment proposal detectors, features, and base classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of theoretical justification:** The derivation of the loss function, while intuitive, lacks a strong theoretical justification. Why is the Bernoulli assumption appropriate? What are the properties of the resulting optimization problem?
*   **Ambiguous notation:** The notation is inconsistent. For example, *ui* is used to represent both the IoU ratio *and* the positiveness, creating confusion. Similarly, *l+i* and *l-i* are defined but their role isn't explicitly explained in the main text beyond their inclusion in Equation 1.
*   **Implementation details lacking:** The regularization term R(u) in the latent e-SVM is described, but the choice of the constant C in Equation 3 isn't discussed, nor is any sensitivity analysis provided. The implementation choices regarding the spatial confidence map (e.g., bin size *m*) should be justified.
*   **Insufficient baselines:** While SVC and SVR are used as baselines, they are not optimally tuned, and more recent or state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods are not considered. The claim of outperforming the “state-of-the-art” RCNN is questionable without a more comprehensive comparison.
*   **Redundant/vague statements:** The introduction contains several redundant phrases (e.g., repeatedly stating the need for weakly supervised learning). Phrases like “this is an advantage” are unnecessary and weaken the argument. The paper overstates the generality of the approach; for example, stating that it "can handle other classification problems where only the 'positiveness' of the samples instead of binary labels are available" adds little value.
*   **Minor formatting issues:** Several minor formatting issues exist, including inconsistencies in citation formatting (e.g., [6] vs. [16]) and occasional grammatical errors. The table in Section 5.1 has unclear formatting.
* **Unclear comparison metrics:** The meaning of "TAPC NDCG" in table 1 isn't explained. It isn't clear if it refers to two metrics or one.
* **Figure 1 isn’t referenced:** The description in the introduction mentions Figure 1, but the content of the figure isn't discussed in the subsequent sections.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper lacks theoretical depth and contains numerous minor flaws in presentation and implementation detail.
*   The empirical evaluation, while positive, is not sufficiently comprehensive to strongly support the claims of superiority.
*   The writing quality is subpar, with redundant statements and a lack of clarity in certain sections.

**For:**

*   The proposed method addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   The core idea of the expectation loss formulation is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a promising improvement over existing methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novel idea and promising experimental results, the numerous minor flaws and lack of rigorous presentation significantly detract from the overall quality of this submission. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the level of polish expected for NIPS. While the work has potential, it requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the points raised in this review. Specifically, they should provide a more rigorous theoretical justification, clarify the notation, provide more detailed implementation details, conduct a more comprehensive evaluation with stronger baselines, and improve the clarity and conciseness of the writing.
