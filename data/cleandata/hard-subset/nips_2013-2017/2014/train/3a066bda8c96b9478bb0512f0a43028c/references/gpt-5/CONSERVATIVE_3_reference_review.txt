Summary
The paper proposes “expectation-loss SVM” (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where each proposal has a continuous “positiveness” score in [0,1], instantiated as IoU with ground-truth. When IoU is known, the method minimizes the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with mean g(u) (set to u). When only bounding boxes are available, the IoU per proposal is treated as a latent variable and learned by alternating minimization, with a KL regularizer that encourages the latent u to stay near the proposal’s IoU against the ground-truth box. The authors evaluate on CPMC segments for semantic segmentation and on PASCAL VOC object detection by concatenating a spatial confidence map (from e-SVM) with RCNN features, reporting small gains over SVC/SVR on segment-scoring measures and ~1.2–1.5 AP improvements over RCNN on VOC07.

Relation to prior work
The expected hinge-loss formulation corresponds to learning with soft/probabilistic labels and is closely related to weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs, fuzzy SVMs, SVMs with uncertain labels, and duplicating each instance into positive and negative examples weighted by µ and 1−µ. The latent extension and block-coordinate optimization are in the spirit of latent SVM/EM-style approaches. The paper cites weighted SVMs and MIL but underplays the extensive literature on soft labels/uncertain labels and ranking-based learning for proposal scoring.

Strengths
- Simple formulation: expected hinge loss with soft labels is convex (given u) and easy to implement with standard solvers.
- The latent version with a KL prior to ubb is a sensible way to regularize under weak supervision.
- The spatial confidence map feature is straightforward and broadly applicable, and the reported detection improvements over a strong RCNN baseline are non-trivial in absolute AP terms.
- Segment-ranking evaluations (TAPC, NDCG) attempt to align metrics with the learning objective.

Weaknesses and concerns

Quality
- Novelty of the core formulation is limited. The objective L = E_Y[max(0, 1 − Y w^T x)] with µ = g(u) is equivalent to training on two copies of each example with labels +1 and −1 weighted by µ and 1−µ, i.e., an instance-weighted cost-sensitive SVM. This equivalence is neither stated nor analyzed. The claimed distinction from WSVM (“weights labels instead of data points”) is semantic rather than substantive.
- The latent optimization is only sketched. With g(u)=u, each ui update solves a simple 1D convex problem; a closed-form or explicit derivation is not provided, nor are details on λR, initialization, stopping, or convergence guarantees beyond “until converge.” Given the non-convexity in (w,u), sensitivity to initialization and λR should be reported.
- Baselines are incomplete. For known u: compare to logistic regression with soft labels, isotonic regression on scores, RankSVM/listwise ranking (especially since NDCG is used), and modern WSVM variants. For weak labels: compare to latent SVM, MIL variants adapted to soft positivity, and soft-label logistic regression with the same KL prior.
- The improvements on segment tasks are small. The paper reports “slightly better than SVR” on TAPC/NDCG, yet semantic segmentation accuracy is only “comparable” to SVR. Without strong application-level gains, the utility of e-SVM over SVR is unclear.
- The detection gains are confounded. In addition to concatenating e-SVM features, the authors “modified the bounding box regularization” in RCNN. It is unclear whether the baseline RCNN was also reimplemented with identical modifications. An ablation (RCNN baseline as in [14], RCNN + modified bbox reg only, RCNN + e-SVM only, RCNN + both) is necessary to attribute gains.
- No statistical significance or error bars are provided; results are on a single split (VOC07/11) with relatively small datasets.

Clarity
- The writing has numerous typos and formatting issues; several references are incomplete. The math in Eq. (1) is hard to parse; implementation details (how exactly LibLinear is used to handle different weights for l+ and l− per instance) are missing. The definition of TAPC is underspecified (range and spacing of thresholds, class averaging).
- The choice of g(u)=u is not justified or ablated (other monotone mappings? calibration?). Hyperparameter selection for λw and λR is not described.

Originality
- The expected hinge-loss with soft labels and the idea of alternating with latent soft labels under a KL prior are incremental and closely related to established methods (WSVM, fuzzy/uncertain-label SVM, latent SVM/EM). The paper does not clearly delineate what is novel beyond this packaging.

Significance
- The practical impact is uncertain. On segmentation, e-SVM does not substantially improve over SVR. On detection, +1.2–1.5 AP on VOC07 is meaningful but modest and lacks rigorous ablation to isolate the cause. Claims of generality are not supported by broader experiments (other proposal methods, larger datasets, stronger detectors).

Questions for authors
1. Please formalize the equivalence (or difference) between e-SVM and instance-weighted/cost-sensitive SVM with duplicated examples. How do you encode Eq. (1) in LibLinear?
2. Provide the closed-form or explicit update for ui in the latent step and describe λR selection, initialization, and convergence behavior. Sensitivity analysis?
3. Why g(u)=u? Did you try calibrated or parametric g (e.g., sigmoid/temperature scaling)?
4. For detection, report a clean ablation isolating the effect of e-SVM features separate from changes to RCNN’s bbox regression. Also report per-class AP and variance.
5. Compare against RankSVM/listwise methods targeting NDCG/TAPC directly, and against logistic regression with soft labels.

Suggestions
- Strengthen related work on soft/probabilistic labels (e.g., fuzzy SVMs, SVM with uncertain labels) and clearly position novelty.
- Provide comprehensive ablations and stronger baselines as above.
- Improve clarity of the objective, optimization, and hyperparameter protocols; release code or pseudo-code mapping to standard solvers.
- Evaluate on additional datasets and with modern detectors/proposal methods to support generality.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, implementable idea; convex for fixed u.
- Addresses an important practical regime (weak supervision, proposal scoring).
- Some empirical gains on a standard detection benchmark.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; close to established WSVM/soft-label SVM.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations; methodological confounds.
- Modest empirical improvements; mixed results on segmentation.
- Clarity and polish issues; missing details reduce reproducibility.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is reasonable but incremental, and the current empirical and methodological evidence does not convincingly establish a clear advantage over established alternatives. A revised version with stronger positioning, rigorous comparisons, and clearer optimization details could be more compelling.
