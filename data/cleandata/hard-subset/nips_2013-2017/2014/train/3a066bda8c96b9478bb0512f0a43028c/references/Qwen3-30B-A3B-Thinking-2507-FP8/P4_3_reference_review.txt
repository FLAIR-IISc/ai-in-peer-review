

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values (positiveness) in [0,1] rather than binary labels. The authors address a practical problem in computer vision where obtaining pixel-level annotations is expensive, but bounding box annotations are more readily available. The e-SVM model treats the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the observed positiveness (e.g., IoU), and formulates a loss function that takes the expectation over this random variable. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision settings where positiveness is unobserved.

The work relates to several existing NIPS and computer vision literature streams. It builds on recent work in weakly supervised learning (e.g., [19, 20, 22, 26]) but differs significantly from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, which cannot handle partial overlaps between proposals and ground truth. It also relates to Weighted SVMs (WSVMs) [23, 27], but the authors clearly distinguish their method by weighting labels rather than data points. The paper also extends the segment proposal confidence prediction framework from [5, 18], which typically uses SVC or SVR for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the e-SVM model, showing how it generalizes standard SVMs. The latent e-SVM extension for weak supervision is well-motivated and supported by a convex optimization approach with convergence guarantees. The authors provide sufficient mathematical details to understand the model and its implementation.

2. **Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes comprehensive experiments on two major computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) using standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC). The evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) are well-chosen for the segment classification task, and the results consistently show improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. The 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is meaningful and competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The problem statement, methodology, and results are clearly presented. The authors provide sufficient detail about their implementation (e.g., feature extraction, parameter choices) to allow reproducibility.

4. **Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem in computer vision (reducing annotation effort) with a method that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. The approach can be easily integrated with existing systems (e.g., RCNN) with minimal modifications, making it immediately applicable to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Claims:** While the e-SVM formulation is novel for this specific problem setting, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how it differs from existing weighted SVM approaches. The authors cite WSVMs but don't clearly articulate why their label-weighting approach is fundamentally different from existing weighted SVM techniques. Some related work on label-dependent weighting [17] is mentioned but not properly contrasted.

2. **Comparison to Alternative Weak Supervision Methods:** The paper focuses on comparing to SVC and SVR, but doesn't compare to other weakly supervised learning approaches that could be relevant (e.g., MIL variants, [26]). This limits the demonstration of the method's relative advantages.

3. **Analysis of Limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach. For instance, it doesn't analyze how the method performs when the positiveness values are highly noisy or when there's significant class imbalance.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how sensitive the method is to key parameters (e.g., the regularization strength λR, the initialization of u). This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

- The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach to an important problem in computer vision.
- The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines.
- The method is general and could be applied to other domains beyond computer vision.
- The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

- The novelty claim is somewhat overstated, as the core idea of using continuous labels in SVMs has some precedents.
- The comparison to alternative weakly supervised learning methods is limited.
- The paper doesn't adequately analyze the limitations and sensitivity of the proposed method.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The e-SVM approach is well-justified, technically sound, and demonstrates practical improvements on standard benchmarks. While the novelty claims could be more carefully articulated, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field. The method's simplicity and ease of integration with existing systems are particularly appealing. The authors have addressed an important practical problem with a theoretically grounded solution that shows measurable improvements in real-world applications.
