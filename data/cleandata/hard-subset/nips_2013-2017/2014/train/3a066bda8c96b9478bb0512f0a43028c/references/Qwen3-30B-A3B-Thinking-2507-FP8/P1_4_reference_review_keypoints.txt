- Introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that models continuous "positiveness" labels as Bernoulli random variables with probability tied to the positiveness value.  
- Proposes a latent e-SVM extension for cases where positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., only bounding boxes available).  
- Applies method to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC, outperforming SVC/SVR baselines and yielding a 1.5% AP gain in detection.  
- Weights labels rather than instances, handling partial overlaps better than MIL approaches.  
- Technical formulation is well-motivated and optimization (Algorithm 1) is clearly described.  
- Experiments are thorough with appropriate baselines and metrics (TAPC, NDCG) and show consistent improvements.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis of convergence for the latent e-SVM optimization despite convexity in separate variables.  
- Paper is clearly written and organized, with a particularly clear description of the spatial confidence map feature.  
- Some notation inconsistencies (e.g., reuse of "u") and presentation of results could better compare to wider state-of-the-art.  
- Novel contribution: expectation loss for continuous labels in SVMs differs from prior weighted SVMs and MIL.  
- Related work coverage is insufficient; should cite and compare to more weakly supervised and bounding-box-based methods (e.g., Hoiem et al. 2008 and recent segmentation work).  
- Significant practical impact by reducing need for pixel-level labels and easily integrating with existing detectors.  
- Limitation: experiments do not leverage ImageNet annotations, weakening claims about large-scale applicability.  
- Strengths: important problem, sound theory, consistent empirical gains, simple and generalizable method, well-structured experiments.  
- Weaknesses: limited SOTA comparisons, insufficient related work discussion, missing convergence theory, no ImageNet experiments, and weak motivation for latent-variable regularization.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions—expand related work, compare to more state-of-the-art detectors, discuss computational complexity, and consider ImageNet experiments.
