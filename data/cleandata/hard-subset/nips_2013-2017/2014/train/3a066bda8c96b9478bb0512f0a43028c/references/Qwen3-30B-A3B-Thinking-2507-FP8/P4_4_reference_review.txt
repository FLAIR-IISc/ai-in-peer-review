

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training examples have continuous "positiveness" scores (e.g., IoU overlap) rather than binary labels. The authors address the problem of segment classification where proposals may only partially match ground truth, and positiveness values fall in [0,1]. The e-SVM model treats the label as a random variable with a Bernoulli distribution whose success probability depends on the observed positiveness. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision scenarios where positiveness is unobserved (only bounding box annotations available), using an iterative optimization approach. The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The work relates to previous NIPS papers on weakly supervised learning (e.g., MIL [7, 2, 20, 22, 26] and WSVMs [23, 27]), but distinguishes itself from these approaches. Unlike MIL, which assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag, the authors argue their problem setting (where most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels) is fundamentally different. The e-SVM also differs from WSVMs by weighting labels rather than data points, which leads to a different optimization problem.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem formulation is well-motivated and addresses a significant practical challenge in computer vision: reducing the need for expensive pixel-level annotations.

2. The theoretical formulation of the expectation loss is sound and clearly presented. The connection to existing SVM formulations is well-explained.

3. The experimental validation is comprehensive, with evaluations on both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using standard PASCAL VOC datasets.

4. The paper introduces two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically designed for the segment classification problem, which provide more direct performance assessment than task-specific metrics.

5. The results consistently show improvements over SVC (which requires ad-hoc thresholding) and SVR (which is not designed for bounded targets), with the e-SVM achieving 1.5% higher average AP on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The improvements over baselines are modest (1.5% AP gain on detection), and the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate why this is a significant advancement over existing approaches.

2. The connection to recent MIL work for segmentation (e.g., [19, 26]) is not thoroughly explored. The authors claim their problem setting is "in the middle" between MIL and strong supervision, but do not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art MIL approaches for their specific task.

3. The regularization term in Equation 3 (KL divergence) is introduced without strong theoretical justification. The paper states it's "a strong regularization term" but doesn't explain why this specific form is optimal.

4. The paper doesn't adequately discuss limitations or failure cases of the approach. For example, how does the algorithm perform when the initialization (ubb) is poor?

5. The experimental section lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the approach (e.g., the effect of the regularization term).

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel approach to a well-motivated problem in weakly supervised learning.
- Clear theoretical formulation with sound mathematical foundation.
- Consistent experimental improvements over relevant baselines.
- Applicable to multiple computer vision tasks with practical significance.

*Con:*
- Modest performance gains that may not be sufficient for high-stakes applications.
- Limited comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods.
- Some technical details (e.g., regularization term) lack sufficient justification.
- The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this approach is fundamentally better than alternatives.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach to a meaningful problem in weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM formulation is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results consistently show improvements over SVC and SVR. However, the significance of the contribution is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains and lack of comparison with the most recent weakly supervised learning methods. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, but it doesn't represent a major advance over existing work.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to experimental validation. The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics for segment classification is a valuable contribution to the field.

Given the modest but consistent improvements over baselines, the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. It should be accepted as a solid, incremental contribution to the area, though it doesn't represent a breakthrough. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a practical problem with potential applications in computer vision.
