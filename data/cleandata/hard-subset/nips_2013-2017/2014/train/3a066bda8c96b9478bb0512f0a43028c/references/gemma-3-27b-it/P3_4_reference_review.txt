## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM-based approach, e-SVM, for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing the scenario where only partial or noisy labels (positiveness values) are available. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates promising empirical results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. While the improvements aren't revolutionary, the method is a solid contribution with clear advantages over existing approaches (SVC, SVR) in the specific setting it targets. I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenging problem of learning with weak supervision, a crucial area for scaling machine learning to large datasets where full annotation is impractical. Instead of relying on binary labels, e-SVM leverages continuous “positiveness” values indicating the degree of correctness of a proposed label.  The core idea is to formulate an SVM loss that expects these positiveness values, effectively weighting samples based on their estimated quality.  The paper extends this to a latent variable setting when positiveness is unobserved, making it directly applicable to scenarios with only bounding box annotations.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering multiple instance learning and weighted SVMs.  The authors correctly position their work between strong and weak supervision, highlighting the limitations of existing MIL techniques for their scenario.  The connection to weighted SVMs is also well-explained, differentiating e-SVM through its weighting of *labels* rather than data points. However, it could benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of recent advances in weakly supervised learning that leverage proposal-based methods (beyond the cited MIL papers), such as those using expectation-maximization or contrastive learning.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a clever extension of the standard SVM, directly addressing the challenges of continuous label values and weak supervision. The latent variable extension is particularly valuable.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the e-SVM loss function and discusses its properties, including its convexity and relationship to standard SVM solvers.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on PASCAL VOC consistently demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR in both fully supervised (pixel-level annotations) and weakly supervised (bounding box annotations) settings.  The improvement on object detection by coupling e-SVM features with a state-of-the-art detector (RCNN) is a significant achievement.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the flexibility of their approach, noting its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors, feature extractors, and base detectors. This makes it a broadly applicable technique.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and algorithms.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** While consistently outperforming baselines, the performance gains aren't always dramatic. The 1.5% average improvement in AP on object detection, while positive, might not be considered groundbreaking. Further analysis of *where* the method shines (e.g., specific object categories) would be helpful.
* **Regularization Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the importance of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model, but doesn’t provide much guidance on how to select its value.  A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparative Scope:**  While comparisons to SVC and SVR are appropriate, a comparison to more recent and advanced weakly-supervised learning methods, even those with different underlying assumptions, would strengthen the paper. This could include methods leveraging pseudo-labeling or self-training techniques.
* **Feature Engineering:** The concatenation of confidence maps with CNN features seems somewhat ad-hoc. A brief exploration of other fusion strategies might yield further improvements.




**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.2 (Latent e-SVM):** The explanation of why regularization is crucial to avoid the algorithm getting stuck in a local minimum is clear. However, a more intuitive explanation of *why* the proposed KL divergence regularization is particularly effective would be valuable.
* **Section 5.1.1 (Evaluation Criteria):** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics is a strength, as it directly addresses the segment-wise prediction task. However, more justification for choosing these specific metrics over standard classification metrics (e.g., AUC) could be provided.
* **Section 5.3 (Object Detection):**  The statement that the improvements are expected to be further increased by using ImageNet data for pre-training is a bit vague. Could the authors elaborate on how they envision leveraging ImageNet data in this context?
* **Figure 2:** The figure illustrating the spatial confidence map feature is helpful, but could benefit from a visual example showing the confidence scores overlaid on an image.



**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to learning with weak supervision.
* Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines.
* Flexibility and generality of the method.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental performance gains.
* Limited comparative scope with recent related work.
* Lack of detailed guidance on parameter tuning.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Expand the related work section** to include a more comprehensive overview of recent advances in weakly supervised learning with proposal-based methods.
2.  **Provide more details on parameter tuning**, particularly for the latent e-SVM model's regularization parameter.
3.  **Consider adding a comparison to a more recent and relevant weakly-supervised learning method.**
4.  **Elaborate on the potential benefits of leveraging ImageNet data.**
5.  **Add a visual example to Figure 2** showcasing the confidence map feature.



I believe that addressing these minor points will significantly strengthen the paper and further highlight its contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning.
