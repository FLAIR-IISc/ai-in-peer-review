## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a method designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where the “positiveness” of data points, rather than binary labels, is available. The authors address challenges in applying standard SVM techniques to such problems in computer vision tasks like semantic segmentation and object detection, and demonstrate performance improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. While the core idea of weighting samples by their estimated correctness is conceptually sound, and the experimental results demonstrate gains, numerous minor flaws and a lack of rigorous justification for certain design choices raise concerns regarding the overall quality of the work.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the growing need for learning algorithms capable of leveraging weakly supervised data. The core idea of the e-SVM – weighting samples based on their “positiveness” (IoU in their experiments) – builds upon existing weighted SVM approaches [23, 27], but frames it specifically in terms of weighting labels rather than data points. The paper situates itself appropriately within the landscape of weakly supervised learning, acknowledging connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [7, 2] and highlighting the distinctiveness of their problem setting. The application of e-SVM to segmentation and detection, coupled with comparisons to SVR and SVC, provides a concrete demonstration of its utility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem Setting:** The focus on learning from weakly supervised data is timely and addresses a significant challenge in computer vision.
*   **Conceptual Soundness:** The idea of weighting samples based on their estimated correctness (positiveness) is intuitively appealing and potentially effective.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on PASCAL VOC datasets demonstrate consistent performance gains over the baseline methods (SVC and SVR). The 1.5% AP improvement in object detection, while not massive, is a positive indicator.
*   **Generality:** The authors highlight the flexibility of their approach to incorporate different segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the authors claim their approach is an extension of SVC, the theoretical benefits of weighting labels *instead* of data points (as in standard weighted SVMs) are not clearly articulated. A more rigorous analysis comparing e-SVM to WSVMs beyond the statement in Section 2 would be valuable.
*   **Ad-hoc Positiveness Function:** The choice of setting µi = ui (positiveness = IoU) is presented without justification. Other functional forms relating positiveness to IoU could potentially yield better performance. This lack of exploration feels arbitrary.
*   **Regularization in Latent e-SVM:** The justification for the specific regularization term used in the latent e-SVM (Equation 3), based on KL divergence, is weak. Why is this particular regularization preferred over alternatives? The assertion that it prevents “sticking” at 0 or 1 without further analysis is not entirely convincing.
*   **Algorithm 1 Details:** The convergence criterion for Algorithm 1 is unspecified. This omission hinders reproducibility.
*   **Implementation Details & Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks crucial details regarding implementation (e.g., SVM solver used beyond “LibLinear”) and hyperparameter tuning. This significantly impacts the replicability of the results. What values were used for the regularization parameters (λ and λR)? How was the threshold τb determined for SVC?
*   **Minor Inconsistencies & Sloppiness:**
    *   Equation (1) introduces λ as a scalar, but it's not explicitly defined whether it is positive or negative.
    *   The sentence "It turns to be similar to a latent SVM and can lead the algorithm to stuck in the local minimal" contains a grammatical error (“stuck” should be “stuck”).
    *   Figure 2’s caption refers to “additive kernels approximation methods [25]” but this reference seems misplaced—additive kernels are a *technique* for kernel approximation, not a method in and of themselves.
    *   The conclusion states “We believe that, with the ever growing size of datesets…” (datesets should be datasets).
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study is needed to isolate the contributions of different components. For instance, how much improvement comes specifically from using the e-SVM loss function versus simply using the spatial confidence map features?

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods.
*   Conceptually simple and potentially generalizable.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical grounding for key design choices.
*   Insufficient detail regarding implementation and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall presentation.
*   The improvements, while consistent, are not transformative, and the work feels somewhat incremental given the lack of deeper analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its merits, the paper suffers from a noticeable lack of rigor and attention to detail. While the core idea is promising, the insufficient justification for design choices, the omission of crucial implementation details, and the presence of numerous minor flaws significantly weaken the contribution. The experimental results, while positive, are not strong enough to outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. I recommend rejection. While the topic is relevant and the authors demonstrate some improvement, the paper requires significant revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered a high-quality NIPS submission. Specifically, a more thorough theoretical analysis, a more detailed description of the implementation, a more rigorous ablation study, and a careful correction of the minor flaws are essential.
