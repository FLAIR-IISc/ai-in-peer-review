### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) to address learning scenarios where training labels are not binary but are instead continuous "positiveness" values within the range [0, 1]. This is particularly relevant for computer vision tasks like semantic segmentation and object detection, where a proposal (e.g., a segment) may partially overlap with the ground truth, and this overlap (e.g., IoU) can be used as a soft label.

The core idea is to model the binary label of a training example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being positive is given by its known positiveness score. The e-SVM loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this latent variable. This formulation elegantly handles the continuous labels and is shown to be equivalent to a weighted SVM problem that can be solved with standard solvers.

The paper extends this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM), where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are inferred jointly with the SVM model parameters using an alternating optimization procedure.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on two major vision tasks. First, for semantic segmentation, they show e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR for classifying segment proposals. Second, and more significantly, they apply the latent e-SVM to object detection, creating a "spatial confidence map" feature that, when concatenated with features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN model, yields a notable improvement of 1.5% average precision on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Review and Justification**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and practically useful extension to the SVM framework. The contributions are clear and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its value.

**Quality: (High)**

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the e-SVM as an expectation of the hinge loss is principled and provides a solid theoretical foundation for handling continuous labels. The derivation in Equation 1 is clear and leads to an objective function that can be optimized efficiently. The extension to the latent e-SVM for the weakly supervised case is a logical and powerful next step. The proposed alternating optimization scheme is a standard and appropriate method for this type of problem. The introduction of the KL-divergence based regularization term `R(u)` is a thoughtful addition that prevents the latent variables from collapsing to a trivial binary solution, which is a common pitfall in similar latent variable models.

The experimental methodology is rigorous. A key strength is the introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the core task of segment proposal classification. This allows for a direct and fair comparison of different models (e-SVM, SVC, SVR) for this specific problem, rather than relying solely on downstream task performance. The results in Figure 3 clearly support the claim that e-SVM is better suited for this task than the alternatives. The subsequent application to object detection, where the method improves upon a very strong and contemporary baseline (R-CNN), provides compelling evidence of the work's practical utility.

**Clarity: (Good)**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated in the introduction. The mathematical formulation of both the basic and latent e-SVM models is presented concisely. The overall framework and its application to vision tasks are illustrated well (Figures 1 and 2).

However, there are minor areas for improvement:
*   The description of the spatial confidence map feature in Section 4.2 could be slightly more explicit. For instance, it should be clarified that the confidence score `CfdScore(S)` for a segment is the direct output of the trained e-SVM classifier, i.e., `w^T * x_S`.
*   The optimization details for the latent e-SVM are presented in a high-level algorithm. While sufficient, adding a sentence about the convergence properties (e.g., that it converges to a local minimum) and perhaps the typical number of iterations required would add completeness.

**Originality: (High)**

The core idea of the expectation loss SVM is novel. While the paper correctly relates its work to weighted SVMs, it also highlights the key difference: e-SVM effectively weights the positive and negative loss components for a single sample, whereas traditional weighted SVMs assign a single weight to the entire sample's loss. This is a subtle but important distinction that makes the model particularly suitable for this problem domain. The formulation is a creative synthesis of probabilistic modeling (the latent Bernoulli label) and max-margin learning.

Furthermore, the application of this idea to generate segmentation-based features to improve a powerful detection model like R-CNN is a novel contribution. At a time when deep learning features were becoming dominant, this work shows that complementary cues from structured, segment-based models can still provide significant gains.

**Significance: (High)**

The work has significant practical and research implications. The problem of learning from weak or incomplete labels is of immense importance, as it directly addresses the bottleneck of manual annotation in creating large-scale datasets. The e-SVM provides a general and effective tool for a specific, yet common, type of weak supervision where labels are continuous scores.

The demonstrated 1.5% AP improvement over the R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a substantial gain on a highly competitive benchmark and demonstrates a clear advance in the state of the art. The method is general and not tied to a specific feature extractor or proposal generator, suggesting that the ideas presented could be integrated into various other computer vision pipelines. Researchers working on object detection, segmentation, and machine learning with non-standard supervision are likely to build upon these ideas.

---

### **Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novel and Principled Model:** The e-SVM formulation is an elegant, original, and well-founded solution for learning with continuous [0, 1] labels.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates superior performance against relevant baselines (SVC, SVR) on the direct task and shows a significant, state-of-the-art improvement on a challenging and important downstream task (object detection).
*   **High Significance:** The work addresses the crucial problem of learning from weak supervision and provides a practical method that could be widely adopted.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The introduction of new, task-specific metrics (TAPC, NDCG) strengthens the experimental section and the paper's conclusions.

### **Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Limited Discussion on Optimization:** The discussion on the convergence and sensitivity of the alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is brief.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some small details in the methodology could be spelled out more explicitly for better reproducibility.

These weaknesses are minor and can be easily addressed in a final version. They do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the paper.

### **Conclusion**

This paper is a high-quality contribution that presents a novel and effective method for weakly supervised learning. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and its significance is demonstrated through strong experimental results on challenging benchmarks. I strongly recommend acceptance.
