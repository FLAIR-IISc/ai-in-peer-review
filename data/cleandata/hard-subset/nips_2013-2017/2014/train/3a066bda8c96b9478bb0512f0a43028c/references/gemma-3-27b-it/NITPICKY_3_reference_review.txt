This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically targeting scenarios where continuous "positiveness" values (like IoU) are available for segment proposals instead of binary labels. The core idea of weighting samples by their positiveness to extend traditional SVMs is conceptually sound and addresses a practical challenge in weakly supervised learning. The paper presents experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating improvements over standard SVC and SVR baselines.

However, despite the potentially valuable contribution, a multitude of minor – yet pervasive – flaws significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. The writing style is often imprecise, the experimental details are lacking rigor, and a consistent attention to detail appears absent.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a critical area, and handling continuous positiveness values is a practical concern.
*   **Conceptually Sound:** The e-SVM formulation, extending standard SVMs to incorporate positiveness weights, is a reasonable approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate improvements over SVC and SVR in the tested scenarios, indicating a potential benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Imprecise Writing & Lack of Clarity:** The paper suffers from repeated instances of vague language. For example, “this problem is transferred to a standard binary classification problem” is misleading – it *requires* a heuristic threshold, which the authors themselves acknowledge negates its benefit. The explanation of the latent e-SVM's optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) is superficial and lacks details crucial for implementation. The meaning of "strongly supervised case" is unclear when the positiveness *is* observed; it doesn’t equate to full supervision.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. What SVM solver was used? What parameters were tuned, and how? The regularization parameter λR in the latent e-SVM is mentioned but not specified in experiments. The "additive kernels approximation method" in object detection is cited [25] but its role and specifics aren’t explained within the paper.  The statement that "we modified the bounding box regularization method" requires specification – how was it modified? The descriptions of the baselines (SVC, SVR) are minimal.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** Notation is inconsistent. ‘ui’ is defined as IoU, then used to represent positiveness generally.  F(xi) is defined as wTxi+b, then later as wTxi, causing confusion.
*   **Redundant/Superfluous Content:** The lengthy review of related work on MIL feels tangential to the core contribution. While acknowledging its difference is appropriate, the extensive discussion feels unnecessary and dilutes the focus.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of a "1.5 percent average improvement" in object detection AP feels understated without providing standard deviations or confidence intervals. The statement that the approach is "general" is too broad without further clarification of limitations.
* **Minor Errors:** There are numerous minor grammatical errors and typos throughout the manuscript (e.g. "data is only weakly labeled," should be "data are only weakly labeled").
* **Evaluation Criteria:** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG feels unmotivated. There is no justification for *why* these metrics are more appropriate than standard metrics used in these tasks (e.g., mAP for object detection, mean IoU for segmentation).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The core idea addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates empirical improvements.

**Con:** The numerous flaws in clarity, experimental detail, and writing quality are substantial. The lack of rigorous evaluation and the overstated claims raise concerns about the reliability of the results. The tangential related work section weakens the focus of the paper. 

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the e-SVM algorithm has potential, the paper, in its current form, falls short of the quality expected for NIPS. The numerous minor flaws accumulate to create a significantly weakened presentation. A major revision addressing the clarity issues, providing comprehensive experimental details, removing extraneous content, and revising claims would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors must demonstrate a commitment to precision and rigor in both their methodology and presentation.
