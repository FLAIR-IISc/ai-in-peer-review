

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values (e.g., Intersection-over-Union scores) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a principled way to handle these continuous labels by treating them as probabilistic indicators of class membership, rather than converting them to binary labels via heuristics as is commonly done with SVC. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision scenarios where only bounding box annotations are available. The approach is applied to two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection, with experiments on PASCAL VOC datasets showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive, and bounding box annotations are more readily available. The e-SVM approach provides a principled alternative to ad-hoc thresholding methods used in SVC.

2. The theoretical foundation is sound and well-explained. The extension to latent variables for weak supervision is logically developed and the optimization algorithm is clearly presented.

3. The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR on the PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset, and a 1.5% average precision gain on object detection when combined with RCNN.

4. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical details for reproducibility. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification is particularly useful.

5. The method is generalizable to other tasks with continuous labels, which broadens its potential applicability beyond the specific computer vision tasks demonstrated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical novelty is somewhat limited. While the approach is well-executed, it builds directly on standard SVM formulations with a modified loss function. The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how this approach differs from existing weighted SVM methods in a way that justifies the novelty claim.

2. The experimental evaluation is somewhat narrow. The paper compares primarily to SVC and SVR, but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods in semantic segmentation (e.g., deep learning approaches) or object detection (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO). This limits the demonstration of the approach's true significance.

3. The paper claims the method can "use any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers" but doesn't provide empirical evidence of this flexibility. The results are limited to CPMC segments and specific feature extraction methods.

4. The regularization term for the latent variables (Equation 3) is well-justified but appears somewhat ad-hoc. The paper doesn't explore alternative regularization strategies or analyze their impact on performance.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address computational complexity or scalability to large datasets, which is particularly important given the authors' claim about the importance of weak supervision for large-scale datasets like ImageNet.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a solid technical contribution with clear experimental validation on two important computer vision tasks. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical application and clear improvements over baselines make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Strengthen the novelty claim by more thoroughly differentiating the e-SVM from existing weighted SVM approaches and discussing how it specifically addresses limitations that other methods don't.

2. Expand the experimental comparison to include more recent state-of-the-art methods in semantic segmentation and object detection, particularly deep learning approaches that might handle weak supervision differently.

3. Provide evidence of the method's flexibility by testing with different segment proposal methods (e.g., superpixels vs. CPMC) and feature extraction techniques.

4. Include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability, especially for large datasets like ImageNet.

5. Consider adding a brief analysis of the impact of the regularization term's strength (λR) on performance, to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a practical contribution to weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The results are significant, the methodology is sound, and the presentation is clear. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
