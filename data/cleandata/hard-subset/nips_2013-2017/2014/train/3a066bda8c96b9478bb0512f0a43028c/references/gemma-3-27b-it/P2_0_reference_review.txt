## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification of the standard Support Vector Machine designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where data labels are not binary but continuous values representing “positiveness” (e.g., IoU overlap). The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness in segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, showing improvements over standard SVC and SVR approaches, particularly when only bounding box annotations are available. They provide a theoretical justification for the method and detail an iterative optimization algorithm for the latent variable case.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a crucial area of research, particularly with the increasing availability of weakly labeled datasets (like those with bounding boxes) and the cost of pixel-level annotation.
* **Novel Formulation:** The e-SVM offers a principled approach to handling continuous label values, moving beyond ad-hoc binarization strategies used with SVC. The idea of weighting samples by their “positiveness” is intuitive and well-motivated.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the e-SVM and the latent variable extension, and justifies its connection to weighted SVMs.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors present comprehensive experimental results on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC), demonstrating performance gains in both fully supervised and weakly supervised settings for semantic segmentation and object detection.  The 1.5% average AP improvement in object detection is a significant result.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the flexibility of their approach, capable of integrating diverse segment proposal methods, features, and base detectors.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Application:** While the e-SVM formulation itself is novel, its application to semantic segmentation and object detection largely follows established pipelines. The paper doesn’t explore potentially more innovative applications where the e-SVM’s benefits would be particularly pronounced.
* **Evaluation Metric Choices:** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG for segment proposal evaluation is appreciated, but their practical significance beyond this specific task isn't fully clear. More common metrics related to downstream task performance (e.g., mAP for object detection) would strengthen the results.
* **Algorithm Complexity:**  The iterative optimization for the latent variable case, while described, could benefit from further discussion of its practical convergence properties and computational cost.  Is it significantly more expensive than training a standard SVM?
* **Ablation Studies:** While comparisons to SVC and SVR are present, more focused ablation studies would be helpful. For example, how sensitive is performance to the choice of regularization parameter in the latent e-SVM?
* **Presentation:** The paper suffers slightly from repetitive phrasing and could benefit from more concise writing, particularly in the experimental sections.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is convincing, and the experimental results are robust.  However, the incremental nature of some of the application work slightly lowers the score.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally clear, but sections detailing the experimental setup and algorithm optimization could be more concise and accessible. The repetition detracts from the overall clarity.
* **Originality (8/10):** The e-SVM formulation is a novel contribution. While the application areas are well-explored, the specific integration of the e-SVM within those pipelines represents a reasonable degree of originality.
* **Significance (8/10):** The potential impact of this work is considerable. The ability to effectively leverage weakly supervised data is crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets. The 1.5% improvement in object detection is a tangible contribution, and the generality of the approach is promising.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and timely problem in machine learning.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm (e-SVM).
* Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing methods.
* Offers a flexible framework applicable to various computer vision tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental nature of some application work.
* Limited discussion of algorithmic complexity and convergence.
* Could benefit from more focused ablation studies.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM is a well-motivated and effective algorithm, and the empirical results are convincing. The potential impact of this work is significant, and it aligns well with the themes of the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and conciseness of the presentation and providing a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity.
