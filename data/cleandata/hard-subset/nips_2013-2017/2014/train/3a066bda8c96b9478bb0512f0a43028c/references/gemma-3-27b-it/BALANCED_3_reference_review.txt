This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically targeting scenarios where only partial or noisy labels are available – a common challenge in computer vision. The core idea is to treat the ‘positiveness’ of segment proposals as a continuous variable, rather than forcing a binary classification, and to model this positiveness as an expectation in the loss function. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over standard SVC and SVR approaches, particularly in weakly supervised settings.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of learning from weakly labeled data is crucial given the cost of full annotation. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods (SVC requiring ad-hoc thresholding, SVR not well-suited for bounded outputs) and position e-SVM as a principled alternative.  The approach of modelling the positiveness as an expectation is novel and sensible. The experimental validation on PASCAL VOC for both segmentation and detection, coupled with comparisons to strong baselines (SVR, SVC, and a state-of-the-art detector RCNN), is a significant strength. The presentation of both theoretical formulation and practical implementation details is clear.  The inclusion of a latent variable extension for truly weak supervision is also a valuable contribution, addressing a realistic use case.  The iterative algorithm described for the latent variable case is also clearly articulated.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The novelty of the core e-SVM idea feels somewhat incremental. While the motivation for avoiding hard thresholds is valid, the paper doesn’t sufficiently contrast itself with existing approaches that *also* use continuous label assignments (e.g., various forms of regression losses), and doesn’t deeply analyze *why* e-SVM performs better in these scenarios. The experimental evaluation, while thorough, lacks depth in analysis. The improvement of 1.5% AP in object detection, while statistically significant, feels relatively modest given the complexity of the approach. More extensive ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of individual components (e.g., the choice of regularization term in the latent variable model, the spatial confidence map feature design). The explanation for the choice of the KL divergence regularization in the latent e-SVM is weak. A justification for its suitability over other possible regularizers is needed. The writing quality, while generally good, suffers from some redundancy. Certain phrases and explanations are repeated across sections.  The references are not completely up to date; there's a missed opportunity to discuss more recent advances in weakly supervised learning or related areas. The paper would benefit from a discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially given the need to solve SVMs iteratively. Finally, the visualization of the spatial confidence maps in the object detection experiments is missing - this would significantly improve understanding of the method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a practically important problem (weakly supervised learning).
* Proposes a principled and novel approach (expectation loss SVM).
* Provides solid experimental validation on standard datasets.
* Demonstrates improvements over strong baselines.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty - the core idea isn’t dramatically different from existing approaches.
* Limited analysis of experimental results.
* Lack of ablation studies.
* Weak justification for certain design choices (e.g. KL divergence).
* Scalability considerations are not addressed.
* Missing visualization of results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**.  While the work is solid and the experimental results are positive, the incremental nature of the novelty and the lack of in-depth analysis prevent it from meeting the bar for NIPS acceptance. The authors have presented a technically sound and well-executed piece of work, but it doesn't offer a sufficiently substantial advance over existing techniques. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly conducting more thorough ablation studies, providing a deeper analysis of the results, and justifying design choices – would significantly strengthen the paper. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit this work to a future conference.
