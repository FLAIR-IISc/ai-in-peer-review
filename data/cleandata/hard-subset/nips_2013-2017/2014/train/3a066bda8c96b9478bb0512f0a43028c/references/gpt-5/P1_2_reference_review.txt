Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple extension of the hinge-loss SVM that handles “soft” labels in [0,1] by minimizing the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with success probability µi=g(ui), where ui is a “positiveness” score (e.g., IoU with ground-truth). This yields a per-example loss g(ui)·max(0,1−w⊤xi) + (1−g(ui))·max(0,1+w⊤xi), which can be viewed as cost-sensitive hinge loss with instance-specific positive/negative weights summing to 1. A latent version treats ui as unobserved when only bounding boxes are available and regularizes ui toward an initialization ubb (segment–box IoU) using a convex KL (cross-entropy) penalty. Optimization alternates between minimizing w (convex SVM) and u (convex in u given w). The method is evaluated on segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation (with pixel and box supervision), and object detection, where the authors derive a class-specific spatial confidence map feature from segment scores and concatenate it to R-CNN features, reporting +1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Relation to prior work: The formulation is closely related to SVMs with soft/probabilistic/fuzzy labels and cost-sensitive SVMs, as well as weakly supervised learning (MIL, MIM) and instance-weighted SVMs. The latent version is biconvex akin to latent SVM with a stabilizing regularizer. The application to detection connects to segmentation-aware features.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective: expected hinge loss with per-example label probabilities; easy to implement and compatible with linear solvers after a straightforward transformation.
- Practical extension to weakly supervised settings via latent ui with a convex regularizer toward ubb, avoiding degenerate 0/1 solutions and enabling alternating convex optimization.
- Empirical utility: consistent gains over SVC and small gains over SVR on proposal scoring under both pixel and box supervision by TAPC and NDCG; non-trivial improvement (+1.5 mAP) when augmenting R-CNN on VOC07 with the proposed confidence map features.
- The spatial confidence map addresses partial overlap between segments and detection windows and yields a simple, general way to inject segmentation cues.

Weaknesses
- Originality is limited: minimizing expected hinge with soft/probabilistic labels is known (e.g., fuzzy/probabilistic SVMs, label-confidence SVMs, cost-sensitive hinge with instance-specific costs). The paper cites WSVM but omits closely related soft-label SVM literature and does not clearly position the novelty.
- Theory is minimal: no analysis of calibration, robustness to noisy µi, or properties of the alternating scheme; no convergence guarantees beyond biconvexity; no closed-form or algorithmic details for the u-update beyond “argmin.”
- Clarity/notation issues: equations contain typos (e.g., λw·1 w⊤w instead of λw/2 · ||w||²), undefined constants, and the solver mapping from expected hinge to a standard SVM is not explained (standard solvers do not directly accept two label-dependent weights per example). Algorithm 1 omits stopping criteria and optimization details for u. Hyperparameters (λw, λR), choice of g(.), and sensitivity analyses are largely absent.
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigor in parts:
  - Proposal scoring: TAPC and NDCG are sensible, but definitions and protocol details are terse; missing standard deviations or statistical tests. The table mixes scenarios and formatting is unclear.
  - Semantic segmentation: reported accuracies are “comparable to SVR,” seemingly contradicting earlier claims of outperforming SVR; more thorough comparisons (per-class IoU, ablations) are needed.
  - Detection: The +1.5 mAP gain is promising, but the setup relative to R-CNN must be carefully matched (training data, bbox regression settings, feature dimensionality, regularization, and selection of candidate windows). No runtime or memory analysis for generating confidence maps is given. Comparisons to other segmentation-aware detection methods are missing.
- The choice of KL regularizer to ubb is ad hoc; alternative priors (e.g., squared loss, beta prior) and their impact are not explored. Using segment–box IoU as a prior for segment–mask IoU may be weak; empirical justification is limited.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level: the objective is convex in each block and the regularizer is convex. However, important implementation details (how to solve the expected hinge with off-the-shelf solvers; the u-update; hyperparameter selection; convergence behavior) are missing. Claims are moderately supported; the detection improvement is interesting but needs a stronger, more controlled evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has numerous typos, missing reference years, and incomplete notation. Sections 3.1–3.2 would benefit from a clear derivation showing equivalence to duplicating each example with labels +1 and −1 and weights g(ui) and 1−g(ui), and how to implement this with LibLinear (e.g., sample duplication and per-sample weights or a custom weighted hinge). Provide explicit algorithms for the u-update (closed-form? projected gradient? Newton?), initialization, stopping criteria, and complexity.

Originality
Incremental. The main idea (expected hinge with soft labels) is known; the latent extension with a KL prior toward a box-derived proxy is a practical twist but not conceptually deep. The spatial confidence map feature is a reasonable engineering contribution.

Significance
Moderate. Many practitioners face weak supervision; a simple, robust soft-label SVM and a straightforward way to inject segment cues into detectors could be useful. The reported +1.5 mAP over R-CNN is non-trivial for VOC07, but broader impact would require stronger evidence and positioning against related soft-label and segmentation-aware methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Related work: discuss fuzzy/probabilistic/soft-label SVMs and SVMs with uncertain labels; e.g., Lin & Wang (FSVM), Shilton et al. (probabilistic labels), and recent works on learning with label confidences/noisy labels (e.g., Natarajan et al., NeurIPS 2013), and MIL variants used for segmentation/detection.
- Provide a clear solver path: show how to convert the expected hinge to a standard SVM objective (e.g., by duplicating examples with per-instance weights), and confirm LibLinear supports this; otherwise, supply a custom optimization.
- Detail the u-update, including gradients, convexity domain (u∈(0,1)), projection, and computational cost; report convergence behavior and sensitivity to λR and initialization.
- Explore g(u): beyond identity, try calibrated mappings; report sensitivity.
- Strengthen experiments: full metrics for segmentation (mean IoU, per-class), ablations (with/without KL, different priors, different proposal generators), comparisons to logistic regression with soft labels and pairwise ranking. For detection, match R-CNN training exactly, report per-class AP, and runtime overhead.
- Correct typos/equations and improve references.

Questions for authors
1) How exactly do you implement the expected hinge with LibLinear? Do you duplicate examples with opposite labels and assign instance-specific weights? If so, how do you handle weights in LibLinear?
2) What is the optimizer for argminu in Algorithm 1, and what are typical iteration counts and convergence criteria?
3) How are λw and λR selected? Provide sensitivity plots.
4) Did you try alternative regularizers (e.g., L2, beta prior) or alternative g(u)?
5) For detection, what is the dimensionality of the spatial confidence map feature, and what is the added compute per image?

Pros
- Simple, practical formulation; easy to apply in weak supervision.
- Empirical improvements on proposal scoring and detection.
- General idea applicable beyond vision.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to soft-label/cost-sensitive SVM literature; missing citations.
- Incomplete optimization details; some clarity issues.
- Empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines/ablations and clearer protocols.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is practical and the results are promising, but the contribution is incremental and the paper needs clearer positioning, stronger empirical evidence, and more precise algorithmic details to meet NeurIPS standards.
