Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly or ambiguously labeled data, where each training instance has a “positiveness” score u in [0,1] (e.g., IoU with a ground-truth mask). The method treats the true binary label Y as Bernoulli with mean g(u) and minimizes the expected hinge loss: g(u)·max(0,1−w⊤x)+(1−g(u))·max(0,1+w⊤x) plus an L2 penalty. For weak supervision, u is latent; the paper proposes alternating minimization over w and u with a convex regularizer on u given by the cross-entropy to an initialization ubb computed from IoU against ground-truth boxes. The approach is evaluated on segment proposal ranking (with new metrics TAPC and NDCG), semantic segmentation, and object detection, where segment confidences are turned into a spatial confidence map feature and concatenated with RCNN features, giving approximately +1.2–1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of expected hinge loss under probabilistic labels; convex in w and simple to optimize; natural extension to latent u.
- Practical weakly supervised variant with a KL/cross-entropy regularizer that keeps u near its bounding-box-derived initialization.
- Useful and simple feature integration for detection via spatial confidence maps; empirically yields consistent, if modest, gains over a strong baseline (RCNN).
- New segment-ranking evaluation measures (TAPC, NDCG) better aligned with the training objective than task-specific metrics.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: The “expected hinge with probabilistic labels” formulation is closely related to prior work on SVMs with soft/probabilistic/fuzzy labels and uncertain labels; equivalently, it is the convex combination of two hinge losses and can be recast as an instance-weighted SVM on duplicated examples. The paper positions e-SVM vs instance-weighted SVM but does not acknowledge this equivalence or cite the extensive related literature (e.g., fuzzy SVMs, SVM with probabilistic labels/uncertain labels, robust SVMs with label noise). The latent extension is a standard biconvex alternating scheme with a KL prior; similar ideas appear in latent structural SVMs and posterior regularization.
- Methodological clarity: 
  - The link g(u) is fixed to g(u)=u without justification or calibration; alternatives or learned links are not explored.
  - The claim that e-SVM can be “effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver” needs a concrete reduction: in practice one requires per-instance weights and duplicated labels, or a custom solver; details are missing.
  - The update for u in the latent step is not derived; while the subproblem is convex, the closed-form or numerical procedure, complexity, and convergence criteria are not specified.
  - Regularization strength λR and its sensitivity are not reported; no ablation to show that optimizing u improves over using ubb directly.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Segment-ranking results show only small gains over SVR; variance/error bars are absent. The segmentation accuracy is reported as “comparable” to SVR, contradicting the abstract’s stronger claims.
  - The detection improvement is modest and could depend on feature engineering choices. The RCNN comparison is not fully controlled (bounding box regression is modified), and per-class tables and statistical significance are missing.
  - No comparisons to other plausible baselines better aligned with probabilistic labels, e.g., logistic regression with soft labels, calibrated SVMs, ordinal regression, or learning-to-rank approaches.
  - Generality claims (any proposals/features/classifiers; applicability beyond vision) are not validated experimentally.
- Writing and presentation:
  - Notation has errors/typos (e.g., regularizer term “λw·1/w⊤w”; likely 0.5λ||w||2). Several references are incomplete or malformed.
  - Algorithm 1 is high-level; practical details are insufficient for reproduction (initialization with multiple boxes, multi-instance handling per image, stopping criteria, runtime).
  - Related work on weakly supervised segmentation/detection and on probabilistic/fuzzy SVMs is incomplete.

Quality
The core objective is sound and convex in w; the latent variant is a reasonable biconvex alternating procedure with a convex u-regularizer. However, theoretical analysis is limited (no convergence guarantees beyond monotonicity, no generalization discussion), and several implementation details are underspecified. Empirical support is mixed: consistent but small improvements, with limited baselines and analysis.

Clarity
The main idea is understandable, but the paper suffers from notational errors, incomplete algorithmic detail, and weak connections to related work, which impede reproducibility and proper positioning.

Originality
Limited. The expected-hinge formulation with probabilistic/soft labels and the equivalence to instance-weighted SVM variants are known; the latent extension with a KL prior is a straightforward application of alternating minimization. The spatial confidence map feature is simple and reasonable but incremental.

Significance
The problem is important, and any simple method that plugs into standard solvers and yields gains on PASCAL is valuable. However, the incremental nature of the contribution and modest empirical gains limit impact, especially given missing comparisons to more appropriate baselines.

Questions for the authors
- How does e-SVM compare to fuzzy SVMs and SVMs with probabilistic labels (please cite and compare)? Can you show the explicit reduction to instance-weighted SVM on duplicated examples?
- How sensitive are results to g(u) and λR? Do you learn g or calibrate u?
- What is the exact procedure and complexity for optimizing u? Do you use closed-form updates per instance or numerical methods?
- Does optimizing u outperform simply fixing u=ubb? Please add this ablation.
- Provide controlled RCNN comparisons without modifying bounding box regression and include per-class AP improvements with significance tests.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important weakly supervised setting with a simple, practical method.
- Clean objective; easy to implement and integrate.
- Demonstrates improvements on standard benchmarks.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty over existing “soft/probabilistic label SVM” literature is weak and not well-situated.
- Empirical gains are modest; baselines and ablations are insufficient.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; several notational and bibliographic errors.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sensible and practical, but the contribution is incremental and underdeveloped relative to existing literature. Strengthening related-work positioning, adding key baselines and ablations, clarifying optimization details, and tightening the empirical analysis would substantially improve the paper.
