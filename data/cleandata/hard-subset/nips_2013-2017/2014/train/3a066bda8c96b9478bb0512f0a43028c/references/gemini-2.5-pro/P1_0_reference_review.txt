### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" values within the [0, 1] interval (e.g., Intersection over Union - IoU). The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a training example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is the given positiveness value. The SVM objective is then formulated to minimize the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. This elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM-like problem that can be solved with standard solvers.

The paper further extends this to a more weakly supervised setting where the positiveness values themselves are unknown (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this "latent e-SVM," the positiveness values are treated as latent variables and are optimized alternately with the model weights. A regularization term based on KL-divergence is introduced to prevent the latent variables from collapsing to 0 or 1.

The authors validate their approach on two computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For the latter, they propose a novel "spatial confidence map feature" to integrate segment-level predictions from e-SVM into a window-based detector like R-CNN. Experimental results show that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) on a segment proposal classification task, and that integrating its output significantly improves the performance of the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation (Originality & Quality):** The core e-SVM formulation is the paper's main strength. It provides a theoretically sound and elegant way to handle continuous-valued labels in a max-margin framework. It directly addresses the shortcomings of ad-hoc approaches like thresholding for SVC or using SVR, which is designed for unbounded regression. The interpretation of the label as a random variable leading to an expected loss is a clean and well-motivated contribution.

2.  **Significant Empirical Results (Significance):** The improvement on the object detection task is substantial. Achieving a 1.5% absolute AP gain over a very strong and contemporary baseline like R-CNN on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset is a significant result. This demonstrates that the proposed segmentation-based features provide complementary information to the powerful CNN features and that the e-SVM is effective at learning these features from weak bounding-box supervision.

3.  **Methodological Contribution in Evaluation (Quality):** The authors identify a gap in how to evaluate segment proposal classification, where the target is a continuous value. Their introduction of the Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and the use of Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) are thoughtful contributions that allow for a more direct and nuanced evaluation of the model's performance on its direct task, independent of the downstream application.

4.  **Generality and Flexibility (Significance):** The proposed e-SVM framework is general. As the authors note, it is not tied to a specific proposal generator, feature type, or base detector. This makes the core idea potentially applicable to a wide range of problems beyond the specific instantiations shown in the paper, including other domains where soft or probabilistic labels are common.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Analysis for Latent e-SVM Optimization (Quality):** The optimization for the latent e-SVM is an alternating minimization scheme over a non-convex objective. While this is a standard heuristic, the paper provides no discussion of its convergence properties, sensitivity to initialization, or computational cost (e.g., number of iterations to converge). A more thorough analysis or at least an empirical study of these aspects would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Unconvincing Semantic Segmentation Results (Quality):** The paper reports that for semantic segmentation, the e-SVM's performance is "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art" (SVR). This is a weak claim that somewhat undermines the demonstrated superiority of e-SVM on the direct segment classification task (measured by TAPC and NDCG). The paper would be stronger if it either showed a clear win on segmentation or provided some analysis as to why the superior segment-level scores do not translate into improved pixel-level accuracy in their pipeline.

3.  **Missing Implementation Details (Clarity & Reproducibility):** Some key details for reproducibility are sparse. For example, when creating the spatial confidence map feature, the process of encoding it "by additive kernels approximation mapping" is mentioned without specifying the kernel or its parameters. Similarly, the exact mechanism for concatenating this new feature with the R-CNN features (e.g., relative weighting, normalization) is not described.

4.  **Positioning Relative to Weighted SVM (Clarity & Originality):** The e-SVM loss can be implemented by creating two data points for each original sample `(x_i, u_i)`: one positive sample `(x_i, +1)` with weight `u_i` and one negative sample `(x_i, -1)` with weight `1-u_i`. While the paper correctly distinguishes its motivation from prior work on weighted SVMs (which typically weight data points, not labels), the resulting optimization problem is mechanically very similar. A more explicit discussion of this connection would better position the work and clarify its technical novelty.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core e-SVM model is a novel, well-motivated, and elegant solution to the important problem of learning from soft labels.
*   The paper demonstrates significant, state-of-the-art performance improvement on a challenging and important task (object detection) by building on a very strong baseline.
*   The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC) for the intermediate task is a valuable methodological contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The optimization for the latent model lacks theoretical or empirical analysis regarding convergence.
*   The results on the semantic segmentation task are weak and not well explained.
*   Some implementation details are missing, which could hinder reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty of the e-SVM formulation and the strong empirical results in object detection, outweigh its weaknesses. This is a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, be encouraged to address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version, especially by providing more details on the latent model's optimization and the feature construction for object detection.
