Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), which replaces binary labels with “positiveness” scores in [0,1] and minimizes the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with mean µi=g(ui) (taken as µi=ui, e.g., IoU). This yields a soft-label, cost-weighted hinge objective solvable by standard linear SVM solvers. A latent variant is introduced for weak supervision (only bounding boxes), where the ui are latent and regularized towards an initialization from segment–box IoU via a convex KL/cross-entropy term; optimization alternates between w and u (each block convex). Applications include (i) segment scoring for semantic segmentation with CPMC+O2P features, and (ii) object detection by deriving a per-pixel “spatial confidence map” from segment scores and pooling it into m×m bins per candidate box, then concatenating with RCNN features. The paper also proposes two segment-scoring metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and reports that e-SVM outperforms SVC and slightly outperforms SVR in segment ranking; achieves 36.8% vs 27.7% accuracy on semantic segmentation in strong vs weak settings; and improves RCNN mAP on VOC07 by ~1.5 points after bounding-box regression.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits between fully supervised segment scoring (e.g., regression on IoU or binarized classification) and MIL-style image-level supervision, arguing that neither SVC nor SVR matches the bounded “positiveness” setting. The latent formulation is reminiscent of latent SVM/EM with additional convex regularization. There is relevant prior work on weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs, SVMs with probabilistic/soft labels, learning with label noise/label proportions, and weakly supervised segmentation/detection, some of which is cited, but the connection to “soft-label SVM” formulations is underdeveloped.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive formulation: expected hinge loss with soft labels and a block-convex latent extension that remains solvable with standard SVM tooling.
- Practical: plugs into standard pipelines (CPMC, O2P, RCNN) and shows non-trivial gains in detection on VOC07.
- The spatial confidence map feature is a clear, implementable way to inject segment-level cues into box-based detectors.
- Proposes task-agnostic segment-scoring metrics (TAPC, NDCG) that better reflect the ranking nature of the problem than binarized classification.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: minimizing expected hinge loss with µi∈[0,1] is essentially a soft-label/cost-weighted SVM; closely related ideas exist (probabilistic labels for SVM, cost-sensitive SVMs, learning with soft labels/label proportions). The paper does not clearly position itself against these lines or clarify what is fundamentally new beyond the specific regularizer and application.
- The latent scheme is heuristic: g(ui)=ui is fixed; identifiability and calibration are not analyzed; optimization details for the u-update are omitted (closed form? constraints? step size?), and there is no convergence behavior or sensitivity analysis (e.g., λR).
- Clarity and rigor issues: equations are misformatted (regularizer term), several references are incomplete or malformed, and many implementation details are missing (hyperparameters, stopping criteria, initialization, runtime). The new metrics are introduced but not thoroughly validated (e.g., correlation with downstream performance).
- Experimental evidence is mixed/incomplete: e-SVM is “slightly better” than SVR on segment ranking but semantic segmentation results are only “comparable” to SVR; significance/variance is not reported. The detection improvement is promising but lacks ablations (e.g., using SVR scores in the same spatial map pipeline; effect of latent vs non-latent; effect of m, pooling choice; contribution of modified bbox regression). Baseline fairness is unclear given modifications to RCNN features.
- Claims of generality are not matched by breadth of evaluation (one segmentation pipeline and one detector; limited comparison to other weakly supervised segmentation/detection methods).

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level (block-convex objective; sensible regularizer), but theoretical support (generalization, calibration of expected hinge with soft labels) is absent. Optimization details for the u-step are missing. Empirical claims are supported by results but not with sufficient ablation or statistical rigor.

Clarity
- The paper needs significant editing: fix equations (e.g., regularization term notation), provide all hyperparameters (λ, λR, g(.)), convergence criteria, and algorithmic details; clean up references; better describe TAPC and NDCG computation protocols; and clarify conflicting claims (e.g., “slightly better than SVR” vs “comparable”).

Originality
- Moderate to low. The core idea aligns with known soft-label/weighted SVM formulations; the latent extension with KL regularization and the spatial confidence map feature are incremental.

Significance
- Practically useful if the reported +1.5 mAP over RCNN holds robustly, but the absence of strong ablations and clarity on baseline parity weakens the case. Segment-scoring improvements over SVR are small. As a method, its simplicity could encourage adoption, but the contribution is incremental for NeurIPS.

Questions for the authors
- How do you solve the u-update in Eq. (2) with KL regularization? Is there a closed form per ui? What are the constraints and stopping criteria?
- Sensitivity to λR and to the choice g(u)=u? Did you try learning or calibrating g?
- Can you report ablations comparing spatial confidence maps built from SVR scores vs e-SVM, and from non-latent vs latent e-SVM?
- Are the RCNN baselines reimplemented with the same bbox regression modification you introduce? Please ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Report variance across runs and statistical significance for TAPC/NDCG and mAP improvements.

Suggestions
- Strengthen positioning vis-à-vis soft-label/probabilistic-label SVMs and cost-sensitive SVMs; add appropriate citations and clarify novelty.
- Provide full optimization details, hyperparameters, and code for reproducibility.
- Add ablations and analyses to isolate where gains come from.
- Improve writing, notation, and references substantially.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method; easy to implement; promising empirical gains on a high-profile detector; could be of practical interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; clarity issues; missing ablations and theoretical/empirical rigor; unclear baseline fairness.

Overall assessment
Borderline reject. The idea is practical and may yield gains, but the novelty is limited and the paper, as written, does not meet NeurIPS standards for clarity and experimental rigor. With stronger positioning, cleaner presentation, and thorough ablations, it could be competitive.
