### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, termed the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), for learning from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, referred to as "positiveness". This scenario is common in computer vision, where an object proposal's quality is often measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea is to model the binary label of a training sample as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is its known positiveness score. The e-SVM then minimizes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random label. This elegantly results in a weighted SVM-like objective where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative class loss terms, weighted by its positiveness `u` and `1-u` respectively.

The paper extends this model to a weakly supervised setting (the "latent e-SVM"), where the positiveness scores are unknown and treated as latent variables. This is particularly relevant for tasks where only bounding box annotations are available. The latent positiveness scores are estimated via an alternating optimization procedure, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on two key computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For segment classification, they introduce new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and show that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR. For object detection, they integrate features derived from a latent e-SVM into the state-of-the-art R-CNN model, achieving a notable 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The proposed e-SVM is a novel and elegant solution to the problem of learning from soft labels. Instead of resorting to ad-hoc thresholding (for SVC) or using a regression formulation (SVR) that ignores the bounded nature of the target, e-SVM provides a principled classification framework. The probabilistic interpretation of the label as a random variable is intuitive, and the resulting expected loss objective is well-motivated.

2.  **Significance and Practicality:** The work addresses the highly significant problem of reducing the cost of data annotation in machine learning. By effectively leveraging weak supervision (bounding boxes), the method offers a practical way to train powerful models without requiring expensive pixel-level labels. The demonstrated improvement over a very strong baseline like R-CNN highlights its practical value and potential for real-world impact. The formulation is also general and could be applied to other domains beyond computer vision.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors not only show performance gains on downstream tasks but also conduct a direct evaluation of the core segment classification problem. The introduction of the TAPC and NDCG metrics for this task is a valuable contribution in its own right, allowing for a more nuanced comparison than task-specific metrics. The consistent outperformance of e-SVM over SVC and SVR across different settings strongly supports the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-established, the mathematical formulation is easy to follow, and the connection to and distinction from related work (MIL, weighted SVMs) is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Details of the Latent Model Optimization:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is presented at a high level.
    *   **Convergence:** The paper states the algorithm runs "until Converge". As this is an alternating minimization procedure for a non-convex joint objective function L(w, u), convergence to a global minimum is not guaranteed. A brief discussion of the convergence properties (e.g., that the objective is non-increasing and guaranteed to converge to a stationary point) would strengthen the technical soundness of the paper.
    *   **Update Step for `u`:** The update step `u(new) ← argmin_u L(w(new),u)` is a key part of the algorithm. Given the form of the objective in Eq. 2 and the KL-divergence regularizer in Eq. 3, this optimization problem likely has a simple, closed-form solution. It would significantly improve clarity and reproducibility if the authors provided this analytical solution or described the optimization procedure for `u` in more detail.

2.  **Model Assumptions and Hyperparameters:**
    *   **Choice of `g(u)`:** The assumption that the success probability `µi` is equal to the positiveness `ui` (i.e., `g(u) = u`) is simple and effective. However, it would be beneficial to include a short discussion on this choice. Is the model sensitive to this assumption? Could other monotonic functions be used, and what might their effect be?
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The model includes regularization parameters `λ` and `λR`. The paper lacks details on how these crucial hyperparameters were selected. A brief description of the validation strategy for setting these parameters would improve the completeness and reproducibility of the work.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The proposed method adds an extra component to the R-CNN pipeline (training the latent e-SVM, extracting spatial confidence maps). An analysis of the additional computational overhead, both in terms of training time and inference time, would be a valuable addition. This would help practitioners assess the trade-off between the performance gain and the increased complexity.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and well-motivated loss function for learning from weakly supervised and soft-labeled data.
*   Addresses a problem of high significance and practical importance in machine learning.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results, including a clear improvement over a state-of-the-art method (R-CNN) on a competitive benchmark.
*   The technical quality is high, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Some technical details regarding the optimization of the latent model could be elaborated for better clarity and reproducibility.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion on hyperparameter selection and the computational cost of the proposed method.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM framework is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically powerful. The strengths, particularly the originality of the method and its significant practical impact, far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to missing details that can be easily addressed in a final version. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
