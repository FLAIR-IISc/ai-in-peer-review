This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach to learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing scenarios where proposals for the true label exist with associated confidence scores (or “positiveness”) that may be latent. The core idea – weighting samples by their estimated correctness – is a clever extension of standard SVM formulations and offers a principled way to handle the ambiguity inherent in weakly supervised settings. The application to semantic segmentation and object detection, coupled with improvements over SVR and SVC baselines, demonstrates the potential of the approach.

The novelty is a key strength. While weighted SVMs exist, the framing of weighting *labels* based on an expectation of correctness, especially in the context of segment proposals and continuous positiveness scores, feels genuinely new. The ability to seamlessly handle both strongly and weakly supervised cases through a latent variable formulation is also a significant advantage. The exploration of the method within two established computer vision tasks is appropriate and provides a reasonable validation of the concept. The connection to existing work, particularly MIL and weighted SVMs, is clearly articulated, successfully positioning the contribution within the existing literature.

However, the paper suffers from some shortcomings, particularly regarding the depth of analysis and experimental validation. The regularization term in the latent variable formulation (Equation 3) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While justified as preventing ui from collapsing to 0 or 1, a more thorough exploration of alternative regularization strategies or a sensitivity analysis to the regularization parameter would be beneficial. The improvements over SVR and SVC, while present, are not overwhelming. A more detailed ablation study, isolating the contribution of different components (e.g., the specific form of the positiveness function g(ui)) would strengthen the claims.

The experiments, while demonstrating positive results, lack a critical assessment of failure cases. What types of proposals does e-SVM struggle with? Where does the performance gap between e-SVM and the baselines widen or narrow? The paper relies heavily on established datasets (PASCAL VOC), which limits the demonstration of generalizability. Testing on more diverse datasets or tasks would enhance the significance of the work. Furthermore, while the spatial confidence map feature concatenation in object detection yields a 1.5% AP improvement, a deeper analysis of *why* this works would be valuable. Is the spatial confidence map truly capturing useful information, or is it simply providing an additional feature dimension?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a clever and potentially impactful extension of standard SVMs for weakly supervised learning.
*   **Principled approach:** The expectation loss concept provides a theoretically sound way to handle continuous confidence scores.
*   **Positive empirical results:** The paper demonstrates improvements over existing methods in relevant computer vision tasks.
*   **Generality:** The method is presented as being applicable to a wide range of problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest performance gains:** The improvements over baselines, while statistically significant, are not dramatic.
*   **Limited analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough investigation of failure cases, ablation studies, and sensitivity analyses.
*   **Dataset dependence:** The experiments are restricted to a single, well-studied dataset.
*   **Ad-hoc regularization:** The regularization term in the latent variable formulation feels somewhat arbitrary.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite some methodological imperfections and limited validation, I lean towards acceptance. The novelty of the e-SVM formulation and its potential impact on weakly supervised learning are significant. The paper presents a conceptually sound idea with promising preliminary results. While more thorough experimentation and analysis would undoubtedly strengthen the work, the boldness of the approach and the potential for future research warrant acceptance. The authors are tackling a challenging problem with a fresh perspective, and that is something NIPS should encourage. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed analysis of failure cases and an exploration of alternative regularization strategies.
