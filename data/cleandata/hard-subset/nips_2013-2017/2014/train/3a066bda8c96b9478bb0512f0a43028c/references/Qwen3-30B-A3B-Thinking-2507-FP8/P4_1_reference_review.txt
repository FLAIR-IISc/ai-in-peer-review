

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

The paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data, where training examples have continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU scores between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors present both a strong supervision version (where positiveness is observed) and a weak supervision extension (where positiveness is latent). They demonstrate applications to semantic segmentation and object detection, using PASCAL VOC datasets as benchmarks. The approach builds on SVM formulations but modifies the loss function to account for continuous labels, contrasting with related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs. The paper correctly positions its contribution as distinct from MIL (which requires at least one positive instance per bag) and from standard weighted SVMs (which weight examples rather than labels).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM loss function (Equation 1) is well-motivated and logically derived. The latent extension (Equation 2) and optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) are clearly presented and theoretically sound. The authors demonstrate convergence properties and provide a reasonable regularization approach to prevent the latent variables from collapsing to 0/1.

2. **Experimental Rigor:** The paper introduces two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically designed for the segment proposal classification task, addressing a gap in the literature. The experiments are thorough, comparing against appropriate baselines (SVC with different thresholds and SVR) across both strong and weak supervision settings. The object detection results (1.5% AP improvement over RCNN) are non-trivial and well-documented.

3. **Practical Significance:** The problem addressed (reducing labeling effort through weak supervision) is highly relevant to computer vision. The method's flexibility to work with existing segment proposal detectors and feature extractors makes it immediately applicable to real-world systems. The paper's application to state-of-the-art object detection (RCNN) demonstrates practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty Concerns:** While the core idea is sound, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work on "soft labels" or "probabilistic labels" in segmentation. The approach resembles the "soft SVM" or "probabilistic SVM" formulations (e.g., in [17, 23]), which also handle continuous labels. The authors should more explicitly contrast with these methods and clarify the specific novelty of their expectation-based loss.

2. **Clarity Issues:** The connection between "positiveness" and the actual segmentation/detection task could be clearer. The paper states that "positiveness is set as IoU," but doesn't thoroughly explain why IoU is the appropriate measure for the learning task. The spatial confidence map feature (Section 4.2) is described without sufficient detail on how it handles partial overlaps between segments and candidate windows.

3. **Weak Supervision Justification:** The paper claims their problem setting is "in the middle between MIL and strong supervision," but doesn't adequately justify why MIL cannot be adapted to this scenario. The argument against MIL (that "most proposals will contain both foreground and background pixels") is valid, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach specifically overcomes this limitation better than MIL adaptations.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The e-SVM formulation is technically sound and provides a principled approach to handling continuous labels.
- The method demonstrates clear empirical improvements over baselines (SVC and SVR) on standard benchmarks.
- The paper introduces useful evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for segment proposal classification.
- The applications to semantic segmentation and object detection are well-motivated and demonstrate practical value.

*Con:*
- The novelty is somewhat limited compared to existing work on probabilistic SVMs and soft labels.
- The connection to prior work on similar problems is not sufficiently established.
- The weak supervision extension, while reasonable, doesn't appear to be a major conceptual leap from standard latent SVM approaches.
- The performance gains (1.5% AP) are modest compared to recent advances in object detection.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed method for handling continuous labels in weakly supervised learning. The core idea has merit, and the empirical results are convincing for the specific problem of segment proposal classification. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by overlap with existing approaches for handling probabilistic labels in SVMs. The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, but it doesn't significantly advance the state of the art beyond incremental improvements over existing techniques.

The paper meets NIPS quality standards in terms of technical soundness and experimental rigor. The significance is moderate, as it addresses a practical problem but with a method that has limited conceptual novelty. The clarity is generally good, though some sections could be improved with more precise explanations of key concepts.

Given the focus of NIPS on significant theoretical and algorithmic advances, this paper leans more toward a solid engineering contribution than a groundbreaking scientific advance. It would be a good fit for a conference emphasizing applied machine learning, but for NIPS, the novelty and significance could be stronger. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the incremental nature of the contribution makes it a borderline case for acceptance.
