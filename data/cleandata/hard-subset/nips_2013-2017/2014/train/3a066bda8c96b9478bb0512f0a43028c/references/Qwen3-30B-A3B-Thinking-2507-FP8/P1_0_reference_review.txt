

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised settings where labels are continuous values (e.g., Intersection over Union scores) rather than binary labels. The authors propose modeling the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the observed "positiveness" value (for strong supervision) or as a latent variable (for weak supervision). The approach extends standard SVMs to handle continuous label values directly, avoiding the need for arbitrary thresholding required by SVC or the inappropriate application of SVR to bounded output spaces. The authors validate their method on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using PASCAL VOC datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The work relates to recent advances in weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs. However, the authors correctly note that their problem setting differs from MIL (where at least one positive instance is assumed to exist in a positive bag) and from standard weighted SVMs (which weight data points rather than labels). This paper contributes a principled approach to handle continuous labels in bounded intervals, which is an important gap given the prevalence of bounding box annotations in large-scale datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem formulation**: The paper effectively identifies a significant gap in the literature—standard SVM approaches require binary labels or thresholding, which is suboptimal for continuous label values. The motivation is strong and well-justified.

2. **Novel technical contribution**: The e-SVM formulation is genuinely novel. The authors provide a principled way to incorporate continuous labels directly into the SVM framework without arbitrary thresholding. The latent extension for weak supervision is a meaningful contribution.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (SVC with multiple thresholds, SVR) on two important computer vision tasks. The results consistently show improvements over baselines across both tasks.

4. **Practical significance**: The method is simple to implement (using standard SVM solvers) and provides meaningful improvements on standard benchmarks, making it valuable for practitioners. The authors demonstrate how to integrate their approach with state-of-the-art object detection systems.

5. **Well-organized presentation**: The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and provides sufficient detail for understanding the methodology and experiments.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited theoretical analysis**: While the authors mention convexity, they provide no formal theoretical guarantees about convergence, optimality, or generalization bounds. For a NIPS paper, stronger theoretical justification would be expected.

2. **Incomplete comparison with related work**: The paper mentions MIL but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other weakly supervised learning approaches that handle partial annotations (e.g., [19, 20, 22]). The authors should discuss how their approach relates to recent advances in weakly supervised learning.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to understand the contribution of key components, such as the regularization term R(u) or the choice of g(ui)=ui. This makes it difficult to assess the necessity of these design choices.

4. **Modest performance gains**: The 1.5% average AP improvement on object detection is modest, and the paper doesn't establish statistical significance for this improvement. The semantic segmentation results (27.7% accuracy with bounding box annotations) are not compared to state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods, only to SVR.

5. **Implementation details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., number of iterations in the alternating optimization, how hyperparameters were chosen, specific implementation details of the spatial confidence map feature).

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**For acceptance:**
- The problem is well-motivated and addresses a significant practical challenge in computer vision (reducing annotation effort).
- The technical approach is novel and well-justified by empirical results.
- The method is simple to implement and integrates well with existing systems.
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Against acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is limited, lacking formal guarantees about the approach.
- The comparison with relevant weakly supervised learning literature is incomplete.
- The performance gains, while consistent, are modest (1.5% AP improvement).
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- The method appears to be a relatively straightforward extension of standard SVMs rather than a major conceptual advance.

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed and clearly presented paper that addresses a practical problem in computer vision with a novel but relatively simple extension to standard SVMs. The empirical results are strong and consistent, demonstrating the value of the approach. However, the lack of substantial theoretical analysis and the modest performance gains (compared to the state of the art in computer vision) are significant drawbacks for a NIPS paper.

The paper makes a useful contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, but it does not significantly advance the theoretical understanding of the problem or provide transformative improvements over existing methods. The approach is practical and could be valuable to practitioners, but it may not represent the kind of high-impact contribution expected at NIPS.

## Recommendation
I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem, the theoretical contributions are limited and the performance gains, while consistent, are not sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors should strengthen the theoretical analysis, provide more comprehensive comparisons with relevant weakly supervised learning literature, and include ablation studies to better understand the contribution of different components before reconsidering for acceptance.
