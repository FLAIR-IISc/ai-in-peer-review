

# Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) Algorithm

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm for weakly supervised settings where training data has continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels. The authors formulate the problem by treating labels as random variables following a Bernoulli distribution, with success probability proportional to the positiveness metric. The approach extends naturally to weak supervision by treating positiveness as latent variables. The paper demonstrates applications to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC datasets, comparing against SVC and SVR baselines.

The work relates to existing weakly supervised learning methods, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. However, the authors correctly note that their setting differs from standard MIL (where at least one positive instance must exist in a bag) and differs from instance-weighted SVMs by weighting labels rather than data points.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a clear mathematical formulation of the e-SVM model. The derivation of the expectation loss function (Equation 1) is logically sound and well-justified. The optimization algorithm for the latent case (Algorithm 1) is straightforward and correctly handles the convex optimization problem.

The experimental evaluation is appropriate for the problem, with careful comparisons to SVC and SVR using two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The results on segment proposal confidence prediction are consistently better for e-SVM than the baselines. For semantic segmentation, the method achieves comparable results to state-of-the-art (36.8% with pixel-level annotations), and for object detection with RCNN, it provides a modest 1.5% average AP improvement.

However, the paper could better address limitations. The 1.5% AP improvement is modest in the context of modern object detection (where 1-2% improvements are often considered significant), and the paper doesn't thoroughly explore why e-SVM outperforms SVR despite both being margin-based methods. The comparison to more recent object detection methods (beyond RCNN) would strengthen the significance claim.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and clearly explains the problem and proposed solution. The mathematical formulations are presented with sufficient detail. However, some aspects could be clarified:

- The distinction between e-SVM and existing weighted SVM methods (citing [23, 27]) needs more precise explanation. The authors state "the difference... lies in that it weights labels instead of data points," but don't fully elaborate how this difference leads to practical advantages.

- The implementation details for the object detection application (e.g., how the spatial confidence map features are integrated with RCNN) could be more specific.

- The regularization term in Equation 3 is well-motivated, but the paper doesn't discuss how the regularization strength λR is chosen in practice.

## Originality

The core contribution is the expectation loss formulation for SVMs with continuous-valued labels. While the idea of using probabilistic interpretations of labels isn't entirely new, the specific formulation as an expectation loss is novel and well-motivated for the weak supervision setting. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for why this approach works better than standard SVC (which requires thresholding) or SVR (which isn't designed for bounded targets).

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing work. The connection to weighted SVMs is superficial, and the paper doesn't adequately address how their method differs from other approaches that use continuous labels (e.g., the "learning with noisy labels" literature). The application to segmentation and detection is standard, not novel.

## Significance

The problem of weakly supervised learning is highly relevant, especially as large datasets like ImageNet become more prevalent but pixel-level annotations remain expensive. The paper makes a useful contribution to this problem space by providing a principled approach for handling continuous label values.

The modest 1.5% AP improvement in object detection is significant in the context of a mature field where gains are often small. However, the paper could better argue why this improvement is meaningful compared to other approaches. The authors note they achieve "state-of-the-art" performance, but without comparison to more recent object detection methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN), this claim is difficult to verify.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem formulation for weakly supervised learning with continuous labels
- Sound mathematical derivation of the e-SVM model
- Well-designed experiments with appropriate baselines
- Novel evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for segment classification
- Appropriate application to two important computer vision tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Improvements over baselines are modest (1.5% AP in object detection)
- Limited comparison to more recent methods in the object detection literature
- Insufficient distinction from related work (weighted SVMs, MIL)
- Superficial discussion of limitations and failure cases
- Lack of thorough analysis of why e-SVM outperforms SVR

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels. While the contribution is incremental rather than revolutionary, it's a solid methodological improvement that addresses a relevant problem in computer vision. The paper is clearly written and well-supported by experiments.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Strengthen the comparison to more recent object detection methods
2. Provide a clearer distinction between e-SVM and existing weighted SVM work
3. Discuss limitations and potential failure cases more thoroughly
4. Provide more insight into why e-SVM outperforms SVR

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to weakly supervised learning, which is an important area for practical applications where labeling is expensive. While the improvements are modest, the approach is principled and could be useful for other weakly supervised problems beyond computer vision.
