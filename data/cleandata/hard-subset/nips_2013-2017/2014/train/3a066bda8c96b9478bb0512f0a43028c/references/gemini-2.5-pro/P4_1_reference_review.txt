### Summary

This paper introduces the "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]), rather than binary. The motivation stems from computer vision tasks where obtaining precise pixel-level annotations is costly, but weaker labels like bounding boxes are available.

The core idea of e-SVM is to model the true, unobserved binary label of a training sample as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its known "positiveness" value (e.g., Intersection over Union - IoU). The e-SVM loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random label. This formulation results in a convex optimization problem that can be solved by standard SVM solvers.

The paper extends this to a more practical, weakly supervised scenario with the "latent e-SVM". Here, the positiveness values themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available for image segments). The positiveness is treated as a latent variable, and the model is trained by alternating between optimizing the SVM weights and inferring the latent positiveness values. The inference step is guided by a regularization term that encourages the latent values to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The authors validate e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, they propose a novel "spatial confidence map feature" derived from the latent e-SVM scores of segment proposals. This feature is used to augment the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, leading to significant performance gains on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark. The work is positioned relative to prior approaches like standard Support Vector Classification (SVC), Support Vector Regression (SVR), and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), clearly distinguishing its problem setting and methodology.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Elegance of the Formulation:** The e-SVM model is a novel, principled, and elegant way to handle continuous-valued labels in a classification setting. By reformulating the hinge loss as an expectation over a random binary label, it provides a well-motivated alternative to common heuristics like thresholding the continuous labels for SVC or using SVR, which is not designed for bounded outputs. The extension to the latent e-SVM is a logical and practical step for handling weak supervision.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Key Task:** The paper demonstrates significant practical value by applying the latent e-SVM to object detection. The proposed spatial confidence map feature, when combined with the very strong R-CNN baseline, yields a 1.5% absolute improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on PASCAL VOC 2007. Improving upon such a state-of-the-art model is a non-trivial achievement and underscores the significance of the contribution.

3.  **Thorough and Direct Evaluation:** The authors introduce two new evaluation criteria for the intermediate task of segment proposal classification: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). This is a valuable contribution, as it allows for a more direct and task-agnostic assessment of the model's ability to rank proposals. The experiments using these metrics clearly show that e-SVM outperforms both SVC (across a range of thresholds) and SVR.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the model is developed logically from the basic to the latent case, and the connection to related work is well-articulated. The overall framework and its applications are explained effectively.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / for revision):**

1.  **Limited Justification for Design Choices:** In the latent e-SVM model, the regularization term `R(u)` is based on the KL-divergence between Bernoulli distributions. While this choice is reasonable, the paper does not provide a strong justification for it over other potential convex regularizers (e.g., a simple squared L2 norm). A brief discussion or ablation study on the choice of this regularizer would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of Convergence Details:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM is described as an alternating minimization procedure that runs "until Converge" (Algorithm 1). However, the paper provides no details on the convergence criteria used in practice, nor does it include any theoretical discussion or guarantee of convergence for this biconvex optimization problem.

3.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Contributions:** In the object detection experiments (Section 5.3), the authors mention modifying the bounding box regression of the R-CNN baseline. It is unclear if the reported 1.5% mAP gain is solely due to the addition of the e-SVM spatial confidence map feature or if this separate modification also contributes. The contributions should be disentangled to clearly isolate the impact of the proposed e-SVM method.

4.  **Weaker Results in Semantic Segmentation:** The paper reports that for semantic segmentation, the performance of e-SVM is "comparable" to that of SVR. While not a negative result, it is less compelling than the strong improvements shown in the direct proposal classification and object detection tasks. Providing the exact performance numbers for both methods would allow for a more precise comparison and add transparency.

### Overall Assessment

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated learning algorithm, e-SVM, for a relevant and challenging problem in weakly supervised learning. The formulation is technically sound, and the extension to the latent case is practical. The primary strength of the work lies in its significant empirical success in improving a state-of-the-art object detection system, which demonstrates clear significance and impact. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the justification of certain design choices and a lack of detail on convergence and experimental setup, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is a solid and valuable piece of work that advances the field.
