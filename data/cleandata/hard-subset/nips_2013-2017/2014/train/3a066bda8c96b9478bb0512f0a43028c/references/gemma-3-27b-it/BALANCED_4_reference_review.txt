This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where proposals for the true label are available with associated "positiveness" scores. The core idea is to treat these positiveness scores as potentially latent variables and formulate an SVM loss function that accounts for the uncertainty in these scores. The paper demonstrates the algorithm’s performance on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming standard SVC and SVR approaches, and achieving competitive results when integrated into a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN).

The paper tackles a relevant and important problem in computer vision – learning from weakly labeled data. The motivation is clear and well-articulated, given the cost of acquiring fully annotated datasets. The proposed e-SVM builds upon established machine learning techniques (SVMs) but introduces a novel loss function tailored to the specific challenges of weakly supervised learning with continuous correctness estimates. The experimental results, particularly the 1.5% average improvement in AP on object detection using R-CNN, are promising. The algorithm appears to be flexible, capable of handling both strongly and weakly supervised settings and accommodating various segment proposal methods and image features. The connection to related work, including weighted SVMs and Multiple Instance Learning, is discussed, and the paper clearly distinguishes its contribution.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the formulation of the expectation loss is a reasonable adaptation of the SVM framework, the core concept of weighting examples based on their confidence or correctness is not entirely novel (see weighted SVMs mentioned in the related work). The paper needs to more strongly emphasize *how* this weighting differs from existing approaches and *why* it’s particularly effective in the specific context of segment proposals and continuous positiveness scores.
* **Clarity of the Latent Variable Handling:** The explanation of the latent variable handling (Section 3.2) is somewhat dense. The iterative optimization process could benefit from a more intuitive explanation and potentially a graphical illustration.  The regularization term’s rationale and impact aren't fully clear, and the connection to preventing the algorithm from getting stuck in local minima is not thoroughly explained. The choice of the KL divergence as the regularization term seems somewhat arbitrary – why was this chosen over other options?
* **Experimental Detail and Ablation Studies:** While the experiments demonstrate performance improvements, the level of detail is somewhat limited.  Specifically, the impact of different regularization parameters (lambda R) needs to be explored.  An ablation study demonstrating the contribution of each component (e.g., the spatial confidence map feature in object detection) would be valuable.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the segment proposal method (CPMC vs. superpixels)?
* **Comparison to More Recent Baselines:**  The comparison to R-CNN, while relevant, is somewhat dated. More recent object detection frameworks (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO, SSD) would provide a more challenging and informative benchmark.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the e-SVM approach. For example, it doesn’t address the potential sensitivity to the quality of the segment proposals – what happens when the proposals are poor?



**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:**  Learning from weak supervision is a critical area of research.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:**  The algorithm is built upon a sound theoretical foundation.
* **Promising Experimental Results:**  The performance gains on semantic segmentation and object detection are encouraging.
* **Flexibility and Generality:** The algorithm's ability to work with different segment proposal methods and image features is a plus.
* **Clear Presentation (Overall):** The paper is generally well-written and organized.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core idea is not entirely novel, and the paper needs to more clearly articulate its unique contribution.
* The explanation of the latent variable handling is somewhat complex.
* The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and ablation studies.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**.  While the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, the concerns regarding the limited novelty, the clarity of the latent variable handling, and the lack of thorough experimentation prevent me from recommending acceptance.  However, the idea is promising and has the potential to be a valuable contribution with further refinement and more comprehensive evaluation. I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference. Specifically, a stronger emphasis on the *novelty* of the approach, a more detailed explanation of the latent variable handling, and a more thorough experimental evaluation (including ablation studies and comparisons to more recent baselines) would significantly improve the paper's quality.
