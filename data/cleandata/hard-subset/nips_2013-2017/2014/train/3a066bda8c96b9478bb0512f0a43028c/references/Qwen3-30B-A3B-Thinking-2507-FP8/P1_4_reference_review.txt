

# Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

## Summary

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data is labeled with continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap ratios) rather than binary labels. The e-SVM treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, with the success probability being a function of the positiveness value. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM model for weak supervision scenarios where positiveness values are unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The authors apply their method to two fundamental computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. Their approach outperforms SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets, with a 1.5% improvement in average precision for object detection.

The work relates to recent NIPS papers on weakly supervised learning (e.g., [19] on weakly-supervised dual clustering) and extends the concept of weighted SVMs [23, 27] by weighting labels rather than data points. Unlike Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), which requires at least one completely positive instance per bag, the authors' approach handles partial overlaps between segment proposals and ground truth.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-motivated theoretical formulation. The e-SVM model correctly addresses the problem of continuous label values in a bounded interval [0,1] by modeling the label as a random variable. The latent extension for weak supervision is logically derived and the optimization procedure is clearly described in Algorithm 1.

The experimental results are well-designed and thorough, with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The authors provide detailed results for both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks on standard PASCAL VOC datasets, showing consistent improvements over baselines. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as not using ImageNet annotations for further improvement.

One weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis regarding convergence properties of the latent e-SVM optimization. While the paper states that the optimization is convex in w and u separately, it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of convergence guarantees.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, though some notation could be more consistent (e.g., the use of "u" for both the latent variable and the bounding box IoU).

The introduction effectively sets up the problem of weakly supervised learning in computer vision. The motivation for using continuous positiveness values rather than binary thresholds is well-explained. The description of the spatial confidence map feature for object detection is particularly clear and well-illustrated.

One minor issue is the presentation of the results in Table 1 and Figure 3, which could benefit from more explicit comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond just SVC and SVR.

## Originality

The core idea of using expectation loss for continuous label values is novel in the context of SVMs for weakly supervised learning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from existing work on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs by addressing the specific problem of continuous positiveness values in [0,1] rather than binary labels or instance weights.

However, the paper could better contextualize its contribution by citing more relevant prior work on weakly supervised learning. For instance, the seminal work by Hoiem et al. (2008) on "Geometric Context for Weakly Supervised Object Detection" addresses similar problems with bounding box annotations, and more recent work on weakly supervised segmentation (e.g., [19]) should be more thoroughly compared.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from related techniques like "Learning from Weakly Labeled Data" [20] or "Weakly Supervised Structured Output Learning for Semantic Segmentation" [26].

## Significance

The problem addressed by this paper is highly significant, as manual pixel-level annotation for large-scale datasets is prohibitively expensive. The authors demonstrate that their method can effectively leverage bounding box annotations (which are widely available in datasets like ImageNet) to achieve strong performance in both semantic segmentation and object detection.

The 1.5% average precision improvement in object detection is non-trivial in the context of the field, and the method's simplicity (just concatenating features with existing detectors) makes it highly practical. The paper also provides a general framework that could be applied to other tasks beyond computer vision.

However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the authors don't actually use ImageNet annotations in their experiments (as mentioned in the conclusion), which would have been a stronger validation of their approach.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Addresses an important problem in computer vision with practical implications
2. Presents a theoretically sound extension to SVM for continuous label values
3. Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across two major tasks
4. Provides clear, well-structured experiments with appropriate metrics
5. The method is simple and generalizable to other applications

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods in object detection
2. Insufficient discussion of related work in weakly supervised learning
3. Lack of theoretical analysis of optimization convergence
4. Does not leverage ImageNet annotations in experiments as suggested in the conclusion
5. The regularization term for latent variables could be better motivated

## Recommendation

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, with a novel approach that addresses a specific limitation of existing methods. The technical quality is high, and the experimental results are convincing. While the paper could be strengthened by better contextualizing the work against existing literature and providing more thorough comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, the core contribution is significant.

I recommend acceptance with the following suggestions for revision:
1. Expand the related work section to include more recent weakly supervised learning methods
2. Provide a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art object detection methods beyond RCNN
3. Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the latent e-SVM
4. Consider using ImageNet annotations in the experiments to better validate the main claim about leveraging large datasets

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics in machine learning for computer vision and weakly supervised learning, and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
