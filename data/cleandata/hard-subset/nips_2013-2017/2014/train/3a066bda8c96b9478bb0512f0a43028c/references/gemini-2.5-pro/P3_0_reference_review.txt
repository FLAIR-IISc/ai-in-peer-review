### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine variant, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" score, such as the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) overlap. The core idea is to model the true binary label of a training sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of the observed positiveness score. The e-SVM objective function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This formulation elegantly handles samples that are partially correct.

The authors extend this model to a more weakly supervised setting, the latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved and treated as latent variables. This is particularly relevant for tasks like object detection where only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks. The latent e-SVM is trained using an alternating optimization procedure, regularizing the latent positiveness scores to remain close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The proposed methods are evaluated extensively on two fundamental computer vision tasks. First, on semantic segmentation proposal classification, e-SVM is shown to outperform standard baselines like Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) using novel and appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). Second, the latent e-SVM is used to generate a "spatial confidence map feature" that provides complementary information to the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector, leading to a significant 1.5% mAP improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The e-SVM is a very elegant and well-motivated approach to the common problem of learning from "soft" labels. It provides a sound probabilistic interpretation for handling continuous-valued supervision, which is a clear improvement over ad-hoc binarization (SVC) or treating it as a standard regression problem (SVR). The idea of taking the expectation of the hinge loss is simple yet powerful.

2.  **Effective Extension to Weak Supervision:** The latent e-SVM is a natural and strong extension of the core idea. It directly addresses the highly practical scenario of learning from bounding box annotations. The inclusion of a KL-divergence based regularization term on the latent positiveness variables is a clever design choice that prevents the model from collapsing to a hard-assignment latent SVM, thereby retaining the soft nature of the supervision.

3.  **Strong and Significant Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling experimental evidence.
    *   The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation criteria for segment proposal ranking is a valuable contribution in itself, allowing for a more direct and nuanced assessment than task-specific final metrics.
    *   The consistent outperformance of e-SVM over SVC and SVR on these metrics validates the core model.
    *   Most impressively, the method demonstrates practical significance by improving upon the R-CNN detector, which was a very strong state-of-the-art baseline at the time. A 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is non-trivial and demonstrates that the learned segmentation cues provide meaningful, complementary information to powerful deep features.

4.  **Generality and Potential Impact:** The framework is general and not tied to specific proposal generators or feature types. This makes it a widely applicable tool for many computer vision problems that rely on proposal-based methods and can benefit from learning with supervision that is better than image-level tags but cheaper than full pixel-level masks.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Optimization and Relation to Weighted SVM:**
    *   The paper states that the basic e-SVM can be solved by a standard SVM solver. This is true, but the connection could be made more explicit. The objective in Eq. (1) is equivalent to training a standard SVM on a duplicated dataset where each sample `x_i` appears twice: once with label `+1` and instance weight `g(u_i)`, and once with label `-1` and instance weight `1 - g(u_i)`. Clarifying this would strengthen the reader's understanding and highlight the connection/distinction to traditional weighted SVMs.
    *   For the latent e-SVM, the optimization step for the latent variables `u` in Algorithm 1 (`argmin_u L(w, u)`) is underspecified. While the objective is convex in `u` (given the chosen regularization), it is not clear if a closed-form solution exists. The authors should specify how this subproblem is solved (e.g., via gradient descent, numerical optimization). This is a crucial missing implementation detail.

2.  **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper lacks some details needed for full reproducibility. The values for the regularization hyperparameters (`λ_w`, `λ_R`) are not provided, nor is there a discussion on how they were selected or the model's sensitivity to them. Furthermore, information on the convergence of the alternating optimization in Algorithm 1 (e.g., number of iterations) would be beneficial.

3.  **Context of Segmentation Results:** The semantic segmentation results (36.8% mIoU on VOC 2011) are presented as "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art". This claim may be slightly overstated, as top-performing methods from that era (e.g., the original CPMC paper [5]) reported higher scores. While the goal here is a controlled comparison to SVC/SVR rather than pushing the SOTA in segmentation, the wording should be more precise to avoid confusion. The key finding is the relative improvement, which is well-supported.

**Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with a novel, well-motivated method and significant empirical results. The e-SVM framework provides a principled way to learn from continuous-valued weak labels, a problem of great practical importance. The successful application to improving a state-of-the-art object detector underscores the significance of the contribution. While there are minor weaknesses related to clarity and missing experimental details, these do not detract from the core merit of the work and could be easily addressed in a final version. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the fields of machine learning and computer vision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation for learning from continuous-valued supervision.
*   Addresses a practical and important problem (learning from bounding boxes).
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvements on a challenging, state-of-the-art baseline (R-CNN).
*   High potential for impact and broader applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Some lack of clarity regarding the optimization of latent variables.
*   The relationship to weighted SVMs could be more thoroughly discussed.

Overall, the strengths strongly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**.
