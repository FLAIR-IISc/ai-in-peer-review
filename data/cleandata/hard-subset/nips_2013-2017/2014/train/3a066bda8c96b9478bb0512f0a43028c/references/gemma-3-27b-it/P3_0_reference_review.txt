## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM-based approach, e-SVM, for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing the scenario where only partial or imprecise labels (positiveness scores) are available. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and supported by thorough experiments across semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. While not a revolutionary leap, the e-SVM offers a practical and effective improvement over existing methods like SVC and SVR, particularly in the weakly supervised setting. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles a critical problem in computer vision: reducing the reliance on expensive, fully-annotated datasets.  Instead of binary labels, e-SVM leverages continuous "positiveness" scores indicating the degree to which a proposal (e.g., a segment) represents the target object. The core idea is to modify the SVM loss function to directly incorporate these positiveness values as weights, effectively performing a weighted classification. 

The authors correctly position their work within the landscape of weakly supervised learning, contrasting it with Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) which assumes *at least one* positive instance exists, a condition not met in their setting. They also acknowledge and compare against standard SVC and SVR approaches, highlighting their limitations when dealing with continuous labels. The connection to weighted SVMs is also acknowledged, with a clear distinction made: e-SVM weights *labels* instead of data points, leading to a different loss function. The paper demonstrates awareness of relevant literature, including recent advancements in semantic segmentation and object detection.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The e-SVM formulation is a sensible and effective extension of standard SVMs to handle continuous label information.  While the concept of weighting is not new, applying it to the *labels* themselves in this manner is a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the loss function is clear and logical, and the algorithm is well-defined. The latent e-SVM formulation for handling unobserved positiveness is also well-motivated and provides a practical solution for common weakly supervised scenarios.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive. The paper demonstrates improvements over SVC and SVR on both segment proposal classification and downstream tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC).  The improvements, while not massive, are consistent and statistically relevant.
* **Practicality:** The e-SVM algorithm can be easily implemented using existing SVM solvers (like LibLinear), making it readily accessible to researchers.  The paper also emphasizes the generalizability of the approach to different segment proposal methods, feature extractors, and base detectors.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the methodology and experiments easy to follow. 

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Modest Improvements:** While the improvements over baselines are statistically significant, they aren't dramatic.  The 1.5% average improvement in object detection AP, while welcome, might be perceived as incremental.  A more in-depth analysis of *why* e-SVM works better (e.g., error analysis) could strengthen the paper.
* **Regularization in Latent e-SVM:** The choice of the KL divergence regularization term for the latent variable *u* seems somewhat arbitrary.  The justification provided is reasonable (preventing u from collapsing to 0 or 1), but a more extensive exploration of different regularization options and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to hyperparameter choices (e.g., the regularization parameter λR).  Providing guidance on how to tune these parameters would enhance the practical value of the method.
* **Figure Quality:** While the figures are generally helpful, some (e.g., Figure 3) could benefit from improved readability (larger font sizes, clearer labels).



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and practical problem in computer vision.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm.
* Demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Easily implementable.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Improvements are somewhat modest.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*  The regularization choice in the latent setting could be more thoroughly justified.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed error analysis to understand *why* e-SVM performs better than SVC and SVR?  Are there specific types of segments where e-SVM excels?
*  Have you explored alternative regularization terms for the latent variable *u* in the latent e-SVM formulation? If so, what were the results? If not, could you briefly discuss the potential impact of other regularization options?
* Could you provide some guidelines for choosing appropriate values for the key hyperparameters (e.g., λR)?
*  While you mention the generalizability of the method, have you explored applying e-SVM to other weakly supervised tasks beyond segmentation and object detection?



**Overall, I believe that the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning.  The e-SVM algorithm is a practical and effective approach for leveraging continuous label information, and the thorough experimental validation supports the authors' claims.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.**  I recommend acceptance.
