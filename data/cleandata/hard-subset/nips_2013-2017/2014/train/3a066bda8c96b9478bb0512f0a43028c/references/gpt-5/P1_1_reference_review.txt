Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data in which training “labels” are continuous positiveness scores in [0,1], e.g., IoU between a segment proposal and ground-truth. The core idea is to treat the (binary) label Yi as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability µi=g(ui), and minimize the expected hinge loss: for each example i, the loss becomes µi·max(0,1−w⊤xi) + (1−µi)·max(0,1+w⊤xi), plus L2 regularization on w. The paper then considers a weakly supervised setting where ui is unobserved and introduces a latent-variable variant that alternates between optimizing w and the latent u, with a convex regularizer R(u) that is the cross-entropy between ui and a bounding-box-derived proxy ubb_i. Applications are shown on: (i) segment confidence prediction, with new evaluation metrics TAPC and NDCG; (ii) semantic segmentation using CPMC segments; and (iii) object detection by pooling per-pixel maxima of segment confidences into a spatial confidence map feature concatenated with R-CNN features. Reported results show e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on the segment prediction metrics, comparable segmentation performance to SVR, and +1.2–1.5 mAP improvement over R-CNN on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Relation to prior work
The problem aligns with weakly supervised vision (MIL, latent SVM, proposal scoring) and weighted SVMs. The loss is the expected hinge under a Bernoulli label, which is closely related to “SVM with probabilistic/uncertain labels” and can be implemented by duplicating each example with two labels (+1 and −1) weighted by µi and (1−µi), i.e., a special case of instance-weighted SVM. The latent variant with alternating updates echoes EM/CCCP-style latent SVM. The spatial pooling of per-class confidence maps resembles segment-aware features used in detection.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The expected hinge formulation is convex in w (given known µi), and the latent version is bi-convex with a reasonable alternating scheme. However:
  - The latent update for ui is only described qualitatively; no derivation, gradients, or solver details are provided. Each ui update is a 1D convex problem; a closed-form condition exists, but is not shown, nor is convergence behavior or step-size/regularization sensitivity discussed.
  - The choice g(u)=u is ad hoc; no calibration or justification (e.g., monotone transforms, Platt-style calibration, or learning g) is explored.
  - Using ubb (IoU with bounding box) as a surrogate prior for the true IoU with pixel masks is biased; the strength of the cross-entropy regularizer may unduly tether ui and limit the benefit of learning. There is no ablation on λR or on the regularizer form.
  - Experiments lack ablations to separate the contributions of (a) the e-SVM loss versus SVR/SVC when used to generate the same spatial features; (b) the proposed spatial pooling versus other fusions; and (c) the effect of alternating optimization iterations.
  - Statistical rigor is limited: no error bars, seed variation, or significance tests; the reported 1.2–1.5 mAP gain may be within run-to-run variance for R-CNN pipelines.
- Empirical support: The segment confidence prediction metrics (TAPC, NDCG) favor e-SVM over SVR/SVC; this is useful but task-agnostic. For semantic segmentation, reported “accuracy” (36.8% and 27.7%) is ambiguous (mIoU? pixel accuracy?) and appears only comparable to SVR; no per-class breakdown is shown. For detection, the +1.5 mAP is promising but needs stronger baselines and analysis (e.g., per-class gains, comparison to SVR-based confidence maps, runtime).

Clarity
- The paper is readable but has many grammatical issues, inconsistent notation, and several malformed or incomplete references (e.g., “?”, “and A, M. Everingham,...”).
- TAPC is not precisely defined (threshold range, spacing, averaging). Key implementation details are missing: solver for ui-step, hyperparameters (λ, λR) and their tuning, convergence criteria, runtime.
- The segmentation evaluation metric is not clearly defined; the object detection setup should specify training/validation splits and bounding box regression configuration more precisely.

Originality
- The core e-SVM objective is an expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label, which is well-known and equivalent to a weighted combination of positive/negative hinge terms. This can be realized by weighted SVM with duplicated instances; the claimed distinction from WSVM (weighting labels vs instances) is largely superficial.
- The latent extension and cross-entropy regularizer are straightforward; alternating convex optimization is standard. The spatial pooling feature is a simple and reasonable design but not particularly novel.
- The proposed evaluation metrics (TAPC/NDCG) are standard IR constructs adapted to this setting; useful but not novel.

Significance
- Practically, the idea of embracing soft labels (IoU) rather than binarizing is sensible and can be impactful for tasks relying on proposal scoring. The +1.5 mAP improvement on VOC07 suggests utility.
- However, the incremental nature of the contribution, modest gains, and limited analysis reduce broader impact. Without stronger comparisons, ablations, and clarity, it is hard to judge whether the approach will be adopted over existing regression and probabilistic methods.

Pros
- Simple, general formulation; can be used with standard SVM solvers.
- Handles soft labels naturally; extends to weak supervision via a latent-variable approach.
- Empirical improvements on proposal scoring and small but consistent gains when coupled with R-CNN.
- Class-specific spatial confidence map feature is intuitive and easy to integrate.

Cons
- Limited novelty; expected hinge with probabilistic labels is essentially a special case of weighted SVM with duplicated examples; relation to prior “uncertain label SVM” work not discussed.
- Insufficient technical detail for the latent update; no analysis of g(u), λR, convergence, or sensitivity.
- Experimental evaluation lacks ablations, clear metrics for segmentation, and statistical rigor; improvements are modest.
- Writing and references need significant polishing.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the segmentation metric and provide per-class results and comparisons to SVR/SVC-based pipelines end-to-end.
2. Provide details for the ui optimization (derivation, solver, complexity) and sensitivity to λR and the choice of g(u).
3. Can you show that e-SVM is (or is not) equivalent to a WSVM on duplicated examples with weights µi and 1−µi? If not equivalent, please clarify the difference.
4. Report ablations: spatial feature without e-SVM (using SVR or raw IoU), effect of the max-over-segments vs alternatives, and impact per class on VOC07 with variance across runs.
5. Runtime and scalability: how much overhead do segment scoring and spatial pooling add to R-CNN?

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the paper addresses a relevant problem and shows some empirical utility, the core methodological novelty is limited, and the current presentation lacks technical detail, rigorous analysis, and thorough evaluation needed for NeurIPS.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high
