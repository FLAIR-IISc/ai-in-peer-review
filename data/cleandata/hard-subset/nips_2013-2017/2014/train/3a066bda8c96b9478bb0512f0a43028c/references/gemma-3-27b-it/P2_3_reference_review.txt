## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data where labels aren’t precise (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level segmentations).  The key idea is to treat the “positiveness” of proposals as a continuous variable (between 0 and 1) representing the degree of correctness, and to learn a model that predicts this positiveness. The paper extends the standard SVM framework to handle this expectation, and provides a latent variable extension for cases where positiveness is unobserved.  The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating improvements over standard SVC and SVR approaches, and achieving competitive results when incorporated into a state-of-the-art object detector (RCNN).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem:** Learning from weakly supervised data is crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets where full annotation is infeasible.
* **Novel formulation:**  Treating positiveness as a continuous variable and incorporating it into the SVM loss function is a sensible and effective approach.  The latent variable extension for weak supervision is well-motivated and practical.
* **Strong empirical results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on three tasks (segment classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection), consistently outperforming baseline methods.  The 1.5% improvement on PASCAL VOC object detection with RCNN is particularly noteworthy.
* **Theoretical justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical formulation of the e-SVM model and demonstrates its connection to weighted SVMs.  The convexity arguments are important for practical implementation.
* **Generality:** The authors explicitly highlight the algorithm’s flexibility regarding segment proposal methods, image features, and classifiers.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty in application areas:** While the *method* is novel, applying it to semantic segmentation and object detection is fairly standard.  The contribution lies more in *how* it is applied rather than *where* it is applied.
* **Regularization sensitivity:** The latent e-SVM relies heavily on the regularization term for the latent variable 'u'. The paper mentions the importance of preventing 'u' from collapsing to 0 or 1, but doesn’t provide a rigorous justification for the chosen regularization function (KL divergence).  A more thorough ablation study on the impact of different regularization schemes would strengthen the paper.
* **Comparison to recent methods:** While the comparison to SVC and SVR is adequate, it would be stronger if the paper compared to more recent weakly-supervised learning techniques, particularly those that leverage consistency or multi-instance learning. The related work section acknowledges these methods but doesn’t fully engage with them in the experimental evaluation.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper could benefit from more details regarding the implementation. For instance, specific hyperparameter settings (e.g., lambda values in the loss function, regularization strength) are not provided which limits reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in machine learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a novel and effective approach to learning from weakly supervised data, and the experimental results are convincing. While the application areas are somewhat standard, the method itself is a valuable contribution.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The analysis of convexity and the discussion of the latent variable extension demonstrate careful consideration of the theoretical aspects. (Score: 8/10)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are easy to follow. (Score: 9/10)
* **Originality:**  The e-SVM formulation is novel, and the latent variable extension represents a significant extension of the standard SVM framework. (Score: 7/10)
* **Significance:** The results are important and demonstrate the potential of the e-SVM algorithm to improve performance on a variety of computer vision tasks. The algorithm could be readily adopted by other researchers and practitioners. (Score: 7/10)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and effective algorithm for weakly supervised learning.
* Strong empirical results across multiple tasks.
* Clear theoretical formulation and justification.
* Addresses a practically relevant problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Application areas are somewhat standard.
* Limited comparison to recent weakly-supervised learning methods.
* Lack of detailed implementation information (hyperparameter settings).

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper for publication, contingent on the authors addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above, specifically:

1.  Expand the related work discussion to more thoroughly address recent advances in weakly-supervised learning.
2.  Provide a more detailed justification for the choice of regularization function in the latent e-SVM, and potentially include an ablation study.
3.  Include more implementation details (hyperparameter settings) to improve reproducibility.
