This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for learning from data with continuous labels in the [0, 1] range, a common scenario in weakly supervised computer vision tasks. The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its continuous "positiveness" score (e.g., IoU). The e-SVM then minimizes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegant formulation means each sample contributes to both the positive and negative margin constraints, weighted by its positiveness. The authors further extend this to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness score is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box supervision is available), treating the score itself as a latent variable to be optimized jointly with the model weights. The method is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection, where it is shown to outperform SVC/SVR baselines and provide a significant boost to a state-of-the-art object detector.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. The e-SVM formulation is a bold and elegant way to reframe the problem of learning from continuous-valued labels. Instead of resorting to ad-hoc thresholding for classification (SVC) or treating it as a standard regression problem (SVR), the authors propose a more principled approach that is grounded in the probabilistic nature of the label uncertainty. This is precisely the kind of creative, foundational thinking that pushes the field forward.

The extension to the latent e-SVM is a significant contribution. It directly addresses the more practical and challenging problem of learning from weak supervision like bounding boxes, which is of immense importance given the prohibitive cost of pixel-level annotation for large-scale datasets. The iterative optimization scheme is straightforward and effective.

The practical significance of the work is convincingly demonstrated. While the results on semantic segmentation are comparable to prior work, the application to object detection is particularly impressive. Achieving a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 over the very strong R-CNN baseline is a non-trivial result. It proves that the segmentation cues learned by the latent e-SVM provide complementary information that even powerful deep learning features miss, validating the utility of the proposed method.

The paper is also well-written and clearly presents the core ideas. The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment ranking sub-task is a thoughtful addition that allows for a more direct evaluation of the proposed model against baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper is not without its minor flaws, though in my view, they are overshadowed by the strength of the core contribution.

The performance gains in the fully supervised semantic segmentation task are modest, showing only slight improvements over SVR. This suggests that in the presence of strong supervision, the advantage of the e-SVM formulation is less pronounced.

The choice of the KL-divergence-based regularizer for the latent positiveness variables feels somewhat heuristic. While the authors provide a reasonable justification, the sensitivity to this choice and a comparison to other potential regularizers (e.g., an L2 penalty) is not explored. A deeper analysis of this component would have strengthened the paper.

Finally, the alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is guaranteed to converge only to a local minimum. While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks any theoretical analysis or discussion of the quality of the solution or potential initialization issues.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The introduction of the expectation loss SVM is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a novel and principled direction for weakly supervised learning. The paper prioritizes a bold new idea over safe, incremental improvements, and it successfully demonstrates the idea's practical value by improving a state-of-the-art object detection system. While some aspects of the experimental validation and theoretical analysis could be more thorough, these are minor weaknesses in the context of a paper that introduces such a compelling new approach. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research and provides a valuable new tool for a critical problem area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and elegant formulation (e-SVM) for learning with continuous labels.
*   Addresses the critical and practical problem of learning from weak supervision (bounding boxes) with the latent e-SVM extension.
*   Demonstrates significant practical impact by improving a very strong object detection baseline (R-CNN).
*   The core idea is general and can be widely applied.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Methodological perfection is not fully achieved; the choice of regularizer is heuristic and the convergence of the latent model is not analyzed.
*   Performance gains on the semantic segmentation task are not state-of-the-art, but merely comparable.
