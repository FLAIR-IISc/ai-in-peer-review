

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where training examples have continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU scores between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors propose a model that treats the label as a random variable with probability determined by the positiveness, and extend it to weak supervision by treating positiveness as latent variables. The method is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC datasets, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Problem Relevance:** The paper addresses an important practical challenge in computer vision—reducing labeling costs while maintaining performance. The motivation for weakly supervised learning is well-justified, especially given the prevalence of bounding box annotations versus pixel-level annotations.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The e-SVM formulation is logically developed, with clear mathematical derivation of the loss function. The extension to latent variables for weak supervision is well-motivated and implemented through a straightforward optimization procedure (Algorithm 1). The experimental evaluation is thorough with appropriate metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment classification task.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The mathematical formulations are clear, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed. The application to semantic segmentation and object detection is well-explained with concrete examples.

4. **Empirical Results:** The method shows consistent improvement over SVC and SVR baselines on both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. The 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is a meaningful contribution, especially for a relatively simple integration with RCNN.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper presents a clear optimization procedure, it lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence for the latent e-SVM model. The authors assume convexity but don't prove it for their specific formulation, which is crucial for understanding the algorithm's reliability.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work:** The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from existing weakly supervised learning approaches, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods. The authors claim MIL cannot solve their problem, but don't provide a rigorous comparison or analysis of why their approach is better suited for their specific problem setting (where proposals are partially correct).

3. **Overstated Significance:** The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is misleading. The paper only improves upon RCNN by 1.5% AP, which is modest compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN). The paper doesn't compare with more recent object detection methods beyond RCNN.

4. **Ad Hoc Regularization:** The choice of regularization term (Equation 3) appears arbitrary. The authors don't justify why this specific KL-based regularization is superior to alternatives (e.g., L2 regularization on the latent variables) or provide ablation studies to support this choice.

5. **Limited Analysis of Weaknesses:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach. For example, how does the method perform when the initial bounding box annotations are poor? How sensitive is the model to the quality of the segment proposals?

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. **Strengthen Theoretical Foundation:** Provide convergence proofs or analysis for the latent e-SVM optimization. Discuss the impact of different regularization terms on performance and convergence.

2. **Expand Related Work Comparison:** Compare with recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL, such as those using deep learning for weak supervision (e.g., [1,2]). Clarify the theoretical distinction between the authors' problem setting and existing methods.

3. **Update Experimental Comparison:** Compare with more recent object detection methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO, SSD) to better demonstrate the significance of the improvements. Consider including ablation studies on the impact of different regularization terms.

4. **Clarify Problem Setting:** More clearly articulate the specific problem setting where e-SVM is uniquely advantageous compared to existing approaches. Provide examples where e-SVM outperforms MIL-based approaches on the same datasets.

5. **Discuss Limitations:** Add a section discussing the limitations of the approach, such as sensitivity to initial bounding box annotations or performance on datasets with challenging object shapes.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels, and the empirical results demonstrate modest but meaningful improvements over baselines. However, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the significance is overstated compared to recent advances in the field. The theoretical foundation is insufficient, and the comparison with related work is inadequate.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and significance to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors should address the theoretical gaps and strengthen the comparison with recent work before resubmission. The improvements suggested above would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
