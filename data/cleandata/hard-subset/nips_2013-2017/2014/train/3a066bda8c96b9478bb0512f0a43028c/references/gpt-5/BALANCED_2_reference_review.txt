Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “soft” labels in [0,1], where each segment proposal has a positiveness score u (e.g., IoU with ground-truth mask). e-SVM minimizes the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label with success probability μ=g(u) (taken as μ=u), i.e., each example contributes to both positive and negative hinge losses weighted by μ and 1−μ. The authors then extend to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM), where u is unobserved and optimized jointly with the classifier using a convex regularizer R(u) (cross-entropy/KL to an initialization u_bb based on IoU with the ground-truth bounding box). They alternate between optimizing w (standard linear SVM) and u (convex in u given w).

Applications are demonstrated on segment proposal scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection. For detection, they derive a spatial confidence map feature by max-pooling segment confidences to pixels, then pooling over m×m bins per candidate window; concatenated with RCNN features this yields +1.2–1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007. They also introduce two segment-scoring metrics: TAPC (AP averaged over IoU thresholds) and NDCG.

The formulation is close to prior work on SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels and uncertain/fuzzy labels, where labels are given as probabilities or membership degrees and the hinge loss is weighted accordingly; and to latent SVMs and EM-style alternating optimization for weak supervision, including MIL-style and DPM-style latent variable models. The use of KL regularization toward bounding-box-derived u resembles regularized latent variable estimation. The detection integration is related to prior use of segmentation cues (e.g., CPMC, superpixels) as features for detection.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear mathematical formulation for the supervised case; expected hinge loss is convex in w and can be handled efficiently.
- The latent extension is well-motivated for bounding-box supervision; the cross-entropy regularizer is convex and prevents degenerate u∈{0,1}.
- Simple, modular integration with existing pipelines; compatible with any proposal method and base detector.
- Empirical gains on VOC07 detection (+1.2–1.5 mAP over RCNN) are non-trivial for that era; segment-scoring metrics are reasonable and useful.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty of the core supervised formulation appears limited: minimizing expected hinge loss with probabilistic/soft labels is well-studied (fuzzy SVMs, SVMs with probabilistic labels/uncertain labels). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate from or cite these lines, nor clarify equivalences (e.g., duplicating each sample as +1/−1 with weights μ and 1−μ).
- The latent part is an alternating minimization over w and u with a convex per-sample regularizer; this is quite close to standard latent SVM/EM formulations and is presented as new without deeper analysis (no convergence guarantees beyond standard coordinate descent; no identifiability or consistency discussion).
- Experimental evidence is mixed and somewhat thin: e-SVM modestly improves TAPC/NDCG over SVC/SVR but segmentation accuracy is only “comparable” to SVR; detection gains are modest and lack statistical significance, ablations, or comparisons to using SVR-based segment confidences in the same spatial pooling.
- Important details are missing: how exactly the expected hinge is solved with “any standard SVM solver” (e.g., data duplication trick or per-instance weighting support); hyperparameters (λw, λR), sensitivity analyses; number of alternations; convergence criteria and runtime; exact proposal types used per experiment (CPMC vs superpixels).
- Clarity and presentation issues: numerous typos, incomplete/broken references, and some contradictory claims (earlier claiming consistent gains over SVR, later “comparable”). Figures/tables are referenced but not fully described; datasets splits are not always clear.

Clarity
The high-level idea is understandable, but the paper needs substantial polishing. Provide a precise mapping to standard solvers (e.g., create two weighted copies per sample). Clearly define g(u), its calibration, and discuss alternatives. Specify optimization details, hyperparameters, and provide pseudocode with stopping criteria and complexity. Clean up references and language.

Originality
The combination—expected hinge with soft labels plus a KL-regularized latent extension for weak supervision—is reasonable but incremental relative to fuzzy/soft-label SVMs and latent SVM literature. The spatial confidence map feature is a straightforward pooling scheme over per-pixel maxima; similar segmentation-to-detection augmentations have appeared. The contribution would benefit from situating e-SVM among: fuzzy SVM, SVM with probabilistic labels, interval/uncertain labels SVM, label smoothing, and latent/EM-style SVMs (e.g., DPMs), clarifying what is genuinely new.

Significance
Weakly supervised learning for segmentation/detection is important. A simple, scalable approach that leverages soft labels is valuable. However, given the limited novelty and modest empirical improvements without strong ablations, the current impact is limited. Stronger evidence (diverse datasets, base detectors, and ablations) could raise the significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate e-SVM in the literature on soft/probabilistic labels and fuzzy SVM, and articulate the exact differences. If equivalent to duplicating samples with weights μ and 1−μ, state and exploit that.
- Provide theory: properties of expected hinge with soft labels (e.g., calibration/Bayes consistency under certain g), relation to cost-sensitive SVM, and behavior as u→{0,1}.
- Calibrate g(u): justify using μ=u; consider monotone link functions or learned calibration (isotonic/platt-like) from IoU to label probability.
- Detail optimization: mapping to LibLinear, hyperparameters, sensitivity to λR and the choice of R(u); convergence behavior and runtime.
- Stronger experiments: (i) compare spatial map features built from SVR/SVC to isolate the benefit of e-SVM vs the feature design; (ii) ablate m, pooling, max vs average; (iii) significance/error bars; (iv) results on additional datasets/base detectors; (v) clearer segmentation metrics (mean IoU) and comparisons to published SVR-based systems.
- Improve writing and references; fix incomplete citations and figure/table presentations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (weak supervision with segment proposals) with a simple, scalable method.
- Clean convex subproblems and practical integration into existing pipelines.
- Some empirical gains on a canonical benchmark; proposed evaluation metrics for segment scoring are sensible.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core idea closely matches known soft-label/fuzzy SVM formulations; limited novelty is not adequately acknowledged.
- Latent extension is standard alternating optimization without new analysis.
- Experimental evidence and ablations are insufficient to establish clear, general benefits.
- Clarity and reference quality below NeurIPS standards.

Recommendation: Reject
A promising direction with a simple and potentially useful formulation, but the current submission lacks sufficient novelty, rigor, and experimental depth for NeurIPS. Strengthening theory, positioning within prior work, and more thorough empirical evaluation could make this a solid journal or workshop paper.
