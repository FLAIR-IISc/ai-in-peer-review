This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where continuous “positiveness” values (e.g., IoU) are available instead of hard binary labels. The core idea is to treat these positiveness values as probabilities and incorporate them into the SVM loss function, effectively weighting examples based on their estimated correctness. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM across segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, consistently outperforming SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in weakly supervised learning, particularly methods addressing semantic segmentation and object detection with limited annotation. It appropriately acknowledges and contrasts with Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVM approaches, clearly articulating how e-SVM differs in its handling of continuous positiveness values. The connection to existing work is well established, and the cited references are relevant and comprehensive. Recent NIPS proceedings (and other venues) do not contain a directly comparable approach – the framing of weighting *labels* instead of data points is a subtle but important distinction from standard weighted SVM techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation, particularly the extension to latent variables for weakly supervised settings, presents a novel approach to handling continuous label information. The idea of directly incorporating positiveness into the SVM loss is intuitive and effective.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results across all three tasks (segment classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection) are compelling. Consistent improvements over strong baselines (SVC and SVR) demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed method.  The 1.5% improvement in AP for object detection, while seemingly small, is significant in this competitive field.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The authors correctly emphasize the generality of the approach, highlighting its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors, image features, and base detectors. This flexibility enhances the potential applicability of e-SVM.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the explanations are easy to follow. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concrete understanding of the optimization procedure.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors perform a reasonably comprehensive evaluation, considering both strong and weak supervision scenarios and introducing new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically tailored for segment proposal classification.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the motivation behind e-SVM is sound, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of the algorithm’s properties. A discussion of convergence guarantees or a more detailed characterization of the loss landscape would strengthen the contribution.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the regularization parameter (lambdaR in the latent e-SVM) on performance is not thoroughly explored. A more systematic investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and guidelines for their selection would be beneficial.
* **Dependence on Proposal Quality:** The performance of e-SVM is inherently tied to the quality of the segment proposals. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't extensively explore the robustness of the algorithm to noisy or suboptimal proposals. A brief analysis of how e-SVM behaves with different proposal generation methods (beyond CPMC and superpixels) would be valuable.
* **KL Divergence Regularization:** The choice of the KL divergence as a regularization term for the latent variable seems somewhat arbitrary. While it encourages the estimated positiveness to be close to the initial IoU values, the justification for this specific choice could be more detailed. Exploring alternative regularization strategies might lead to improved performance.
* **Incremental Improvement in Segmentation:** While the results on semantic segmentation are positive, the gains over existing methods aren't as substantial as those achieved in object detection. This suggests that the benefits of e-SVM may be more pronounced in tasks where precise localization and confidence estimation are crucial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The approach is general and can be applied to a variety of computer vision tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated.
* The dependence on proposal quality is a potential limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is novel, effective, and well-motivated. While some theoretical and practical aspects could be further explored, the compelling empirical results and clear presentation make this a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the hyperparameter sensitivity and potential for further theoretical analysis. Specifically, adding a section discussing the computational complexity of the algorithm would be helpful.
