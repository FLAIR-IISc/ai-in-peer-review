### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine (SVM) formulation, termed the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from data with continuous "positiveness" labels in the [0, 1] range. This problem setting is common in computer vision, where the Intersection over Union (IoU) score between a proposal (e.g., a segment) and a ground truth annotation serves as a natural, non-binary label. The core idea is to model the true binary label of a sample as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is the given positiveness score. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. This elegant formulation results in a weighted SVM objective that can be solved with standard solvers, where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative class loss terms, weighted by its positiveness `u` and `1-u` respectively.

The paper extends this model to a weakly supervised setting (the "latent e-SVM"), where the positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM model parameters via an alternating optimization scheme. A KL-divergence-based regularization term is introduced to prevent the latent variables from collapsing to deterministic (0/1) values.

The authors validate their method on two fundamental computer vision tasks. First, on segment proposal classification, they introduce two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and show that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR). Second, they apply the latent e-SVM to object detection, creating a novel "spatial confidence map feature" that is concatenated with features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector. This combined system achieves a significant 1.5% mean Average Precision (mAP) improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The core contribution, the e-SVM loss function, is both novel and theoretically elegant. It provides a principled way to handle continuous-valued labels in a bounded interval, directly addressing the shortcomings of ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or the mismatch of using SVR for a classification-like task. The connection to an expected loss over a latent binary label is intuitive and well-motivated.

2.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** A major strength of the e-SVM is that its final objective function is equivalent to a weighted SVM. This means it can be implemented and solved using widely available, highly optimized SVM libraries (like LibLinear, as mentioned). This significantly lowers the barrier to adoption and makes the method very practical.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Key Task:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant improvement on the challenging PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark. Improving upon a powerful baseline like R-CNN by 1.5% mAP is a non-trivial achievement and convincingly demonstrates the value of the appearance cues learned by the latent e-SVM. This is a strong piece of evidence for the method's significance.

4.  **Well-Designed Evaluation:** The authors rightly point out the lack of direct evaluation criteria for the segment proposal classification task. The introduction of TAPC (Threshold Average Precision Curve) and NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) for this purpose is a thoughtful contribution in itself and allows for a direct and fair comparison between e-SVM, SVC, and SVR, where their method shows clear superiority.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Latent Model:** The latent e-SVM relies on an alternating optimization scheme and a specific KL-divergence regularizer. The justification for the regularizer is sound (to prevent collapse to a latent SVM-like solution), but the paper lacks an ablation study on its importance or a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter `λR`. The choice of this specific regularizer over other potential forms (e.g., an L2 penalty on the deviation from the initial `ubb`) is not fully justified.

2.  **Modest Results on Semantic Segmentation:** The paper reports that for semantic segmentation, the performance is "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art" (SVR). While not a negative result, it is less compelling than the object detection results. A deeper analysis of why e-SVM did not provide a clear advantage over SVR in this specific task would have strengthened the paper.

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** While generally well-written, some details could be clarified for better reproducibility. For example, the description of how the spatial confidence map feature is constructed is brief. The modification to the bounding box regression method used in R-CNN is mentioned but not detailed, making it difficult to fully replicate the final reported result.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

The paper presents a compelling solution to a prevalent problem. The idea of weighting the positive and negative hinge loss terms by `u_i` and `1-u_i` is an excellent insight derived from the expectation formulation.

-   **Regarding the Latent e-SVM Optimization:** The alternating optimization is a standard approach, but it is prone to local minima. How sensitive is the final model to the initialization of `u`? The paper initializes with `ubb` (IoU with the bounding box), which is a sensible choice. Have the authors experimented with other initializations or observed the convergence behavior of the algorithm? Does it typically converge in a few iterations?

-   **Comparison to Weighted SVMs (WSVM):** The paper does a good job of differentiating e-SVM from traditional WSVMs, noting that e-SVM weights labels/losses rather than data points. This is a key distinction. It might be even clearer to state that a single data point `i` contributes to the overall loss through *two* terms (one for the +1 label and one for the -1 label), whereas in WSVM, each data point contributes only one weighted slack term.

-   **Significance of Object Detection Results:** The 1.5% mAP gain is impressive. Could the authors comment on which classes benefited the most? The paper notes a 3.65% gain for "potted plant," which is interesting. Is there a pattern to the classes that see the largest improvements (e.g., non-rigid objects, objects with complex boundaries where segmentation cues are more valuable)? This could provide further insight into where the e-SVM features are most helpful.

### **Summary of Evaluation (Pros and Cons for Acceptance)**

**Pros:**
*   Presents a novel, elegant, and well-motivated loss function (e-SVM) for learning from continuous positiveness scores.
*   The resulting model is practical and can be trained efficiently using standard SVM solvers.
*   The extension to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) is straightforward and effective.
*   Achieves a significant state-of-the-art improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task by augmenting the strong R-CNN baseline.
*   Introduces new, appropriate metrics for the sub-task of segment proposal classification.

**Cons:**
*   Lacks a deeper analysis or ablation studies of the latent e-SVM model, particularly concerning the regularizer.
*   Reported results on semantic segmentation are only "comparable" to the baseline, not superior.
*   Minor details regarding the experimental setup could be clarified for full reproducibility.

### **Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with a clear and valuable contribution. The proposed e-SVM model is novel and addresses a real-world problem in a principled manner. The strength of the paper lies in its simple yet effective core idea and the compelling experimental results on object detection, where it improves a very strong baseline. The weaknesses are relatively minor and relate more to a lack of deeper analysis than to fundamental flaws in the method. The work is technically sound, original, and the results are significant enough to be of broad interest to both the machine learning and computer vision communities. I recommend acceptance.
