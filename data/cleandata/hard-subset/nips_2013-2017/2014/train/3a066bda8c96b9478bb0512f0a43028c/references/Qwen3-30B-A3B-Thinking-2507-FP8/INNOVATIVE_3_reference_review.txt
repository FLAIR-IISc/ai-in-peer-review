

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces an innovative learning framework called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that addresses a significant gap in weakly supervised learning for computer vision. The core novelty lies in treating classification labels as continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels, and formulating a loss function that takes the expectation over these continuous labels. This approach elegantly bridges the gap between standard supervised learning (where pixel-level annotations are available) and weakly supervised learning (where only bounding box annotations exist).

The paper's main contribution is the e-SVM model that can handle continuous label values within [0,1], which is a fundamentally different problem setting than standard binary classification or regression. The authors show how this formulation naturally extends to weak supervision by treating the unobserved positiveness as latent variables. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches like SVC (which requires arbitrary thresholding) and SVR (which doesn't account for bounded label ranges).

The paper demonstrates the utility of e-SVM in two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For semantic segmentation, they show that using e-SVM with either pixel-level or bounding box annotations outperforms previous methods. For object detection, they integrate their approach with the RCNN framework, achieving a 1.5% average precision improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 without any additional training data or complex modifications.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its conceptual simplicity and broad applicability. The authors present a clean mathematical formulation that extends standard SVMs with minimal modifications, yet it addresses a pervasive problem in computer vision (the high cost of pixel-level annotations). The paper's strength lies in its bold conceptual framing rather than methodological complexity - it's a fresh perspective on a longstanding problem.

The experimental validation is solid, with appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR) and the introduction of two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that are well-motivated for the continuous label setting. The results consistently show e-SVM outperforming SVC across all thresholds and slightly outperforming SVR, which is impressive given SVR's popularity for this task.

I particularly appreciate how the paper acknowledges the limitations of existing approaches. The authors correctly identify that SVC requires ad-hoc thresholding (which degrades performance) and that SVR isn't designed for bounded [0,1] label spaces. Their solution to this problem is elegant and conceptually fresh.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While there's related work on weighted SVMs and weakly supervised learning, the e-SVM formulation represents a distinct approach to continuous label handling that hasn't been previously explored in this context. The connection to MIL is well-articulated, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach from existing methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in computer vision - the need for expensive pixel-level annotations. By demonstrating that bounding box annotations can be effectively leveraged with minimal additional labeling cost, the approach has the potential to significantly reduce annotation burdens for large-scale vision tasks. The modest but consistent improvements (1.5% AP gain) are meaningful in the context of state-of-the-art object detection.

Minor weaknesses include:
- The regularization term for the latent variable (Equation 3) seems somewhat ad-hoc without deeper justification
- The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how the approach might generalize to other domains beyond computer vision
- The comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods (e.g., those using deep learning) would strengthen the contribution

However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong conceptual contribution. The authors have made a clear, novel contribution to the field that directly addresses an important problem. The paper's value lies in its conceptual innovation rather than methodological complexity, which aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on bold new ideas.

This work represents a significant conceptual advance in weakly supervised learning, with clear applicability to important computer vision tasks. The e-SVM formulation is elegant, well-motivated, and effectively bridges the gap between strong and weak supervision. The paper demonstrates this approach in two major applications with consistent improvements over baselines.

Given NIPS's emphasis on novelty and bold conceptual advances, I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The authors have identified an important problem and proposed a fresh, well-motivated solution that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The paper's conceptual novelty far outweighs the minor methodological limitations, and it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way for weakly supervised learning.

I recommend acceptance for its significant conceptual contribution and potential to influence future work in weakly supervised learning. The paper introduces a new perspective that could inspire further research beyond the specific applications demonstrated here.
