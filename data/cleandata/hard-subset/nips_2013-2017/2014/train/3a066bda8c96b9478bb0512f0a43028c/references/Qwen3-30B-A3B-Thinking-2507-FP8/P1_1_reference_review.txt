

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary

This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training labels are continuous "positiveness" values (between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors argue that standard approaches like SVC (which requires arbitrary thresholding) and SVR (which doesn't account for the bounded nature of the labels) are suboptimal for this problem. Their e-SVM model treats the label as a random variable and incorporates the expectation of the loss function over this variable. They extend this to a latent version for weak supervision where positiveness is unobserved, and apply it to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks on PASCAL VOC datasets.

The work relates to previous NIPS research on weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature for handling continuous labels in a bounded interval, which their approach addresses. The paper also connects to related work on segment proposal confidence prediction, such as the work by Carreira et al. [5] on semantic segmentation.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound in its formulation of the e-SVM model. The mathematical presentation is clear, and the optimization algorithm for the latent case is well-described. The experimental setup is appropriate for the problem, and the results on PASCAL VOC 2011 and 2007 datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. The authors introduce two new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) that are better suited to the continuous label problem.

However, there are some concerns about the completeness of the evaluation:
- The paper claims "our model outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these comparisons.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the method to the choice of regularization term or to the initialization of positiveness values.
- The theoretical analysis is limited - while the optimization is convex, there's no discussion of convergence rates or guarantees.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the problem is compelling, and the technical details are presented in a logical sequence. The figures (particularly Figure 1 and Figure 2) effectively illustrate the key concepts. However, there are some minor issues with clarity:
- The term "positiveness" is used without a clear definition beyond "a continuous value between 0 and 1" - it would be helpful to clarify how this relates to other concepts in weakly supervised learning.
- The connection between e-SVM and existing weighted SVM literature (cited as [23, 27, ?]) is not sufficiently developed. The authors state "it weights labels instead of data points" but don't provide a detailed mathematical distinction.
- The regularization term in Equation 3 is well-motivated but the connection to the KL divergence is not fully explained.

## Originality

The core idea of using the expectation of the loss over a random label variable is novel compared to standard SVMs. However, the paper's differentiation from existing work is somewhat weak:
- The authors correctly identify that MIL cannot handle their problem setting, but don't clearly explain why their approach is different from other weakly supervised learning methods.
- The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored - the paper states "it weights labels instead of data points" but doesn't provide a clear mathematical comparison.
- The use of IoU as a "positiveness" measure is not novel (it's standard in object detection), so the real novelty is in how the authors use this continuous value in the SVM framework.

The paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from prior work, particularly the weighted SVM literature.

## Significance

The problem of weakly supervised learning is important for reducing annotation costs in computer vision. The authors demonstrate that their method can achieve better results than existing approaches on two important computer vision tasks. The 1.5% improvement in average AP on PASCAL VOC 2007 for object detection is non-trivial and could be useful in practice.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by:
- The modest performance gains (1.5% AP improvement is significant but not revolutionary)
- The paper doesn't provide a thorough discussion of the practical limitations of the approach (e.g., how it scales to larger datasets, or how sensitive it is to the quality of bounding box annotations)
- The paper claims their approach is "general" but doesn't provide examples of applications beyond the two tasks they tested.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to the weighted SVM literature. The authors cite [23, 27, ?] but don't explain how their approach relates to these works beyond the high-level statement about "weighting labels instead of data points."

2. The paper claims "our model outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests. It would be valuable to include p-values or confidence intervals for these comparisons.

3. The paper mentions that "the regularization term will prevent this situation under the assumption that the true value of u should be around ubb" but doesn't provide empirical evidence for this assumption. It would be helpful to see how sensitive the results are to the choice of ubb.

4. The paper states that "we can use any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers" but doesn't discuss how the performance might vary with different choices. A small ablation study would strengthen the claim of generality.

5. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the latent e-SVM training compared to the standard e-SVM or other baselines, which is important for practical adoption.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a novel approach to weakly supervised learning that addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision. The technical contributions are sound, and the experimental results are promising. The paper would be significantly strengthened with a more thorough comparison to related work, statistical significance tests, and a more detailed discussion of limitations.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, and with the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision (reducing annotation costs)
- Novel approach to handling continuous labels in SVM framework
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines on two important tasks
- Well-written and well-organized
- Provides a clear framework for weakly supervised learning

**Con:**
- Limited theoretical analysis (no convergence guarantees)
- Modest performance gains (1.5% AP improvement)
- Some weak connections to related work (weighted SVMs)
- Limited discussion of limitations and practical considerations
