### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for weakly supervised learning tasks where training labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" values in the range [0, 1]. The core idea is to model the binary label for each training example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its known positiveness score. The SVM loss function is then formulated as the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM, where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative class loss terms, weighted by its positiveness `u_i` and `1-u_i` respectively.

The authors extend this model to the weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness `u_i` is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available instead of pixel-level masks). In this case, `u_i` is treated as a latent variable and optimized jointly with the SVM model parameters `w` via an alternating optimization scheme. A regularization term based on KL-divergence is introduced to prevent `u_i` from collapsing to binary values and to keep it close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, introducing two new metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for this task. It is then applied to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC datasets. For object detection, the e-SVM scores are used to create a "spatial confidence map feature," which, when concatenated with features from the strong R-CNN baseline, yields a significant 1.5% improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on VOC 2007.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the e-SVM as an expected loss is a principled and well-motivated approach to handling continuous-valued labels. The extension to the latent variable case is a logical next step, and the use of a regularizer on the latent positiveness values to prevent trivial solutions is sensible. The experimental validation is strong, particularly the object detection results. Improving a state-of-the-art method like R-CNN by 1.5% mAP is a non-trivial and convincing demonstration of the proposed method's utility. The introduction of new, appropriate evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for the sub-task of segment classification is a commendable contribution that strengthens the experimental analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts of e-SVM and latent e-SVM are explained clearly in Section 3. The motivation for the problem is well-established in the introduction. However, certain key details could be improved. The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 is somewhat vague. While the general idea of pooling confidence scores in spatial bins is understandable, the precise construction of the final `(M x K)`-dimensional feature is not detailed enough for easy reproduction. For instance, it is unclear how the confidence scores are pooled (max, average?) and how the features from different classes are combined. Figure 2 is referenced but not provided in the text, which hampers understanding. The optimization for the latent e-SVM is presented as a standard alternating minimization, but there is no discussion of convergence properties or the number of iterations required in practice.

**Originality:** The core idea of the expectation loss for an SVM is novel. While it results in a formulation that can be solved by standard weighted SVM solvers, the derivation and motivation are distinct from prior work on weighted SVMs, which typically weight entire instances to handle class imbalance or outliers. The paper correctly points out the distinction that in e-SVM, a single sample contributes to both positive and negative losses, which is a direct consequence of the expectation formulation. The application of this idea to weakly supervised segmentation and detection is a novel combination of techniques. The work is well-situated within the context of related work on weakly supervised learning and MIL, clearly articulating how its problem setting differs.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant. The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weaker, cheaper forms of supervision than pixel-level annotations. The demonstrated 1.5% mAP improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 detection task over the R-CNN baseline (which was state-of-the-art at the time) is a strong result and shows that segmentation-based cues, learned effectively with the proposed method, can provide valuable complementary information even to powerful deep learning features. This suggests the e-SVM framework could be a useful tool for other researchers. The general nature of the approach means it could potentially be integrated with other features, proposal methods, and base detectors, increasing its potential impact. While the semantic segmentation results are less impressive ("comparable" to SVR), the success in object detection is a major strength.

### Arguments for/against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The expectation loss SVM is an elegant and theoretically sound way to handle training data with continuous "positiveness" labels, avoiding the ad-hoc heuristics of thresholding required by SVC.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves a significant 1.5% mAP improvement on object detection over a very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007, demonstrating clear practical value and advancing the state of the art.
3.  **General and Practical Framework:** The method is applicable to different levels of supervision (pixel-level vs. bounding box) and can be integrated as a modular component into larger systems, as shown with R-CNN. This makes it a useful contribution to the community.
4.  **Useful Methodological Contribution:** The paper introduces new and sensible evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the intermediate task of segment proposal classification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Details:** The description of the spatial confidence map feature, a critical component for the object detection results, is insufficient for reproducibility.
2.  **Limited Gains in one Task:** The performance on semantic segmentation is only "comparable" to the SVR baseline, which slightly tempers the claims of superiority. The main strength rests heavily on the object detection results.
3.  **Limited Ablation/Analysis:** The paper sets `μ_i = u_i` for simplicity. An exploration of other functions `g(u_i)` or a discussion on the sensitivity to the regularization parameter `λ_R` in the latent model would have strengthened the paper.

### Final Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, solid execution, and significant results on a challenging and important task. The weaknesses, primarily related to clarity in the experimental description, are minor and could be addressed in a final version. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the e-SVM formulation and the impressive improvement on a strong object detection baseline, far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. I recommend acceptance.
