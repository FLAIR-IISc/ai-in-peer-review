### **Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from data with continuous-valued labels in the range [0, 1], which the authors term "positiveness". The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being a positive sample is given by its "positiveness" score. The e-SVM loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent label. This formulation elegantly handles samples that are partially correct. The paper extends this to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores are unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available) by treating them as latent variables and optimizing them iteratively with the model weights. The authors validate their method on the task of segment proposal classification and apply it to two major computer vision problems: semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, they propose a novel "spatial confidence map feature" derived from e-SVM scores and show that concatenating it with features from the state-of-the-art R-CNN model yields a significant improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The core e-SVM formulation is simple, elegant, and directly addresses a real problem in weakly supervised learning. Instead of resorting to ad-hoc thresholding to binarize continuous scores for SVC or using a generic SVR, the e-SVM loss is tailored to the problem of learning from labels in a bounded [0, 1] interval. This is a theoretically sound and principled approach.

2.  **Significant Application and Results:** The paper demonstrates the practical utility of its method by improving the performance of a very strong, state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN). An average improvement of 1.5% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a non-trivial and significant result, demonstrating that segmentation-based cues learned via the proposed method provide complementary information to powerful deep features.

3.  **General and Flexible Framework:** The approach is general. The e-SVM can be used with any feature representation, and the latent e-SVM can be applied to any problem where ground truth labels can be initialized and then refined. The proposed spatial confidence map feature for object detection is also a flexible way to integrate segment-level predictions into region-based detectors.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a solid experimental evaluation.
    *   They rightly identify that a direct evaluation of the segment classification task is needed and propose two new, sensible metrics for it (TAPC and NDCG).
    *   They compare against the most relevant baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR) and demonstrate superior performance in this sub-task.
    *   They show the model's effectiveness in both the fully supervised (pixel-level labels) and weakly supervised (bounding box labels) settings.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, some details are sparse, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 is brief. The paper mentions pooling confidence scores but does not specify the pooling method (e.g., average, max). It also mentions encoding the feature with "additive kernels approximation mapping," but the motivation and details for this step are not provided, making it difficult to reimplement.
    *   The optimization of the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is a form of alternating minimization. A brief discussion on the convergence properties of this algorithm would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the objective function guaranteed to decrease at each step?

2.  **Depth of Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a deeper analysis of the proposed model.
    *   The choice of the KL-divergence regularizer `R(u)` is justified as keeping `u` close to its initialization `u_bb`. However, the sensitivity of the model to the regularization parameter `λ_R` is not discussed. How critical is this parameter to achieving good performance?
    *   The distinction from weighted SVMs (WSVM) is made, but it is subtle. The e-SVM loss for a single sample is `u_i * max(0, 1-w^T x_i) + (1-u_i) * max(0, 1+w^T x_i)`. This can be viewed as a WSVM where each sample appears twice—once as a positive example with weight `u_i` and once as a negative example with weight `1-u_i`. While the motivation is different, the resulting optimization problem is very similar to standard WSVM solvers. A more explicit discussion of this connection would be beneficial.

3.  **Limited Scope of Segmentation Results:** The semantic segmentation results in Section 5.2 are presented very briefly. The accuracies are reported as "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art," but a direct comparison table with the SVR baseline [5] would be more convincing and provide a clearer picture of the performance trade-offs.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and well-motivated model (e-SVM) for learning from continuous-valued weak labels.
*   The problem is highly relevant to the NIPS community, particularly in the context of large-scale learning where detailed annotation is a bottleneck.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical success by improving a strong state-of-the-art model (R-CNN) on a standard benchmark.
*   The contributions are general and could inspire further work in weakly supervised learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Some key implementation details (e.g., spatial confidence map) are underspecified, affecting reproducibility.
*   The theoretical analysis is light (e.g., no discussion of convergence for the latent model).
*   The experimental section on semantic segmentation is cursory compared to the object detection section.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The core idea of the expectation-loss SVM is novel and elegant, and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated through strong experimental results on a challenging computer vision task. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to a lack of detail and deeper analysis rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. These are aspects that can be improved in a final version. The paper's originality, significance, and quality of results merit its acceptance at NIPS.
