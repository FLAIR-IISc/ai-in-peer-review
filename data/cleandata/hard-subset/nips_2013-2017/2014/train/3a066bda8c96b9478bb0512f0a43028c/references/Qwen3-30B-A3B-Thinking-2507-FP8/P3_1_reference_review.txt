

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values in [0,1] rather than binary. The authors propose an expectation-based loss function that treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the observed "positiveness" (e.g., IoU overlap). The method extends naturally to weak supervision by treating the positiveness as a latent variable. The authors demonstrate applications to semantic segmentation and object detection using PASCAL VOC datasets, claiming improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper sits at the intersection of weakly supervised learning and SVM literature. While related to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs, the authors correctly argue that their problem setting differs from standard MIL (which assumes at least one completely positive instance per bag) and from standard weighted SVMs (which weight data points rather than labels).

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of treating continuous labels as binary (via thresholding) or as regression targets. The motivation for using an expectation-based loss is well-justified.

2. **Practical Application**: The paper demonstrates the approach on two important computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) with clear experimental results. The object detection application with RCNN shows a meaningful 1.5% AP improvement.

3. **Theoretical Foundation**: The derivation of the expectation loss function and the latent extension provide a solid theoretical basis for the approach. The connection to Bernoulli distributions is appropriate for the problem setting.

4. **Novel Metrics**: The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics for segment classification is well-motivated and addresses a gap in the literature.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Latent Model**: While the paper proposes a latent e-SVM model, it lacks theoretical analysis of the optimization process. The convergence properties of the alternating optimization (Algorithm 1) are not discussed, and the paper doesn't address whether the solution is a global minimum or local minimum. The regularization term (Equation 3) is justified through intuition but lacks theoretical grounding.

2. **Insufficient Differentiation from Weighted SVMs**: The authors claim their approach differs from weighted SVMs, but the distinction is not clear. Weighted SVMs (WSVMs) in literature like [23, 27] also use instance-dependent weights on the loss function, which is essentially what the e-SVM does with the positiveness as weights. The paper should more thoroughly compare with and differentiate from this well-established literature.

3. **Modest Performance Gains**: While the 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is statistically significant, the gains are relatively modest compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., 5-10% improvements are common in the literature). The paper doesn't adequately address whether these gains are sufficient to justify the additional complexity of the approach.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the e-SVM approach. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the specific regularization term (Equation 3) versus the expectation loss formulation itself.

5. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper presents results showing improvements but doesn't analyze cases where the method fails or where the improvements are marginal. Understanding these limitations would strengthen the paper's contribution.

6. **Overstated Generality**: The claim that "it can use any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers" is not substantiated with experiments. The paper only uses CPMC segments and second-order pooling features, which limits the demonstration of this claimed flexibility.

## Technical Concerns

1. The paper uses IoU as the positiveness measure but doesn't discuss how this choice affects performance. Alternative measures (e.g., F1 score, precision-recall) could be explored.

2. In the latent e-SVM formulation, the initialization using ubb (IoU between segment and bounding box) is critical but not thoroughly justified. The paper should discuss how sensitive the method is to this initialization.

3. The paper claims the method "can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver," but the latent extension requires an alternating optimization procedure. The computational cost of this procedure relative to standard SVMs is not discussed.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of MIL for their problem setting (since most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels), but it could better connect to more recent weakly supervised learning approaches that handle continuous labels. For example, the work by [Li et al., CVPR 2015] on "Object recognition as ranking holistic figure-ground hypotheses" uses a similar ranking-based approach for weakly supervised learning.

The connection to weighted SVMs is underdeveloped. The paper cites [23, 27] but doesn't demonstrate how e-SVM differs from these approaches in a formal sense. A clearer theoretical distinction would strengthen the paper's novelty claim.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision (reducing annotation requirements through weak supervision), which is highly relevant to current trends in the field. The modest but consistent improvements across multiple tasks demonstrate practical value. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains and the lack of substantial theoretical novelty.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a solid approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels that is well-motivated and demonstrated on important computer vision tasks. The methodology is technically sound, and the results are promising. However, the paper would benefit from:

1. A clearer theoretical justification for the latent e-SVM optimization
2. More thorough differentiation from weighted SVM literature
3. Additional ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components
4. Discussion of failure cases and limitations

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, the modest performance gains and insufficient theoretical analysis prevent it from being a major advance. The paper would be strengthened by addressing these concerns before acceptance.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 7/10 - The methodology is sound but lacks theoretical depth for the latent extension.

**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-written, but some technical details could be clarified.

**Originality**: 6/10 - The core idea is somewhat novel but lacks sufficient differentiation from related work.

**Significance**: 7/10 - The problem is important, but the practical gains are modest.

**Overall**: The paper presents a reasonable approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels, but the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. With the suggested revisions, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, I recommend **weak acceptance**.
