

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU scores between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors present two variants: a basic e-SVM for strong supervision (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved). The approach is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with experiments on PASCAL VOC datasets showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. The work relates to previous weakly supervised learning methods (MIL and WSVMs), correctly identifying the distinction between their problem setting (partial overlap of segments with ground truth) and MIL's assumption of at least one fully positive instance.

## Quality
The technical formulation of e-SVM is sound, with proper mathematical derivation of the expectation loss function and clear presentation of the latent extension. The method extends standard SVM theory to handle continuous labels, which is well-justified. The experiments are thorough in showing improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple tasks (segment classification, semantic segmentation, object detection). However, the paper lacks statistical significance tests for the reported improvements, and there is insufficient ablation study to isolate the contribution of the e-SVM component versus other methodological choices (e.g., the specific feature extraction method). The paper also does not compare against more recent weakly supervised learning approaches beyond SVC and SVR, which would better establish the novelty.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The mathematical formulations are clear, and the experimental setup is adequately described. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the main results, though some implementation specifics (e.g., exact hyperparameter values for the latent e-SVM optimization) are missing. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the framework. The explanation of the new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) is clear and well-motivated for the segment classification task.

## Originality
The core idea of using an expectation loss over continuous labels rather than discretizing them is novel for the specific problem setting of segment classification with partial overlap. The paper correctly identifies this as distinct from MIL (which assumes at least one fully positive instance in a positive bag) and from weighted SVMs (which weight data points rather than labels). However, the approach itself is a relatively straightforward application of expectation-maximization and weighted SVM concepts to a computer vision problem, rather than introducing fundamentally new theoretical concepts. The novelty is more in the application and specific formulation for this problem rather than in the underlying machine learning principle. The paper adequately references related work but could better contextualize how their approach differs from recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL and WSVMs.

## Significance
The problem of weakly supervised learning is indeed important for reducing annotation costs in computer vision. The paper demonstrates practical utility by applying the method to two major computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) and shows consistent improvements over baselines. The 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is modest but meaningful in the context of object detection, where small gains are often significant. However, the paper does not sufficiently contextualize this improvement relative to other recent advances in object detection (e.g., how this compares to other weakly supervised approaches). The claim that the method "achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance" is somewhat overstated, as the improvement over RCNN is incremental (1.5% AP), and the paper does not compare against other state-of-the-art detectors.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary
**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem setting with clear justification for weakly supervised learning
- Sound technical formulation of the e-SVM algorithm
- Comprehensive experiments across multiple tasks and evaluation metrics
- Clear presentation of the method and results
- Appropriate comparison to relevant baselines (SVC and SVR)

**Weaknesses:**
- Novelty is somewhat incremental; builds on existing weighted SVM concepts
- Lacks statistical significance testing and thorough ablation studies
- Insufficient comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods
- Overstates the significance of the 1.5% AP improvement
- Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases

## Recommendation
The paper presents a technically sound approach to a meaningful problem in weakly supervised learning, with consistent improvements over baselines in multiple computer vision tasks. While the novelty is incremental rather than transformative, the work is of sufficient quality to be accepted. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods, better contextualization of the significance of the performance gains, and additional ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the e-SVM component. Given the importance of weakly supervised learning and the paper's solid technical contribution, I recommend acceptance.
