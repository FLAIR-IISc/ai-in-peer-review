Summary
This paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “soft” labels (positiveness in [0,1], instantiated as IoU between a segment proposal and ground-truth). The key idea is to replace the hinge loss with its expectation under a Bernoulli label whose mean µi = g(ui) (set to µi=ui). This yields a sample-wise combination of positive and negative hinge losses weighted by the soft label. The method extends to weak supervision by treating ui as latent and regularizing it towards an initial estimate ubb (IoU to ground-truth bounding boxes) via a KL penalty, with alternating minimization over w and u. Applications to segment confidence prediction, semantic segmentation, and object detection are demonstrated. For detection, the authors introduce a spatial confidence map feature by max-pooling per-pixel segment confidences inside candidate windows and concatenating with RCNN features, reporting ~1.2–1.5 mAP gains on PASCAL VOC 2007. They also propose TAPC and NDCG metrics for directly evaluating segment confidence prediction.

Strengths
- Simple, general formulation that handles soft labels without thresholding; compatible with standard linear SVM solvers.
- Natural latent extension to leverage bounding boxes as weak supervision with a convex regularizer and straightforward alternating optimization.
- Practical contribution: spatial confidence map feature integrates segmentation cues into region-based detectors with minimal engineering.
- Empirical improvements over SVC and SVR for segment confidence prediction and a modest but nontrivial boost to RCNN on VOC07.
- Proposal of task-agnostic evaluation measures (TAPC, NDCG) for segment confidence ranking.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the expected hinge loss with probabilistic/soft labels and alternating updates over latent labels are closely related to prior work on soft-label SVMs, label uncertainty/noise models, and latent SVM/MIL variants; this connection is not fully acknowledged or compared empirically.
- The latent e-SVM is essentially a biconvex alt-min with a strong anchoring prior (KL to ubb); no analysis of convergence/stability or sensitivity to λR and choice of g(u).
- Modest empirical gains on detection (+1.2–1.5 mAP) with few ablations; unclear statistical significance and contribution breakdown (e.g., how much comes from the spatial confidence map vs. the underlying e-SVM training).
- Baselines are limited: no comparisons to logistic regression with cross-entropy on [0,1] targets, ranking SVM/listwise ranking, calibrated regression, probabilistic SVMs with label uncertainty, or MIL/latent-SVM style baselines under weak supervision.
- Clarity and presentation issues: missing/garbled citations, unclear reduction to “standard SVM solver,” and ambiguity in the optimization of u (closed-form? constraints?).
- Naming conflict: “e-SVM” is commonly used for Exemplar-SVM in vision, which may cause confusion.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level: the expected hinge loss is well-defined, and the latent variant is convex in each block with a reasonable regularizer. However, the paper lacks theoretical justification (e.g., calibration, consistency under soft labels), convergence guarantees beyond heuristic alternation, and ablations to support design choices (µi=ui, KL regularizer, spatial binning m, max vs. average pooling). Experimental comparisons are too narrow to fully substantiate broad claims.

Clarity
The core idea is easy to grasp, but the manuscript needs tightening. Specific issues:
- The connection to standard SVM solvers should be explicit (e.g., show that the expected hinge can be implemented by duplicating each sample with positive/negative labels and weights g(ui), 1−g(ui), or by weighted losses).
- Details of the u-update (convex program, constraints ui∈(0,1), solver, complexity) are missing.
- Hyperparameters (λw, λR), initialization, and stopping criteria should be reported; sensitivity analysis is absent.
- Several references are malformed or missing; figures/tables are referenced but not fully described.

Originality
The framing is a modest, intuitive extension of SVM to soft labels; similar ideas exist in soft/probabilistic label SVMs, cost-sensitive/weighted hinge losses, and latent SVM/MIL. The spatial confidence map feature is a reasonable engineering trick. Overall, the conceptual novelty is limited; the contribution is primarily a repackaging and application to segmentation/detection.

Significance
The method is easy to implement and could be useful for weakly supervised settings where soft labels arise naturally. However, gains over strong baselines are modest, and the lack of broader comparisons/analysis limits impact. The proposed TAPC/NDCG evaluation for segment confidence is potentially useful but underexplored.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the exact reduction that allows using LibLinear: do you reweight positive/negative hinge terms per sample or expand the dataset with weighted duplicate examples?
- How is ui constrained and optimized in practice in the latent step (closed-form vs. numerical solver)? Any convergence diagnostics?
- Sensitivity of results to g(·) (e.g., identity vs. calibrated mapping), λR, and m. Do results hold with different proposal methods (beyond CPMC, superpixels)?
- Can you provide comparisons to (i) logistic regression with cross-entropy on [0,1], (ii) ranking SVM/listwise ranking, (iii) latent SVM/MIL under weak supervision, and (iv) soft-label SVMs from prior literature?
- Are the detection gains statistically significant across runs? What is the overhead of generating spatial confidence maps?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple method for soft/weak supervision with demonstrated improvements.
- General framework likely transferable to other weakly supervised problems.
- Introduces task-agnostic evaluation metrics for segment confidence.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to soft-label and latent SVM literature.
- Narrow baselines and modest empirical gains; unclear significance.
- Missing theoretical and implementation details; presentation issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the idea is sensible and the engineering is neat, the conceptual novelty is limited and the empirical evidence is not strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening related work positioning, adding stronger baselines (including soft-label SVMs/logistic, ranking methods, and latent SVM/MIL), deeper analysis/ablations, and clarifying optimization details would make the contribution more compelling.
