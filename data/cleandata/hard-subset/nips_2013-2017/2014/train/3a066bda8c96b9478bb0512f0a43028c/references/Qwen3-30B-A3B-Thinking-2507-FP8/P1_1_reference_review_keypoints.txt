- Introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for continuous "positiveness" labels in [0,1] and a latent extension for unobserved positiveness.
- Treats labels as random variables and minimizes expected loss; applied to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC.
- Technical formulation and optimization are clear and experimentally appropriate; shows consistent improvements over SVC and SVR.
- Proposes two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) suited to continuous-label problems.
- Lacks statistical significance tests for reported performance improvements.
- No sensitivity analysis to regularization choices or initialization of positiveness values.
- Theoretical analysis is limited: convex optimization noted but no convergence rates or guarantees provided.
- "Positiveness" is not clearly defined relative to existing weakly supervised concepts.
- Insufficiently developed connection and mathematical comparison to weighted SVM literature.
- Connection between the regularization term and KL divergence is under-explained.
- Novelty: expectation-of-loss idea is new for SVMs, but differentiation from prior weakly supervised methods is weak.
- Use of IoU as positiveness is standard; primary novelty is integrating continuous values into SVMs.
- Significance: addresses an important problem and shows modest but non-trivial gains (e.g., 1.5% AP on VOC2007).
- Practical limitations not fully discussed: scalability, sensitivity to bounding box quality, and generality beyond tested tasks.
- Specific suggestions: compare more thoroughly to weighted SVMs, add significance tests, empirically test ubb assumption sensitivity, run ablation studies for generality, and report computational cost.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—meaningful contribution but needs stronger evaluation and related-work discussion.
- Pros: meaningful problem, novel method, consistent improvements, clear presentation, useful weakly supervised framework.
- Cons: limited theory, modest gains, weak related-work connections, limited discussion of practical limitations.
