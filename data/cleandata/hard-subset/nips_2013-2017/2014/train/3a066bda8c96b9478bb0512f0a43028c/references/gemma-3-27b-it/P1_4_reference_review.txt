## Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data where the “ground truth” is not a hard label but a continuous value representing the “positiveness” of a proposal.  The core idea is to treat the positiveness as an expected value and incorporate it into the SVM loss function.  The authors demonstrate the versatility of e-SVM, applying it to semantic segmentation and object detection, and showcase its superior performance compared to standard SVC and SVR methods in both strongly and weakly supervised scenarios. They effectively leverage existing segmentation techniques (CPMC) and object detection frameworks (R-CNN) to demonstrate the utility of their approach.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of weakly supervised learning, highlighting the distinction between their approach and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL).  They accurately acknowledge prior work using SVMs for related tasks (SVC, SVR) and explain the limitations of directly applying these methods to the problem of continuous label prediction. The connection to weighted SVMs is also noted, with a clear explanation of the key difference: weighting labels rather than data points.  The cited literature appears relevant and supports the authors’ claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a principled approach to handling continuous “positiveness” values in a classification setting.  It provides a coherent and theoretically sound extension to the standard SVM framework.
* **Versatility:** The algorithm's ability to operate in both strongly and weakly supervised settings is a significant strength. The latent e-SVM extension offers a practical solution for leveraging readily available, but imperfect, annotations (e.g., bounding boxes).
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements over baseline methods (SVC, SVR) across multiple tasks (segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, object detection).  The 1.5% average AP improvement on object detection using R-CNN is a particularly encouraging result.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM loss function is clear, and the explanations of the experimental setup are sufficiently detailed.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm doesn't require a specialized solver and can leverage readily available SVM implementations (e.g., LibLinear). The spatial confidence map feature for object detection is a clever and effective way to integrate e-SVM predictions with existing detection pipelines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of Regularization:** While the paper mentions the importance of regularization in the latent e-SVM, the justification for the specific KL-divergence regularization term feels somewhat ad-hoc.  A more thorough analysis of different regularization strategies and their impact on performance would strengthen the paper.
* **Sensitivity to Segment Proposals:** The performance of e-SVM inherently depends on the quality of the initial segment proposals (e.g., CPMC segments).  The paper doesn't fully address the potential for poor segment proposals to negatively impact results. A discussion of robustness to different segment proposal methods would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to More Recent Methods:** While comparisons to SVC and SVR are valid baselines, the object detection experiments could be further strengthened by comparing against more recent and sophisticated object detection frameworks beyond R-CNN, such as Faster R-CNN or YOLO.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study exploring the contributions of different components of the framework (e.g., the spatial confidence map, the choice of image features) would provide a better understanding of the key factors driving performance.
* **Figure 3 Resolution:** The TAPC figure 3 is difficult to interpret due to low resolution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The e-SVM algorithm offers a novel and principled approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods.
* The algorithm is versatile, practical, and well-integrated with existing computer vision techniques.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The analysis of regularization could be more thorough.
* The sensitivity to segment proposal quality is not fully addressed.
* The comparison to more recent object detection methods is limited.
* A more detailed ablation study would further strengthen the conclusions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a well-motivated and effective solution to a challenging problem, and the empirical results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen regularization term in the latent e-SVM.
2.  Discuss the potential impact of segment proposal quality and consider exploring robustness to different proposal methods.
3.  Consider adding a comparison to more recent object detection frameworks.
4.  Improve the resolution of Figure 3.
5.  Consider including a brief ablation study.
