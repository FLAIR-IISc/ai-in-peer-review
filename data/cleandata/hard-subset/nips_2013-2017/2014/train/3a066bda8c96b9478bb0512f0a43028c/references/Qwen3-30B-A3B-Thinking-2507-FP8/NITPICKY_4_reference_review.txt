

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, where the training data consists of segment proposals with continuous "positiveness" scores (e.g., IoU with ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors claim that their method can handle both strong supervision (when positiveness is observed) and weak supervision (when positiveness is latent), and demonstrate applications in semantic segmentation and object detection.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper attempts to address a gap in weakly supervised learning where segment proposals have continuous positiveness values rather than binary labels. The authors position their work against SVC (which requires thresholding) and SVR (which is not designed for bounded outputs). They claim their e-SVM model can handle this problem more naturally by treating the label as a random variable with probability determined by the positiveness.

The paper draws connections to weighted SVMs (WSVM) but fails to properly distinguish how their approach differs. The authors also briefly mention MIL (Multiple Instance Learning) but provide an inadequate justification for why their problem setting differs from MIL.

## Major Weaknesses

### 1. Mathematical Errors in Core Formulation (Critical)
The most serious issue is the mathematical formulation of the e-SVM loss function in Equation (1). The authors write:
"L(w) = λw · 1 / wTw + N N∑ i=1 EYi [max(0, 1− YiwTxi)]"

This is mathematically nonsensical. The term "λw · 1 / wTw" should be λ/2 ||w||² (standard SVM regularization), but the authors have written it incorrectly as λw · 1 / wTw, which is not a valid mathematical expression. Similarly, "N N∑ i=1" should be "1/N ∑_{i=1}^N" but is written incorrectly as "N N∑ i=1".

The paper then claims their loss function "is equivalent to 'weight' each sample with a function of its positiveness," but the mathematical formulation doesn't support this claim due to the errors above. The standard SVM loss function for classification is not defined this way, and the authors fail to properly derive how their formulation relates to standard SVMs.

### 2. Inconsistent and Confusing Notation
The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example:
- "u" vs "ui" vs "ubbi" for the positiveness variable
- "l+i" vs "l_i^+" for the hinge loss terms
- "w" vs "w" vs "[w b]" for the weight vector

This inconsistency makes it extremely difficult to follow the mathematical derivations, especially in the critical Equation (1).

### 3. Misunderstanding of IoU as a Segment Property
The paper states: "the positiveness of each segment proposal is set as the Intersection over Union (IoU) overlap ratio between the proposal and the pixel level instance groundtruth." This is fundamentally incorrect. IoU is a measure of overlap between a segment proposal and the ground truth, not a property of the segment itself. The segment proposal doesn't "have" an IoU value; the IoU value is computed between the proposal and the ground truth. This misunderstanding undermines the entire foundation of the paper.

### 4. Lack of Proper Experimental Comparison
The paper claims "our model outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios" but fails to provide sufficient experimental details to verify this claim. The paper states "average improvement of 1.5 percent on per-class average precision (AP)" without comparing against state-of-the-art methods. The paper also claims "an average AP of 60.0" but doesn't specify what the previous best was (RCNN was 58.5, but they don't cite this properly).

### 5. Weak Justification for Methodological Choices
The paper states "we simply set µi = ui" but provides no theoretical justification for why the success probability of the Bernoulli distribution should equal the IoU value. This is a critical assumption that is not properly motivated.

The paper also uses a regularization term based on KL divergence but provides no justification for why this specific form is appropriate for their problem, nor does it compare alternative regularization approaches.

## Minor Weaknesses

- The paper claims "e-SVM can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver" but the mathematical formulation is incorrect, so this claim is invalid.
- The paper uses inconsistent capitalization ("Expectation Loss SVM" vs "e-SVM" vs "expectation loss SVM").
- The paper refers to "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but doesn't include these figures in the provided text.
- The paper states "we can use any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers" but provides no evidence that the method is robust to these choices.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine problem in weakly supervised learning where segment proposals have continuous positiveness values. The idea of using the positiveness as a probability rather than thresholding it is conceptually interesting. The applications to semantic segmentation and object detection are relevant to computer vision.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a fundamentally flawed mathematical formulation that undermines the entire contribution. The core loss function (Equation 1) is mathematically incorrect, making it impossible to understand how the proposed method works or to reproduce the results. The paper also contains numerous notational inconsistencies and misunderstandings of basic concepts like IoU.

While the problem addressed is relevant to computer vision, the mathematical errors are so severe that the paper cannot be accepted as is. The authors would need to completely revise the mathematical formulation, provide a proper derivation of the loss function, and clarify the conceptual misunderstandings before the paper could be considered for publication.

For these reasons, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from major revisions to address the critical mathematical errors before it could be considered for publication.
