## Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios, specifically where continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap) are available for segment proposals instead of binary labels. The authors propose a loss function that weights samples by their positiveness, extending the standard SVM framework. They demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM and its latent variable extension (for truly weakly supervised settings with bounding box annotations only) on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, consistently outperforming Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work appropriately positions itself within the landscape of weakly supervised learning. It differentiates itself from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, which assume the presence of *at least one* fully positive instance, a condition that doesn’t hold in their problem setting where proposals often contain both foreground and background.  The paper acknowledges and builds upon previous work utilizing weighted SVMs, highlighting the key distinction of weighting *labels* instead of data points. The authors are aware of recent advances in semantic segmentation and object detection, referencing relevant publications and positioning their work as a contribution towards reducing reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a clever extension of the standard SVM, addressing the limitations of applying binary classification or regression directly to continuous positiveness values.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the loss function and the justification for treating positiveness as latent variables in the weakly supervised case are well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results across three tasks (segment classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection) on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC).  The consistent outperformance of e-SVM over SVC and SVR provides strong support for the proposed approach.
* **Practicality & Generality:** The authors highlight the algorithm’s flexibility, noting its compatibility with various segment proposal methods, image features, and base detectors.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the concepts clearly, making it accessible to a broad audience. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is particularly helpful.
* **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** Reducing the need for expensive pixel-level annotations is a crucial challenge in computer vision, making this work highly relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains, a more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different regularization terms (particularly in the latent e-SVM) and feature choices would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter settings (e.g., the regularization parameter in the latent e-SVM).  Providing some insight into how these parameters were tuned would increase confidence in the results.
* **NDCG Metric:** The justification for using Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) in Section 5.1.1 feels slightly weak. While ranking is relevant, the connection to segment classification isn’t fully established, and a clearer explanation would strengthen the argument for its inclusion.
* **Incremental Improvements:** The 1.5% average improvement in object detection, while statistically significant, may be viewed as relatively modest given the complexity of the method. A discussion of potential reasons for this and directions for future improvement would be valuable.
* **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Weakly Supervised Methods:** While the comparison to SVC and SVR is relevant, the field of weakly supervised learning has evolved. A comparison against more recent, sophisticated approaches (e.g., those employing consistency regularization or contrastive learning) would provide a stronger benchmark.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous label information.
*  The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over established baselines across multiple tasks.
*  The method is practical, flexible, and addresses a significant challenge in computer vision.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a thorough ablation study and discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity limits the understanding of the method’s robustness.
* The incremental improvement in object detection, while positive, might not be substantial enough to warrant strong acceptance.
* The absence of comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is a clever and well-motivated extension of the standard SVM framework. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.  While some aspects (e.g., ablation study, hyperparameter analysis) could be strengthened, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a more detailed ablation study, exploring the impact of different regularization terms in the latent e-SVM and feature choices.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter settings.
3.  Provide a clearer justification for using the NDCG metric.
4.  Consider including a comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods in their experimental evaluation.
