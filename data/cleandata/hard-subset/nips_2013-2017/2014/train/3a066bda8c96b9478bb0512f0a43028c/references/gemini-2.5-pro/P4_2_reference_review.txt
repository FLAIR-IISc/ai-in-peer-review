**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for scenarios where training labels are continuous "positiveness" values within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]), rather than binary. This is common in computer vision tasks where, for instance, a segment proposal's quality is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea of e-SVM is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of the observed positiveness score. The objective function then minimizes the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this latent label.

This formulation leads to a weighted SVM-like problem that can be solved efficiently. The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting, termed latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are optimized alternately with the SVM model parameters. A regularization term is introduced to guide the estimation of these latent variables.

The paper evaluates the proposed methods on two fundamental computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection, using the PASCAL VOC datasets. For evaluating segment proposal classification, the authors introduce two new metrics: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). Experiments show that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) on this task. Furthermore, by generating a novel "spatial confidence map feature" from the latent e-SVM, the authors demonstrate that their method can provide complementary information to a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN), improving its average precision by a significant margin of 1.5%. The work positions itself as an alternative to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) for this type of weak supervision and relates its formulation to, while distinguishing itself from, instance-weighted SVMs.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the expected loss is principled and provides a clear probabilistic interpretation for handling continuous labels. The extension to the latent variable model for weak supervision is a logical and powerful generalization. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against relevant and strong baselines (SVC, SVR, and R-CNN). The claims of outperforming SVC/SVR are well-supported by results on the newly proposed and well-motivated evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG). The claim of improving a state-of-the-art object detector is backed by a notable 1.5% mAP increase on PASCAL VOC 2007, which is a significant result on a competitive benchmark. The work appears complete, presenting a novel algorithm, an extension to weak supervision, and successful applications to major vision tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the proposed model is described methodically. Algorithm 1 provides a clear high-level overview of the optimization procedure for the latent e-SVM. However, some areas could benefit from additional clarification:
*   The update step for the latent variables `u` in Algorithm 1 (line 4) is not explicitly derived or stated. Given the form of the loss function and the KL-divergence regularizer, this update is not trivial, and its inclusion would improve the reproducibility of the work.
*   The distinction from weighted SVMs (WSVM) is subtle. The paper states that e-SVM weights labels instead of data points. While formally correct, the resulting objective is very similar to a WSVM where positive and negative slack variables for each instance are weighted differently. A more formal comparison in the main text or an appendix would strengthen this point.
*   More detail on the construction of the spatial confidence map feature would be helpful. For example, the pooling method (average pooling is mentioned later) and the application of additive kernel approximation could be described more explicitly in Section 4.2.

**Originality:** The paper presents several novel contributions.
*   The core e-SVM formulation, which recasts the problem of learning from continuous labels as minimizing an expected hinge loss, is novel and elegant.
*   The application and extension of this idea to the weakly supervised setting using bounding boxes is a valuable contribution to an important area of research.
*   The proposed spatial confidence map feature is a novel way to integrate segmentation-based cues into a window-based object detector.
*   The introduction of the TAPC and NDCG metrics for evaluating segment proposal classification is a useful contribution in its own right, providing a more principled way to measure performance on this task than task-specific final accuracies.
The paper does a good job of citing and differentiating its approach from related work in weakly supervised learning (e.g., MIL) and weighted SVMs.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. Learning from weak supervision is a critical problem for scaling machine learning models to large datasets where detailed annotation is infeasible. The specific problem of leveraging bounding box annotations—which are far more common than pixel-level masks—is of great practical importance. The e-SVM algorithm is general and could potentially be applied to other domains outside of computer vision where labels are noisy or continuous. The empirical results, particularly the improvement over the very strong R-CNN baseline, demonstrate that the proposed method provides tangible benefits and advances the state of the art in object detection. This is likely to inspire follow-up work on integrating weakly supervised segmentation cues into detection pipelines.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The e-SVM model is an elegant and novel method for handling continuous-valued labels in a max-margin framework.
2.  **High Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses the important problem of learning from weak supervision (bounding boxes), which has broad practical implications.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform relevant baselines on segment classification and, more importantly, achieves a significant improvement over a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN).
4.  **Useful Methodological Contributions:** The paper introduces novel evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) and a new feature representation (spatial confidence map) that are valuable contributions on their own.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
1.  **Lack of Detail on Optimization:** Key details of the optimization algorithm for the latent e-SVM are omitted, which could hinder reproducibility.
2.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from an analysis of the key hyperparameters, such as the regularization weight `λR`, and a more explicit experimental comparison of the latent e-SVM with and without the regularization term `R(u)`.
3.  **Clarity on Relation to Prior Work:** The distinction from instance-weighted SVMs could be made more formal and clear.
