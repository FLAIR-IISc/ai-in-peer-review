

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces an innovative learning framework called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where training labels are continuous "positiveness" values between 0 and 1 (e.g., IoU scores), rather than binary labels. The authors propose treating the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the observed positiveness, then formulating a loss function that takes the expectation over this random variable. This approach elegantly bridges the gap between standard classification (SVC) and regression (SVR) problems in weakly supervised settings.

The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty of their approach. Standard SVC requires arbitrary thresholding to convert continuous positiveness to binary labels, while SVR doesn't properly account for the bounded [0,1] nature of the target variable. The authors demonstrate that their e-SVM formulation avoids these pitfalls and provides a principled way to handle continuous labels. The extension to latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved) is particularly valuable, as it allows the method to work with common bounding box annotations rather than requiring pixel-level ground truth.

The authors validate their approach on two important computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation (with both pixel-level and bounding box annotations) and object detection (using bounding box annotations). Their experiments show consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines, with the most notable result being a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection when combined with RCNN. The method is also shown to be flexible and generalizable to different segment proposal methods and feature representations.

This work stands out for its novel conceptual approach to handling continuous labels in weak supervision, which is a genuine gap in the literature. While the performance gains are modest (1.5% AP improvement), the authors present a clean, principled solution to a real problem in computer vision where manual pixel-level labeling is prohibitively expensive. The paper's strength lies in its conceptual novelty rather than technical breakthroughs.

The main weaknesses are: (1) the theoretical analysis is limited (no convergence guarantees for the latent e-SVM optimization), (2) the regularization term (Equation 3) is somewhat ad-hoc, and (3) the performance gains, while consistent, are not dramatic for a top-tier venue. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over incremental improvements, I find these limitations acceptable given the paper's strong conceptual contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the growing literature on weakly supervised learning. The authors adequately address related work, particularly in the context of Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs, and clearly articulate how their approach differs from these methods.

In terms of significance, this work addresses an important practical problem in computer vision (reducing labeling costs) with a novel, generalizable approach that could be applied to many other domains beyond computer vision. The authors successfully demonstrate its utility on two fundamental computer vision tasks with reasonable improvements.

The paper's novelty is substantial: it introduces a fundamentally different way to formulate the learning problem when labels are continuous, rather than simply improving existing methods. This conceptual shift is the most valuable aspect of the work, and aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on bold, novel approaches.

While the performance gains are modest, they are consistent across multiple tasks and evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). The paper also provides clear implementation details and shows how the method can be easily integrated into existing systems (e.g., the object detection framework), making it practically useful.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and principled approach to weakly supervised learning that addresses a genuine gap in the literature. While the experimental results are not revolutionary, the conceptual contribution is strong and the method is well-motivated and well-executed. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and should be accepted. The paper's conceptual innovation outweighs the modest performance gains and limited theoretical analysis.
