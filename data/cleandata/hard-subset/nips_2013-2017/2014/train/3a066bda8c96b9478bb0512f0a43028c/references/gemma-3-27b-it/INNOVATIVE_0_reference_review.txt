This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a method designed for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where only partial labels (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level annotations) are available. The core idea – weighting samples by their ‘positiveness’ reflecting the degree of correctness of a proposal – is conceptually elegant and addresses a practical challenge in large-scale dataset annotation. The authors convincingly argue why standard SVM and SVR approaches are ill-suited for this problem and demonstrate improvements on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.

The novelty of this work lies in the formulation of the e-SVM and its latent extension, adapting SVM principles to handle continuous, probabilistic labels rather than strict binary classifications. This is a non-trivial extension and opens avenues for applying SVM-based methods to more realistically labeled datasets. The generalizability claim – that the method is adaptable to various segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors – further adds to its potential impact. While weighted SVMs exist, the weighting *of labels* rather than data points, as presented here, provides a distinct perspective and practical advantage.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a fresh approach to weakly supervised learning, offering a principled way to handle continuous label estimates.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – learning from weakly labeled data – is incredibly important given the cost of full annotation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods (SVC and SVR). The 1.5% AP improvement on object detection, while not massive, is significant given the state-of-the-art baseline (RCNN).
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and the justification for extending standard SVMs is well-reasoned.
*   **Generality:** The claim of adaptability across various components is appealing and suggests broad applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Depth of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for the specific form of the loss function. While it’s motivated intuitively, a more rigorous analysis of its properties would be beneficial.
*   **Reliance on IoU:** The use of IoU as the ground truth for 'positiveness' is a simplifying assumption. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t explore alternative measures or sensitivity to IoU thresholds.
*   **Algorithm 1 – Latent SVM Optimization:** The optimization algorithm for the latent SVM is described briefly. Details regarding convergence criteria, regularization parameter selection, and computational cost are missing. A more detailed description would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to SVC and SVR are solid, the authors could further strengthen the paper by comparing to more recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond those mentioned in the related work section, particularly those leveraging MIL or related techniques.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** There's minimal analysis of the individual components' contribution. For example, what is the specific impact of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a critical problem in machine learning.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM.
*   The method is well-motivated and theoretically sound, despite some areas for deeper analysis.
*   The potential impact on weakly supervised learning is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is somewhat limited.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons to a wider range of recent methods and ablation studies.
*   The practical details of the latent SVM optimization are lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While there are minor flaws and areas where the work could be extended, the novelty, practicality, and promising empirical results outweigh these concerns. The e-SVM offers a compelling framework for leveraging imperfectly labeled data and has the potential to influence future research in this area. I recommend **acceptance** despite the minor issues. The boldness of the approach and the clear potential for impact justify overlooking the incomplete validations. I suggest the authors address the points raised regarding theoretical analysis, optimization details, and comprehensive comparisons in a future revision.
