This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing scenarios where only partial or noisy labels (positiveness scores) are available for segment proposals. The core idea is to treat these positiveness scores as latent variables and formulate a modified SVM loss function that accounts for their uncertainty. The authors demonstrate the method on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving improvements over standard SVC and SVR baselines, and even a modest gain when integrated into a state-of-the-art object detector (RCNN).

While the problem addressed – learning with weakly supervised data – is undoubtedly important and relevant to the NIPS community, this paper presents a fairly incremental advance that relies heavily on empirical validation. The core concept of weighting samples based on ‘positiveness’ isn’t particularly novel; weighted SVMs have a significant history (as acknowledged by the authors), and the extension to treat positiveness as latent isn't groundbreaking. The novelty lies more in the specific application to segment proposal confidence prediction and the combination with existing techniques. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments on both semantic segmentation and object detection are reasonably comprehensive, utilizing established datasets (PASCAL VOC) and comparing against relevant baselines. The inclusion of both pixel-level and bounding-box annotation scenarios is a positive aspect. The NDCG and TAPC metrics, while not standard, are a sensible attempt to directly evaluate segment-wise prediction performance.  The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the simplicity of the integration with RCNN.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the theoretical justification for the proposed loss function is weak. While the authors state it’s an extension of SVC, the connection to established weighted SVM literature remains superficial. A more detailed analysis of the loss function’s properties (e.g., convexity, relationship to other loss functions) would be beneficial. Secondly, the performance gains, while statistically significant in some cases, are often modest (e.g., 1.5% AP improvement on object detection). This raises the question of whether the added complexity of e-SVM is justified, particularly given the need for parameter tuning and the iterative optimization procedure.  Thirdly, the regularization term introduced in the latent e-SVM formulation feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The justification based on KL divergence seems contrived, and a more rigorous derivation or sensitivity analysis would be needed. The description of how this term prevents the algorithm from getting stuck in local minima is also unclear. Finally, the paper relies heavily on IoU as a measure of "positiveness." While common, this is a potentially flawed proxy, as it doesn't account for the semantic correctness of segment proposals – a segment with high IoU might still contain irrelevant background.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem (weakly supervised learning).
* Thorough empirical evaluation on standard datasets.
* Demonstrates improvements over established baselines.
* Clear presentation of the proposed method and experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Incremental contribution; lacks significant theoretical novelty.
* Modest performance gains in some cases.
* Ad-hoc regularization term in the latent e-SVM formulation.
* Reliance on IoU as a potentially flawed "positiveness" metric.
* Limited theoretical analysis of the proposed loss function.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the work is technically sound and demonstrates a practical improvement, it doesn't represent a significant breakthrough. The paper is well-executed, but lacks the originality and impact typically expected of a NIPS publication.  The authors have performed a good engineering effort, but it reads more like a well-documented application of existing techniques than a truly novel contribution to machine learning. I would recommend acceptance only if there is limited competition in this area and the program committee needs to fill slots. Major revisions are not warranted, but the authors should be encouraged to address the theoretical weaknesses and explore more robust measures of segment "positiveness" in future work.
