This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing scenarios where only partial or noisy labels are available, like bounding boxes instead of pixel-level segmentations. The core idea is to model the “positiveness” of proposals (e.g., segments) as a continuous value between 0 and 1, and to use this value as a weight in the SVM loss function. The authors address both the observed positiveness (strong supervision) and latent positiveness (weak supervision) cases, presenting an iterative optimization algorithm for the latter. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines, and achieving competitive results when combined with a state-of-the-art object detector (RCNN).

The paper builds upon prior work in weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. It distinguishes itself from MIL by addressing a scenario where proposals are not necessarily fully positive or negative, and from standard weighted SVMs by weighting labels rather than data points. The work also relates to existing techniques for segment proposal confidence prediction using SVR, but argues that e-SVM is better suited for the bounded nature of the positiveness values.  The problem setting is relevant and increasingly important, given the cost of detailed labeling for large datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation and its extension to latent variables provide a novel approach to handling continuous, proposal-specific labels in weakly supervised learning. Weighting labels rather than instances is a sensible adaptation of SVMs for this problem.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the loss function is clear and well-motivated. The iterative optimization algorithm for the latent variable case appears reasonable, and the paper provides sufficient detail for implementation. The use of standard SVM solvers is an advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on three tasks (segment classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection).  The comparisons to SVC and SVR demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The improvement on the RCNN baseline for object detection is encouraging.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the key contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent and understandable.
* **Generality:** The authors explicitly highlight the generality of their approach, noting that it can be applied with different segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the mathematical formulation is clear, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of the properties of the e-SVM algorithm. For example, it would be beneficial to discuss the convergence properties of the iterative optimization algorithm, or to provide bounds on the generalization error.
* **Regularization in Latent SVM:** The choice of the regularization term for the latent variable (Equation 3) feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors provide justification, a more detailed discussion of alternative regularization strategies and their potential impact on performance would be helpful.  The claim that without the regularization term, the algorithm "can lead to stuck in the local minimal" is not fully supported by experimental evidence.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** While the introduction of TAPC and NDCG for segment classification is a positive step, their practical relevance compared to more standard metrics (like average precision on the segmentation task itself) is not fully established.
* **Comparison Depth:** While results show performance improvements, a discussion of *why* e-SVM outperforms SVR and SVC beyond simply stating that SVR is not designed for bounded values would be valuable. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different components of the e-SVM model could also strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a variety of tasks.
* The paper addresses a practically important problem with significant potential impact.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed theoretical analysis limits the understanding of the algorithm's properties.
* The choice of regularization term in the latent SVM case could benefit from further justification.
* The evaluation metrics, while novel, require stronger justification for their relevance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The proposed e-SVM algorithm is novel, technically sound, and empirically well-validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above (particularly regarding theoretical analysis and regularization).
