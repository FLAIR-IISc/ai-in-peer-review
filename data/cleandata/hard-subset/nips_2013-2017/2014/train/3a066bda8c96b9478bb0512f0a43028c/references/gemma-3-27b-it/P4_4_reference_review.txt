The paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data, particularly where labels are continuous values representing “positiveness” rather than binary classifications. The core idea is to model the label as a Bernoulli random variable with a success probability dependent on this positiveness, allowing the algorithm to handle scenarios with bounding box annotations where precise segmentation is unavailable. The paper demonstrates the e-SVM’s effectiveness in segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, outperforming SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

This work builds upon the established field of weakly supervised learning, specifically addressing limitations of existing methods like SVC and SVR when dealing with continuous label values. Related work includes Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs, which are appropriately discussed and differentiated from the proposed approach. The authors highlight the practical relevance of this work by focusing on scenarios where bounding box annotations are readily available but pixel-level annotations are costly to obtain, a common situation in large-scale datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM introduces a novel loss function that directly addresses the problem of continuous label values in weakly supervised learning.  Modeling the label as a Bernoulli distribution based on positiveness is a sensible and effective approach.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM is clear and well-defined. The iterative optimization algorithm for the latent e-SVM variant is also presented with sufficient detail. The use of convex optimization ensures a robust solution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC) for both semantic segmentation and object detection. The comparison to SVC and SVR demonstrates consistent performance improvements.  The inclusion of both pixel-level and bounding box annotation scenarios strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a comprehensible manner.  Figure 1 provides a helpful overview of the framework.
* **Generality:** The authors rightly point out the flexibility of their approach, highlighting its compatibility with different segment proposal detectors, image features, and base detectors.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Comparisons:** While the comparison to SVC and SVR is valuable, the paper could benefit from comparing e-SVM against more recent and sophisticated weakly supervised learning methods, particularly those leveraging deep learning beyond simply concatenating features with RCNN. Methods like MIM or recent contrastive learning approaches for weakly supervised segmentation/detection would be relevant comparisons.
* **Regularization Parameter Sensitivity:** The description of the latent e-SVM regularization term mentions the importance of preventing `u` from becoming 0 or 1 but doesn’t provide guidance on selecting the appropriate regularization strength (`λR`).  An ablation study demonstrating the impact of this parameter on performance would be helpful.
* **Detailed Implementation:** While the framework is explained well, details on the specific implementation of feature extraction, segment proposal generation (CPMC parameters) and hyperparameter tuning could be more transparent to facilitate reproducibility.
* **NDCG Metric Interpretation:** The justification for using NDCG as an evaluation metric could be strengthened. While relevant for ranking problems, its direct applicability to segment classification beyond the TAPC curve isn’t fully explained.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The e-SVM presents a novel and technically sound approach to weakly supervised learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over established baselines on standard datasets.
* The approach is flexible and applicable to various computer vision tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to existing literature is not exhaustive and could be strengthened by including more recent and relevant methods.
* The lack of detailed implementation information and sensitivity analysis on key parameters (e.g. regularization strength) hinders reproducibility and full understanding.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm offers a principled approach to handling continuous label values and demonstrates strong performance on standard datasets. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation and implementation details could be improved, the novelty, technical soundness, and clarity of the paper make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should expand the related work section to include more recent methods and provide more details on implementation and hyperparameter tuning.
