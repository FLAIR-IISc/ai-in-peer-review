

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous "positiveness" values rather than binary labels. The authors frame their approach as a solution to the problem of segment proposal classification, where the ground truth is represented by an IoU overlap ratio between segment proposals and ground truth. They claim their method outperforms SVC and SVR on both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.

The paper builds on the well-established SVM framework, introducing a loss function that treats labels as random variables following a Bernoulli distribution parameterized by the "positiveness" value. The authors present both a basic e-SVM model (for cases where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM model (for cases where positiveness is unobserved, such as with bounding box annotations). The experimental validation focuses on PASCAL VOC datasets, with the authors claiming a 1.5% improvement in average precision for object detection compared to RCNN.

**Strengths:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of weakly supervised learning in computer vision, where bounding box annotations are common but pixel-level annotations are expensive. The proposed method is well-motivated by the limitations of existing approaches (SVC and SVR) for handling continuous labels. The experimental setup is generally sound, with reasonable baselines and evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that are appropriate for the task. The extension to object detection via spatial confidence maps is a practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:** The paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. First, the core idea appears to be a special case of weighted SVMs, which have been extensively studied in the literature (e.g., Vapnik, 1998; Suykens et al., 2002). The authors' claim that their approach differs fundamentally from weighted SVMs is not substantiated by theoretical or experimental evidence. In fact, their loss function (equation 1) is a direct application of weighted SVM principles, with the weight being a function of the positiveness value.

Second, the experimental validation is insufficient. The authors report only marginal improvements (1.5% AP gain in object detection), which is not significant in the context of current computer vision research where even small gains (e.g., 0.5-1.0%) are considered noteworthy. The paper lacks ablation studies to determine what aspects of their approach are actually responsible for the improvement. They do not compare against state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods, such as those based on Multiple Instance Learning or other recent approaches to weak supervision.

Third, the paper's theoretical justification is weak. The authors claim their method "treats labels instead of data points," but this distinction is not meaningful in the context of SVMs, where both label and instance weighting are common. The Bernoulli distribution assumption (section 3.1) is not rigorously justified, and the authors do not explore the sensitivity of their approach to different parametric assumptions.

Finally, the paper does not adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, the regularization term R(u) (equation 3) is described as "strong" but the authors do not explain why this specific form is optimal or how it might affect performance on different datasets. The paper also does not discuss computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets.

**Overall Assessment:** While the problem of weakly supervised learning is important, this paper does not present a novel or significant contribution to the field. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward application of weighted SVM principles, with limited experimental validation that fails to demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods. The marginal performance improvements reported are not sufficient to justify the claim of a new method. The paper also fails to properly contextualize its work within the extensive literature on weighted SVMs and weakly supervised learning.

The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with existing methods, a stronger theoretical foundation, and more substantial experimental validation demonstrating the method's advantages. Without these improvements, the contribution appears incremental rather than novel.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an incremental extension of existing weighted SVM methods with limited experimental validation of its claims. The improvements over existing approaches are marginal and not convincingly demonstrated. The paper would require substantial revisions to establish a significant contribution to the field.
