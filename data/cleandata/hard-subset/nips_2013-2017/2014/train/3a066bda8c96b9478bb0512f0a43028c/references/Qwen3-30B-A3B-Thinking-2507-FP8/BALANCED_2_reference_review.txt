

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values (positiveness) in [0,1] rather than binary labels. The authors formulate the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability derived from the observed positiveness (e.g., IoU with ground truth), enabling the model to naturally handle continuous-valued labels without thresholding. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for weak supervision scenarios where positiveness is unobserved, using a regularized optimization approach. The authors apply e-SVM to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear problem formulation:** The paper effectively identifies the gap between standard classification (SVC) and regression (SVR) approaches for continuous-valued labels, providing a theoretically sound alternative. The justification for modeling labels as Bernoulli random variables is compelling and well-motivated.

2. **Strong empirical results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) in segment proposal classification. The application to semantic segmentation (27.7% vs. 36.8% with pixel-level labels) and object detection (1.5% AP improvement over RCNN) shows practical value.

3. **Practical implementation:** The method is straightforward to implement using standard SVM solvers (e.g., LibLinear), making it accessible to practitioners. The spatial confidence map feature for object detection is well-designed and effectively integrates with existing systems.

4. **Appropriate comparison to related work:** The paper clearly differentiates e-SVM from MIL and weighted SVM approaches, explaining why these methods are unsuitable for their problem setting. The comparison to SVC (with different thresholds) and SVR is thorough and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited theoretical justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis explaining *why* e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR. A more rigorous comparison of the optimization landscapes or generalization bounds would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods:** The paper focuses on comparing to SVC and SVR, but doesn't compare to more recent weakly supervised segmentation methods (e.g., those using MIL with better handling of partial labels). The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance on object detection is somewhat misleading since it's only compared to RCNN without contextualizing against more recent detectors.

3. **Insufficient justification for regularization:** The choice of the KL-divergence-based regularization term (equation 3) is not sufficiently justified. The authors should provide more theoretical or empirical evidence for why this specific regularization is optimal compared to alternatives.

4. **Implementation details:** Some critical implementation details are missing, such as specific hyperparameter settings (λ, λR), the exact features used, and the number of iterations for the latent optimization. These would be important for reproducibility.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address scenarios where the positiveness (IoU) is not well-correlated with the true label quality, which could be a significant limitation in some applications.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a well-motivated and practically useful approach to weakly supervised learning that addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The method is technically sound, well-implemented, and demonstrates clear improvements over relevant baselines. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the empirical results are convincing and the method is broadly applicable to other problems with continuous-valued labels.

The main weakness is the insufficient comparison to more recent weakly supervised segmentation methods and the lack of theoretical depth. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is a clean and effective solution to an important problem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel formulation of continuous-label SVM that avoids thresholding heuristics
- Strong empirical results across multiple computer vision tasks
- Simple implementation using standard SVM solvers
- Clear connection to practical applications with real-world annotation constraints

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited comparison to recent weakly supervised methods beyond SVC/SVR
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the approach
- Some implementation details missing for reproducibility

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised segmentation methods (e.g., [19, 20]) to better contextualize the results.
2. Provide more theoretical justification for the e-SVM formulation and the choice of regularization.
3. Include more implementation details (hyperparameters, optimization iterations) to improve reproducibility.
4. Discuss limitations more thoroughly, particularly regarding scenarios where the positiveness metric (IoU) may not correlate well with label quality.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of weakly supervised learning in computer vision. With minor revisions to address the theoretical depth and comparative analysis, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
