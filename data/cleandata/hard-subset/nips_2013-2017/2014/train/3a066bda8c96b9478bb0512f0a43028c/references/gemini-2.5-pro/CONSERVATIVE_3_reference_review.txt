**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel support vector machine formulation, termed the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning scenarios where supervision is provided as a continuous "positiveness" score between 0 and 1, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to model the true binary label as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, with the success probability given by the positiveness score. The SVM objective is then formulated as minimizing the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. The authors extend this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores are unknown and are treated as latent variables, optimized jointly with the model parameters via an alternating algorithm. The proposed method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. For object detection, features derived from the latent e-SVM are used to augment the R-CNN model, yielding a reported 1.5% improvement in mean Average Precision on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem in machine learning and computer vision: how to effectively learn from supervision that is weaker than full annotation but richer than simple binary labels (e.g., object bounding boxes providing coarse localization).
2.  **Principled Formulation:** The e-SVM formulation provides an elegant and theoretically grounded approach to incorporate continuous-valued labels into the max-margin framework. It presents a compelling alternative to ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or direct regression with SVR, which may not be perfectly suited for bounded interval targets.
3.  **Demonstrated Empirical Success:** The method shows consistent, albeit sometimes modest, improvements over standard SVC and SVR baselines in segment classification tasks. More significantly, its application to object detection demonstrates the ability to improve upon a very strong, state-of-the-art baseline (R-CNN), suggesting practical value.

**Weaknesses**

The paper's primary weaknesses lie in the rigor of its experimental validation and a lack of depth in the methodological discussion.

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The main empirical result is the 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 when augmenting R-CNN. However, this result is presented without a crucial baseline comparison. The improvement is attributed to the superiority of the e-SVM model, but it could simply be due to the addition of a segmentation-based confidence feature. A strong baseline would be to generate the same spatial confidence map feature using a standard SVR model trained to regress the IoU with the ground-truth bounding boxes. Without this ablation, it is impossible to conclude that the novel e-SVM formulation is the source of the performance gain, rather than the feature engineering itself. This is a significant omission that undermines the central claim of the paper.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Analysis:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM is presented as a standard alternating minimization procedure. However, the overall objective function is non-convex. The paper provides no discussion of the algorithm's convergence properties, its sensitivity to initialization, or the influence of the regularization hyperparameter λR. For a novel method, a more thorough analysis of the optimization landscape and algorithm behavior is expected.

3.  **Limited Novelty in Context:** While the "expectation loss" framing is new, the resulting objective function (Equation 1) is a weighted hinge loss where each sample `x_i` contributes two terms, `g(u_i) * l_i^+` and `(1-g(u_i)) * l_i^-`. This can be viewed as a specific instance of a cost-sensitive or weighted SVM. The paper mentions the connection to weighted SVMs but could benefit from a more detailed discussion situating the e-SVM within this broader literature and clarifying the key differences more formally.

4.  **Clarity and Justification:** The details of how the baselines (SVC, SVR) were trained in the weakly supervised setting (Figure 3b) are not explicitly stated. One presumes they used the IoU with the bounding box (`u_bb`) as the training target, but this should be made clear. Furthermore, the introduction of a new evaluation metric, TAPC, is not sufficiently motivated. It is unclear why this ad-hoc metric is preferable to more established methods for evaluating ranked predictions or regression outputs.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting and elegant idea for extending SVMs to handle continuous-valued labels, with a natural application to weakly supervised learning. The formulation is sound, and the results on a challenging object detection task are promising. However, the evaluation lacks the necessary rigor to fully support the claims of superiority over established methods. The missing comparison against an SVR-derived feature in the object detection experiment is a critical flaw. While the work has potential, it does not yet meet the high standards of validation required for publication at this venue. I would encourage the authors to conduct the necessary ablation studies and provide a more thorough analysis of their proposed optimization algorithm.
