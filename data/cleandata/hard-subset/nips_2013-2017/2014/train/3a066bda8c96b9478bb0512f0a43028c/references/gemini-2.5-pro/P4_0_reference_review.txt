**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for supervised learning problems where labels are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" score within the [0, 1] interval. This scenario is common in computer vision, for example, when evaluating segment proposals against ground truth using the Intersection over Union (IoU) metric. The core idea of e-SVM is to model the true binary label of a sample as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, with the success probability given by its known positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random variable. This results in a convex objective where each sample contributes weighted terms to both the positive and negative sides of the hinge loss, which can be solved using standard SVM solvers.

The paper further extends this model to a weakly supervised setting, termed latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are optimized alternately with the model weights. A regularization term, based on the KL divergence from an initial estimate of the positiveness, is introduced to guide the optimization.

The authors evaluate their method on two fundamental computer vision tasks. First, on segment proposal classification, they introduce two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) and show that e-SVM outperforms Support Vector Classification (SVC) with various thresholds and Support Vector Regression (SVR). Second, they apply latent e-SVM to object detection, integrating their learned segmentation cues as a "spatial confidence map feature" into the strong R-CNN baseline. This combined approach achieves a 1.5% mean Average Precision (mAP) improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, advancing the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the e-SVM loss function from an expected loss principle is clear and well-founded. The extension to the latent e-SVM using an alternating optimization scheme is a standard and appropriate approach for this type of problem. The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments. The authors compare their method against suitable baselines (SVC, SVR) on the segment classification sub-task. A particular strength is the introduction of two novel evaluation criteria, Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG), which are well-suited for evaluating predictors of continuous positiveness scores and represent a useful contribution for future work in this area. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of e-SVM over the baselines in this direct evaluation. The improvement shown on the object detection task, while applied to a very strong baseline (R-CNN), supports the claim that the method provides complementary information.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for addressing learning with continuous-valued labels is compelling. The mathematical formulation of both the basic and latent e-SVM models in Section 3 is presented clearly. The application of the model to generate spatial confidence map features for object detection is also well-explained and illustrated. The overall research contribution is easy to understand.

However, the manuscript could be improved by providing more detail for reproducibility. For example, the choice of the function `g(u_i) = u_i` is stated without justification or exploration of alternatives. The selection of the regularization parameters `λ` and `λ_R` is not discussed. Furthermore, the convergence criteria for the alternating optimization in Algorithm 1 are not specified.

**Originality:**
The formulation of the e-SVM is novel. While it has connections to weighted SVMs (WSVMs), as the authors note, the motivation from an expected loss perspective is new and provides a principled framework for handling labels in the [0,1] range. This contrasts with ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or direct application of SVR, which is not designed for a bounded target interval. The authors acknowledge the connection to WSVM but correctly point out the difference in weighting labels versus instances. The latent e-SVM is a logical extension to handle weak supervision. Integrating these ideas to improve a state-of-the-art object detection system is a novel application that demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed by the paper is highly significant. Reducing the reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations by effectively leveraging weaker forms of supervision, such as bounding boxes, is a critical goal for scaling machine learning models in computer vision and other fields. The proposed e-SVM provides a general and effective tool for this purpose. The demonstration that this method can provide a non-trivial performance boost (1.5% mAP) to a powerful deep learning-based detector like R-CNN is a strong result. This suggests that the approach captures useful appearance and segmentation cues that are complementary to the features learned by convolutional networks. The algorithm is general and could potentially be adopted by researchers in other domains facing similar learning problems with continuous confidence scores instead of hard labels.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel, principled, and intuitive learning algorithm (e-SVM) for handling continuous labels in a [0, 1] range.
2.  The latent variable extension effectively adapts the model to weakly supervised settings, which is a problem of high practical importance.
3.  The method is shown to outperform relevant baselines on the direct task of segment classification, evaluated with newly proposed and appropriate metrics.
4.  The work demonstrates significant practical impact by improving the state-of-the-art on the competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The final objective function of e-SVM bears a strong resemblance to certain weighted SVM formulations. A more in-depth theoretical comparison could strengthen the paper's claim to novelty.
2.  Reproducibility is somewhat hampered by a lack of detail regarding hyperparameter selection and convergence criteria.
3.  The semantic segmentation results in Section 5.2 are presented briefly as "comparable" to the SVR baseline, which seems to understate the superiority shown in Section 5.1.2. A more consistent and detailed presentation would be beneficial.
