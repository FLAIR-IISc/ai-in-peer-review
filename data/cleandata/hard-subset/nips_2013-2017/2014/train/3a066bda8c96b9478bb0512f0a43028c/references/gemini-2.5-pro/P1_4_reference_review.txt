### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, a scenario termed "weak supervision." The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a training example as a latent random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, where the success probability is given by the observed continuous "positiveness" score (e.g., Intersection over Union - IoU). The standard SVM hinge loss is then replaced by its expectation over this latent label. This formulation results in an objective function that can be solved efficiently using standard linear SVM solvers.

The paper extends this model to a more weakly supervised setting where the positiveness scores themselves are unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this "latent e-SVM," the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and are estimated jointly with the model parameters via an alternating optimization procedure. A KL-divergence based regularization term is introduced to guide the estimation of these latent variables, encouraging them to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The authors validate their approach on two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. For evaluation, they propose two new metrics for segment proposal classification: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). Experiments on PASCAL VOC 2011 show that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) baselines on these new metrics. When applied to object detection, the features derived from the latent e-SVM are combined with the R-CNN framework, leading to a significant 1.5% improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset, establishing a new state-of-the-art result at the time.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of modeling the SVM loss as an expectation over a latent binary label is both novel and well-suited for the problem of learning from continuous positiveness scores. It provides a principled alternative to the common ad-hoc practice of thresholding continuous labels for classification (SVC) or using a regression model (SVR) that is not designed for a bounded [0, 1] output.

2.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** A major strength is that the final e-SVM objective function is equivalent to a weighted SVM problem and can be solved using off-the-shelf, highly optimized SVM solvers like LibLinear. This significantly lowers the barrier to adoption and implementation.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its method with convincing experimental results. The 1.5% absolute mAP improvement over the very strong R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.

4.  **Novel and Relevant Application:** The idea of generating a "spatial confidence map" as a new feature to augment an existing object detector is a clever way to integrate segmentation-based cues into a window-based detection pipeline. This addresses a non-trivial integration challenge effectively.

5.  **Contribution to Evaluation:** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification is a valuable contribution in its own right. These metrics are more appropriate for the task than task-specific downstream metrics (like pixel accuracy) and provide a more direct way to assess the quality of segment classifiers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Distinction from Weighted SVMs:** The paper states that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points," differentiating it from weighted SVMs (WSVMs). This distinction is subtle and could be explained more clearly. The final loss for a sample `i` is `g(u_i) * l+_i + (1-g(u_i)) * l-_i`. This can be interpreted as applying two different weights to the positive and negative loss components for the same sample. A more detailed comparison to the standard WSVM formulation, where a single weight `C_i` is typically applied to the entire hinge loss `C_i * max(0, 1 - y_i f(x_i))`, would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty and help readers better understand the key difference.

2.  **Analysis of the Latent e-SVM Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is guaranteed only to find a local minimum. The paper mentions this briefly by noting that setting the regularizer `λR` to zero leads to a latent SVM-like behavior that can get stuck. However, a more thorough discussion would be beneficial. For instance, how sensitive is the final performance to the initialization of `u`? How many iterations does the algorithm typically take to converge?

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization term `R(u)` and its weight `λR` are critical for the latent e-SVM. The paper provides a good motivation for the KL-divergence form of the regularizer, but there is no discussion on how `λR` was chosen or how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice. This is an important detail for reproducibility and understanding the robustness of the method.

4.  **Limited Ablation on Feature Design:** For the object detection task, the spatial confidence map feature is constructed using `m x m` bins where `m=5`. An ablation study on the choice of `m` and the pooling strategy (average pooling is used) would provide more insight into why this feature representation is effective.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical method for weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM formulation is elegant, and its extension to the latent variable case is logical and effective. The problem it addresses—learning from continuous labels between fully supervised and weakly supervised—is of significant importance in machine learning and computer vision.

The strengths, particularly the strong empirical results that advance the state-of-the-art on a competitive benchmark and the practical advantage of using standard solvers, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily related to a lack of deeper analysis and some missing experimental details, which could be addressed in a final version.

The work is original, technically sound, and its results are significant. It is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled formulation for an important learning problem.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on a challenging and standard benchmark (PASCAL VOC 2007 detection).
*   High practical value due to its compatibility with standard SVM solvers.
*   Contributes useful new evaluation metrics to the community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The distinction from weighted SVMs could be clarified.
*   Lack of analysis on the convergence and sensitivity of the latent variable optimization.
