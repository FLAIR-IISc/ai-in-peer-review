

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning where segment proposals have continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU with ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their approach outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) in segment classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

The paper's core idea is to treat the continuous label (positiveness) as a Bernoulli probability and formulate an expectation-based loss function for SVM. For the weak supervision case, where positiveness is unobserved, they introduce a latent variable model with iterative optimization. The authors test their method on PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation and PASCAL VOC 2007 detection tasks, reporting modest improvements (1.5% AP gain on detection) over baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the methodological details.
- The experimental design is reasonable, with appropriate comparisons to SVC and SVR.
- The approach appears technically sound, and the implementation seems straightforward (using standard SVM solvers).

**Major weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper fails to convincingly explain why the expectation loss formulation should outperform standard approaches. The authors claim their method "weights labels instead of data points" but provide no theoretical basis for why this should lead to better performance. The connection to existing weighted SVM literature is superficial, with minimal comparison to relevant work like [17, 23, 27].

2. **Mischaracterization of weak supervision:** The paper's "weakly supervised" setting is not the standard weakly supervised learning problem. Standard weak supervision (e.g., MIL) assumes only image-level labels are available, while the authors assume bounding box annotations (which are already a form of weak supervision, but not the most challenging). This mischaracterization weakens the paper's contribution.

3. **Marginal improvements:** The reported gains (1.5% AP on object detection) are modest, especially considering the paper claims to outperform state-of-the-art methods. The paper doesn't compare to recent strong baselines in semantic segmentation (e.g., U-Net, DeepLab) or object detection (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN), making it difficult to assess the true significance of their contribution.

4. **Inadequate comparison to relevant work:** The paper mentions MIL and weighted SVMs but fails to properly compare with recent, relevant approaches. For instance, recent work by [19] on weakly-supervised dual clustering for segmentation should be compared to. The paper also doesn't address how their method compares to more recent approaches to weakly supervised learning that directly handle continuous labels.

5. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the e-SVM formulation versus other components (e.g., the spatial confidence map feature). It's unclear whether the improvement comes from the e-SVM itself or from the feature engineering.

6. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance on PASCAL VOC 2007, but the baseline they compare to (RCNN) is already outperformed by many more recent methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN, which achieves 69.7% AP on PASCAL VOC 2007). This undermines their significance claim.

**Critical evaluation:**

The paper presents a technically sound extension of SVMs, but the novelty is limited. The core idea (using continuous labels to weight examples) is not substantially different from existing weighted SVM approaches. The marginal performance gains reported do not justify the claim of a novel contribution to the field, especially given the lack of rigorous theoretical foundation.

The paper's mischaracterization of the weak supervision problem is particularly concerning. The authors present their setting as a "middle ground" between strong and weak supervision, but this doesn't address the more challenging weakly supervised learning problem where only image-level labels are available. This misalignment with the standard weakly supervised learning problem significantly weakens the paper's contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This paper does not meet NIPS's standards for a significant contribution. While the method is technically sound, the improvements are marginal, the theoretical justification is lacking, and the contribution does not substantially advance the state of the art. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent work in weakly supervised learning, a clearer theoretical justification for the approach, and more substantial empirical results that demonstrate the method's value over current state-of-the-art techniques.

I recommend rejection, as the paper presents a minor variation of existing techniques with insufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and significance. The authors should either substantially strengthen their theoretical foundation and empirical results or significantly narrow their claims to reflect the actual contribution.
