

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" values (between 0 and 1) rather than binary labels. The authors address the problem of segment proposal classification, where the ground truth for each segment can be represented as an overlap ratio (e.g., IoU) rather than a binary label. The e-SVM model treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, with success probability equal to the observed positiveness. For weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved), they extend the model to a latent e-SVM that optimizes both model parameters and the latent positiveness values. The paper evaluates their approach on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating improvements over standard SVC and SVR baselines.

The work relates to several lines of research at NIPS: weakly supervised learning (particularly MIL), weighted SVMs, and applications in computer vision. While the paper acknowledges connections to weighted SVMs, it doesn't fully clarify how their approach differs from existing weighted SVM formulations. The paper builds upon recent work in semantic segmentation (e.g., Carreira et al.) and object detection (e.g., RCNN).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature: standard SVMs are not well-suited for problems where labels are continuous values in [0,1] rather than binary. The e-SVM formulation provides a principled solution to this problem.

2. **Clear Theoretical Development**: The derivation of the expectation loss is well-justified, with appropriate mathematical formulation. The extension to the latent e-SVM is logically presented with a clear optimization procedure.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) and under different supervision settings (pixel-level vs. bounding box annotations). The use of novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification is particularly valuable.

4. **Practical Impact**: The 1.5% average precision improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is significant in the field, and the method is simple enough to integrate with existing detection pipelines.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is generally well-organized, with clear sections for motivation, related work, methodology, and experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Approach**: While the problem setting is interesting, the expectation loss formulation is relatively straightforward extension of standard SVMs. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this approach from existing weighted SVM work (e.g., [23, 27]), which also uses instance-dependent weights. The claim that "it weights labels instead of data points" is somewhat misleading as the weights are still applied to the data points in the loss function.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence properties or generalization bounds for the latent e-SVM, which would strengthen the contribution. The optimization procedure is described but not analyzed.

3. **Comparison with More Recent Methods**: The comparison is primarily against SVC and SVR, but the paper doesn't compare against more recent weakly supervised approaches (e.g., the work of [19] on weakly-supervised dual clustering for segmentation).

4. **Ambiguity in Implementation Details**: The paper states that "the regularization term will prevent this situation under the assumption that the true value of u should be around ubb" but doesn't provide evidence for this assumption or discuss what happens when the assumption is violated.

5. **Modest Performance Gains**: While the 1.5% improvement on object detection is meaningful, it's relatively modest for a NIPS paper. The paper could better justify why this small improvement represents a significant contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated approach that shows consistent improvements over strong baselines. While the core idea is not radically novel, the practical application to computer vision tasks with meaningful performance gains makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the Connection to Weighted SVMs**: Explicitly state how the e-SVM differs from existing weighted SVM formulations and provide a more precise mathematical distinction.

2. **Add Theoretical Analysis**: Include at least a brief discussion of convergence properties or generalization bounds for the latent e-SVM.

3. **Expand Related Work**: Compare against more recent weakly supervised learning methods, particularly those that address similar problems.

4. **Justify the Regularization Term**: Provide more evidence for why the KL divergence regularization is appropriate, and discuss its limitations.

5. **Clarify the "Weak Supervision" Claim**: The paper claims their method handles "weak supervision," but the bounding box annotation setting is still relatively strong supervision (since it provides bounding boxes for objects, not just image-level labels). The connection to MIL should be more thoroughly discussed.

## Conclusion

This paper presents a practical and well-motivated solution to a real problem in weakly supervised learning with clear applications in computer vision. The e-SVM approach is conceptually sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate meaningful improvements over standard baselines. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical contribution is significant and aligns well with NIPS' focus on impactful machine learning research. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the NIPS proceedings.

*Overall Assessment: The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. It would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.*
