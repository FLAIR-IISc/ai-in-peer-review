**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces an algorithm called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data. The key idea is to handle labels that are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" value in [0, 1], such as the Intersection over Union (IoU) ratio for segment proposals. The proposed e-SVM model formulates the SVM loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, where the expectation is weighted by the given positiveness value. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM where the positiveness itself is treated as a latent variable, allowing the model to be trained with even weaker supervision like bounding boxes. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, the authors propose a "spatial confidence map feature" derived from e-SVM scores and show that combining it with the R-CNN detector improves performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a relevant and practical problem. Learning from continuous-valued labels (like IoU) is a common scenario in computer vision, and framing it as a middle ground between fully supervised (pixel-level) and weakly supervised (image-level) learning is insightful. The proposed e-SVM formulation is an intuitive and elegant way to incorporate this continuous label information directly into an SVM objective.

2.  **Latent Variable Extension:** The extension to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where only bounding boxes are available is a logical and valuable contribution. The alternating optimization scheme and the choice of a KL-divergence-based regularizer to keep the latent positiveness values close to an initial estimate are methodologically sound.

3.  **Empirical Significance:** The reported 1.5% absolute improvement in mean Average Precision (AP) on PASCAL VOC 2007 over the very strong R-CNN baseline is a significant result. Demonstrating a non-trivial improvement on a state-of-the-art method highlights the potential utility of the proposed segmentation-based features.

4.  **Practicality:** A notable advantage is that the basic e-SVM model can be optimized using standard linear SVM solvers, lowering the barrier to implementation and adoption.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, despite its interesting contributions, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, experimental methodology, and clarity, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is unpolished and contains numerous errors that detract from its readability and professionalism.
    *   **Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** The text is replete with errors (e.g., "continues value", "segmentic segmentation", "simple concatenating", "push the detection performance into a very high level", "datesets").
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** In Section 3.1, the definition of the weight vector `w` is ambiguous. It is first augmented to include the bias term `b`, but the regularizer is written as `wTw`, which typically applies to the un-augmented vector. The notation `λw · 1 wTw` is non-standard and confusing.
    *   **Formatting:** The reference list is poorly formatted and highly inconsistent. For example, entries [5], [6], [8], [9], and [12] are incomplete. Most critically, a citation to a weighted SVM paper is left as `?`, which is unacceptable for a formal submission and suggests a lack of diligence.

2.  **Experimental Rigor and Reproducibility:**
    *   **Confounding Variables:** The primary result in object detection (1.5% AP gain) is scientifically compromised. The authors state, "We also modified the bounding box regularization method used in [14]... It will lead to a slightly improved performance." This introduces a confounding variable. It is impossible to determine how much of the reported gain is due to the proposed e-SVM features versus this separate modification. A proper ablation study isolating the effect of the spatial confidence map feature is essential but absent.
    *   **Vague Reporting:** The paper relies on vague, unquantified statements. For semantic segmentation, performance is described as "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art," but the baseline SVR numbers are not provided for a direct comparison. The performance boost from the modified bounding box regression is only described as "slightly improved." This lack of precision is problematic.
    *   **Missing Details:** The paper omits key details needed for reproducibility. Algorithm 1 specifies iterating "until Converge," but no convergence criterion is defined.

3.  **Methodological Depth:**
    *   **Novelty vs. Weighted SVMs:** The paper claims a key difference from weighted SVMs (WSVMs) is that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points." However, the resulting loss function for sample `i`, `g(ui)·l+i + (1-g(ui))·l-i`, can be interpreted as a form of instance-weighting where a single sample has different costs for being misclassified as positive or negative. The distinction is not clearly articulated, and the novelty claim relative to the broad WSVM literature feels overstated without a more thorough discussion.
    *   **Model Choices:** The choice of the identity function `g(ui) = ui` is presented without any justification or exploration of alternatives. While simple, it is a critical design choice, and its impact on performance is not analyzed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core e-SVM model is a clean, novel idea for an important problem.
*   The performance improvement on the strong R-CNN baseline is significant on its face.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript's quality is far below the standards of NIPS due to numerous typos, grammatical errors, and a severely flawed reference list.
*   The main experimental result in object detection is not scientifically sound due to a confounding variable and the lack of a necessary ablation study.
*   Claims are often vague and unquantified, and critical implementation details are missing, hindering reproducibility.
*   The methodological discussion lacks depth, particularly concerning the relationship to weighted SVMs and key design choices.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the core idea of e-SVM is promising and the headline results are intriguing, the paper in its current form is not acceptable for NIPS. The flaws are not merely cosmetic; they include a critical lapse in experimental rigor that invalidates the main conclusion regarding object detection improvement, as well as a general lack of polish and detail that undermines the work's credibility and reproducibility. The paper requires a major revision to address these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication.
