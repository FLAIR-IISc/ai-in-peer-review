### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine (SVM) formulation, termed the "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous "positiveness" values in the range [0, 1], rather than binary. The core idea is to treat the true binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable whose parameter is the given positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This results in a formulation where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative sides of the hinge loss, weighted by its positiveness. The authors extend this to a "latent e-SVM" for cases where the positiveness score itself is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available) by treating it as a latent variable and solving via an alternating optimization scheme. The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, where it is shown to modestly improve upon the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector by providing a complementary feature.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem in machine learning, particularly for computer vision applications. Learning from continuous-valued scores like Intersection-over-Union (IoU) is a common scenario that is not perfectly addressed by standard classification (SVC) or regression (SVR) frameworks. The proposed method provides a principled way to incorporate this information directly into a max-margin framework.

2.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** The basic e-SVM model is elegant in its simplicity. The formulation leads to a convex optimization problem that, as the authors' state, can be solved using standard linear SVM solvers. This is a significant practical advantage, lowering the barrier to adoption and implementation.

3.  **Empirical Results on a Strong Baseline:** The most compelling result is the improvement shown in object detection. The authors integrate their method with the R-CNN framework, which was the state-of-the-art at the time of writing. Achieving a consistent 1.5% mAP improvement on top of such a powerful baseline is non-trivial and demonstrates the utility of the proposed segmentation-based features learned by the latent e-SVM.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty and Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern with this work is its incremental nature. The final loss function in Equation 1 is essentially a re-weighting of the hinge loss for each sample. The paper acknowledges a connection to weighted SVMs (WSVM) but argues the distinction is that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points." This is a subtle semantic difference, as the effect is still an instance-dependent weighting on the cost. A more thorough experimental comparison against established WSVM methods is critically missing. For instance, one could easily formulate a WSVM baseline where positive samples (defined by `u_i > 0.5`) are weighted by `u_i` and negative samples by `1 - u_i`. It is not clear from the paper how e-SVM is fundamentally different or superior to such a direct and established approach. The contribution feels more like a clever re-derivation of a weighted SVM for a specific problem rather than a new class of algorithm.

2.  **Methodological Rigor of Latent e-SVM:** The extension to the latent e-SVM model raises several questions. The optimization proceeds via alternating minimization, a standard heuristic for such problems, but no discussion of convergence properties is provided. More importantly, the choice of the KL-divergence-based regularizer `R(u)` seems somewhat arbitrary. While it intuitively serves to keep the latent `u_i` close to the initial estimate `u_bbi`, the paper does not explore or justify this specific choice over other possible regularizers (e.g., an L2 penalty on `u - u_bb`). The sensitivity of the model to the hyperparameter `λR` is also not analyzed, which is a crucial detail for reproducibility and understanding the model's robustness.

3.  **Unconvincing Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   For the core task of segment proposal classification, the authors introduce two new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG). While motivated, proposing new metrics and then showing a new method performs best on them is not as convincing as demonstrating superiority on widely accepted, standard benchmarks.
    *   In the semantic segmentation task, the authors report their method achieves results that are merely "comparable" to the SVR baseline from Carreira et al. [5]. This does not provide strong evidence for the superiority of e-SVM. If the performance is only comparable, the argument for adopting a new method over an existing one is weakened.
    *   While the object detection results are positive, the gain is modest. The proposed pipeline adds significant complexity (generating segment proposals, training e-SVMs, creating spatial confidence maps, etc.). The paper does not analyze whether this complexity is justified by the ~1.5% mAP gain.

### **Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a simple and intuitive method for incorporating continuous-valued supervision into an SVM framework. The approach is well-motivated by practical problems in computer vision and shows some promising empirical results, particularly in improving a strong object detection baseline.

However, the work's primary weaknesses are its limited novelty over established weighted SVM techniques and a lack of rigorous experimental validation against these closely related methods. The methodological justification for the latent model's regularizer is thin, and the performance gains in semantic segmentation are not compelling. While the idea is clever, it feels more like an application-specific adaptation of existing principles than a fundamental contribution to learning theory or algorithms. The paper is well-written and the problem is interesting, but it does not meet the high bar for originality and rigorous validation expected for a NIPS publication. I am therefore leaning towards rejection.
