Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “positiveness” labels u ∈ [0,1], e.g., IoU between a segment proposal and ground-truth. The core idea is to treat the binary label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as a Bernoulli variable with success probability µi = g(ui) (instantiated as µi = ui) and minimize the expected hinge loss: L = λ||w||^2 + ∑i [µi max(0, 1 − w⊤xi) + (1 − µi) max(0, 1 + w⊤xi)]. This yields a convex problem in w that can be solved via standard linear SVM solvers (by equivalently weighting losses for both labels). For weak supervision (only bounding boxes), the authors introduce a latent e-SVM: treat ui as latent, initialize via IoU with the bounding box ubb, and alternate between optimizing w and u with a convex regularizer on u given by the cross-entropy DKL[Bern(ubb) || Bern(u)], to avoid collapse to {0,1}. Applications are shown to (i) segment proposal confidence prediction (with new evaluation metrics TAPC and NDCG), (ii) semantic segmentation trained with pixel- or box-level annotations, and (iii) object detection by converting per-segment scores into a spatial confidence map pooled over candidate windows and concatenated with RCNN features, yielding +1.2–1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Relation to prior work
The loss is a convex combination of positive and negative hinge losses per sample, i.e., using “soft” labels. This is very close to fuzzy/soft-label SVMs, cost-sensitive/weighted SVMs, and SVMs for uncertain/probabilistic labels; the paper cites instance-weighted SVMs but argues the difference is “weighting labels instead of points,” which is largely a reparameterization. The latent variant and alternating minimization are reminiscent of latent SVM. The detection feature is a spatial pooling of a per-pixel max of segment scores, akin to a class-specific score map plus spatial pyramid pooling.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective: expected hinge with soft labels. Convex in w; easy to implement in existing solvers.
- Practical weakly supervised extension with a convex regularizer on latent “positiveness,” and a straightforward alternating scheme.
- New task-agnostic evaluation measures (TAPC, NDCG) for segment confidence prediction are reasonable and useful.
- Demonstrated on two canonical tasks. For detection, a simple integration into RCNN yields a measurable mAP gain (+1.5 with bbox regression).
- The approach is flexible wrt proposal generators and features.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. Training with soft labels for SVM has been studied (fuzzy SVMs, probabilistic/uncertain-label SVMs, cost-sensitive formulations). The proposed expected hinge is essentially a convex combination of two hinge losses per example. The paper does not convincingly differentiate e-SVM from these lines nor provide theoretical insight beyond implementational convenience.
- Latent e-SVM is a fairly standard alternating minimization with a KL prior to initialization; no convergence analysis beyond blockwise convexity, and no ablation on the effect of λR or the choice of g(u). As written, with a strong KL regularizer, u may stay close to ubb, limiting what is learned from data.
- Empirical gains on segment confidence (vs SVR) are small; segmentation accuracy is “comparable” rather than clearly better. The +1.2–1.5 mAP on VOC 2007 with RCNN is welcome but modest and may be sensitive to implementation details; there’s no analysis of variance/significance.
- Missing/malformed references and typos (e.g., “continues value,” “segmentic,” many reference entries incomplete or with “?”). Equation notation is sloppy (e.g., λw term), which hurts clarity.
- Claims about MIL not handling partially positive instances are too sweeping; there are MIL variants and soft-label formulations that could be competitive. Baselines lack comparisons to fuzzy/soft-label SVMs or logistic regression with soft labels, which are natural and widely used.
- The choice µi = ui is ad hoc; no calibration or learning of g(.), and no discussion of the mismatch between hinge loss and probabilistic labels.

Questions for the authors
- Please position e-SVM against fuzzy SVMs/probabilistic-label SVMs more rigorously. Is your loss identical to training two weighted copies per example with labels ±1? If not, how does it differ?
- How is λ (regularization) selected, and what exactly is the regularization term (Equation 1 has a formatting error)? Please provide ablations on λR and on the strength of the KL regularizer.
- Did you try logistic regression or square loss with soft labels, or calibrated SVMs? How do they compare on TAPC/NDCG and downstream tasks?
- Sensitivity to the initialization ubb and to alternative g(u)? Can g be learned from data?
- For detection, can you report per-class gains and significance (e.g., bootstrapped mAP CIs)? How does the performance change with different m and pooling types?

Minor comments and clarity
- Clean up notation and fix equation typos. Define all variables once; use standard 1/2||w||^2 regularization.
- Provide pseudocode details for the u-update (closed-form? projected gradient?).
- Improve references; several entries are incomplete or malformed.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and easy-to-use formulation; could be adopted broadly where soft/weak labels appear.
- Demonstrates utility across tasks and supervision regimes; modest but consistent detection gains.
- The TAPC/NDCG evaluation setup for segment confidence is a useful contribution.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical novelty is limited; closely overlaps with existing soft/fuzzy/uncertain-label SVM literature and latent SVM.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines are modest; segmentation results are not clearly state-of-the-art.
- Clarity and scholarship need significant polishing.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the paper is practical, but the conceptual novelty is too incremental for NeurIPS as written, and the empirical gains are modest. Strengthening the positioning vs prior soft-label SVM work, adding stronger baselines (soft-label logistic/hinge, fuzzy SVM), deeper analysis of the latent variant, and clearer writing could tip this to accept.

Confidence
High (4/5): familiar with SVM variants, weak supervision, and RCNN-era detection.
