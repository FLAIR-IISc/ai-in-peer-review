Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a margin-based learner for settings where per-instance supervision is a degree of “positiveness” in [0,1] rather than a binary label. The core idea is to take the expectation of the hinge loss under a Bernoulli label whose success probability is g(u_i), with u_i the observed (or latent) positiveness (e.g., IoU). This yields a convex objective in w that blends positive and negative hinge losses per sample according to u_i. The weakly supervised extension treats u as latent when only bounding boxes are available, with a convex regularizer R(u) given by the cross-entropy/KL divergence to an initialization u_bb (IoU with box), and uses alternating minimization over w and u. The method is applied to segment confidence prediction, semantic segmentation, and object detection via a simple spatial confidence-map feature that aggregates per-pixel maxima of segment scores into spatial bins and concatenates them with RCNN features. The authors also introduce two task-agnostic segment-evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG). Empirically, e-SVM beats SVC and SVR for segment ranking, achieves comparable semantic segmentation to SVR, and improves RCNN by 1.2–1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

This sits between strong supervision and MIL-style image-level supervision: unlike MIL, the unit of supervision is a soft degree for each proposal. The formulation is related to weighted/fuzzy/uncertain-label SVM variants, but differs in weighting positive and negative losses per example using a probabilistic label model, and in the latent u regularized toward bounding-box IoUs. The spatial confidence map is in the spirit of segmentation-aware features used in detection, but here derived from the learned e-SVM scores.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and general formulation: expected hinge loss with probabilistic labels that naturally handles soft supervision and bounded targets.
- Weak supervision handled by a simple, convex-in-blocks latent extension with a principled KL regularizer tied to bounding-box overlap; easy alternating optimization.
- Practical and modular: plugs into standard pipelines (segment ranking, semantic segmentation, object detection) and leverages off-the-shelf proposals and solvers.
- Empirical gains over SVC/SVR on segment ranking under both strong and weak supervision; modest but consistent mAP improvements when combined with RCNN.
- The TAPC and NDCG criteria for segment-confidence evaluation are sensible and task-agnostic.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty relative to prior “uncertain/probabilistic-label SVM,” “fuzzy SVM,” and expected risk minimization with hinge loss is not fully clarified. The paper should precisely position e-SVM against these lines (where instance weights vs. label probabilities vs. loss expectations differ) and cite representative work.
- The claim that the objective “can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver (e.g., LibLinear)” needs more detail. The expected hinge mixes l+ and l− with different weights per example; how exactly is this encoded for a standard solver without modification? Is it implemented by duplicating examples with class-dependent weights, or by a custom per-example loss?
- Theory is light: no generalization or identifiability discussion for the latent model, convergence properties of the alternating scheme, or sensitivity/consistency of u estimates under different R(u) and g(·).
- Experimental scope is limited: one proposal method (CPMC), one dataset per task, and comparisons only to SVC/SVR for segment ranking. For weak supervision, comparisons to alternatives (e.g., latent SVM/MISVM, logistic models with probabilistic targets, or recent weakly supervised segmentation/detection approaches) would strengthen claims.
- Practical details are underspecified: choice and tuning of λ and λ_R, the form of g(u) beyond g(u)=u, initialization sensitivity, runtime and scalability to large N, and ablations (e.g., effect of the KL regularizer, other regularizers, number of bins m, max vs. average aggregation).
- Clarity/presentation: several typos and broken references, and some notation issues (e.g., the regularization term formatting). The spatial feature pipeline would benefit from a more precise description and complexity analysis.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the expected hinge and the convex-in-blocks latent extension are reasonable. However, the solver claim requires clarification, and the empirical section lacks some key baselines and ablations. The object detection improvement is positive but modest.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but the paper needs careful editing (typos, broken cites), a clearer derivation of the loss and solver, and more detail on hyperparameters and training.

Originality
The overall direction—expected hinge with probabilistic labels plus a KL-regularized latent variant tied to bounding boxes—is interesting and, to my knowledge, not standard in the weakly supervised segmentation/detection literature. That said, there is significant overlap with existing SVMs for uncertain/fuzzy labels; the distinction should be sharpened.

Significance
The formulation is general and could be useful beyond vision whenever soft supervision in [0,1] arises. The empirical gains in detection demonstrate practical value, though not groundbreaking. If better positioned and substantiated, this could seed follow-on work on probabilistic-label margin methods and weakly supervised learning with soft proposal qualities.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely relate e-SVM to fuzzy/weighted/probabilistic-label SVMs and expected hinge risk; add missing citations and a small theoretical comparison.
- Explain how the expected hinge is implemented with standard solvers or provide a custom solver description.
- Add ablations: effect of λ_R and R(u), alternative g(u), initialization, proposal quality, and pooling choices.
- Compare to latent SVM/MISVM and probabilistic logistic regression with soft targets for the segment task; include an end-to-end deep baseline if possible.
- Improve clarity and fix references.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with a clear path to broader impact.
- Addresses a relevant weak-supervision regime and yields measurable gains.
- Introduces useful, task-agnostic evaluation measures for segment ranking.

Cons against acceptance
- Novelty w.r.t. uncertain/fuzzy-label SVM literature is not convincingly established.
- Limited comparisons and missing ablations; modest improvements on detection.
- Some clarity and rigor issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept (7). The expected-hinge formulation with a KL-regularized latent extension is a neat and potentially influential idea; I’m willing to overlook some experimental and presentation gaps if the authors clarify the relationship to prior uncertain-label SVMs and the solver details.

Reviewer confidence: 3/5.
