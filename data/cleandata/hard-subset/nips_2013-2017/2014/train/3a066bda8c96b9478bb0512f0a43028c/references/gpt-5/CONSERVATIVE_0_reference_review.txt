Summary
The paper proposes “expectation loss SVM” (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where targets are real-valued “positiveness” scores in [0,1], instantiated as IoU between segment proposals and ground-truth. The core idea is to treat the unknown binary label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability µi = g(ui) (set to ui), and minimize the expected hinge loss: µi·max(0,1−f(xi)) + (1−µi)·max(0,1+f(xi)) plus L2 regularization. For weak supervision (only bounding boxes), ui is latent and regularized to be close to an initialization ubb (IoU with boxes) via KL divergence, and the authors alternate between optimizing w (a standard linear SVM with per-instance weights) and u (convex in u with the KL term). They evaluate on segment proposal scoring (introducing TAPC and NDCG metrics), semantic segmentation, and object detection. For detection, they derive a “spatial confidence map feature” from segment scores and concatenate it with R-CNN features, reporting ~1.2–1.5 mAP gains on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is a convex combination of positive/negative hinge losses weighted by a per-instance “soft label” µi. This is closely related to soft-label or probabilistic-label SVMs, fuzzy SVMs (per-instance memberships), and cost-sensitive/instance-weighted SVMs. The latent extension resembles latent SVM with a convex regularizer on the latent ui. On the application side, segment proposal scoring/regression (e.g., SVR on IoU) and using segmentation cues to improve detection are established directions; the proposed spatial confidence pooling is akin to spatial pyramids over per-pixel scores.

Strengths
- Simple, broadly applicable formulation that reduces to standard SVM machinery with per-instance weights; practical and easy to implement.
- Clear biconvex optimization with convex subproblems and a convex regularizer in u; sensible initialization and rationale.
- Experiments span segment scoring, segmentation, and detection; the detection gains with R-CNN are non-trivial in absolute mAP.
- The spatial confidence map feature is intuitive and practically useful for coupling segment cues to window-based detectors.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: the expected hinge loss with µ∈[0,1] is essentially an SVM with soft/probabilistic labels; closely related formulations exist (fuzzy SVM, soft-label SVM, learning with uncertain/ambiguous labels). The paper does not adequately position itself against this body of work, instead contrasting mainly to SVC/SVR and weighted SVMs.
- The key modeling assumption µi=g(ui) with g(u)=u is ad hoc and unvalidated. There is no calibration of IoU to class probability nor exploration of alternative g(·). This undermines the methodological soundness.
- The latent u optimization lacks detail. For the u-step, is there a closed form per-example update or do the authors use iterative Newton steps? What is the computational cost? Convergence is only to a stationary point; no analysis of sensitivity to λR or initialization ubb.
- Empirical evidence is not rigorous enough to isolate the contribution of e-SVM. For detection, the main gains may stem from the spatial confidence map engineering rather than the learning objective. There is no ablation comparing the same pipeline where segment scores come from SVR or SVC. For segmentation, results are only “comparable” to SVR; the segment scoring metrics show small margins over SVR.
- The proposed TAPC metric is nonstandard (averaging AP over thresholds); while reasonable, no justification is provided and no statistical variability is reported. NDCG is appropriate but again lacks error bars.
- Reproducibility is hampered by missing details: hyperparameters (λw, λR), g(·) choices, optimization details (stopping criteria), runtime, and data preprocessing. The RCNN setup deviates from the original paper (feature mapping, bbox regression modification) but exact training details are sparse.
- Writing and references need attention (several malformed citations, missing years, typos). Claims of “state-of-the-art” on VOC 2007 require careful, controlled comparisons to contemporary baselines.

Quality
Technically, the objective is sensible and the subproblems are convex. However, the mapping from IoU to µ and the latent regularization are heuristic; there is no theoretical justification or sensitivity analysis. Experimental comparisons to the most relevant baselines (soft-label/fuzzy/cost-sensitive SVM variants) are missing. Ablations are insufficient to support the core claim that e-SVM is superior to SVR/SVC in practice.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has issues: incomplete/malformed references, missing methodological details for the u-update, and insufficient specification of experimental protocols. Figures/tables summarizing per-class detection improvements and variance across runs would help.

Originality
Low to moderate. The expected hinge loss over uncertain labels and per-instance weighting are known ideas; the latent variant with KL regularization is a straightforward extension. The spatial pooling over per-pixel confidence is an engineering contribution rather than a principled advance.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice due to simplicity and compatibility with standard solvers, and the detection gains are interesting. However, without stronger ablations and positioning against prior soft-label SVM work, the impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- How does e-SVM compare to fuzzy SVM/soft-label SVM baselines where µ is treated as a probabilistic label? Please include these baselines.
- Can you learn g(·) (e.g., via calibration) rather than fixing g(u)=u? How sensitive are results to g and λR?
- Provide details (and complexity) of the u-step optimization. Is there a closed-form update or per-example Newton iterations?
- Ablate the detection pipeline: replace e-SVM segment scores with SVR/SVC scores in the same spatial pooling framework; report mAP.
- Report standard deviations or confidence intervals and per-class AP changes.

Pros
- Simple, general formulation; easy to adopt.
- Convex subproblems; practical alternating scheme.
- Demonstrated improvements on VOC detection when combined with R-CNN.

Cons
- Limited novelty; weak positioning vs established soft-label/fuzzy SVM literature.
- Heuristic modeling choices without validation.
- Insufficient ablations; segmentation results not clearly better than SVR.
- Clarity and reference issues; reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is reasonable but too close to established formulations, and the empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate a methodological advance over strong baselines. A revised version that (i) situates the work within soft-label/fuzzy SVM literature, (ii) rigorously ablates the pipeline to isolate the contribution of e-SVM, (iii) justifies/calibrates g(u) and analyzes sensitivity, and (iv) strengthens experimental reporting could be much more compelling.
