

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning, specifically targeting segmentation and object detection tasks. The authors claim their method handles continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU) rather than binary labels, addressing limitations in existing Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) approaches.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a valid problem in weakly supervised learning where bounding box annotations are common but pixel-level annotations are expensive. The authors provide a clear theoretical framework for their e-SVM model and demonstrate its application to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. The experimental results on PASCAL VOC 2011 and 2007 datasets show modest improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Errors
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies in references (e.g., "A, J. Deng, Berg" instead of "J. Deng et al.") and technical errors. The mathematical formulation in Section 3.1 contains an inconsistency: the authors define Yi ∈ {-1, +1} but then refer to it as a Bernoulli distribution with success probability µi (which would require Yi ∈ {0, 1}). This fundamental inconsistency undermines the theoretical foundation of the proposed model.

### Methodological Issues
The paper claims "our model outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios" but the results are modest (1.5% average improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection). The paper fails to provide statistical significance testing for these improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the gains are meaningful. The claim that e-SVM "can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver" is misleading - the latent e-SVM requires iterative optimization (Algorithm 1) that is not "effortless" to implement.

The regularization term R(u) is defined as R(u) = -1/N Σ [ubbi · log(ui) + (1-ubbi) · log(1-ui)], but the paper fails to explain why this specific form is optimal. The authors state this is "a strong regularization term" but provide no theoretical justification or empirical evidence that this choice leads to better performance than alternatives.

### Experimental Shortcomings
The paper introduces two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) but fails to provide sufficient detail about their calculation or why they are better than standard metrics. The results in Table 1 show only 1.5% improvement on object detection, yet the paper claims "non-trivial improvement" without justification. The paper also fails to compare against recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL and SVC/SVR.

### Related Work and Originality
The paper claims novelty but doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing weighted SVM approaches. The related work section mentions weighted SVMs but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how e-SVM differs from these methods. The paper states "the loss function of e-SVM model is different from those of WSVMs" but doesn't provide a clear mathematical distinction that would justify the novelty claim.

### Clarity Issues
The paper's description of the spatial confidence map feature (Section 4.2) is confusing. The authors state "For each pixel, the confidence score is set as the maximum confidence score of all the segments that contain this pixel" but don't explain how this handles segments that partially overlap with window candidates. The explanation of how the spatial confidence map feature is integrated with RCNN is too brief to be reproducible.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a relevant problem in weakly supervised learning, it suffers from multiple significant technical and methodological flaws that undermine its contribution. The inconsistent mathematical formulation, modest performance gains without statistical significance, and unclear novelty claims are substantial issues that prevent this from being a strong NIPS submission. The paper also contains numerous formatting errors that suggest insufficient attention to detail.

The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The 1.5% improvement on object detection is marginal in the context of current literature, and the theoretical claims are undermined by fundamental inconsistencies. The authors' assertion that their method is "more effective" than SVC and SVR is not adequately supported by the results.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper tackles an important problem, the technical flaws, modest results, and lack of clear novelty make it unsuitable for NIPS. The authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical foundation, provide statistical significance for their results, and more clearly differentiate their approach from existing weighted SVM methods before this work could be considered for publication. The current version contains too many inconsistencies to be taken seriously as a scientific contribution.
