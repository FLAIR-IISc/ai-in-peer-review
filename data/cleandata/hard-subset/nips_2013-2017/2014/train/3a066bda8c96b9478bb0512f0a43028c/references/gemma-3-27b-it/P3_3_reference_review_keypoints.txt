- Overall: Proposes e-SVM that weights samples by “positiveness” for weak supervision; reviewer rates it a weak accept due to practical value but incremental technical novelty.  
- Problem & approach: Addresses learning from partially/noisily labeled data by incorporating continuous positiveness into SVM loss and a latent variant to infer unknown positiveness.  
- Positioning: Well-positioned between MIL and fully supervised methods; compares to SVC/SVR and weighted SVMs but omits recent consistency-regularization and pseudo-labeling work.  
- Mathematical contribution: Clear and concise e-SVM derivation with a well-explained latent extension and iterative optimization.  
- Practical relevance: Tackles an important real-world problem (reducing annotation cost) applicable to vision tasks.  
- Empirical evaluation: Comprehensive experiments on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection demonstrating consistent gains.  
- Performance gains: e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR; object detection shows a modest ~1.5% per-class AP improvement.  
- Generality: Method works with different proposal detectors, features, and base detectors, highlighting applicability.  
- Novelty concerns: Core idea of weighting by correctness is incremental relative to existing weighted SVM literature.  
- Object detection critique: The detection application is simplistic (feature concatenation with R-CNN) and the gains may not justify the extensive coverage.  
- Regularization issue: KL-divergence regularizer in latent e-SVM seems arbitrary and lacks ablation or stronger justification.  
- Metric explanation: New metrics (TAPC, NDCG) are introduced but not sufficiently explained or motivated versus standard precision-recall.  
- Missing analyses: Paper lacks detailed error analysis (failure modes) and discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Computational concerns: No clear comparison of e-SVM computational cost versus SVC and SVR on large datasets.  
- Acceptance pros: Relevant problem, solid formulation, consistent empirical improvements, and clear writing.  
- Acceptance cons: Incremental novelty, modest detection gains, weak regularization justification, and limited analytical depth.  
- Questions for authors: Request justification for KL regularizer; details on common error types; hyperparameter sensitivity; computational cost comparisons; and potential integration with modern detectors beyond simple feature concatenation.
