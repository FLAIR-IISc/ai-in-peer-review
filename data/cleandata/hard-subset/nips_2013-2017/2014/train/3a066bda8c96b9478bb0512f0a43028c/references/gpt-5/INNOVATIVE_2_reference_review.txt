Summary
This paper proposes the Expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple extension of linear SVM that optimizes the expected hinge loss under a “soft” label Yi with success probability µi = g(ui), where ui ∈ [0,1] measures positiveness (e.g., IoU). This allows learning from partially correct segment proposals without heuristic binarization. For weak supervision (only bounding boxes), the paper treats ui as latent and alternates between optimizing w (a convex SVM subproblem) and u (a convex problem with a strong KL regularizer pulling u toward the segment–box IoU). The method plugs into standard SVM solvers. Applications are shown on segment scoring, semantic segmentation, and object detection, the latter by constructing class-specific spatial confidence maps from segment scores and concatenating them with RCNN features, yielding ~1.2–1.5 AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Relation to prior work
The idea of soft/probabilistic labels in large-margin learning has antecedents (e.g., fuzzy SVMs, SVMs with probabilistic labels/label uncertainty, instance-weighted SVMs, latent SVM for weakly supervised detection). Compared to WSVMs, this model “weights labels” (both positive and negative hinge contributions per example) rather than just examples, which can be seen as duplicating each sample with opposite labels and different weights. The latent extension resembles EM/alternating minimization with a convex regularizer. The detection integration produces a spatial confidence map feature, related to objectness/heatmap pooling.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: a principled expected hinge loss bridges classification and regression for bounded targets, with an easy-to-implement alternate-minimization latent extension.
- Practicality: can be reduced to standard SVM solvers; general across features, proposal generators, and base detectors.
- Empirical utility: consistent segment-scoring gains over SVC/SVR by TAPC/NDCG; comparable semantic segmentation; nontrivial +1.5 AP on top of RCNN on VOC07.
- The spatial confidence map is a straightforward, scalable way to inject segmentation cues into detection.
- Proposes task-agnostic evaluation for segment scoring (TAPC; NDCG use is standard but appropriate).

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate. Soft-label SVMs (fuzzy/probabilistic labels), label smoothing, and latent SVM are closely related; the main novelty is the specific expected-hinge formulation with latent ui and KL regularization to box IoU. Positioning versus this literature is insufficient (no direct comparisons to soft-label SVM baselines).
- The choice g(ui)=ui is ad hoc; no calibration/ablation (e.g., nonlinear g, temperature, calibration to actual success probabilities).
- Optimization details are sparse. How exactly is Equation (1) “effortlessly” solved by off-the-shelf SVM? The standard trick (duplicate x and −x with weights g and 1−g) should be stated. For the u-update, a closed-form or per-sample update and complexity discussion are missing; convergence behavior and sensitivity to λR are not analyzed.
- The weak supervision assumption hinges on regularizing u toward segment–box IoU, which is a poor proxy for segment–mask IoU; this design choice deserves justification beyond intuition, and alternatives (e.g., shape priors, spatial consistency, pairwise constraints) are not explored.
- Empirical scope is limited relative to claims. Segmentation improvements are modest; comparisons are only to SVC/SVR, not to other soft-label SVMs, probabilistic large-margin methods, or modern weakly supervised segmentation approaches (MIL variants, EM-CRF). For detection, ablations on the spatial map (bin size m, pooling type, max vs sum across segments) and on the contribution beyond added feature dimensionality are missing.
- Presentation quality: numerous typos/formatting issues; several references are incomplete; some notation is sloppy (e.g., repeated N, missing years/pages). This hampers reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with reasonable alternating convex subproblems, but missing derivations/implementation details for both the soft-label reduction to SVM and the latent u update. Experimental design is sensible but limited in baselines and analysis.

Clarity
Readable, but the manuscript needs significant polishing (typos, incomplete citations, clearer derivations, explicit algorithmic details, and hyperparameter selection). The proposed evaluation metrics should be more formally defined.

Originality
Moderate. The expected hinge loss with soft labels and the latent variable extension are natural, but close to existing ideas (fuzzy/probabilistic SVM, latent SVM). The specific KL regularization to box IoU is a useful twist.

Significance
Practical and potentially useful: easy-to-adopt soft-label SVM lens for weak supervision with small but real gains on a strong detector. The idea could be reused in other weakly labeled settings. However, the conceptual advance is not large, and the empirical evidence, while positive, is not comprehensive.

Questions and suggestions
- Please clarify how Equation (1) is mapped to a standard SVM optimization (e.g., duplicating examples x and −x with weights g(ui), 1−g(ui)). Provide exact formulation and any impact on class imbalance and C.
- Derive the per-sample u-update for Equation (2) with KL regularization (closed form? numeric? bounds?). Report runtime and convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to λR.
- Explore other g(·) (e.g., calibrated sigmoid, temperature scaling) and report calibration plots.
- Compare against soft/fuzzy SVM baselines and weakly supervised segmentation methods beyond SVC/SVR.
- Provide ablations of the spatial confidence map choices and quantify complementarity with RCNN features (e.g., gains vs adding random features of same dimension).

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the approach is clean and useful, the novelty over known soft/latent SVM variants is limited, and the paper lacks critical algorithmic details and stronger comparative evaluation. With clearer positioning against prior soft-label SVMs, expanded baselines, and fuller optimization/explanation, I would be inclined to accept; as is, this feels incremental for NeurIPS.
