

# NIPS Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

## Summary
This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, where the goal is to handle segment proposals with continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap with ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors extend standard SVMs to treat labels as random variables following a Bernoulli distribution, where the success probability is a function of the observed positiveness. They further develop a latent e-SVM variant for weak supervision settings where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The approach is applied to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, with experiments showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach that builds on standard SVM formulations. The expectation loss formulation is mathematically well-justified, with the authors correctly identifying the problem with standard binary classification (requiring heuristic thresholding) and regression (not respecting the [0,1] bounded range). The convexity of the optimization problem is properly established, and the extension to latent variables with the regularization term is reasonable.

However, the experimental evaluation has notable weaknesses. While the authors introduce two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that are more appropriate for their task, they do not sufficiently compare against relevant weakly supervised learning methods. The paper claims their approach is distinct from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) but fails to provide a thorough comparison. For instance, they state "this weakly supervised problem cannot be solved by MIL" without demonstrating why MIL would fail on their specific task. A comparison with state-of-the-art weakly supervised segmentation methods (e.g., [20, 22, 26]) would have strengthened the evaluation.

The reported improvements (1.5% average AP gain in object detection) are modest, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this is significant compared to other approaches. While the authors claim state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007, they don't compare against more recent detection methods beyond RCNN. The paper also lacks sufficient implementation details for reproducibility (e.g., hyperparameters, computational complexity).

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the technical sections flow logically from the basic e-SVM formulation to the latent extension. The application to segmentation and detection is well-explained, with sufficient detail about the spatial confidence map feature.

However, some sections could be clearer. For instance, the distinction between their approach and weighted SVMs (WSVM) is not sufficiently emphasized. They state "the difference between our e-SVM and WSVMs mainly lies in that it weights labels instead of data points," but the paper doesn't provide enough explanation to make this distinction clear to readers unfamiliar with WSVMs.

The figures (particularly Figure 1 and 2) are helpful for understanding the framework, but the paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations of the results (e.g., showing segmentation quality comparisons, not just numerical metrics).

## Originality

The core idea of using an expectation loss for continuous labels is somewhat novel, though it has clear connections to existing weighted SVM approaches. The paper acknowledges this relationship but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their contribution. The key innovation appears to be applying this formulation to the specific weakly supervised segmentation/detection setting where positiveness is either observed (pixel-level annotations) or latent (bounding box annotations).

However, the novelty is limited by the paper's failure to thoroughly compare with relevant weakly supervised learning methods. The authors claim their problem setting is "in the middle between" MIL and strong supervision, but they don't provide a clear theoretical or empirical justification for why existing MIL approaches don't apply to their setting. This weakens the claim of originality.

While the approach is general and could be applied to other weakly supervised problems, the paper doesn't demonstrate this by applying it to other tasks beyond segmentation and detection, which limits the perceived novelty.

## Significance

The problem of weakly supervised learning is indeed important, as obtaining pixel-level annotations for large datasets is prohibitively expensive. If the approach is effective, it could significantly reduce annotation costs for large-scale vision tasks. The paper's application to semantic segmentation and object detection demonstrates practical utility.

However, the significance of the contribution is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains (1.5% AP improvement) and the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper claims their method "achieves state-of-the-art object detection performance" on PASCAL VOC 2007, but doesn't compare against methods that have emerged since RCNN (e.g., Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN).

The paper also doesn't sufficiently address whether the computational overhead of their approach is justified by the modest gains. For instance, does training the e-SVM model add significant computational cost compared to standard SVC or SVR? This would be important for practitioners considering adoption.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable extension of SVMs to handle continuous labels in weakly supervised learning settings, with a clear application to computer vision tasks. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate modest improvements over baselines.

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem with clear practical significance
- Mathematically sound formulation that addresses a real limitation in standard approaches
- Appropriate introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) for the task
- Demonstrates practical application to two important vision tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with relevant weakly supervised learning methods (MIL, etc.)
- Modest performance gains (1.5% AP) without sufficient justification for their significance
- Insufficient implementation details for reproducibility
- Overstated claims about novelty and state-of-the-art performance

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, but the novelty is limited, the significance is modest, and the evaluation is insufficiently thorough. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their comparison with existing weakly supervised learning methods, provide more detailed implementation information, and better justify the significance of their results to be competitive for NIPS.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with MIL and other weakly supervised learning approaches
2. Comparison against more recent state-of-the-art detection methods beyond RCNN
3. More detailed experimental results (e.g., segmentation visualizations, error analysis)
4. Clearer explanation of how their approach differs from weighted SVMs

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution, while technically sound, doesn't represent a significant advancement over existing work in the field.
