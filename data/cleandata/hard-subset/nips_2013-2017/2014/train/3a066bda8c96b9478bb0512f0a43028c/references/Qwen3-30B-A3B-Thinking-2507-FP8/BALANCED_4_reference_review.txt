

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, where labels are continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors propose modeling the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability determined by the observed positiveness. The e-SVM framework handles both strong supervision (when positiveness is observed) and weak supervision (when positiveness is latent). The paper applies e-SVM to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks on PASCAL VOC datasets, claiming improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The work relates to prior weakly supervised learning literature, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs. However, the authors argue their problem setting differs from MIL, as they don't assume at least one "completely positive" instance per bag.

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem formulation**: The paper effectively identifies a practical need for weakly supervised learning in computer vision, where pixel-level annotations are expensive but bounding box annotations are readily available.

2. **Sound theoretical foundation**: The e-SVM model is well-motivated and mathematically rigorous. The expectation loss formulation elegantly handles continuous labels without requiring ad-hoc binarization thresholds.

3. **Good experimental validation**: The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) on the PASCAL VOC segmentation dataset. The application to object detection with RCNN shows a modest but non-trivial 1.5% average AP improvement.

4. **Practical implementation**: The paper provides clear guidance on implementation details, including the latent e-SVM optimization procedure and the spatial confidence map feature for object detection.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient comparison to relevant prior work**: The paper mentions MIL but fails to adequately distinguish its approach from existing MIL methods. The authors claim their problem setting is "in the middle" between MIL and strong supervision, but don't provide sufficient justification or comparative analysis with MIL methods that handle similar scenarios. For instance, [26] developed MIM to handle segmentation with bounding box annotations, which appears highly relevant but isn't properly compared.

2. **Modest performance gains**: The 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is modest in the context of recent object detection literature, where improvements of 2-3% are often considered significant. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this small gain is meaningful for the field.

3. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports average performance improvements without any statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess whether the observed gains are reliable.

4. **Ad hoc regularization choice**: The regularization term R(u) based on KL divergence is not sufficiently justified. The authors claim it "achieves its minimal when u = ubb" but don't demonstrate why this specific form is optimal for their problem.

5. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on PASCAL VOC datasets. To demonstrate the general applicability of the approach, experiments on additional datasets or tasks would strengthen the contribution.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a meaningful problem in weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM model is well-motivated and provides a principled way to handle continuous labels without ad-hoc thresholding. The experimental results are generally positive, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods.

However, the contribution is somewhat incremental given the modest performance gains and insufficient distinction from related work in weakly supervised learning. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to relevant MIL methods, statistical significance testing, and additional experiments demonstrating broader applicability.

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak reject** for NeurIPS. The paper has a solid theoretical foundation and addresses a relevant problem, but the contribution is incremental and the empirical validation is not sufficiently rigorous. The performance gains are modest, and the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from existing methods in the literature.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Conduct a more thorough comparison with MIL methods that handle similar weak supervision settings (e.g., Vezhnevets et al. [26], Liu et al. [19]).

2. Include statistical significance testing for the reported performance improvements.

3. Provide a more detailed justification for the regularization term R(u) used in the latent e-SVM.

4. Extend experiments to additional datasets or tasks to better demonstrate the generality of the approach.

5. More clearly articulate the significance of the 1.5% AP improvement in the context of recent advances in object detection.

The paper has potential for improvement, and with the suggested revisions, it could become a stronger contribution to the field.
