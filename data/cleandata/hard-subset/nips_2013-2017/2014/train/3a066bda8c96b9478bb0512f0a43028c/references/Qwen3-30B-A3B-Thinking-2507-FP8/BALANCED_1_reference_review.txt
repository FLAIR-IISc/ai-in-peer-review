

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data where the label for each segment proposal is a continuous "positiveness" value in [0,1] (e.g., IoU with ground truth). The authors propose two variants: a basic e-SVM for strong supervision (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved and treated as latent). The approach extends standard SVMs by framing the label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability equal to the positiveness value. The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation (using PASCAL VOC 2011) and object detection (using PASCAL VOC 2007), reporting improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

## Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively identifies a relevant problem in computer vision - learning from weakly supervised data where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. The authors clearly articulate why standard SVC and SVR are suboptimal for this task.
2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The e-SVM formulation is theoretically grounded, with a clear derivation of the expectation loss function. The extension to the latent case is logically developed and supported by convex optimization arguments.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation**: The introduction of TAPC and NDCG metrics for segment proposal classification is well-motivated and directly measures performance on the underlying problem.
4. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across both segmentation and detection tasks, with a 1.5% AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection. The ablation studies provide useful insights into the performance of different thresholds.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Novelty**: The core idea of using continuous labels with SVMs is not substantially different from existing weighted SVM approaches (cited in the paper), and the connection to these methods is not sufficiently clarified. The paper doesn't convincingly differentiate their approach from prior work on weighted SVMs (e.g., [23, 27]).
2. **Modest Performance Gains**: While the 1.5% AP improvement is positive, it's relatively small compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., 5-10% gains are common with more substantial methodological changes). The significance of this improvement for the field is debatable.
3. **Insufficient Theoretical Analysis**: The paper lacks convergence guarantees for the latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1), which is crucial for a method that relies on alternating optimization. The analysis of the regularization term is superficial.
4. **Comparison to Recent Weakly Supervised Methods**: The paper doesn't compare to more recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond MIL and weighted SVMs, such as the work by Zhang et al. (2016) or other recent NeurIPS papers on weakly supervised learning.
5. **Lack of Scalability Analysis**: The paper doesn't address computational complexity or scalability to large datasets, which is particularly important given the authors' claim about the importance of weak supervision for large-scale datasets like ImageNet.

## Recommendation
This paper presents a reasonable extension to SVM for handling continuous labels in computer vision applications. While the approach is technically sound and shows consistent improvements over baselines, the novelty is limited compared to existing literature on weighted SVMs, and the performance gains are modest. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods, clearer differentiation from weighted SVM work, and stronger theoretical justification for the latent extension.

The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem, but it does not present a sufficiently novel contribution to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The improvements are incremental rather than transformative, and the paper doesn't clearly establish how this approach advances the state of the art beyond existing methods.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify Novelty**: Explicitly state how the e-SVM differs from weighted SVMs in the literature, with a more detailed comparison to prior work.
2. **Expand Related Work**: Include recent weakly supervised learning methods from NeurIPS proceedings (e.g., 2016-2018) that might be more relevant than the MIL approaches cited.
3. **Strengthen Theoretical Analysis**: Provide convergence guarantees for the latent e-SVM optimization and analyze the impact of the regularization term more thoroughly.
4. **Include Scalability Analysis**: Demonstrate how the method scales with dataset size and number of proposals, which is crucial for the authors' claim about large-scale applications.
5. **Consider More Substantial Experiments**: Test on additional datasets or compare to more recent detection frameworks (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN) to better establish the significance of the improvement.

Overall, this is a solid but incremental contribution that would be better suited for a conference with more modest novelty requirements, such as ECCV or CVPR. With significant revisions addressing the novelty gap and theoretical shortcomings, it could be strengthened for NeurIPS consideration.
