Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple extension of linear SVM that learns from “soft” labels in [0,1]. Given a segment proposal Si with feature xi and a “positiveness” score ui (IoU to ground truth), the method assumes a latent binary label Yi ~ Bernoulli(µi), with µi = g(ui), and optimizes the expected hinge loss E[max(0,1−Yi f(xi))] plus L2 regularization. In practice the authors set g(u)=u, so the objective becomes a convex combination of positive and negative hinge losses: g(ui) max(0,1−f(xi)) + (1−g(ui)) max(0,1+f(xi)). They also introduce a latent variant for weak supervision where ui is unobserved (only bounding boxes), optimizing alternately over w and u, with a convex regularizer R(u) that is the cross-entropy between Bernoulli(ubb) and Bernoulli(ui), where ubb is the IoU to the bounding box.

They evaluate e-SVM on: (i) segment proposal scoring, using new criteria (TAPC and NDCG); (ii) semantic segmentation using CPMC segments and second-order pooling; and (iii) object detection by converting segment scores into a class-specific “spatial confidence map” per window, concatenated with RCNN features, yielding +1.2–1.5 AP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The idea of training with soft/probabilistic labels and expected hinge loss is related to prior work on SVMs with uncertain/probabilistic labels, cost-sensitive SVMs, label smoothing/soft labels, and learning from label proportions; the latent extension echoes latent SVM and weakly supervised segmentation/detection with bounding-box supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective: the expected hinge loss yields a convex problem in w given u and is easy to implement with standard solvers (e.g., LibLinear).
- Natural weakly supervised extension: the alternating optimization over w and u with a convex regularizer is conceptually straightforward and practical.
- Practical contribution for detection: the spatial confidence map feature is a simple way to inject segment-level cues into window-based detectors, with non-trivial gains (+1.5 AP on VOC07 over RCNN baseline).
- Empirical comparisons against SVC and SVR on segment scoring using TAPC and NDCG suggest consistent improvements.
- Generality: can plug in different segment generators and base detectors.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- Methodological clarity: While the w-step uses standard SVM solvers, the u-step lacks details. The per-instance subproblem (linear in u plus cross-entropy) is convex on (0,1) but requires constraints and a numerical update (no closed-form given). Convergence criteria, number of iterations, and computational cost are not reported.
- Choice of g(u): Setting µ=u (IoU) as a Bernoulli parameter is ad hoc; IoU is not a calibrated probability that a proposal is positive. No calibration or sensitivity analysis is provided; learning g (e.g., monotonic link) could matter.
- Claims vs baselines: For semantic segmentation, the e-SVM appears “comparable” to SVR in final pixel accuracy, despite earlier claims of superiority on TAPC/NDCG. The benefit in end-task performance is therefore modest.
- Attribution of detection gains: The RCNN comparison is potentially confounded: you alter the bounding-box regression features (add additive kernels), which by itself could improve AP. An ablation isolating the spatial confidence feature vs. changes to RCNN would strengthen the claim.
- Missing comparisons: Given the close connection to cost-sensitive/weighted SVM and SVMs with uncertain/probabilistic labels, related baselines (e.g., duplicating each instance as +1 and −1 with weights u and 1−u, or logistic regression with soft targets) are important to include.

Clarity
- The presentation contains typos, incomplete/broken citations, and formatting issues that hinder readability.
- Algorithm 1 omits key constraints (ui ∈ (0,1)) and optimization details for the u-step.
- Hyperparameters (λw, λR), link function choice, feature dimensionalities, and implementation details (e.g., m for spatial bins, exact CPMC settings) are scattered or missing, limiting reproducibility.

Originality
- The expected hinge loss with soft labels is a well-known construction; it can be viewed as training with “soft” labels or as an instance weighting of positive/negative hinge losses (equivalently, duplicating each sample with label weights). The paper’s main novelty is applying this to segment scoring with an IoU-derived soft label, plus the particular KL regularization for latent u. The latent formulation is akin to biconvex alternation used in latent SVMs, with a prior/regularizer anchored at ubb. Consequently, the conceptual novelty is incremental.
- The spatial confidence map feature is a practical but straightforward engineering choice.

Significance
- The detection improvement (+1.5 AP on VOC07) is useful, but incremental relative to the base detector and may depend on confounded tweaks. The segmentation results are comparable to strong baselines rather than clearly advancing the state of the art.
- The general recipe (learn from soft/latent “positiveness”) is potentially reusable beyond vision, but the paper does not provide theoretical insight (e.g., generalization guarantees, robustness to mis-specified g) or strong empirical breadth.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide full details and code for the u-step: constraints, solver, convergence, and runtime per iteration. Show convergence curves and sensitivity to λR.
- Justify or learn g(u). Consider calibrating IoU to µ via a monotonic link learned from pixel-level data, or compare g(u)=u vs learned isotonic/logistic mapping.
- Add ablations: (i) RCNN baseline with your additive-kernel change alone; (ii) spatial confidence feature alone; (iii) using SVR/SVC to produce segment scores in your spatial confidence pipeline.
- Compare against soft-label baselines: logistic regression with soft targets; SVM implemented by duplicating examples with weights u and 1−u; rank-SVM for the ranking view.
- Report computational costs (training and inference), especially the pixel-wise max over segments and the spatial pooling.
- Clarify evaluation: define TAPC precisely (threshold grid, averaging), and show per-threshold PR/AP curves. Also report standard segmentation metrics (mean IU) for comparability.
- Clean up references and relate to prior work on SVMs with uncertain/probabilistic labels and weakly supervised segmentation/detection with box supervision.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, practical formulation that is easy to implement and extend.
- Empirical gains in object detection with a clear, explainable feature.
- Addresses an important setting (weak supervision from boxes).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing soft-label/weighted SVM and latent SVM literature.
- Incomplete methodology details and potentially confounded comparisons.
- Modest end-task improvements; segmentation gains over SVR are not compelling.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper targets a relevant problem and proposes a practical approach, but the conceptual novelty is limited, the empirical support is not fully convincing or disentangled, and important methodological details are missing. With stronger baselines, clearer optimization and calibration, cleaner experiments/ablations, and improved writing, this work could be competitive.
