## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM formulation, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where only partial or noisy labels are available. The core idea of weighting samples by their “positiveness” (a continuous value representing the degree of correctness) is sound and addresses a practical challenge in many computer vision tasks. While the technical contribution isn't groundbreaking, the paper demonstrates a solid understanding of existing methods (SVC, SVR, weighted SVMs), a clear presentation of the e-SVM formulation, and empirical results that show improvements over baselines in semantic segmentation and object detection. I believe this paper is a **weak accept**, leaning towards acceptance. It addresses a relevant problem with a reasonable solution, but the significance of the improvements, particularly in object detection, feels incremental and the analysis could be deeper.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning from weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where labels aren't binary but represent a degree of correctness (e.g., IoU overlap).  The authors motivate this by highlighting the expense of full pixel-level annotations. They propose e-SVM, which extends traditional SVMs by incorporating a "positiveness" value into the loss function, effectively weighting samples based on their correctness.  A latent version is then introduced to handle situations where these positiveness values are unknown and need to be inferred. 

The related work section is reasonably thorough, covering weakly supervised learning approaches like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and comparing e-SVM to Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR).  The authors correctly position their approach as lying between MIL and fully supervised learning and clearly articulate why MIL isn’t directly applicable to their setting. The discussion of weighted SVMs is also relevant. However, there's a missed opportunity to discuss more recent advances in weakly supervised learning using consistency regularization or pseudo-labeling, which are becoming increasingly popular.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Formulation:** The mathematical derivation of the e-SVM model is clear and concise. The extension to the latent variable case is well-explained, and the iterative optimization algorithm is described logically.
* **Practical Relevance:** Addressing weakly supervised learning is highly relevant, particularly given the challenges of obtaining large, fully annotated datasets.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on three tasks: segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. The inclusion of both pixel-level and bounding box supervision is a strength.
* **Outperformance of Baselines:** e-SVM consistently outperforms SVC and SVR in the reported experiments. The 1.5% improvement on per-class average precision in object detection, while not massive, is noteworthy.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the generality of their approach, highlighting its ability to work with different segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** While the e-SVM formulation is novel, the core idea of weighting samples based on correctness isn't entirely new. Weighted SVMs have been explored, and the extension to a continuous weighting scheme feels like a natural adaptation. The paper doesn’t fully convince me that this is a fundamentally new paradigm.
* **Limited Novelty in Object Detection:**  The most significant application, object detection, feels somewhat bolted-on. Simply concatenating spatial confidence map features with R-CNN features is a straightforward approach.  While it improves performance, the improvement doesn't seem to justify the extensive presentation of the method. More sophisticated integration strategies could have been explored.
* **Regularization Details:** The choice of the KL divergence-based regularization term in the latent e-SVM model feels somewhat arbitrary.  A more detailed ablation study or justification for this choice would be beneficial. Why is this regularization better than others?
* **Evaluation Metrics:** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG for segment proposal classification is appreciated, but the explanation of these metrics could be more thorough.  It’s not immediately clear *why* these are better choices than standard precision-recall curves.
* **Missing Error Analysis:** There is a lack of detailed error analysis. What types of segments or objects does e-SVM struggle with? Understanding these limitations would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters. A discussion of how different hyperparameters affect performance would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in computer vision.
* Presents a mathematically sound and clearly explained approach.
* Demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines in multiple tasks.
* The paper is well-written and organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The technical contribution is incremental and builds on existing ideas.
* The object detection results, while positive, are not particularly dramatic.
* The regularization term choice lacks strong justification.
* Limited error analysis and discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the KL divergence-based regularization term in the latent e-SVM model?
* What types of errors does the e-SVM model make most frequently?  Are there specific object categories or segment characteristics that are particularly challenging?
* How sensitive is the performance of the e-SVM model to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the regularization strength (λR)?
* How does the computational cost of e-SVM compare to SVC and SVR, particularly for large datasets?
* Could you discuss the potential for combining e-SVM with more advanced object detection frameworks beyond simply concatenating features with R-CNN?



**Overall, while this paper is not a groundbreaking advance, it presents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The clear formulation, empirical validation, and practical relevance make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**
