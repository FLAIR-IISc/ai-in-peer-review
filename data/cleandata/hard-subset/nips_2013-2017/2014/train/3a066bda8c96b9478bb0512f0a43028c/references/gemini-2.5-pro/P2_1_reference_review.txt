### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from data where labels are not binary but are represented by a continuous "positiveness" value between 0 and 1. The core idea is to treat the binary label of a training example as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is determined by its known positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random variable. This formulation elegantly handles training data with continuous labels (e.g., IoU scores for segment proposals) and is solvable using standard SVM solvers. The authors extend this to a weakly supervised setting by proposing a latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores are treated as unobserved variables and learned iteratively via an alternating optimization scheme. The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. For object detection, the learned segment classifiers are used to generate a "spatial confidence map" feature, which is shown to provide a notable performance boost when combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN model on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### **Review and Justification**

This is a well-written and solid paper that proposes an interesting and practical solution to an important problem in computer vision and machine learning: learning from weakly or continuously-labeled data. The method is simple, intuitive, and effective.

**Quality: (High)**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the e-SVM loss function in Equation 1 is clear and correct. The formulation is a clever way to incorporate non-binary supervision into a classic max-margin framework. The extension to the latent e-SVM for weakly supervised learning is a logical and well-reasoned next step. The proposed iterative optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a standard approach for this type of problem (alternating minimization) and appears appropriate.

The experimental validation is a significant strength.
1.  The authors don't just evaluate the method on a final application. They first perform a direct evaluation of the core e-SVM model against strong baselines (SVC with various thresholds, SVR) on the task of segment proposal classification. The introduction of new, suitable evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for this specific task is commendable and shows careful thought.
2.  The experiments demonstrate clear superiority over SVC and SVR in the primary task (Figure 3), providing strong evidence for the proposed formulation.
3.  The application to object detection is compelling. Improving upon a very strong state-of-the-art method like R-CNN, even by a modest 1.5% average AP, is a significant achievement and convincingly demonstrates the practical value of the features learned by the latent e-SVM.

**Clarity: (Good)**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated in the introduction. The model is described systematically, starting with the basic supervised case and then moving to the more complex latent variable model. The figures, particularly the framework diagram (Figure 1) and the feature generation process (Figure 2), are helpful in understanding the overall pipeline.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved:
*   The relationship to weighted SVMs (WSVM) is briefly mentioned but could be explored in more detail. The e-SVM loss function can be rewritten as a sum of weighted hinge losses for each sample, effectively creating a positive and negative instance from each data point `x_i` with weights `g(u_i)` and `1-g(u_i)` respectively. A more explicit discussion of whether e-SVM is a novel formulation or a specific, well-motivated instantiation of WSVM would strengthen the paper's positioning.
*   The presentation of the semantic segmentation results in Section 5.2 is too brief. Stating that the accuracy is "comparable" to the state-of-the-art is vague. Providing the exact numbers for the SVR baseline and a more direct comparison would make the results much clearer and more impactful.
*   Minor details are missing, such as the setting of the regularization parameter `λR` for the latent model and any analysis of its sensitivity.

**Originality: (Good)**
The core idea of formulating the loss as an expectation over a random label variable derived from a "positiveness" score feels novel in the context of SVMs. While related to instance weighting and other weakly supervised techniques, the specific formulation and its direct application to continuous IoU scores for training segment classifiers is a new contribution. Previous work has often forced this problem into either a regression (SVR) or a thresholded classification (SVC) framework. The e-SVM provides a more principled alternative that sits naturally between the two. The "spatial confidence map" feature is a simple but effective way to transfer segment-level predictions to a window-based detection framework, which is also a nice practical contribution.

**Significance: (High)**
The significance of this work is twofold. First, it provides a general and effective tool for a common problem: learning from imperfect or non-binary labels. The fact that it can be solved with standard, highly optimized SVM solvers is a major practical advantage that increases its likelihood of adoption. As datasets grow larger, the need for methods that can leverage weaker forms of supervision (like bounding boxes instead of pixel-level masks) becomes paramount. This paper directly addresses this need.

Second, the paper demonstrates a clear, practical impact on a challenging and competitive computer vision task. The improvement over the R-CNN baseline on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a strong result that advances the state of the art. This shows that incorporating segmentation-based cues, learned with a principled weakly supervised model, can provide complementary information to even powerful deep learning features. Researchers in object detection and semantic segmentation are likely to build upon these ideas.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The e-SVM model is an intuitive and mathematically sound way to handle continuous-valued labels in a classification setting.
2.  **Practicality:** The model can be trained using standard SVM solvers, making it easy to implement and deploy.
3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper includes a thorough evaluation with direct model comparisons, novel evaluation metrics, and successful application to two major vision tasks, including an improvement over a state-of-the-art object detector.
4.  **Important Problem:** The work addresses the highly relevant problem of learning from weak supervision, which is critical for scaling up learning systems.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Clarity on Novelty vs. WSVM:** The distinction from and relationship to weighted SVMs could be more clearly articulated.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Latent Model:** The paper lacks discussion on the convergence of the alternating optimization for the latent model and sensitivity to the new hyperparameter `λR`.
3.  **Vague Reporting of Segmentation Results:** The results for the semantic segmentation task are presented without sufficient detail for a full comparison.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with a clear and useful contribution. The proposed e-SVM method is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically validated on challenging tasks. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed in a final version. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it a clear candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
