## Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm designed for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where continuous “positiveness” values (e.g., IoU overlap) are available for proposals instead of binary labels. The authors address limitations of standard SVC and SVR approaches in this context by formulating a loss function that explicitly incorporates the positiveness as a weighted contribution. They present theoretical justification and empirical validation on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating improvements over existing methods, particularly in weakly supervised settings. The work is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in computer vision where obtaining fully labeled datasets is expensive.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a crucial area, and the problem of dealing with continuous positiveness values is well-defined and practical. The work directly tackles a limitation of standard classification/regression approaches.
* **Clear Motivation & Formulation:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges with applying SVC and SVR to this problem and provide a well-motivated and mathematically sound formulation for e-SVM. The connection to weighted SVMs is also thoughtfully discussed.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are promising, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across different datasets (PASCAL VOC) and tasks (segmentation, object detection). The 1.5% average improvement on object detection is a significant result.
* **Generalizability:** The algorithm’s stated ability to integrate with various segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors makes it potentially widely applicable.  The modular design is a strength.
* **Latent Variable Extension:** The extension to handle truly weakly supervised scenarios (bounding box only) with the introduction of the latent variable and associated regularization is a valuable contribution.
* **Novel Evaluation Metrics:** The introduction of TAPC and NDCG for segment-wise confidence prediction is a useful addition and provides a more direct evaluation for this specific task.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results on PASCAL VOC are strong, the experiments are somewhat limited to a single dataset.  Evaluating on larger and more diverse datasets (e.g., ImageNet, COCO) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not adequately discuss the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters, particularly the regularization parameter in the latent e-SVM formulation.  A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Weakly Supervised Methods:** The related work section mentions multiple instance learning (MIL), but the discussion feels brief. A more thorough comparison to recent and powerful MIL-based approaches for segmentation and detection, particularly those building on MIM, would be useful to better position the e-SVM.
* **Clarity of Spatial Confidence Map Feature:** While the spatial confidence map feature is a creative idea, the explanation in Section 4.2 could be more detailed. It’s not immediately obvious why additive kernel approximation is necessary, and a visual example could help understanding.
* **Minor Presentation Issues:** A few typos and instances of awkward phrasing were noted throughout the paper. A final proofread would improve clarity.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical formulation is correct, and the experimental results support the claims. The completeness of the work is good, but could be improved with broader dataset evaluation and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and core ideas are clearly explained. However, as noted above, some sections (e.g., spatial confidence map feature) could benefit from further clarification and visual aids.
* **Originality:** The e-SVM algorithm, particularly its handling of continuous positiveness values and the latent variable extension, represents a novel contribution. The introduction of new evaluation metrics is also a positive aspect.
* **Significance:** The results are promising and suggest that e-SVM has the potential to be a valuable tool for learning from weakly supervised data. The average performance improvement in object detection is a significant finding. The work advances the state of the art in this area, providing a more effective approach than existing methods for handling continuous labels.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial problem in computer vision (weakly supervised learning).
* Novel and well-motivated algorithm with a sound theoretical foundation.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating improvements over existing methods.
* Potential for broad applicability due to its modular design.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of experiments (single dataset).
* Lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Insufficient comparison to cutting-edge weakly supervised learning methods.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The authors have presented a novel algorithm with promising empirical results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly expanding the experimental validation and performing a more thorough comparison to existing MIL-based approaches – would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe these revisions would make this a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
