**Summary**

This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values between 0 and 1, referred to as "positiveness" (e.g., Intersection over Union). The core idea is to model the binary class label as a latent Bernoulli random variable, with its success probability equal to the given positiveness value. The e-SVM then minimizes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This formulation effectively re-weights the positive and negative hinge loss terms for each sample. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness values are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available), treating them as latent variables to be optimized alternately with the model weights. The method is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, where it is shown to outperform classical baselines like Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) and provide a modest improvement when combined with the R-CNN object detector.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a practical and relevant problem in computer vision and machine learning: how to learn effectively from supervision that is stronger than image-level tags but weaker than precise pixel-level labels. Framing this through the lens of continuous "positiveness" scores like IoU is a natural and useful perspective.

2.  **Model Simplicity:** The basic e-SVM formulation is elegant and intuitive. By taking the expectation of the hinge loss, it provides a principled way to incorporate continuous label information into a max-margin framework. A significant advantage is that the resulting optimization problem is a standard weighted SVM problem, which can be solved efficiently with off-the-shelf solvers.

3.  **Empirical Results on Core Task:** The experiments on segment proposal classification are a clear strength. The authors compare their method against sensible baselines (SVC with various thresholds and SVR) and demonstrate superior performance according to the proposed TAPC and NDCG metrics. This provides good evidence that the e-SVM formulation is better suited for this specific type of label structure than converting it to a standard classification or regression problem.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The primary concern is the method's novelty. While presented as a new model, the final loss function for the basic e-SVM is equivalent to a weighted SVM where each data point `x_i` contributes to two terms: a positive sample with weight `g(u_i)` and a negative sample with weight `1 - g(u_i)`. This is a known technique for handling ambiguous labels or instance weighting. The paper acknowledges a connection to weighted SVMs (WSVM) but argues it is different because it "weights labels instead of data points." This distinction is subtle and largely semantic; from an optimization perspective, the final objective is a form of WSVM. The contribution therefore appears to be more of a clever and effective re-framing of a problem to fit an existing framework, rather than a fundamentally new learning algorithm.

2.  **Methodological Rigor and Assumptions:** The paper makes several simplifying assumptions without sufficient justification.
    *   The relationship `μi = g(ui) = ui` is a strong assumption. There is no theoretical or empirical justification provided for why the probability of a proposal being positive should be linearly related to its IoU. An ablation study exploring other functions for `g(.)` would have strengthened the paper.
    *   The latent e-SVM relies on an alternating optimization procedure that is not guaranteed to converge to a global optimum. The quality of the solution is highly dependent on the initialization `u_bb` and the strength of the KL-divergence regularizer, which heavily penalizes deviations from this initialization. The paper lacks an analysis of the sensitivity to these choices or a discussion of the convergence properties.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Validation:** While the segment classification results are positive, the validation on downstream tasks is less convincing.
    *   For semantic segmentation, the authors state their results are "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art" method which uses SVR. However, they do not provide the actual SVR baseline numbers, making this claim impossible to verify. If the performance is merely "comparable" and not superior, it weakens the argument for adopting the proposed method for this task.
    *   For object detection, the 1.5 mAP improvement over a strong R-CNN baseline is commendable. However, this gain comes at the cost of a significantly more complex pipeline: running a segment proposal method, training class-specific latent e-SVMs, generating spatial confidence maps, and then adding these as extra features. It is unclear if this performance gain is significant enough to justify the added complexity, or if similar gains could be achieved through simpler means, such as further fine-tuning of the base R-CNN model.

4.  **Use of Non-Standard Metrics:** The evaluation of the core segment classification task relies on two new metrics, TAPC and NDCG, introduced in this paper. While they appear reasonable for the task, their novelty makes it difficult to compare the reported performance against any prior or future work that does not adopt them.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a well-motivated and cleanly formulated approach (e-SVM) for learning from continuous-valued labels. The idea is intuitive, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness for the direct task of segment proposal classification compared to standard SVC and SVR approaches.

However, the work is weakened by its limited novelty over established weighted SVM techniques, a lack of rigorous justification for key modeling assumptions, and incomplete experimental validation on downstream tasks. The claims for semantic segmentation are unsubstantiated, and the modest gains in object detection may not warrant the substantial increase in system complexity.

While the core idea is interesting and shows promise, the paper in its current form does not provide the extensive evidence and thorough validation required for a top-tier conference like NIPS. It feels more like a strong computer vision application paper than a fundamental machine learning contribution. Therefore, I lean towards rejection, but would be open to reconsidering if the authors can provide a more thorough experimental analysis (especially a direct comparison for segmentation) and a clearer discussion of the method's novelty in relation to existing instance-weighting schemes in SVMs.
