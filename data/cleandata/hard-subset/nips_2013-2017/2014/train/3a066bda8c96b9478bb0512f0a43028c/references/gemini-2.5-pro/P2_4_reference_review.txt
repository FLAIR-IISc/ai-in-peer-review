### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the Expectation-Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, which the authors term "positiveness". The core idea is to model the label of each data point as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of its known positiveness score. The loss function then becomes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegantly reframes the problem, allowing each sample to contribute weighted positive and negative loss components.

The paper extends this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available for segmentation). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized alternately with the SVM weights, guided by a KL-divergence-based regularization term. The authors demonstrate the utility of e-SVM on segment proposal classification, introducing two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG). They then apply it to the downstream tasks of semantic segmentation and object detection. For object detection, they propose a "spatial confidence map feature" derived from e-SVM scores and show that concatenating it with features from the R-CNN model yields a significant 1.5% average precision improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007, achieving a new state-of-the-art result.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed e-SVM formulation is sound and provides a principled way to handle continuous labels within a max-margin framework, which is a significant improvement over ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or repurposing SVR. The latent e-SVM extension is a logical and well-motivated approach for the weakly supervised case.

The experimental evaluation is a major strength.
1.  The authors first validate their core model on the direct task of segment proposal classification, rather than just on a downstream application. The comparisons to SVC (with multiple thresholds) and SVR are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the e-SVM on their proposed metrics.
2.  The application to object detection is particularly strong. Improving upon a powerful state-of-the-art baseline like R-CNN by a 1.5% absolute margin on a competitive benchmark like PASCAL VOC 2007 is a non-trivial and impressive result. This clearly demonstrates the practical value of the features learned by the latent e-SVM.

A minor weakness is that the results on semantic segmentation are presented as merely "comparable" to the state-of-the-art (SVR-based methods). Given that e-SVM outperforms SVR on the direct segment classification task, it is somewhat surprising that this advantage does not translate into a clear win on the final segmentation task. The authors should elaborate on why this might be the case.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The core concepts of e-SVM and latent e-SVM in Section 3 are explained with clarity and the mathematical formulation is easy to follow. The connection to and distinction from weighted SVMs is also helpfully discussed.

However, a few areas could be improved for clarity and reproducibility:
*   The description of the spatial confidence map feature in Section 4.2 could be more detailed. While the text mentions pooling, the specifics (e.g., average vs. max pooling) are only clarified later in the experiments section. This key component of their object detection pipeline should be described more fully in the methodology section.
*   The optimization of the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is presented, but there is no discussion of its convergence properties. Is convergence guaranteed? How sensitive is it to initialization, and how many iterations are typically required in practice?

**Originality:**
The core idea of the expectation loss for an SVM is novel and elegant. While weighted SVMs exist, the e-SVM formulation, where a single data point contributes to both positive and negative loss terms based on its "positiveness", is a new and clever perspective. This provides a principled bridge between regression (predicting the IoU) and classification (predicting the label) within a single framework.

The application to weakly supervised learning by treating positiveness as a latent variable is a natural extension, but the combination with a KL-divergence regularizer to ground the latent variables is a solid contribution. The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation criteria for segment classification is also a novel, if minor, contribution to address a gap in evaluation for this specific problem.

**Significance:**
The paper's contributions are significant for several reasons:
1.  It addresses a very common and practical problem in computer vision and other fields: learning from supervision that is not cleanly binary. The IoU score for proposals is a prime example, and the e-SVM offers a better tool for this than existing alternatives.
2.  The ability to learn effectively from weak labels (bounding boxes) is critically important for scaling up learning systems, as pixel-level annotation is prohibitively expensive. The latent e-SVM provides a concrete and effective method for this scenario.
3.  The most significant result is the demonstrated improvement on the object detection task. By showing that their segmentation-based cues can boost a powerful, CNN-based detector like R-CNN, the authors prove that their method provides complementary information and can advance the state of the art on a major computer vision challenge. This is a strong indicator that other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A novel, principled, and elegant SVM formulation (e-SVM) for handling continuous labels.
*   A strong and convincing experimental validation, including direct model evaluation and application to downstream tasks.
*   State-of-the-art results on the challenging PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection benchmark, demonstrating clear practical utility and significance by improving a very strong baseline (R-CNN).
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weak supervision, which is highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The semantic segmentation results are underwhelming ("comparable"), which slightly weakens the claim of the model's general superiority.
*   Some minor details regarding the experimental setup (e.g., spatial confidence map pooling) and optimization (e.g., convergence) could be clearer.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated core idea, supported by excellent experimental results on a very challenging task. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the significance of the contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents results that are likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
