This paper proposes the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a modification of SVM to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where labels are not binary but represent a “positiveness” score. The core idea is to weight samples based on this positiveness during training, addressing limitations of direct binarization or standard regression approaches. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines, and achieving competitive results when coupled with a state-of-the-art detector.

The paper presents a reasonably well-defined problem – learning from continuous, rather than discrete, labels – and proposes a straightforward extension to SVM that seems theoretically sound. The motivation is clear: weakly supervised data is common, and relying on ad-hoc thresholds or inappropriate regression techniques can be suboptimal. The experimental setup is also fairly standard, using established datasets (PASCAL VOC) and techniques (CPMC segments, RCNN). However, while the idea is not *wrong*, the paper fails to deliver convincing evidence of substantial novelty or impact, and the validation lacks sufficient rigor.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem of learning from “soft” labels is well-articulated and relevant.
*   **Straightforward Approach:** The e-SVM formulation is a logical extension of standard SVM, and the mathematical derivation appears correct.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate improvements over SVC and SVR on the chosen tasks.
*   **Generalizability:** The authors correctly point out the method's flexibility concerning segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the *combination* of SVM with continuous weighting isn’t entirely trivial, the core idea isn’t a breakthrough. Weighted SVMs are well-established (references [23, 27] are briefly mentioned but not thoroughly discussed concerning the specific weighting strategy employed here). The contribution feels incremental rather than transformative.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to SVC and SVR feels somewhat superficial. These are convenient baselines, but they are not the *strongest* available. For instance, comparing against more recent or sophisticated weakly supervised learning methods (beyond the limited Related Work section) is crucial. For object detection, simply adding the e-SVM features to RCNN, while yielding an improvement, doesn’t demonstrate the full potential of the method – a more integrated approach might be more insightful.
*   **IoU as Positiveness:** The reliance on IoU as the sole measure of “positiveness” is a significant limitation. IoU is a crude metric and doesn’t capture semantic correctness. A segment with high IoU can still be semantically incorrect. The paper acknowledges this implicitly but doesn't explore alternative measures or weighting schemes.
*   **Regularization in Latent SVM:** The regularization term in the latent e-SVM (Equation 3) feels arbitrarily chosen and its impact isn't thoroughly analyzed. The explanation for its necessity is intuitive but lacks detailed justification.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the proposed method. For example, how crucial is the specific regularization term in the latent e-SVM?
*    **Overstated Claims:** Phrases like "state-of-the-art object detection performance" (while technically true given the +1.5% improvement) are slightly misleading and overstate the significance of the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method is technically sound and addresses a practical problem.
*   The experimental results show some improvement over basic baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is limited, and the contribution feels incremental.
*   The experimental validation is not rigorous enough, lacking comparison to strong baselines and thorough ablation studies.
*   The reliance on IoU as the sole measure of “positiveness” is a significant weakness.
*   The paper doesn’t clearly establish why this method is significantly better than existing weighted SVM approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable, but ultimately unconvincing, contribution. The idea is sound, but the validation is lacking, and the novelty is limited. While the results are positive, they don't justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more appropriate for a more applied venue. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider expanding the related work, performing more comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art methods, conducting ablation studies, and providing a more rigorous analysis of the impact of their design choices.
