Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning with “positiveness” labels ui in [0,1] (e.g., IoU between a segment proposal and ground-truth). The supervised version assumes ui observed and defines an expected hinge loss where the label Yi is Bernoulli with success probability µi=g(ui), instantiated as g(u)=u. This yields a convex objective in w equivalent to a weighted combination of positive and negative hinge losses per sample. A latent extension handles weak supervision when only bounding boxes are provided: ui becomes a latent variable, optimized by alternating minimization with a convex regularizer R(u) equal to the cross-entropy between Bern(ubb) (IoU with bounding boxes) and Bern(ui), keeping u near ubb.

Applications include (i) semantic segmentation via segment classification with CPMC proposals and second-order pooling features, and (ii) object detection by forming spatial confidence map features from segment confidences and concatenating with RCNN features. The paper reports modest gains over SVC and SVR on segment ranking metrics (TAPC, NDCG), comparable segmentation accuracy to SVR, and +1.2–1.5 mAP improvement when augmenting RCNN on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Related work discussed includes MIL for weak supervision, WSVM, and RCNN. However, prior work on SVMs with uncertain or probabilistic/soft labels and latent SVMs is not fully covered; the proposed expected hinge loss closely relates to soft-label SVM variants and instance-weighted SVMs.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: learning from weak supervision where labels reflect graded overlap rather than binary class.
- Simple, general formulation that reduces to standard convex optimization in w; practical to implement by reweighting.
- Latent extension is well posed (convex in blocks) and regularized with a convex KL-based term; intuitive and likely to stabilize optimization.
- Empirical integration into RCNN shows measurable detection gains (+1.5 mAP on VOC07), suggesting usefulness as a complementary cue.
- Introduces direct segment-confidence evaluation using ranking-style metrics (NDCG) and threshold-averaged AP.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears incremental: the expected hinge loss with µ in [0,1] is equivalent to weighting positive and negative hinge losses per example and is closely related to instance-weighted SVMs and soft/probabilistic label SVMs. These connections and prior art are not rigorously analyzed or cited.
- The mapping g(u) is fixed to identity without justification or calibration; no analysis of sensitivity or learning g from data.
- The latent optimization lacks detail: no closed-form update or algorithmic specifics for u-step, choice and tuning of λR, convergence behavior, or runtime/iteration counts; no ablations on the effect of the regularizer vs. unregularized latent updates.
- Semantic segmentation results are only “comparable” to SVR and do not surpass strong baselines; segment-ranking gains over SVR are small.
- For detection, potential confounds: the paper modifies bounding-box regression features; it is unclear if identical modifications are applied to the baseline, and per-class deltas and statistical significance are not reported.
- Clarity issues: numerous typos and incomplete references; some implementation details (TAPC threshold schedule, NDCG gain/cutoff, solver setup for per-example weights, parameter settings) are missing, hindering reproducibility.

Quality
- The supervised e-SVM objective is technically sound and convex. The claim that standard SVM solvers can be used is plausible if the loss is implemented as per-example weighted hinge losses for both labels (e.g., by duplicating examples or using instance weights), but this should be explicitly described.
- The latent e-SVM uses alternating minimization over convex blocks with a convex regularizer; it should converge to a local minimum. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., monotonic decrease) and practical details are not provided.
- Empirical validation is limited in scope: comparisons are primarily to SVC/SVR; no comparisons to other soft-label or weakly supervised segmentation approaches beyond MIL are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable, but organization and writing need attention (typos, formatting, incomplete citations). Some definitions (g, R, algorithmic steps) should be expanded, and evaluation protocols clarified.

Originality
- Moderate. The core idea effectively applies soft/probabilistic labels to hinge loss and alternates over latent soft labels with a KL regularizer. Similar ideas have been explored in WSVM, latent SVM, and SVMs with uncertain labels; the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself.

Significance
- Moderate. The method is simple and could be adopted in settings with graded labels. The reported +1.5 mAP improvement on VOC07 is nontrivial but modest. The segmentation improvements over SVR are small, limiting impact in that domain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal equivalence or implementation recipe showing how e-SVM can be solved with standard SVM solvers (e.g., duplicate examples with labels ±1 and weights µi, 1−µi).
- Explore and report the effect of different g(u) (e.g., calibrated logistic mappings), and/or learn g.
- Detail the u-update (derivation, closed form or numerical method), discuss λR sensitivity, initialization, convergence, and runtime; include ablations with/without regularization and with different regularizers.
- Expand related work to include SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels and uncertain labels, and clarify differences.
- For detection, ensure fair baselines (identical preprocessing/feature augmentations), report per-class AP changes and statistical significance.
- Improve writing and fix references; fully specify evaluation protocols (TAPC thresholds, NDCG variant).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, simple approach to incorporate graded/weak labels into max-margin learning.
- Demonstrated utility as a plug-in cue that improves a strong detector (RCNN).
- Convex, modular components with straightforward adoption potential.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing soft/weighted/uncertain-label SVM literature; missing citations.
- Modest empirical gains, especially for segmentation; limited breadth of baselines.
- Insufficient algorithmic detail for the latent component; clarity and reproducibility concerns.
