This paper proposes the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), an extension to traditional SVMs designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where continuous “positiveness” values (e.g., IoU) are available for segment proposals instead of binary labels. The authors motivate this work by the cost of fully labeled datasets and present a method to handle both observed and latent positiveness values, applying it to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.

The core idea—weighting samples by their positiveness—is relatively straightforward and builds upon existing weighted SVM literature. While the formulation is mathematically sound and leverages existing SVM solvers, the novelty feels incremental. The problem setup, dealing with continuous labels arising from imprecise localization, *is* a practical one, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that e-SVM offers a substantial advantage over existing approaches like SVR, beyond demonstrating statistically significant but often modest improvements. The extension to latent variables appears well-formulated, and the use of a KL-divergence regularization term is a reasonable choice, but the justification for this specific regularization is somewhat thin.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of learning from weakly supervised data with continuous positiveness scores.
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of e-SVM is logical and appears correct, building on established SVM principles.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors present results on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC) for both semantic segmentation and object detection.
*   **Practical Application:** The method is applicable to real-world scenarios where only weak labels are available.
*   **Efficient Implementation:** Leveraging existing SVM solvers is a pragmatic choice.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of weighting samples by confidence/positiveness is not entirely new. The contribution feels more like a specialized application of weighted SVMs than a fundamentally new learning algorithm.
*   **Modest Improvements:** While statistically significant, the performance gains over SVR (and in some cases, SVC) are often small (e.g., 1.5% AP improvement in object detection). This doesn't strongly justify the added complexity of the e-SVM formulation.
*   **Limited Baselines:** While SVR and SVC are included, a more thorough comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods, particularly those beyond simple regression/classification, would strengthen the paper. Methods leveraging MIL or more sophisticated techniques for handling imprecise labels are not discussed or compared.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the results to the choice of regularization parameter (lambda_R) in the latent variable case. This is a crucial parameter and its impact needs further analysis.
*   **Overly Detailed Algorithm Description:** Some sections (e.g., Algorithm 1) are overly detailed and could be streamlined. The description of the spatial confidence map feature is also a bit convoluted.
*   **Lack of Error Analysis:**  A deeper error analysis, investigating *why* e-SVM succeeds or fails in certain cases, would provide valuable insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound method for a practical problem.
*   The empirical results, while modest, are positive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The code is available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental and the performance gains are not substantial enough to warrant strong endorsement.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to relevant state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and error analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the method is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, the limited novelty and modest performance improvements, coupled with the lack of a thorough comparison to existing work, raise concerns. The paper feels more like a well-engineered application of existing techniques than a significant breakthrough. If the authors can strengthen the comparison to related work, provide a more compelling justification for the e-SVM formulation (perhaps through theoretical analysis), or demonstrate more substantial performance gains, then I would be inclined to increase my rating. Currently, it doesn’t quite meet the threshold for a strong NIPS paper.
