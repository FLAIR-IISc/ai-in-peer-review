The paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing the problem of segment proposal confidence prediction. The core idea is to treat the positiveness of segment proposals as a continuous value rather than a binary label, adapting the SVM loss function to handle this continuous range. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM compared to SVC and SVR on segmentation and object detection tasks, showing improvements in both strongly and weakly supervised scenarios.

This work builds upon a significant body of research in weakly supervised learning, particularly focusing on methods to leverage incomplete or imprecise labels. The problem setting is well-motivated, as acquiring precise pixel-level annotations is expensive, while bounding box annotations are more readily available. Existing approaches like SVC and SVR are identified as baselines, and the paper clearly articulates their limitations in handling continuous positiveness values. The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged, and the authors highlight the distinction of weighting labels instead of data points. The use of IoU as a measure of positiveness is standard in this field, and the adoption of CPMC segments and R-CNN as building blocks is reasonable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem of learning from weakly labeled data with continuous segment positiveness is clearly defined and motivated.
*   **Novel Approach:** The e-SVM formulation, adapting the SVM loss function for continuous labels, offers a novel and potentially effective approach.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on multiple tasks (segmentation, object detection) with both strong and weak supervision. The comparisons to SVC and SVR demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm is applicable to real-world scenarios where weak labels are more abundant than strong labels.
*   **Algorithmic Clarity:** The latent e-SVM optimization algorithm is clearly described (Algorithm 1).
*   **General Applicability:** The authors correctly point out the general applicability of their method to different segment proposal detectors, image features, and classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty of Applications:** While the e-SVM formulation is novel, the application to semantic segmentation and object detection follows established pipelines (CPMC + SVM, R-CNN + feature concatenation). The novelty primarily lies in the confidence prediction component, and a stronger argument could be made about the significance of improving confidence estimation.
*   **Regularization Term Justification:** The justification for the specific form of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM model (using KL divergence) is somewhat weak. While it prevents the solution from collapsing to 0 or 1, a more detailed explanation of why this particular regularization is optimal would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λR) on performance is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While comparisons with baselines are provided, more detailed ablation studies exploring the contribution of different components (e.g., spatial confidence map features in object detection) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Table 1 lacks necessary context. It's unclear what "RCNN" refers to in this context and whether it’s the baseline or the combined model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on multiple tasks.
*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The applications are relatively standard and do not represent a major breakthrough in semantic segmentation or object detection.
*   The regularization term in the latent e-SVM model lacks strong justification.
*   The analysis could be strengthened with more ablation studies and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a well-motivated and technically sound approach with promising experimental results. While the applications are not groundbreaking, the improvement in confidence prediction could be valuable in various computer vision tasks. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed with further analysis and justification. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on providing a stronger rationale for the regularization term and including more detailed ablation studies.
