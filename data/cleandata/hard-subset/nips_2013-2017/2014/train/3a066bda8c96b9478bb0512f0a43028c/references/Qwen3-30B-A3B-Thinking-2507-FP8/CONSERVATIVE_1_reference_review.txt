

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning, particularly for computer vision tasks where labels are not binary but continuous values (referred to as "positiveness") between 0 and 1. The authors claim their method outperforms standard SVC and SVR approaches on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using PASCAL VOC datasets.

The core idea involves treating the continuous "positiveness" (e.g., IoU values) as a probabilistic label rather than binarizing it with heuristics. The paper introduces two variants: a basic e-SVM for cases where positiveness is observed (strong supervision) and a latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness must be inferred from bounding box annotations (weak supervision). The method appears to be a straightforward extension of weighted SVM approaches, where the weights are derived from the continuous positiveness values.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides clear problem formulation for the segment proposal classification task, which is a well-motivated problem in computer vision. The experimental evaluation on PASCAL VOC datasets is reasonably structured, and the authors demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC across different threshold values. The method is implemented using standard SVM solvers, which is a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Novelty:** The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor variation of existing weighted SVM approaches. The authors acknowledge this in Section 2, stating their method "differs from WSVMs mainly in that it weights labels instead of data points." However, this distinction is minimal and not well-justified theoretically. The approach is essentially a special case of weighted SVM with weights derived from the positiveness values, which is a standard technique in machine learning. The paper fails to demonstrate how this differs meaningfully from established methods.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The reported improvements (1.5% average AP gain on object detection) are modest and not convincingly significant for a NIPS submission. The paper does not compare to more recent or stronger baselines in weakly supervised learning (e.g., recent MIL approaches that handle partial labels more effectively). The experiments are limited to PASCAL VOC datasets without testing on larger-scale benchmarks like ImageNet, which is a major shortcoming given the paper's claim about scalability.

3. **Theoretical Weaknesses:** The probabilistic interpretation of the label (Section 3.1) is superficially introduced without strong justification. The Bernoulli distribution assumption for Yi seems arbitrary and not well-motivated by the problem context. The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis to explain why the expectation loss formulation should outperform standard approaches.

4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance on PASCAL VOC 2007, but the reported 60.0% AP is only slightly better than the original RCNN (58.5%) with bounding box regression. The marginal improvement does not justify the "state-of-the-art" claim. The authors also overstate the general applicability of their method without demonstrating it across diverse tasks or datasets.

5. **Comparison to Relevant Work:** The related work section acknowledges existing weakly supervised learning methods but fails to properly contextualize their approach within the broader literature. The paper does not adequately address how their method differs from or improves upon established weakly supervised learning techniques like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) or other recent approaches that handle partial labels more effectively.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a method that is largely incremental and lacks sufficient theoretical justification or experimental validation to warrant acceptance at NIPS. While the problem addressed is important, the proposed solution does not demonstrate substantial novelty or clear advantages over well-established methods. The claimed improvements are marginal, and the experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more rigorous theoretical analysis explaining why the expectation loss formulation should outperform standard weighted SVM approaches
2. Comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond SVC and SVR
3. Testing on larger-scale datasets to demonstrate scalability
4. A more nuanced discussion of the relationship to existing weighted SVM literature

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and significant contributions over incremental improvements, I recommend rejection. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the proposed e-SVM offers a meaningful advancement over existing techniques, particularly given the marginal performance gains reported. The method appears to be a straightforward application of weighted SVM with a minor reinterpretation of the weighting scheme, which does not meet the high bar for novelty and significance expected at NIPS.

The authors should consider whether their approach offers a truly novel perspective or if it could be more appropriately positioned as a minor extension of existing weighted SVM techniques with a more thorough comparison to the relevant literature. Without these improvements, the paper does not advance the field sufficiently to warrant publication at NIPS.
