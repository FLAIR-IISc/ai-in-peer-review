## Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically scenarios where only partial or imprecise labels are available (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level segmentations). The core idea is to treat the “positiveness” of proposals (segments) as a continuous value representing the degree of correctness, and formulate learning as an expectation over a Bernoulli distribution representing the true label.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM, and its latent variable extension for fully weakly supervised scenarios, on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, consistently outperforming Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of weakly supervised learning. It acknowledges and differentiates itself from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches commonly used with image-level labels, arguing that their setting, with continuous positiveness scores, doesn't fit MIL's assumptions. The connection to weighted SVMs is also appropriately discussed, highlighting the key difference: e-SVM weights *labels* rather than data points, leading to a different loss function. The authors cite relevant work in segmentation and object detection, providing a good understanding of the state-of-the-art.  They build upon existing segment proposal methods (CPMC, superpixels) and detection frameworks (RCNN), integrating their approach as a complementary component.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:**  The e-SVM formulation is a clever adaptation of SVMs to handle the nuances of weakly supervised data with continuous positiveness scores.  The expectation loss formulation is well-motivated and addresses the limitations of naive binarization (SVC) or regression (SVR) approaches.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the loss function and the optimization algorithm appear sound. The authors highlight the convexity properties, ensuring efficient solution via standard SVM solvers.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling experimental evidence across three tasks (segment classification, segmentation, detection) demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods.  The 1.5% average AP improvement on object detection with RCNN is notable.
* **Generality and Flexibility:** The authors emphasize the algorithm’s flexibility – it can work with any segment proposal detector, image features, and base detector, making it widely applicable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The use of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1, Figure 2) helps to illustrate the framework.
* **Addressing Weak Supervision:** The latent e-SVM effectively tackles the challenging scenario where positiveness is unobserved, a common setting in real-world weakly supervised learning problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the comparisons to SVC and SVR are informative, the paper lacks detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the e-SVM.  For example, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of regularization term in the latent e-SVM? What is the impact of different segment proposal methods?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn't provide much detail on hyperparameter tuning. It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λR in the latent e-SVM).
* **Dependence on Segment Proposals:** The performance is naturally tied to the quality of segment proposals.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully explore how variations in proposal quality impact results. A more thorough investigation of different proposal generators would strengthen the analysis.
* **NDCG as Evaluation Metric:** The use of NDCG for segment classification (TAPC NDCG) seems slightly unusual for this task. While valid, its relevance compared to more standard classification metrics (e.g., AUC, precision@k) could be better justified.
* **Overly Optimistic Detection Results:** While the 1.5% AP improvement is positive, it's worth noting that this is on top of an already strong baseline (RCNN). A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors reduced by the e-SVM component would provide more insight.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for weakly supervised learning.
* The experimental results are consistently positive across multiple tasks.
* The algorithm is general and flexible, making it potentially useful to a wide range of researchers.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed ablation studies limits understanding of the algorithm's behavior and sensitivity.
* The hyperparameter tuning process is not discussed in detail.
* Dependence on the quality of segment proposals is acknowledged but not fully investigated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a well-motivated and effective approach for handling continuous positiveness scores in weakly labeled data. While some areas could benefit from further exploration (ablation studies, hyperparameter analysis, dependence on proposals), the overall quality of the work and the positive experimental results justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by including more ablation studies and discussing hyperparameter sensitivity.  Clarifying the rationale for using NDCG as an evaluation metric would also be beneficial.
