### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from data where labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" scores within the [0, 1] interval. This problem setting is common in computer vision, where, for instance, a segment proposal's quality is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea is to treat the binary label of a training sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is given by its observed positiveness score. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This formulation cleverly results in each sample contributing to both the positive and negative sides of the loss function, weighted by its positiveness.

The authors extend this to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM weights via an alternating minimization scheme, guided by a regularization term that encourages them to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box). The authors validate their method on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, demonstrating that e-SVM outperforms standard SVC and SVR baselines and can provide a complementary feature that improves the performance of a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The formulation of the expectation loss is the main strength of this paper. It is an elegant and principled approach to a common problem that is often handled with ad-hoc heuristics (e.g., thresholding IoU scores to create binary labels for SVC). By re-framing the loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, the method avoids arbitrary thresholds and provides a smooth, continuous way to incorporate supervisory signals of varying strength. The distinction from instance-weighted SVMs, where e-SVM weights the *labels* rather than the *data points*, is well-articulated and highlights the novelty of the approach.

2.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The problem of learning from weak supervision is highly significant, particularly in domains like computer vision where fine-grained annotation is a major bottleneck. The proposed method directly addresses this. The fact that the approach can be solved using standard SVM solvers is a significant practical advantage, lowering the barrier to adoption. Furthermore, demonstrating a 1.5% AP improvement on a very strong baseline like R-CNN on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset is a noteworthy achievement and clearly shows the practical value of the proposed segmentation-based cues.

3.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of both the basic and latent e-SVM is sound. The alternating optimization procedure for the latent model is a standard and appropriate choice for the problem structure. The experimental design is solid:
    *   It correctly identifies SVC and SVR as the most relevant baselines and performs a thorough comparison.
    *   The introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal ranking task shows careful thought about how to properly evaluate the direct output of their model, rather than relying solely on downstream task performance.
    *   The experiments cover both the fully supervised (pixel-level labels) and weakly supervised (bounding box labels) scenarios, validating the model's flexibility.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Missing Details:** While the core idea is well-explained, certain parts of the methodology and experiments could be clarified:
    *   **Latent Variable Optimization:** The choice of the KL-divergence-based regularizer `R(u)` is not deeply motivated. While it is convex and serves its purpose, the authors could briefly explain why this form was chosen over simpler alternatives like a squared L2-norm penalty `||u - u_bb||^2`. A short discussion on the properties of this regularizer (e.g., its "strong" nature) would strengthen the paper.
    *   **Semantic Segmentation Results:** Section 5.2 is underdeveloped. The paper states the results are "comparable" to the state-of-the-art SVR model but does not provide the concrete numbers for SVR under the same experimental setup. This makes it difficult to assess the performance of e-SVM on this task. A table comparing the pixel accuracy of e-SVM and SVR for both the supervised and weakly-supervised settings would make the evaluation much more compelling.
    *   **Feature Generation:** The "additive kernels approximation mapping" is mentioned without explanation. While citing the original paper is appropriate, a single sentence explaining its role (e.g., "to efficiently approximate a non-linear kernel map, allowing a linear SVM to learn a non-linear classifier") would improve the paper's self-containedness for readers not familiar with the technique.

2.  **Depth of Analysis:** The object detection results are significant, but the analysis could be deeper. The paper shows an average improvement and highlights one class ("potted plant"). A more detailed per-class breakdown or analysis of failure cases could provide more insight into *why* and *where* the segmentation-based confidence map helps. For instance, does it primarily help with objects that have characteristic shapes but ambiguous textures, or objects that are heavily occluded? Such analysis would elevate the paper from a demonstration of a working technique to a source of deeper understanding.

### **Overall Evaluation and Justification**

This is a strong paper with a clear, novel, and well-motivated contribution.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-formulated, and the claims are supported by solid experimental results on standard benchmarks against appropriate baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are easy to grasp. However, as noted, some sections could benefit from additional detail and justification to improve reproducibility and completeness.
*   **Originality:** The expectation-loss formulation is novel and provides a new perspective on learning from continuously-valued labels in a max-margin framework. It is clearly distinguished from prior work.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a crucial problem in machine learning and computer vision. The method is practical, effective, and likely to be used or built upon by other researchers working on weakly supervised learning, semantic segmentation, and object detection. Improving a state-of-the-art model like R-CNN demonstrates clear progress.

In conclusion, the paper presents a valuable and elegant contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. Its strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality far outweigh its minor weaknesses in clarity and analytical depth, which can be easily addressed in a final version. The work is a good fit for NIPS and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience. I recommend acceptance.
