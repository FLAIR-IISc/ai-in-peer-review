

# NIPS Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels. The e-SVM treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the observed positiveness, leading to an expectation-based loss function. The method extends naturally to weak supervision via latent variables (latent e-SVM) when positiveness is unobserved. The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection on PASCAL VOC datasets, claiming improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The work connects to related weakly supervised learning approaches like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs (WSVMs), but the paper's problem setting (where positiveness is a continuous value between 0 and 1) differs from MIL's bag-level labeling and WSVM's instance weighting. The authors position their contribution as a natural extension of standard SVMs for this specific problem.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively articulates the practical need for weakly supervised learning, noting the high cost of pixel-level annotations while acknowledging the availability of bounding box annotations in large datasets like ImageNet.

2. **Technical Clarity**: The e-SVM formulation (Equation 1) is conceptually simple and well-motivated. The extension to latent e-SVM (Equation 2) for weak supervision is logically sound, and the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward to implement using standard SVM solvers.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on semantic segmentation and object detection are well-designed, using standard datasets and baselines. The authors introduce two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that better match the continuous label problem than traditional binary classification metrics.

4. **Practical Impact**: The 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is significant in a field where gains are typically marginal, and the method is simple to integrate with existing systems like RCNN.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of why the expectation loss formulation is superior to alternative approaches for handling continuous labels. Why not use a regression model with bounded output (e.g., logistic regression) instead of the expectation loss? The claim that "it can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver" doesn't address why this specific loss function is better than alternatives.

2. **Inadequate Comparison to Relevant Work**: The paper cites MIL and WSVMs but fails to properly distinguish its contribution from recent weakly supervised learning methods. For example, the 2012 Carreira et al. paper "Semantic Segmentation Using Regions and Parts" (cited as [5]) already uses bounding box annotations for semantic segmentation and could be a more direct comparison point. The paper also doesn't discuss how e-SVM compares to more recent weakly supervised approaches like those using deep learning with bounding box supervision.

3. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims e-SVM "outperforms SVC and SVR in both scenarios" but the improvements are modest (TAPC: 36.69 vs 35.23 for SVR). The 1.5% AP gain in object detection, while meaningful, is not transformative compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN).

4. **Limited Analysis of the Regularization Term**: The choice of the KL-divergence regularization (Equation 3) is not sufficiently justified. The authors claim it's "strong" but provide no analysis of how different regularization terms affect performance or why this specific form was chosen.

5. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the latent e-SVM compared to SVC/SVR, which is important for practical applications.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision with a technically sound, simple extension of SVMs. While the method is well-motivated and the experiments are well-conducted, the theoretical justification is weak, and the improvements over baselines are modest. The paper's contribution is more of a clever application of probabilistic reasoning to a specific problem rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic insight.

The work shows promise for practical applications where weak supervision is available, but it doesn't represent a major advance in the field. The significance is limited by the modest gains (1.5% AP) and the lack of theoretical novelty. While the method could be useful in practice, it doesn't significantly advance the state of the art in a way that would be transformative for the field.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the problem of weakly supervised learning, with a clear formulation and promising empirical results. However, the theoretical foundation is insufficient, the comparisons to the most relevant recent work are inadequate, and the improvements over baselines are modest. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods, and clearer articulation of the novelty relative to existing work.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add a theoretical analysis of why the expectation loss is superior to other approaches for continuous label problems
2. Compare more thoroughly with recent weakly supervised learning methods (e.g., those using deep learning with bounding box supervision)
3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach
4. Justify the choice of regularization term with empirical evidence

The paper is acceptable for NIPS as a modest but technically sound contribution to weakly supervised learning, but it doesn't represent a major advance in the field.
