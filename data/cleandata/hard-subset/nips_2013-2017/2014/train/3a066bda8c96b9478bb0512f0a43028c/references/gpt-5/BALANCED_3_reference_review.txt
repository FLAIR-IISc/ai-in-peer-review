Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple modification of hinge-loss SVMs to handle “soft” labels in [0,1] (positiveness), defined as the IoU between a segment proposal and ground-truth. The key idea is to model the unobserved binary label Yi ∈ {−1,+1} as Bernoulli with mean µi=g(ui) and minimize the expected hinge loss, yielding a convex objective in w when ui is known: each example contributes a convex combination of positive/negative hinge losses weighted by µi and 1−µi. For weak supervision (bounding boxes only), ui is treated as latent and learned jointly with w via alternating minimization, regularizing u toward the segment–bbox IoU ubb through a convex cross-entropy penalty. The method plugs into segment-ranking pipelines for semantic segmentation and contributes “spatial confidence map” features to window-based object detectors. On PASCAL VOC, e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on two proposed segment-ranking metrics (TAPC, NDCG). For downstream tasks, the method yields comparable semantic segmentation performance to SVR, and a reported +1.5 AP improvement when concatenated with R-CNN features for detection.

This work sits between supervised segment scoring (e.g., regression or thresholded classification on IoU) and weakly/latent supervision (MIL, latent SVM). The expected hinge formulation closely relates to SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels and learning under label uncertainty/noise; the latent extension is reminiscent of latent SVM with a convex regularizer toward a weak prior (bbox IoU). The spatial pooling of per-pixel segment confidences to augment window detectors relates to prior segmentation-aware detection (e.g., coupling region/segment cues with detectors).

Strengths
- Simple, general formulation: expected hinge loss with soft labels is intuitive, convex in w, and solvable with off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers; the latent variant is an easy alternating scheme with a convex u-step.
- Practical applicability: works with arbitrary proposal generators and features; the spatial confidence map addresses the practical coupling of segment scores to window detectors.
- Empirical evidence across tasks: consistent gains over SVC/SVR on segment ranking; non-trivial improvements for detection when augmenting R-CNN.
- The weakly supervised setting is important in practice (bbox annotations much cheaper than masks).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Novelty of the core loss is limited: minimizing the expected hinge loss under probabilistic labels is well-studied (soft-label/probabilistic-label SVMs, learning with label proportions, and learning with label noise). The paper does not acknowledge or compare to this literature; instead it contrasts only with WSVM, SVC, and SVR.
- Limited theoretical analysis: no generalization, calibration, or consistency results; the latent optimization lacks convergence guarantees beyond block coordinate descent to a local minimum. The u-step requires constraints u∈(0,1); numerical details (e.g., clipping, step sizes) are missing.
- Regularizer choice is somewhat ad hoc (cross-entropy to ubb). There is no ablation versus alternatives (e.g., quadratic or TV-like smoothness within boxes), and no sensitivity analysis for λR or initialization.
- Experimental evaluation is thin where it matters most:
  - Semantic segmentation results are “comparable” to SVR rather than clearly better; metrics (mIoU vs pixel accuracy) and per-class breakdowns are unclear.
  - For detection, +1.2–1.5 mAP over R-CNN is promising but modest; there is no ablation to isolate whether e-SVM is the driver versus the spatial pooling pipeline (e.g., what if we build the same feature using SVR or SVC?). Statistical significance and variance are not reported.
  - Missing comparisons to closely related segmentation-aware detection approaches (e.g., simultaneous detection and segmentation; segment-based features atop R-CNN).
Clarity
- Numerous typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., “λw · 1 wTw,” “N N∑,” missing 1/2 factor), making the objective ambiguous; several references are malformed. Figures are referenced but not clearly described, and algorithmic details (constraints, stopping criteria) are sparse.
Originality
- The latent regularization toward bbox overlap and the specific spatial confidence map feature are the main novel pieces. The core “expected hinge” loss itself is not new in spirit; the paper should position itself against soft-label SVM/uncertain-label SVM literature.
Significance
- Practical gains exist, but the incremental nature and limited analysis reduce the potential impact. Without stronger baselines and ablations, it is hard to attribute the detection gains to e-SVM per se.

Suggestions for improvement
- Positioning and citations: discuss and cite SVMs with soft/probabilistic labels and learning with label proportions/label noise; clarify how your loss differs and why it is preferable here. Also compare to segmentation-aware detection (e.g., simultaneous detection and segmentation) and modern proposal/segment methods.
- Theory and formulation: clean up notation; provide a clear derivation of Eq. (1) and constraints on u; include details for the u-step optimization (closed form? projected gradient?); analyze sensitivity to g(u) (try learned monotone mappings), λR, and initialization ubb; report convergence behavior.
- Experiments:
  - Semantic segmentation: report standard metrics (mIoU), per-class results, and compare to strong baselines on the same features; show the effect of replacing SVR with e-SVM in an otherwise identical pipeline.
  - Detection: include ablations replacing e-SVM with SVR/SVC in the same spatial pooling feature; vary m and pooling; compare to SDS-like baselines; report runtime and training details; provide confidence intervals over multiple runs.
  - Weak supervision: demonstrate robustness to annotation noise or missing boxes; evaluate on larger datasets if possible.
- Clarity: fix typos and references; provide code or sufficient details to reproduce results.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important weakly supervised setting with a simple, general method.
- Easy to implement with standard solvers; practical improvements on VOC detection.

Cons for acceptance
- Core loss closely mirrors known soft-label SVM ideas; novelty is limited.
- Clarity and rigor issues in the formulation and writing.
- Experimental analysis lacks key baselines/ablations; downstream gains are modest and hard to attribute.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is useful and the empirical direction is promising, but the paper, as written, does not sufficiently establish novelty or provide the depth of analysis and comparisons expected for NeurIPS. Strengthening positioning, clarity, and experimental rigor could make this a solid contribution.
