Summary
The paper proposes the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), which replaces the standard hinge loss on a binary label with the expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli “soft label” Yi with success probability µi = g(ui), where ui is a “positiveness” score in [0,1] (instantiated as IoU with ground-truth segments). This allows training on continuous supervision without binarizing labels. A latent e-SVM handles weak supervision when only bounding boxes are available by treating ui as latent and adding a convex regularizer encouraging ui to stay close to the IoU with the ground-truth box (using cross-entropy/KL). Optimization alternates between convex subproblems in w and u. The method is evaluated on segment confidence prediction (introducing TAPC and using NDCG), semantic segmentation (with pixel and box supervision), and object detection by concatenating a spatial “confidence map” feature from e-SVM with RCNN features on PASCAL VOC, yielding 1.2–1.5 mAP improvement over RCNN.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The expected hinge loss with soft labels is reasonable and convex in w (for fixed µ). The latent version is a block-coordinate descent over two convex subproblems (given the chosen convex regularizer). However, key details are underspecified:
  - Optimization of u: for fixed w, the objective per sample is linear in ui plus a convex KL term; a closed-form update seems possible, but is not derived, and algorithmic/practical details (constraints, step sizes, initialization, stopping) are missing.
  - “Can be solved by any standard SVM solver”: e-SVM’s loss is l+ g(ui) + l− (1 − g(ui)), i.e., a weighted sum of positive and negative hinge terms for each example. This is not a standard SVM objective. It can be reduced to SVM on duplicated examples with per-instance weights, but most off-the-shelf solvers only support class weights, not instance weights. Please clarify the reduction and the specific solver/implementation.
  - Hyperparameters: values/selection for λw, λR, g(·), and convergence criteria are not provided. Reproducibility is therefore limited.
- Empirical support: The segment prediction metrics (TAPC/NDCG) show small gains over SVR and larger gains over SVC. Semantic segmentation accuracy is reported as “comparable” to SVR, which contradicts earlier claims of outperforming SVR. For detection, +1.2–1.5 mAP over RCNN is promising, but the ablation is incomplete: it jointly changes features (concatenation) and modifies bounding box regression, making it hard to isolate the contribution of e-SVM vs feature engineering. Variance or statistical significance is not reported.

Clarity
- The core ideas are understandable, but the presentation has many typographical errors, broken formulas (e.g., the regularization term appears as “λw · 1/w^T w” instead of a standard quadratic penalty), and incomplete references. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading.
- Notation is sometimes overloaded (e.g., g(ui) set to ui without discussion of alternatives), and Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail to implement.
- The new evaluation metric TAPC is only briefly described (what thresholds, how averaged), and figures/tables appear referenced but not shown here; ensure all details are explicit.

Originality
- Using expected hinge loss with probabilistic/soft labels and learning with uncertain/fuzzy labels has prior art in the SVM and weak supervision literature. The paper cites weighted SVMs but does not adequately position e-SVM against SVMs with probabilistic/fuzzy labels or partial/ambiguous labels, nor against latent SVMs with entropy/regularized E-steps. The latent KL-regularized extension resembles posterior/prior-regularized EM. The novelty rests mainly on applying these ideas to segment IoU supervision and the specific KL regularizer towards box-IoU.
- The spatial confidence map feature for detection is a reasonable engineering contribution, but related ideas of pooling class-specific spatial confidence have appeared in detection/segmentation pipelines; the paper should better contextualize this.

Significance
- Practically, a simple approach that leverages soft/latent supervision and yields 1–1.5 mAP gains on a strong baseline is useful. The idea is general and could be adopted with modern proposal generators and backbones.
- Scientifically, the methodological advance over existing soft/latent SVM formulations seems modest without stronger theoretical or empirical differentiation. The segmentation results are only comparable to SVR; the detection gains may be attributable to feature concatenation rather than the specific loss.

Pros
- Addresses an important setting: learning from weak and soft supervision at scale.
- Conceptually simple, convex in blocks, and broadly applicable.
- Proposes task-agnostic segment evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG).
- Demonstrates non-trivial gains when augmenting RCNN with spatial confidence maps.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to SVMs with uncertain/fuzzy labels and latent SVMs; related work positioning is incomplete.
- Technical details insufficient for reproduction; solver claim likely overstated.
- Inconsistencies between claims and results (e-SVM “outperforms” vs “comparable” to SVR).
- Detection ablations insufficient; confounds with feature engineering and bbox regression changes.
- Many typos, broken equations, and incomplete references.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a rigorous reduction from e-SVM to a standard SVM solver, or describe the custom optimization used (including per-instance weights, duplication strategy, and solver settings).
- Derive the closed-form or numerical update for ui with the KL regularizer; report λR, λw, initialization, and convergence criteria.
- Report ablations: (i) RCNN + spatial confidence map built from SVC/SVR; (ii) RCNN + confidence map from e-SVM vs latent e-SVM; (iii) with/without modifying bbox regression features; (iv) sensitivity to m (grid size) and pooling.
- Clarify TAPC details and include statistical variance across runs.
- Position against prior work on SVMs with probabilistic/uncertain labels and latent/regularized EM, and add proper citations.
- Fix equations, notation, and references; provide implementation details sufficient to reproduce results.

Overall recommendation
The problem is important and the empirical improvements on detection are encouraging, but the paper in its current form falls short on clarity, positioning, and rigor. With stronger related work coverage, clearer optimization details, and cleaner ablations, this could be a solid contribution. As is, I lean toward rejection (weak reject), primarily due to limited novelty and insufficient technical detail, despite the positive practical signal.
