## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM-based approach, e-SVM, for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing the scenario where data points have a continuous “positiveness” value rather than a binary label. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates promising empirical results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming standard SVC and SVR baselines. While the improvements aren’t revolutionary, the method is a solid contribution with potential for wider applicability. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning with weakly supervised data, a critical issue for scaling computer vision models due to the high cost of pixel-level annotation. The authors propose e-SVM, which extends the standard SVM framework to incorporate continuous positiveness values representing the degree to which a proposal aligns with the ground truth. This elegantly bridges the gap between fully supervised and multiple instance learning (MIL) scenarios.

The authors correctly position their work within the context of weakly supervised learning, referencing relevant methods like MIL and its variants (Vezhnevets et al., Liu et al.). They also clearly delineate their approach from existing solutions using standard SVC and SVR, highlighting the limitations of binary thresholding for SVC and the mismatch of SVR for bounded positiveness values. The connection to weighted SVMs is also appropriately discussed, noting the difference in weighting scheme (labels vs. data points).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation, allowing for continuous positiveness values, is a meaningful extension to standard SVMs. This offers a more nuanced approach compared to forcing a binary classification.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the loss function is clear and mathematically well-motivated. The latent variable extension for handling truly weakly supervised scenarios (bounding boxes only) is logical and justified.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates improvements over SVC and SVR on both segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. The use of PASCAL VOC datasets provides a standard benchmark for comparison.  The 1.5% average AP improvement in object detection is respectable.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the adaptability of e-SVM to various segment proposal methods, feature types, and base detectors, making it a potentially versatile tool.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Modest Improvements:** While statistically significant, the performance gains are not dramatic. The authors should discuss potential reasons for this and avenues for further improvement. Is the performance limited by the segment proposals themselves?
* **Regularization of Latent Variable:**  The choice of the KL divergence-based regularization for the latent variable *u* seems somewhat arbitrary. A more comprehensive ablation study exploring different regularization terms (L1, L2) and their parameters would strengthen the justification.  The explanation of why *not* regularizing leads to issues is good, but a more nuanced exploration of regularization strength is needed.
* **Limited Comparison:**  While SVC and SVR are good baselines, comparing to more recent, state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods for semantic segmentation and object detection (beyond the mentioned MIL approaches) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method's competitiveness.
* **Spatial Confidence Map Feature:** The description of how the spatial confidence map feature is used for object detection is relatively brief.  More details on the additive kernels approximation and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices (e.g., the regularization parameter λR). A brief analysis of hyperparameter tuning would add to the robustness of the findings.



**4. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 5, Equation 2:**  Explain why R(u) is added to the loss function. It’s stated it’s a regularization term, but the motivation for regularizing the latent variable's distribution isn't explicitly clear here.
* **Page 6, Algorithm 1:**  Clarify the stopping criterion for the iterative optimization.  “Converge” is vague.  Is it based on a change in the loss function, a maximum number of iterations, or something else?
* **Page 7, Semantic Segmentation:** Provide more details about the specific implementation of the second-order pooling features.
* **Page 8, Object Detection:**  Expand on the benefit of using additive kernels approximation.  What specific improvement does it provide?
* **Figures:**  The figures are generally informative, but Figure 1 could be made more visually appealing with better labeling and clarity.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The e-SVM formulation is a novel and theoretically sound extension to standard SVMs for handling continuous positiveness values.
* The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines on multiple tasks.
* The method is versatile and potentially applicable to a wider range of problems.
* The writing is clear and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The performance gains, while statistically significant, are modest.
* The regularization of the latent variable could be further justified with more extensive ablation studies.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods is limited.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above – specifically, a more in-depth analysis of the latent variable regularization and a broader comparison to recent work – would significantly strengthen the contribution. The paper presents a valuable addition to the field of weakly supervised learning and has the potential to be influential.
