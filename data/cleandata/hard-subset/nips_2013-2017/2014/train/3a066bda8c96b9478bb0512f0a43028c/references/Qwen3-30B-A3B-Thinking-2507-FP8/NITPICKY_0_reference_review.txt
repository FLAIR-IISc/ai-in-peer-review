

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised learning scenarios where training labels are continuous values (positiveness) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their approach outperforms SVC and SVR in segment proposal confidence prediction, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks.

**Strengths:** The problem of weakly supervised learning is important and relevant to computer vision. The paper provides a reasonable theoretical foundation for the e-SVM model, and the experimental results on PASCAL VOC datasets appear to demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR in the specific tasks tested.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies in Formulation:** The paper contains several critical mathematical errors. In section 3.1, the loss function is written as "L(w) =λw · 1 wTw + N N∑ i=1 EYi [max(0, 1− YiwTxi)]" which is clearly not properly formatted (missing spacing, inconsistent notation). The standard SVM loss should be L(w) = (λ/2)w^T w + (1/N)Σ_i max(0, 1 - y_i w^T x_i). This is a fundamental error that undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

2. **Confusing Notation:** The paper uses "ui" to represent both the positiveness (IoU) and the Bernoulli success probability (µi = g(ui)), creating significant confusion. When they state "we simply set µi = ui," this is mathematically inconsistent with their later equation (1) where they use "g(ui)".

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper claims "it can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver" but fails to provide any theoretical analysis showing why their loss function is convex or why the optimization converges. The claim that "it is equivalent to 'weight' each sample with a function of its positiveness" is not rigorously justified.

4. **Inadequate Experimental Details:** The paper claims "average improvement of 1.5 percent on per-class average precision" but doesn't provide the exact baseline values or statistical significance. The statement "our method can improve the average AP by 1.2 before applying bounding boxes regression" lacks context - what is the baseline AP without bounding box regression?

5. **Misleading Comparison to SVR:** In section 5.1.2, the paper claims "the TAPC and NDCG of our method are higher than those of SVR" but SVR is a regression model while this is a classification problem. The authors don't explain why SVR is the appropriate baseline for this classification task, which undermines their comparison.

6. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper has inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "w" vs "w" for weight vectors), inconsistent capitalization ("Expectation Loss SVM" vs "e-SVM"), and formatting errors in references (e.g., extra commas and spaces). Figure 1 is mentioned but not provided in the text.

7. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims "our approach is general. It can use any segment proposal detectors, any image features, and any classifiers" without providing any evidence beyond their specific experiments. The statement "it can handle other classification problems where only the 'positiveness' of the samples instead of binary labels are available" is vague and lacks supporting examples.

8. **Inadequate Related Work Comparison:** While the paper mentions MIL in section 2, it doesn't properly compare to MIL-based methods that address similar weakly supervised problems. The claim that "this weakly supervised problem cannot be solved by MIL" needs stronger justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem in weakly supervised learning and presents some empirical results, the technical flaws in the formulation, inconsistent notation, and lack of theoretical grounding significantly undermine its contribution. The mathematical errors in the core algorithm are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, where technical correctness is paramount. The paper also fails to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, which is a critical requirement for NIPS.

The experimental results, while seemingly positive, are presented with insufficient detail to assess their significance. The comparison to SVR is fundamentally flawed as SVR is designed for regression tasks, not classification with continuous labels. The paper's theoretical claims are not properly supported by analysis.

Given the serious technical errors in the core algorithm formulation and the lack of sufficient theoretical grounding, I recommend rejection. While the problem is important, the paper's fundamental technical flaws make it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the mathematical formulation, provide proper theoretical justification, and address the many technical inconsistencies before this work could be considered for publication.
