**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the "expectation loss SVM" (e-SVM), a method for learning from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, which the authors term "positiveness." The core idea is to model the binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable whose parameter is the known positiveness value. This leads to a loss function that is an expectation of the standard hinge loss, effectively weighting the positive and negative loss terms for each sample. The paper extends this to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness values are unknown (e.g., when only bounding boxes are available) and are treated as latent variables to be optimized alternately with the SVM weights. The authors apply this framework to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating that their method can improve performance over standard SVC and SVR baselines and can provide a complementary feature to boost a strong object detector like R-CNN.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the relevant and practical problem of learning from supervision that is weaker than pixel-level segmentation but stronger than image-level labels. The concept of "positiveness" (e.g., IoU) is an intuitive way to frame this intermediate level of supervision.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed e-SVM is a simple and elegant extension of the standard SVM. Its formulation as a weighted hinge loss allows it to leverage existing, efficient SVM solvers, which is a significant practical advantage.
3.  **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the utility of the proposed method on challenging computer vision tasks. Showing any improvement over a strong baseline like R-CNN on the PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection task is noteworthy, suggesting that the segmentation-based cues captured by the latent e-SVM provide useful complementary information.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its interesting contributions, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical presentation, experimental methodology, and overall polish. My concerns are detailed below.

**1. Clarity and Technical Soundness:**

*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** The manuscript is replete with typographical and grammatical errors that impede readability and suggest a lack of careful proofreading (e.g., "continues value" instead of "continuous," "segmentic segmentation" instead of "semantic," "one of the advantage" instead of "advantages," "et.al." instead of "et al.").
*   **Imprecise Notation and Definitions:**
    *   The redefinition of `w` to include the bias `b` and `x` to include a constant 1 is a common but potentially confusing abuse of notation that should be stated explicitly.
    *   In Section 3.1, the positiveness `ui` for a segment `Si` is defined with respect to "the groundtruth instances labeling" for "each object class." This implies a segment has a separate `ui` for each class, but the notation does not reflect this, creating ambiguity in the multi-class setting.
    *   The regularization term in Equation (3) is described as being based on cross-entropy but written using KL-divergence (`DKL`), which is confusing. While related, the terms are not identical, and the presentation should be precise.
*   **Missing Theoretical Justification:** Algorithm 1 presents an alternating minimization scheme. While this is a standard approach, the paper simply states to repeat the steps "until Converge" without any discussion or proof of convergence guarantees. Does this procedure converge to a local minimum? Under what conditions? This is a critical omission for an optimization-based method.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The authors introduce TAPC and NDCG as "two new evaluation criteria," claiming they "have not found any work on this sort of direct performance evaluation." NDCG is a widely used, standard metric for ranking, and its application here, while appropriate, is not novel. The claim of novelty is an overstatement.

**2. Experimental Rigor:**

*   **Confounding Factors:** In the object detection experiments (Section 5.3), the authors report improving upon R-CNN by combining its features with their new spatial confidence map feature. However, they also mention they "modified the bounding box regularization method used in [14]... It will lead to a slightly improved performance." This is a significant confounding variable. The reported 1.5 percentage point gain cannot be uniquely attributed to the e-SVM features; it is partially due to an undescribed modification of the baseline itself. The contribution of the proposed features must be isolated by testing with and without this modification.
*   **Inconsistent and Vague Reporting:**
    *   The paper claims e-SVM "outperforms" SVC and SVR in the introduction, but the semantic segmentation results in Section 5.2 are described merely as "comparable" to SVR. The exact performance figures for the SVR baseline are not provided, making a direct comparison impossible.
    *   The use of "1.5 percent" improvement is imprecise; this should be stated as "1.5 percentage points" to avoid ambiguity.
    *   No statistical significance tests are performed to validate whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

**3. Presentation and Scholarship:**

*   **Bibliography:** The references section is in a state of severe disrepair. It is poorly formatted, with numerous missing years, venues, and other essential details. Most critically, it contains a glaring error: reference [6] points to "The PASCAL Visual Object Classes Challenge 2010" but is used to cite the ImageNet dataset. The correct citation for ImageNet is Deng et al., 2009. Furthermore, a citation in the related work section is literally a question mark (`[?]`). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a scientific publication and severely undermines the credibility of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles an important and practical problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   The proposed e-SVM method is simple, intuitive, and builds on well-understood principles.
*   The latent e-SVM provides a principled way to learn from bounding-box-level supervision for segmentation tasks.
*   The method shows empirical promise, achieving a SOTA result on VOC 2007 detection (though with the caveats mentioned above).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript suffers from a pervasive lack of care, reflected in numerous typos, grammatical errors, and imprecise technical descriptions.
*   The experimental methodology is flawed, particularly by the inclusion of a confounding factor in the object detection experiments.
*   The originality is incremental; the e-SVM is a straightforward weighted SVM, and the latent version is a standard alternating minimization scheme.
*   The bibliography is exceptionally sloppy and contains critical errors, which calls into question the authors' diligence and familiarity with the literature.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core ideas of this paper are interesting and address a relevant problem, the execution of the manuscript is far below the standards expected for NIPS. The combination of unclear technical descriptions, methodological flaws in the experiments, and an egregious lack of care in the paper's preparation (especially the bibliography) makes it impossible to confidently assess the work's contributions. The sheer number of minor and major flaws overshadows the paper's potential significance. I cannot recommend this paper for publication in its current form. A thorough revision addressing the issues of clarity, experimental rigor, and presentation would be required before it could be reconsidered.
