

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary

This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap ratios) rather than binary labels. The authors present two variants: a basic e-SVM for strong supervision (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved and treated as a latent variable). They apply their approach to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

The paper builds upon previous work in weakly supervised learning, particularly distinguishing their problem setting from Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) by noting that their weak supervision setting (bounding box annotations) cannot be handled by MIL due to the partial overlap of segments with ground truth. The authors claim their method outperforms SVC and SVR on segment proposal classification and provides modest improvements in object detection.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses an important practical problem in computer vision - reducing the need for expensive pixel-level annotations by leveraging more readily available bounding box annotations. This aligns with current trends in reducing annotation costs for large-scale datasets.

2. **Clear Formulation**: The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM model is well-structured, with a logical progression from the basic model to the latent extension. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from SVC and SVR.

3. **Appropriate Experiments**: The experimental design is reasonable, using standard datasets (PASCAL VOC 2011 and 2007) and appropriate baselines (SVC and SVR). The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment classification is a useful contribution.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates a concrete application of their method to object detection (coupling with RCNN), showing a 1.5% improvement in average AP, which is non-trivial for a simple feature augmentation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Novelty**: While the authors claim their approach is novel, it appears to be a straightforward extension of weighted SVMs, which have been studied in the literature (e.g., [23, 27]). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate their work from existing weighted SVM approaches or demonstrate how their expectation loss formulation provides theoretical advantages.

2. **Modest Results**: The reported improvements (1.5% AP gain) are modest compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., Faster R-CNN improved upon RCNN by over 20% AP). The paper doesn't adequately contextualize these gains within the broader landscape of object detection research.

3. **Insufficient Experimental Detail**: The paper lacks crucial details about implementation:
   - No discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity or tuning process
   - Unclear how much of the improvement is due to the e-SVM features versus other modifications (e.g., "modified bounding box regularization")
   - No comparison to other weakly supervised methods (e.g., MIL variants) that could be relevant for their problem setting

4. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art object detection performance" but the improvement is marginal compared to RCNN itself. The paper also claims their method "can be easily extended to learn segment classifiers under weak supervision" but doesn't provide evidence that this is actually easier than other approaches.

5. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides limited theoretical analysis to support why the expectation loss formulation should work better than existing approaches. The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonably well-executed approach to a relevant problem in computer vision. The e-SVM algorithm is a reasonable extension of existing SVM techniques, and the empirical results demonstrate modest but consistent improvements over baselines. However, the contribution lacks sufficient theoretical novelty to be compelling for a venue like NeurIPS, where the bar for methodological contributions is high.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough comparison to existing weakly supervised learning methods
2. Better contextualization of their results within the broader object detection literature
3. Deeper theoretical analysis of why the expectation loss formulation is advantageous
4. More detailed experimental analysis (hyperparameters, ablation studies)

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. While the problem addressed is important, the contribution is incremental rather than novel, and the results are modest. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the e-SVM approach represents a significant advance over existing methods, which is required for acceptance at NeurIPS. The method is well-described and the experiments are appropriately conducted, but the contribution does not meet the threshold for a NeurIPS publication.

The paper would be suitable for a more application-focused venue (e.g., CVPR or ICCV) where the focus is on practical improvements rather than methodological novelty. For NeurIPS, the authors would need to substantially strengthen their theoretical contribution or demonstrate significantly larger improvements to be competitive with other submissions.
