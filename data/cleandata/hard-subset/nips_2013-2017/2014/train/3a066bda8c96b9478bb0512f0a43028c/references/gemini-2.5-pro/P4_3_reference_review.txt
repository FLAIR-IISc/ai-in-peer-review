**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) for weakly supervised learning problems where training labels are continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded interval (e.g., [0, 1]), rather than binary labels. The primary motivation comes from computer vision tasks like semantic segmentation and object detection, where generating precise pixel-level annotations is costly, but weaker forms of supervision, such as bounding boxes, are more readily available. For a given training sample (e.g., a segment proposal), its "positiveness" can be quantified by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object mask.

The core idea of e-SVM is to treat the unobserved binary label of a sample as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of its known positiveness score. The e-SVM objective then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random label. This formulation naturally handles the continuous nature of the supervision, weighting the positive and negative loss terms for each sample according to its positiveness.

The paper extends this model to the more challenging weakly supervised case, where the true positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available). In this "latent e-SVM" model, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables. The authors propose an alternating optimization algorithm that iteratively updates the SVM model parameters and estimates the latent positiveness variables. A key contribution is the introduction of a KL-divergence based regularization term on the latent variables to prevent trivial solutions and guide the optimization.

The proposed method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection using the PASCAL VOC dataset. The authors introduce new metrics (TAPC and NDCG) for the segment proposal classification sub-task, showing that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR). For object detection, they use the latent e-SVM to generate a novel "spatial confidence map feature," which is then combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector, achieving a significant 1.5% average precision improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the e-SVM loss function is a principled and well-motivated extension of the standard SVM for handling continuous-valued supervision. The extension to the latent e-SVM via alternating minimization is a standard and appropriate technique for this class of problems. The inclusion of a regularizer on the latent variables is crucial for stable optimization and is well-justified. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on standard, challenging benchmarks. The authors compare against relevant baselines (SVC, SVR) and demonstrate a notable improvement over a very strong object detection system (R-CNN), which validates the practical utility of their approach.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the progression from the basic e-SVM to the latent e-SVM is logical and easy to follow. The description of how the method is applied to computer vision tasks, particularly the construction of the spatial confidence map feature for object detection, is clear. However, a few points could be clarified for better reproducibility:
-   The paper states that the e-SVM model can be "effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver." While plausible, the exact mechanism is not detailed. Since the loss function weights the positive and negative hinge loss components for a single sample differently, it is not a standard weighted SVM. A brief explanation of how to transform the problem for a solver like LibLinear (e.g., by duplicating samples with different weights and labels) would strengthen the paper.
-   The new evaluation criteria, TAPC and NDCG, are interesting additions for the sub-task of segment classification, but their description is very brief. A little more detail on their implementation, especially the assignment of relevance scores for NDCG, would be beneficial.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel approach to learning with continuous-valued supervision. While it builds on the well-established foundation of Support Vector Machines and latent variable models, the core contribution—the expectation loss formulation—is new and specifically tailored to the problem. It provides a more suitable objective function than the alternatives of thresholding the continuous labels for SVC or treating it as an unbounded regression problem for SVR. The paper also does a good job of positioning the work relative to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs, clearly articulating the differences in problem setup and formulation. The spatial confidence map is a simple but effective and novel method for integrating segment-based predictions into a region-based detector.

**Significance:**
The work addresses a problem of high practical and scientific importance: reducing the dependency on expensive, fine-grained annotations in machine learning. The ability to learn effectively from bounding boxes instead of pixel-perfect masks is highly valuable, especially with the proliferation of large-scale datasets. The demonstrated 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 over the R-CNN baseline was a significant result at the time, showcasing that segmentation-like cues learned from weak supervision could enhance even state-of-the-art deep learning-based detectors. The e-SVM formulation is general and could potentially be adopted in other domains beyond computer vision where supervision is imperfect or expressed as a degree of confidence. The model's simplicity and compatibility with standard solvers further enhance its potential for impact and adoption by other researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Model:** The paper proposes the e-SVM, a novel and well-justified loss function for learning from continuous-valued labels, which is a significant problem setting.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method is shown to improve upon a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN) on a standard benchmark, demonstrating clear practical value and advancing the state of the art.
3.  **General and Practical:** The proposed algorithm addresses a general problem in weakly supervised learning and is designed to be compatible with standard optimization tools, making it accessible and easy to adopt.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and provides a complete piece of work from model formulation to extensive evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the loss function is novel, the overall optimization strategy for the latent model (alternating minimization) is a well-known technique. The contribution could be viewed as a clever, but incremental, extension of existing ideas.
2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** As noted above, the paper could provide more implementation details regarding the use of standard SVM solvers, which would improve reproducibility.
3.  **Limited Gains in Segmentation:** The reported performance on semantic segmentation is only "comparable" to the SVR baseline, with the main empirical success being in the object detection application.

In summary, the paper's strengths, particularly its novel and principled formulation for an important problem and its strong empirical results in object detection, substantially outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid and significant contribution to the field of machine learning and its application to computer vision.
