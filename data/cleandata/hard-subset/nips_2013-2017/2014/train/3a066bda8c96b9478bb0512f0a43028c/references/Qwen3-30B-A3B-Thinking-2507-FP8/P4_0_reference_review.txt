

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values (positiveness scores) rather than binary labels. The approach treats the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution, with the success probability derived from the observed positiveness (e.g., IoU overlap). The paper presents both a basic e-SVM for strong supervision (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved). The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines. The work relates to previous weakly supervised learning approaches like MIL and MIM, but argues that these cannot address their specific problem setting (where most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels). The paper also distinguishes from weighted SVMs by noting that e-SVM weights labels rather than data points.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the problem of weakly supervised learning. The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM is clear and well-justified, with a proper derivation of the expectation loss function. The latent e-SVM extension for weak supervision is elegantly implemented using an alternating optimization approach that converges reliably. The paper introduces two novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) specifically designed for segment classification tasks, which are well-motivated and provide a more direct assessment than previous task-specific metrics.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over SVC and SVR on the PASCAL VOC 2011 segmentation dataset across multiple evaluation metrics. The application to object detection (with RCNN) is particularly compelling, showing a 1.5% average precision gain on PASCAL VOC 2007, which represents a meaningful improvement in the field. The ablation studies (e.g., different threshold values for SVC) help validate the robustness of the approach.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth. The connection to practical computer vision problems (semantic segmentation and object detection) is strong, and the authors clearly articulate how their method can be integrated into existing pipelines with minimal modification.

## Weaknesses
The primary weakness is the limited novelty compared to recent work in weakly supervised learning. While the paper correctly differentiates from MIL and WSVM, it does not sufficiently address more recent approaches to weakly supervised learning in computer vision, such as those using deep learning with weak supervision (e.g., [1, 2, 3] in the references). The paper claims the problem setting is "in the middle between these settings and the strong supervision case," but this is not clearly supported with citations to recent NIPS papers that address similar weakly supervised learning scenarios.

The improvement over SVR (the closest baseline) is marginal (TAPC: 36.69 vs. 35.23), which may not be sufficient to justify the new algorithm in the context of NIPS, where significant improvements are typically expected. The paper does not provide sufficient analysis on why the e-SVM outperforms SVR, beyond the mathematical formulation.

The regularization term in Equation 3 is not thoroughly justified. While the authors note it's a strong regularization that prevents u from becoming 0 or 1, they don't explore alternative regularization terms or provide ablation studies to show why this specific formulation is optimal.

The paper lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability. For large-scale applications (which the paper claims are the target domain), it would be valuable to know how the algorithm scales with dataset size or number of proposals.

The paper does not provide failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, it doesn't address how the method performs when the initial ubb (IoU between segment and bounding box) is very low, or when proposals are highly ambiguous.

## Final Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels, with clear mathematical formulation and strong experimental results on standard computer vision benchmarks. The method is well-motivated and addresses a practical problem in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain.

The novelty is moderate - while the expectation loss formulation is novel, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate from recent work in weakly supervised learning that has been presented at NIPS and other top venues. The marginal improvement over SVR (the closest baseline) may not be sufficient for acceptance at NIPS, where significant advances are expected.

The paper's significance is strengthened by its practical application to two important computer vision tasks, with meaningful improvements in object detection performance. The method's flexibility (applicable to any segment proposal detector, feature extraction method, and classifier) is also a positive aspect.

## Recommendation
**Accept** (with minor reservations)

The paper makes a solid contribution to weakly supervised learning, with a clear technical formulation, strong experimental validation, and practical applications. While the novelty is not revolutionary, the work is technically sound and demonstrates meaningful improvements over existing methods. The paper would be strengthened by more thorough comparison with recent weakly supervised learning methods and additional analysis of the regularization term, but these are not critical flaws. The significance of the work is sufficient for acceptance at NIPS.
