**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Support Vector Machine formulation, termed Expectation-Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data. The core idea is to handle training examples that have a continuous "positiveness" score (e.g., Intersection over Union) between 0 and 1, rather than a binary label. The e-SVM frames the true binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is the given positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This elegant formulation effectively weights the positive and negative hinge loss components for each sample. The authors extend this to a fully weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness scores themselves are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box supervision is available). Here, the scores are treated as latent variables and inferred via an alternating optimization scheme. The method is validated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, where it is shown to provide a significant performance boost to the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of the paper is the e-SVM formulation. It provides a theoretically sound and intuitive way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision, which is a common and practical problem in computer vision. It moves beyond ad-hoc thresholding (used for SVC) or treating the problem as standard regression (SVR), which ignores the bounded nature of the target variable and the underlying classification goal.

2.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** A major advantage is that the basic e-SVM can be optimized using standard SVM solvers by appropriately weighting the positive and negative loss for each sample. The latent e-SVM extension uses a straightforward alternating minimization procedure that is easy to understand and implement.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Challenging Task:** The most compelling result is the 1.5% absolute mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 when augmenting the R-CNN detector. Improving upon such a strong, state-of-the-art baseline is non-trivial and clearly demonstrates the practical significance of the features learned by the latent e-SVM. This shows that segmentation-style cues, even when learned from weak bounding-box supervision, provide complementary information to powerful deep features.

4.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The authors rightly point out that standard classification or regression metrics are ill-suited for the segment proposal classification task. The introduction of the Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and the use of Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) are commendable, providing a more appropriate and direct evaluation of the model's core capability.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Analysis of the Latent e-SVM Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is a coordinate descent-like algorithm on a non-convex objective. While this is a standard approach, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its properties. There are no convergence guarantees mentioned, and the quality of the local minimum found is highly dependent on initialization and the regularization term `R(u)`. An ablation study showing the sensitivity to the hyperparameter `λ_R` and the effect of removing the regularizer (which the authors claim leads to a collapse to binary values) would be very valuable.

2.  **Unconvincing Semantic Segmentation Results:** The paper reports that for semantic segmentation, the e-SVM's performance is merely "comparable" to the SVR baseline. This is surprising and somewhat undermines the claims of superiority. If e-SVM outperforms SVR on the direct segment ranking metrics (TAPC and NDCG), why does this advantage disappear in the downstream task? Is the final pasting/inference stage the bottleneck? A brief analysis of this discrepancy would make the paper's claims more cohesive.

3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper is missing visualizations that would help build intuition. For the object detection task, showing the generated "spatial confidence maps" for a few successful (and unsuccessful) detections would be highly insightful. This would help answer the question: what kind of spatial information is the e-SVM capturing that the R-CNN features are missing? Is it better boundary adherence, object part localization, or context?

4.  **Clarity on Relation to Weighted SVMs:** The paper briefly contrasts e-SVM with instance-weighted SVMs (WSVM). The distinction is subtle but important: in e-SVM, a single data point contributes to *both* the positive and negative sides of the margin, weighted by `u_i` and `1-u_i` respectively. In a standard WSVM, a point has a single label and its entire hinge loss term is weighted. A more explicit discussion of the implications of this difference would improve clarity. For example, e-SVM's formulation seems to naturally penalize a point being confidently on the wrong side of the margin, regardless of which side it is.

**Final Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel, well-motivated core idea. The e-SVM formulation elegantly addresses the common problem of learning from continuous-valued labels. The experimental validation, particularly the significant improvement over the R-CNN object detector, provides strong evidence for the method's utility and significance.

While the paper could be improved with a more detailed analysis of the latent model's optimization, a deeper investigation into the semantic segmentation results, and qualitative visualizations, these weaknesses do not detract from the core contribution. The work is original, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with likely impact for researchers in weakly supervised learning and computer vision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled loss function for learning with continuous labels.
*   Demonstrates a significant, non-trivial improvement on a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN).
*   The method is general and can be widely applied.
*   The algorithm is practical and can leverage existing SVM solvers.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The optimization for the latent model lacks theoretical analysis.
*   Downstream results on semantic segmentation are not as convincing as the direct evaluation metrics would suggest.
*   Missing key ablation studies and qualitative visualizations.

I recommend acceptance. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the formulation and the strong object detection results, outweigh the weaknesses, which could be addressed in a final version.
