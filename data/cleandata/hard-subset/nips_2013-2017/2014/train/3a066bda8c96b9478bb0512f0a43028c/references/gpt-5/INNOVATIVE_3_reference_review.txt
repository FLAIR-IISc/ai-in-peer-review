Summary
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), a simple modification of the hinge loss to accommodate soft labels in [0,1] (e.g., IoU-based “positiveness” for segments). Each training example contributes an expected hinge loss under a Bernoulli label Yi with success probability µi=g(ui); in practice g(ui)=ui. This yields a per-example mixture of positive and negative hinge penalties weighted by ui and 1−ui. The authors also introduce a latent variant for weak supervision, where ui is unobserved and is optimized jointly with the classifier under a convex, per-instance regularizer that keeps ui near an initial proxy ubb (IoU w.r.t. bounding boxes), specifically a KL/cross-entropy penalty. They alternate between optimizing w (a standard linear SVM subproblem) and the u’s (convex 1D problems per instance).

They evaluate on segment proposal scoring, introduce two direct metrics for that task (TAPC and NDCG), show small gains over SVR and clear gains over SVC on PASCAL VOC 2011. They then plug e-SVM-derived segment confidences into RCNN via a spatial confidence map feature pooled within m×m bins, reporting +1.2 to +1.5 mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: expected hinge loss with soft/probabilistic labels is intuitive, solver-friendly, and immediately applicable to scenarios with continuous “positiveness.”
- Weak supervision extension is clean: treating ui as latent with a convex regularizer tied to bounding box overlap is a reasonable, practical design; alternating optimization is straightforward.
- Empirical add-on value: adding the spatial confidence map feature to RCNN yields consistent mAP improvements without rearchitecting the detector.
- New task-level evaluation measures (TAPC, NDCG) for segment proposal scoring are sensible and decouple evaluation from specific downstream tasks.

Weaknesses
- Originality/positioning: The core idea closely resembles existing soft/probabilistic/fuzzy-label SVMs and cost-sensitive hinge formulations that take expectations over label uncertainty, as well as instance-weighted SVMs where positive/negative sides of the hinge can be weighted. The paper does not adequately situate e-SVM relative to fuzzy SVM, SVMs with uncertain/probabilistic labels, or label-noise robust hinge losses, and the claimed distinction from weighted SVMs (“weights labels instead of points”) is not fully convincing.
- Limited technical depth: No theoretical analysis beyond convexity of subproblems (no calibration/consistency or generalization guarantees). The choice g(u)=u is ad hoc; no exploration of alternatives or calibration. The u-update is stated as argmin with a KL penalty but lacks derivation or closed-form/algorithmic details, convergence criteria, or complexity.
- Empirical isolation of contributions is incomplete:
  - Segment scoring: e-SVM is only slightly better than SVR; practical significance is unclear.
  - Detection: the improvement could plausibly come from adding any reasonable segment-derived heatmap feature. There is no ablation comparing e-SVM vs SVR-derived confidence maps within the same pipeline, nor sensitivity to m, pooling, or λR. Thus, the unique contribution of e-SVM (vs using SVR or a calibrated regressor) to the mAP gain is not isolated.
- Clarity and polish: Numerous typos and citation issues (missing years/pages, “?” placeholder), and some methodological details are under-specified (hyperparameters λw, λR and how they are chosen; stopping criteria; exact SVM weighting implementation; whether standard solvers support per-instance asymmetric hinge weights as used here).
- Scope of validation: Results are on VOC 2007/2011 only; semantic segmentation gains are reported as “comparable” to SVR rather than improved; no comparisons to more recent weakly supervised segmentation methods or to probabilistic logistic models trained with soft labels.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the derivations and optimization details are too sparse for reproduction. Empirical evidence is mixed: moderate detector gains but small segment-scoring gains; missing ablations to attribute improvements specifically to e-SVM.

Clarity
Readable overall but needs significant editing and more precise experimental and optimization details. Figures/tables are referenced but not all are present in the text provided; citations are often malformed.

Originality
Moderate. The expected hinge with soft labels and a latent extension are natural evolutions of well-known ideas (soft/fuzzy SVMs and latent SVM/EM-style alternation). The spatial confidence map feature is a straightforward pooling design reminiscent of prior heatmap/segmentation-cue features.

Significance
Practical, modest improvements and a clean formulation that could be adopted by practitioners. However, without stronger novelty or deeper analysis/ablations, the impact seems limited for NeurIPS.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please relate e-SVM explicitly to fuzzy/soft-label SVMs, uncertain-label SVMs, and cost-sensitive hinge loss. Under what conditions is your expected hinge equivalent to those formulations?
- Provide the u-subproblem solution details (derivative, existence/uniqueness, solver used, complexity), and an ablation on λR and the choice of g(·).
- For detection, run an ablation replacing e-SVM with SVR and SVC to produce the same spatial confidence maps, and report mAP differences. Also report sensitivity to m and pooling choices.
- Clarify how per-example asymmetric hinge weights are implemented with LibLinear or other solvers.
- Calibrate and report the relation between predicted scores and IoU (e.g., reliability diagrams), to justify g(u)=u.
- Fix citations and provide full experimental details (hyperparameters, validation protocol, seeds).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practical formulation for soft labels and weak supervision.
- Plug-and-play with standard solvers; easy to adopt.
- Demonstrated mAP gains when integrated with a strong detector.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty over existing soft/fuzzy/uncertain-label SVMs and latent SVMs is limited and insufficiently discussed.
- Incomplete theoretical and empirical isolation of the contribution; modest improvements on core tasks.
- Clarity and reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is appealingly simple and could be impactful with stronger positioning, analysis, and ablations that clearly establish novelty and isolate the gains due to e-SVM. As it stands, the work reads as an incremental re-packaging of known concepts with modest empirical benefits.
