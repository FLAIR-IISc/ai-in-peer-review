**Summary**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning classifiers from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, which the authors term "positiveness." The core idea is to model the binary label as a Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is the positiveness value. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this variable. This formulation effectively weights the positive and negative hinge loss terms for each sample. The paper extends this to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the positiveness is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available) by treating it as a latent variable and optimizing via an alternating procedure. The method is evaluated on segment classification and applied to semantic segmentation and object detection, where it is shown to provide a modest improvement when combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning from supervision that is weaker than pixel-perfect labels but stronger than image-level tags. The concept of "positiveness" is a natural way to frame this problem, particularly for tasks involving segment proposals.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed e-SVM formulation is intuitive and elegant. A key practical advantage is its equivalence to a weighted SVM problem, which allows it to be solved efficiently using standard, off-the-shelf SVM solvers.
3.  **Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their method by integrating it into a strong object detection pipeline (R-CNN) and achieving a non-trivial performance gain (1.5% mAP) on the competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is appealing, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological detail, and experimental evaluation, which collectively undermine the contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript demonstrates a significant lack of attention to detail, which severely impacts its clarity and professionalism.
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Many references are incomplete, inconsistently formatted, or contain extraneous commas (e.g., [1], [5], [6], [8]). Reference [?] is clearly a placeholder that was never filled. Typographical errors exist in the main text (e.g., "segmentic segmentation" in the abstract).
    *   **Missing Figures:** Figures 1, 2, and 3 are referenced in the text but are not included in the provided paper. Their absence makes it impossible to understand the proposed framework, the feature generation process, or the experimental results presented in them.
    *   **Vague Descriptions:** Key technical details are glossed over, hindering reproducibility. The creation of the "spatial confidence map feature" is insufficiently detailed, particularly the step involving "additive kernels approximation mapping." Similarly, the modification to the R-CNN bounding box regressor is mentioned with no actionable detail.
    *   **Inconsistent/Unclear Notation:** The regularizer is written as `λw · 1 wTw` in one equation and `λw · 1 wTw` in another. This notation is non-standard and confusing. Standard notation like `(λ/2) ||w||^2` would be far clearer.

2.  **Methodological Rigor:**
    *   **Optimization:** The paper proposes an alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) but provides no discussion of its convergence properties. While the subproblems are convex, the joint optimization is not. The claim that the algorithm runs "until Converge" is unsubstantiated without a defined criterion or any theoretical guarantees.
    *   **Modeling Assumptions:** The choice to set the Bernoulli probability `µi` directly to the positiveness `ui` (i.e., `g(ui) = ui`) is made "for simplicity" with no further justification. The implications of this linear mapping assumption are not explored.
    *   **Hyperparameter Analysis:** The model introduces crucial hyperparameters, `λw` and `λR`, but the paper provides no information on how these were set or tuned. A sensitivity analysis is necessary to understand the model's robustness to these parameters, especially `λR`, which controls the reliance on the initial `ubb` values.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Novel Metrics:** The authors introduce two new evaluation criteria, TAPC and NDCG, for the segment classification task. While new metrics can be valuable, their introduction here is not well-motivated. It is unclear why standard metrics for regression (e.g., Mean Squared Error) or ranking (e.g., Spearman's rank correlation) were not used or compared against. This makes it difficult to situate the performance of e-SVM relative to SVR in a standard context.
    *   **Baseline Comparisons:** The performance gain over SVR, the most relevant baseline, is described as "slightly better." This marginal improvement, combined with the lack of rigorous statistical testing, tempers the claimed significance of the e-SVM model.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The regularization term `R(u)` is introduced to prevent the latent `u` from collapsing to binary values. However, the experimental section does not include an ablation study showing the performance of the latent e-SVM without this regularizer, which would have empirically validated its necessity.

4.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:**
    *   The paper claims a key difference from weighted SVMs (WSVM) is that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points." This distinction feels more semantic than fundamental. The final objective can be interpreted as a form of instance weighting (e.g., duplicating each sample into a positive and negative instance with different weights). The connection and distinction to the extensive literature on WSVMs are not explored in sufficient depth.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and intuitive formulation for learning from continuous-valued labels.
*   The problem it addresses is highly relevant to modern computer vision, where fine-grained annotation is scarce.
*   The method shows a positive, albeit small, improvement on a very strong object detection baseline.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Severe presentation issues, including a deeply flawed bibliography and missing figures, suggest a hastily prepared manuscript not ready for publication.
*   Lack of crucial details for reproducibility.
*   Insufficient methodological analysis regarding optimization convergence and hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The experimental evaluation relies on unmotivated custom metrics, and the performance gains over the most relevant baseline are marginal.
*   The novelty relative to weighted SVMs is not convincingly established.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea of e-SVM is interesting, the paper in its current form falls well below the standard required for NIPS. The numerous and significant flaws in presentation, methodological detail, and experimental rigor overshadow the potential contribution. The work feels preliminary and would require a major revision to address the issues of clarity, reproducibility, and analytical depth before it could be considered for publication.
