- Paper proposes Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) and a latent variant to learn from continuous “positiveness” (e.g., IoU) for weak supervision, applied to segmentation and detection.

- Two variants: basic e-SVM for observed positiveness and latent e-SVM when positiveness is unobserved.

- Experiments on PASCAL VOC show improvements over SVC/SVR for segment proposal classification and modest gains for object detection (≈1.5% AP when combined with RCNN).

- Addresses a practical problem: reducing need for pixel-level labels by leveraging bounding-box annotations.

- Clear mathematical formulation and logical progression from basic to latent model, distinguishing from SVC/SVR.

- Reasonable experimental setup using standard datasets and baselines; introduces TAPC and NDCG evaluation metrics for segments.

- Demonstrates practical impact with a non-trivial AP improvement when augmenting RCNN features.

- Limited theoretical novelty: approach resembles weighted SVMs and lacks clear differentiation or theoretical advantages over prior work.

- Reported gains are modest relative to major detection advances and not well contextualized within recent literature.

- Insufficient experimental details: hyperparameter tuning, ablation of contributions (e.g., bounding-box regularization), and comparisons to other weakly supervised methods are missing.

- Some claims are overstated (e.g., "state-of-the-art") given marginal improvements and limited evidence that the method is easier to extend than alternatives.

- Limited theoretical justification for why expectation loss should outperform existing approaches; connection to weighted SVMs not deeply explored.

- Overall assessment: well-executed, practically relevant, and consistent improvements, but incremental and not sufficiently novel for NeurIPS.

- Recommendations to improve: compare to other weakly supervised methods, better contextualize results, provide deeper theoretical analysis, and include detailed experimental/hyperparameter studies.

- Recommendation: weak rejection — suitable for a more application-focused venue unless theoretical contribution or empirical gains are substantially strengthened.
