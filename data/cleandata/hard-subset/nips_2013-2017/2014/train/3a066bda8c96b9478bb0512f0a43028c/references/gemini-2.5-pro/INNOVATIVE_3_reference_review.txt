### NIPS Review

**Summary**
This paper introduces a novel extension to the Support Vector Machine framework, called the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to handle weakly supervised learning problems where labels are not binary but are continuous "positiveness" values within the [0, 1] interval. The core idea is to model the true binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being positive is given by the observed positiveness score (e.g., Intersection over Union). The loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. The paper further extends this to a latent e-SVM, where the positiveness scores themselves are treated as latent variables, initialized by weaker signals (e.g., IoU with a bounding box) and refined during training. The authors demonstrate the utility of this framework on two key computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection. Notably, they show that features derived from their model can be used to improve the performance of the then state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is the introduction of the e-SVM. It is a creative and elegant solution to a common problem in vision and other domains: how to train a classifier when the supervision signal is not a hard {+1, -1} label but a continuous score of "correctness." Instead of resorting to ad-hoc thresholding (to use SVC) or treating it as an unbounded regression problem (to use SVR), the authors propose a principled modification to the SVM objective itself. Framing the label as a random variable and optimizing the expected loss is a fundamentally new and interesting approach in this context.

2.  **Bold Extension to Latent Variables:** The latent e-SVM is a significant and bold conceptual step. It directly addresses a highly practical and challenging scenario: learning fine-grained segmentation models from coarse bounding box annotations. By treating the true IoU as a latent variable to be optimized, the model moves beyond simply using a noisy proxy label. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's structure and offers a novel direction for weakly supervised learning, distinct from more traditional Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) frameworks, whose limitations for this specific problem are correctly identified.

3.  **Demonstrated Practical Significance:** The paper doesn't just present a new model in isolation; it demonstrates its value by improving a very strong, state-of-the-art system (R-CNN). Achieving a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 over an already powerful baseline is a significant result. This shows that the proposed e-SVM is not just a theoretical curiosity but can capture complementary information (segmentation cues) that even deep feature hierarchies miss, leading to tangible performance gains on a difficult task.

4.  **Generality:** The proposed e-SVM framework is general. While applied to computer vision tasks using IoU as the positiveness score, the core idea could be readily applied to other domains where noisy, continuous, or probabilistic labels are available. This potential for broader impact is a key characteristic of a strong NIPS paper.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Evaluation Metrics for Ablation:** The paper introduces two new evaluation criteria, TAPC and NDCG, for the segment classification sub-task. While these metrics are sensible and well-motivated for the problem of ranking proposals, their novelty means it is difficult to calibrate the results against prior work. The connection between improvements on these metrics and the final downstream task performance (e.g., segmentation accuracy or detection AP) is not fully established, making the ablation study in Section 5.1 feel somewhat disconnected from the main application results.

2.  **Optimization of the Latent Model:** The alternating optimization scheme for the latent e-SVM is a standard heuristic for such problems. However, it is susceptible to local minima. While the authors astutely add a KL-divergence based regularization term to prevent trivial solutions, a more in-depth discussion of the optimization's properties, sensitivity to initialization, or an empirical analysis of the learned latent `u` values would have strengthened the paper's technical depth. This is a minor point, as the proposed method clearly works, but it leaves some theoretical questions open.

3.  **Simplicity of Feature Integration:** The method for integrating the e-SVM scores into the R-CNN pipeline (creating a spatial confidence map and concatenating features) is straightforward and effective. This is a strength in its simplicity, but it may also be suboptimal. The paper could be strengthened by briefly discussing or experimenting with alternative fusion strategies to better understand the interplay between the segmentation-based features and the CNN features.

**Recommendation**
Accept.

**Justification**
This paper presents a novel, bold, and conceptually interesting idea with the e-SVM and its latent extension. It tackles the important and practical problem of learning from continuous-valued weak supervision in a principled manner that is a clear departure from existing ad-hoc approaches. The core contribution lies in the novelty of the formulation itself, which opens up a new way to think about incorporating non-binary supervisory signals into max-margin classifiers.

While the experimental validation has some minor weaknesses, such as the use of non-standard intermediate metrics and a simple optimization scheme for the latent model, these are far outweighed by the strength and novelty of the core idea. As a reviewer who values new directions over incremental perfection, I find this work highly compelling. The fact that this novel concept leads to a significant performance improvement on a very strong and contemporary baseline (R-CNN) provides convincing evidence of its practical utility and significance. This paper has the potential to inspire follow-up work both in weakly supervised learning theory and in practical applications across various domains. It is a strong contribution that fits well within the scope of NIPS.
