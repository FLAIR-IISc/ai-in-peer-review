**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, termed Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training examples are associated with a continuous "positiveness" score between 0 and 1, rather than a binary label. The core idea is to treat the true binary label as an unobserved Bernoulli random variable, with its success probability given by the positiveness score. The loss function is then the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. This formulation naturally handles training data like object segment proposals, whose quality is measured by Intersection over Union (IoU). The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for cases where the positiveness score itself is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box supervision is available), treating the score as a latent variable to be optimized. The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection. For the latter, a "spatial confidence map feature" is proposed to integrate the e-SVM segment scores into window-based detectors like R-CNN, leading to improved performance on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper's primary strength is its elegant and principled formulation for a common problem in vision. Using an expected loss is a theoretically sound way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision into a classification framework, offering a more natural alternative to ad-hoc thresholding for SVC or using SVR, which is not designed for a bounded [0,1] target. The latent variable extension is a logical next step for handling weaker supervision.

However, the paper suffers from a significant number of quality issues, both major and minor, that undermine its contributions:
1.  **Technical Clarity and Notation:** The notation is frequently non-standard and confusing. For instance, the regularization term in Equation (1) is written as `λw · 1 wTw`, which is highly unconventional. It should likely be `(λ/2) ||w||^2` or similar. The paper redefines `w` and `xi` to absorb the bias term, which is standard, but this change is made abruptly.
2.  **Unsupported Assumptions:** The model assumes the probability of a positive label is linear in the positiveness score (`µi = ui`). This is a strong assumption made for simplicity with no justification or analysis of alternatives. The model's performance could be highly sensitive to this choice of `g(u)`.
3.  **Questionable Experimental Claims:** In Section 5.2, the semantic segmentation results (36.8% mIoU) are described as "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art" method of Carreira et al. [5]. However, the cited work reports a mIoU of 47.7% on the same PASCAL VOC 2011 dataset. A performance drop of over 10 percentage points is not "comparable"; it is substantially worse. This discrepancy is a major concern and is not addressed.
4.  **Confounding Factors:** In the object detection experiments (Section 5.3), the authors state they "modified the bounding box regularization method used in [14]". This introduces a confounding variable. Without an ablation study, it is impossible to determine how much of the reported 1.5% mAP improvement comes from the proposed e-SVM features versus their modification to the R-CNN baseline.
5.  **Lack of Detail:** The optimization procedure in Algorithm 1 specifies the stopping criterion as "until Converge," which is too vague for reproducibility. The experimental setup lacks crucial details that would allow for a complete understanding and replication of the results.

**Clarity:**
The overall clarity of the paper is poor. It is marred by numerous typographical errors ("continues value," "segmentic segmentation"), grammatical mistakes ("simple concatenating"), and formatting inconsistencies. The structure is logical, but the prose is often imprecise. The reference section is exceptionally problematic; it is riddled with formatting errors, incomplete entries (e.g., [12]), a missing reference denoted by `[?]`, and inconsistent styling. This lack of attention to detail severely detracts from the paper's professionalism and readability, making it difficult to trust the technical content.

**Originality:**
The core idea of e-SVM appears novel. While related to instance-weighted SVMs (WSVM), the authors correctly distinguish their approach as "weighting labels instead of data points," where each sample contributes to both positive and negative loss terms. This is a subtle but important distinction. The spatial confidence map feature proposed for object detection is also a novel contribution for integrating segment-based cues into region-based detectors. The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation metrics for this specific task is also a useful, if minor, contribution.

**Significance:**
The work addresses the important and practical problem of learning from weaker, non-binary supervision. The reported 1.5% mAP improvement on object detection over a very strong R-CNN baseline is a significant result and demonstrates the potential of the proposed method. If the methodological and clarity issues were resolved, the e-SVM could be a useful tool for researchers in computer vision and other domains where supervision is noisy or continuous. However, as presented, the paper's impact is severely limited by its flaws. The poor segmentation results, in particular, cast doubt on the general applicability of the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The e-SVM formulation is a novel, principled, and elegant solution for learning from continuous-valued supervision in the [0,1] range.
*   The application to object detection yields a significant performance improvement (1.5% mAP) over a state-of-the-art baseline on a standard benchmark.
*   The proposed spatial confidence map feature is an interesting method for bridging segment-based and window-based approaches.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper's presentation is extremely unprofessional, with pervasive typographical, grammatical, and formatting errors, especially in the bibliography.
*   The experimental results for semantic segmentation are presented in a potentially misleading way, claiming to be "comparable" to prior art while appearing substantially worse.
*   The object detection experiments contain a confounding variable (modified bounding box regression) that makes it impossible to isolate the contribution of the proposed method.
*   Key modeling assumptions (e.g., linearity of `g(u)`) are made without justification.
*   The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., vague convergence criteria).

**Decision:**
Despite the novelty of the core idea and the strong object detection results, I recommend **rejection**. The sheer volume of minor and major flaws—from the unprofessional presentation and formatting to the questionable experimental claims and confounding variables—makes the paper fall well below the standards expected for NIPS. The work feels preliminary and rushed. While the underlying ideas have merit, the paper requires a major revision to address the significant issues in its execution, evaluation, and presentation before it can be considered a solid scientific contribution.
