This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data, particularly in computer vision tasks where obtaining precise pixel-level annotations is expensive. The core idea is to treat segment proposals as having a "positiveness" score (representing the degree of correctness) and to learn a model that effectively incorporates this continuous, rather than binary, labeling information. The paper extends this to a latent variable setting where positiveness is unobserved, and demonstrates its effectiveness on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming standard SVC and SVR approaches.

The paper addresses a relevant and important problem: leveraging weakly supervised data to reduce annotation costs. The introduction clearly motivates the need for such algorithms and positions e-SVM within the broader context of related work. The proposed approach is well-motivated, extending SVMs to handle continuous label information, a common scenario in tasks involving proposals. The experimental results on PASCAL VOC datasets for both segmentation and detection are convincing, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of two new evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG) for segment proposal classification is a valuable contribution.

However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement exist.

**Quality:** While the theoretical formulation appears sound, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of the properties of e-SVM compared to standard SVMs. Specifically, understanding the impact of the positiveness weighting on the margin and generalization bounds would strengthen the technical contribution. The iterative optimization process for the latent variable case (Algorithm 1) is described, but convergence guarantees or analysis of its efficiency are not provided. The experiments demonstrate empirical improvements, but a more rigorous statistical significance analysis (e.g., using paired t-tests with p-values) would be beneficial.  The claim of achieving state-of-the-art object detection is somewhat overstated; the improvement is 1.5% AP *over* RCNN, which is already a well-established method. State-of-the-art is a moving target and a more precise phrasing would be appropriate.

**Clarity:** Although generally well-written, certain sections could benefit from further clarification. For instance, the explanation of the regularization term R(u) in the latent e-SVM model is somewhat terse. Providing more intuition behind its design and connection to the problem formulation would be helpful. The paper assumes familiarity with SVMs and related concepts, but some brief explanations or pointers to relevant resources could make it more accessible to a wider audience. Figure 1 is rather abstract and could be improved by showing a more concrete example of the algorithm's operation. The link between the "spatial confidence map feature" and the RCNN system is described adequately, but a visual illustration would greatly assist understanding.

**Originality:** While the idea of weighting samples based on their confidence is not entirely novel (see weighted SVMs), the specific application of this weighting to *labels* within the context of segment proposals is a contribution. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing weighted SVM approaches. A more thorough discussion of the connections and differences with existing weighted SVM literature (specifically citing [23, 27] as mentioned in the paper, but detailing *how* e-SVM differs) is needed.  The use of IoU as a measure of positiveness is standard; the novelty lies in how it’s incorporated into the loss function.

**Significance:** The e-SVM algorithm offers a practical solution for leveraging weakly supervised data, which is particularly valuable in computer vision. The demonstrated improvements in segmentation and object detection are significant, especially considering the simplicity of the approach. The algorithm’s generality, allowing it to be used with various segment proposal detectors, features, and base detectors, enhances its potential impact. However, the practical limitations (e.g., sensitivity to the choice of regularization parameters, computational cost of the iterative optimization) should be further investigated. The performance gains, while demonstrable, are not orders of magnitude improvements, and it remains to be seen how well this method scales to larger and more complex datasets.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in computer vision.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated algorithm (e-SVM).
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over baselines on standard datasets.
*   Presents two new evaluation criteria for segment proposal classification.
*   The algorithm appears to be generalizable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a deep theoretical analysis of the algorithm’s properties.
*   Insufficient comparison to related weighted SVM literature.
*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Statistical significance analysis is missing.
*   The claim of state-of-the-art results needs qualification.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is sound and addresses an important problem, the lack of thorough theoretical analysis, the limited novelty compared to existing weighted SVM approaches, and the missing statistical rigor prevent it from meeting the high standards of NIPS. However, the core idea is promising and the experimental results are encouraging. I believe that with substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this paper could be a valuable contribution to the field. Specifically, the authors should focus on providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for e-SVM, a more detailed comparison to related work, and a more thorough empirical evaluation.
