This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from data where labels are continuous "positiveness" scores within a bounded interval [0, 1]. This setting is common in computer vision, where, for instance, a segment proposal's quality is measured by its Intersection over Union (IoU) with a ground truth object. The core idea is to model the binary label of a training example as a Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is its known positiveness score. The SVM objective then minimizes the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. This elegantly results in a loss function where each sample contributes to both the positive and negative margin constraints, weighted by its positiveness `u_i` and `1-u_i` respectively.

The paper further extends this model to a weakly supervised setting (latent e-SVM) where the true positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this formulation, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized alternately with the SVM model parameters. A KL-divergence-based regularizer is introduced to constrain the latent scores to be close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box). The method is positioned as an alternative to standard Support Vector Classification (SVC), which requires ad-hoc thresholding of positiveness scores, and Support Vector Regression (SVR), which treats the problem as standard regression without considering the bounded nature of the labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection, notably showing a significant 1.5% mAP improvement on top of the strong R-CNN baseline for object detection on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Elegance of Formulation:** The proposed e-SVM is a novel and principled way to handle continuous-valued labels in a classification framework. Framing the hinge loss as an expectation is an elegant idea that directly addresses the ambiguity of samples that are neither fully positive nor fully negative. This provides a well-founded alternative to the common heuristics of thresholding (for SVC) or direct regression (for SVR).

2.  **Significance and Strong Empirical Results:** The practical utility of the method is convincingly demonstrated by its application to object detection. Improving a state-of-the-art method like R-CNN is non-trivial, and the reported 1.5% average AP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a significant result. This shows that the segmentation cues learned by the latent e-SVM provide valuable complementary information, making the approach likely to be of interest to practitioners and researchers in the field.

3.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The authors introduce two new evaluation metrics for the intermediate task of segment proposal classification: Threshold Average Precision Curve (TAPC) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG). This is a valuable contribution in its own right, as it provides a more direct and nuanced way to evaluate models on this specific problem, moving beyond task-specific downstream metrics. The experiments show that e-SVM consistently outperforms baselines on these direct metrics.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core concepts of e-SVM and the latent extension are explained intuitively and mathematically. The motivation for the problem and the distinction from related work like Multiple Instance Learning and weighted SVMs are well articulated.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions:**

1.  **Experimental Comparison with Weighted SVMs:** While the paper discusses the conceptual difference between e-SVM and weighted SVMs (WSVMs) in Section 2, a quantitative comparison is missing from the experiments. The e-SVM loss function `u_i * l_i^+ + (1 - u_i) * l_i^-` bears a strong resemblance to instance-weighting schemes. A strong baseline would be a standard SVC where samples with `u_i > 0.5` are labeled positive and those with `u_i < 0.5` are labeled negative, with each sample `i` weighted by a factor proportional to `|u_i - 0.5|`. Including such a baseline would more concretely establish the superiority of the proposed formulation over simpler weighting schemes.

2.  **Details on Latent Variable Optimization:** The optimization for the latent variables `u` in Algorithm 1 (line 4) is described as `argmin_u L(w(new), u)`, but the paper does not provide the explicit solution or the method used to solve this subproblem. Given the KL-divergence regularizer, it is not immediately obvious if a closed-form solution exists. Clarifying how this minimization is performed (e.g., gradient descent, closed-form update rule) would be crucial for reproducibility and a complete understanding of the algorithm.

3.  **Discrepancy in Segmentation Results:** The experiments in Section 5.1 show e-SVM clearly outperforming SVR on the direct TAPC and NDCG metrics. However, the downstream semantic segmentation result (Section 5.2) is described as merely "comparable" to the SVR-based state-of-the-art. This raises the question of why the superior performance on the intermediate task does not translate to a clear win on the final task. A brief discussion on this would be insightful. Perhaps the final segmentation performance is limited by other factors in the pipeline, such as the proposal generation or the final pasting scheme.

4.  **Ablation Studies:** The latent e-SVM introduces a key hyperparameter, `λR`, for the regularization term. The paper would be strengthened by a brief analysis of the model's sensitivity to this parameter. Furthermore, providing information on the convergence of Algorithm 1 (e.g., typical number of iterations required) would be helpful for practitioners looking to implement the method.

**Summary and Decision:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a clear and novel contribution. The e-SVM formulation is elegant and well-motivated for an important class of weakly supervised learning problems. The experimental results, especially the significant improvement on a strong object detection baseline, demonstrate the effectiveness and impact of the proposed method. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mainly concern the need for additional experimental comparisons and implementation details that would further strengthen the paper. They do not undermine the core contribution. The work is technically sound, original, and significant. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
