### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary**
This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), for learning from weakly supervised data. The key idea is to model the label of a training example as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability corresponds to the "positiveness" of the sample (a continuous value in [0, 1], such as an IoU score). The objective function then becomes the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss. This formulation naturally handles fully supervised scenarios where positiveness is observed, and extends to weakly supervised settings (e.g., using bounding box annotations) by treating the positiveness values as latent variables optimized via an alternating minimization scheme. The authors apply this method to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating that it outperforms Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) on a segment proposal classification task, and provides a modest improvement when integrated with the R-CNN object detector on PASCAL VOC 2007.

**Strengths**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and challenging problem of learning from weak supervision, which is crucial for scaling computer vision models to large datasets where dense, pixel-level annotation is infeasible.
2.  **Novel Formulation:** The core concept of the expectation loss is an elegant and principled way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision (i.e., "positiveness") into a max-margin framework. The probabilistic interpretation is intuitive.
3.  **Practicality:** A significant advantage of the proposed method is its compatibility with standard linear SVM solvers, which lowers the barrier to implementation and adoption.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The authors validate their approach on multiple tasks and against appropriate baselines (SVC, SVR). The introduction of new evaluation criteria (TAPC, NDCG) for the segment classification task is a thoughtful contribution to properly assess model performance on this non-standard problem. The improvement over the very strong R-CNN baseline in object detection, while modest, demonstrates the utility of the proposed features.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments**
Despite its promising direction, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor, yet collectively significant, flaws in its technical presentation, clarity, and experimental reporting.

**1. Quality and Technical Soundness:**
*   **Notation in Objective Function:** The objective function in Equation 1 is presented in a non-standard manner. The regularization term `λw · 1 wTw` is unclear; the standard form is typically `(λ/2) ||w||^2`. Furthermore, the paper states that the weight vector `w` is redefined to absorb the bias term `b`. If so, the regularization term `w^T w` is incorrect, as it would penalize the bias term, which is not standard practice. This is a critical technical oversight.
*   **Distinction from Weighted SVMs:** The paper's attempt to distinguish e-SVM from weighted SVMs (WSVM) in Section 2 is not entirely convincing. The claim that e-SVM "weights labels instead of data points" is semantically vague. The e-SVM loss, `Σ [g(u_i)l_i^+ + (1-g(u_i))l_i^-]`, is structurally very similar to instance-weighted or cost-sensitive SVMs where different misclassification costs are assigned to positive and negative classes for each sample. A more rigorous discussion and formal comparison to existing WSVM literature is needed to clearly demarcate the novelty of the contribution.
*   **Latent Model Optimization:** While Algorithm 1 outlines a reasonable alternating optimization scheme, the paper lacks any discussion of its convergence properties. A statement regarding convergence to a local minimum would strengthen the technical argument. Moreover, the impact of the regularization hyperparameter `λR` is not analyzed. How is this parameter set? How sensitive is the model's performance to its value? This is a crucial detail for reproducibility.
*   **Unexplained Experimental Details:** In Section 5.3, the authors mention a modification to the bounding box regression method of R-CNN. This modification is not explained in sufficient detail, nor is it clear if this same modification was applied to the baseline R-CNN for a fair comparison. This ambiguity undermines the central claim of improving the state of the art in object detection.

**2. Clarity and Presentation:**
*   **Writing and Formatting:** The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "continues value", "segmentic segmentation", "algorithm call e-SVM", "size of datesets").
*   **Missing Figures:** The manuscript repeatedly refers to Figures 1, 2, and 3, none of which are provided. This makes it impossible to fully comprehend the proposed framework (Fig. 1), the feature generation process (Fig. 2), and the experimental results (Fig. 3). This is a major deficit.
*   **Incomplete Citations:** The reference list is poorly formatted and contains a placeholder citation `[?]`. Several entries are incomplete (e.g., [6], [8], [9]). The lack of a consistent citation style (e.g., some include author lists, others do not) reflects a lack of diligence.
*   **Vague Result Reporting:** In Section 5.2, the semantic segmentation results are described as "comparable to the results of the state-of-the-art". This is insufficient for a scientific paper. The specific baseline numbers from the cited work [5] should be provided for a direct and transparent comparison.

**3. Originality:**
*   **Evaluation Criteria:** The authors propose TAPC and NDCG as new evaluation criteria. While useful for their specific task, the claim that "we have not found any work on this sort of direct performance evaluation" may be too strong without a more comprehensive review of evaluation methods for regression or ranking on bounded intervals.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the expectation loss formulation is elegant, its functional similarity to heavily studied weighted/cost-sensitive SVMs tempers its originality. The primary novelty lies in its specific application and extension to the latent variable setting for weakly supervised vision tasks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a very important problem in machine learning and computer vision.
*   The core e-SVM formulation is intuitive, well-motivated, and shows empirical promise.
*   The method is practical and can be readily implemented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is undermined by a high number of presentation flaws, including missing figures, incomplete citations, and grammatical errors, which detract from its professionalism and readability.
*   Key technical details are either incorrect (the regularizer with an absorbed bias term) or missing (analysis of hyperparameters and convergence).
*   The experimental reporting lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier conference (ambiguity in object detection comparison, vague reporting in segmentation).
*   The claims of novelty relative to weighted SVMs are not sufficiently substantiated.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of this paper is interesting and potentially valuable, the manuscript in its current form falls short of the high standards for publication at NIPS. The numerous flaws in technical presentation, clarity, and experimental rigor collectively cast doubt on the work's reliability and completeness. The paper requires a major revision to address these issues before it can be reconsidered. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript, paying close attention to technical correctness, clarity of presentation, and the transparency of their experimental methodology.
