

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm called Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) that addresses a critical gap in weakly supervised learning for computer vision. The core insight is that many vision problems involve "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels, and existing methods like SVC and SVR fail to properly handle this continuous-valued supervision. The authors propose e-SVM, which treats the label as a random variable whose success probability is a function of the observed positiveness. This approach elegantly extends to weak supervision by treating positiveness as latent variables, which the authors optimize using an iterative procedure.

The paper's main contribution lies in this conceptual shift from binary classification to probabilistic label modeling with continuous supervision. This is distinct from related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and weighted SVMs (WSVMs), as it doesn't assume "all positive" or "all negative" instances within bags (as MIL does), and it weights labels rather than data points (as WSVMs do). The authors demonstrate the approach on two important computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation (using pixel-level and bounding-box annotations) and object detection (using bounding-box annotations only), showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Concept:** The expectation loss SVM framework is genuinely new and addresses a well-identified gap in weakly supervised learning. The problem of handling partially correct segment proposals (0 < IoU < 1) is not adequately addressed by existing methods, and the authors' solution is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper effectively motivates why existing approaches (SVC with thresholding, SVR) are suboptimal for this specific problem setting. The mathematical formulation of the expectation loss is elegant and directly addresses the core issue.

3. **Practical Impact:** The approach is immediately applicable to real-world computer vision problems where pixel-level annotations are expensive. The authors demonstrate its utility on standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC) for two major tasks, showing consistent improvements.

4. **General Framework:** The paper emphasizes that the approach is general and can work with any segment proposal detectors, image features, and classifiers, making it broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Modest Experimental Gains:** The improvements over baselines are modest (1.5% AP gain in object detection), which might not be sufficient to excite reviewers who expect dramatic results. The paper could have included more comprehensive comparisons to recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond SVC and SVR.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the algorithm is well-motivated, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the latent e-SVM optimization procedure, which is important for a theoretical contribution.

3. **Narrow Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to PASCAL VOC datasets. More comprehensive experiments on larger datasets or with different vision tasks would strengthen the case for broader applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1. The paper introduces a genuinely novel concept (expectation loss SVM) that addresses a real and significant problem in computer vision - the gap between binary classification and continuous supervision.

2. The approach is conceptually simple yet powerful, and it bridges a gap that existing methods don't address well.

3. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides sufficient technical detail to reproduce the results.

4. The novelty of the core idea outweighs the modest experimental gains and limited theoretical analysis. The framework represents a new direction for weakly supervised learning that could inspire future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The experimental improvements are modest and may not be sufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently compare against more recent weakly supervised learning methods that have emerged since the cited works.

3. The theoretical analysis is limited, which is a drawback for a conference that values theoretical contributions.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. While the experimental gains are modest, the paper's core contribution is genuinely novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The expectation loss framework represents a bold new approach to weakly supervised learning that could influence future work in computer vision. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field, even if the experimental results don't represent a breakthrough in performance. The authors have identified a problem that's not well-served by existing methods and provided a clean, general solution that could be applied to many other domains beyond computer vision.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a framework that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The modest experimental improvements are acceptable given the novelty of the approach, and the authors have clearly demonstrated the framework's applicability to important computer vision tasks. This paper represents a valuable new direction for weakly supervised learning that deserves to be shared with the NIPS community.
