This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from data where supervision is provided as a continuous "positiveness" score (e.g., an IoU value between 0 and 1) rather than a binary label. The core idea is to treat the true binary label as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is a function of the known positiveness score. The objective then becomes minimizing the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this random variable. This elegant formulation naturally extends to a weakly supervised setting (the latent e-SVM) where the positiveness score itself is unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available) by treating it as a latent variable to be optimized jointly with the model weights. The authors demonstrate the utility of their method on semantic segmentation and, most notably, object detection, where they use the e-SVM to generate a novel "spatial confidence map feature" that provides a significant performance boost when combined with the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. The e-SVM formulation is a genuinely new and clever idea. Instead of forcing the problem into existing paradigms through ad-hoc thresholding (for SVC) or using a potentially mismatched objective (like SVR), the authors propose a principled framework derived from first principles. Re-casting the label as a random variable and optimizing the expected loss is an elegant solution that directly models the continuous nature of the supervision. This represents a significant conceptual advance for learning from this type of weak supervision.

2.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The paper demonstrates that this novel idea is not merely a theoretical curiosity. The application to object detection is particularly compelling. At a time when R-CNN represented a massive leap in performance, showing a further 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is a non-trivial achievement. This result is a powerful proof of concept, showing that segmentation-based cues learned via the latent e-SVM can capture complementary information to even very strong deep learning features. This demonstrates the work's potential to have a real impact on challenging, mainstream computer vision tasks.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Bold Approach:** The framework is general. It addresses a fundamental problem that exists between classification and regression and is applicable wherever supervision signals are continuous and bounded. The extension to the latent variable model for bounding-box-level supervision is a bold and important step, tackling a very common and practical scenario in large-scale computer vision where pixel-perfect labels are unavailable.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, and the core concepts of the e-SVM and its latent variant are explained clearly. The connections to and distinctions from related work like Multiple Instance Learning and weighted SVMs are thoughtfully discussed, helping to situate the contribution within the broader literature.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Analysis of Model Components:** The paper makes several simplifying assumptions that could benefit from further analysis. For instance, the mapping from positiveness `u_i` to the Bernoulli probability `µ_i` is simply set as an identity function (`g(u_i) = u_i`). While this is a reasonable starting point, it is not clear if it is optimal. A brief discussion or experiment exploring other monotonic functions could strengthen the paper. Similarly, the design of the regularization term `R(u)` in the latent model, while sensible, is one of many possibilities.

2.  **Optimization:** The alternating minimization scheme for the latent e-SVM is a practical but heuristic approach that is not guaranteed to find a global optimum. The authors rightly note that without regularization, the model can get stuck. While this is a common issue in latent variable models and does not invalidate the approach, the paper would be stronger with a more in-depth discussion of the optimization's properties or potential pitfalls.

3.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The authors introduce two new evaluation metrics for segment classification (TAPC and NDCG). While their motivation is clear and the metrics seem reasonable, their novelty makes it somewhat difficult to compare the reported performance against prior work that has not used them. This is a minor point, as the downstream task improvements are the most convincing evidence, but it's worth noting.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and elegant solution to an important problem in weakly supervised learning. The conceptual contribution of the e-SVM formulation is significant and offers a new way to think about learning from non-binary labels. While there are minor methodological details that could be explored more deeply, these are far outweighed by the strength and novelty of the core idea and its impressive validation on a highly competitive object detection benchmark. This work is not an incremental improvement; it introduces a new direction and a valuable tool for the community. Papers like this, which prioritize a significant new concept and demonstrate its power, are exactly what the NIPS community should encourage.
