This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data where the “ground truth” isn’t a hard label, but a continuous “positiveness” value representing the degree to which a proposal aligns with the true label. The core idea is to treat this positiveness as an expected value in the SVM loss function. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, comparing favorably to SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper tackles a relevant and important problem: leveraging weakly supervised data, especially given the cost of full annotation. The motivation is well-stated. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the proposed method appears technically sound, the claims of novelty and significance are overstated, and the experimental validation feels insufficient to justify strong endorsement.

The core idea of weighting samples based on their “positiveness” isn’t entirely new. Weighted SVMs (WSVMs), as the authors acknowledge in section 2, have been around for a while, employed for various purposes including handling imbalanced data and incorporating privileged information. The authors argue that e-SVM differs by weighting *labels* rather than data points, but the practical effect seems limited; the optimization process remains fundamentally similar. The connection to existing WSVM literature isn’t explored deeply enough – a more thorough discussion of how e-SVM builds upon or diverges from these methods would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive across tasks, lacks sufficient rigor. The comparisons to SVC and SVR are somewhat predictable. SVR, as the authors themselves point out, isn’t ideally suited for a bounded output, and SVC requires ad-hoc binarization. Of course, e-SVM will then perform better; it is designed for this specific problem. More compelling would be a comparison against *state-of-the-art* methods specifically designed for weakly supervised learning or continuous label prediction. For example, in object detection, showing improvement over recent weakly supervised object detection approaches would be more impactful than simply improving upon RCNN with a feature concatenation trick. The 1.5% AP improvement, while positive, feels marginal given the complexity added.

The formulation of the latent e-SVM, while mathematically sound, introduces a regularization term that relies on an initial estimate (ubbi) derived from bounding box IoU. The sensitivity of the results to this initialization isn't thoroughly investigated. The justification for the chosen regularization term (based on KL divergence) feels somewhat arbitrary; other regularization schemes might be equally or more effective.

The presentation is generally clear, but there are areas for improvement. The notation could be more consistent. The discussion of algorithm 1 lacks detail – how are convergence criteria defined? Are there practical issues with iterative optimization? The use of figures could be improved – Figure 1, while illustrating the framework, is fairly abstract and doesn’t convey much concrete information.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in computer vision.
*   Technically sound formulation and optimization procedure.
*   Demonstrates consistent performance gains over basic baseline methods (SVC, SVR).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea builds heavily on existing weighted SVM techniques.
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.
*   Marginal performance improvements in object detection.
*   Sensitivity to initialization in the latent e-SVM formulation needs further investigation.
*   Lack of thorough ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components.

**Overall:**

I currently lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents a technically valid approach, it doesn’t demonstrate sufficient novelty or significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The experiments, while extensive, lack the comparative depth needed to convince me that e-SVM represents a substantial advance over existing methods. I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above—specifically, a more thorough comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods and a more in-depth analysis of the regularization scheme—and resubmit in the future. As it stands, the paper feels like a solid engineering effort, but not a groundbreaking scientific contribution.
