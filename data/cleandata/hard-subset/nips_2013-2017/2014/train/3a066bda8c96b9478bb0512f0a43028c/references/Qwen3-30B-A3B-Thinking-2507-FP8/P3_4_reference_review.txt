

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU scores between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors formulate the problem by treating the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the observed positiveness. The e-SVM model handles both strong supervision (when positiveness is observed) and weak supervision (when positiveness is latent, treated as a hidden variable). The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper relates to recent work in weakly supervised learning, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), but argues that their problem setting differs from MIL since most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels. It also connects to weighted SVMs, though the authors clarify that e-SVM weights labels rather than data points.

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated problem**: The paper correctly identifies the practical challenge of expensive pixel-level annotations in computer vision, making weak supervision highly relevant. The problem setting (continuous positiveness in [0,1]) is well-defined and common in segmentation tasks.

2. **Novel formulation**: The expectation loss approach provides a principled way to handle continuous labels without arbitrary thresholding (unlike SVC) or regression (unlike SVR). This is a clean theoretical extension of standard SVMs.

3. **Strong experimental results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) on PASCAL VOC 2011. The 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is meaningful in the field.

4. **Clear evaluation metrics**: The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as direct evaluation criteria for segment classification is valuable and addresses a gap in the literature.

5. **Practical implementation**: The method can leverage standard SVM solvers (like LibLinear), making it easy to implement and integrate with existing pipelines.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient theoretical analysis**: The paper lacks convergence guarantees for the latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1). The authors state that the problem is convex when w or u is fixed, but don't prove convergence of the alternating optimization. This is particularly important for the weakly supervised setting.

2. **Inadequate comparison to related work**: The paper mentions MIL as a related approach but doesn't thoroughly explain why e-SVM is superior to MIL for their specific problem setting. The authors claim MIL "cannot be applied" because "most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels," but this isn't substantiated with examples or analysis. A more thorough comparison with recent weakly supervised methods (e.g., [19, 22]) would strengthen the paper.

3. **Ambiguous regularization choice**: The paper uses KL divergence as a regularization term for the latent positiveness (Equation 3), but doesn't justify why this is better than other convex regularizers (e.g., L2 or L1). The authors note it's "strong" but don't explore the sensitivity of results to this choice.

4. **Limited scope of experiments**: The method is applied only to two computer vision tasks. While the paper claims generality, it doesn't demonstrate applications to other domains where continuous labels might exist (e.g., medical imaging, time-series analysis).

5. **Implementation details missing**: The paper doesn't provide details about computational complexity or training time comparisons between e-SVM and baselines. The 1.5% AP gain is significant, but it's unclear whether this comes with a substantial computational overhead.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to weakly supervised learning that addresses a genuine problem in computer vision. The e-SVM formulation is theoretically sound and the experimental results are convincing. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more rigorous theoretical analysis, a deeper comparison with relevant weakly supervised learning methods, and additional ablation studies on the regularization choice.

The quality is high, with solid experiments that support the claims. The clarity is good, though some technical details could be elaborated. The originality is moderate - the core idea is novel for the specific problem setting, but the approach builds on existing SVM and weakly supervised learning concepts. The significance is high, as weakly supervised learning is a critical research direction with practical impact.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to weakly supervised learning with clear experimental validation. To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Add theoretical analysis of the convergence of Algorithm 1
2. Expand the related work section to better distinguish e-SVM from MIL and other weakly supervised methods
3. Provide more justification for the KL divergence regularization term
4. Include computational complexity comparisons with baselines

These revisions would address the main weaknesses while preserving the paper's strong points. The work is relevant to NIPS, as it advances the theory and practice of learning from weakly labeled data, a topic of growing importance in machine learning.
