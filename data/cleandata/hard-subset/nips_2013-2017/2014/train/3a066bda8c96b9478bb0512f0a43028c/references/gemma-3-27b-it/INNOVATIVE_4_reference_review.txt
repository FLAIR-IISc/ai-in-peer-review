This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach to learning from weakly supervised data, specifically targeting scenarios where proposals for the true label are available with associated “positiveness” scores. The core idea – weighting samples by their estimated correctness – is conceptually simple yet potentially impactful, especially given the prevalence of weakly labeled data in computer vision. The authors successfully extend this to a latent variable setting, handling scenarios like bounding box annotations where positiveness isn't directly observed. The empirical evaluation on semantic segmentation and object detection, particularly achieving a 1.5% average improvement on PASCAL VOC with R-CNN, is encouraging.

The strengths of this work lie in its novelty and elegance. The formulation addresses a practical problem – leveraging imperfect proposals – in a mathematically sound way. Extending the SVM framework to incorporate this “expectation” of correctness is a non-trivial and well-executed contribution. The applicability to diverse tasks (segmentation, detection) further highlights the method's generalizability. The authors appropriately contrast e-SVM with existing methods like SVC and SVR, demonstrating its advantages. The inclusion of a regularization term in the latent variable formulation shows careful consideration of potential issues like collapsing solutions. The ability to use any segment proposal detector, features, and classifier makes it a flexible framework.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the experimental results are positive, the improvements, particularly the 1.5% gain in object detection, feel incremental. The analysis of *why* e-SVM works better than SVR and SVC is somewhat limited.  A deeper dive into the learned features and the impact of the positiveness weighting would be beneficial. The regularization parameter in the latent e-SVM is not explored extensively. While the algorithm is stated to be efficiently solvable via standard SVM solvers, the computational cost (especially with large numbers of proposals) isn't explicitly addressed.  The paper relies heavily on CPMC for segment proposals; exploring other proposal generation methods more thoroughly could strengthen the claims of generalizability. The introduction frames the problem in terms of "weakly supervised learning" but then mostly focuses on scenarios where some form of proposal positiveness is known (either observed or estimated with IoU).  A more direct comparison to other weakly supervised learning paradigms, such as MIL, would be helpful for contextualizing the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is genuinely novel and addresses a practical challenge in weakly supervised learning.
*   **Soundness:** The mathematical framework appears solid, building upon established SVM principles.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results, while incremental, demonstrate that e-SVM consistently outperforms baselines on multiple tasks.
*   **Generality:** The framework’s ability to incorporate various segment proposal methods, features, and classifiers broadens its applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** The performance gains aren't substantial enough to represent a breakthrough.
*   **Limited Analysis:**  The paper lacks a deep understanding of *why* e-SVM works better, particularly the influence of the positiveness weighting.
*   **Dependence on CPMC:** The reliance on CPMC proposals might limit the generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor flaws, I lean towards **acceptance**. The e-SVM represents a conceptually novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem. The consistent, albeit incremental, improvements in performance, coupled with the method’s generality, make it a worthwhile contribution. I would encourage the authors to address the analysis weaknesses in a future version, perhaps by exploring the learned features and the sensitivity to the regularization parameter. The paper introduces a new direction and framework that, while not immediately transformative, has the potential to inspire further research in weakly supervised learning. It's a good paper that prioritizes a new idea over achieving state-of-the-art results on benchmark datasets.
