This paper presents e-SVM, a novel approach to learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing scenarios where only partial label correctness (a “positiveness” value) is available rather than binary labels. The core idea – extending SVMs to directly handle this continuous label information via an “expectation loss” – is a clever and potentially impactful one. It's well-motivated by the challenges of large-scale data annotation and offers a compelling alternative to binarization heuristics or direct regression. The authors demonstrate empirical success on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, improving upon existing methods like SVC and SVR.

The key strength of this work lies in its *novelty*. While weighted SVMs exist, framing the problem as weighting *labels* rather than data points, and the introduction of the expectation loss, feels genuinely new and offers a principled way to handle this type of weak supervision. The latent variable extension for truly weakly labeled data (bounding boxes only) is also a significant contribution, and the iterative optimization procedure seems reasonable. The application to object detection via spatial confidence maps is a particularly interesting and promising direction. The authors acknowledge limitations and connections to existing work, and the writing is generally clear.

However, there are weaknesses. While the experimental results are positive, the improvements over strong baselines (SVR, especially) are sometimes modest, and the presentation of the results could be clearer – specifically, the benefit of e-SVM over SVR feels more pronounced in the supplemental material than in the main text. The regularization term in the latent e-SVM is somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further justification or exploration of alternatives. The ablation studies, while present, could be more extensive – for instance, a more detailed analysis of the impact of different image features or segment proposal methods.  The paper could also benefit from discussing the computational cost of the iterative optimization in the latent variable setting, and how it scales with the number of proposals. The discussion of related work, while present, could be expanded. For example, recent work on contrastive learning for weakly supervised segmentation might be relevant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The expectation loss SVM is a fresh perspective on learning with partial labels, and the latent variable extension addresses a practical challenge in weak supervision.
*   **Potential Impact:**  The approach is broadly applicable and could be beneficial in many scenarios where acquiring full annotations is expensive. The successful application to object detection suggests real-world relevance.
*   **Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and the optimization procedure is well-defined.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The paper demonstrates improvements over baseline methods on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Improvements:** The gains over existing methods are not always dramatic.
*   **Limited Ablation:** Further investigation into the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters and components would strengthen the results.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of the latent variable learning is not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws and the relatively modest performance gains in certain experiments, I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its weaknesses. The paper introduces a conceptually strong and technically sound algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in machine learning. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation and recommend **acceptance**.  The core idea is strong enough to warrant further investigation by the research community, and I anticipate that future work building upon this foundation will yield more substantial improvements. I would encourage the authors to address the points raised above in a revised version of the paper, particularly regarding the regularization term and a more thorough analysis of computational complexity.
