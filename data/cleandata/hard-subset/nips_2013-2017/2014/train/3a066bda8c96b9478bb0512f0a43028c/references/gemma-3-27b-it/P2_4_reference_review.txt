## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM-based approach (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically focusing on scenarios where only partial or noisy labels (represented by a “positiveness” score) are available. The method extends standard SVM formulations and demonstrates improvements over existing techniques (SVC and SVR) on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. While the technical contribution is sound and the experiments are reasonably thorough, the paper lacks significant novelty and impact to warrant a strong acceptance. I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions, contingent on addressing the points detailed below.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the important problem of learning with weak supervision, motivated by the high cost of fully annotated datasets.  e-SVM introduces a loss function that explicitly models the “expectation” over potential labels, weighted by their estimated correctness (“positiveness”).  The authors correctly position their work within the context of weakly supervised learning, distinguishing it from Multiple Instance Learning and relating it to weighted SVM approaches. The connection to existing work like SVR and SVC is also clearly articulated.  The paper effectively identifies a gap in handling continuous, bounded label estimates within the standard SVM framework.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The proposed e-SVM formulation appears mathematically well-defined, and the extension to the latent variable setting (for fully weakly supervised scenarios) is logically presented.  The iterative optimization algorithm described is standard and leverages existing SVM solvers.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on PASCAL VOC demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods (SVC, SVR) in both the segment proposal classification task and downstream applications (semantic segmentation, object detection).  The results are statistically supported by the TAPC and NDCG metrics, and the average precision improvements in object detection are noteworthy.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the flexibility of e-SVM, highlighting its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors, feature representations, and base classifiers. This generality is a valuable aspect of the contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details and experimental setup.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the e-SVM formulation is a reasonable extension to standard SVMs, the core idea of weighting samples based on their estimated label quality isn’t entirely novel. Weighted SVMs have been explored extensively in the machine learning literature (as acknowledged by the authors). The specific weighting scheme (using the positiveness score directly) feels somewhat intuitive and lacks a strong theoretical justification.
* **Modest Significance:** The performance improvements, while statistically significant, are relatively modest (e.g., 1.5% average AP improvement in object detection).  This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method.  A more substantial gain would be needed to justify the complexity of the approach.
* **Evaluation Limitations:** The experiments are primarily conducted on the PASCAL VOC dataset. Expanding the evaluation to other datasets (e.g., ImageNet, COCO) would strengthen the generalizability claims.  Furthermore, comparing against more recent and sophisticated weakly supervised learning methods (beyond SVC and SVR) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **KL Divergence Regularization:** The choice of KL divergence as a regularization term in the latent variable setting seems somewhat arbitrary. A stronger justification for this specific regularization strategy would be beneficial.  The sensitivity of the results to the regularization parameter isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Spatial Confidence Map Feature:** The concatenation of spatial confidence map features with CNN features in the object detection task seems simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated fusion techniques could potentially yield better results.

**4. Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (3/5):** Technically sound and well-supported by experiments, but lacks a truly novel theoretical contribution. The incremental nature of the work lowers the quality score.
* **Clarity (4/5):**  Well-written and organized, facilitating understanding of the methodology and results.
* **Originality (2/5):**  The core idea of weighted SVMs is not new. While the application to this specific weakly supervised learning setting is relevant, the contribution is more of a practical adaptation than a breakthrough.
* **Significance (3/5):**  The results are positive and demonstrate practical improvement, but the gains are modest and the reliance on a single dataset limits the broader impact.



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in computer vision.
* Technically sound and well-motivated.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over baseline methods.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited novelty compared to existing work on weighted SVMs.
* Modest performance gains.
* Evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* Lacks strong theoretical justification for key design choices.



**6. Recommendations & Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Expand Related Work:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of recent advances in weakly supervised learning, particularly methods that utilize confidence estimates or uncertainty modeling.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  Explore the theoretical properties of e-SVM. Can you provide guarantees on convergence or generalization error?
* **Broader Evaluation:**  Conduct experiments on more diverse datasets (e.g., ImageNet, COCO) to assess the generalizability of the method.
* **Stronger Baselines:**  Compare against more recent and competitive weakly supervised learning approaches.
* **Regularization Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the regularization parameter in the latent variable setting.  Consider alternative regularization strategies.
* **Feature Fusion:** Explore more sophisticated techniques for fusing the spatial confidence map features with CNN features.




In conclusion, while the paper presents a well-executed and technically sound contribution, the limited novelty and modest impact prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above through major revisions would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance.
