

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper introduces an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data has only partial labels (e.g., bounding boxes rather than pixel-level annotations). The core innovation lies in treating the "positiveness" of segment proposals (a continuous value between 0 and 1 representing how well a proposal matches the ground truth) as a random variable and formulating a loss function that takes the expectation over this variable. The paper demonstrates applications in semantic segmentation and object detection, showing consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual novelty. While weakly supervised learning is a well-studied problem, the authors present a clean, principled approach that avoids the heuristic thresholding required by SVC (which converts continuous positiveness into binary labels) and the non-standard regression problem of SVR (which treats bounded [0,1] values as continuous targets). The e-SVM framework naturally handles continuous label information without ad-hoc transformations, which is a significant conceptual advance. The extension to latent variables for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved) is elegant and well-motivated by the practical reality that bounding box annotations are more readily available than pixel-level labels.

The paper's significance is enhanced by its clear demonstration of the approach's versatility. The authors successfully apply e-SVM to two fundamental computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation (where they achieve competitive results with both pixel-level and bounding box annotations) and object detection (where they integrate their spatial confidence map features with RCNN, achieving a 1.5% average AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007). The proposed spatial confidence map feature for object detection is particularly clever, addressing the challenge of incorporating segment-based confidence scores into window-based detection systems.

The technical quality is strong, with proper mathematical formulation of the expectation loss and a clear optimization algorithm for the latent case. The experimental design is appropriate, using standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC) and relevant baselines (SVC and SVR). The authors also introduce two new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) that directly measure performance on the segment confidence prediction task, which is a welcome improvement over task-specific metrics that may be biased toward specific applications.

However, the paper has some limitations. The performance gains are modest (1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC), and while consistent, they don't represent a dramatic leap in performance. The paper doesn't fully explore why e-SVM outperforms weighted SVM approaches that might be more directly comparable (the authors mention this but don't provide a thorough comparison). Additionally, the regularization term for the latent variables is somewhat ad-hoc, and the paper could better justify why the KL divergence-based regularization is superior to simpler alternatives.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper's novelty and conceptual clarity make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The e-SVM approach offers a principled framework for handling continuous label information in weakly supervised settings, which is a significant conceptual contribution to the field. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's core contribution to be substantial and well-executed.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for its approach. It properly situates itself within the literature, distinguishing itself from MIL approaches (which don't fit the problem setting) and weighted SVMs (which operate on data points rather than labels). The application to computer vision tasks is both relevant and well-executed.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and conceptually clean approach to weakly supervised learning that avoids the pitfalls of existing methods. While the performance gains are modest, the conceptual contribution is significant, and the approach is generalizable to other domains. The paper represents a meaningful step forward in the field of weakly supervised learning, and I believe it would be valuable for the NeurIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel concept of treating label positiveness as random variables with expectation loss
- Clear theoretical justification and principled approach to weak supervision
- Well-executed applications to semantic segmentation and object detection
- Introduces new evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) that directly assess the task
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Performance gains are modest (1.5% AP improvement)
- Could better compare to weighted SVM approaches
- Some aspects of the regularization term for latent variables could be better justified
- Limited exploration of the approach's limitations in more depth

The paper advances the state of the art in weakly supervised learning by providing a principled framework that handles continuous label information without ad-hoc transformations. As such, it deserves acceptance at NeurIPS.
