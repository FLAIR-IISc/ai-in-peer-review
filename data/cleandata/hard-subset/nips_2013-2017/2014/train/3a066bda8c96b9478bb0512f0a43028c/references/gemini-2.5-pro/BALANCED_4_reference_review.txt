This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from weakly supervised data. The primary motivation is to train models for computer vision tasks like segmentation and detection using inexpensive annotations like bounding boxes, rather than costly pixel-level masks. The core idea is to model the label of a training example (e.g., an image segment proposal) as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being positive is a function of its "positiveness" (e.g., IoU with the ground truth). The e-SVM loss function is the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this latent label. This formulation elegantly handles training data with continuous-valued labels in [0, 1].

The paper presents two variants: a basic e-SVM for the fully supervised case where positiveness values are known, and a latent e-SVM for the weakly supervised case (e.g., only bounding boxes are available) where positiveness values are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the model parameters via an alternating minimization procedure. The authors demonstrate the utility of e-SVM on two key vision tasks. First, for semantic segmentation, they show it is competitive with standard methods like SVR. Second, and more significantly, they use the latent e-SVM to learn segmentation cues that, when integrated as a "spatial confidence map feature" with the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector, yield a notable 1.5% improvement in mean Average Precision on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The proposed e-SVM loss is an elegant and well-motivated extension of the standard SVM. By taking the expectation over a random label, it provides a principled way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision (positiveness) without resorting to ad-hoc thresholding as in SVC. The extension to the latent e-SVM for weak supervision is a natural and clever application of the latent variable framework.

2.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem: reducing the annotation burden for training complex vision models. The most compelling result is the significant performance boost (+1.5% mAP) achieved by augmenting the very strong R-CNN baseline for object detection. This demonstrates that learning from segmentation cues, even with weak supervision, can provide valuable complementary information to state-of-the-art detection systems.

3.  **Generality and Flexibility:** The e-SVM framework is general. It is not tied to a specific feature type, proposal generator, or application. The authors correctly note its applicability to other problems where supervision signals are continuous and bounded. The fact that the resulting optimization problem can be solved using standard SVM solvers is a major practical advantage.

4.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The introduction of two new evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification, TAPC and NDCG, is a valuable contribution. These metrics allow for a more direct and task-agnostic evaluation of the model's ability to rank proposals, which is more aligned with the learning objective than downstream task performance alone.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of the Method:** While the core idea is clear, the exposition of some technical details could be improved.
    *   The optimization of the latent variables `u` in Algorithm 1 is not fully specified. The loss function in Eq. 2, when `w` is fixed, involves `u` inside a `log` term (from the KL regularizer) and linearly elsewhere. How is `argmin_u L(w(new), u)` computed? Providing the update rule or a pointer to the optimization method used would make the work more reproducible.
    *   The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 is quite dense. A diagram illustrating the process from per-pixel scores to the final M x K dimensional feature vector would greatly enhance clarity.
    *   The notation is slightly inconsistent (e.g., `w` is redefined to include the bias term `b` mid-paragraph in Section 3.1), which could be polished.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is primarily empirical. While the alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM is intuitive, there is no discussion of its convergence properties. Does the objective function in Eq. 2 have guarantees of converging to a local minimum? A brief discussion on this would strengthen the paper's technical soundness.

3.  **Connection to Existing Methods:** The relationship between e-SVM and instance-weighted SVMs (WSVMs) could be made more explicit. The loss function in Eq. 1 can be interpreted as training a standard SVM on a dataset where each original sample `(xi, ui)` is replaced by two samples: `(xi, +1)` with weight `g(ui)` and `(xi, -1)` with weight `1-g(ui)`. Clarifying this equivalence would better position the work and highlight that its novelty lies in the modeling perspective rather than requiring a new solver.

4.  **Analysis of Results:** The semantic segmentation results are described as "comparable" to the SVR baseline (36.8% vs. SVR's state-of-the-art result in [5]). However, on the direct segment classification task (Table in Sec 5.1.2), e-SVM clearly outperforms SVR. The paper would be stronger if it offered some analysis or hypothesis for this discrepancy. Does the sequential pasting inference procedure diminish the advantage of the better-calibrated e-SVM scores?

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and principled loss function (e-SVM) for learning with continuous-valued labels.
*   Addresses the highly significant and practical problem of learning from weak supervision.
*   Demonstrates a strong empirical result by significantly improving a state-of-the-art object detection system (R-CNN).
*   The method is general, flexible, and easy to implement with existing tools.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The presentation lacks clarity in key technical details, particularly the optimization of the latent model and the construction of the features for detection.
*   The work lacks theoretical analysis regarding the convergence of the proposed alternating optimization algorithm.
*   Results on one of the two main applications (semantic segmentation) are not a clear improvement over the baseline, and this is not discussed.

### Decision

Overall, this paper presents a novel and valuable contribution. The elegant formulation of the e-SVM and its successful application to improve a strong object detection baseline are significant strengths. While the paper could be improved in terms of clarity and theoretical depth, the practical importance of the problem and the strength of the empirical results make a compelling case for acceptance. I recommend this paper for acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the clarity of the method, in the final version.
