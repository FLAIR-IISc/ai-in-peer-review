This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" values within [0, 1]. This setting is common in computer vision, for instance, when evaluating segment proposals against ground truth, where the Intersection over Union (IoU) serves as a natural measure of positiveness. The core idea is to model the binary label of a training example as a Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is its positiveness. The e-SVM objective then minimizes the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss. This elegantly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM-like formulation that can be solved with standard solvers.

The authors further extend this model to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness values are unknown (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness values are treated as latent variables and inferred via an alternating optimization procedure, regularized to stay close to an initial estimate (e.g., IoU with the bounding box).

The proposed method is validated on two fundamental vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection, using the PASCAL VOC datasets. For segment proposal classification, e-SVM is shown to outperform standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) on two newly proposed metrics. For object detection, the latent e-SVM is used to generate a novel "spatial confidence map feature" which, when combined with the strong R-CNN baseline, yields a significant 1.5% mAP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007, advancing the state of the art at the time.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is the introduction of the e-SVM. The formulation of the loss as an expectation over a random label is an elegant and theoretically sound way to incorporate continuous-valued supervision into a max-margin framework. This is a clear improvement over ad-hoc approaches like thresholding continuous scores to create binary labels for SVC, or using SVR which is not naturally constrained to a [0, 1] interval. The connection to, and distinction from, weighted SVMs is well-articulated.

2.  **High Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses the critical and highly practical problem of reducing the annotation burden for large-scale machine learning. Learning effective models from bounding boxes instead of expensive pixel-level masks is a significant challenge. The proposed latent e-SVM provides a concrete and effective solution for this specific, yet common, type of weak supervision.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Key Task:** The most compelling result is the improvement in object detection. Boosting the performance of a very strong and state-of-the-art model like R-CNN by 1.5 mAP on the competitive PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark is a non-trivial achievement. This clearly demonstrates the practical value of the appearance cues learned by the latent e-SVM.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation Framework:** The authors evaluate their method on two major vision tasks. Furthermore, for the intermediate task of segment classification, they rightly point out the lack of standard evaluation criteria and propose two reasonable new metrics (TAPC and NDCG) to directly assess model performance, which strengthens their claims of superiority over SVC and SVR.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Underwhelming Semantic Segmentation Results:** While the e-SVM outperforms baselines on the proposed segment classification metrics (TAPC, NDCG), its performance on the downstream task of semantic segmentation is described as merely "comparable" to the SVR-based state of the art (Sec 5.2). This raises questions about the correlation between the proposed proxy metrics and the final task performance. A deeper analysis of this discrepancy would significantly strengthen the paper. Why doesn't the superior segment ranking performance translate into better final segmentation accuracy?

2.  **Limited Analysis of the Latent Model:** The latent e-SVM is a key contribution for the weak supervision setting, but its analysis is somewhat superficial. The paper would be improved by:
    *   A sensitivity analysis of the regularization hyperparameter `λR`. How crucial is this term, and how is its value selected?
    *   A discussion of the convergence properties of the alternating optimization.
    *   A qualitative analysis of the inferred latent variables `u`. It would be highly insightful to visualize some examples and show how the learned `u` values differ from the initial `ubb` (IoU with bounding box). Does the model learn to correctly down-weight proposals that overlap with the bounding box but not the actual object inside it?

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The description of the "spatial confidence map feature" in Section 4.2 could be more self-contained. The type of pooling used (average pooling) is only mentioned later in the experiments section (Sec 5.3). Specifying this in the method description would improve clarity and reproducibility.
    *   The paper has an incomplete reference (`[?]`) in the related work section (Section 2).

### Summary

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and well-motivated loss function (e-SVM) for learning with continuous-valued supervision.
*   Addresses the highly significant and practical problem of learning from weak annotations (bounding boxes).
*   Demonstrates a clear and significant performance improvement on a very strong state-of-the-art baseline for object detection.
*   The work is technically sound and the experiments are thorough.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The performance on the semantic segmentation task is not superior to the baseline, which somewhat weakens the claims of the method's general applicability.
*   The analysis of the latent variable model is lacking in depth, leaving questions about hyperparameter sensitivity and the behavior of the optimization.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core contribution. The novelty of the expectation loss SVM and its successful application to improve a state-of-the-art object detector are significant strengths that, in my opinion, outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily related to the depth of analysis rather than a fundamental flaw in the method itself. I recommend acceptance, and I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate the suggested analyses, particularly regarding the latent model and the semantic segmentation results, to further strengthen the final version of their work.
