Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM) for learning from “soft” supervision where each training instance has a positiveness score in [0,1] (e.g., IoU of a segment to a ground-truth mask). The core idea is to treat the binary label as a Bernoulli random variable with success probability µ=g(u) and minimize the expected hinge loss: for each example, a weighted sum of the positive and negative hinge losses with weights µ and (1−µ). When u is observed (pixel annotations), this yields a convex objective in w. For weak supervision (only bounding boxes), the authors treat u as latent and add a convex regularizer R(u), specifically a cross-entropy (KL) between u and the box-IoU ubb, and alternate minimizing over w and u. They demonstrate the method on segment confidence prediction (introducing TAPC and NDCG metrics), semantic segmentation using CPMC proposals, and object detection by constructing a class-specific spatial confidence map per window and concatenating it with RCNN features, reporting ~+1.5 AP on PASCAL VOC 2007.

The work sits between WSVM and MIL. Compared to MIL and MIM variants for weak supervision, e-SVM does not assume a fully positive instance exists; instead it uses soft/latent positiveness. Compared to weighted SVMs that weight examples, e-SVM weights the positive and negative hinge components per example. The latent extension is reminiscent of latent SVM/EM with a convex regularizer tethering latent u to initialization from box overlaps. There is related literature on learning with probabilistic/uncertain labels and fuzzy SVMs that use expected hinge or soft labels; this connection should be made more explicit.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The observed-u objective is convex and straightforward; it is equivalent to training with per-example, per-label hinge weights. The latent-u extension is biconvex and solved by alternating minimization; convergence to a local minimum is expected. The KL regularizer renders the u-subproblem strictly convex in u (with g=identity), but the paper lacks a derivation or algorithm for the u-update (closed-form vs. numeric, constraints ui∈(0,1), complexity).
- Claims vs. evidence: The segment ranking results (TAPC/NDCG) indicate improvements over SVC and marginal gains over SVR; however, some statements are inconsistent (earlier claiming “outperforms SVR,” later “comparable” on segmentation). The object detection gain of +1.5 mAP on VOC07 is potentially meaningful, but the confounding changes (modifying bbox regression features) and absence of an ablation against SVR-based segment confidence plus the same spatial map feature weaken attribution to e-SVM per se. Important experimental details (hyperparameters λw, λR, initialization sensitivity, runtime) are missing, limiting reproducibility.
- Empirics: No variance/error bars, no per-class tables beyond a single anecdote, limited ablations (e.g., form of g(u), strength of λR, alternative regularizers), and no comparison to ranking-oriented baselines (e.g., RankSVM) despite evaluating via NDCG.

Clarity:
- The exposition of the basic idea is clear, but the manuscript has multiple notational/typographical issues (e.g., the regularization term “λw·1 wTw” is unclear; scaling by N is inconsistent; some references are incomplete). The reduction to “any standard SVM solver” is asserted but not explained; in practice one needs per-instance/per-label weights or an equivalent construction—please clarify how LibLinear was used. The algorithm for the u-update is not specified beyond “argmin.” Experimental setup lacks necessary detail for reproduction (splits, CPMC parameters, SVM C, feature dimensionalities, training schedule, convergence criteria, timing).

Originality:
- The expected-hinge formulation with probabilistic labels and the idea of weighting positive/negative hinge terms are known variants in soft-label/fuzzy SVM literature; the paper does not cite or clearly distinguish from these. The latent extension with a KL tether to box overlaps is a reasonable and simple choice but conceptually close to latent SVM/EM. The spatial confidence map feature for detection is a sensible engineering contribution; similar ideas of using segment scores in window-aligned spatial bins have appeared in segmentation-aware detection works. Overall, the combination is incremental rather than fundamentally novel.

Significance:
- The setting is relevant: learning from weak (box-level) supervision for segmentation/detection remains important. If robust, a simple convex expected-hinge model that plugs into standard solvers is attractive. The reported +1.5 mAP on VOC07 over RCNN is practically interesting, though modest and potentially confounded. The segment-ranking metrics are useful additions, but broader impact would require stronger baselines (Ranking SVM, calibrated regression), stronger gains, and clearer ablations.

Strengths:
- Simple, convex formulation for observed soft labels; easy to understand and implement.
- Latent extension with convex per-block updates; principled regularization towards box overlaps.
- Practical integration into RCNN via a class-specific spatial confidence map; reported mAP gains.
- Introduction of task-agnostic segment-ranking metrics (TAPC, NDCG).

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to soft/fuzzy/probabilistic-label SVM literature and latent SVM.
- Missing algorithmic details for u-updates; no convergence analysis or sensitivity studies.
- Incomplete/messy notation and references; reproducibility concerns.
- Experimental evaluation lacks key ablations and stronger baselines; attribution of gains is unclear.
- No analysis of computational cost and scalability.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Situate the method within prior work on SVMs with probabilistic/soft labels and fuzzy SVMs; clarify differences.
- Provide the explicit reduction to standard SVM solvers or implement per-instance weights; detail the u-update (derivation, constraints, complexity) and show convergence behavior and sensitivity to λR and initialization.
- Compare against RankSVM/learning-to-rank and robust regression tailored to [0,1], and against using SVR-derived confidence in the same spatial map framework.
- Separate the effect of the spatial confidence map from the e-SVM training (ablations).
- Report comprehensive detection results (per-class AP tables, splits, variance), and avoid confounding changes to bbox regression unless matched in baselines.
- Clean up notation and complete references; add hyperparameters and code details.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important weakly supervised setting with a simple, general method.
- Empirical gains on a strong detector and reasonable segment-ranking improvements.
- Method is easy to adopt in practice.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental originality; overlaps with existing soft-label SVM ideas.
- Missing critical technical and experimental details; limited ablations.
- Modest and somewhat confounded empirical gains; unclear attribution.

Overall: Interesting, practical idea with promise, but the current submission lacks rigor and clarity to meet NeurIPS standards without substantial revision.
