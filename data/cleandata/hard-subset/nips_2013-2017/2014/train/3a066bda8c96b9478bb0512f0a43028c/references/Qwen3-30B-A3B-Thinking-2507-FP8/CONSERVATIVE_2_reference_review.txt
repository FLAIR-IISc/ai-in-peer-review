

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data is labeled with continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their approach outperforms standard SVC and SVR methods in semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, particularly when working with bounding box annotations rather than pixel-level labels.

The paper presents a reasonable problem statement, acknowledging the limitations of current weakly supervised learning approaches in computer vision. The authors correctly identify that standard SVC requires heuristic thresholding to convert continuous labels into binary labels, while SVR is not designed for bounded interval targets. Their e-SVM approach aims to handle this by treating the label as a random variable following a Bernoulli distribution with success probability equal to the positiveness value.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to proposed method to experiments. The authors provide sufficient mathematical details of their e-SVM formulation, and the experimental setup on PASCAL VOC datasets appears reasonable. The paper demonstrates modest improvements (1.5% average AP on object detection) over baseline methods.

However, I have significant concerns about the quality, novelty, and significance of this work that make me question its suitability for NIPS.

**Major concerns about quality:**
The paper lacks rigorous theoretical justification for why their approach should work better than existing methods. The core contribution appears to be a minor modification to standard SVM training, where each example is weighted by its positiveness. The authors claim this "addresses the issue" with SVC and SVR, but fail to provide convincing evidence beyond empirical results on a single benchmark dataset. The claimed improvement of 1.5% average AP on object detection is modest, and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing to validate this difference. The experimental section is limited to PASCAL VOC, which is a well-trodden benchmark in computer vision, and doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods for weakly supervised learning.

**Concerns about novelty:**
The authors cite weighted SVMs as related work but fail to demonstrate how their approach differs substantially from existing weighted SVM formulations. The "expectation loss" formulation is essentially a weighted SVM where the weight is the positiveness value. The paper doesn't establish how this differs from standard weighted SVM approaches in the literature, which have been extensively studied for handling class imbalance and outlier robustness. The paper's claim that their method "can be effortlessly solved by any standard SVM solver" suggests it's merely a reparameterization of existing techniques rather than a novel algorithm.

**Concerns about significance:**
The significance of the work is limited by the modest performance gains and narrow scope of evaluation. In the current computer vision landscape, weakly supervised learning is increasingly being addressed through deep learning approaches with sophisticated architectures and training schemes. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why their SVM-based approach would be preferable to these methods, especially given the minimal performance gains reported. The paper's claim that "the results are expected to be further improved if the data in ImageNet is used" suggests their method might not be particularly strong even with more data.

**Additional concerns:**
- The paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations or failure cases of their approach.
- The regularization term for the latent variables is somewhat ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification.
- The paper doesn't provide enough detail for reproducibility (e.g., exact hyperparameter settings, training procedures beyond "standard SVM solver").
- The connection to the broader weakly supervised learning literature (e.g., Multiple Instance Learning) is superficial and doesn't establish how their approach differs from or improves upon established methods in that domain.

**Strengths:**
- The problem statement is well-motivated.
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
- The experimental evaluation on semantic segmentation and object detection is reasonably thorough for the scope of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
- Marginal novelty compared to existing weighted SVM approaches.
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed method.
- Modest performance gains that don't convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods.
- Limited evaluation scope (only PASCAL VOC datasets).
- Lack of comparison to more recent weakly supervised learning methods.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision, the proposed solution represents a marginal modification of standard SVM techniques without sufficient theoretical justification or empirical evidence of substantial improvement. The claimed performance gains are modest and not convincingly demonstrated to be statistically significant. Given the current state of weakly supervised learning in computer vision, where deep learning approaches have largely superseded traditional SVM-based methods, this work does not present a compelling advancement that would be suitable for publication at NIPS. The paper would need to demonstrate significantly greater improvements over state-of-the-art methods, provide stronger theoretical justification, and conduct more comprehensive comparisons to justify its novelty and significance.
