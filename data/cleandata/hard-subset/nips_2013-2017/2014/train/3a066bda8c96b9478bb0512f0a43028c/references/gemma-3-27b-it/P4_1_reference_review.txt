The paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised data, particularly in computer vision tasks where complete pixel-level annotations are unavailable. The core idea is to model the “positiveness” of segment proposals as a continuous value between 0 and 1, treating it as an expectation and incorporating it into an SVM loss function. The paper explores both scenarios: observing the positiveness (strong supervision) and treating it as a latent variable (weak supervision). The algorithm is evaluated on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines, and achieving competitive results on standard datasets like PASCAL VOC.

This work addresses a crucial problem in computer vision – the cost of manual annotation. Several recent NIPS papers have also focused on weakly supervised learning, including approaches based on multiple instance learning (MIL) and exploiting image-level labels (e.g., Vezhnevets et al., NIPS 2012). However, the e-SVM approach distinguishes itself by focusing on continuous positiveness values associated with segment proposals, a formulation not typically seen in MIL frameworks. Related work on weighted SVMs is discussed, highlighting the difference between weighting data points vs. labels. The paper builds upon previous work utilizing segment proposals for segmentation and detection (e.g., Carreira et al., NIPS 2012), but offers a novel loss function tailored to the continuous positiveness representation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation, specifically the expectation loss function handling continuous positiveness values, is a novel contribution. It addresses a limitation of standard SVC/SVR approaches when dealing with segment proposal confidence prediction.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivation of the e-SVM loss function is clear and well-justified. The iterative optimization procedure for the latent variable case is presented with sufficient detail.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper provides thorough experimental results across three tasks. Demonstrating improvements over strong baselines (SVC, SVR) and achieving competitive performance on object detection (RCNN+e-SVM) is significant.
*   **Generality:** The authors correctly point out the algorithm's generality, applicable to various segment proposal detectors, feature representations, and classification models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the method is explained in a step-by-step manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While results are presented for different methods, a more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different regularization terms (in the latent e-SVM case) or feature representations would be beneficial.
*   **Choice of CPMC:** The consistent use of CPMC segments as proposals might limit the generalizability of the findings. Exploring other proposal generation methods could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
*   **Comparison to More Recent Methods:** The comparison to baselines is solid, but including recent advancements in weakly supervised learning, especially those employing deep learning techniques directly, could further strengthen the evaluation.  For example, comparing against recent contrastive learning based methods for weakly supervised segmentation would be interesting.
*   **Tuning of Hyperparameters:** The paper doesn't detail the specific hyperparameter tuning process. Reporting the search space and selection criteria would enhance reproducibility.
*   **KL Divergence Regularization**: While the rationale for the KL divergence regularization is explained, the strong regularization could be investigated further to assess its necessity and impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound algorithm addressing a significant problem in computer vision – learning from weakly supervised data.
*   The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating improvements over existing methods on multiple tasks.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a solid contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive ablation study and comparison to more recent state-of-the-art techniques.
*   The reliance on CPMC segments might limit the generalizability of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated approach, and the experimental results are promising. While some improvements could be made through more extensive ablation studies and comparisons to recent methods, the paper demonstrates sufficient originality and technical quality to warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
