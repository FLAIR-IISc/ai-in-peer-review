## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel SVM-based approach (e-SVM) for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing the scenario where proposals for the true label are available with associated “positiveness” scores. The method shows promising results on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming SVR and SVC baselines. While the idea is conceptually sound and the experimental validation is reasonably thorough, the paper lacks a deep engagement with related work on weakly supervised learning *beyond* segmentation and detection, and some aspects of the methodology (particularly the regularization in the latent variable setting) feel somewhat ad-hoc. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**, contingent on addressing the concerns detailed below.

**1. Summary & Related Work (20% of Score):**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of learning with weak supervision, motivating the need for algorithms that can effectively leverage partial or imprecise labels. The introduction effectively sets the stage by highlighting the limitations of fully supervised approaches and the practical relevance of weak supervision scenarios like bounding box annotations. The core idea of e-SVM – weighting samples based on their estimated "positiveness" – is presented logically. 

The related work section is adequate but feels somewhat limited. While the authors correctly identify relevant work in segmentation and detection, the discussion of general weakly supervised learning techniques (beyond MIL) is relatively shallow.  For example, the connection to contrastive learning, or techniques that explicitly model ambiguity in labels, are not explored. The discussion of weighted SVMs is also cursory; the differences between e-SVM and existing weighted SVMs, beyond the weighting of labels *vs.* data points, could be further elaborated.  It’s unclear if the authors are aware of, and can differentiate from, approaches like label smoothing in deep learning which also implicitly address noisy labels.

**2. Strengths (25% of Score):**

* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation is a clever extension of standard SVMs to handle continuous label estimates (positiveness). This allows it to naturally bridge the gap between fully supervised and weakly supervised settings.
* **Generality:** The authors rightly emphasize the flexibility of the approach – its ability to work with any segment proposal method, feature representation, and base classifier. This generality is a significant strength.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results, particularly the 1.5% average improvement on PASCAL VOC object detection, demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed method. The comparison to SVR and SVC provides a good baseline.  The ablation study with different regularization strengths in the latent variable setting is also a positive point.
* **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM model is well-presented and easy to follow. The diagrams are helpful for visualizing the framework.

**3. Weaknesses (35% of Score):**

* **Regularization in Latent Setting:** The choice of the KL divergence-based regularization term for the latent variable setting (Section 3.2) feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors provide some justification (preventing u from collapsing to 0 or 1), a more thorough exploration of alternative regularization schemes, and a sensitivity analysis, would strengthen the paper.  Why is the KL divergence a suitable measure of distance for this specific problem?
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks deeper theoretical justification for the proposed method. For instance, it would be beneficial to analyze the bias-variance trade-off introduced by the positiveness weighting.  Is there a guarantee of convergence under specific conditions?
* **Tuning & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn’t explicitly detail the hyperparameter tuning process. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the regularization parameter (λR)? A more rigorous analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would increase confidence in the reported performance.
* **Dependence on Segment Proposals:** The performance of e-SVM is inherently tied to the quality of the segment proposals. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of different proposal methods would be valuable. The experiments primarily focus on CPMC; exploring other methods (e.g., superpixels) more thoroughly might reveal limitations of the approach.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Weakly Supervised Methods:** The paper primarily compares e-SVM to SVR and SVC, which are not necessarily the most competitive baselines for weakly supervised learning.  A comparison to more recent and specifically designed weakly supervised learning techniques, even if outside the segmentation/detection domain, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**4. Significance (20% of Score):**

The work is significant in its contribution to the broader field of weakly supervised learning. Addressing the problem of leveraging imprecise labels is crucial for scaling machine learning to real-world applications where obtaining fully labeled data is often prohibitively expensive. The e-SVM approach provides a principled way to incorporate confidence information into the learning process. The improvement in object detection performance, while not revolutionary, is encouraging and demonstrates the potential of the method.  The ability to integrate with existing state-of-the-art detectors (like RCNN) is a practical benefit.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to learning with weak supervision.
* Strong empirical performance on relevant tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection).
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for broad applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical analysis.
* Ad-hoc choice of regularization term in the latent variable setting.
* Shallow engagement with broader weakly supervised learning literature.
* Lack of comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.



**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Expand Related Work:**  Thoroughly review and discuss relevant work on weakly supervised learning beyond segmentation and detection.
* **Justify Regularization:**  Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen regularization term and explore alternative options.
* **Hyperparameter Analysis:**  Include a more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** Add some theoretical discussion on the properties of the e-SVM model (e.g., bias-variance trade-off).
* **Explore Segment Proposals:** Investigate the impact of different segment proposal methods on performance.




I believe that addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
