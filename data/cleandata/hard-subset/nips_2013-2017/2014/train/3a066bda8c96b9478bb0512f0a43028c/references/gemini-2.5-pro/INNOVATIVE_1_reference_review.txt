### Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel SVM formulation, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed to learn from data with continuous-valued labels in the [0, 1] range, which the authors term "positiveness". The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the probability of being positive is given by the observed positiveness value (e.g., IoU score). The SVM loss is then formulated as the expectation of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This clever formulation results in a weighted SVM problem that can be solved with standard solvers, where each sample contributes weighted terms to both the positive and negative sides of the classification margin.

The paper further proposes a "latent e-SVM" for weakly supervised settings where the positiveness itself is unobserved (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available for a segmentation task). In this extension, the positiveness values are treated as latent variables and are optimized iteratively along with the SVM model parameters. The authors validate their approach on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. For the latter, they show that features derived from their latent e-SVM model provide a complementary signal that improves the performance of the strong R-CNN baseline.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and conceptual elegance. The e-SVM formulation is a bold and highly original approach to a significant and practical problem in machine learning and computer vision: how to learn effectively from supervision that is richer than a binary label but not perfectly precise.

1.  **Novel Formulation:** Instead of forcing the problem into existing frameworks like binary classification (SVC with a threshold) or standard regression (SVR), the authors propose a new loss function that is specifically tailored to the problem. Treating the label as a random variable and optimizing the expected loss is a principled and clever way to incorporate the uncertainty or partial correctness inherent in "positiveness" scores. This represents a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Latent Variable Extension:** The extension to the latent e-SVM is particularly compelling. It addresses the very common and challenging scenario of learning fine-grained models (like for segmentation) from coarse annotations (like bounding boxes). By treating the IoU scores as latent variables and optimizing them jointly with the classifier, the model provides a new direction for weakly supervised learning that moves beyond standard MIL assumptions. This is a brave and interesting idea.

3.  **Practicality and Generality:** A major advantage is that the basic e-SVM reduces to a weighted SVM, making it solvable by off-the-shelf tools like LibLinear. This lowers the barrier to adoption and experimentation. The authors also rightly point out that the overall framework is general and can be combined with different proposal generators, features, and base detectors.

4.  **Demonstrated Utility:** The paper demonstrates the value of its idea by successfully integrating it into a state-of-the-art object detection pipeline (R-CNN) and showing a consistent, albeit moderate, performance improvement. This is a crucial validation, as it proves the proposed concept is not just a theoretical curiosity but can provide a meaningful signal to strong, complex models.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are strong, the paper has some minor weaknesses, which are largely acceptable given the novelty of the contribution.

1.  **Incremental Performance Gains:** The empirical improvements, particularly the 1.5% mAP gain on PASCAL VOC 2007 detection, are solid but not groundbreaking. A reviewer focused purely on state-of-the-art performance might view this as a limitation. However, improving a very strong baseline like R-CNN at all is non-trivial, and the value here lies more in the novel mechanism by which the improvement is achieved.

2.  **Heuristic Optimization:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM is an alternating minimization scheme. While sensible and standard for such problems, it is susceptible to local minima, and the paper does not provide a deep analysis of its convergence properties. The KL-divergence regularizer is a reasonable choice to anchor the latent variables, but the overall optimization remains somewhat heuristic.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The semantic segmentation results are reported as "comparable" to the SVR-based state of the art, which is a somewhat weak validation. A more thorough comparison on this task would have strengthened the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a genuinely new way of thinking about learning from continuous, bounded labels. The e-SVM concept and its latent extension could inspire a new line of work in weakly supervised learning. This is exactly the kind of creative, thought-provoking research that NIPS should champion.
*   **Bold Approach:** Tackling weakly supervised learning by defining a new loss function and a latent variable model is a much bolder step than simply engineering a new feature or tweaking an existing network architecture.
*   **Soundness of Idea:** The core mathematical formulation is sound, elegant, and provides a principled bridge between classification and regression for this specific problem type.

**Arguments for Rejection**
*   The empirical results, while positive, do not represent a massive leap in performance.
*   The work is built upon a classical ML framework (SVMs) at a time when deep, end-to-end learning was rapidly becoming dominant.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The core contribution—the expectation-loss SVM—is a highly novel, elegant, and intellectually stimulating idea. It addresses a difficult and important problem in a principled manner. The latent extension is a bold step towards bridging the supervision gap between bounding boxes and pixel masks. While the empirical gains are not massive, they are sufficient to demonstrate that the idea has practical merit. This paper prioritizes a powerful new concept over incremental SOTA-chasing, and its potential to influence future research in weakly supervised learning is significant. This is precisely the type of foundational, idea-driven work that enriches the NIPS community.
