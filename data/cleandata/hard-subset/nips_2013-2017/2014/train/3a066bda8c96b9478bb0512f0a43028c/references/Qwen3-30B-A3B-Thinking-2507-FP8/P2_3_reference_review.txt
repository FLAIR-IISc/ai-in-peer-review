

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where training labels are continuous values within [0,1] (e.g., IoU overlap ratios) rather than binary labels. The e-SVM model treats labels as random variables following a Bernoulli distribution, with success probability determined by the "positiveness" measure. The paper extends this to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where positiveness is unobserved (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available). The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear mathematical formulation. The basic e-SVM model (Equation 1) properly handles continuous-valued labels by using an expectation loss, which is a reasonable extension of standard SVMs. The latent e-SVM extension (Section 3.2) with the iterative optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated for weakly supervised scenarios. The regularization term (Equation 3) is appropriately designed to prevent the optimization from converging to binary solutions.

However, the paper lacks sufficient statistical validation. The reported performance improvements (e.g., 1.5% AP gain on object detection) are not accompanied by statistical significance tests or standard deviations across multiple runs. This weakens the confidence in the claimed improvements. The paper also doesn't thoroughly analyze the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (e.g., λ and λR), which is important for practical applications.

The experimental evaluation is generally strong, with appropriate datasets (PASCAL VOC 2011 and 2007) and standard baselines (SVC and SVR). The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as new evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification is a thoughtful contribution. However, the paper could have provided more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the e-SVM component versus other aspects of the implementation.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to model formulation to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key contributions (e-SVM model and its latent extension) are well-explained. The application to semantic segmentation and object detection is presented with sufficient technical detail.

The paper could improve clarity by providing more intuitive explanations of the expectation loss concept. While the mathematical formulation is sound, a more intuitive description of why this approach handles continuous labels better than SVC or SVR would help readers grasp the core insight. The paper also omits some implementation details that would aid reproducibility (e.g., specific values of λ and λR used in experiments).

## Originality

The core idea of using an expectation loss for continuous-valued labels in SVMs represents a novel contribution to weakly supervised learning. The paper effectively distinguishes this from related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and Weighted SVMs (WSVMs), which are not directly applicable to the problem setting. The authors correctly note that MIL cannot handle cases where most proposals contain both foreground and background pixels (Section 2).

However, the paper could strengthen its originality claim by more thoroughly surveying related work. While it mentions "weighted SVM" approaches, it doesn't cite several relevant papers that have explored similar concepts (e.g., "Learning using privileged information" by Vapnik, or "Probabilistic SVM" by Wang et al.). The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with these approaches.

## Significance

The problem of weakly supervised learning is highly significant in computer vision, as pixel-level annotations are expensive to obtain. The paper demonstrates that e-SVM can effectively utilize bounding box annotations (which are widely available in large datasets like ImageNet) to achieve performance comparable to methods requiring pixel-level annotations. The 1.5% average AP improvement on object detection (Table 1) represents a meaningful advancement for a simple extension to existing detection pipelines.

The method's versatility is a major strength - it can be applied to various segmentation methods, feature extractors, and classifiers. The paper's application to both semantic segmentation and object detection shows the broad applicability of the approach. The claim that this could reduce labeling costs for large-scale vision tasks is well-supported by the results.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel formulation of expectation loss for continuous labels
- Clear distinction from related work (MIL, WSVM)
- Introduction of appropriate evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG)
- Consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks
- Practical implementation with meaningful performance gains

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of statistical significance testing for performance improvements
- Insufficient discussion of method limitations
- Incomplete comparison with more recent weakly supervised learning methods
- Missing implementation details that would aid reproducibility
- Limited theoretical analysis of why the expectation loss is superior

## Recommendation

The paper makes a solid contribution to weakly supervised learning with a technically sound approach and meaningful empirical results. While the paper has some weaknesses in statistical validation and related work comparison, the core idea is novel and the results are promising. The method has clear potential for broader application in computer vision.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should strengthen the statistical analysis by including significance tests, provide more detail about hyperparameter choices, and expand the comparison with recent weakly supervised learning methods.

This paper addresses an important practical problem in computer vision with a principled solution that advances the state of the art in weakly supervised learning. The method is simple to implement yet yields meaningful improvements over baselines, making it likely to be adopted by practitioners. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insight with practical application in a significant problem domain.
