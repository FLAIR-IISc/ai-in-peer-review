

# NIPS Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU between segment proposals and ground truth) rather than binary labels. The authors present both a basic e-SVM for cases where positiveness is observed (strong supervision) and a latent e-SVM for cases where positiveness must be treated as a latent variable (weak supervision, such as when only bounding box annotations are available). The method is applied to two major computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation (with both pixel-level and bounding box annotations) and object detection (using RCNN as a base detector). The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines across multiple evaluation metrics.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of SVM. The derivation of the expectation loss function is mathematically rigorous, and the latent e-SVM formulation with the iterative optimization algorithm is well-justified. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from weighted SVMs (WSVMs) and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), noting that e-SVM weights labels rather than data points, which is crucial for their problem setting. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using PASCAL VOC datasets with appropriate baselines (SVC with multiple thresholds, SVR) and novel evaluation metrics (TAPC, NDCG) that directly measure segment classification performance. The results show consistent improvements over baselines (1.5% AP gain in object detection), which is significant in computer vision.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem statement, motivation, and technical details are presented logically. The introduction effectively establishes the importance of weakly supervised learning, and the related work section properly contextualizes the contribution. The experimental setup is detailed, and the figures (Figures 1 and 2) help illustrate the framework and feature generation process. The authors make a good effort to define new evaluation metrics and justify their use.

**Significance and Impact:** The problem addressed—reducing annotation costs in computer vision—is highly relevant and important. The ability to leverage bounding box annotations (which are readily available for large datasets like ImageNet) instead of expensive pixel-level annotations has significant practical implications. The paper demonstrates clear advancements over previous work in both semantic segmentation and object detection, with the object detection results showing a 1.5% improvement over the state-of-the-art RCNN method.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** While the e-SVM formulation is well-motivated, the core idea of handling continuous labels with SVM is somewhat similar to existing weighted SVM approaches. The distinction between e-SVM and WSVMs is clear, but the novelty is more in the specific application to weakly supervised segmentation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic concept. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the most recent weighted SVM literature.

**Technical Depth:** The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why e-SVM outperforms SVR, which is also designed for regression problems. SVR is mentioned as a baseline, but the theoretical connection between the two methods isn't explored in depth. The paper also doesn't discuss computational complexity or training time compared to baselines, which would be valuable for practical adoption.

**Experimental Details:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks sensitivity analysis for key parameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λR). The paper mentions using m=5 for spatial bins but doesn't justify this choice or explore its impact on performance. The comparison to other weakly supervised learning methods (beyond MIL) could be more thorough.

**Application to Object Detection:** The integration of e-SVM with RCNN is straightforward (simply concatenating features), and the paper doesn't explore more sophisticated integration methods that might yield greater improvements. The authors note that "this simple approach yields an average improvement of 1.5 percent," but don't discuss whether this is the maximum possible gain with their method.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound approach to weakly supervised learning with continuous labels, with clear experimental validation showing improvements over relevant baselines. The problem is important, the solution is well-motivated, and the results are significant in the context of computer vision. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (extending SVM to handle continuous labels with a specific expectation loss), the paper makes a compelling case for its practical utility.

The paper meets NIPS's quality criteria with strong theoretical grounding and comprehensive experimental validation. The clarity is excellent, and the significance of the work is clear, particularly for the computer vision community where annotation costs are a major bottleneck. The originality is moderate—while the specific formulation is novel, it builds on established SVM theory and is most significant for its practical application.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is solid, well-executed, and addresses an important practical problem in computer vision. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical significance and clear experimental validation make it a valuable contribution to the field. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the relationship between e-SVM and SVR, and by sensitivity analysis of key parameters, but these are minor issues that don't detract from the overall quality.

The work is clearly within NIPS scope, as it addresses a fundamental machine learning problem (weakly supervised learning) with a novel algorithmic approach and significant experimental validation on important computer vision tasks. The paper's focus on practical machine learning for real-world problems aligns well with NIPS's mission.

In summary, this is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. It advances the state of the art in semantic segmentation and object detection, and provides a general framework that could be applied to other domains. The results are reproducible, the methodology is sound, and the contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
