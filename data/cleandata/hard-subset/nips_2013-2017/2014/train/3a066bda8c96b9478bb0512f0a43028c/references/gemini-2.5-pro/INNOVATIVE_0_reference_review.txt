### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation-loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised settings where training labels are not binary but continuous "positiveness" values within the [0, 1] range (e.g., Intersection over Union). The core idea is to treat the true binary label of a sample as a latent Bernoulli random variable, where the success probability is determined by its known positiveness score. The e-SVM then minimizes the *expectation* of the standard hinge loss over this latent variable. This formulation cleverly transforms the problem into a weighted SVM, where each sample contributes differently to the positive and negative class margins, and which can be solved with standard solvers.

The paper further extends this to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness scores are unknown (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available, not pixel-level masks). In this case, the positiveness scores are treated as latent variables and optimized jointly with the SVM model parameters via alternating minimization. The authors apply this framework to semantic segmentation and object detection. They demonstrate that e-SVM outperforms standard Support Vector Classification (SVC) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) for segment proposal classification. Crucially, they show that features derived from their model can be integrated with the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector, yielding a significant 1.5% mean AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its core idea. The e-SVM formulation is a genuinely novel and principled method for handling continuous-valued supervision within a max-margin classification framework. It moves far beyond the typical ad-hoc approaches of thresholding the continuous values for SVC or forcing the problem into a standard regression (SVR) mold. Framing the loss as an expectation over a latent binary label is both elegant and powerful. This represents a significant conceptual contribution that could influence how researchers approach problems with non-binary supervision signals.

2.  **Bold and Practical Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem in modern machine learning and computer vision. As datasets grow, the cost of fine-grained annotation (like pixel-level segmentation) becomes prohibitive. Learning effectively from coarser annotations like bounding boxes is critical. The latent e-SVM directly addresses this "middle ground" of weak supervision, which is arguably more common in practice than the heavily-studied case of image-level tags. This is a forward-looking approach that addresses a real-world bottleneck.

3.  **Generality and Simplicity of Implementation:** A major advantage of the e-SVM is its practicality. Despite its novel formulation, the resulting optimization problem is equivalent to a weighted SVM that can be solved efficiently using existing, off-the-shelf solvers like LibLinear. This lack of a need for a bespoke solver is a huge practical benefit and dramatically increases the likelihood that other researchers will adopt and build upon this method. The idea itself is general and could be applied to many other domains beyond computer vision where supervision is noisy or non-binary.

4.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed model. The direct comparison against SVC and SVR on the segment classification task, using the authors' well-motivated evaluation criteria (TAPC and NDCG), clearly establishes the superiority of the e-SVM formulation. Furthermore, achieving a 1.5% mAP improvement on top of the very strong R-CNN baseline is a non-trivial result. It proves that the segmentation cues learned by the latent e-SVM provide meaningful, complementary information that even a powerful deep learning model can benefit from.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Heuristic Optimization for the Latent Model:** The optimization for the latent e-SVM uses an alternating minimization scheme. While this is a standard and pragmatic approach, it is not guaranteed to find a global optimum and can be sensitive to initialization. The paper acknowledges that without proper regularization, the method can get stuck in poor local minima. While the chosen KL-divergence regularizer is sensible, the optimization aspect of the latent model is not fully resolved. However, for a paper introducing a novel formulation, this is an acceptable and practical choice.

2.  **Limited Exploration of Model Assumptions:** The model makes some simplifying assumptions, for instance, by setting the Bernoulli success probability equal to the IoU (`g(u_i) = u_i`). The performance of the model might be sensitive to this choice, and the paper does not explore alternatives. A more complete investigation could strengthen the work, but this feels more like a promising direction for future research than a critical flaw in the current submission.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. Its primary value lies in the novelty and elegance of the e-SVM formulation. It introduces a bold, new way to think about learning from continuous supervision signals, which is a significant and broadly applicable problem. While a critic could point to the modest performance gains on the downstream detection task or the heuristic nature of the latent variable optimization, such criticisms would miss the main point. This is not a paper about incrementally pushing a state-of-the-art number; it is a paper introducing a foundational new idea. The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate its superiority over logical baselines. The e-SVM is the kind of creative and conceptually clean contribution that has the potential to inspire a new line of inquiry. It prioritizes a novel idea over methodological perfection, which is something our community should encourage.
