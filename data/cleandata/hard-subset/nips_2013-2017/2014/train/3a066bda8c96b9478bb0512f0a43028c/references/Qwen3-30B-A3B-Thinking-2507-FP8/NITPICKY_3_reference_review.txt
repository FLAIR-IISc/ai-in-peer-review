

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training data has continuous "positiveness" labels (e.g., IoU values) rather than binary labels. The authors claim their method outperforms standard SVC and SVR in both strong and weakly supervised settings for semantic segmentation and object detection tasks.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision where pixel-level annotations are expensive but bounding box annotations are more readily available. While related to MIL and weighted SVMs, the authors argue their formulation is distinct because it handles continuous positiveness values rather than binary or bag-level labels. The core idea extends standard SVM by treating labels as random variables with Bernoulli distributions parameterized by the observed positiveness.

**Strengths:** The problem addressed is relevant to current computer vision challenges. The experimental results on PASCAL VOC datasets show promising improvements (1.5% average AP gain in object detection). The extension to weak supervision via latent variables is conceptually interesting.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Errors and Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper contains critical mathematical errors that undermine its technical credibility. In Section 3.1, Equation (1) contains the confusing term "λw · 1/wTw" which should be "λ/2 ||w||^2" (standard SVM notation). The loss function also incorrectly defines l+i and l-i without the label variable Yi, which is essential for SVM loss formulation. These errors suggest a lack of careful mathematical review.

2. **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for the positiveness variable (u, ui, ubbi, ubb). In Algorithm 1, "ubb" is used without prior definition, while "ubbi" appears in the text. This confusion makes the algorithm difficult to implement.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors assume "the true value of u should be around ubb" but provide no theoretical or empirical justification for this assumption. The regularization term (Equation 3) is defined as the negative cross-entropy, which is unusual as a regularization term (typically used to prevent overfitting, not to encourage proximity to an initial estimate).

4. **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The paper claims e-SVM outperforms SVR but doesn't provide sufficient details about the SVR implementation (kernel, parameters, etc.). The comparison in Table 1 lacks statistical significance testing for the 1.5% improvement, which is critical for a 1.5% gain on standard benchmarks.

5. **Formatting and Citation Issues:** The paper contains multiple typos (e.g., "pixel level" vs "pixel-level"), inconsistent reference formatting, and missing citation details (e.g., [18] is referenced but not properly cited). The title contains a grammatical error ("The Expectation Loss SVM" should be "Expectation Loss SVM").

6. **Inadequate Discussion of Limitations:** The paper fails to discuss scenarios where the method might fail, computational complexity of the latent e-SVM training, or how it scales with dataset size. The claim of "generality" is unsupported by experiments with different segment proposal detectors or feature extractors.

7. **Inconsistent Dataset Usage:** The semantic segmentation experiments use PASCAL VOC 2011, while object detection uses PASCAL VOC 2007. This inconsistency could confuse readers and raises questions about whether the results are comparable.

8. **Unclear Evaluation Metrics:** The proposed "Threshold Average Precision Curve" (TAPC) is introduced without sufficient justification for why it's superior to standard metrics like mAP. The paper doesn't explain why this metric was chosen over more common evaluation criteria.

**Conclusion:** While the problem addressed is important and the general idea has merit, the paper contains too many technical errors, inconsistent notations, and inadequate experimental details to be considered a sound scientific contribution. The mathematical formulation is flawed, the experimental comparisons are incomplete, and the paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for its key assumptions. These issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility as a technical contribution to NIPS.

The authors would need to thoroughly revise the mathematical formulation, provide complete experimental details, justify their regularization approach theoretically, and fix the numerous notational inconsistencies before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's technical flaws and lack of detail prevent it from meeting NIPS' standards for scientific rigor and reproducibility.
