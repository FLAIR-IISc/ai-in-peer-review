This paper introduces the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), a method for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically addressing scenarios where proposals for the true label exist with varying degrees of correctness quantified by a “positiveness” value. The core idea is to treat this positiveness as a random variable and formulate a loss function that accounts for the uncertainty. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness in segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, showcasing improvements over standard SVC and SVR approaches.

While the core idea of adapting SVMs to handle continuous label estimates (the 'positiveness' value) is conceptually sound and addresses a practical challenge in weakly supervised learning, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws in its presentation and execution. These flaws detract from the potential impact of the work and raise concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a crucial area, and handling continuous, probabilistic labels is a logical step beyond binary classifications.
*   **Clear motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard SVC and SVR when applied to this problem.
*   **Competitive results:**  The experimental results demonstrate improvements over baseline methods, suggesting the e-SVM offers a practical benefit.
*   **Generality:** The authors rightly emphasize the flexibility of their approach, accommodating various segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational inconsistency:** The notation is jarring. For example, the use of *u<sub>i</sub>* to represent both the positiveness *and* the IoU is confusing. Similarly, *l<sup>+</sup><sub>i</sub>* and *l<sup>-</sup><sub>i</sub>* are defined but then immediately replaced by a redundant expansion in the loss function equation. This pervasive lack of precision diminishes clarity.
*   **Superficial novelty:**  The core contribution, weighting the SVM loss by positiveness, feels incremental. The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged but dismissed too quickly, failing to adequately position the work within the existing literature. The paper doesn't demonstrate a deep understanding of *why* simply weighting the loss is a significant advance or what differentiates it meaningfully from existing WSVM approaches.
*   **Insufficient experimental detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. The exact implementation of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM (Equation 3) requires more justification. Why that specific KL divergence formulation? What are the sensitivity of results to the regularization parameter λ<sub>R</sub>?
*   **Evaluation concerns:** The TAPC and NDCG metrics are novel, which is commendable. However, they’re not explained sufficiently well to understand their relevance or interpret the results. Just stating they are “direct performance evaluation criteria” is insufficient.  The results tables (e.g., Table 1) are presented without standard deviations, making it difficult to assess statistical significance. The claim of a "1.5 percent on per-class average precision (AP)" improvement is vague – 1.5 percentage *points* or 1.5 percent *relative* improvement?
*   **Language and presentation:** The writing quality is uneven. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that detract from readability. The sentence structure is often convoluted, hindering comprehension. Several references are missing year information (e.g., "[6]" throughout the paper).
*   **Algorithm 1 clarity:** The pseudo-code for the latent e-SVM optimization is too high-level. It doesn't specify how the *u* values are updated in line 4, and assumes a convergence criterion without defining it.
*    **Figure 2 description is lacking:** The description of how spatial confidence map features are created is too brief to be fully understood.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a relevant and practical problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   Demonstrates improvements over baseline methods.
*   The idea of weighting the SVM loss function is conceptually reasonable.

**Con:**

*   Incremental novelty, with insufficient differentiation from existing weighted SVM approaches.
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and experimental detail.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices (e.g., the regularization term).
*   Presentation quality hinders understanding and assessment.
*   Lack of statistical rigor in presenting experimental results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the core idea is valid and the results are promising, the numerous flaws in presentation, experimental detail, and theoretical justification raise serious concerns. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.  Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the work. The weaknesses are not catastrophic individually, but cumulatively, they create a significant barrier to understanding and trusting the claims made by the authors. A major revision with substantial improvements to clarity, rigor, and completeness would be required for reconsideration.
