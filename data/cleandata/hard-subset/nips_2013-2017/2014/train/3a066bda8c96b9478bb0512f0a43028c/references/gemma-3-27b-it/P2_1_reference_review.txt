## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed to handle weakly supervised data where only partial or imprecise labels are available.  The core idea is to treat the “positiveness” of data points (e.g., IoU overlap for segment proposals) as a random variable, allowing training even when this positiveness is unobserved.  The authors demonstrate the algorithm's efficacy in semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, showcasing improvements over standard SVM and SVR approaches. They provide both theoretical justification and empirical results on PASCAL VOC datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem:** Weakly supervised learning is increasingly vital due to the cost of fully labeled datasets. This paper tackles a specific, well-defined challenge within this domain.
* **Novel approach:** The formulation of e-SVM, treating positiveness as a random variable and incorporating it into the loss function, is a clever extension of standard SVMs. The latent variable extension for truly weakly supervised scenarios is a significant contribution.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC) for both semantic segmentation and object detection. The results consistently show improvements over strong baselines (SVC, SVR, and a state-of-the-art object detector).
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the e-SVM and latent e-SVM models, demonstrating a good understanding of the underlying theory.
* **Generality:** The authors explicitly highlight the versatility of the approach, noting its compatibility with different segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty in application tasks:** While the *algorithm* itself is novel, its application to semantic segmentation and object detection relies heavily on existing frameworks and datasets.  The improvements shown, while statistically significant, are incremental (e.g., 1.5% AP improvement in object detection).
* **Regularization Choice:** The regularization term chosen for the latent e-SVM (based on KL divergence) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough exploration of different regularization strategies and their impact would strengthen the results.  The authors acknowledge that this regularization is strong and prevents u from being 0 or 1, but further justification would be beneficial.
* **Lack of comparison to recent weakly-supervised methods:**  The related work section mentions MIL and other weakly supervised learning approaches, but doesn’t compare against more recent techniques specifically designed for weakly-supervised *segmentation* or *detection*, especially those leveraging deep learning. For example, comparing against recent contrastive learning approaches for weakly supervised segmentation would be valuable.
* **Spatial Confidence Map Implementation Details:** The description of how the spatial confidence map feature is generated for object detection is a bit vague. More details on the pooling strategy and its effect on performance would be useful.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** While the introduction of TAPC and NDCG for segment evaluation is useful, their relevance to real-world applications isn't fully articulated.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations are correct, and the experimental results are well-supported. However, the incremental nature of the improvements raises questions about the practical significance of the contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, though some sections (e.g., the regularization term in the latent e-SVM) could benefit from more detailed explanations.
* **Originality:** The e-SVM algorithm itself is original and represents a worthwhile extension of traditional SVMs. The application of this algorithm to weakly supervised tasks is also a novel contribution, but the tasks themselves aren't new.
* **Significance:** The results are important in demonstrating a viable approach to learning from weakly supervised data. However, the limited magnitude of the improvements suggests that the impact may be relatively modest. The potential for broader application across different domains is a positive factor.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for weakly supervised learning.
* The empirical results consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baselines.
* The approach is general and can be applied to various computer vision tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements shown are incremental and may not be sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like NeurIPS.
* The lack of comparison to more recent weakly-supervised learning methods weakens the claims of novelty.
* The regularization choice in the latent e-SVM could be better justified.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **above average** and represents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. While the improvements are not revolutionary, the e-SVM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated approach with strong empirical support.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

*  Expand the related work section to include more recent and relevant weakly-supervised learning methods.
*  Provide a more detailed justification for the choice of regularization term in the latent e-SVM and perhaps explore alternatives.
*  Clarify the implementation details of the spatial confidence map feature in object detection.
*  Discuss the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research, particularly regarding improving the magnitude of performance gains.
