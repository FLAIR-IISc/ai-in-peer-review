### Summary

This paper introduces a novel learning algorithm, the expectation loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where training labels are continuous values in the range [0, 1], representing a degree of "positiveness." The core idea is to treat the binary label of a sample as a Bernoulli random variable whose success probability is its given positiveness. The e-SVM loss is then the expectation of the standard SVM hinge loss over this random variable. This formulation elegantly bridges the gap between Support Vector Classification (SVC), which requires hard binary labels, and Support Vector Regression (SVR), which is not tailored for bounded-interval targets.

The authors present two versions of the model: (1) a basic e-SVM for the fully supervised case where the positiveness values (e.g., IoU with pixel-level masks) are known, and (2) a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings where positiveness is an unobserved latent variable (e.g., when only bounding box annotations are available). The latent model is trained using an alternating optimization scheme.

The proposed method is validated on two fundamental computer vision tasks: semantic segmentation and object detection, using the PASCAL VOC dataset. The paper introduces two new evaluation metrics for segment proposal classification (TAPC and NDCG) and shows that e-SVM outperforms SVC and SVR on this sub-task. More significantly, when integrated with the state-of-the-art R-CNN object detector, the features derived from the latent e-SVM model provide a substantial 1.5% improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on PASCAL VOC 2007, achieving a new state-of-the-art result at the time.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the expectation loss is mathematically correct and provides a principled extension of the SVM framework. The resulting objective for the basic e-SVM is equivalent to a weighted SVM, making it solvable with standard tools, which is a significant practical advantage. The latent variable model with alternating optimization is a standard and appropriate technique for the weakly supervised problem. The choice of a KL-divergence-based regularizer for the latent positiveness values is well-motivated, preventing the solution from degenerating to a simple latent SVM and encouraging it to stay close to a plausible initial estimate.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is strong.
    1.  The authors do not just evaluate on a final task but first perform a direct evaluation of the core contribution (segment classification), for which they astutely propose new and suitable metrics (TAPC, NDCG). This provides clear evidence that e-SVM is superior to SVC and SVR for this specific type of problem.
    2.  The application to object detection is particularly compelling. Improving a powerful and contemporary baseline like R-CNN is non-trivial, and the reported 1.5% mAP gain is a significant result that clearly demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.
    3.  The work is presented as a complete piece, from model formulation to extensive validation on challenging, large-scale benchmarks.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for the problem is clearly established in the introduction. The model is described logically, starting with the simpler supervised case before moving to the latent variable extension. The connection to and distinction from related work like weighted SVMs and Multiple Instance Learning is explained well. The figures, particularly the framework illustration in Figure 1 and the feature generation process in Figure 2, are helpful in understanding the overall system. The inclusion of pseudo-code for the latent e-SVM optimization (Algorithm 1) further enhances clarity and aids reproducibility.

While the core ideas are clear, providing more details on hyperparameter selection (e.g., the regularization parameters `λ` and `λ_R`, and the convergence criteria for the alternating optimization) would further improve the paper's completeness and make it easier for others to reproduce the results exactly.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel and original contribution to the field.

*   The central idea of the "expectation loss" for SVMs appears to be new. While it has connections to weighted SVMs, the derivation from a probabilistic interpretation of continuous labels is a novel and intuitive perspective that is well-suited to many problems in computer vision where ground truth overlap is naturally represented as a score between 0 and 1.
*   The paper tackles a specific, important, and previously under-addressed flavor of weak supervision—learning from proposals where correctness is a continuous, and possibly latent, variable. This is distinct from the more common MIL setting.
*   The proposed "spatial confidence map feature" is a simple but effective way to integrate segmentation-based cues into a bounding-box-based object detector, demonstrating a novel application of the learned segment classifiers.

### Significance

The work is highly significant and has clear potential for impact.

*   The problem of learning from imperfect or weak labels is of paramount importance, as it helps to alleviate the bottleneck of expensive manual annotation. This paper provides a practical and effective tool for leveraging bounding-box level supervision, which is far more abundant than pixel-level masks.
*   The demonstrated improvement over the state-of-the-art R-CNN model on a standard benchmark like PASCAL VOC 2007 is a strong indicator of the method's importance. It shows that incorporating segmentation-like reasoning can benefit even top-performing detection models.
*   The e-SVM algorithm itself is general and not limited to computer vision. It could be useful in any domain where supervision comes in the form of continuous confidence or agreement scores rather than hard binary labels. The ease of implementation for the supervised case further increases its potential for adoption by other researchers and practitioners.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The expectation loss is an elegant and effective method for training SVMs with continuous [0, 1] labels.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper shows consistent improvements over relevant baselines (SVC, SVR) and, most impressively, boosts the performance of a state-of-the-art object detector (R-CNN).
3.  **High Significance and Practicality:** The work addresses the critical problem of reducing annotation cost by learning from weak labels and provides a practical algorithm that achieves state-of-the-art results.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper does not provide any analysis of the convergence properties of the alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM. While the algorithm is likely to converge, a discussion of the quality of the local minimum would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The paper argues for the importance of the regularization term `R(u)` in the latent model. An ablation experiment directly comparing the performance with and without this regularizer (`λ_R = 0`) would have provided quantitative evidence for this claim.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical algorithm, supported by strong experimental results on significant tasks. It is a solid contribution to the field of machine learning and its application to computer vision.
