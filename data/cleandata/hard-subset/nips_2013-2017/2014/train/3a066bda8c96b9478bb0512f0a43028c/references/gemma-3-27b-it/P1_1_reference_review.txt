## Peer Review: Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel learning algorithm designed for weakly supervised learning scenarios where continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap) represent the degree to which a proposal corresponds to the true label, rather than binary labels. The e-SVM extends the standard SVM framework to handle this continuous feedback, and includes a latent variable version for situations where positiveness is unobserved (bounding box only supervision). The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection tasks, showing improvements over SVC and SVR baselines.  The core idea of weighting samples based on their "positiveness" rather than binarizing them is intuitive and addresses a significant challenge in weakly supervised learning.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of weakly supervised learning, acknowledging Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) and related approaches. The authors clearly articulate how their problem setting differs from both fully supervised learning and MIL, highlighting the intermediate nature of their approach where partial correctness is quantifiable. They also appropriately contrast e-SVM with weighted SVMs, noting the key distinction of weighting *labels* rather than data points. The cited literature is relevant and demonstrates awareness of existing work in related areas (segmentation, object detection, SVMs).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Weakly supervised learning is a crucial area for scaling machine learning to large datasets where full annotation is prohibitive.  The proposed approach tackles a specific and important aspect of this challenge – handling continuous feedback on proposal quality.
* **Novelty:** The e-SVM formulation, particularly the extension to latent variables for weak supervision, is a novel contribution.  The concept of weighting labels based on positiveness is a clever adaptation of the SVM framework.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on multiple tasks (segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, object detection).  The results on object detection, specifically the 1.5% average precision gain, are encouraging.
* **Generalizability:** The authors emphasize the generality of their approach, highlighting its compatibility with various segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers. This is a significant strength for potential adoption.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation of the e-SVM loss function is clear. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 for the latent e-SVM optimization is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a mathematical formulation, there is a lack of deeper theoretical analysis. For example, exploring the convergence properties of the latent e-SVM optimization algorithm, or providing insights into the conditions under which e-SVM outperforms SVC/SVR, would strengthen the contribution.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss hyperparameter tuning or sensitivity.  The choice of the regularization term in the latent e-SVM (using KL divergence) seems somewhat arbitrary, and the impact of its coefficient (λR) isn’t extensively explored.
* **Evaluation Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while solid, is primarily focused on PASCAL VOC datasets. Expanding the evaluation to other datasets (e.g., ImageNet for object detection) would increase the generalizability of the findings.
* **Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of each component of the framework.  For example, what is the impact of different feature types or segment proposal methods? How does the choice of the positiveness function *g(ui)* affect performance?
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Weakly Supervised Methods:** The paper primarily compares against SVC and SVR, which are less direct competitors for weakly supervised learning. Comparing against more recent, specialized weakly supervised learning approaches (beyond the mentioned MIL works) would provide a more comprehensive assessment.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial problem in weakly supervised learning with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines.
* Offers a flexible and generalizable framework applicable to various computer vision tasks.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks in-depth theoretical analysis.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Evaluation is primarily confined to PASCAL VOC datasets.
* Could benefit from more comprehensive ablation studies and comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. The e-SVM algorithm is a novel and promising approach for handling continuous feedback on proposal quality. The empirical results are encouraging, and the paper is well-written.  However, the lack of theoretical analysis and the limited scope of the evaluation prevent me from giving it a strongly positive recommendation. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically:

1.  Adding a discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity, especially for the latent e-SVM regularization term.
2.  Expanding the evaluation to at least one additional dataset (e.g., ImageNet for object detection).
3.  Conducting more detailed ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the framework.
4.  Including a comparison to more relevant state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.
5. Adding a brief discussion on the theoretical properties of the proposed method.
