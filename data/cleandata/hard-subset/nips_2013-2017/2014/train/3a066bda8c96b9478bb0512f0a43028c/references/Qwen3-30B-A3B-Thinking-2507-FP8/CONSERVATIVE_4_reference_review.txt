

# Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes an "Expectation Loss SVM" (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, where the label is a continuous "positiveness" value between 0 and 1 rather than binary labels. The authors claim their method outperforms SVC and SVR in semantic segmentation and object detection tasks when using either pixel-level or bounding box annotations.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

The paper addresses a relevant problem in computer vision: learning from weakly supervised data (e.g., bounding box annotations instead of pixel-level labels). The authors position their work as an extension of SVMs to handle continuous label values. However, their approach appears to be a variant of weighted SVMs rather than a fundamentally new method, as acknowledged in Section 2 where they cite related work on weighted SVMs. The paper's main contribution is a practical implementation of this variant, but it doesn't clearly distinguish itself from existing weighted SVM approaches or other weakly supervised learning methods.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a reasonable problem statement and experimental setup. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be applied to two important computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) with modest improvements over baselines.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Inadequate comparison to relevant prior work**: The paper fails to compare against state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, which are the standard for handling weak supervision in computer vision. Section 2 mentions MIL but doesn't demonstrate how e-SVM compares to MIL methods, which is a critical omission for a paper claiming to address weak supervision. The authors state that their problem setting "cannot be solved by MIL," but don't provide evidence for this claim or show how their method outperforms MIL approaches.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The performance gains reported are modest (1.5% AP improvement on object detection), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these gains are statistically significant or robust across different settings. The experiments are limited to PASCAL VOC datasets without testing on larger-scale datasets like ImageNet. There's no ablation study to demonstrate why e-SVM works better than existing methods or how sensitive the approach is to hyperparameters.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper presents the e-SVM model as a natural extension of SVMs, but doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why this particular formulation is better than alternatives. The connection to weighted SVMs is acknowledged, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how e-SVM differs from existing weighted SVM approaches or why it would be superior.

4. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims "significant" improvements, but the actual gains (1.5% AP) are relatively small compared to recent advances in object detection (e.g., 5-10% gains from major methodological changes). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this modest improvement warrants publication at NIPS.

5. **Limited scope of validation**: The paper only tests the method on two computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation and object detection) without demonstrating generalizability to other domains or problem settings. The claim that "our approach is general" is not substantiated with additional experiments.

**Overall Assessment**

While the paper addresses an important problem, the technical contribution appears to be incremental rather than novel. The authors present a variant of weighted SVMs that works well on two specific computer vision tasks with modest improvements over baselines, but they fail to demonstrate that this approach is fundamentally better than existing methods or that it represents a significant advance in the field.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A thorough comparison against state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods (particularly MIL approaches)
2. More rigorous statistical validation of the reported improvements
3. A clearer theoretical justification for why their approach differs from and improves upon existing weighted SVM methods
4. Additional experiments on larger datasets to demonstrate scalability

**Recommendation**

I recommend rejection. The paper presents a modest improvement on existing approaches without sufficient evidence of novelty or significant impact. The lack of comparison to relevant prior work (particularly MIL methods) is a critical weakness, and the claimed improvements are not substantial enough to justify publication at NIPS. The authors should either provide more compelling evidence of their method's superiority or demonstrate a fundamentally new approach to weakly supervised learning.
