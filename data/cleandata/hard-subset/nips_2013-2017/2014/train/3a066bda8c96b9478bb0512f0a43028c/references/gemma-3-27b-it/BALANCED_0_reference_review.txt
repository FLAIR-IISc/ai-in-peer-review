This paper proposes an Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically focusing on scenarios where continuous "positiveness" values (like IoU overlap) are available for segment proposals, rather than binary labels. The core idea is to treat the positiveness as a latent variable when ground truth is only weakly annotated (e.g., bounding boxes), and to directly incorporate the positiveness value into the loss function when fully annotated data is present. The paper demonstrates the efficacy of e-SVM on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, achieving competitive results and improvements over existing methods like SVC and SVR.

The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision: reducing the reliance on expensive pixel-level annotations. The proposed e-SVM is a sensible extension to standard SVM formulations, designed to handle the continuous nature of segment proposal quality. The clear articulation of the problem, the introduction of new evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG for segment proposal confidence prediction), and the empirical validation on established datasets are significant strengths. The iterative optimization procedure for the latent variable setting is well-defined, and the theoretical justification for the regularization term is sound. The claim of generality, applicable to diverse segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers, is also positive. The experimental results are well-presented, and the improvements over baseline methods, particularly in object detection, are encouraging. The inclusion of ablation studies comparing against multiple variants of SVC is helpful.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, while the motivation for handling continuous positiveness values is well-stated, the connection to existing work on weighted SVMs isn’t fully explored. The authors acknowledge the similarity but downplay the differences. A more thorough comparison of the loss functions and optimization procedures would be beneficial. The work cites weighted SVMs, but doesn’t discuss how e-SVM differs in its weighting mechanism—it's weighting *labels* instead of data points, which is a critical distinction.

Secondly, the experimental section, while extensive, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning. The choice of parameters for e-SVM, SVR, and SVC is not clearly explained, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparisons. The selection of the regularization parameter in the latent e-SVM is particularly crucial and needs further justification.

Thirdly, the improvements reported in object detection, while positive (1.5% average AP), are relatively modest. While stated as significant, the paper doesn't sufficiently analyze *why* e-SVM helps. Is it learning better segment features? Is it more robust to noisy segment proposals?  A deeper analysis would strengthen the claims. The spatial confidence map feature appears promising, but its contribution isn't fully isolated.

Fourthly, the paper lacks a discussion of computational complexity.  While the authors state that standard SVM solvers can be used, the iterative optimization procedure in the latent variable setting could be computationally expensive. A discussion of runtime performance and scalability would be valuable.

Finally, the writing could be more concise. Several sentences are unnecessarily verbose, and the presentation of some equations could be improved for clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in weakly supervised learning.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated algorithm (e-SVM).
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on established benchmarks.
*   Introduces new evaluation metrics for segment proposal confidence prediction.
*   Clear and logical presentation of the method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Insufficiently explores connections to related work on weighted SVMs.
*   Lacks detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning.
*   Modest improvements in object detection without detailed analysis.
*   Missing discussion of computational complexity.
*   Writing could be more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning. However, the aforementioned weaknesses prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I believe the paper has potential, but requires substantial revision to address the concerns raised. Specifically, a more thorough comparison to related work, a detailed discussion of experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning, and a more in-depth analysis of the object detection results are necessary. 

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.  The core idea is strong, and with significant revisions addressing the weaknesses, this paper could be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
