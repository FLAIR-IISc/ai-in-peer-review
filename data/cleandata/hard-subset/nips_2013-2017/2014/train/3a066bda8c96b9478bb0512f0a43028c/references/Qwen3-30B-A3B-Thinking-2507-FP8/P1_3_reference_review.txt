

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel approach for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values in [0,1] rather than binary. The authors address a significant problem in computer vision: the high cost of pixel-level annotations for large datasets. The e-SVM model extends standard SVMs by treating labels as random variables with success probabilities derived from "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU overlap). The paper presents two variants: a basic e-SVM for strong supervision (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weak supervision (where positiveness is unobserved). The authors apply their method to semantic segmentation and object detection, demonstrating improvements over SVC and SVR baselines on PASCAL VOC datasets.

The paper relates well to previous work in weakly supervised learning, acknowledging the limitations of MIL (which doesn't apply to their problem setting) and differentiating from weighted SVMs (which weight examples rather than labels). It builds upon recent advances in segment-based classification and object detection, particularly referencing Carreira et al.'s work on semantic segmentation and Girshick et al.'s RCNN for object detection.

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated problem**: The paper correctly identifies the growing need for weakly supervised learning as datasets scale, where pixel-level annotations become prohibitively expensive.

2. **Theoretical foundation**: The e-SVM model is well-motivated and theoretically sound, extending SVMs with a probabilistic interpretation that naturally handles continuous labels. The derivation of the expectation loss function is clear and logically follows from the problem statement.

3. **Novel evaluation metrics**: The introduction of TAPC and NDCG as evaluation criteria for segment classification is a valuable contribution, providing more appropriate metrics for the specific problem.

4. **Strong experimental results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SVC and SVR across multiple tasks and settings. The 1.5% average precision gain on PASCAL VOC 2007 object detection is significant given the competitive nature of this benchmark.

5. **Practical applicability**: The method is general and can be integrated with existing computer vision pipelines (e.g., RCNN), making it immediately useful to practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison to recent weakly supervised methods**: The related work section focuses primarily on SVC and SVR, but doesn't adequately compare with contemporary weakly supervised learning methods (e.g., the recent work by Zhang et al. on weakly supervised semantic segmentation or the multiple instance learning approaches that have evolved beyond traditional MIL).

2. **Ad-hoc regularization choice**: The regularization term R(u) is justified as convex and minimizing at u=ubb, but the paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical rationale for choosing the KL divergence-based regularization over other convex alternatives. The authors should provide more justification or ablation studies on this choice.

3. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: While the optimization algorithm is presented, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence properties for the latent e-SVM. It's unclear how the iterative optimization (Algorithm 1) behaves in practice.

4. **Limited experimental depth**: The object detection results report only average AP improvements without per-class analysis or error analysis. The paper also doesn't provide computational complexity analysis, which would be important for large-scale applications.

5. **Unclear novelty**: The core idea of handling continuous labels through an expectation-based loss has some similarity to existing approaches (e.g., the "probabilistic SVM" concept). The paper could more clearly articulate how this differs from previous work.

## Quality and Clarity Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported experimental results. The methodology is clearly described, though some details (particularly regarding the spatial confidence map feature construction) could be clarified. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though the justification for the specific choice of regularization term could be strengthened.

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the weakly supervised learning literature by addressing a specific problem setting (continuous labels in [0,1]) that isn't well-handled by existing methods. While the core idea of using continuous labels has some similarity to previous work, the specific formulation and application to computer vision tasks are novel. The significance is demonstrated by the consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks, particularly the 1.5% AP gain on a challenging object detection benchmark.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison to recent work and theoretical justification, these don't undermine the overall contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the related work section to include recent weakly supervised learning methods beyond SVC and SVR.

2. Provide more justification for the choice of R(u) and include ablation studies on the regularization term.

3. Add per-class results and error analysis for the object detection experiments.

4. Include computational complexity analysis to help readers understand scalability.

5. Clarify how the e-SVM differs from previous probabilistic SVM approaches.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised learning, and with the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS proceedings. The approach is general and could be applied to many other computer vision tasks beyond those tested here.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pro:**
- Addresses an important real-world problem (reducing annotation cost)
- Clear theoretical foundation with sound mathematical formulation
- Demonstrated significant improvements on standard benchmarks
- General approach applicable to multiple computer vision tasks

**Con:**
- Insufficient comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods
- Ad-hoc choice of regularization term without sufficient justification
- Limited experimental depth in some areas (per-class analysis, computational complexity)
- Some overlap with existing probabilistic SVM concepts

Overall, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I believe it makes a meaningful contribution to the field.
