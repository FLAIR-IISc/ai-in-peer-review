## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel learning algorithm, the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), designed for learning from weakly supervised data where labels aren't precise (e.g., bounding boxes instead of pixel-level segmentation).  The core idea is to treat the “positiveness” of proposals (like segment overlap with the ground truth) as a random variable, and formulate a loss function that expects this variable.  The authors demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in segment proposal classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, outperforming standard SVM and SVR approaches.  They provide both theoretical justification and empirical validation on standard datasets (PASCAL VOC).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Weakly supervised learning is crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets where full annotation is impractical.
* **Novel Formulation:** The e-SVM formulation is a clever extension of standard SVMs to handle continuous, probabilistic labels, providing a more nuanced approach than simple thresholding used in existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the e-SVM and the latent variable extension.  The connection to weighted SVMs is also discussed, providing context within existing literature.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents convincing experimental results on semantic segmentation and object detection, consistently outperforming baseline methods (SVC, SVR) in multiple scenarios (fully supervised vs. weakly supervised). The 1.5% average improvement in object detection is significant.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the flexibility of their approach, highlighting its compatibility with various segment proposal methods, features, and base detectors.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the e-SVM formulation *is* novel, it's arguably an incremental improvement on existing techniques rather than a paradigm shift.  It’s a smart adaptation of SVMs, but doesn't fundamentally alter the landscape of weakly supervised learning.
* **Limited Scope of Baselines:** While comparisons to SVC and SVR are relevant, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods beyond those mentioned in the related work (e.g., methods leveraging consistency constraints, or more sophisticated MIL approaches).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization term in the latent e-SVM (Equation 3) appears crucial for performance, yet the justification for its specific form (KL divergence) isn't fully explored. A sensitivity analysis to hyperparameter settings for this regularization term is missing.
* **Computational Cost:** The iterative optimization procedure for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) could be computationally expensive, particularly for large datasets. A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability would be valuable.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper could benefit from more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components.  For example, how much performance is gained by incorporating the spatial confidence map feature in object detection versus simply concatenating features?



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

*   **Quality (8/10):**  The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experiments. The theoretical formulation is clear. However, the incremental nature and lack of extensive comparison to recent methods slightly reduce the quality score.
*   **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is very well written and easy to understand. The organization is logical, and the mathematical notation is clear.
*   **Originality (7/10):** The e-SVM formulation is novel, but it’s an evolution of existing SVM-based methods. The originality is moderate.
*   **Significance (7/10):** The results are significant, particularly the improvement in object detection.  However, the impact may be limited by the incremental nature of the work and the lack of broader comparisons to advanced weakly supervised learning techniques.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning (weakly supervised learning).
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm (e-SVM).
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on standard datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Con:**

*   Incremental novelty – doesn’t represent a major breakthrough.
*   Limited comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised learning methods, a discussion of computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity, and some additional ablation studies would significantly strengthen the paper.  While not a revolutionary contribution, the e-SVM is a solid and valuable addition to the literature on weakly supervised learning.
