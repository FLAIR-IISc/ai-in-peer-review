

# Peer Review: "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

## Summary
This paper introduces Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where training labels are continuous "positiveness" values (e.g., IoU scores) rather than binary labels. The authors propose two variants: a basic e-SVM for fully supervised settings (where positiveness is observed) and a latent e-SVM for weakly supervised settings (where positiveness is unobserved, treated as latent variables). The method is evaluated on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks using PASCAL VOC datasets, with claims of superior performance over SVC and SVR.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear algorithmic formulation. The theoretical foundation is reasonable, extending SVMs to handle continuous labels through an expectation loss function. The optimization procedure for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is well-described and appears convex under the chosen regularization. However, the paper lacks critical theoretical analysis: it doesn't prove convergence properties of the latent e-SVM optimization or rigorously justify why their specific regularization term (Equation 3) is superior to alternatives. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both segmentation and detection tasks, but the performance gains (1.5% AP improvement on detection) are modest for NIPS. The paper also lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the problem setting and method. The motivation for moving beyond SVC (which requires arbitrary thresholding) and SVR (which isn't designed for bounded interval outputs) is compelling. However, some technical details could be improved: the connection between their approach and weighted SVMs (Section 2) is superficial, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why IoU is the optimal positiveness measure rather than other metrics. The experimental setup is adequately described, but hyperparameter choices (λ, λR) are not justified.

## Originality
The core idea of using expectation loss for continuous labels is somewhat novel within the SVM framework, but the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The authors claim their problem setting is "in the middle" between standard MIL and full supervision, but this distinction isn't sufficiently justified against recent weakly supervised learning work (e.g., Vezhnevets et al. on MIM, or Liu et al. on weakly-supervised dual clustering). The paper also fails to adequately differentiate itself from related work on weighted SVMs (e.g., Lapin et al. on "Learning using Privileged Information" which uses similar weighting approaches). While the application to segmentation and detection is relevant, the method itself is a straightforward extension of standard SVMs.

## Significance
The problem addressed (reducing need for expensive pixel-level annotations) is important and timely. However, the significance of the contribution is undermined by modest performance gains (1.5% AP improvement) and lack of substantial theoretical novelty. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance, but the improvement over RCNN is marginal and doesn't represent a major advance. The method's flexibility to work with any segment proposal detectors and feature extractors is a positive, but the paper doesn't demonstrate this with diverse applications beyond segmentation and detection.

## Critical Weaknesses
1. **Overstated contributions:** The paper claims "the most important and useful" weakly supervised problem without sufficient evidence, and overstates the significance of modest performance gains.

2. **Insufficient comparison to relevant work:** The paper doesn't compare with recent weakly supervised methods that might be more directly applicable (e.g., Li et al. 2013, Vezhnevets et al. 2010), which would better contextualize their contribution.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper doesn't rigorously justify why expectation loss is superior to simpler alternatives like weighted SVMs with weights proportional to positiveness.

4. **Modest results:** The 1.5% AP improvement on PASCAL VOC 2007 is not impressive for a top-tier conference like NIPS, especially given the paper's claims.

## Strengths
- Clear problem formulation addressing an important practical challenge in computer vision
- Well-organized presentation with comprehensive experiments across two major tasks
- Practical implementation details that would enable reproduction
- The expectation loss concept is conceptually clean and well-motivated

## Recommendation
This paper presents a reasonable approach to a meaningful problem, but the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The method is technically sound, but the theoretical novelty is limited, and the empirical gains are modest. The paper would be significantly stronger with (1) more rigorous theoretical analysis, (2) comprehensive comparison to recent weakly supervised learning methods, (3) ablation studies to isolate component contributions, and (4) more convincing evidence of the method's significance.

**Overall assessment:** The paper is acceptable but not exceptional for NIPS. It addresses a relevant problem with a sound technical approach, but the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and impact to warrant acceptance at this top-tier venue. The authors should substantially strengthen the theoretical foundation and provide more compelling evidence of the method's advantages over existing approaches.

**Decision: Reject** (with the understanding that the authors could strengthen the paper significantly with major revisions addressing the critical weaknesses noted above).
