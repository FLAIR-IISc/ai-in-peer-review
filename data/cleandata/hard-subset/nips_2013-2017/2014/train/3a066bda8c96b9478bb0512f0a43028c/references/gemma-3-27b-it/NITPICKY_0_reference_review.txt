## Review of "Learning From Weakly Supervised Data by The Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM) algorithm"

This paper proposes the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), an SVM-based approach designed for learning from weakly supervised data, specifically where a continuous “positiveness” value indicates the degree of correctness of a proposal instead of a binary label. The authors address both scenarios – where positiveness is observed (strong supervision) and where it is latent (weak supervision) – and demonstrate its effectiveness on semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming SVC and SVR baselines.

While the core idea of weighting samples based on their “positiveness” is intuitive and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a concerning level of detail-oriented shortcomings that, cumulatively, detract from its overall impact and raise questions about the rigor of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** Addressing weakly supervised learning is highly relevant given the cost of large-scale labeled datasets.
* **Intuitive Approach:** The concept of incorporating positiveness as a weight within an SVM framework is logically sound.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods (SVC, SVR) on several tasks.
* **Generality:** The claim of adaptability to different segment proposal detectors, features, and classifiers is appealing.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** While the specific formulation of the “expectation loss” is presented, the fundamental concept of weighting samples based on confidence or estimated correctness is not entirely novel in machine learning. The paper fails to adequately position its contribution within the broader landscape of weighted SVMs and related approaches beyond superficial mentions.
* **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The motivation for choosing the Bernoulli distribution to model the label is weak.  A more detailed justification for this distributional assumption, and a discussion of alternatives, would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the method.
* **Implementation Details are Vague:** The paper frequently alludes to “simple” choices (e.g., µi = ui) without providing sufficient explanation. Why is a simple identity mapping the optimal choice?  What are the implications of using more sophisticated functions? This lack of exploration diminishes the perceived robustness of the approach.
* **Experimental Rigor Concerns:** The experiments, while extensive, are plagued by a series of minor but accumulating issues:
    * **Evaluation Metrics:** Introducing *two* novel evaluation metrics (TAPC and NDCG) is unusual and warrants a much more thorough justification. Why are existing metrics inadequate? The paper doesn’t convincingly explain this.  Furthermore, the interpretation and significance of these metrics are not fully explained.
    * **Parameter Tuning:** Details regarding hyperparameter tuning (e.g., regularization parameters) are sparse.  Were these parameters chosen using cross-validation? If so, details are missing. This raises concerns about overfitting and the generalizability of the results.
    * **Statistical Significance:** The paper presents percentage improvements without reporting statistical significance tests.  Given the potential for random fluctuations, demonstrating statistically significant improvements is crucial.
    * **Baseline Implementations:** The details of the SVC and SVR implementations are not fully disclosed.  Were the same kernels and regularization parameters used as in e-SVM?  Any discrepancies could confound the comparison.
    * **Feature Engineering:** While the authors mention using additive kernels in the object detection experiments, they lack detail on implementation, particularly, how the feature concatenation with RCNN features is handled.
* **Formatting and Writing Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of math mode, missing punctuation) and stylistic issues detract from the professionalism of the paper. The writing is occasionally verbose and could benefit from more concise phrasing. For example, the repeated emphasis on “simple” choices feels unnecessary.
* **Citation Errors:** Several citations are missing year information (e.g., [6], [7], [8]).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in computer vision.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods.
* Presents a logically sound and potentially useful approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of substantial novelty.
* Insufficient theoretical justification.
* Concerns regarding experimental rigor and reproducibility.
* Numerous minor but distracting flaws in presentation and formatting.
* Weak connection to related work beyond surface-level comparison.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting core idea, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to a combination of insufficient novelty, lack of theoretical depth, and concerns about the rigor and reproducibility of the experimental results. The accumulation of minor flaws significantly detracts from the overall quality of the work. While the authors demonstrate improvements over existing baselines, the extent to which these improvements are reliable and generalizable remains questionable.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  I believe a major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for this paper to be considered for acceptance in the future. Specifically, a more thorough theoretical analysis, a more rigorous experimental validation with appropriate statistical testing, and a more careful presentation of the results are required.
