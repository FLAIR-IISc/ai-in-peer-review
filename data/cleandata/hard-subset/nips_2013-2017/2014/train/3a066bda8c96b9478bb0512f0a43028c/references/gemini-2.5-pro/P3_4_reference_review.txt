This paper introduces the Expectation Loss SVM (e-SVM), a novel algorithm for learning from weakly supervised data where labels are continuous values in [0, 1], referred to as "positiveness." The core idea is to re-formulate the standard SVM hinge loss as an expectation over a latent binary label, whose probability is a function of the known positiveness score. This elegant formulation results in a weighted SVM-like objective that can be solved with standard solvers. The authors extend this to a latent e-SVM for scenarios where the positiveness is unknown (e.g., only bounding box annotations are available), treating it as a latent variable and solving via alternating optimization. The method is validated on semantic segmentation and object detection, where it is used to learn confidence scores for segment proposals. Notably, when integrated with the R-CNN detector, it yields a significant improvement on the PASCAL VOC 2007 benchmark.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The central idea of the e-SVM is clever and theoretically appealing. It provides a principled way to handle continuous, bounded labels (like IoU scores) that is more natural than the common alternatives: binarizing the labels via an arbitrary threshold for SVC, or using SVR which doesn't inherently respect the [0, 1] bounded nature of the target. The expectation-based loss is a clean way to bridge this gap.

2.  **Practical Relevance and Significance:** The problem addressed is of high practical importance. Obtaining dense pixel-level annotations is a major bottleneck in computer vision. Methods that can effectively learn from weaker supervision, such as bounding boxes, are extremely valuable. The paper's focus on learning from segment proposals with IoU-like scores is a direct and useful application. The latent e-SVM, in particular, directly tackles the common scenario of having only bounding box supervision.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its approach on challenging, standard benchmarks. The most compelling result is the 1.5% average precision improvement over the state-of-the-art R-CNN detector on PASCAL VOC 2007. Improving upon such a strong baseline is non-trivial and clearly demonstrates the value of the learned segmentation cues. The direct comparisons against SVC and SVR also convincingly show the superiority of the e-SVM formulation for the segment classification task.

4.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The proposed method is general and can be easily integrated into existing pipelines. A significant advantage is that the final optimization problem for the basic e-SVM can be solved using standard, off-the-shelf linear SVM solvers, lowering the barrier to adoption and reproduction. The proposed spatial confidence map is a straightforward and effective way to convert segment-level scores into a feature for region-based detectors.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Details of the Latent e-SVM Optimization:** The alternating optimization for the latent e-SVM (Algorithm 1) is non-convex. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this procedure. For example:
    *   How sensitive is the algorithm to the initialization of `u`? It is initialized with the IoU with the bounding box (`ubb`), which is a sensible choice, but what happens if the initial estimate is poor?
    *   Does the algorithm converge in practice, and how many iterations are typically required? A plot of the objective function value vs. iteration would be informative.
    *   The choice of the KL-divergence-based regularizer `R(u)` is presented without much justification. While it is a reasonable choice, a discussion of why this form was chosen over others (e.g., a simple L2 penalty `||u - ubb||^2`) or an ablation study would make the design choice more convincing.

2.  **Clarity on Experimental Contributions:** The improvement over R-CNN is the paper's strongest result. However, Section 5.3 mentions modifications to the bounding box regression method and the use of additive kernel maps. It is not perfectly clear if the reported 1.5% AP gain is solely due to the addition of the e-SVM spatial confidence map feature, or if these other modifications also contribute. An ablation study that isolates the contribution of the e-SVM feature would be crucial to accurately assess its impact.

3.  **Introduction of New Evaluation Metrics:** The paper introduces two new evaluation criteria, TAPC and NDCG, for the segment proposal classification task. While evaluating the model on its direct output is a good principle, the introduction of new, non-standard metrics requires strong justification. It makes it difficult to compare these intermediate results to other potential methods in the literature. It would be clearer to either stick to the final task-based evaluation (segmentation/detection accuracy) or use established metrics for ranking/regression if applicable. The justification for these specific metrics over others could be expanded.

4.  **Discussion of the Function `g(ui)`:** The paper sets `g(ui) = ui`, which maps the positiveness score directly to the probability of the positive class. This is the simplest choice, but it is a strong assumption. Have the authors considered or experimented with other monotonic functions, such as a sigmoid, that might better model a probabilistic relationship? A brief discussion of the implications of this design choice would be welcome.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The core e-SVM formulation is solid and the experimental evaluation is performed on relevant, challenging tasks. However, it could be improved with more detailed analysis of the latent variable optimization and ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components in the object detection experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The core ideas are presented clearly in Section 3. Some details regarding optimization and experimental setup could be clarified as noted above.
*   **Originality:** The work is original. The expectation-based loss for handling continuous labels in SVMs is a novel contribution. While it builds upon concepts from weighted SVMs and latent SVMs, the specific formulation and its application to weakly supervised learning from segment proposals are new and insightful.
*   **Significance:** The significance is high. The paper addresses a critical problem in machine learning and computer vision. By providing a principled and effective method for learning from weak supervision and demonstrating a clear improvement on a state-of-the-art object detection system, this work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper with a novel, elegant, and practically useful contribution. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed in a final version. The work presents a clear advance in learning from weak supervision and its empirical success is compelling.
