Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures (Eq. 4). The model distinguishes depth in structure (tree recursion) from depth in space (stacking), akin to recent work on deep recurrent networks, and shows how stacking can create a hierarchy of representations across layers while retaining shared recursive composition within each layer. The authors also “untie” parameters between leaf and internal nodes (Eq. 3), enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and large, fixed pretrained word embeddings, and employ dropout applied to the hidden states shared across all nodes in a layer. Training uses AdaGrad, early stopping, and L2 penalties on weights and activations for stability.

Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) evaluate fine-grained (5-way) and binary sentiment classification. The paper compares shallow vs. deep recursive nets under fixed parameter budgets, and against prior baselines including RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. The authors report that (i) stacking improves over shallow tree models with the same parameter count, (ii) an untied shallow RNN with ReLU, dropout, and fixed pretrained embeddings already improves over earlier RNN baselines, and (iii) deep RNNs achieve new state of the art on SST. Qualitative analyses suggest different layers capture different aspects of composition (e.g., layer-specific sensitivity to intensity vs. polarity), supported by input perturbation and nearest-neighbor inspections.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The modeling extension is well-defined and straightforward to implement with backpropagation through structure and layers. The training setup is reasonable, and the use of dropout and AdaGrad is appropriate. The qualitative analyses are informative though anecdotal.
- Empirical support: The paper provides compelling evidence that depth-in-space helps, showing improvements at equal parameter counts and a depth sweep with best performance at 2–4 layers depending on the width. However, some confounds remain (see weaknesses): ablation for the individual contributions (untied leaves vs. ReLU vs. dropout vs. pretrained embeddings vs. stacking) is limited, and fairness of comparisons to external baselines is not always clear. Statistical significance and multiple runs are not reported.
- Experimental details are generally sufficient, including optimization, initialization (Whh ≈ 0.5I), and regularization. More detail on compute cost and parameter counts vs. baselines would aid reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for separating depth in structure from depth in space is articulated, and the architecture is easy to follow. Figures (not provided here) are referenced helpfully. Some reporting gaps remain (see below), but overall the exposition is strong.

Originality:
Stacking recursive layers over tree structures is a natural but nontrivial extension of stacked recurrent nets to trees. The untying of leaf vs. internal parameters and the use of ReLUs/dropout in tree models are sensible innovations for this setting. While components are drawn from known techniques, their combination for tree-structured composition is novel and timely for NIPS 2014.

Significance:
Achieving state of the art on SST with a relatively simple stacked architecture is valuable. If the gains hold under careful controls, this is a meaningful advance that is likely to influence subsequent work on tree-based deep models. The qualitative analyses provide intuition about what layers learn and may inspire follow-up research.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled extension of deep stacking to recursive/tree models.
- Strong empirical performance; stacking improves over shallow models at fixed parameter budgets.
- Sound engineering choices (ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad, untied leaf/internal transforms) that make tree models practical and performant.
- Insightful qualitative analysis suggesting layer-wise specialization (intensity vs. polarity, syntactic vs. semantic similarity).

Weaknesses and questions:
- Fairness and controls:
  - External resources: The model uses fixed 300d embeddings trained on ∼100B words. Do all baselines (RNTN, DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) use comparable pretrained embeddings or external data? If not, please report variants with/without pretrained embeddings to isolate their effect.
  - Supervision regime: The proposed model uses phrase-level supervision (all nodes). Some baselines (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) may use only sentence-level labels; this can advantage tree models. Please clarify and, if possible, compare to sentence-level-only training of your model.
  - Parameter count: You control parameters across your own depth variants, but do not report parameter counts vs. RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN. Please add exact counts to ensure fair SOTA claims.
- Ablations: The paper attributes gains to stacking and dropout, but the only quantitative ablation cited (L2 vs. dropout) also changes other factors. Please include ablations for:
  - With/without stacking (depth=1), with identical preprocessing and pretrained embeddings;
  - With/without untying leaves/internals;
  - ReLU vs. tanh;
  - With/without pretrained embeddings; with vs. without fine-tuning.
- Variance and significance: Report mean±std over multiple random seeds and significance tests; current results may be within variance for SST.
- Training/runtime: No complexity or wall-clock comparison is provided. Please quantify training time and memory vs. shallow trees and RNTN.
- Deeper models degrade when narrowed to match parameter budgets. It would be informative to also test deeper models at constant width to separate width vs. depth effects.
- Robustness to parse errors: Since the approach relies on parses, discuss performance under noisy parses and compare to parse-free models.
- Output connections: You connect supervision only to the top layer. A comparison to deep supervision (loss at all layers) would be useful, especially for deeper stacks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a comprehensive ablation table isolating the contributions of stacking, untying, ReLU, dropout, and pretrained embeddings.
- Report exact parameter counts and external data usage for all baselines; add variants to normalize these factors where possible.
- Add multiple runs with confidence intervals; include McNemar or bootstrap tests on SST test set.
- Include computational costs (training/inference time, memory) and convergence behavior as depth increases.
- Add error analysis on negation, intensifiers, and scope to corroborate the qualitative claims with quantitative probes.
- Explore sentence-level-only training to compare with non-tree baselines, and/or add a parse-free stacked RNN baseline for apples-to-apples.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated architectural contribution.
- Empirically strong with new SOTA on a competitive benchmark.
- Clear writing and useful qualitative insights.
- Likely to be adopted and extended by the community.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete ablations; some gains may come from pretrained embeddings/dropout rather than stacking.
- Fairness of comparisons to baselines is not fully controlled (external resources, supervision granularity, parameter counts).
- Lack of statistical significance and multi-run reporting.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a solid and timely extension of recursive neural models with promising empirical results. Addressing the ablation and fairness issues would strengthen it to a full accept.
