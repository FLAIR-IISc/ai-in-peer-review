

This paper proposes a novel "deep recursive neural network" (deep RNN) architecture that stacks multiple recursive layers to better model compositionality in language. The main claim is that this deep architecture outperforms shallow recursive networks with the same number of parameters and previous state-of-the-art methods on fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).

The authors provide strong experimental support for their claims. They demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various network depths and widths, showing that deeper networks (up to 4 layers) outperform shallower counterparts while maintaining the same parameter count. The quantitative results (Table 1) clearly show their model achieves new state-of-the-art performance on both fine-grained and binary sentiment classification tasks, surpassing baselines including multiplicative RNN variants and paragraph vectors. The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) effectively illustrate how different layers capture distinct aspects of compositionality, with earlier layers focusing on word-level features and later layers capturing more abstract sentiment relationships.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by bridging the conceptual gap between depth in structure (traditional recursive networks) and depth in space (stacked layers), building on recent work in deep recurrent networks. The methodology is well-justified, particularly the untying of leaf and internal node representations, which enables the use of rectifier activations and better handling of pretrained word vectors. The experimental design is thorough, with appropriate baselines, careful regularization, and comprehensive evaluation metrics.

A minor limitation is that the authors use fixed word vectors rather than fine-tuning them, which could potentially yield further improvements. Additionally, the paper focuses solely on sentiment analysis, so the broader applicability of the architecture to other compositional NLP tasks isn't thoroughly explored.

Overall, this is a technically sound, well-executed paper with significant novelty in extending recursive neural networks. The results are clearly supported by rigorous experiments, and the qualitative analyses provide valuable insights into how the deep architecture processes linguistic compositionality. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of compositional modeling in NLP, with clear implications for future work on hierarchical representation learning. I recommend acceptance.
