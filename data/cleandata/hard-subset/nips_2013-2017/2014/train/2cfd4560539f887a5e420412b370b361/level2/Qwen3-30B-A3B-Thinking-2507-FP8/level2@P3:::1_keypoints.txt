- Proposes deep recursive neural networks (stacked recursive layers) to model linguistic compositionality.  
- Distinguishes structural depth (tree recursion) from spatial depth (layer stacking) and argues spatial depth enables hierarchical representations.  
- Applies architecture to fine-grained sentiment analysis on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results, with best deep RNN at 49.5% vs prior Paragraph Vectors 47.6%.  
- Quantitative results show consistent improvements with increased depth up to four layers.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest each layer captures distinct compositional aspects.  
- Paper is high quality and clearly written with precise architectural definitions (Eqs. 3–5).  
- Methodology is technically sound and experiments are thorough and well-validated.  
- Ablation study rigorously demonstrates deeper networks outperform parameter-equivalent shallower models.  
- Experimental design follows best practices (fixed pretrained word vectors, dropout, AdaGrad, early stopping).  
- Originality is strong: novel stacking mechanism bridges prior recursive and deep RNN work.  
- Untying leaf/internal node parameters is justified for rectifier compatibility and parameter reduction.  
- Significance is moderate: strong for supervised sentiment tasks but broader applicability is not explored.  
- Computational cost of stacking layers is not discussed, though parameter-equivalent experiments partially mitigate scalability concerns.  
- Minor weaknesses: lack of explicit depth-vs-width tradeoff analysis and explanation for performance drop beyond four layers/with wider nets.  
- Minor weakness: need clearer justification of why untying isn’t applied to deeper-layer matrices (V^(i)).  
- Suggested revisions: expand depth-width tradeoff analysis, clarify deeper-layer untying rationale, and briefly address computational overhead.  
- Overall: technically sound, novel contribution with strong empirical support and interpretable layer specialization; minor issues are easily addressable.
