This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure” of tree-structured models. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal node transformations to allow word and phrase spaces to differ, enabling ReLU activations and dropout. Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way fine-grained sentiment, deep RNNs outperform shallow recursive nets with matched parameter budgets, as well as RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vector, and DCNN baselines, attaining state of the art. Exploratory analyses suggest layer specialization: lower layers track lexical content/syntax, intermediate layers capture intensity, and the top layer focuses on sentiment polarity. The work situates itself against prior recursive models for compositionality (Socher et al.) and deep recurrent architectures that distinguish depth in time vs. space (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), bringing the latter idea to tree structures.

Quality: The model is clearly specified with equations, training details (AdaGrad, dropout, initialization), and an evaluation protocol. Results are compelling, including matched-parameter comparisons and qualitative diagnostics. However, statistical significance, variance across runs, and ablations (depth vs. ReLU vs. dropout vs. untying vs. pretraining) are missing, making it hard to isolate which design choices drive gains. Fairness of comparisons that rely on published numbers (e.g., PV, DCNN) is not fully discussed.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The distinction between depths, the untying rationale, and the training setup are explained clearly. Figures/tables (referred) seem informative; minor additions (explicit parameter counts per configuration) would aid reproducibility.

Originality: Stacking recursive layers is a natural, but to my knowledge underexplored, extension of tree RNNs. The combination with leaf/internal untying and ReLU/dropout is incremental yet effective. Related work is largely covered.

Significance: Achieving SOTA on SST and showing depth-vs-width tradeoffs for tree models are valuable. Practical usefulness is plausible for parse-based NLP tasks, though demonstrated only on SST.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated extension of recursive models; clear architectural contribution.
- Strong empirical gains with matched parameter budgets; new SOTA on SST.
- Insightful qualitative analyses indicating layer specialization.
- Solid training recipe that others can adopt.

Cons for acceptance:
- Single dataset; no evidence beyond sentiment or beyond English binary trees.
- No statistical tests or run-to-run variability; limited ablations hinder causal attribution.
- Comparisons rely on reported numbers; training-data/regime parity not fully established.
- No analysis of computational cost or inference speed.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add ablations isolating depth, untying, ReLU, dropout, and embedding fine-tuning; report multiple seeds with confidence intervals.
- Evaluate on additional tree-structured tasks/datasets (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, other sentiment corpora) and discuss parsing dependency/cost.
- Provide parameter counts per configuration and, if possible, release code to aid reproducibility.
- Consider analyses with outputs attached to intermediate layers to further probe layer roles.

Overall, a solid, timely contribution with clear promise; addressing the experimental rigor and breadth would strengthen it further.
