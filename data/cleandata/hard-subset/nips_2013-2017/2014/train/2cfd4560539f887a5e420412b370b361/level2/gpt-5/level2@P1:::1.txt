Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers “in space” on top of the depth that already arises “in structure” from tree composition. The authors also introduce an untied parametrization that distinguishes leaves from internal nodes, enabling the use of pretrained word vectors and rectified linear units. The architecture passes each node’s hidden state to its counterpart in the next layer, while within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up along a parse tree. The model is trained with Adagrad, dropout (with shared masks per sentence/layer), and a small activation/weight penalty to stabilize ReLU dynamics. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), the deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art performance on fine-grained sentiment classification and competitive/better binary results, outperforming shallow recursive nets with matched parameter budgets, RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Exploratory analyses (input perturbations; nearest-neighbor phrases per layer) suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on recursive nets for composition (Goller & Kuchler; Socher et al.) and follows the “depth in time vs depth in space” distinction for recurrent networks (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen) by bringing spatial stacking to tree-structured models. Compared to MV-RNN and RNTN, the novelty is architectural (stacking) and practical (untied leaf/internal parameters with ReLUs and dropout). The comparisons to Paragraph Vectors and DCNN situate the contribution among contemporaneous sentence representation methods.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architectural extension of recursive nets; stacking is simple yet nontrivial to implement with backprop through structure across layers.
- Thoughtful training choices (ReLU, dropout with shared masks, Adagrad, identity-plus-noise init) and detailed reporting that should aid reproduction.
- Careful comparisons including parameter-matched shallow vs deep models, demonstrating depth benefits independent of model size.
- New state-of-the-art on SST fine-grained classification; competitive binary results without training a separate binary model.
- Insightful qualitative analyses indicating layer-wise specialization (e.g., intensity vs polarity) and syntactic vs semantic roles at different depths.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope is narrow: a single dataset and task (SST sentiment), both parse-tree based with phrase-level supervision. It is unclear how broadly the gains translate to other structured NLP tasks or to sentences without reliable parses.
- Baseline fairness: several improvements (ReLU, dropout, leaf/internal untying, fixed pretrained embeddings) are only applied to the proposed model. Prior baselines’ published numbers may not reflect these improvements; re-implementing RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN with matched training tricks would strengthen the claims.
- Limited ablations: while the paper notes gains from pretrained vectors, untying, and dropout, there is no systematic ablation quantifying each factor (tanh vs ReLU; with/without dropout; with/without untying; fine-tuning vs fixed embeddings; output tapped from last vs all layers).
- No variance/significance analysis or multiple random restarts. Given small absolute differences in some settings, reporting variability would be helpful.
- Computational aspects (training time, memory, scaling with depth/width and tree size) are not discussed; stacked tree models can be costly.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The parameter-controlled depth comparison is particularly convincing. Training details are thorough. However, broader empirical validation and more systematic ablations would better isolate where the gains come from.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The mathematical description of stacked layers and untying is easy to follow, and implementation details (dropout sharing, initialization) are explicit. A brief complexity analysis and pseudo-code for the interleaved backprop through structure and layers would further aid readers.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is bringing “depth in space” to recursive models and demonstrating its benefits. The leaf/internal untying paired with ReLUs is a practical but incremental modification. The qualitative analyses are a nice addition.

Significance
Solid. Achieving SOTA on a widely used benchmark and providing empirical evidence that deepening tree composition helps are useful contributions that should influence subsequent work. The impact would be higher with broader tasks/datasets or stronger, matched baselines.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to random initialization? Please report mean/std over several runs.
- What is the impact of fine-tuning word vectors versus keeping them fixed?
- Did you try connecting the output layer to all hidden layers (e.g., deep supervision)? How did this affect performance and interpretability?
- What are training time and memory costs as a function of depth and width? Any efficiency tricks besides shared dropout masks?
- How do results change without phrase-level supervision (sentence-level only)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a systematic ablation study isolating the effects of ReLU vs tanh, dropout, untying leaves/internals, pretrained vs random/finetuned embeddings, and output attachment strategy.
- Re-run at least one strong tree baseline (e.g., RNTN) with the same pretrained embeddings and dropout to assess how much stacking per se contributes.
- Report variability across multiple seeds and perform significance testing on test accuracy.
- Include training/runtime comparisons and a brief complexity discussion.
- Consider a second dataset/task (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment) to test generality.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea with solid execution
- Strong empirical results and SOTA on SST
- Careful parameter-controlled comparisons demonstrating the value of depth
- Useful qualitative analyses and training details

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow empirical scope (single dataset/task)
- Baseline fairness and missing ablations limit causal attribution
- Modest conceptual novelty

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept to accept. The paper presents a well-executed, impactful empirical contribution with a straightforward architectural idea, advancing results on a standard benchmark and offering insights into compositional depth. Strengthening ablations and baseline parity would make this a clear accept.
