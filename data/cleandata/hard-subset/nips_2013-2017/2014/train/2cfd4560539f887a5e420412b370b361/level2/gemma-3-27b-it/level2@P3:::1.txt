This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs), constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers, to improve compositionality modeling in language. The core idea is to extend existing recursive neural networks with depth, analogous to deep feedforward and recurrent networks, to capture hierarchical representations. The work is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, demonstrating state-of-the-art results in fine-grained sentiment classification and providing exploratory analyses of layer behavior.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly articulates the benefits of adding depth to recursive networks, drawing a strong parallel to developments in recurrent neural networks. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to multiple baselines and ablation studies investigating the effect of depth. The presentation of results is clear, and the exploratory analyses—input perturbation and nearest neighbor examination—are insightful and provide valuable qualitative understanding of the learned representations. The authors adequately address potential stability issues related to deep networks through regularization techniques. The inclusion of acknowledgements regarding funding is appropriate.

A strength of the paper lies in its careful consideration of the interplay between structural and spatial depth in recursive networks. The untying of leaf and internal nodes, as discussed in section 2.2, is a thoughtful design choice that allows for the effective use of pre-trained word embeddings and rectifier activations. The connection to existing work on deep recurrent networks and the justification for applying similar principles to recursive structures are convincingly presented. The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over shallow counterparts and previous state-of-the-art methods. 

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion of the computational complexity of deep RNNs compared to shallower models. While the authors maintain a constant number of parameters, the increased number of layers likely impacts training time and memory requirements. A brief analysis of these factors would enhance the practical relevance of the work. Additionally, the input perturbation experiment, while insightful, relies on manual selection of perturbation words.  A more systematic exploration of different perturbation strategies, potentially based on semantic similarity metrics, could strengthen the conclusions. The discussion of why increasing depth beyond a certain point degrades performance could also be expanded upon, perhaps considering the potential for overfitting or vanishing gradients in deeper layers. While the paper mentions AdaGrad, it could benefit from a brief discussion on whether other optimization algorithms were explored and why AdaGrad was chosen. Finally, the connection to compositional distributional semantics could be made more explicit. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture combining depth in structure and space for recursive networks.
*   Strong empirical results achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task.
*   Clear and well-motivated presentation of the work.
*   Insightful exploratory analyses providing qualitative understanding of learned representations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Input perturbation experiment relies on manual selection.
*   The discussion of the limits of depth is underdeveloped.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing. The proposed deep RNN architecture is a novel and effective approach to modeling compositionality in language, and the experimental results are compelling. The minor weaknesses identified above do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work and could be addressed through revisions. This reviewer recommends acceptance.
