- Proposes stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures to separate depth-in-structure from depth-in-space.  
- Unties leaf vs. internal node parameters, uses ReLUs, dropout, fixed large pretrained embeddings, AdaGrad, early stopping, and L2 regularization.  
- Evaluates on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (5-way and binary) and compares to RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN.  
- Reports that stacking improves over shallow tree models at equal parameter budgets and that deep RNNs achieve state-of-the-art on SST.  
- Finds that an untied shallow RNN with ReLU, dropout, and fixed embeddings already outperforms earlier RNN baselines.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs. polarity).  
- Modeling is technically sound and straightforward to implement with backprop through structure and layers.  
- Training setup and regularization choices are reasonable; qualitative analyses are informative but anecdotal.  
- Empirical evidence supports depth-in-space benefits and includes a depth sweep, but some confounds remain.  
- Limited ablations: effects of untying, ReLU vs. tanh, dropout, pretrained embeddings, and fine-tuning are not fully isolated.  
- Fairness concerns: unclear whether baselines used comparable pretrained embeddings, supervision regimes (phrase-level vs. sentence-level), and parameter counts.  
- No reporting of multiple runs, variance, or statistical significance for results on SST.  
- Experimental details are mostly sufficient, but compute cost, wall-clock time, and parameter counts vs. baselines are missing.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and motivates the separation of structural vs. spatial depth effectively.  
- The combination of stacked recursive layers, untying leaves, and ReLU/dropout is a novel and timely extension of prior techniques.  
- Achieving SOTA on SST with a simple stacked architecture is significant and likely to influence future work if results hold under controls.  
- Strengths: principled extension to tree models, strong empirical performance under fixed budgets, sound engineering choices, and insightful layer-wise analysis.  
- Weaknesses: incomplete controls/ablations, potential unfair comparisons to baselines, lack of statistical rigor, and missing runtime/memory analysis.  
- Additional concerns: deeper models hurt when narrowed to match budgets; robustness to parse errors and alternatives like deep supervision are not evaluated.  
- Suggested improvements: comprehensive ablation table, report exact parameter counts and external data usage for baselines, multiple runs with confidence intervals and significance tests, computational cost reporting, and targeted error analyses (negation, intensifiers).  
- Arguments for acceptance: clean contribution, strong empirical results, clear writing, and useful qualitative insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: gains may be due to external factors (pretrained embeddings/dropout), unfair comparisons, and insufficient statistical/ablation evidence.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing ablation and fairness issues to strengthen the claim.
