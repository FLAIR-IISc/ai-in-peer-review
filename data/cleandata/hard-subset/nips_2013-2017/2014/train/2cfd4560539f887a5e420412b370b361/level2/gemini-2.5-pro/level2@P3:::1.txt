This paper introduces the deep recursive neural network (deep RNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation is to add "depth in space" to traditional recursive networks, which are only "deep in structure," thereby enabling a hierarchy of representations where each successive layer can learn more abstract compositional features. This idea is analogous to the recent development of deep recurrent neural networks, which stack recurrent layers to add depth to temporal processing. The proposed model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank for fine-grained sentiment classification. The experimental results demonstrate that for a fixed parameter budget, deeper models outperform their shallow counterparts. The best deep RNN model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on this benchmark, surpassing previous recursive models like the RNTN and other strong baselines such as Paragraph Vectors. The paper concludes with qualitative analyses suggesting that different layers in the deep RNN learn to capture distinct aspects of compositionality, from word-level features in lower layers to more abstract sentiment-related semantics in higher layers.

**Quality**
The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The model formulation is a clear and logical extension of existing recursive neural networks. The experimental setup is rigorous, using a standard and challenging benchmark dataset (SST) and comparing against a comprehensive set of relevant and strong baselines. The claims of improved performance are well-supported by the quantitative results presented in Table 1. The authors are careful to control for the number of parameters when comparing deep and shallow models, which provides convincing evidence for the benefits of depth. The inclusion of implementation details such as the use of AdaGrad, dropout, rectifier units, and pre-trained word vectors is valuable for reproducibility. The qualitative analyses, while exploratory, provide insightful initial evidence for the model's inner workings.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea of distinguishing "depth in structure" from "depth in space" is introduced clearly and provides a strong motivation for the proposed architecture. The mathematical formulation of the deep RNN is precise and easy to follow. Figures 1 and 2 are effective visual aids that help clarify the model's structure, the flow of information, and the weight-sharing scheme. The experimental section is logically structured, and the results are presented in a clear and interpretable manner.

**Originality**
The primary contribution—stacking recursive layers to create a deep recursive network—is novel. While the concept of stacking layers to add depth is well-established for feed-forward and recurrent networks, its application to the recursive setting for tree-structured data is a new and significant contribution. This work successfully bridges the gap between the recursive modeling paradigm pioneered by Socher et al. and the broader trend towards deeper architectures in machine learning. The paper also makes a useful contribution by proposing the "untying" of weights for leaf and internal nodes, which better accommodates modern practices like using large pre-trained word embeddings and rectifier activation functions. All relevant prior work appears to be adequately cited.

**Significance**
The results presented are highly significant. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on the competitive Stanford Sentiment Treebank benchmark is a strong empirical contribution. More broadly, the paper proposes a general and principled way to increase the expressive power of models that operate on structured data. This work is likely to inspire further research into deep structured models for a variety of NLP tasks beyond sentiment analysis, such as semantic parsing, relation extraction, and natural language inference. The qualitative findings, suggesting a learned hierarchy of representations, open up interesting questions for future work on analyzing and interpreting deep models for language.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
While this is a strong paper, the following points could be considered to further strengthen it:

*   **Analysis of Layer Contributions:** The qualitative analyses based on input perturbation and nearest neighbors are insightful but limited to a few examples. To make the claims about the hierarchical nature of the learned representations more concrete, a more systematic analysis could be beneficial. For instance, one could design probing tasks to quantitatively assess what linguistic properties (e.g., part-of-speech, syntactic chunks, negation scope) are captured at each layer's representations across the entire dataset. This could provide more robust evidence for the specialization of different layers.
*   **Ablation Study:** The shallow RNN model incorporating untied weights, rectifiers, and pre-trained vectors shows a substantial improvement over the baseline RNN from prior work (48.1 vs. 43.2). An ablation study that isolates the performance contribution of each of these components would be valuable. This would help clarify how much of the gain comes from the architectural modification (untying weights) versus the use of better activation functions, regularization, or word representations.
*   **Generalizability:** The experiments are focused solely on sentiment analysis. While the conclusion rightly points to future work on other tasks, a brief discussion in the main body of the paper about the potential applicability and challenges of applying deep RNNs to other structure-dependent tasks (e.g., parsing, logical inference) would broaden the perceived impact of the proposed architecture.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and well-motivated architecture, supports it with strong state-of-the-art empirical results, and provides intriguing initial analysis. The work represents a significant advance in modeling compositionality in language.
