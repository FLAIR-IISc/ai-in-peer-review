- Proposes stacked recursive neural networks that add “depth in space” by stacking recursive layers over parse trees.  
- Composes phrase representations bottom-up per layer and passes node-specific hidden states upward across layers.  
- Uses untied leaf vs internal node parameters in the first layer, ReLU activations, pretrained word embeddings, Adagrad, and dropout.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (phrase-level supervision) and reports improvements over shallow recursive nets and prior baselines.  
- Finds 2–4 stacked layers outperform shallow counterparts under matched parameter budgets.  
- Builds on prior tree RNNs and stacked recurrent nets; novelty is stacking over tree structures rather than multiplicative composition.  
- Methodology is clearly defined with interleaved backprop through structure and layers and a sensible training recipe.  
- Major attribution concern: improvements may partly come from pretrained embeddings, dropout, ReLUs, or untying rather than stacking alone.  
- Fairness concern: comparisons to RNTN/MV-RNN are confounded because embeddings are fixed here but jointly learned in some baselines.  
- Statistical robustness lacking: no multiple runs, variance, confidence intervals, or significance tests reported.  
- Computational aspects missing: no training time, memory cost, convergence behavior, or use of gradient clipping/residual connections.  
- Analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) support layered specialization but are qualitative.  
- Clarity is good overall but notation could be streamlined and parser/binarization, hyperparameter search, and parameter counts should be explicitly reported.  
- Originality: stacking tree-RNN layers and untying leaf/internal parameters are novel and practical extensions.  
- Significance: useful improvements on SST with potential broader interest, but limited by single-task evaluation and incremental nature.  
- Pros: simple principled idea, consistent gains, practical training recipe, and qualitative evidence for layer specialization.  
- Cons: limited ablations, single benchmark, missing statistical/runtime analyses, and potentially unfair baseline comparisons.  
- Suggestions: run thorough ablations (untie vs tie, ReLU vs tanh, dropout, embeddings, depth), report mean±std over seeds and significance, test deep supervision/residuals, include runtime/memory, and evaluate on at least one additional compositional task.  
- Overall: competent, incremental, and useful work that leans positive but needs stronger ablations and robustness analyses to be fully convincing.
