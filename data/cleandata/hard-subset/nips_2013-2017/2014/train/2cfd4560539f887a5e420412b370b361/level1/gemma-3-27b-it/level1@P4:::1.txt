This paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs), constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers, to improve compositionality modeling in language. The core idea is to extend traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) with depth, similar to the development of deep recurrent networks. The authors evaluate their model on fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and providing exploratory analyses on the effect of multiple layers.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon previous work in both RNNs and deep learning. The idea of extending RNNs to incorporate depth, drawing a parallel with deep recurrent networks, is natural and logical. The application to the Stanford Sentiment Treebank is appropriate, given its fine-grained sentiment labels that facilitate backpropagation and analysis of different layers. The paper clearly explains the methodology, and the experimental setup seems sound with appropriate baselines and regularization techniques. The inclusion of input perturbation and nearest neighbor analysis to interpret the learned representations adds value beyond purely quantitative results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** While building upon established concepts, the introduction of stacking recursive layers to create Deep RNNs is a novel architectural extension.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-defined, and the experiments are adequately described. The use of dropout and L2 regularization demonstrates attention to practical training considerations. The ablation studies regarding depth and width are valuable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures are helpful in visualizing the architecture.
*   **Significance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark like the SST is significant. The exploratory analyses suggesting different layers capture different aspects of compositionality are also valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Depth Exploration:** The paper explores depth up to 4 layers. It would be beneficial to investigate whether increasing depth further (perhaps with architectural modifications to mitigate vanishing gradients) could lead to further improvements, or at what point diminishing returns (or negative effects) set in.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While dropout rate is mentioned as being tuned, the paper lacks a detailed discussion about the sensitivity of performance to other hyperparameters, such as learning rate, L2 regularization strength, and hidden unit size.
*   **Untying Leaves and Internals Justification:** While empirically beneficial, the justification for "untying" leaves and internals relies heavily on the ability to use rectifier activations and large pre-trained word vectors. A more theoretical argument for *why* separating representations is advantageous for compositional learning would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Generalization Discussion:** The paper primarily focuses on sentiment analysis. A discussion of the potential generalization of Deep RNNs to other tasks involving compositional structure (e.g., parsing, semantic role labeling) would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound extension to existing recursive neural network architectures.
*   It achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.
*   The exploratory analyses provide valuable insights into the learned representations.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The depth exploration is limited.
*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The justification for the "untied" variant could be stronger.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field. It introduces a novel architecture, demonstrates its effectiveness on a challenging task, and provides insightful analyses. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
