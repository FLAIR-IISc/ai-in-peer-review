- Proposes deep recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers over parse trees with vertical connections and untied leaf/internal parameters.
- Uses ReLUs, pretrained fixed word embeddings, AdaGrad, dropout (shared mask per sentence per layer), and small L2 penalties for training.
- Achieves state-of-the-art on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank and shows deeper, narrower models outperform shallow, wider ones under a fixed parameter budget.
- Qualitative analyses indicate different layers capture complementary compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs polarity) via substitutions and nearest-neighbor retrievals.
- Architecture is clearly defined and its relation to standard tree RNNs is well explained; untying leaf/internal parameters is sensible.
- Experimental results are consistent and thoughtful, showing gains from both optimization choices and stacking.
- Major missing controls: insufficient ablations to disentangle effects of stacking, untying, ReLU vs tanh, dropout, and pretrained embeddings.
- No statistical significance testing on test results is reported.
- Some baselines (paragraph vectors, DCNN) may not be directly comparable due to differing supervision and embedding finetuning practices.
- Keeping embeddings fixed may understate achievable performance and complicate fair comparisons on binary metrics.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, but lacks details on parser/binarization, exact parameter counts, gradient clipping, matrix dimensions, and runtime/memory.
- Experiment with deep supervision (losses at intermediate layers) is suggested but not performed.
- Novelty: stacking recursive layers for depth-in-space on trees with vertical connections is a useful and (at the time) novel adaptation of stacked RNN ideas.
- Incremental but important engineering contributions: untied leaf/internal parameters and adoption of ReLUs/dropout enable deeper tree models.
- Significance: improves SST SOTA and provides a practical recipe likely to influence practitioners, though results are limited to one dataset.
- Pros: simple, well-motivated architecture; strong SST results; solid training recipe; insightful qualitative diagnostics.
- Cons: insufficient ablations, single-dataset evaluation, lack of significance tests, fixed embeddings, and missing experimental details.
- Suggested improvements: comprehensive ablations, report parameter counts/time/memory, significance testing, train a direct binary classifier baseline, evaluate on additional structured tasks, and explore deep supervision/skip connections.
- Overall: well-executed and timely work with meaningful gains and insights; acceptance recommended conditional on stronger experimental analysis.
