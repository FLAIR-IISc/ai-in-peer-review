Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth in space” on top of “depth in structure.” The model composes phrase representations bottom-up along parse trees within each layer, while passing node-specific hidden states upward across layers. The authors also “untie” parameters between leaves and internal nodes in the first layer (separate Wx→h and Wh→h) and adopt ReLU activations, pretrained word embeddings, Adagrad, and dropout. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with phrase-level supervision, achieving improvements over shallow recursive nets and prior baselines (including Socher et al.’s RNTN, MV-RNN, and Paragraph Vectors), with exploratory analyses suggesting different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The work builds on recursive nets for NLP [Goller & Kuchler 1996; Socher et al. 2011–2013], fine-grained sentiment and RNTN [Socher et al. 2013 EMNLP], and deep recurrent networks that stack recurrent layers [El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Hermans & Schrauwen 2013]. Compared to MV-RNN/RNTN, the novelty is orthogonal: it augments a standard tree RNN with vertical stacking rather than introducing multiplicative composition. Compared to Paragraph Vectors [Le & Mikolov 2014] and DCNN [Kalchbrenner et al. 2014], the method is structure-aware via parses.

Quality
- Methodology: The model is clearly defined (Equation 4) with interleaved backprop through structure and across layers. Untying leaves vs internals in the first layer is well-motivated for using ReLUs. The training recipe (fixed embeddings, Adagrad, dropout, small activation penalty) is sensible and consistent with deep learning practice of the time.
- Empirical support: The experimental setup on SST is standard. The paper emphasizes depth-vs-width under fixed parameter budgets and reports that 2–4 layers outperform the shallow counterpart. However, some concerns remain:
  - Attribution of gains: The paper notes substantial improvements from dropout and pretrained embeddings. Although there is a shallow baseline with these ingredients, a fuller ablation isolating the effects of (i) untying, (ii) ReLUs vs tanh, (iii) dropout, and (iv) depth would strengthen the claim that stacking is the primary driver.
  - Fairness to prior baselines: RNTN and MV-RNN jointly learn embeddings; here embeddings are fixed and large (300d). A comparison where embeddings are fine-tuned for all variants (or all fixed) would better control this factor.
  - Statistical robustness: No variance across runs, confidence intervals, or significance tests are reported. Given relatively small absolute differences in some SST settings, this is important.
  - Computational aspects: There is no discussion of training time or memory cost as depth increases, or of convergence behavior beyond anecdotal notes; gradient clipping or residual/skip connections are not considered as stabilization mechanisms.
- Analysis: The input perturbation and nearest-neighbor analyses are insightful and consistent with the layered-compositionality hypothesis, though they remain qualitative.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The distinction between “depth in structure” vs “depth in space” is helpful. Notation between x/h and tied/untied cases could be streamlined, and explicit mention of the parser and binarization used for SST would aid reproducibility. Details on hyperparameter search space (beyond dropout) and parameter counts per configuration should be consolidated.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers over parse trees appears novel and non-trivial at the time relative to prior tree-structured models (RAE, MV-RNN, RNTN) and stacked recurrent nets in time. The untying of leaf/internal parameters for ReLUs is a practical design choice not widely explored in earlier tree RNNs. While the idea is a fairly direct extension of stacked RNNs to tree structures, demonstrating that such depth adds value over trees is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
Sentiment analysis on SST is a widely adopted benchmark; improvements over strong baselines are valuable. The architecture is likely to be of interest beyond sentiment (paraphrase, entailment, semantic parsing), as many tasks involve compositionality over trees. The qualitative evidence that layers specialize is intriguing and could influence future tree architectures. That said, the empirical scope is limited to one dataset/task, and the incremental nature may temper the broad impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea (stacked tree-RNN) with principled motivation and solid engineering.
- Consistent gains over shallow counterparts under matched parameter budgets; new SOTA on SST at time of writing.
- Sensible training recipe (ReLU, dropout) for tree models; useful practical insights.
- Qualitative analyses supporting the “different layers, different roles” hypothesis.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited ablation isolating depth from other factors (pretrained embeddings, ReLU, untying, dropout).
- Single benchmark; no results on additional compositional tasks or languages.
- Lack of statistical significance/variance and runtime/memory analysis.
- Comparisons to RNTN/MV-RNN may be confounded by embedding treatment and hyperparameter tuning.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide a thorough ablation: tied vs untied leaves, tanh vs ReLU, with/without dropout, with/without pretrained embeddings, across 1–4 layers, all under matched parameter budgets.
- Report multiple random seeds, mean±std, and significance tests vs strongest baselines.
- Clarify whether all phrase nodes’ labels supervise only the final layer; consider “deep supervision” (losses on intermediate layers) and/or residual/skip connections across layers to improve optimization and interpretability.
- Include training time and memory usage as a function of depth; discuss gradient clipping and stability vs the current L2-on-activations workaround.
- Add a fair comparison where all models either fix embeddings or fine-tune them, and report sensitivity to embedding dimensionality.
- Evaluate on at least one additional task involving tree compositionality (e.g., paraphrase detection as in Socher et al. 2011) to demonstrate generality.

Overall evaluation
A well-motivated and competently executed extension of tree-structured neural models that provides consistent improvements and a plausible interpretive story. The contribution is incremental but useful, with some missing controls and broader validation. With stronger ablations and robustness analyses, this would be an easy accept; as is, I lean positive given the novelty, empirical gains, and clarity.
