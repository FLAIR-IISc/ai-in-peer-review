This paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs), constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers, to improve compositionality modeling in language. The core idea is to extend traditional RNNs (which are deep in *structure*) with depth in *space* by stacking layers, inspired by the success of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks. The authors evaluate their approach on fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art results and providing exploratory analyses of layer behavior.

The paper builds directly on prior work in recursive and recurrent neural networks for NLP. Socher et al. (NIPS 2013) pioneered the application of recursive neural networks to sentiment analysis with parse-tree structures.  The concept of “depth in time” vs. “depth in space” for RNNs is well-established (e.g., Sutskever et al., NIPS 2012; El Hihi & Bengio, NIPS 2014), motivating the exploration of a similar concept for recursive networks. The use of the SST dataset (Socher et al., EMNLP 2013) and comparison to Paragraph Vectors (Le et al., 2014) and DCNNs (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) situates the work within the current state-of-the-art in sentiment analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of stacking recursive layers to create Deep RNNs is a natural and potentially powerful extension of existing recursive network architectures. It addresses a limitation of traditional RNNs, which, despite their structural depth, lack the hierarchical representation capability of truly deep networks.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates convincingly that Deep RNNs outperform shallow counterparts and achieve state-of-the-art results on the SST dataset. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments include a good range of baselines and ablation studies (varying depth and width), and the qualitative analysis (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) offers valuable insights into the learned representations.  The analysis of layer behavior is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is explained in a straightforward manner, and the experiments are well-documented.
* **Addressing Practical Considerations:**  The authors thoughtfully address practical challenges like vanishing gradients with the use of the SST dataset and regularization with dropout and L2 penalties. The use of pre-trained word embeddings is also a sensible choice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the qualitative analysis is helpful, it is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive analysis of what *specifically* each layer is learning could strengthen the paper. For example, visualizing the learned weights or activations could provide further insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions tuning dropout rate but doesn’t detail the search space or the impact of other hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, L2 regularization strength). More detailed discussion on the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** Stacking layers naturally increases the computational cost. The paper doesn’t discuss the computational overhead of Deep RNNs compared to shallow RNNs. A brief discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Untying Leaves and Internals Motivation:** While the untying approach improves performance, the motivation provided feels slightly weak.  More justification for this design choice, perhaps relating it to representational differences between words and phrases, would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture (Deep RNNs) that demonstrably improves performance on a challenging NLP task.
* The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides valuable insights into the learned representations.
* The work addresses a relevant problem (compositionality in language) and contributes to the growing body of research on deep learning for NLP.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The qualitative analysis, while insightful, could be more comprehensive.
* The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost is limited.
* The motivation behind the untying of leaves and internals could be stronger.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and should be accepted. The proposed Deep RNN architecture is a promising extension of existing recursive network models, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The novelty, strong empirical performance, and clear presentation make this a valuable addition to the NIPS program.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
