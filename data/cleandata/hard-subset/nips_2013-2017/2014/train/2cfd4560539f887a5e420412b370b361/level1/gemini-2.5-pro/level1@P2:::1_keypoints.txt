- Proposes a novel "deep recursive neural network" that separates depth in structure (tree recursion) from depth in space (stacked layers).
- Constructs deep RNNs by stacking recursive layers where each node receives input from children in its layer and from the corresponding node below.
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment classification.
- Introduces "untying" of weights for leaf (word) vs. internal (phrase) nodes in the first layer to use large pre-trained embeddings and rectifier activations.
- Deep models consistently outperform shallow counterparts with a similar number of parameters.
- Best deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art on SST, beating RNTN and Paragraph Vectors.
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) show layers learn hierarchical representations from lexical/syntactic to abstract semantic/sentiment features.
- High technical quality: well-motivated architecture and rigorous experiments.
- Proper ablation study isolating spatial depth vs. width (Table 1a) convincingly validates the main claim.
- Uses modern training techniques (pre-trained vectors, ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad); dropout shown more effective than L2.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and supported by helpful figures (Figures 1–2).
- Methodology described in enough detail for reproducibility (dataset, hyperparameters, optimization).
- Original contribution: first successful application of spatial depth to tree-structured recursive networks; untying trick is simple and effective.
- Significant impact: new SOTA and a promising direction for deep structured models in NLP.
- Main weakness: reliance on external parse trees ties performance to parser quality.
- Minor weakness: brief analysis on why performance degrades beyond certain depth.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, strong performance, insightful analysis, clarity, and likely high impact.
- Argument against acceptance: inherent dependence on pre-defined parse trees (shared limitation in the field).
