Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers on parse-tree structures. Each layer performs standard tree composition, and nodes also pass their representation “vertically” to their counterparts in the next layer, yielding depth in space in addition to depth in structure. The authors also “untie” parameters for leaves vs internal nodes in the first layer (Wxh vs Whh), enabling the use of large, fixed pretrained word embeddings and rectified linear units (ReLUs). Training uses AdaGrad, dropout (with shared masks per sentence per layer), and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations.

On Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), deep RNNs outperform shallow recursive nets with a comparable parameter budget, and achieve new state-of-the-art (SOTA) results compared to RNTN/recursive tensor networks and paragraph vectors; DCNN is also cited. Analyses suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of composition (e.g., intensity vs polarity), as probed via controlled lexical substitutions and nearest-neighbor phrase retrieval.

This work extends a line of tree-structured neural models for NLP (e.g., Socher et al. 2011–2013; Goller & Küchler 1996) by introducing depth in space analogous to stacked recurrent nets (Schmidhuber 1992; El Hihi & Bengio 1995; Hermans & Schrauwen 2013). It also leverages modern deep learning techniques (ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad, pretrained word2vec) to improve optimization and generalization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is clearly defined (Eq. 4), and its relationship to standard tree RNNs is well explained. The untying of leaf/internal parameters is sensible and matches practice in recurrent nets (separate input-to-hidden vs hidden-to-hidden). Training details are provided (optimizer, initialization, regularization).
- Empirical support: Results on SST show consistent gains: (i) shallow tree RNN improves substantially by using pretrained embeddings, ReLU, and dropout; (ii) deeper, narrower models outperform shallow, wider ones under a fixed parameter budget; (iii) the best deep RNN surpasses prior published baselines, including RNTN and paragraph vectors, on fine-grained and binary sentiment. The qualitative analyses are thoughtful and consistent with the model design.
- Missing controls: While the parameter-budget comparison within the proposed models is appreciated, it is hard to disentangle the contributions of (a) stacking, (b) leaf/internal untying, (c) ReLU vs tanh, (d) dropout, and (e) pretrained embeddings. A fuller ablation (e.g., tied vs untied with ReLU; ReLU without dropout; finetuned vs fixed embeddings; deep vs shallow with identical activations and regularization) would strengthen the causal claims that depth is the main driver. Statistical significance testing on the test set (e.g., bootstrap or approximate randomization) is not reported.
- Fairness of baselines: Paragraph vectors and DCNN are used as published numbers with potentially different training supervision. Some baselines finetune embeddings; the proposed models keep them fixed “for simplicity,” which may understate achievable performance. A binary classifier trained directly (for the binary metric) would enable a more apples-to-apples comparison to prior binary models.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with precise equations and an intuitive figure. The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is helpful.
- The training protocol is described in sufficient detail to reproduce, though a few details would improve clarity: the exact parser and binarization procedure; the exact parameter counts per model in Table 1; whether gradient clipping was used; dimensions of all matrices V(i) for leaf vs internal connections; and runtime/memory considerations for deeper stacks.
- The qualitative analyses are easy to follow and insightful. The choice to connect supervision only to the top layer is justified; still, an experiment with deep supervision (outputs from all layers) would be informative.

Originality
- The main novelty is stacking recursive layers to induce hierarchical representations over tree structures. While the idea parallels deep/stacked recurrent networks in time, its adaptation to tree-structured computation with the specific vertical connections V(i) is, to my knowledge, new at the time, and the analyses linking layers to different compositional phenomena are valuable.
- The untying of leaf vs internal parameters and the adoption of ReLUs/dropout in tree RNNs are incremental but important engineering contributions that enable deeper training.
- Related work is broadly covered (recursive nets, RNTN, paragraph vectors, DCNN, deep RNNs). It would be useful to more explicitly contrast to matrix-vector RNNs (Socher et al. 2012) in terms of representational hierarchy vs parameter sharing, and to mention early work on backpropagation through structure (Goller & Küchler).

Significance
- Improving SST state of the art and showing that depth in space helps over trees is of broad interest to the community. The recipe (pretrained embeddings + ReLU + dropout + stacking) is likely to influence practitioners beyond sentiment tasks.
- The qualitative diagnostics (input perturbation paths, nearest neighbors per layer) provide insights that can guide future tree models and suggest that layering encourages division of labor across levels (syntax vs intensity vs polarity).
- Limitations: Results are limited to a single dataset and task; generalization to other tree-based problems (paraphrase, entailment, semantic role labeling) remains to be shown. The gains attributed to stacking could be partly due to other factors without stronger ablations.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated extension of recursive nets with depth in space; simple architecture.
- Strong empirical results on SST; parameter-controlled comparisons show consistent benefits of depth.
- Solid training recipe (ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad) and practical initialization; no pretraining needed.
- Insightful qualitative analyses illustrating complementary roles of layers.

Cons
- Insufficient ablations to isolate the contribution of stacking from leaf/internal untying, ReLU, dropout, and pretrained embeddings.
- Single-dataset evaluation; lack of statistical significance tests.
- Some experimental choices complicate comparisons (fixed embeddings; binary metric derived from 5-class model).
- Missing details: parser/binarization, parameter counts per configuration, speed/memory, potential gradient clipping.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a comprehensive ablation study: tied vs untied leaves/internals; tanh vs ReLU; with/without dropout; fixed vs finetuned embeddings; deep vs shallow under identical settings.
- Report exact parameter counts per model, training time per epoch, and memory footprint; include significance testing on test results.
- Add a binary-sentiment model trained directly to compare fairly to binary baselines.
- Evaluate on at least one additional structured NLP task (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment on SICK) to demonstrate generality.
- Explore deep supervision (auxiliary losses at intermediate layers) and/or skip connections between non-adjacent layers to assess effects on optimization and interpretability.
- Clarify parsing details and whether binarization choices affect results.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed and timely paper that introduces a simple yet effective way to deepen tree-structured neural models and demonstrates meaningful gains and insights on a standard benchmark. Despite some missing ablations and limited scope of evaluation, the contribution is solid and relevant to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on strengthening the experimental analysis.
