- Proposes stacking recursive layers to add “depth in space” on top of tree-structure depth, untying leaf/internal parameters, using ReLUs, Adagrad, dropout, and an L2 penalty on weights/activations.  
- Builds on recursive neural nets and deep recurrent nets; compares to tree and non-tree baselines and keeps total parameters constant when varying depth.  
- Architecture and training are clearly specified; supervising only the top layer isolates higher-level abstractions.  
- Untying leaves/internal nodes is well motivated for parameter efficiency and enabling ReLU sparsity.  
- Central empirical claim (depth improves performance at fixed parameter budget) is supported on SST; best deep model beats shallow counterparts and reported baselines.  
- Use of fixed 300d word2vec pretrained on ~100B tokens is a strong external signal that partially confounds cross-paper fairness.  
- No statistical significance tests or variance across runs are reported despite small absolute differences among strong models.  
- Results are limited to a single dataset (SST), reducing claims about generality to other tree-structured tasks.  
- No runtime or memory analysis as depth increases, so practical efficiency trade-offs are unclear.  
- L2 penalty on activations is a nonstandard stabilization; comparison to alternatives (e.g., gradient clipping) is missing.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; figures and qualitative analyses (input perturbations, nearest neighbors) are insightful.  
- Minor clarity fixes needed: state parser/binarization, clarify dropout mask sharing, provide full hyperparameters, and report test accuracies with std/significance.  
- Original contributions: stacking tree-structured layers, untying leaf/internal mapping, ReLU+dropout in tree composition, and analyses showing layer specialization.  
- Should discuss related deep RNN stability work (e.g., Pascanu et al.) and more clearly contrast with multiplicative composition (RNTN) beyond parameter counts.  
- Significance: SOTA on SST is meaningful and controlled depth-vs-width comparisons are compelling, but broader impact requires more tasks, ablations, and stats.  
- Pros: simple, well-motivated extension; careful fixed-capacity experiments; practical training recipe; insightful qualitative analyses.  
- Cons: single-task evaluation, fairness concerns from pretrained embeddings, no variance/significance reporting, no runtime/memory analysis, reliance on activation L2 without alternatives, missing key ablations.  
- Suggested fixes: report mean/std and significance over seeds; ablate tied vs untied, tanh vs ReLU, dropout vs L2, and pretrained vs random embeddings; re-run strong baselines with same embeddings; include runtime/memory and gradient-norm analyses; test auxiliary losses and evaluate on an additional task.  
- Overall: well-executed, timely paper with convincing evidence within scope; lean toward acceptance contingent on clarifying fairness, adding variance/significance, and including minimal ablations.
