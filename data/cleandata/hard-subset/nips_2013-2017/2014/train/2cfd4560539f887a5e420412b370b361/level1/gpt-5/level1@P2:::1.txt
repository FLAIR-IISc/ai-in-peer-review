Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers, adding “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure.” Within each layer, composition over a binary parse tree proceeds bottom-up with shared weights; across layers, each node’s representation is fed to its counterpart in the next layer. The authors also “untie” leaves and internal nodes at the first layer, mapping word embeddings to a phrase space via separate parameters, enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs). Training uses Adagrad, dropout (shared per sentence/layer), and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations for stability. The main evaluation is on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with fine-grained (5-way) and derived binary classification.

The work builds on recursive neural networks for composition [Goller & Kuchler; Socher et al.], and parallels prior work on deep recurrent nets in time [El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen]. It compares to tree-structured baselines (RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) and to non-tree baselines (Paragraph Vector, DCNN). The authors control model capacity by keeping total parameters constant when varying depth, and provide qualitative analyses suggesting layer specialization.

Quality:
- Soundness: The architecture is clearly specified (Eqs. 3–5) and training details are sufficiently described (optimizer, initialization, dropout sharing across nodes, early stopping). The choice to connect supervision to only the top layer isolates higher-level abstractions and simplifies layer-wise analysis. The leaf/internal untying is well motivated, both for parameter efficiency with large embeddings and for enabling ReLU sparsity.
- Empirical support: The central claim—depth improves performance for a fixed parameter budget—is supported by comparisons across depths and widths. The best deep model improves over shallow counterparts and reported baselines on SST. However:
  - External resources: The use of fixed 300d word2vec embeddings trained on ~100B tokens is a strong signal; some baselines (e.g., RNTN) did not leverage such pretraining. While the authors note that shallow RNNs also benefit from these choices, the fairness of cross-paper comparisons remains partially confounded.
  - Statistical rigor: No significance tests or variance across multiple runs are reported; given small absolute differences among stronger models, this would be helpful.
  - Generality: Results are confined to one dataset/task; no tests on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) or other treebanks, which limits claims about general utility.
  - Efficiency: There is no runtime or memory analysis as depth increases; this would inform practical trade-offs.
- Method nuances: The L2 penalty on activations is a nonstandard stabilization choice; a brief comparison to alternatives (e.g., gradient clipping) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The motivation for adding spatial depth to recursive nets is well articulated. Figures (as described) help differentiate depth-in-structure vs depth-in-space. The qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest neighbors) are insightful and well interpreted. Minor clarity suggestions:
- Explicitly state which parser/binarization is used and whether it matches SST preprocessing.
- Clarify the exact dropout mask sharing scheme and its effect (same mask per sentence per layer).
- Provide full hyperparameter settings (e.g., initialization noise scale) for reproducibility.
- Report the exact test accuracies in a table with standard deviations and significance vs. baselines.

Originality:
Stacking tree-structured recursive layers is a natural but, to my knowledge at the time, novel extension of deep recurrent ideas to trees. The untying of leaf/internal parameters in a recursive model and the use of ReLUs with dropout in tree composition are also timely and pragmatic contributions. The analyses demonstrating layer specialization (intensity vs polarity) are original and illuminating. Related work is reasonably cited, though it would be good to discuss deep RNN design and stability practices (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2013, on deep/stacked RNNs and gradient clipping) and clarify how the approach contrasts with multiplicative composition (RNTN) beyond parameter counts.

Significance:
Achieving state-of-the-art results on SST fine-grained sentiment in 2014 is a meaningful advance for a widely used benchmark. The controlled comparison at fixed parameter budgets provides compelling evidence that “depth in space” adds value beyond “depth in structure.” The architecture is likely to be reused for other tree-structured NLP tasks. That said, the impact would be stronger with:
- additional tasks to demonstrate generality,
- stronger ablations isolating the effects of pretraining, untying, dropout, and ReLUs,
- and significance testing and efficiency reporting.

Pros:
- Clear, simple extension: stacked recursive layers with strong motivation and clean formulation.
- Solid, careful experimental protocol for depth vs. width at fixed capacity.
- SOTA results on SST; practical training recipe (ReLU + dropout + Adagrad) that others can adopt.
- Insightful qualitative analyses showing layer specialization.

Cons:
- Limited scope: single dataset/task; no evidence of generality to other tree-structured problems.
- Fairness concerns in cross-paper comparisons due to large external word pretraining.
- No statistical significance or variance across runs; small margins may not be robust.
- No runtime/memory analysis; stability relies on an activation L2 penalty without comparison to alternatives.
- Missing ablation on the leaf/internal untying and on top-only supervision vs. multi-layer supervision.

Constructive suggestions:
- Report mean/std over multiple random seeds and statistical tests vs. closest baselines.
- Add ablations: (i) tied vs. untied leaves/internal; (ii) tanh vs. ReLU; (iii) dropout vs. L2 only; (iv) with and without pretrained embeddings (possibly with fine-tuning) to assess reliance on external data.
- Provide a reimplementation or re-run of a strong baseline (e.g., shallow RNN or RNTN) with the same pretrained embeddings, if feasible, to clarify the architecture’s contribution vs. embeddings.
- Include runtime/memory scaling with depth and an analysis of gradient norms; compare activation L2 to gradient clipping.
- Explore attaching auxiliary losses at lower layers (or training with root-only supervision) to test the role of intermediate supervision in deep trees.
- Evaluate on an additional task (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment) to support broader claims.

Overall:
This is a well-executed and timely paper with a clear idea, clean design, and convincing empirical evidence within its chosen scope. Despite some limitations in breadth and statistical reporting, the contribution is of interest to the NIPS community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying fairness, reporting variance/significance, and adding a minimal ablation on untying and pretraining effects.
