Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RecNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers atop a parse tree. Each layer performs bottom-up composition on the tree (with shared structural parameters within the layer), and nodes also pass their hidden state vertically to the corresponding node at the next layer. The model introduces an “untied” parameterization between leaves (word vectors) and internal nodes in the first layer, enabling the use of rectified linear units and large pretrained embeddings without quadratic parameter growth. Supervision is applied only at the top layer. Training uses backpropagation through structure across layers, AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks per sentence-layer, and minor L2 penalties for stability. Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that, under approximately fixed parameter budgets, deeper-and-narrower RecNNs outperform shallow-and-wider ones, and the best deep model achieves state-of-the-art performance against prior baselines, including MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture distinct aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity, sentiment polarity).

The work builds on recursive models applied to parse trees (Socher et al., ICML/EMNLP), multiplicative/tensor compositions (RNTN), and stacked recurrent networks distinguishing depth in time vs. depth in space (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). It relates to earlier NIPS contributions on deep architectures, RAEs, and dropout.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The model is a straightforward and coherent extension of RecNNs: a structural recurrence within layers plus vertical feedforward across layers (Eq. 4). The training procedure is standard and well-motivated. Untying leaves vs. internals is clearly specified and justified. The choice of ReLU, pretrained word vectors (fixed), AdaGrad, and dropout is reasonable and empirically supported.
- Empirical support: Results indicate consistent gains from depth under matched parameter counts, and improved SOTA on SST for both 5-class and derived binary tasks. Ablations partially isolate factors (e.g., dropout impact). However, some aspects could be better controlled: (a) explicit parameter counts for each depth-width combination, (b) variability across runs (seed sensitivity), and (c) statistical significance tests. Comparisons sometimes mix modeling choices (e.g., fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings) and training regimes across baselines; the fairness of those differences is not fully discussed.
- Analysis: The qualitative layer analyses are informative and align with the hypothesis of hierarchical abstraction, though they remain anecdotal; additional systematic probing would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The architecture is described with clear equations and figures. The untying scheme and the sharing of dropout masks are explained. Minor clarity issues:
- The term “RNN” is used for recursive networks, which can be confused with recurrent networks; consistently using “RecNN” vs. “RNN (recurrent)” would reduce ambiguity.
- Details on exact parameter counts per configuration, training time/complexity, and parser assumptions (gold vs. predicted trees) could be more explicit.

Originality:
The main novelty is stacking recursive layers (depth in space) atop structural depth, an extension analogous to stacked recurrent nets but applied to tree-structured composition. The untying of leaves/internals for ReLU-based composition is a pragmatic design. While the components themselves are known, the combination and its systematic evaluation on SST constitute a meaningful and nontrivial contribution beyond prior single-layer RecNNs, MV-RNN, and RNTN. The qualitative analyses of layer behavior are also novel in this context.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art on a widely used benchmark and offers evidence that depth helps compositional modeling in trees under fixed parameter budgets. Given the influence of SST and tree-based composition in NLP, the contribution is likely to be adopted and extended. Limitations include evaluation on a single dataset/task and reliance on gold (or dataset-provided) parse trees, which may reduce applicability in settings with parser noise. Broader impact would be bolstered by results on additional structured tasks or languages, and by examining robustness to parsing errors.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled extension of recursive models to stacked depth with a sound training approach.
- Consistent empirical gains over shallow counterparts under comparable parameter budgets.
- New state-of-the-art on SST with transparent methodological choices.
- Useful qualitative analyses suggesting distinct roles for layers in compositionality.
- In scope for NIPS; builds on and contributes to deep learning for structured data.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation scope (single dataset/task), no robustness tests to parser errors.
- Incomplete experimental controls: lack of reported parameter counts per model, run-to-run variance, and significance testing.
- Baseline comparisons may not fully normalize for training choices (e.g., embedding fine-tuning).
- Analysis of computational cost and scalability is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report exact parameter counts, training time per epoch, and memory/runtime costs for each depth/width setting.
- Provide results over multiple random seeds with mean ± std, and perform statistical significance tests versus strongest baselines.
- Add ablations isolating: (a) effect of untying leaves/internals, (b) ReLU vs. tanh, (c) supervision at intermediate layers vs. final-only, and (d) embedding fine-tuning.
- Evaluate robustness to parsing noise (e.g., using an off-the-shelf parser vs. dataset-provided trees) and test on additional structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment) to demonstrate generality.
- Consider parameter-matched comparisons with RNTN/MV-RNN and DCNN where possible to strengthen fairness claims.
- Expand probing of layer behavior with systematic diagnostics (e.g., controlled composition templates, sentiment intensity and negation benchmarks).

Overall assessment:
A technically solid and clearly presented contribution demonstrating that stacking recursive layers improves compositional modeling and yields state-of-the-art sentiment results at the time. With broader evaluation and tighter experimental controls, the contribution would be even stronger.
