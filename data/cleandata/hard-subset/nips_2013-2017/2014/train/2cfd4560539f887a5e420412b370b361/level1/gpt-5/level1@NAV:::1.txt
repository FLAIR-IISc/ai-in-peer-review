This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of tree-structured composition, thereby adding “depth in space” to the usual “depth in structure.” The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes to allow a word-to-phrase transformation, enabling ReLU activations and the use of large, fixed pretrained word vectors. Trained with dropout and AdaGrad, the model is evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-class and binary sentiment classification. Under a fixed parameter budget, deeper-but-narrower models outperform shallow counterparts, and the best configuration achieves state-of-the-art results compared to RNTN, MV-RNN, DCNN, and Paragraph Vectors. Exploratory analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest layer-wise specialization: lower layers track lexical/syntactic patterns; higher layers capture sentiment intensity and polarity. This relates to prior NeurIPS and ML work on deep recurrent architectures and the distinction between temporal vs spatial depth (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), and extends tree-structured composition models (Socher et al., EMNLP 2013) by introducing stacked recursion and rectified units.

Quality: The technical contribution is clearly specified with equations, training details, and a principled parameter-matched comparison across depths. The empirical methodology is sound, though limited to a single dataset, and significance testing is absent. Ablations (e.g., impact of untying, ReLU, dropout individually) would strengthen causal claims. Computational costs and training stability (beyond a small L2 on activations) are not quantified.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally clear. It would benefit from explicit complexity analysis, reporting of runtimes, and a more detailed description of the dropout sharing scheme across nodes and layers.

Originality: Stacking recursive layers for trees is a natural but nontrivial extension; the untying of leaves/internals with ReLU in tree models is a useful design choice. The novelty is moderate but substantive, reinforced by insightful qualitative analyses.

Significance: Results advance SOTA on SST and suggest a broadly applicable design pattern for structured NLP. Generality is underscored by the architecture, but empirical scope is narrow (only SST).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear architectural advance: depth-in-space for trees with parameter-controlled comparisons.
- Strong results vs. well-known baselines; sensible training recipe.
- Insightful analyses revealing layer roles.

Cons for acceptance:
- Single dataset; no statistical tests.
- Missing ablations and computational cost analysis.
- Dependence on gold parses; robustness to parse errors untested.

Overall: A solid, well-executed contribution likely of interest to NeurIPS; I lean toward acceptance after minor revisions addressing ablations and reporting.
