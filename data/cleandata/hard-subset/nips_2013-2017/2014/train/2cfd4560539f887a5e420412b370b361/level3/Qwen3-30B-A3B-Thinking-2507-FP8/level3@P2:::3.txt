

This paper introduces "deep recursive neural networks" (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers to add hierarchical depth in *space* (across layers) while preserving depth in *structure* (within layers). The authors apply this architecture to fine-grained sentiment analysis on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art results. The work extends prior recursive neural networks (RNNs) [7,8] and deep recurrent networks [10,12] by combining depth in structure (via recursion) and depth in space (via stacking layers), building on the conceptual distinction between these two forms of depth. It relates to Socher et al.’s recursive neural tensor networks (RNTN) [8] and Hermans & Schrauwen’s deep RNNs [12], but generalizes the depth concept to recursive architectures.

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with precise definitions (Section 2), thorough experimental setup (Section 3), and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The architecture is logically motivated, and the results (Table 1) robustly demonstrate that deeper networks (with fixed parameter count) outperform shallower counterparts and prior baselines (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors). The use of dropout and ReLUs (with fixed word vectors) is well-justified and contributes to strong empirical gains.  
*Originality*: The core idea of stacking recursive layers to achieve hierarchical compositionality is novel in the recursive network context, though it is a natural extension of depth concepts in recurrent networks [12]. The exploration of layer-specific compositionality (via input perturbation and nearest neighbors, Figures 3–4) provides valuable qualitative insights.  
*Significance*: Achieving new state-of-the-art results on SST is impactful for sentiment analysis. The framework could generalize to other structured NLP tasks (e.g., paraphrase detection [9]), though the paper does not explore this. The focus on *how* depth in space improves compositionality (vs. depth in structure) advances the understanding of hierarchical representations.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: Several methodological choices lack justification. The decision *not* to fine-tune word vectors (Section 3.1) is questionable: fine-tuning could allow shallow models to match deep RNNs, undermining the "depth advantage" claim. The binary classification protocol (Section 3.2) is flawed—training includes neutral examples but testing discards them, conflating sentiment polarity with neutral labels. This requires re-evaluation following standard protocols. The omission of Kalchbrenner et al.’s convolutional sentence model [18] (a strong CNN baseline for SST) weakens the comparison.  
*Clarity*: Key notation is undefined (e.g., $W^{\eta}$, $W^{xh}$ in Equation 3), and parameter dimensions/distributions are not summarized, hindering reproducibility. The speculative claim (lines 148–154) that "each layer captures different aspects" needs stronger empirical support beyond the perturbation experiment (Figure 3).  
*Originality*: While the architecture is a logical extension, the novelty is moderate—depth in space was already explored in deep RNNs [12], and the recursive stacking is a direct adaptation. The paper could better distinguish its contribution from prior work.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Strong empirical results (new SOTA on SST).  
- Clear, well-structured writing with sufficient detail.  
- Insightful qualitative analysis of layer-specific compositionality.  
- Practical use of dropout/ReLUs for stability.  

*Con*:  
- Unjustified omission of word vector fine-tuning (may invalidate depth claims).  
- Flawed binary classification protocol.  
- Missing key baseline (Kalchbrenner et al. [18]).  
- Speculative claims without empirical backup.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with strong empirical results, but critical methodological gaps weaken its claims. **Recommendation: Weak Accept**. The work is significant for sentiment analysis and advances the understanding of hierarchical compositionality, but the unresolved issues (especially the binary protocol and fine-tuning omission) require clarification in the final version. The authors should address these in revisions to strengthen the argument that depth *in space* (not just width) is beneficial. If revised to include the missing baseline, justify the fixed word vectors, and correct the binary evaluation, this could be a strong contribution. As-is, the paper is valuable but not fully convincing on the depth advantage.
