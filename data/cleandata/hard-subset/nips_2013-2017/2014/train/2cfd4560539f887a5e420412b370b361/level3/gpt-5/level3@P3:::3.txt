Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth across space” in addition to the inherent “depth across structure” induced by a parse tree. Parameters are shared across heights within a layer, and the model uses ReLU activations and dropout. Word representations are kept fixed to pre-trained embeddings. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for both fine-grained (5-way) and binary sentiment classification, with strong empirical results. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality.

The idea is a natural generalization of deep recurrent networks (stacked RNNs) to tree structures: it combines recursive composition within each layer with feed-forward connections across layers. Relative to earlier recursive models (e.g., Socher et al.’s RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN), the contributions are the cross-layer depth, the use of rectifiers, and dropout. Relative to sentence-level architectures (e.g., Kalchbrenner et al.’s DCNN), it remains tree-structured and supervised at all tree nodes, leveraging phrase-level labels in SST.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean and useful generalization of depth-in-time (stacked RNNs) to depth-in-structure (stacked recursive layers), with parameter sharing within layers.
- Strong empirical performance on SST; clear gains over a matched-parameter shallow recursive baseline, and compelling gains from dropout and ReLUs.
- Careful training recipe (AdaGrad, identity-based initialization, early stopping) and equal-parameter comparisons between shallow vs deep architectures.
- Clear writing and generally sufficient detail for reproduction; helpful qualitative analyses showing layer-specific behaviors.
- The choice to keep embeddings fixed simplifies the analysis and isolates the contribution of architectural depth and regularization.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Notation and clarity:
  - The notation W^η (or W^eta) and W^xh is never explicitly defined; please define these clearly on first use. Split equations by input-to-hidden vs hidden-to-hidden cases and provide a concise table summarizing parameter sets, dimensions, and sharing/untied choices per layer (for leaves vs internals and across layers).
  - Equations (3)–(4) would benefit from separating leaf vs internal cases and explicitly listing V matrices for leaves vs internals in higher layers, with shapes.
- Speculative claims (lines 148–154): Several statements about what different layers learn (e.g., “intensity vs direction”) read as hypotheses. Please provide quantitative evidence: controlled ablations, diagnostic classifiers probing what each layer encodes, or quantitative perturbation analyses across many sentences with statistical tests.
- Baselines:
  - The dynamic convolutional sentence model (Kalchbrenner et al., DCNN) is a key contemporary baseline. Ensure DCNN is included and clearly reported in the main results table with the same data split and evaluation protocol. Provide a discussion analyzing where deep RNNs outperform/underperform relative to DCNN.
  - Report variance over multiple random seeds for your models and, if available, for baselines. Single-run results can be noisy on SST.
- Embedding fine-tuning:
  - The decision to fix word vectors may confound the architectural claims. Fine-tuning often yields large gains on SST and could allow a strong single-layer recursive model to match or exceed the deep model. Please add ablations that fine-tune embeddings for shallow and deep variants to clarify the unique contribution of depth.
- Dropout design:
  - You share the same dropout mask across all nodes in a sentence and layer. This is an unusual choice; please justify theoretically or empirically. Compare to independent node-wise dropout masks and to variational/locked dropout variants, and report the effect on performance and stability.
- Stability:
  - Instead of penalizing activations with a small L2 term, consider and/or compare to better-established stability techniques: gradient clipping/truncation, max-norm or spectral-norm constraints on W, orthogonal/identity initialization for recursive matrices, or weight normalization. These can improve optimization without dampening activations.
- Binary classification protocol:
  - The binary evaluation is not comparable to standard SST binary results because training uses neutral phrases/sentences and the decoder simply collapses 5-way predictions. Please retrain binary classifiers following the standard protocol that excludes neutral examples during training and evaluation, and report results alongside prior work.
- Scope of claims:
  - The central promise of depth across space should be validated beyond settings with dense phrase-level supervision. On datasets with only a single root label per sentence, does depth across space still help? Please add experiments on global-label datasets (e.g., sentence/sentence-pair tasks without phrase labels) and include ablations on:
    - proper binary SST protocol,
    - with/without embedding fine-tuning,
    - depth vs width under fixed parameter budgets.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with good training details and strong results on SST. However, some design choices (fixed embeddings, shared dropout mask, binary evaluation protocol) weaken the interpretability and comparability of the gains. Additional ablations and proper binary training are needed to solidify the empirical claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow, with useful qualitative analyses. The undefined notation (W^η, W^xh) and mixing of cases in the equations should be fixed. Please add a parameter/dimension summary and make baseline comparisons (especially DCNN) prominent.

Originality
The architecture is a natural and novel extension of stacked recurrent networks to recursive structures. While conceptually incremental, it is executed well and contextualized appropriately.

Significance
Given the centrality of compositionality and trees in NLP, the idea is timely and likely to influence future work. The demonstrated gains with dropout and ReLUs in recursive settings are noteworthy. Broader significance would be strengthened by results on datasets without phrase-level supervision and by cleaner binary comparisons.

Requested additional experiments/analyses
- Proper SST binary protocol results (train/test without neutral).
- Embedding fine-tuning ablations for shallow vs deep.
- Dropout mask ablation (shared vs independent) and stability ablations (e.g., gradient clipping).
- Prominent and thorough comparison to DCNN.
- Report mean ± std over multiple runs.
- Optional: experiments on sentence-level datasets without phrase labels to assess generality.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated extension of depth to recursive models.
- Strong SST results; compelling depth vs width comparisons under fixed parameter budgets.
- Useful training recipe (ReLUs + dropout) for tree-structured models.
- Good qualitative insights and generally reproducible.

Cons for acceptance
- Some claims are speculative and under-validated.
- Key empirical choices (fixed embeddings, shared dropout) not sufficiently justified; missing ablations.
- Binary results not following the standard protocol.
- Need to ensure strong baselines (DCNN) are included and analyzed prominently.

Overall
Promising and well-presented work with strong results and a simple, general idea. With the requested ablations, corrected binary protocol, clearer notation, and a more prominent DCNN comparison, this would be a solid contribution. I lean toward accept contingent on addressing these empirical and clarity issues.
