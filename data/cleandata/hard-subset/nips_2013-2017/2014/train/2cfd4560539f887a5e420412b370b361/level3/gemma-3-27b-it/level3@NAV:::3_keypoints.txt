- Introduces deep recursive neural networks by stacking recursive layers to model hierarchical compositionality in language.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) and reports improved performance over shallow models and baselines.  
- Leverages prior work on recursive/recurrent networks and uses pre-trained embeddings, dropout, and ReLU activations.  
- Presents layer-specific analysis via input perturbation and nearest-neighbor inspection to probe learned representations.  
- Novelty is incremental—a natural generalization of depth to recursive nets rather than a major paradigm shift.  
- Fails to justify why adding depth across the tree structure is necessary given trees already encode hierarchical depth.  
- Decision not to fine-tune word embeddings is questionable and may bias results; a shallow model with fine-tuning could match performance.  
- Using shared dropout units across layers is unjustified; layers might need separate dropout rates.  
- Omits comparison to a key baseline (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014 convolutional sentence model).  
- Claims about layer-specific compositionality are speculative and lack strong empirical support.  
- Binary classification results are suspect because neutral examples were included contrary to standard practice.  
- Notation (e.g., W^eta, W^xh) is undefined and equations should more clearly separate inputs, hidden layers, and parameters.  
- The claim that depth “helps” is weak because experiments focus only on root-label datasets; global-label datasets are needed.  
- Overall recommendation: reject in current form but encourage revisions to address methodological issues and missing comparisons.
