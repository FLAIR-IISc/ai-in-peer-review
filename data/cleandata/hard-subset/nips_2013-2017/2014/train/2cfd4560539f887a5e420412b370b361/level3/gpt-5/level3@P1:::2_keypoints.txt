- Paper proposes deep recursive neural networks that add "depth in space" via stacked recursive layers with inter-layer connections and untied leaf/internal parameters, using ReLUs and dropout.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank with supervision at every tree node and shows consistent improvements over shallow recursive nets.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art accuracy versus RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN.  
- Qualitative analyses indicate lower layers capture lexical/syntactic patterns and intensity while higher layers encode sentiment polarity and composition.  
- Well-situated relative to prior recursive and deep recurrent work; main novelty is per-node vertical connections and untied leaf/internal mapping.  
- Model specification and training details (AdaGrad, shared-dropout masks, L2 regularization, initialization) are clear and technically sound.  
- Empirical depth-vs-width comparisons at fixed parameter budgets support the claim that depth helps beyond width.  
- Missing key ablations: remove V (vertical) connections, output attached to multiple layers, tied vs. untied leaf/internal, ReLU vs. tanh, with vs. without dropout, fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings.  
- Protocol concern: Table 1(a) seems to use test-set performance for model/hyperparameter selection, risking overfitting; model selection should be done on dev and test reported once.  
- Exposition is clear and well-organized, but there are typos and at least one malformed/repeated sentence to fix.  
- Authors should clarify computational cost and parameter counts per configuration and compare training/inference time to RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN.  
- Using ReLUs in tree composition and untying leaf/internal parameters are thoughtful and contribute to effectiveness.  
- Results are significant: simple modular architecture achieving SOTA on SST and offering interpretable compositional hierarchies with likely applicability to other tree-structured NLP tasks.  
- Pros: coherent motivation, strong gains at comparable parameter budgets, effective design choices, and insightful qualitative studies.  
- Cons: potential test-set tuning, missing ablations, limited evaluation scope (only SST), and minor editorial issues.  
- Suggested actions: redo dev-only model selection, add (at least) the critical ablation dropping V and analyze output-layer attachment, report runtimes/parameter counts, discuss gradient clipping trade-offs, and proofread.  
- Verdict: Technically solid, clear, and likely influential; recommend acceptance if authors fix evaluation protocol and add key ablation(s) and clarifications.
