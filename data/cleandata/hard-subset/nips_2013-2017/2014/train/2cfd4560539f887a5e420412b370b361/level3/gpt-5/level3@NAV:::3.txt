Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) that add “depth across space” by stacking multiple recursive layers while sharing parameters across heights within each layer. The model uses ReLUs and dropout, and fixes word embeddings to pre-trained vectors. The approach is analogous to deep recurrent networks that combine depth-in-time and depth-in-space, now instantiated for tree-structured computation. Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), the method reports strong results, outperforming shallow recursive baselines and prior work such as RNTN and Paragraph Vectors. The idea is conceptually a natural generalization of stacking known from recurrent nets (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen) to tree-recursive models (Goller & Küchler; Socher et al.).

Quality: The experimental section is thorough on SST and the paper is largely reproducible. Notably, performance gains from ReLUs and dropout are compelling. However, several choices are insufficiently justified: (i) fixing rather than fine-tuning embeddings may confound the attribution of gains to depth; a tuned shallow model might close the gap. (ii) The binary results are problematic since training uses neutral labels; this deviates from the standard protocol and should be redone. (iii) Shared dropout units across nodes require justification. (iv) Stability is handled with small L2 on weights/activations; alternative, standard remedies (gradient clipping/truncation, weight-norm constraints) should be considered and compared. Some statements (lines 148–154) about what layers capture are speculative; provide empirical probes/ablations.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and detailed, but notation needs fixes. W^eta and W^xh are undefined; split equations for leaf inputs vs hidden nodes and add a summary of parameter sets and dimensions.

Originality: The architecture is novel for recursive nets, though primarily an expected extension of known depth-for-recurrence ideas. The empirical observation that ReLUs+dropout substantially improve recursive models is noteworthy.

Significance: Results on SST are strong, yet broader significance would be clearer with ablations (depth vs dropout/activation; with/without embedding fine-tuning) and with datasets that only have root labels, to test whether depth-across-space helps without per-node supervision. Include a direct comparison to Kalchbrenner et al.’s convolutional sentence model under the same protocol.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, reproducible paper; simple idea with strong results on SST
- Interesting and useful regularization/activation choices for recursive models
- Novel stacking of recursive layers

Cons against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to deep recurrent stacking
- Confounded design (fixed embeddings), missing/unclear baseline comparison
- Nonstandard binary evaluation; speculative claims without quantitative support
- Notation issues and under-justified regularization choices
