- Summary: Introduces Deep Recursive Neural Network that stacks recursive layers over tree structures, uses ReLU/dropout and fixed pre-trained embeddings, and reports state-of-the-art results on SST.

- Strength — Originality: Novel extension of depth to recursive networks ("spatial" depth) with strong empirical gains on a challenging benchmark.

- Strength — Technical quality: Successfully adapts ReLU/dropout to recursive setting and provides qualitative analyses suggesting hierarchical representations.

- Strength — Clarity/reproducibility: Well-written with visualizations and sufficient experimental/hyperparameter detail for reproduction.

- Weakness — Fixed embeddings: Keeping word vectors fixed is unjustified and may confound depth benefits; ablation with fine-tuning is needed.

- Weakness — Binary evaluation protocol: Binary-task results are invalidly computed by training on 5-way data then discarding neutrals at test time; must follow standard train/validate/test removal of neutrals.

- Weakness — Shared dropout: Using the same dropout mask across all nodes within a sentence/layer is non-standard and unexplained; its impact should be analyzed.

- Weakness — Notation/equations: Combined equations for leaves and internal nodes are dense; separate formulations and a parameter-dimension table would improve clarity.

- Weakness — Stability technique: Using L2 penalty on activations to prevent instability is unconventional; compare to standard fixes like gradient clipping or weight constraints.

- Weakness — Hierarchical claims unsupported: Anecdotal qualitative examples don't prove layered hierarchy learning; require rigorous quantitative analysis to substantiate.

- Weakness — Generalizability/ supervision dependence: Results rely on dense SST supervision; it's unclear if benefits hold when only root labels are available—experiments on global-label datasets are needed.

- Final assessment: Promising and elegant contribution with strong SST results, but experimental flaws (embedding tuning, binary protocol, ablations) weaken claims about the benefits of depth.
