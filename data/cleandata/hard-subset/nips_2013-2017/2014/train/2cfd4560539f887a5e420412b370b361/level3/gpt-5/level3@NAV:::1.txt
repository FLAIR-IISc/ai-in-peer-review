This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers, thereby distinguishing depth in structure (tree recursion) from depth in space (stacking). The model propagates information through parse-tree structure within each layer and vertically across layers via inter-layer connections, with supervision applied at the final layer across all tree nodes. The authors evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment classification, comparing against shallow recursive nets, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Results show consistent gains from adding depth under parameter-matched comparisons and a new state of the art on SST. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrase retrieval across layers) suggest that different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality and sentiment intensity.

Quality: The work is technically sound and carefully executed. Training and regularization choices (rectifiers, dropout, AdaGrad, untied leaf/internal parameters) are well motivated and likely contribute to robustness. The parameter-controlled comparisons are appreciated. However, statistical significance is not reported, and the scope is limited to a single dataset. The ablation between depth and other factors (rectifiers, dropout, untying) could be more thorough to isolate the source of gains over prior shallow baselines and over RNTN/MV-RNN.

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The architecture and training procedure are clearly described, and the qualitative probes are illuminating. A deeper discussion of why stacking helps in tree-structured composition—and why the specific design choices (only top-layer supervision, rectifiers, width–depth trade-offs) were selected—would improve clarity.

Originality: Stacking recursive layers is a simple but appealing extension that parallels deep recurrent models in time (e.g., Elman, Schmidhuber; more recently hierarchical/stacked RNNs). The contribution is incremental but novel within tree-structured models, and the qualitative analyses are insightful.

Significance: The idea is practical and likely to be adopted; achieving state-of-the-art SST results strengthens the case. Still, broader impact is tempered by evaluation on a single task and lack of analysis across domains or structures.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated architecture; simple to reproduce.
- Strong, parameter-matched empirical gains and SOTA on SST.
- Useful qualitative analyses indicating layer specialization.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited dataset and task scope; no significance testing.
- Insufficient disentanglement of depth vs. other design factors.
- Limited justification for architecture choices and depth selection.

Suggestions: Add statistical tests, multi-dataset evaluations (e.g., paraphrase, entailment), ablations for rectifiers/dropout/untied leaves, comparisons to alternative inter-layer connections (e.g., residuals, gated/tree-LSTM variants), and discussion on computational cost and inference speed.
