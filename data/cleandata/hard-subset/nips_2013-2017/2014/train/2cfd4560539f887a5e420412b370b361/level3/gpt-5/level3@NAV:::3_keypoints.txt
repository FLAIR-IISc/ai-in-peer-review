- Paper proposes deep recursive neural networks that add “depth across space” by stacking recursive layers with shared parameters, using ReLUs, dropout, and fixed pre-trained embeddings.  
- Evaluated on SST with strong results, outperforming shallow recursive baselines and prior work like RNTN and Paragraph Vectors.  
- Conceptually a natural extension of depth-for-recurrence ideas from recurrent nets to tree-structured computation.  
- Experimental section is thorough and largely reproducible; ReLUs and dropout show compelling performance gains.  
- Fixing rather than fine-tuning embeddings is insufficiently justified and may confound attribution of gains to depth.  
- Binary-label results use neutral labels in training, deviating from standard protocol and requiring re-evaluation.  
- Use of shared dropout units across nodes is not justified and needs explanation or comparison.  
- Stability is handled via small L2 penalties on weights/activations; the paper should compare standard alternatives (gradient clipping, truncation, weight-norm constraints).  
- Claims about what individual layers capture (lines 148–154) are speculative and need empirical probes or ablations.  
- Notation problems: W^eta and W^xh are undefined; equations should separate leaf inputs vs hidden nodes and include a parameter/dimension summary.  
- Architecture is novel for recursive nets but is largely an expected extension of known stacking ideas from recurrent networks.  
- The empirical finding that ReLUs + dropout substantially improve recursive models is a noteworthy contribution.  
- Broader significance is unclear without ablations (depth vs activation/regularization), tests on root-only label datasets, and a direct comparison to Kalchbrenner et al.’s convolutional sentence model.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear, reproducible work; simple idea with strong SST results; useful regularization/activation choices; novel stacking for recursive layers.  
- Cons against acceptance: incremental novelty, confounded design (fixed embeddings), missing/unclear baselines and nonstandard evaluation, speculative claims, and notation/regularization justifications lacking.
