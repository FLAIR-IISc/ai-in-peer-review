This paper introduces the deep Recursive Neural Network (deep RNN), an architecture that extends standard recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. This introduces a notion of "depth in space" analogous to what has recently been explored for recurrent neural networks, in addition to the inherent "depth in structure" of tree-based models. The proposed model leverages several modern techniques, including rectified linear units (ReLUs), dropout for regularization, and pre-trained word embeddings which are kept fixed during training. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating that deeper, narrower models outperform shallower, wider counterparts with a similar parameter count. The best-performing deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art results on the fine-grained sentiment classification task, outperforming previous methods including the RNTN and Paragraph Vectors.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this work is its compelling empirical results. The paper presents a novel and intuitive extension of recursive neural networks, providing a clear path to increasing model capacity and representational power. It successfully demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach, achieving new state-of-the-art performance on a widely used and challenging benchmark. Another notable contribution is the successful application of techniques like ReLUs and dropout in this recursive setting. The finding that these components, along with pre-trained embeddings and the "untying" of leaf and internal node transformations, significantly boost the performance of even a shallow baseline (48.1% vs. the original 43.2%) is an important result in its own right. The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, providing sufficient detail for the core model and experiments to be reproducible. The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) provide interesting, though preliminary, evidence that different layers may indeed learn different aspects of compositionality.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses in its experimental design and justification of certain modeling choices that temper the main conclusions.

In terms of clarity, the methodology section could be improved. The notation in Equation (3) (W^η·, W^xh·) is not explicitly defined, leaving the reader to infer its meaning from the text. It would be clearer to present separate equations for compositions involving leaf nodes versus internal nodes and to provide a summary of the parameter sets and their dimensions. Furthermore, the claims in lines 148–154 regarding the benefits of depth (e.g., that a single layer must learn a representation that is "both useful and sufficient") are speculative and would be stronger if supported by empirical analysis or more formal arguments.

My main concerns, however, lie with the experimental methodology:

1.  **Fixed Word Vectors:** A major concern is the decision to keep word vectors fixed. While this simplifies the model, it introduces a significant confounder. It is plausible that a shallow RNN, when allowed to fine-tune its embeddings, could learn representations that match or exceed the performance of the deeper models. Without this ablation study, it is difficult to isolate the benefit of depth from the specifics of the training setup. The gains may come from the model learning to operate on the fixed general-purpose embeddings, a task that might be easier for a deeper architecture.

2.  **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment is methodologically flawed. By training on a 5-class problem and then mapping the outputs to binary decisions (while discarding neutral examples at test time), the model is not optimized for the binary task. For the results to be comparable to prior work, the model should be trained specifically on the binary-labeled data, following the standard protocol for this dataset. As presented, these results are not a reliable measure of the model's performance on this task.

3.  **Justification of Choices:** Several design choices lack justification. The use of shared dropout units across all nodes in a tree for a given layer (line 251) is an interesting variant, but its advantages over independent dropout are not discussed or empirically validated. Similarly, the use of a small L2 penalty on activations for stability is pragmatic, but alternatives common in the RNN literature, such as weight-norm constraints or gradient clipping, are not mentioned as potential alternatives.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Pro:**
*   Proposes a novel architecture (deep RNN) that is a logical and well-motivated extension of existing recursive and recurrent models.
*   Achieves new state-of-the-art results on the challenging Stanford Sentiment Treebank fine-grained task, outperforming strong baselines.
*   Provides interesting qualitative analyses suggesting that different layers learn different compositional properties, opening avenues for future work.
*   The work is clearly presented and appears to be reproducible.

**Con:**
*   Significant methodological concerns in the experiments, particularly the lack of word vector fine-tuning and the incorrect protocol for binary classification, which weaken the claims about the superiority of the deep architecture.
*   Key modeling choices (e.g., shared dropout) and statements about the benefits of depth are not sufficiently justified or empirically supported.
*   Minor lack of clarity in the mathematical notation in the methodology section.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting idea with strong headline results. However, the methodological issues, particularly the lack of fine-tuning as a baseline comparison and the flawed binary evaluation, are significant concerns that prevent a confident assessment of the core claims. I would be inclined to raise my score if the authors could address these points in a revision, for instance, by adding an experiment with fine-tuned embeddings for both shallow and deep models and re-running the binary classification experiment with the correct protocol.

A final question for the authors: how do you expect the benefits of this spatial depth to translate to tasks where supervision is only available at the root of the tree (i.e., a single sentence-level label)? The rich, node-level supervision signal of the SST might be a critical factor for effectively training these deeper models, and its absence could pose a significant challenge.
