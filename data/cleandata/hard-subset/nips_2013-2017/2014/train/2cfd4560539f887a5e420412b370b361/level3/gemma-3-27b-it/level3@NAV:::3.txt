## Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers, aiming to capture hierarchical representations in language compositionality. The core idea leverages the success of deep learning by extending the depth concept, previously applied to recurrent networks, to the recursive domain. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improved performance compared to shallow counterparts and existing baselines. 

The work builds upon previous research in recursive neural networks [6, 7, 8] and deep recurrent networks [10, 11, 12], offering a natural generalization of depth. The authors’ choice to use pre-trained word embeddings, dropout, and ReLU activations is well-motivated by prior successes in deep learning [15, 20, 21]. Achieving state-of-the-art results on the SST is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. The exploration of layer-specific representations through input perturbation and nearest neighbor analysis provides initial insights into how the network learns compositional meaning.

However, several aspects require attention. While the concept of extending depth to recursive networks is novel, its necessity feels somewhat incremental, being a “natural generalization” rather than a paradigm shift. The authors don't fully justify why depth *across space* is crucial when the underlying structure already provides inherent depth. The experimental design choices are also questionable; specifically, the decision to *not* fine-tune the word embeddings is concerning. This could artificially inflate the results, as a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might achieve comparable or better performance. The justification for shared dropout units (line 251) is lacking – why not allow each layer to learn its own optimal dropout rate? 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comparison to Kalchbrenner et al.’s (2014) convolutional sentence model, a crucial baseline in the field. The claims made in lines 148-154 regarding layer-specific compositionality are speculative and need stronger empirical support. The binary classification results are also suspect, given the inclusion of neutral examples in the training data—a standard protocol requires excluding these for fair comparison. Finally, the notation (W^eta and W^xh) is undefined, and the equations could benefit from clearer organization to delineate inputs versus hidden layers and parameter sets. The claim that depth “helps” is weak when only root-label datasets are used; experiments on global-label datasets would be more convincing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel extension of depth to recursive networks, strong empirical results on SST, clear writing and reproducibility.
**Arguments for Rejection:** Questionable experimental choices, missing baseline, speculative claims, undefined notation.

**Overall:** While the paper presents a sound and clearly written contribution, the methodological weaknesses and lack of comprehensive comparisons prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the outlined concerns.
