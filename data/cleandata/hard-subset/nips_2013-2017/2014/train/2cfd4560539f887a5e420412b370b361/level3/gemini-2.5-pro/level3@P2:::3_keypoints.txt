- Proposes deep recursive neural networks by stacking recursive layers to add "depth in space" on top of recursive "depth in structure."
- Model evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank and achieves new state-of-the-art on fine-grained sentiment classification.
- Novel and meaningful extension of recursive networks that increases expressive power for structured data.
- Strong empirical evidence: deeper, narrower models outperform shallower, wider models with similar parameter counts.
- Paper is generally well written with sufficient methodological detail for reproduction.
- Qualitative analyses suggest different layers capture different compositional aspects (words → syntax → sentiment).
- Notation in Equation 3 is unclear; terms like W^(η)· and W^(xh)· are not defined and should be separated for leaf vs. internal nodes.
- Intro claims about how shallow vs. deep layers handle composition are speculative and should be softened or empirically supported.
- Major experimental concern: word embeddings were kept fixed; fine-tuning embeddings may change the depth benefit and requires an ablation study.
- Binary classification evaluation uses a non-standard protocol (training on 5-class then discarding neutrals at test); results are not comparable to prior work and must follow the standard setup.
- Missing comparisons to other strong sentence models (e.g., DCNN), weakening the baseline set.
- Unclear justification for sharing dropout units across nodes in a layer and for using activation L2 penalty over standard stability techniques like gradient clipping or weight norm constraints.
- Experiments are limited to SST where node-level supervision is abundant; unclear if spatial depth benefits tasks with only root-level supervision.
- Overall: promising, state-of-the-art contribution but methodological and experimental issues (fixed embeddings, binary protocol, shared dropout, limited scope) must be addressed to fully support the paper’s claims.
