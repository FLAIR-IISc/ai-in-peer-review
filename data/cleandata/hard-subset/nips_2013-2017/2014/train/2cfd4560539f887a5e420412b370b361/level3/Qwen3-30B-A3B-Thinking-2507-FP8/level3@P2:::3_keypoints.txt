- Proposes "deep recursive neural networks" by stacking recursive layers to add hierarchical depth in space while preserving structural recursion.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).  
- Positions the work as an extension combining depth in structure (recursion) and depth in space (layer stacking), relating to RNTN and deep RNN literature.  
- Paper is very well-written, with clear definitions, thorough experimental setup, and reproducible details.  
- Empirically shows deeper networks (with fixed parameter count) outperform shallower models and prior baselines.  
- Use of dropout and ReLUs, with fixed word vectors, is practically effective and justified for stability in experiments.  
- Novelty: stacking recursive layers to achieve hierarchical compositionality is new in recursive networks, though conceptually a natural extension of deep RNNs.  
- Provides qualitative analysis of layer-specific compositionality (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) offering useful insights.  
- Methodological concern: authors do not fine-tune word vectors, which could let shallow models match deep RNNs and undermines the depth claim.  
- Evaluation issue: binary classification protocol is flawed—training includes neutral examples while testing discards them, requiring re-evaluation under standard protocols.  
- Missing comparison to a strong baseline (Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional sentence model), weakening empirical claims.  
- Clarity gaps: some notation (e.g., W^η, W^{xh}) and parameter dimensions/distributions are undefined, hindering full reproducibility.  
- Some claims (e.g., layers capture different aspects) are speculative and need stronger empirical support beyond the provided perturbation experiment.  
- Overall judgment: technically sound with strong results but critical methodological gaps; recommendation = Weak Accept pending fixes (include missing baseline, justify fixed vectors, correct evaluation, and bolster empirical claims).
