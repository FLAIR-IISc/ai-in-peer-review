## Review of “Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language”

This paper introduces a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) architecture, extending traditional recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The core idea is to capture hierarchical representations in language, analogous to the depth achieved in recurrent and feedforward networks. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art results for fine-grained sentiment classification. 

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in recursive and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) [5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11, 12], and effectively draws parallels between depth in time (RNNs) and depth in structure (RNNs). The proposed deep RNN can be viewed as a combination of feedforward and recursive networks, allowing for richer compositional representations. The authors also make a compelling argument for “untying” leaf and non-terminal node parameters [2.2], enabling the use of pre-trained word vectors with smaller hidden representations and facilitating the application of rectified linear units (ReLUs). This is a clever design choice, well-motivated and supported by the experimental results.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical presentation is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to several strong baselines [8, 16, 17, 18]. The results are impressive, achieving new state-of-the-art performance on SST. The exploratory analyses, such as input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase analysis, provide valuable insights into the behavior of the different layers. The authors also demonstrate awareness of potential issues like exploding gradients and address them with appropriate regularization techniques.

However, there are a few weaknesses. The most significant is the missing ablation study regarding the V matrix connection [2.3]. It would be valuable to understand the impact of solely using the last hidden layer as parent inputs, potentially simplifying the architecture. Furthermore, Table 1(a) incorrectly reports results on the *final test set* instead of the development set, raising concerns about potential overfitting and tuning on the test data. This needs to be rectified. Finally, the paper contains minor typos and a repeated/malformed sentence that require attention.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated architecture achieving state-of-the-art results.
*   Clear and thorough presentation of the method and experiments.
*   Strong experimental validation and insightful qualitative analysis.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in NLP with a promising approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Incorrect reporting of results on the test set.
*   Missing ablation study regarding the V matrix connection.
*   Minor textual errors.



**Overall:** Despite the identified weaknesses, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed deep RNN architecture is a promising approach to capturing compositional semantics, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the issues with the test set reporting and including the requested ablation study. A careful proofread to correct the minor textual errors is also necessary.
