This paper proposes a novel architecture, deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs), which combines the strengths of recursive neural networks and deep feedforward neural networks. The authors introduce a new way of adding depth to recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers, allowing for a hierarchical representation of the input data. This architecture is evaluated on the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, and the results show that deep RNNs outperform their shallow counterparts and achieve state-of-the-art performance.

The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the effects of adding depth to recursive neural networks, including the impact on compositionality and the capture of different aspects of meaning at each layer.

One of the strengths of the paper is the use of dropout and rectified linear units (ReLUs), which are shown to improve performance and provide sparse representations. The authors also use pre-trained word embeddings, which are kept fixed during training, and provide a good baseline for comparison.

However, there are some model and experimental design choices that are questionable or not well justified. For example, the use of shared dropout units is not clearly justified, and the decision not to fine-tune the word vectors could potentially confound the results. Additionally, the binary classification results are problematic because the training included neutral examples, and the authors should redo the experiments following standard protocol.

The paper also lacks a comparison with other relevant baselines, such as Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional sentence model. Furthermore, the authors should provide more empirical evidence to support their claims about the benefits of adding depth across space, particularly in cases where only a single root label exists.

In terms of notation, the paper could benefit from clearer explanations of the notation used, particularly for the weights and parameter sets. The equations could also be split to distinguish between inputs and hidden layers, and the dimensions of the parameter sets should be summarized.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and interesting architecture, but there are some limitations and areas for improvement that need to be addressed.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, there are some areas for improvement, such as the use of shared dropout units and the decision not to fine-tune the word vectors.
