- Proposes a "deep" recursive neural network by stacking multiple recursive layers to add spatial depth analogous to deep recurrent networks.  
- Evaluates on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank using ReLU, dropout, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings, reporting new state-of-the-art results.  
- Originality: novel extension of depth to recursive models, building on prior recursive and deep recurrent work.  
- Significance: strong empirical results suggest spatial depth helps learn compositional representations.  
- Writing and reproducibility: paper is clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient experimental detail.  
- Empirical contribution: reports useful findings on the effectiveness of dropout and ReLUs in recursive models.  
- Missing baseline: fails to compare against the convolutional sentence model of Kalchbrenner et al.  
- Confounder: decision not to fine-tune word embeddings may bias results; shallow models with fine-tuning could match/exceed performance.  
- Methodological flaw: binary classification results are invalid because the model was trained on 5 classes instead of following the standard positive/negative-only protocol.  
- Presentation issues: some notation (e.g., W^xh) is undefined and key design choices like shared dropout lack justification.  
- Speculative claims: assertions about hierarchical nature of representations (lines 148–154) are unsubstantiated and need empirical support.  
- Recommended experiments: perform ablation on embedding fine-tuning, redo the binary experiment correctly, include the missing baseline, and test on datasets with only global labels to validate generality.
