

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language" (NIPS 2014)

This paper introduces "deep recursive neural networks" (deep RNNs), which extend traditional recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other. The authors argue that while recursive networks are "deep in structure," they lack the hierarchical representation capacity of deep feedforward and recurrent networks. By stacking recursive layers (adding "depth across space" with parameter sharing across heights within each layer), they create a model that can process information hierarchically. The approach builds upon recent advances in deep recurrent networks and extends them to structured inputs.

The paper makes several important contributions: It demonstrates that deeper recursive networks outperform shallow counterparts with the same number of parameters on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), achieves state-of-the-art results compared to previous baselines (including multiplicative RNN variants and paragraph vectors), and provides qualitative analyses suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of linguistic compositionality.

**Strengths**

The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient technical detail for reproduction, including specific implementation choices (e.g., using ReLUs, dropout, and fixed pre-trained embeddings). The authors' use of the SST dataset with node-level labels is particularly well-suited for evaluating hierarchical representations, as it provides richer supervision for backpropagation. The performance gains from dropout and ReLUs are well-documented and noteworthy, with the paper showing that a 3-layer RNN with dropout outperforms the same network with simple L2 regularization (49.5 vs. 46.06 on the fine-grained task). The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) provide valuable insights into how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

**Weaknesses**

Several methodological choices lack sufficient justification. The decision to keep word vectors fixed (not fine-tuned) is questionable, as this could confound results—fine-tuning might allow a single-layer RNN to match or exceed the performance of deeper models. The paper fails to include Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional sentence model as a baseline, which is a significant omission given its relevance to sentence representation learning. The binary classification results are problematic because training included neutral examples, yet the binary evaluation discarded these during testing—this violates standard protocol and requires re-evaluation following proper binary classification procedures.

The paper contains undefined notation (W^eta and W^xh are not explained) and makes speculative claims in lines 148-154 without empirical support. The use of shared dropout units (line 251) requires justification, as does the stability approach (small L2 penalty) rather than more standard methods like weight-norm constraints or gradient truncation. Additionally, the paper does not provide experiments on global-label datasets to test whether depth across space helps when only a single root label exists, which would strengthen the main claim.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound and well-executed, with sufficient experimental evidence to support its main claims. However, the lack of proper binary classification protocol and unaddressed methodological questions somewhat undermine the quality of the evaluation.

*Clarity*: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproduction. However, the undefined notation and lack of explanation for key choices (e.g., fixed word vectors) reduce clarity.

*Originality*: The core idea of stacking recursive layers is a natural generalization of depth in recurrent networks, but the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work. The authors would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent deep recurrent network approaches.

*Significance*: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical modeling of language, with strong empirical results on a standard benchmark. The insights about different layers capturing different aspects of compositionality are significant. However, the lack of fine-tuning experiments and proper binary protocol limits the significance of the reported performance gains.

**Recommendation**

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: Clear writing, strong empirical results on SST, valuable qualitative analyses of layer-wise representations, and the demonstration of depth's benefits in recursive networks.

**Con-acceptance arguments**: Unjustified methodological choices (fixed word vectors), missing baselines (Kalchbrenner et al.), problematic binary classification protocol, and speculative claims without empirical support.

This paper presents an interesting and well-executed extension of recursive neural networks with practical value for sentence representation learning. While the core idea is a natural generalization of depth in recurrent networks rather than a fundamentally novel approach, the empirical results and qualitative analyses provide valuable insights. The main concerns are methodological rather than conceptual, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the issues related to word vector fine-tuning, baseline comparisons, and classification protocol. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address these concerns in a revised version.
