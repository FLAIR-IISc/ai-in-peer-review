This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) that add depth “in space” by stacking multiple recursive layers, while retaining parameter sharing “in height” within each layer. The model distinguishes leaves from internal nodes (untied weights), uses rectified linear units and dropout, and connects supervision only to the top recursive layer. Training uses AdaGrad with pretrained (fixed) word embeddings. The method is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained and binary sentiment, showing improvements over shallow recursive baselines and previously reported systems (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors). Exploratory analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work: The architecture generalizes “deep in time” stacked recurrent models (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen) to tree-structured computation, complementing Socher et al.’s RAE/MV-RNN/RNTN lines that increase compositional capacity within a single layer. The use of ReLUs and dropout follows findings from deep feed-forward and convolutional networks. The evaluation on SST aligns with recent RNTN and DCNN results.

Strengths
- Clear core idea: stacking recursive layers to induce hierarchical abstraction over structures, separate from tree depth.
- Strong empirical performance on SST with careful parameter matching across depths; evidence that depth helps beyond width given similar parameter budgets.
- Practical contributions: untying leaves vs internals enables ReLUs and large pretrained embeddings with modest hidden sizes; dropout improves generalization.
- Good exposition overall, with insightful qualitative analyses illustrating layer roles.
- Sufficient training details to roughly reproduce (optimizer, initialization, dropout, early stopping).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Attribution of gains: While depth helps, several improvements (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout) also contribute. Ablations isolating each factor are limited; it remains unclear how much of the delta over strong shallow baselines is due to depth vs modern regularization/nonlinearity.
- Fixed embeddings: Not fine-tuning may understate shallow models; fine-tuning could let a single-layer model match deep performance. This confound should be addressed with controlled comparisons (fine-tuned shallow vs fine-tuned deep, fixed shallow vs fixed deep).
- Binary classification protocol: Mapping 5-way posteriors to binary without retraining is non-standard for SST. The usual protocol trains a binary classifier after removing neutral examples from train/dev/test. The current numbers should be clearly labeled and the standard binary setup reported.
- Missing/unclear baselines: The convolutional sentence model of Kalchbrenner et al. (DCNN) is listed but it is not clear whether its results are included in Table 1 nor how preprocessing/tokenization differences are handled. Please report DCNN numbers on the same splits, and, if possible, include other strong CNN baselines for 2014 (if available).
- Dropout design: The “shared dropout units across all nodes in a sentence-layer” choice (locked/structured dropout) should be justified with an ablation against independent per-node masks. Given shared weights across the tree, the bias-variance implications differ from standard feedforward dropout.
- Stability: Using an L2 penalty on activations is an unusual fix for exploding activations. Consider and/or compare with gradient norm clipping and weight norm constraints, which are common for recurrent/recursive training and may preserve capacity better.
- Overinterpretation: Claims that layers capture sentiment intensity vs direction (and more general statements about hierarchical representation, lines 148–154) are intriguing but speculative. Add targeted probing (e.g., linear classifiers trained on frozen layer representations for polarity vs intensity) or diagnostics across many examples to substantiate.
- Notation/clarity: The untying section overloads notation (W^η, W^xh) without a clear parameter map. Split equations for leaf vs internal cases; explicitly define parameter shapes (e.g., Wxh_L ∈ R^{|h|×|x|}, Whh_R ∈ R^{|h|×|h|}, V^(i) ∈ R^{|h_i|×|h_{i−1}|}), and summarize tied/untied sets in a table. This will help reproducibility and resolve ambiguity.

Quality
- Sound model and optimization choices; empirical comparisons are mostly fair with parameter controls across depth.
- However, some experimental protocols (binary) and missing ablations limit the strength of the causal claims about depth.

Clarity
- Generally well written and easy to follow; training details are appreciated. Please fix notation, add a parameter/shape table, and make the baseline reporting unambiguous.

Originality
- The stacking of recursive layers is a natural but novel extension of stacked RNNs to tree structures. Other components (ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, untied leaves) are known, but the combination and analysis are new.

Significance
- Likely to influence future tree-structured modeling by highlighting benefits of depth-in-space for compositionality. The gains on SST are meaningful, but broader evidence (datasets with only root-level labels, other tree tasks) would strengthen impact.

Requested additional experiments/analyses
- Proper SST-binary experiment (train/test without neutrals).
- Ablations: (a) shallow vs deep with/without embedding fine-tuning, (b) ReLU vs tanh, (c) independent vs shared dropout masks, (d) output attached to last layer vs all layers.
- Additional datasets where only root labels exist (e.g., MR, CR, SUBJ, MPQA, Pang & Lee MR, TREC) to test whether depth helps without per-node supervision.
- Diagnostic probes to support layer-interpretation claims.

Pros
- Clear idea; solid engineering; SOTA or competitive results.
- Insightful qualitative analyses.
- Well scoped and reproducible.

Cons
- Causal role of depth not fully isolated.
- Non-standard binary evaluation; unclear CNN baseline reporting.
- Some speculative claims; notation issues.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper presents a timely and useful architectural contribution with strong empirical results and thoughtful analysis. Addressing the evaluation protocol, clarifying baselines, and adding a few targeted ablations would solidify the claims and elevate the paper.
