

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs), a novel architecture that stacks multiple recursive layers to capture hierarchical compositionality in language, achieving state-of-the-art results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST). The work builds directly on Socher et al.'s recursive neural networks (RNNs) for sentiment analysis (NIPS 2013) but addresses a key limitation: while traditional recursive nets are "deep in structure" (depth = tree depth), they lack the hierarchical representation benefits of stacked deep feedforward networks. The authors propose stacking recursive layers (with inter-layer connections via matrix *V*), enabling each layer to learn distinct aspects of compositional meaning. This distinguishes their approach from deep recurrent networks (Hermans et al., NIPS 2013) that stack *temporal* layers.

**Strengths**  
The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The deep RNN architecture is well-motivated and cleanly implemented: untied leaf/internal parameters (Section 2.2) resolve a critical mismatch between dense word vectors and sparse phrase representations, enabling effective ReLU usage (a key insight validated by results). Leveraging pretrained 300D word vectors with small hidden layers (|h|=340) is both efficient and effective, avoiding quadratic complexity. Empirical validation is thorough: deep RNNs outperform shallow counterparts with identical parameter counts (Table 1a: 49.5% vs. 48.1% on SST 5-class), surpassing prior baselines (RNTN, Paragraph Vectors) and achieving SOTA. Qualitative analyses—input perturbation (Fig. 3) and nearest-neighbor phrases (Table 2)—convincingly show layered compositionality: Layer 1 captures word-level similarity, Layer 2 adds syntactic/semantic nuance, and Layer 3 encodes sentiment. The writing is exceptionally clear, with precise figures and methodological details enabling reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**  
The primary weakness is a critical methodological flaw: Table 1a reports test-set results for hyperparameter tuning (depth/width), violating standard ML practice. This risks overfitting to the test set (e.g., the "best" depth may be spuriously chosen). The authors should re-run experiments using development-set tuning and report those results. Second, the paper omits an essential ablation study: whether the inter-layer connection matrix *V* (Eq. 4) is necessary. If the network performs equally well without *V* (i.e., output layer connected only to the final hidden layer), the "deep" aspect loses novelty. The authors should clarify this by comparing models with and without *V*.

**Originality and Significance**  
The work makes a clear original contribution: stacking recursive layers (not just deeper trees) to create a true "depth in space" hierarchy, distinct from prior recursive nets. This advances the field beyond Socher et al. (NIPS 2013) and RNTN (NIPS 2013) by enabling multi-layer compositional learning. The significance is high: SST SOTA performance demonstrates practical value for fine-grained sentiment analysis, and the layered compositionality insights (e.g., Layer 2 capturing intensity vs. direction) offer new theoretical understanding of recursive processing. The approach could generalize to other structured NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, paraphrase detection), though the paper focuses narrowly on sentiment.

**Recommendation**  
This paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to deep learning for structured data. The core innovation (deep RNNs) is well-justified, and results are compelling. However, the test-set tuning error in Table 1a and missing *V*-matrix ablation weaken the claims. These are fixable issues (e.g., re-running experiments, adding ablation), and the paper’s strengths outweigh these weaknesses. The work is highly relevant to NIPS, advancing the state-of-the-art in compositional NLP with a novel architecture. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions** to address the methodological concerns. The authors should: (1) correct Table 1a to report development-set results for hyperparameter selection, and (2) include an ablation study comparing deep RNNs with and without *V* (i.e., output layer connected only to the final layer). The typos and repeated sentence noted in the key points are trivial and should be fixed in revision.
