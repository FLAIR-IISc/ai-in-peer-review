- Paper proposes stacking multiple recursive layers (“depth across space”) with shared parameters per layer, ReLUs, dropout, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings.  
- Evaluated on SST (5-way and binary) with strong empirical results and qualitative analyses suggesting layer-specific compositional behaviors.  
- Conceptual novelty: natural extension of stacked (time) RNNs to tree-structured (space) depth with parameter sharing.  
- Main contributions vs prior work: cross-layer depth, rectifiers, and dropout in recursive models; supervised training at all tree nodes leveraging SST phrase labels.  
- Strong empirical performance on SST and clear gains over a matched-parameter shallow recursive baseline.  
- Careful training recipe (AdaGrad, identity-based init, early stopping) and fair equal-parameter depth vs width comparisons.  
- Writing and methodological detail are generally sufficient for reproduction; useful qualitative analyses provided.  
- Keeping embeddings fixed simplifies attribution of gains to architecture and regularization.  
- Notation and clarity problems: undefined symbols (W^η, W^xh), mixed leaf/internal cases in equations, and missing parameter/dimension summary.  
- Speculative claims about layer semantics (e.g., “intensity vs direction”) lack quantitative validation and need diagnostic classifiers or statistical perturbation analyses.  
- Missing key baseline (DCNN) comparison in main results and lacking analysis of where deep RNNs win/lose relative to DCNN.  
- No reported variance across random seeds; single-run results on SST may be noisy and need mean±std.  
- Fixing embeddings may confound claims; ablations with embedding fine-tuning for shallow and deep models are needed.  
- Dropout design is unusual (same mask across all nodes per sentence/layer); justify or compare to independent/variational dropout variants.  
- Stability/optimization: consider and compare alternatives (gradient clipping, max-norm/spectral constraints, orthogonal init, weight normalization) instead of only L2 activation penalties.  
- Binary classification protocol is non-standard (includes neutrals and collapses 5-way outputs); retrain with standard SST binary protocol for fair comparison.  
- Scope concern: benefits of depth across space should be tested on datasets without dense phrase-level supervision to assess generality.  
- Requested additional experiments: proper SST binary protocol, embedding fine-tuning ablations, dropout mask ablation, stability/optimization ablations, DCNN comparison, and multiple-run statistics.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear idea, strong SST results, good depth-vs-width comparisons, and practical training recipe for tree models.  
- Cons for acceptance: speculative/unvalidated claims, unjustified design choices (fixed embeddings, shared dropout), non-standard binary evaluation, and missing strong baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: promising and well-presented; likely acceptable if authors address empirical ablations, clarity (notation), standard binary protocol, and stronger baseline comparisons.
