This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) that add “depth in space” to standard tree-structured models by stacking multiple recursive layers and introducing inter-layer connections at corresponding tree nodes. The model also unties parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes to enable a mapping from dense, pretrained word embeddings into a lower-dimensional phrase space, and it uses rectified linear units (ReLUs) and dropout for effective training without unsupervised pretraining. The architecture is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at every tree node. Empirically, deep RNNs consistently outperform shallow recursive nets with roughly equal parameter counts and achieve state-of-the-art accuracy versus RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases across layers) suggest a meaningful hierarchy: lower layers capture lexical/syntactic patterns and intensity, while higher layers better encode sentiment polarity and composition.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-positioned relative to recursive models for compositionality (Socher et al., EMNLP 2013; Socher et al., EMNLP 2012) and deep recurrent architectures that distinguish depth in time vs. space (e.g., Schmidhuber 1992; El Hihi & Bengio 1995; Hermans & Schrauwen 2013). It also compares to contemporary representation learning baselines such as Paragraph Vectors and CNN/DCNN approaches to sentence modeling. The key novelty is stacking recursive layers with explicit inter-layer connections V at each tree node, plus the untied leaf/internal parametrization that enables large pretrained embeddings with compact hidden states and ReLUs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The model is clearly specified (eqs. 3–5), training uses AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks per layer/sentence, stability via small L2 penalties on weights and activations, and a sensible initialization (Whh ≈ 0.5I). The qualitative analyses are thoughtful and consistent with the hierarchical interpretation.
- Empirical validation: Depth-vs-width comparisons at fixed parameter budgets support the central claim that adding depth (to a point) helps beyond just adding width. Strong results vs. RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN substantiate significance.
- Missing ablations: A key missing comparison is what happens if the inter-layer V term is removed (i.e., stacking without explicit vertical connections), or if the output is attached to multiple layers vs. only the last. Similarly, ablations for (i) tied vs. untied leaf/internal parameters, (ii) ReLU vs. tanh, (iii) with vs. without dropout, and (iv) fixed vs. fine-tuned word vectors would quantify which ingredients drive the gain.
- Protocol concern: Table 1(a) appears to report test-set performance for hyperparameter/model selection that should have been conducted on the development set. This risks overfitting to the test set. The authors should redo model selection on dev and report test only once for the selected configuration.

Clarity:
- The exposition is very clear and well-organized, with helpful figures and precise equations. The qualitative case studies are easy to follow and insightful.
- There are typos and at least one repeated/malformed sentence; these should be corrected.
- Please clarify computational cost and parameter counts per configuration and discuss training/inference time relative to RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN.

Originality:
- Stacking recursive layers with per-node inter-layer connections is a novel and well-motivated extension of structured composition models, closely paralleling “deep” recurrent networks but in tree space. Using ReLUs in tree-structured composition and untying leaf/internal spaces are also thoughtful design choices. The combination is original and practically effective.

Significance:
- Achieving SOTA on SST with a conceptually simple, modular architecture is valuable. The analyses offer evidence that depth yields interpretable compositional hierarchies, which is of broad interest to the NIPS community. The approach should be applicable beyond sentiment to other tree-structured NLP tasks (parsing-related classification, paraphrase, entailment), increasing impact.

Pros:
- Clear, coherent motivation and architecture; strong empirical gains at comparable parameter budgets.
- Clever, well-justified design choices (untied leaf/internal mapping, ReLUs, dropout with shared masks).
- Qualitative studies that illuminate what each layer learns and how depth helps compositionality.
- Uses large pretrained embeddings effectively while keeping hidden states compact.

Cons:
- Potential test-set tuning in Table 1(a); results should be recomputed with dev-based selection.
- Missing ablations obscure the relative contributions of V connections, ReLUs, untying, dropout, and embedding fine-tuning.
- Limited task scope (only SST); broader evidence would strengthen generality claims.
- Minor typos and one malformed/repeated sentence.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Please provide a dev-only model selection protocol and report test numbers only for the selected model. If Table 1(a) includes test-guided choices (depth, width, dropout), re-run with dev selection and update the table.
- Add ablations: (a) drop V (purely stacked layers without vertical connections); (b) connect outputs to all layers vs. only last; (c) tie leaf/internal weights; (d) tanh vs. ReLU; (e) dropout vs. L2 only; (f) fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings. Even a subset would clarify what drives improvement.
- Report parameter counts and wall-clock training/inference times vs. RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN.
- Discuss whether gradient clipping was tried vs. the L2-on-activations stabilization; include any observed trade-offs.
- Proofread for typos and fix the malformed sentence.

Verdict:
- The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and likely to be influential. Provided the authors address the test/dev protocol and add at least the most critical ablation (dropping V) plus a brief analysis of output-layer attachment, I recommend acceptance. Even without extensive new experiments, cleaning up evaluation protocol and clarifying the contributions of the architectural components would make this a strong NIPS paper.
