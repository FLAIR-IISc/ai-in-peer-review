Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers to separate “depth in structure” (tree recursion) from “depth in space” (stacked layers). Within each layer, information propagates along parse-tree edges; across layers, each node also feeds its hidden state to its counterpart in the next layer via a learned V connection. The model also “unties” parameters for leaf vs. internal nodes in the first layer, enabling different transformations from dense word vectors to phrase-space vs. within phrase-space. Rectified linear units (ReLUs) are used for hidden activations, and large, fixed, pretrained word vectors are leveraged while keeping smaller hidden representations at internal nodes.

The authors evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way and binary sentiment. They compare to shallow recursive nets (with and without their proposed untied/rectifier configuration), MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. They report that deeper, narrower deep RNNs outperform parameter-matched shallow RNNs, and their best model achieves state-of-the-art results on SST. Qualitative analyses suggest that lower layers capture local/lexical and syntactic regularities, while upper layers capture sentiment intensity and polarity.

This work is well-situated relative to prior recursive models for compositionality (MV-RNN, RNTN), convolutional sentence models (DCNN), and deep/stacked recurrent networks (Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio). The main novelty is a stacked architecture for tree-structured computation that couples structural recursion with across-layer node-to-node connections, plus practical design improvements (untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad).

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated architectural contribution: disentangling structural depth from representational depth and coupling them via V connections.
- Strong empirical performance on SST with careful parameter-matched comparisons: deeper is better up to a point.
- Pragmatic, effective design choices: untying leaf/internal weights, using ReLUs in tree recursion, leveraging large fixed word embeddings with small hidden phrase spaces, dropout regularization.
- Insightful qualitative analyses (input perturbation; layer-wise nearest neighbors) that support the claim that layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
- Exposition is generally very clear and thorough; training details (optimizer, initialization, regularization) are well documented.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Experimental protocol concern: Table 1(a) appears to report test results for many configurations that are then used to select hyperparameters, implying tuning on the test set. The standard protocol is to report development-set results for model selection and a single test result for the final chosen configuration. This needs to be corrected; otherwise, the reported gains may be optimistic.
- Missing ablations:
  1) Role of the inter-layer connection V: a variant that drops V (or replaces it with identity/skip, or only uses V at the final layer) would clarify how much benefit comes from explicit cross-layer coupling vs. simply stacking independent recursive layers.
  2) Output connectivity: the paper motivates attaching supervision only to the final hidden layer; please compare to attaching outputs to all layers (deep supervision) to assess whether this helps optimization or changes what layers learn.
  3) Component-wise contributions: quantify the effect of untying leaf/internal parameters, ReLUs vs. tanh, dropout vs. L2 only, and fixing vs. fine-tuning embeddings.
- Statistical robustness: no reporting of variance across runs/seeds or significance testing; given known variance on SST, this would strengthen claims.
- Reporting details: It would help to clarify the parameter budget matching across depths (exact counts per configuration) and provide training time/complexity.
- Minor clarity issues and typos: there are malformed sentences in Section 3 (e.g., “Recursive weights … 0.5I + where … is a small uniformly random noise” is missing a symbol; “All other weights are initialized as .” is incomplete), and reference typos (e.g., “Jeffrey Pennin(g)ton”).

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The modeling and training choices are reasonable and well justified, and results are compelling. However, the hyperparameter selection/reporting protocol must be rectified. Additional ablations and variance reporting would further substantiate the causal contribution of each design choice.

Clarity:
The paper is very clearly written and well organized. Figures help explain the architecture. Please fix the noted typos/malformed sentences, and make the evaluation protocol unambiguous (dev vs. test). Adding a concise table of parameter counts per configuration would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
Stacking recursive layers with explicit node-to-node connections across layers for tree-structured computation is a novel and natural extension of deep RNNs to the recursive setting. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and effective use of ReLUs in recursive composition are also noteworthy in this context.

Significance:
The results advance the state of the art on a widely used benchmark and provide a practical, general approach to deep compositional modeling. The qualitative analyses offer insight into how depth helps in tree-structured models. This is likely to influence subsequent work on deep structured and tree-based architectures.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, principled architectural advance for deep compositional modeling on trees.
- Strong empirical performance with fair parameter-matched comparisons.
- Effective, well-motivated engineering choices (untied params, ReLUs, dropout).
- Insightful qualitative evidence for layer-wise specialization.
- Generally clear exposition and thorough training details.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Apparent tuning on the test set in Table 1(a); must be corrected.
- Missing ablations on V connections and supervision strategy across layers.
- No variance/significance analysis; limited robustness reporting.
- Minor but noticeable typos/malformed sentences.

Requests for the authors:
- Re-run or at least re-report model selection strictly on the development set; report a single test result for the chosen model. If possible, provide results averaged over multiple seeds with standard deviations.
- Add ablations: (i) remove or modify V connections; (ii) attach outputs to all layers; (iii) isolate contributions of untying, ReLUs, dropout, and embedding fine-tuning.
- Include exact parameter counts per configuration and a brief note on training time/compute.
- Fix the typos/malformed sentences and reference errors; define the initialization noise explicitly and complete the sentence “All other weights are initialized as …”.

Overall assessment:
A strong paper with a clean idea, solid empirical gains, and good analysis. Provided the evaluation protocol is corrected and key ablations are addressed (or at least partially in the rebuttal with a commitment for the camera-ready), I recommend acceptance. The work is clearly in scope for NIPS and makes a meaningful contribution to deep learning for structured language.
