This paper introduces a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) for modeling compositionality in language. The architecture is constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers, where each node in the parse tree of a sentence has a representation at each layer. Information flows both recursively within a layer (from children to parent) and feed-forwardly across layers (from a node in layer `i-1` to the same node in layer `i`). This approach is analogous to the recent development of deep recurrent neural networks, distinguishing between "depth in structure" (the recursive composition) and "depth in space" (the stack of layers). The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, a challenging fine-grained sentiment classification task. The paper makes several additional contributions, including a parameterization that "unties" the weights for leaf (word) and internal (phrase) nodes and the successful application of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and dropout in a recursive setting. The proposed deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art results on this benchmark, outperforming previous recursive models and other strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—a deep recursive neural network—is a novel, clever, and natural extension of existing recursive and recurrent architectures. While recursive networks are "deep in structure," this work introduces a hierarchical "depth in space" that allows for progressively more abstract representations of phrases, as supported by the qualitative analysis. The impressive state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark demonstrate the significance of this new architecture and suggest it will be influential for future work on modeling structured data.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. The architectural choices are well-motivated and experimentally validated.
    *   The decision to untie the parameters for leaf and internal nodes is a particularly insightful contribution. It elegantly solves two problems: it allows the model to leverage large, pre-trained word vectors without a quadratic explosion in the number of recursive parameters, and it facilitates the use of non-negative rectifier activations for phrase representations while using standard dense embeddings for words.
    *   The successful integration of recent deep learning techniques like ReLUs, pre-trained word vectors, and dropout into the recursive framework is a solid piece of engineering that contributes significantly to the model's strong performance.

3.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for deep recursive models is clearly articulated by drawing an analogy to deep recurrent networks. The architecture is explained precisely with equations and a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The experiments are well-designed (with one exception noted below) and the results are presented clearly.

4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The inclusion of exploratory analyses, such as the input perturbation experiment and the nearest neighbor phrase examples, is a major strength. These analyses provide valuable intuition about the model's inner workings, suggesting that different layers indeed learn to capture different aspects of compositionality—from word-level similarity in the first layer to syntactic patterns in the second and more abstract sentiment in the final layer. This goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics and strengthens the paper's scientific contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Improper Use of Test Set:** A significant concern is the reporting in Table 1(a). The table compares models of varying depths and widths (`l` and `|h|`) based on their performance on the final test set. This implies that model selection (i.e., choosing the best hyperparameters) was performed using the test set. Standard machine learning practice requires that such tuning be done on a validation/development set to avoid reporting results that are overfitted to the test data. The results for hyperparameter sweeps should be on the development set, with only the final, selected model's performance reported on the test set. The authors should clarify their procedure and, if necessary, correct the table to report development set accuracies for the model comparison.

2.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The proposed deep RNN architecture (Eq. 4) includes a connection from a node's representation in layer `i-1` to its representation in layer `i` via the `V` matrix. This means a parent node in layer `i` receives input from its children in layer `i` *and* from its own representation in layer `i-1`. An interesting and important ablation would be to evaluate a simpler "stacked" model where the `V` matrix connection is removed. In such a model, a parent's representation would depend only on its children's representations within the same layer. This would help isolate the contribution of the direct inter-layer connections at each node versus the effect of simply stacking recursive computations.

3.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains a few typos and at least one repeated/malformed sentence in the introduction that should be corrected. For example, the sentence "Recursive neural networks, comprise a class of architecture that operates on structured inputs, and in particular, on directed acyclic graphs" is grammatically awkward. A careful proofread would improve the manuscript's polish.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and powerful architecture with compelling empirical results. The ideas are well-motivated, the exposition is clear, and the analyses are insightful. The main weaknesses relate to the experimental reporting methodology and a missing ablation study. The issue with reporting model selection results on the test set is a serious one that needs to be addressed. However, given the strength and significance of the core contribution, I believe this is a correctable flaw. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise their experimental reporting to follow standard practices and, if possible, include the suggested ablation study to further strengthen their analysis. This work is a clear step forward for modeling compositionality and is an excellent fit for NIPS.
