## Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers, aiming to capture hierarchical representations in language compositionality. The core idea builds upon existing recursive neural networks and draws parallels to the concept of depth in recurrent networks, proposing a novel way to combine structural and spatial depth. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating strong performance and achieving state-of-the-art results. 

The work is a logical extension of previous research in recursive and recurrent neural networks (e.g., Socher et al., 2012, Schmidhuber, 1992). It addresses a recognized limitation of standard recursive networks – a lack of hierarchical representation comparable to deep feedforward or recurrent architectures. The use of dropout and ReLU activations is a sensible choice, aligning with best practices in deep learning, and the performance gains observed are noteworthy. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction, including clear explanations of the architecture and training procedure.

However, while the method is novel, its connection to simply generalizing depth, as the authors acknowledge, feels somewhat incremental. The core contribution lies in applying the concept of stacked layers to recursive networks, but the theoretical justification for *why* this spatial depth is beneficial beyond simply increasing model capacity isn’t fully explored. Several experimental choices are questionable. The decision to fix word vectors is particularly concerning; fine-tuning them could significantly impact performance and potentially allow simpler models (like a single-layer RNN) to achieve comparable results. The justification for shared dropout units is also lacking. Furthermore, the binary classification results are suspect due to the inclusion of neutral examples during training, violating standard protocol. 

Several key baselines are missing, notably Kalchbrenner et al.'s (2014) convolutional sentence model, a strong contender on SST. The speculative statements in lines 148-154 require empirical support.  Stability concerns could be addressed with techniques like weight normalization or gradient clipping. Finally, the benefit of spatial depth is less clear when dealing with datasets having only a single root label; additional experiments on global-label datasets and ablations (fine-tuning embeddings, proper binary protocol) are needed to strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of depth to recursive networks.
*   Strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark.
*   Clear writing and reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution; lacks strong theoretical justification.
*   Questionable experimental choices (fixed embeddings, shared dropout).
*   Missing baselines and flawed binary classification evaluation.



**Overall Score:** Borderline Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding baseline inclusion, binary classification protocol, and justification for key design choices. A more thorough ablation study and discussion of the theoretical underpinnings would also significantly strengthen the paper.
