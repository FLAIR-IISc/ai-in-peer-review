## Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper introduces a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) architecture, extending traditional recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The authors motivate this approach by drawing parallels to the success of deep architectures in other domains and aim to capture more abstract compositional representations in language. They evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art results for fine-grained sentiment classification.

The work builds upon the established field of recursive neural networks (RNNs) [6, 7, 8] and connects it to the recent advancements in deep learning, specifically drawing inspiration from deep recurrent networks [10, 11, 12].  The core idea – adding depth to recursive structures – is a logical progression and a potentially fruitful direction for modeling hierarchical compositional semantics.  The authors’ careful consideration of untying leaf and non-terminal node parameters [Section 2.2] and the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) are clever design choices, well-motivated by the properties of word vectors and the challenges of deep network training. The experimental validation, achieving strong results on the SST dataset, lends further credibility to their approach.  The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) provide valuable insights into what each layer of the network may be learning. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear exposition and strong empirical performance. The authors thoroughly explain the motivation, methodology, and results. However, a critical weakness is the missing ablation study. Specifically, the paper doesn't investigate the impact of the V matrix connection (Eq. 4) – whether performance degrades if parent inputs solely come from the last hidden layer. This would help to isolate the benefits of the multi-layer recursion.  Additionally, Table 1(a) incorrectly reports results on the *test* set instead of the *development* set, raising concerns about potential test set tuning. This needs to be rectified. Finally, the paper contains several minor issues: typos and a repeated sentence require attention. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture extending RNNs with depth, leading to SOTA results.
*   Clear and well-written paper with insightful qualitative analysis.
*   Well-motivated design choices (untied parameters, ReLUs) and solid experimental validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incorrect reporting of development set results in Table 1(a).
*   Missing ablation study to evaluate the importance of the V matrix connection.
*   Minor issues with typos and repeated content.



Overall, despite the identified weaknesses, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the reported issues, particularly the correction of Table 1(a) and the inclusion of the requested ablation study. The ideas are novel, the results are compelling, and the paper has the potential to stimulate further research in deep compositional models of language.
