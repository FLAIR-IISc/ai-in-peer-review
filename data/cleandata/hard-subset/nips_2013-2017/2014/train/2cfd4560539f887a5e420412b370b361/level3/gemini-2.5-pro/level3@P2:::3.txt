This paper introduces the deep recursive neural network (deep RNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers. This approach draws inspiration from recent work on deep recurrent networks, distinguishing between "depth in structure" (the recursive application of weights within a tree) and "depth in space" (stacking layers). Each layer in the deep RNN processes the representations from the layer below it, in addition to performing its own recursive compositions. The authors evaluate this model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, using modern deep learning techniques such as pre-trained word vectors, rectified linear units (ReLUs), and dropout for regularization. The proposed deep RNNs are shown to outperform their shallow counterparts and achieve new state-of-the-art results on the fine-grained sentiment classification task, surpassing previous methods like the Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN) and Paragraph Vectors.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of adding spatial depth to recursive neural networks is a novel and logical extension of contemporary trends in deep learning, particularly the development of deep recurrent networks. This work provides a new direction for increasing the expressive power of models that operate on structured data.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging and widely used benchmark (Stanford Sentiment Treebank). The quantitative results in Table 1 clearly demonstrate that deeper, narrower models consistently outperform shallower, wider models with a similar parameter count, providing strong evidence for the proposed architecture's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The methodology section, along with the detailed description of the experimental setup, provides sufficient information for an expert reader to reproduce the key results.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The exploratory analyses in sections 3.2 (Input Perturbation and Nearest Neighbor Phrases) provide valuable, albeit preliminary, insights into the model's inner workings. These experiments suggest that different layers may indeed learn to capture different aspects of compositionality, from word-level features to syntactic patterns and finally to sentiment-focused semantics.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The paper is promising, but several aspects of the methodology and experimental design require clarification and revision to solidify its contributions.

*   **Clarity of Methodology:**
    *   The notation in Equation 3 is not fully defined. While the text explains the concept of "untying" leaves and internal nodes, the terms `W^(η)·` and `W^(xh)·` are not explicitly defined, which makes the equation hard to parse. It would be clearer to present two separate equations for the leaf and internal node cases. A table summarizing the different parameter matrices and their dimensions would also improve clarity.
    *   The claims in the introduction (lines 148–154) about how shallow vs. deep layers handle composition are speculative. While the qualitative analysis provides some support, these statements are presented as facts and should be softened or more directly linked to empirical evidence later in the paper.

*   **Experimental Design:**
    *   **Fixed Word Vectors:** A major experimental choice is to keep the word vectors fixed during training. This is not well-justified and may confound the comparison between shallow and deep models. It is plausible that a shallow RNN could achieve similar or better performance if its word embeddings were fine-tuned for the task. To isolate the benefit of depth, an ablation study where embeddings are fine-tuned for both shallow and deep models is necessary.
    *   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification results are evaluated using a non-standard protocol. The model is trained on the 5-class problem (which includes neutral examples) and then evaluated on binary sentiment by discarding neutral examples at test time. The standard protocol for this dataset is to remove neutral sentences from the training set as well before training a binary classifier. The reported results are therefore not directly comparable to prior work and need to be re-evaluated using the standard setup.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The comparison could be strengthened by including results from other contemporary sentence modeling architectures, such as the Dynamic Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) of Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL 2014), which has shown strong performance on related tasks.
    *   **Regularization and Stability:** The justification for sharing dropout units across all nodes in a given layer (line 251) is absent. This is an unusual implementation choice, and its motivation and impact should be discussed. Additionally, while the L2 penalty on activations is reported to solve stability issues with ReLUs, more standard techniques like gradient clipping or weight norm constraints should be considered and compared as alternatives.

*   **Scope of the Contribution:**
    *   The experiments are conducted exclusively on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, where rich supervision is available at every node of the parse tree. This is an ideal scenario for this architecture, as error signals are plentiful throughout the structure and depth of the model. A key question is whether the benefits of spatial depth hold for more common NLP tasks where supervision is only available at the root level (i.e., a single sentence-level label). Including experiments on such datasets would significantly broaden the impact and generality of the paper's claims.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** Novel architecture that logically extends recursive networks, strong state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark, clear presentation, and interesting qualitative analysis.
*   **Con-acceptance:** Several methodological choices are not well-justified (fixed embeddings, non-standard binary protocol, shared dropout) and may weaken the central claims about the benefits of depth. The generalizability of the approach to tasks with only sentence-level supervision is not demonstrated.

In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable and interesting contribution. However, the experimental weaknesses, particularly the lack of fine-tuning on embeddings and the incorrect binary classification protocol, need to be addressed to make the conclusions fully convincing. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can address these points in their rebuttal and commit to including revised experiments in the final version.
