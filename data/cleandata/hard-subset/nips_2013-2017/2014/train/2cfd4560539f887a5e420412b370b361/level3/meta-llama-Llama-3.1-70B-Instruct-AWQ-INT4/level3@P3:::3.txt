This paper proposes a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) architecture by stacking multiple recursive layers. The authors apply this architecture to the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using binary parse trees as the structure. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

The proposed architecture is a generalization of the deep feedforward neural network with an additional structural processing within each layer. The use of multiple layers allows for a hierarchical representation of the data, which is not present in conventional recursive neural networks. The authors also employ techniques such as dropout and rectifier linear activation units to improve the performance of the network.

The paper presents experimental results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) dataset, which show that deep RNNs outperform shallow recursive nets of the same size. The authors also compare their results to previous work on the task, including a multiplicative RNN and the more recent Paragraph Vectors method. The results demonstrate that deep RNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance on the task.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its components, making it easy to follow and understand. The paper also includes a thorough analysis of the experimental results, which provides valuable insights into the performance of the proposed architecture.

However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement. One of the main limitations is the lack of a clear justification for the use of shared dropout units. The authors mention that dropped units are shared for a single sentence and a layer, but it is not clear why this is necessary or beneficial. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation for why they did not fine-tune the word vectors during training. This could potentially confound the results, as fine-tuning might allow a single-layer RNN to match or exceed the performance of the deep RNN.

Another limitation is the lack of a baseline comparison to Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional sentence model. This model has been shown to achieve state-of-the-art results on several sentence classification tasks, and it would be interesting to see how the proposed architecture compares to this model.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel architecture that combines the benefits of deep feedforward neural networks with the structural processing of recursive neural networks. The use of multiple layers and the employment of techniques such as dropout and rectifier linear activation units are also novel contributions.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in natural language processing, namely fine-grained sentiment classification. The proposed architecture achieves state-of-the-art results on the SST dataset, which demonstrates its potential for practical applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a novel architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging task. However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement that need to be addressed.
