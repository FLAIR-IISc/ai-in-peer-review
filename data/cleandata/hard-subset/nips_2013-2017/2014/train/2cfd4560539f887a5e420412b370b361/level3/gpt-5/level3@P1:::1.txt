This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) that add depth-in-space to depth-in-structure by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree-structured inputs. Within each layer, composition is performed recursively over parse trees; across layers, node representations are propagated vertically, enabling hierarchical abstraction akin to stacked feed-forward or recurrent networks. The authors further “untie” leaves and internal nodes at the first layer to allow different transformations from word vectors to phrase space, enabling the use of rectifiers and pretrained embeddings without quadratic parameter growth. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks per layer/tree, small L2 on weights and activations, and supervised signals only at the top layer. The method is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way and binary sentiment classification, showing that deeper, narrower models outperform shallow counterparts with matched parameter budgets, and achieving state-of-the-art results over RNTN, MV-RNN, paragraph vectors, and DCNN. Exploratory analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrase analyses) suggest that different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on recursive neural networks for compositionality (Socher et al. EMNLP’11–’13) and connects to deep recurrent networks that distinguish depth in time vs space (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). It leverages rectifiers (Glorot et al.), dropout (Hinton et al.), and pretrained embeddings (Mikolov et al.). Comparisons to paragraph vectors (Le & Mikolov) and DCNN (Kalchbrenner et al.) situate the contribution within contemporary representation learning for text.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated extension: stacking recursive layers is a simple, appealing idea that addresses the lack of hierarchical abstraction in standard tree RNNs.
- Solid empirical evidence: consistent gains over parameter-matched shallow baselines; new SOTA on SST for both fine-grained and binary settings.
- Thoughtful engineering: untying leaves vs internals to accommodate rectifiers and large pretrained word vectors; careful regularization (dropout, L2 on activations), initialization, and optimization.
- Informative analyses: input perturbation and nearest-neighbor studies offer insights into what different layers learn.
- Writing and organization: the paper is pleasant to read, with sufficient details to reproduce training settings.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited justification of architectural choices: The paper does not deeply analyze why depth (vs width) helps in tree settings, beyond empirical gains. Suggestions:
  - Provide ablations isolating the contributions of depth, rectifiers, dropout, and untied leaves/internals (e.g., shallow ReLU vs tanh; tied vs untied; with/without dropout) under matched parameter budgets.
  - Examine gradient norms and signal propagation across layers and tree depth to substantiate claims about alleviating vanishing gradients.
- Output connection only at the top layer: While motivated by interpretability, it would be informative to compare to auxiliary supervision at intermediate layers and to connecting outputs at all layers (with parameter sharing or hierarchical losses), which might improve optimization and performance.
- Scope and robustness: Results are restricted to a single dataset and task that relies on high-quality parses. Suggestions:
  - Evaluate robustness to parsing errors (automatic vs gold parses) and report results on additional tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment) to demonstrate generality.
  - Report runtime, memory, and parameter counts per configuration to clarify the cost of adding depth.
- Statistical rigor: No confidence intervals or significance tests are provided; given modest improvements in some settings, include variance across random seeds.
- Embeddings are fixed: While simplifying, not fine-tuning may understate performance; include a comparison with fine-tuning to clarify trade-offs.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound, with careful training and regularization. The experimental protocol is reasonable and comparisons are appropriate for the time. However, more comprehensive ablations and statistical analysis would strengthen the causal interpretation of the gains and the understanding of design choices.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Architectural equations and training settings are described in detail. Minor improvements: explicitly tabulate parameter budgets across depth/width settings; add pseudocode for forward/backward passes highlighting interleaved backprop-through-structure and across layers.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers for trees is a natural but, to my knowledge, novel extension at the time, distinct from prior RNTN/MV-RNN formulations and from depth-in-time RNNs. The untying of leaves/internals combined with rectifiers is also a clean, useful design choice. Overall the contribution is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
The paper advances state of the art on a widely used benchmark and proposes a general architectural pattern that is likely to be adopted in tree-structured modeling. While demonstrated on SST only, the idea should transfer to other structured NLP tasks, making the contribution of practical interest.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general architectural idea with clear motivation and good execution.
- Strong empirical results with careful baselines and parameter control.
- Insightful qualitative analyses; clear writing and reproducibility details.
- Likely to influence follow-up work on deep tree models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of why depth helps and why specific design choices were made.
- Evaluation confined to one dataset/task; no robustness or speed/complexity analysis.
- Lack of statistical significance reporting and comprehensive ablations.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed paper with a clear idea, good results, and useful insights. Despite some missing analyses, the contribution is timely and of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to add ablations, statistical tests, and broader evaluation.
