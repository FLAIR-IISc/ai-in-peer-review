Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of a parse-tree-structured network. Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up via shared structural weights; across layers, each node passes its hidden state to its counterpart in the next layer, introducing “depth in space” in addition to the usual “depth in structure.” The authors also “untie” weights between leaves and internal nodes (mapping word vectors to phrase vectors) and use rectified linear units with dropout.

The work is positioned against prior recursive architectures for sentiment (e.g., semi-supervised RAEs, MV-RNN, RNTN) and recent deep/stacked recurrent networks that distinguish depth in time vs. space. It evaluates on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with fine-grained (5-way) and binary sentiment, comparing to BINB, shallow RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. The proposed deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art results at the time of writing and outperforms shallow counterparts with a comparable parameter budget. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs. polarity).

Strengths
- Clear idea and presentation: The paper is well written, easy to follow, and pleasant to read. Figures and equations make the architecture concrete.
- Technically sound: The formulation, training scheme (AdaGrad, dropout), and stability measures (L2 on activations/weights, initialization) are appropriate. The parameter-matched comparisons across depths are careful.
- Appealing core idea: Stacking recursive layers is a simple, compelling extension that bridges deep RNNs and tree-structured nets.
- Solid empirical support: New SOTA on SST; deep models outperform shallow variants with the same parameter budget. The qualitative analyses are informative and align with the compositionality narrative.
- Sensible engineering choices: Untying leaves and internals, fixed pretrained embeddings, rectifiers with dropout, and shared dropout masks per sentence/layer are well-motivated and yield gains.

Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of “why depth helps”: Beyond qualitative probes, the paper lacks a more principled explanation or controlled ablations isolating which elements (untied weights, ReLU, dropout, fixed embeddings) drive the gains vs. depth per se.
- Architectural choices under-explained: Why connect supervision only to the top layer? Why this specific inter-layer connection (simple affine V) vs. alternatives (e.g., residual/skip connections, tying across layers)? Some ablations would clarify.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are focused on SST; no tests on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, NLI), no analysis of robustness to parse errors, and no comparisons across constituency vs. dependency trees.
- Statistical rigor and variability: No report of variance over runs or statistical significance testing; given the modest margins typical on SST, variances would help.
- Efficiency characterization: Training/inference costs, memory usage as a function of depth, and convergence behavior are not quantified.

Quality
The work is technically sound, and claims are supported by experiments and clear training details. The parameter-controlled depth study is commendable. However, stronger ablations and significance/variance reporting would strengthen the empirical case. The qualitative analyses are insightful but could be more systematic (e.g., category-wise error analysis: negation, intensifiers, contrast).

Clarity
Very clear and well-organized. The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is well articulated. The untying of leaves vs. internals and the use of ReLUs are explained with practical motivation. Suggestions: briefly discuss computational complexity vs. depth and include a schematic for the shared dropout mask.

Originality
The contribution—stacking recursive layers for tree-structured computation—is a natural yet novel extension relative to prior recursive models (RAE, MV-RNN, RNTN) and complements earlier work on deep recurrent nets. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior art and cites relevant literature.

Significance
Moderate to high. The idea is simple and likely to be adopted; the results advance the state of the art on a widely used benchmark. The conceptual framing (layer-wise compositional roles) is useful. Broader impact depends on generalization beyond sentiment; demonstrating benefits on other structured NLP tasks would raise significance further.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide ablations: (a) shallow vs. deep with/without ReLU; (b) tied vs. untied leaves/internals; (c) with/without dropout; (d) output connected to last layer only vs. to all layers or with intermediate supervision. This would clarify the source of gains.
- Analyze variance: Report mean±std over multiple random seeds and significance tests vs. strongest baselines.
- Efficiency: Include wall-clock time, memory, and depth-vs-width trade-offs; discuss practical depth limits and whether degradation at higher depth is due to narrowing or optimization.
- Parsing sensitivity: Evaluate on noisy parses or different parsers; consider dependency trees.
- Generalization: Test on at least one additional structured task (e.g., paraphrase detection as in prior work) to demonstrate that benefits are not SST-specific.
- Error analysis: Break down performance on linguistic phenomena (negation, intensifiers, coordination) to connect qualitative insights to quantitative outcomes.
- Architectural alternatives: Comment on residual/skip connections or gating across layers (motivated by stabilization and information flow), even if left as future work.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea with clear novelty for tree-structured models
- Strong, carefully controlled empirical results; new SOTA on SST
- Clear, polished writing and thorough experimental details
- Useful qualitative analyses indicating different roles for different layers

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical/empirical analysis of why depth helps beyond qualitative probes
- Evaluation largely confined to one dataset/task; no robustness or cross-task validation
- Missing variance/significance reporting and efficiency characterization
- Architectural choices (top-only supervision, inter-layer connection form) not ablated

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clean and appealing extension to recursive networks, demonstrates state-of-the-art results with reasonable analyses, and is likely to influence follow-up work on deep structured composition. With a few additional ablations and broader evaluation, it would be even stronger.
