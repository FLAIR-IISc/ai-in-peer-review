This paper proposes a "deep" recursive neural network by stacking multiple recursive layers, thereby adding depth "in space" in a manner analogous to how deep recurrent networks add depth to the temporal dimension. The authors evaluate this architecture on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, employing modern deep learning techniques such as ReLU activations, dropout, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings. They demonstrate strong empirical performance, achieving new state-of-the-art results on this benchmark.

The originality of the work lies in its direct and novel extension of depth to recursive models for language, building upon prior work in both recursive and deep recurrent networks. The significance of the paper is highlighted by its impressive results, suggesting that this spatial depth is a powerful mechanism for learning compositional representations. Furthermore, the paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for its experiments to be reproduced. The authors' findings on the effectiveness of dropout and ReLUs in this context are also a noteworthy contribution.

However, the quality of the experimental evaluation has several weaknesses that undermine the central claims. A key baseline, the convolutional sentence model of Kalchbrenner et al., is missing from the comparison. The decision not to fine-tune word vectors is a major confounder; a shallow network with fine-tuned embeddings might match or exceed the deep model's performance, which would weaken the argument for depth. The binary classification results are methodologically flawed, as the model was trained on 5 classes (including neutral examples) instead of following the standard protocol of training only on positive/negative examples, and must be redone.

For clarity, some notation (W^xh) is undefined, and key design choices, such as the use of shared dropout units, lack justification. The claims in lines 148–154 about the hierarchical nature of representations are speculative and need empirical support.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should perform ablation studies on fine-tuning embeddings, conduct a methodologically sound binary classification experiment, and include the missing baseline. Additional experiments on datasets with only global labels would also help validate whether the benefits of depth extend beyond the richly supervised treebank setting. While the proposed model is promising, its contribution would be more significant with a more rigorous and complete evaluation.
