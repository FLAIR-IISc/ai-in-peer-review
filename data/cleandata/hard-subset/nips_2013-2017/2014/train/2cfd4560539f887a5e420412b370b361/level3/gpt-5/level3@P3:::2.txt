Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of tree structures and adding inter-layer connections at every node. Within a layer, composition is standard bottom-up tree recursion; across layers, each node’s representation is also connected to its counterpart in the next layer via a V matrix, producing depth in “space” in addition to depth in “structure.” The model cleanly unties parameters for leaves and internal nodes in the first layer, enabling large, fixed, pretrained word vectors at leaves and smaller hidden states for nonterminals; higher layers share a common phrase space. The authors adopt rectified linear units (ReLUs), dropout with shared masks across nodes in a layer, AdaGrad, and simple but careful initialization.

The work is well-situated in the literature on compositional models over trees (Socher et al.’s RAE/MV-RNN/RNTN), document/paragraph vectors, and deep recurrent networks in time (Schmidhuber; El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). The novelty is bringing depth-in-space to recursive models with explicit inter-layer, per-node skip connections, rather than simply stacking independent recursive modules or increasing width. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), deep RNNs outperform similarly parametrized shallow recursive baselines and achieve state-of-the-art results at the time.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural idea: per-node inter-layer connections (V) yield hierarchical representations across layers, not just deeper trees.
- Clever, well-motivated design choices that are experimentally validated:
  - Untying leaf and nonterminal parameters allows using large unsupervised word vectors (e.g., 300-d word2vec) while keeping compact hidden phrase representations, improving both modeling and parameter efficiency.
  - ReLUs are effective in the recursive setting, yielding better performance, faster convergence, and sparse activations; untying resolves the dense-to-sparse mismatch that would otherwise complicate shared parameters.
  - Dropout with shared masks across a tree is sensible and appears to contribute substantially.
- Strong empirical results and useful analyses:
  - Consistent gains from depth under fixed parameter budgets; best models outperform RNTN and Paragraph Vectors on fine-grained SST.
  - Input-perturbation and nearest-neighbor analyses suggest layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., syntactic patterns at middle layers, task-relevant sentiment at top).
- Exposition is generally very clear; the training details are reproducible.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Missing ablation critical to understanding the architecture: Evaluate models that remove the V connection (i.e., parent inputs come only from the composition within the current layer; no per-node input from the previous layer). This would test whether gains stem from inter-layer skip connections versus simply stacking more recursive transformations. Relatedly, please also compare connecting the classifier to all layers versus last-only.
- Table 1(a) appears to report performance for multiple hyperparameter settings on the final test set rather than the development set, implying test-set-informed tuning. Please report model-selection results strictly on the dev set and reserve a single evaluation on the test set for the chosen configuration.
- Typos and minor malformations in the text (e.g., stray commas, “All other weights are initialized as .”, duplication/malformed sentences) should be fixed.
- Fairness and robustness:
  - Since pretrained word vectors are fixed here, whereas some prior baselines learn or fine-tune embeddings, discuss the trade-offs and, if possible, include a shallow RNN baseline with the same pretrained fixed vectors and ReLUs (you partially do this) and a version that fine-tunes embeddings for completeness.
  - Provide variance across runs or multiple random seeds; deep models with ReLUs and dropout can be sensitive to initialization.
  - Depth-vs-width under constant parameter budgets is handled well, but a complementary comparison at matched widths (increasing parameters) would help isolate scaling behavior.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound and thoughtfully executed. The architectural formulation, training scheme, and regularization are appropriate. Claims are supported by results and insightful qualitative analyses. The main gap is the missing ablation for the V connection and the potential test-set tuning issue around Table 1(a), which should be remedied.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce. Please fix the noted typos/malformed sentences, clarify the mask-sharing scheme for dropout, and explicitly state which tables/rows reflect dev vs test performance.
- Originality: Bringing depth-in-space to recursive models with per-node inter-layer connections is a novel and nontrivial extension relative to prior recursive architectures (RNN/RAE/MV-RNN/RNTN). Untying leaves vs nonterminals to enable ReLUs and large fixed embeddings is a practical and impactful twist.
- Significance: Results advance the state of the art on a widely used benchmark, and the architectural idea is likely to influence subsequent tree-based models. The analysis indicating different roles for different layers is valuable.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations:
  - Remove V (no per-node inter-layer input), and report performance and learning dynamics.
  - Connect outputs to all layers vs last-only; report whether supervision to intermediate layers helps or hinders.
  - Compare ReLU vs tanh with the untied leaf/internal scheme, holding other factors fixed, to isolate the benefit of ReLU.
  - Quantify the gain from untying leaves vs internal nodes directly.
- Correct evaluation protocol: Use dev set for hyperparameter selection and only one final test evaluation; move Table 1(a) to dev results and add a single test row for the selected model.
- Fix typos and the repeated/malformed sentence; e.g., Section 3.1 “All other weights are initialized as .” and extraneous commas in early sections.
- Report mean±std over multiple seeds, or at least provide two runs for key settings.
- Discuss computational cost and training time as depth increases, and whether identity-plus-noise initialization is essential.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated architectural contribution with strong empirical gains.
- Effective and principled use of ReLUs, dropout, and pretrained embeddings in a tree setting.
- Solid analysis supporting the “different layers capture different aspects” claim.
- Relevance and potential impact for NLP compositional modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing ablations on the core inter-layer mechanism.
- Apparent test-set-informed hyperparameter reporting in Table 1(a).
- Minor but noticeable typos/formatting issues.
- Limited discussion of variance and computational trade-offs.

Overall recommendation: Accept if the authors address the evaluation protocol and add the key ablation on the V connection; otherwise, strong borderline/weak accept based on novelty and solid empirical gains. Confidence: high.
