The paper "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language" proposes a new architecture for modeling compositionality in natural language using recursive neural networks. The authors introduce a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers, allowing for a hierarchical representation of language. This approach is novel but essentially a generalization of the concept of depth used in recurrent networks.

The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The authors evaluate their model on the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank and achieve very strong performance, outperforming previous baselines. The use of dropout and ReLUs is noteworthy, and the performance gains from these techniques are interesting.

However, some model and experimental design choices are questionable or not well justified. For example, the authors do not explain the notation W^eta and W^xh, and the equations should be split for inputs vs hidden layers, with parameter sets/dimensions summarized. Additionally, the statements in lines 148-154 are speculative and need empirical backing.

Furthermore, the paper is missing a baseline comparison with Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional sentence model. The authors also do not justify why they did not fine-tune the word vectors, which could potentially confound the results. Fine-tuning might allow a single-layer RNN to match or exceed the performance of the deep RNN.

The use of shared dropout units is also not well justified, and the authors should consider alternative approaches such as weight-norm constraints or gradient truncation for stability. The binary classification results are problematic because the training included neutral examples, and the authors should redo the experiments following the standard protocol.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a deep recursive neural network architecture, which is a novel application of the concept of depth used in recurrent networks. However, the authors should provide more justification for their design choices and consider additional experiments to strengthen their claims.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, some design choices and notations are not well explained or justified.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction. However, some notations and equations could be improved for clarity.

Originality: The paper proposes a novel architecture for modeling compositionality in natural language using recursive neural networks. However, the idea is essentially a generalization of the concept of depth used in recurrent networks.

Significance: The paper achieves very strong performance on the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank. However, the authors should consider additional experiments to strengthen their claims and provide more justification for their design choices.
