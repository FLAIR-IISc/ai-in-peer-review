Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of parse trees. The key architectural ideas are: (i) depth in structure (from tree recursion) combined with depth in space (from stacking layers), (ii) “untying” parameters between leaves and internal nodes to map word vectors into a phrase space at the first layer, and (iii) using rectified linear units and dropout. The model is trained with AdaGrad and early stopping, and evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way and binary sentiment classification. Under a fixed parameter budget, deeper-but-narrower models outperform shallow counterparts; the best deep model achieves state-of-the-art performance over prior baselines including RNTN/multiplicative models and Paragraph Vectors. Exploratory analyses (input perturbation along a parse path and layer-wise nearest neighbors) suggest that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The work is well-positioned relative to: recursive models for parsing and sentiment (Socher et al., 2011–2013: RAE, MV-RNN, RNTN), distributed word vectors (Mikolov et al., 2013), document/paragraph vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014), and sentence CNNs (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). On the recurrent/deep learning side, it relates to notions of depth in time vs. stacked depth in recurrent nets (El Hihi & Bengio, 1996; Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013), and imports those ideas into tree-structured recursion.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-written paper with good motivation and background. The exposition of “depth in structure” vs. “depth in space” is helpful.
- Simple, appealing architectural extension: stacking recursive layers with a cross-layer connection per node; untying leaf/internal parameters enables the use of ReLUs with pretrained embeddings.
- Solid empirical grounding on a widely used benchmark (SST), controlling for parameter count across depths, and using standard regularization and optimization.
- Achieves new state-of-the-art results against strong baselines (RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN as reported).
- Useful qualitative analyses suggesting layer specialization (e.g., intensity vs. polarity), which provides insight beyond accuracy numbers.
- Training details are sufficiently specified (activations, dropout sharing across nodes, AdaGrad, initialization), which aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and points for improvement:
- Limited analysis of why additional stacked depth helps in trees beyond empirical gains. A more principled discussion or diagnosis (e.g., gradient flow across structure vs. space, representational capacity, or information separation) would strengthen the contribution.
- Ablation coverage is incomplete. The paper attributes gains partly to dropout and pretrained embeddings, but it does not systematically isolate the contributions of: untying leaves vs. internals; ReLU vs. tanh; connecting the classifier only to the top vs. to all layers; fixed vs. fine-tuned word vectors.
- Evaluation is on a single dataset and task. While SST is canonical for compositional sentiment, demonstrating generality on another structured task (e.g., paraphrase detection or other treebanked tasks) would increase significance.
- No statistical significance testing or variability estimates across runs; with relatively small absolute gains, confidence intervals would be helpful.
- Practical considerations (training speed, memory, and asymptotic complexity vs. depth; effect of parse errors) are not discussed.
- The comparison to DCNN and Paragraph Vectors mixes supervision regimes and input structures; a brief caveat or controlled setting would clarify fairness.

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The architecture is well-defined, and training details are careful. The parameter-controlled comparison across depths is a strong design choice. However, the lack of a thorough ablation/diagnostic suite makes it harder to attribute gains to depth vs. other design choices.

Clarity:
Very clearly written and easy to follow. Figures and equations convey the architecture well. Suggestions: explicitly report parameter counts per model, add training/runtime statistics, and clarify implementation details for shared dropout masks in trees and for initialization noise magnitudes.

Originality:
Moderate. Stacking in recurrent architectures is known; applying “depth in space” to tree-structured recursion with leaf/internal untying and ReLUs is a sensible and useful adaptation. The qualitative analyses provide novel insight into layer roles in tree models.

Significance:
Good. Achieving SOTA on SST and showing consistent gains over shallow tree models under fixed capacity makes this a meaningful empirical advance. If the approach generalizes to other structured NLP tasks, the impact would be stronger.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you provide ablations isolating the effect of (a) untying leaves/internals, (b) ReLU vs. tanh, (c) dropout, and (d) fixed vs. fine-tuned word vectors? Which are necessary for depth to help?
- What happens if the output layer is connected to all hidden layers (multi-layer supervision)? Does it change the layer specialization you observe?
- How sensitive are results to the “shared dropout mask per sentence/layer” choice?
- Do you observe training instabilities as depth increases? Any vanishing/exploding gradient diagnostics across structure vs. space?
- Could you report training time and memory as a function of depth/width?

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and well-motivated extension of recursive models with strong empirical results.
- State-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark under controlled comparisons.
- Insightful qualitative analyses indicating complementary roles of stacked tree layers.
- Practical recipe (ReLU + dropout + pretrained embeddings + AdaGrad) likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical/diagnostic explanation for why depth helps in tree recursion.
- Incomplete ablation of key design choices; results could conflate multiple factors.
- Single-task evaluation; generality remains to be demonstrated.
- No statistical significance or variance across runs.

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and offers a simple, impactful idea with new SOTA results and compelling analyses. Addressing the ablations and adding a brief discussion on computational trade-offs and significance testing would further strengthen the submission.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
