Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers “in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure” induced by tree composition. The model distinguishes leaf and internal nodes in the first layer (separate X→H and H→H mappings), enabling the use of ReLUs and large fixed, pretrained word embeddings; higher layers operate in a shared phrase space with layer-to-layer connections. Training uses softmax supervision at all tree nodes but connects the classifier only to the top recursive layer, AdaGrad, dropout (with shared masks across nodes within a sentence/layer), and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations for stability. The main empirical evaluation is on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way and binary sentiment. With a fixed parameter budget, deeper-but-narrower stacks outperform single-layer recursive nets, and the best model achieves state of the art on SST, exceeding RNTN and Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors per layer) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

The work is a natural extension of “deep recurrent networks” [El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen] to tree-structured computation, and builds on recursive nets for compositionality [Socher et al.] and strong pretrained embeddings [Mikolov et al.]. Comparisons include RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and (cited) DCNN.

Quality
- Empirical support: The paper shows consistent gains from adding stacked recursive layers under a fixed-parameter budget, and improved absolute accuracy over strong baselines on SST. The qualitative layer analyses are thoughtful and align with the paper’s motivation. However, several experimental choices reduce the conclusiveness of some claims:
  - Binary SST: you decode from 5-way models trained with neutral examples. The standard protocol trains binary models on the positive/negative subset. Direct comparison to published binary baselines is therefore not apples-to-apples and should be redone.
  - Word embeddings are fixed throughout. It remains unclear whether gains come from depth vs. the decision not to fine-tune. A single-layer recursive net with fine-tuned embeddings may close the gap. An ablation is needed.
  - Dropout: the decision to share dropout masks across all nodes in a sentence/layer is nonstandard; an ablation vs. independent per-node dropout would clarify its contribution.
  - Stability: you mitigate activation explosions via a small L2 on weights and unit activations. Alternatives like gradient clipping and/or weight-norm constraints are common and should be evaluated or at least discussed as they may interact differently with depth.
  - Reporting: results appear to be single runs; no variance/confidence intervals are given. Parameter counts are held constant in groups, but explicit parameter numbers per configuration would help. It would also be useful to report depth vs width trade-offs at equal width to complement the fixed-parameter study.

- Baselines: DCNN (Kalchbrenner et al.) is cited but it is unclear whether the comparison is done on identical SST splits and whether fine-grained (5-way) numbers exist for DCNN. Please include or clarify this baseline on the same splits and protocols.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and largely reproducible; training details are provided. A few notational and exposition issues should be fixed:
- Section 2.2: notation W^η and W^xh is introduced informally; please define all symbols explicitly and summarize parameter shapes (e.g., Wxh ∈ R|H|×|X|, Whh ∈ R|H|×|H|, V(i) dimensions, etc.). Consider splitting the composition equations for leaf vs. internal inputs to avoid overloading.
- Equation (4): make explicit whether V(i) is tied for leaves vs internals in higher layers and clarify its dimensions.
- The claims in the introduction that depth-in-structure “is unlikely to involve a hierarchical interpretation” and that different layers capture specific semantic notions (lines 148–154) read as speculative. The qualitative analyses are suggestive but not definitive; please temper/qualify these statements or add quantitative probes.
- Justify the choice to connect the classifier only to the final hidden layer vs. to all layers (perhaps with an ablation).

Originality
Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward but novel extension of deep recurrent ideas to trees. The practical design (untied leaf/internal parameters in the first layer, ReLUs, shared dropout) is a useful engineering contribution. The idea itself is incremental rather than conceptually radical, but the paper articulates it well and demonstrates concrete benefits.

Significance
Demonstrating consistent gains from “depth in space” for tree models on a canonical benchmark is valuable. The approach is likely to be adopted in subsequent work on tree-structured data. That said, the current evidence is limited to SST; additional experiments on tasks with only root-level labels (where internal node supervision is absent) would strengthen the generality of the claims about depth.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architecture; clear extension of deep RNNs to trees.
- Strong empirical results on SST; consistent improvements under fixed parameter budgets.
- Sensible training recipe (ReLUs + pretrained embeddings + dropout) and useful qualitative analyses.
- Paper is generally clear and reproducible.

Cons
- Binary SST evaluation not following the standard training protocol; comparisons may be unfair.
- No ablations for embedding fine-tuning, per-node vs shared dropout, or classifier connections to intermediate layers.
- Limited scope (only SST); unclear whether depth helps when only a single root label is available.
- Some claims are speculative; lack of multiple runs/variance and explicit parameter counts.
- Notational ambiguities in Section 2.2.

Requests and questions for the authors
- Re-run binary SST following the standard protocol (train on pos/neg only, exclude neutral) and compare to reported baselines; report both fine-grained and binary under matched settings.
- Provide ablations:
  - Fine-tuning vs. freezing embeddings (for shallow and deep variants).
  - Shared-mask vs. independent per-node dropout; report dropout rate sensitivity.
  - Classifier attached only to the top layer vs. to all layers.
- Report parameter counts per configuration, and variance across multiple random seeds.
- Compare against DCNN on the same splits; if 5-way numbers are unavailable, at least include a matched binary comparison.
- Consider stability alternatives (gradient clipping and/or norm constraints) and discuss their effect.
- Evaluate on a dataset with only root-level supervision to test whether added depth helps without internal labels (or simulate by masking internal-node losses on SST).

Minor/comments
- Define W^η, W^xh, and all matrix dimensions explicitly; consider separating equations for leaf vs internal cases.
- Clarify whether V(2) has separate X→H(2) and H(1)→H(2) blocks and how they are tied.
- Briefly justify the choice of shared dropout masks theoretically or empirically.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is incremental but useful; results are strong and likely to influence subsequent work. Addressing the protocol and ablation concerns would solidify the case for acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
