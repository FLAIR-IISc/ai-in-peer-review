Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on parse trees. Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up via shared weights over the tree; between layers, each node passes its hidden state upward in “depth-in-space,” analogous to stacked recurrent networks. The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes in the first layer to map word embeddings to a phrase space and use rectified linear units and dropout. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with fine-grained (5-class) and binary sentiment classification.

The work builds on recursive neural networks for compositionality (e.g., Socher et al., EMNLP 2013), which are deep in structure but have a single representation space. It is inspired by stacked recurrent architectures (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), bringing the “depth-in-space” notion to tree-structured models. Comparisons include shallow recursive nets, MV-RNN, the tensor-based RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. The paper reports new state-of-the-art results on SST and presents qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of composition (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and learning procedure are clearly specified. The interleaving of backpropagation through structure (within layers) and across layers is standard. The choice to connect supervision only to the final layer is sensible for interpretability.
- Methodological choices: Untying leaves from internal nodes to allow separate X→H and H→H mappings, coupled with ReLUs and fixed pretrained word vectors, is well justified. Dropout is applied consistently across the nodes of a layer for a sentence; an additional small L2 penalty on activations addresses potential explosion with ReLUs.
- Experiments: Results are strong on SST. The authors compare models of matched parameter budgets across depth/width configurations and show consistent gains from depth up to an optimal number of layers. However:
  - Fairness and attribution: Some improvements may come from modern choices (pretrained word2vec, ReLUs, dropout) rather than depth per se. While the paper compares shallow vs deep variants under matched parameters and notes gains, a more comprehensive ablation (e.g., with/without untying, with/without dropout, ReLU vs tanh) would better isolate the contribution of stacking.
  - Statistical rigor: No variance across runs or significance tests are reported; SST results can vary with initialization. Reporting mean/std over multiple seeds would strengthen claims.
  - Scope: Evaluation is limited to one dataset and task. Demonstrating generality on additional tree-structured tasks (e.g., sentence-level sentiment without phrase supervision, entailment, paraphrase) would increase confidence in broader applicability.
  - Efficiency: Training cost, memory usage, and convergence behavior with increasing depth are not reported; such details would help practitioners.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Figures help convey the architecture.
- Potential confusion arises from the acronym “RNN” being used for “recursive” (common in earlier work) while also discussing “recurrent” networks. A consistent disambiguation (e.g., “RecNN” vs “RNN”) would improve readability.
- The depth/width/parameter-matching protocol should be summarized in a compact table to make comparisons transparent.
- Specify precisely whether output-layer parameters are shared across nodes and how class thresholds map to binary labels at test time.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers to introduce depth-in-space for tree models is a natural but nontrivial extension of prior recursive architectures. The untying of leaves and internals and the use of ReLUs in tree models are practical contributions.
- The idea relates to deep recurrent architectures, but applying it to tree-structured composition and analyzing layer-wise behavior is novel for the sentiment setting at the time.
- Compared to MV-RNN/RNTN (which increase composition expressivity via multiplicative interactions), this work increases capacity via depth; the contrast is interesting and well motivated.

Significance
- The model achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on SST and shows consistent gains over shallow counterparts with similar parameter counts, supporting the core claim that depth helps.
- The qualitative analyses provide useful intuition about what different layers capture (e.g., intensity vs polarity), which can guide future design.
- Given the prominence of tree-structured representations in NLP, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on deep tree models.

Pros
- Clear motivation and well-described architecture; simple to implement with standard components.
- Strong empirical performance on a widely used benchmark; parameter-matched comparison shows benefits of depth.
- Practical training recipe (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) that stabilizes deep tree models.
- Insightful qualitative analyses suggesting a hierarchy of compositional abstractions across layers.

Cons
- Limited scope: evaluation only on SST; no tests on additional datasets or tasks to establish generality.
- Incomplete ablations: unclear how much each ingredient (untied leaves, ReLUs, dropout) contributes vs stacking depth.
- No reporting of variance or significance tests; single-run results may overstate improvements.
- Fairness of some baseline comparisons is not fully controlled (e.g., baselines may lack pretrained embeddings/modern regularization).
- Minor clarity issues around nomenclature (recursive vs recurrent RNN) and parameter matching details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations isolating the effects of (i) depth vs width, (ii) untying leaves/internals, (iii) ReLU vs tanh, (iv) dropout vs L2, and (v) connecting outputs to last vs all layers.
- Report mean/std over multiple random seeds and, if possible, statistical significance.
- Include training time and memory footprint as depth increases, and discuss any practical limits.
- Evaluate on additional tasks (e.g., binary sentiment trained directly, SNLI/entailment, paraphrase) to demonstrate broader utility.
- Clarify notation and nomenclature to avoid confusion between recursive and recurrent models.

Recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated and carefully executed extension of recursive networks with clear empirical gains and useful analysis. While the novelty is incremental, the results and insights are valuable to the NIPS community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding ablations and variance reporting to strengthen causal claims about depth.
