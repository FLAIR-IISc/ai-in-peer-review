This paper proposes deep recursive networks by extending depth across the tree structure, a strategy motivated by the success of depth in recurrent neural networks. Each layer employs shared parameters across different positions within that layer. The authors also utilize dropout and ReLU activations, while keeping word representations fixed using pre-trained embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the sentiment treebank dataset of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, representing a natural generalization of approaches used with recurrent nets. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the sentiment treebank, and the gains achieved through dropout and ReLU activations are noteworthy. However, several model and experimental design choices are questionable or lack sufficient justification, and I believe additional experimentation is necessary before I can confidently recommend acceptance.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail to enable reproduction of the results.

Detailed comments / questions:

- line 138: What are W<sup>eta</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup>? These symbols are not previously defined. Do they refer to W<sup>eta</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>eta</sup><sub>R</sub>? It would improve clarity to present two separate equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. Additionally, summarizing the parameter space—{W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c}—would be helpful. Including the dimensionality of each matrix upon its definition would further enhance understanding.

- lines 148-154: This section appears speculative. If these observations are based on empirical results, please state this explicitly. I am not yet convinced that this represents a significant issue.

- Baseline: The authors should include results from “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) as a baseline.

- Word vectors: Why were the word vectors not fine-tuned? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows the embeddings to capture sentiment-specific information. It seems inconsistent to have phrase and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment while the individual word vectors are not. (I recognize this is less of an issue with deep RNNs, as information can propagate through the layers.) Is it possible that the improvements observed with deep RNNs are attributable to *not* fine-tuning the word vectors? A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform comparably or better. This requires further investigation.

- line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this particular design choice?

- lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, consider constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, techniques commonly used to improve stability in recurrent networks.

- Binary classification: The binary classification experiments should be performed separately. The current training procedure includes examples from the neutral class, which deviates from the standard experimental protocol. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to those reported in existing work.

- Tree structure vs. single label: The analogy with deep recurrent nets is particularly apt when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case with the treebank experiments. It would be valuable to investigate whether a similar advantage exists when only a single, root-level label is available. Would improvement still be observed over a single-layer RNN in this scenario? While deep RNNs appear promising, I would like to see further exploration. I recommend the authors redo the binary classification experiment following the proper protocol, investigate the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that utilize only a global label. These additional experiments would significantly strengthen the paper and provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across the tree structure.
