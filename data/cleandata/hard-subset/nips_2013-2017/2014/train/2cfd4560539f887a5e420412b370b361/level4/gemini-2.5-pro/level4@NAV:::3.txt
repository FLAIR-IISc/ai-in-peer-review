This paper introduces a deep recursive neural network architecture, extending the concept of depth from recurrent networks to the spatial domain of tree structures. In this model, each layer is defined by a unique set of parameters that are shared across all nodes at that specific depth. The authors also incorporate ReLUs and dropout into their framework and utilize static, pre-trained word embeddings for their input representations. The model's performance is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method presents a novel extension of depth to recursive models, drawing a logical parallel from its success in recurrent architectures. The empirical results achieved on the sentiment treebank are state-of-the-art, and the reported performance gains from using dropout and ReLUs are interesting findings on their own. Nevertheless, several design and experimental choices lack sufficient justification, and I believe further experimentation is required to fully validate the contributions before the paper can be accepted.

The manuscript is generally well-written and provides adequate detail for the work to be reproduced.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   **line 138:** The notation for `W^eta` and `W^xh` is undefined. It is unclear if these are meant to be general placeholders for the left and right weight matrices (`W^{eta}L` and `W^{eta}R`). Clarity could be improved by providing separate equations for the input-to-hidden and hidden-to-hidden transformations. Furthermore, a summary of the full parameter space, such as `{WL, WR, U, b, c}`, along with the dimensionality of each matrix, would be a helpful addition.
*   **lines 148-154:** The argument presented in this section appears highly speculative. Is this claim based on empirical results from your experiments? If so, this should be explicitly stated. As written, the argument is not convincing.
*   **Baseline:** The experimental comparison should include a recent and highly relevant baseline from Kalchbrenner et al., "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (ACL 2014).
*   **Word Embeddings:** What was the rationale for not fine-tuning the word vectors? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task would allow the embeddings to capture sentiment-specific semantic properties. It seems counter-intuitive for phrase and sentence representations to be sentiment-aware while the constituent word vectors are not. Could the performance improvements from the deep architecture be an artifact of using static embeddings? A single-layer model with fine-tuned word vectors might perform just as well, if not better. This should be investigated as a control experiment.
*   **line 251 (Shared Dropout Units):** Please provide the motivation for sharing dropout units across the network.
*   **lines 255-256 (Stability):** As an alternative to the described approach for maintaining stability, have the authors considered methods commonly used in recurrent networks, such as constraining the norms of the weight matrices or applying gradient clipping?
*   **Binary Classification:** The binary classification results are not directly comparable to previous work. The inclusion of neutral examples during training deviates from the established experimental protocol for this task. This experiment should be conducted again following the standard setup.
*   The paper's core motivation, drawing an analogy with deep recurrent networks, is most applicable when predictions are made at every node in the tree. To generalize the findings, it would be valuable to assess whether the benefits of depth hold for tasks that only require a single prediction at the root of the tree. Would a deep recursive network still outperform a single-layer one in such a setting? While the approach shows promise, the paper would be significantly strengthened by additional experiments. I recommend that the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to the proper protocol, analyze the impact of fine-tuning word embeddings, and evaluate their model on one or two additional datasets that use a single global label. These additions would provide much stronger evidence for the general utility of adding depth across space.
