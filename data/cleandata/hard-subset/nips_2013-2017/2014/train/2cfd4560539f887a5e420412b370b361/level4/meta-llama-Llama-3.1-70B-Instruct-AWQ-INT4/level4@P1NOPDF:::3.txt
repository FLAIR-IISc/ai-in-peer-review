This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, inspired by the success of similar approaches with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, although it can be seen as a generalization of the approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are interesting in themselves. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable or not well justified, and I believe some additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is given for the reader to reproduce the results on their own.

Detailed comments / questions:

- line 138: The definitions of W^eta and W^xh are missing. Are these just alternative notations for W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to have two separate equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. A summary of the parameter space, including the dimensionalities of each matrix (i.e., {WL, WR, U, b, c}), would also be helpful.

- lines 148-154: This section appears speculative. Is this based on empirical results? If so, it should be mentioned. I am not convinced that this is a significant issue.

- The authors should include a comparison with the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year as a baseline.

- It is surprising that the word vectors were not fine-tuned. Fine-tuning them on a sentiment task allows the word embeddings to capture sentiment information. It seems counterintuitive to have phrases and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment but where individual word vectors are not. While this might be acceptable with a deep RNN, it is possible that the improvements obtained with deep RNNs are due to the lack of fine-tuning. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform better. I think this needs to be controlled for.

- line 251 (shared dropout units): What was the motivation behind this choice?

- lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, the authors could try constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- The binary classification experiment should be performed separately. The current approach deviates from the standard experiment protocol, as it includes neutral class examples during training. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case in the treebank experiments. However, it would be useful to see whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. Would the authors still expect to see an improvement over a single-layer RNN? The use of deep RNNs seems like a promising approach, but I would like to see some additional experiments. I recommend that the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to the proper protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would result in a much stronger paper and stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
