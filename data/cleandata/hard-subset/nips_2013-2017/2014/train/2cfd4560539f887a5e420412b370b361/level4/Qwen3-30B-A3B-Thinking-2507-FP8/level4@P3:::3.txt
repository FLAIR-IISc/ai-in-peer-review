

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs), which stack multiple recursive layers to combine depth in *space* (via layer stacking) with depth in *structure* (via recursive composition), inspired by analogous advances in deep recurrent networks. The authors apply this architecture to fine-grained sentiment analysis on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating that deep RNNs outperform shallow recursive networks with equivalent parameter counts and achieve state-of-the-art results compared to prior baselines (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors). Key innovations include using rectified linear units (ReLUs), dropout regularization, and fixed pretrained word vectors, alongside qualitative analyses showing distinct compositional roles per layer.

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The methodology is clearly articulated, with well-motivated design choices (e.g., untied leaf/internal weights enabling ReLU use). The experimental setup is thorough: the authors rigorously evaluate depth/width trade-offs, include comprehensive baselines (though missing Kalchbrenner et al., 2014, as noted below), and provide insightful qualitative analyses via input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrase comparisons. These analyses convincingly demonstrate that deeper layers capture increasingly abstract compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs. sentiment direction). The paper is well-written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The results are significant for sentiment analysis, achieving new state-of-the-art performance on SST, and the architecture generalizes existing recursive neural network paradigms in a principled way.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality & Significance Gaps*:  
1. **Critical Baseline Omission**: The authors omit Kalchbrenner et al.’s (2014) convolutional neural network (CNN) baseline, a strong recent method for sentence modeling. This weakens the significance claim, as the paper’s "state-of-the-art" status cannot be confidently asserted without comparison to this work.  
2. **Unjustified Word Vector Treatment**: The decision to fix pretrained word vectors (without fine-tuning) is unexplained and potentially problematic. Sentiment tasks often benefit from sentiment-adapted embeddings; the authors should test whether a shallow RNN *with fine-tuned embeddings* outperforms their deep RNN *without fine-tuning*. Without this control, the claimed superiority of depth may simply reflect the absence of fine-tuning in the baseline comparison.  
3. **Flawed Binary Classification Protocol**: The binary results (positive/negative) are derived by thresholding the fine-grained model’s 5-class output, which incorrectly includes neutral examples in training. This violates the standard protocol (which uses only positive/negative examples), rendering comparisons to prior work (e.g., Socher et al., 2013) invalid. The authors should retrain a dedicated binary classifier.  
4. **Limited Scope**: The paper only evaluates on SST (with tree-structured labels). The potential advantage of deep RNNs for *single-label* tasks (e.g., standard sentence sentiment with only root labels) is unexplored, despite being a common application. This weakens the generality of the contribution.  

*Clarity & Notation*:  
Minor issues persist in notation (e.g., unclear definitions of $W^{\eta}$ and $W^{xh}$ on line 138). The authors should explicitly define all parameters (e.g., dimensionality of $W^L$, $W^R$) and use consistent notation for input vs. hidden layer weights. The "shared dropout" rationale (line 251) is underdeveloped; a brief justification would improve clarity.

**Overall Assessment**  
The core idea—stacking recursive layers to add spatial depth—is a natural and well-motivated extension of prior work (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013; Schmidhuber, 1997). The results on SST are compelling, but the significance is undermined by the omitted baseline, unresolved fine-tuning question, and protocol errors. The paper is technically sound and clearly written, but the experimental validation lacks rigor. The qualitative analyses are a strong asset, but the quantitative claims require stronger support.  

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. The paper makes a plausible contribution but falls short on critical validation. The omission of Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) and the unaddressed fine-tuning issue are major flaws that prevent a fair comparison with state-of-the-art. Without addressing these, the "state-of-the-art" claim is unsupported. The binary classification error further compromises results. While the architecture is interesting, the current evidence does not sufficiently demonstrate that depth *across space* is the true driver of performance. With the suggested revisions (adding Kalchbrenner baseline, testing fine-tuning, re-running binary classification), the paper could be strengthened significantly. As-is, the weaknesses outweigh the strengths for acceptance at NIPS.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*: Novel architecture extension, strong SST results, insightful qualitative analyses, clear writing.  
*Con*: Critical baseline omission, unjustified word vector treatment, flawed binary protocol, limited task scope.
