- Proposes stacking multiple recursive layers (“depth in space”) with untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLUs, and dropout for tree-structured composition.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), showing deeper/narrower stacks outperform shallower/wider baselines under a matched-parameter budget and achieve state-of-the-art.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) indicate different layers capture different compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs. polarity).  
- Builds on prior recursive models and deep recurrent ideas, integrating pretrained vectors, dropout, and ReLUs into a tree setting; novelty is in stacking layers with cross-layer connections.  
- Methodology is sound, clearly specified, and training choices (AdaGrad, dropout, L2, early stopping) are appropriate to stabilize ReLUs.  
- Experimental design thoughtfully matches parameter budgets and uses competitive baselines on the same data split.  
- Results show clear improvements over RNTN, paragraph vectors, and shallow RNNs, with useful qualitative insights.  
- Limitations include evaluation on only one dataset/task, lack of statistical significance tests, and incomplete ablation studies.  
- Paper is well written and organized; figures effectively illustrate parameter sharing and layer information flow.  
- Suggested clarifications: explicit parameter counts, runtime/training costs per depth, effects of intermediate supervision connections, and stronger intuition for why stacking helps.  
- The idea of stacked recursive layers is simple, plausible, and appears novel; untied parameters and ReLUs are sensible complements.  
- Contribution is meaningful due to careful empirical validation under parameter-controlled conditions despite using known components.  
- Significance: advances SST state-of-the-art and suggests stacked depth benefits tree-structured NLP tasks like sentiment, entailment, and paraphrase.  
- Broader impact would be strengthened by more datasets, robustness to parsing errors, and word vector fine-tuning experiments.  
- Strengths: clear model articulation, strong parameter-controlled empirical gains, and qualitative analyses clarifying layer roles.  
- Weaknesses: single-dataset evaluation, missing statistical and runtime analyses, incomplete ablations, and underdeveloped intuition for why deeper tree composition helps.  
- Suggested improvements: add statistical tests/variance, report parameter counts and speed, ablate activations/dropout/untying, test binary training and more datasets, and discuss parse sensitivity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated architecture with clear empirical gains, fair comparisons, and state-of-the-art results with actionable analyses.  
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow empirical scope, limited statistical rigor, missing ablations and runtime details, and insufficient theoretical explanation.  
- Overall: strong empirical paper introducing an effective extension to recursive models that merits acceptance if statistical reporting and expanded ablations/discussion are addressed.
