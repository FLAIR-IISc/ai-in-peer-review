Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth in space” in addition to the usual “depth in structure” of recursive models. Within each layer, composition proceeds over binary parse trees using shared weights; across layers, each node’s representation is connected vertically via learned matrices V(i). The model also “unties” parameters for leaf and internal nodes in the first layer, enabling distinct mappings from word vectors (X) to phrase space (H) and permitting rectified linear units (ReLUs). Regularization uses dropout with shared masks across a tree and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations for stability. Training uses AdaGrad without pretraining beyond fixed, pretrained word vectors.

The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way classification, with binary results derived from the 5-way posterior. Deep RNNs are compared to shallow recursive baselines (including improved shallow variants that use pretrained word vectors and ReLUs), matrix-vector RNNs, tensor-based RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNNs. The paper reports new state-of-the-art performance and provides qualitative analyses showing different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity) via input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrase analyses.

The work is well-situated among recursive models for compositionality (e.g., Socher et al. 2011–2013), deep recurrent architectures (El Hihi & Bengio, Hermans & Schrauwen), and leveraging pretrained embeddings (Mikolov et al.). The proposed stacking for recursive nets parallels “deep RNNs” in the temporal setting, extending that intuition to tree structures.

Strengths
- Well-motivated architecture: clean separation of depth in structure vs depth in space; vertical connections per node are a natural analogue of stacked recurrent networks.
- Solid engineering choices: untying leaves/internals enables ReLUs and efficient use of large pretrained embeddings with smaller hidden phrase dimensions; dropout with shared masks is appropriate for structured inputs.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements of deep over shallow recursive models at matched parameter budgets; new state of the art on SST.
- Clear exposition of training details (AdaGrad, initialization, early stopping) that supports reproducibility.
- Insightful qualitative analyses suggesting layer-wise specialization (e.g., middle layer tracking intensity, top layer consolidating sentiment polarity).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Potential test-set tuning leakage: Table 1(a) appears to compare many depths/widths on the test set. Model selection (depth, width, dropout) should be performed on the development set, with a single final test evaluation. Please clarify the selection protocol and, if necessary, move these comparisons to dev-only, reporting test performance only for the chosen configuration.
- Statistical reliability: Report variance across multiple random seeds and/or significance tests (e.g., bootstrap on sentences) for key comparisons, especially when differences are 0.5–1% absolute.
- Ablations to isolate contributions: Include controlled ablations for (i) untying leaves vs tying, (ii) ReLU vs tanh within the same untied setup, (iii) dropout on/off (you mention preliminary numbers), and (iv) fine-tuning word vectors vs freezing. This would more cleanly attribute gains beyond depth.
- Vertical connection design: Evaluate a variant that removes or modifies the per-node cross-layer connection V(i) to disentangle the benefit of stacking from the benefit of direct node-wise skip-like connections. For example, a configuration where higher layers consume only the structured representations produced at the previous layer (no same-node V(i)) or a residual/normalized V(i) would strengthen the architectural case.
- Training stability: Consider gradient clipping as a complementary stabilization to the L2 penalty on activations; report whether clipping was explored.
- Notation and terminology: The paper uses “RNN” for recursive networks, which can be confused with recurrent networks. A brief note at the outset to fix terminology would aid readers.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical claims are well supported by experiments on SST and careful training descriptions. The depth-vs-width study at fixed parameter budgets is valuable. The main concern is the rigor of the model-selection protocol vis-à-vis the test set and the absence of variance estimates.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with intuitive figures and clear layer-wise motivation. A few places could use clarification (definition of positional DAGs; explicit statement of development vs test usage for all sweeps).
- Originality: Stacking recursive layers with per-node vertical connections is a clear and novel extension of prior recursive models, paralleling developments in deep recurrent networks but adapted to tree structures. The leaf/internal untying with ReLUs is a practical and useful design choice.
- Significance: Results advance the state of the art on a prominent benchmark and offer insights into hierarchical compositionality. The approach should be of interest to both NLP practitioners and researchers studying deep structured architectures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated architectural idea that generalizes stacked RNNs to trees.
- Strong empirical performance with controlled comparisons at fixed parameter budgets.
- Practical contributions (ReLUs, dropout over trees, untying leaves) that others can adopt.
- Qualitative analyses that illuminate what deeper recursive layers learn.

Arguments against acceptance
- Possible test-set tuning leakage and lack of statistical significance reporting.
- Limited ablation coverage; contributions of individual design choices are not fully disentangled.
- Evaluation confined to a single dataset/task; broader applicability remains to be demonstrated.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify exactly which hyperparameters/architectures were selected on the development set versus evaluated on the test set in Table 1(a). If any choices used test performance, can you provide dev-only selection with a single test report?
- Did you try fine-tuning word vectors? If so, how did it affect performance and training stability?
- How sensitive are results to the shared-dropout masking scheme across nodes? Did per-node independent dropout perform worse?
- Did you explore gradient clipping, layer normalization, or other stabilization methods?

Minor comments and typos
- “classificationd” → “classification”
- “comprise a class of architecture” → “comprise a class of architectures” or “constitute a class of architectures”
- “you’re tuning ont he final test set here!” → “on the final test set” (and avoid tuning on test)
- “In order to evaulate” → “In order to evaluate”
- Consider briefly disambiguating “recursive neural networks (RNNs)” from “recurrent neural networks,” as both appear in related work.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. Addressing the test/dev protocol, adding a few ablations, and reporting variance would make the empirical case airtight. I lean toward acceptance.
