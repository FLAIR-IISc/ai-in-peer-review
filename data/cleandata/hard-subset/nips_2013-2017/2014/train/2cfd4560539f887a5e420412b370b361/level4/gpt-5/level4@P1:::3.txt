Summary
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers to add “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure.” Each layer has its own parameters shared across the tree within that layer, and the output is attached to the final layer to encourage hierarchical abstraction. The model further distinguishes leaf and internal nodes (untied parameters), uses rectified linear units and dropout, and keeps word embeddings fixed to pretrained vectors. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improvements over shallow recursive models with comparable parameter counts and over prior baselines (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors). The authors also present qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggesting different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on recursive neural networks for compositionality and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al.) and parallels developments in deep recurrent networks where stacking layers improves temporal modeling. The proposed architecture is a natural extension of stacking to structural computation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated idea: bringing depth across space to recursive models is a logical generalization of stacked recurrent networks.
- Strong empirical results on SST, with careful parameter-matched comparisons that show consistent gains from depth.
- Effective practical choices (ReLUs, dropout, pretrained embeddings) that deliver sizable improvements even for shallow models.
- Useful qualitative analyses that illustrate how deeper layers may separate sentiment polarity and intensity.
- Generally clear exposition with enough detail to reproduce most experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns
While the approach is promising, several modeling and experimental choices are insufficiently justified, and some comparisons are incomplete. Additional studies would strengthen the evidence that depth, rather than other factors, is responsible for the observed gains.

Detailed comments and questions
- Line 138 (notation): W^η and W^xh are not defined. Are these shorthands for W_L^η and W_R^η versus input-to-hidden and hidden-to-hidden mappings? It would be clearer to present two explicit equations: one for leaves (input-to-hidden) and one for internals (hidden-to-hidden), and to summarize the full parameter set, e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c, V^(i)}. Please also include dimensionalities when introducing each matrix/vector.
- Lines 148–154 (rectifiers and space mismatch): The argument about sparse, nonnegative hidden activations versus dense word vectors is plausible but reads as speculative. Is this claim supported by your own ablations or diagnostics? If so, please cite them; if not, consider tempering the claim or adding evidence.
- Baselines: Please include results from “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014). This is a relevant, contemporary baseline for sentence modeling and sentiment.
- Word embedding fine-tuning: Why are word vectors kept fixed? For sentiment, fine-tuning typically helps by aligning word representations with sentiment polarity and intensity. It is possible that the observed benefits of depth partially compensate for not adapting embeddings. A necessary control is to compare a well-regularized single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings against your deep RNN with fixed embeddings. Please report both configurations (and, ideally, deep RNNs with fine-tuning as well).
- Line 251 (shared dropout masks): What motivated using the same dropout mask across all nodes in a layer for a sentence? Please justify this design (e.g., variance reduction, regularizing tree-level features) and, if possible, compare to independent masks per node.
- Lines 255–256 (stability): In addition to L2 penalties on weights and activations, have you tried norm constraints or gradient clipping (common in recurrent nets) to address exploding activations/gradients? A brief comparison would be informative.
- Binary classification protocol: For fair comparison to prior work, binary results should be obtained by training a model specifically for the binary task (excluding neutral examples during training). Decoding a 5-way model into binary labels changes the training distribution and makes results not directly comparable. Please report binary results under the standard protocol.
- Scope beyond node-level supervision: The analogy to deep recurrent nets is strongest when supervision is available at all nodes, as in SST. It would be valuable to test scenarios with only a single root label (no phrase-level annotations) to assess whether depth across space still helps over a single-layer recursive model. Including 1–2 additional datasets with only sentence-level labels would significantly strengthen the paper.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and timely extension of recursive models with clear motivation from deep recurrent networks.
- Strong state-of-the-art performance on SST with careful parameter-controlled depth comparisons.
- Interesting qualitative analyses suggesting layer-specific roles in compositionality.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baseline (CNN) and nonstandard binary evaluation protocol limit comparability.
- Lack of embedding fine-tuning ablations makes it hard to isolate the contribution of depth.
- Limited evidence that benefits persist without node-level supervision or on additional datasets.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and promising paper with strong results on SST and a clear architectural contribution. However, before I can fully recommend acceptance, I would like to see: (1) binary results under the standard protocol; (2) controlled experiments with fine-tuned embeddings and a competitive single-layer baseline; and (3) inclusion of at least one additional sentence-level-only dataset and the CNN baseline. These additions would provide more definitive evidence that depth across space is the key driver of the gains and that the approach generalizes beyond SST.
