This paper introduces a novel deep recursive neural network architecture and applies it to sentiment classification, achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark dataset. The proposed model is well-motivated and the paper is clearly written, making a strong contribution to the area of recursive deep learning models for natural language processing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors clearly explain their model architecture and the motivations behind their design choices, making the work easy to follow and understand.
*   **Impressive Empirical Results:** The model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a difficult fine-grained sentiment classification task, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Novel and Well-Motivated Architectural Contributions:** The authors propose several clever and effective modifications to previous recursive neural network models. These improvements are well-justified and their impact is demonstrated experimentally. Key contributions include:
    1.  **Parameter Untying:** The decision to use separate parameter matrices for leaf nodes (word vectors) and non-terminal nodes is a simple but powerful idea that allows the model to learn more specialized representations for words versus composed phrases.
    2.  **Use of ReLUs:** The paper successfully incorporates Rectified Linear Units (ReLUs) into the recursive architecture, which is a non-trivial and valuable contribution given their benefits in other deep learning settings.
    3.  **Efficient Parameterization:** The strategy of using large, pre-trained, unsupervised word vectors for the input layer while maintaining smaller hidden representations for non-terminal nodes is an effective way to leverage rich lexical information without an explosion in model parameters.
    4.  **Novel Deep Architecture:** The proposed deep model is more sophisticated than simply stacking identical recursive layers. The introduction of direct connections from the input vectors to the composition functions at each layer is an interesting architectural choice that appears crucial to the model's success.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

My primary concern relates to the experimental methodology for hyperparameter tuning. The results presented in Table 1(a), which appear to be used for model selection (e.g., choosing the activation function, comparing against a shallow network), are reported on the final test set. Tuning hyperparameters or making model design decisions based on performance on the test set is a serious methodological issue, as it can lead to over-optimistic results and calls into question the generalization performance of the final model. The validity of the state-of-the-art claims is significantly undermined if the test set was used for any part of the model development or selection process. The authors should clarify their experimental setup and ideally report final results on a held-out test set that was not used for tuning.

**Questions and Suggestions:**

*   To better isolate the benefits of the novel deep architecture, an ablation study would be very insightful. The current design includes a direct connection from the input word vectors (via matrix V) to the composition function at every layer. It would be an interesting and valuable comparison to evaluate a variant of the model where this connection is removed (i.e., drop the term with matrix V), so that each layer's composition function only takes the hidden representations from the layer below as input. This would help clarify the specific contribution of this multi-layer connectivity.
*   There are a few minor typographical errors that should be corrected in the final version. For instance, the first sentence of the introduction appears to be duplicated.
