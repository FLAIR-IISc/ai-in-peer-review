- Introduces Deep Recursive Neural Network: stacking multiple recursive layers to make models "deep in space" and "deep in structure."  
- Key design choices: untied leaf vs. internal node parameters, ReLU activations, dropout, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) and achieves state-of-the-art on fine-grained and binary sentiment tasks.  
- Qualitative analyses indicate lower layers capture lexical/syntactic features while higher layers capture abstract semantic/sentiment features.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and technically sound with reproducible detail.  
- Empirical strength: large performance gains and insightful qualitative evidence for hierarchical representations.  
- Interesting demonstration that modern deep-learning techniques benefit recursive architectures.  
- Justification for untying leaf/internal parameters is speculative and lacks empirical support.  
- Notation (Equation 3) is unclear; W_l(η) and W_r(η) are not explicitly defined and parameter dimensionalities should be summarized.  
- Binary classification protocol is non-standard (trained on 5 classes then decoded to binary), making comparisons to prior work unfair.  
- Not fine-tuning word embeddings is a confound; depth gains may partly reflect learning task-specific embedding transforms that fine-tuning could achieve.  
- Experiments are limited to a single densely-supervised dataset (SST); unclear if benefits hold with only sentence-level supervision.  
- Omits comparison to a relevant baseline (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014 CNN), which reports results on the same dataset.  
- Questions/suggestions: rationale for sharing dropout mask across nodes, alternatives to L2 activation penalty (e.g., gradient clipping, weight norm constraints).  
- Recommended additional experiments: re-run binary task with standard protocol, ablation with fine-tuned vs. fixed embeddings for shallow and deep models, and tests on 1–2 sentence-level labeled datasets.  
- Conclusion: promising and novel idea with strong results, but requires additional controlled experiments to conclusively attribute gains to architectural depth.
