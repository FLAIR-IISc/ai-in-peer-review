- Proposes "deep" recursive networks by adding depth across tree space, sharing layer parameters across heights.  
- Uses dropout and ReLUs, with word representations fixed to pre-trained embeddings.  
- Experiments conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).  
- Method is novel but essentially a generalization of depth used in recurrent nets.  
- Achieves strong/state-of-the-art performance on the sentiment treebank.  
- Performance gains from dropout and ReLUs are interesting and noteworthy.  
- Some model and experimental design choices are questionable or not well justified.  
- Paper is generally clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Missing definitions/equations: unclear notation for W^η and W^xh and lack of parameter dimensionalities.  
- A speculative claim (lines 148–154) lacks empirical support or citation.  
- Missing baseline comparison to Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL 2014) convolutional sentence model.  
- Not fine-tuning word embeddings is questionable and may confound results; need control experiments.  
- Motivation for using shared dropout units is not explained.  
- Stability concerns could be addressed with weight-norm constraints or gradient truncation/clipping.  
- Binary classification setup deviates from standard protocol by including neutral examples during training; results are not directly comparable.  
- Unclear whether deep RNNs help when only a single root label exists; need experiments on global-label datasets.  
- Recommended additional experiments: proper binary protocol, fine-tuning embeddings, and tests on at least one or two global-label datasets.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel application of depth to recursive nets, strong empirical results, clear writing, and demonstrated benefit of depth in this setting.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unjustified experimental choices (e.g., frozen embeddings), missing relevant baselines, limited experimental scope, and some speculative layer-role claims without strong evidence.
