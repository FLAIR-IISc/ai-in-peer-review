

This paper introduces 'deep' recursive networks by extending depth across spatial dimensions, drawing inspiration from successful depth applications in recurrent networks. Each layer employs shared parameters across different heights within that layer, incorporates dropout and ReLUs, and utilizes fixed pre-trained word embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the sentiment treebank dataset from Socher et al.

The method is novel as a generalization of recurrent network approaches and achieves strong performance on the sentiment treebank. The effectiveness of dropout and ReLUs is noteworthy. However, several model and experimental choices lack sufficient justification, necessitating additional experiments before full acceptance can be recommended.

The paper is generally well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Line 138: Define W^eta and W^xh. Are these references to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? Clarify with separate equations for inputs and hidden layers, and summarize the parameter space {WL, WR, U, b, c} with dimensionalities.
- Lines 148-154: This claim appears speculative. Cite empirical evidence if available; otherwise, reconsider its inclusion.
- Baseline: Include results from Kalchbrenner et al.'s "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (ACL 2014).
- Word embeddings: Justify not fine-tuning embeddings. Fine-tuning is standard for sentiment tasks to make embeddings sentiment-sensitive; omitting it may confound results (e.g., deep RNN gains could stem from fixed embeddings rather than depth). A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings should be compared.
- Line 251 (shared dropout): State the motivation for this design choice.
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Consider weight norm constraints or gradient clipping as alternatives to the proposed stability method.
- Binary classification: Re-run this experiment per protocol—exclude neutral class examples during training to ensure comparability with existing methods.
- Depth utility: Demonstrate the advantage of deep RNNs for root-only prediction (not node-wise), as the current treebank experiments assume per-node labeling. Recommend redoing binary classification per protocol, testing fine-tuning effects, and adding 1-2 datasets using only global labels. These experiments would substantially strengthen the paper's claims.
