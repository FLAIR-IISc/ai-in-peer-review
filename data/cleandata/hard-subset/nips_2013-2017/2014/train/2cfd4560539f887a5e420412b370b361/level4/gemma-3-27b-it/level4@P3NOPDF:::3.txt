## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks, extending the successful concept of depth found in recurrent neural networks to a tree-structured setting. The core idea is to add depth across the spatial dimensions of a parse tree, with each layer employing shared parameters across different tree heights. The authors complement this with the use of dropout and ReLU activations, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The approach is novel and represents a logical generalization of established techniques in recurrent networks. The reported performance on the Sentiment Treebank is strong, and the observed gains from applying dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, several modeling and experimental choices lack sufficient justification, and further experimentation is necessary to confidently recommend acceptance.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail to enable reproduction of the reported results. However, addressing the following points would significantly strengthen the work.

**Detailed Comments/Questions:**

* **Notation and Clarity (line 138):** The notation W<sup>eta</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup> is introduced without prior definition. It is unclear if these correspond to W<sup>eta</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>eta</sup><sub>R</sub> respectively. To enhance clarity, separate equations for input and hidden layer transformations would be beneficial. Furthermore, a concise summary of the model’s parameter space—{W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c}—along with the dimensionality of each matrix upon definition, would improve readability and understanding.

* **Speculative Claims (lines 148-154):** The discussion regarding the potential limitations of shallow models appears speculative. If these claims are based on empirical observation, the authors should explicitly state this and provide supporting evidence. Without such support, the assertions are not entirely convincing.

* **Missing Baseline:** The paper should include a comparison against the convolutional neural network approach presented in “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014). This represents a strong and relevant baseline for the sentiment analysis task.

* **Word Embedding Fine-tuning:** The decision not to fine-tune the word embeddings is questionable. Fine-tuning allows word vectors to adapt to the specific nuances of the sentiment task, potentially capturing sentiment-specific information. The current approach results in discriminative phrase and sentence vectors built from word embeddings that remain sentiment-agnostic. Given the success of fine-tuning in deep recurrent networks, where word vectors are propagated through multiple layers, it's plausible that the observed improvements with deep recursive networks are partly attributable to *not* fine-tuning the word vectors. A single-layer recursive network with fine-tuned embeddings should be included as a control experiment. This investigation is crucial to isolate the benefits of network depth from those of fine-tuning.

* **Shared Dropout Motivation (line 251):** The rationale behind using shared dropout units across layers should be clarified. What benefit is expected from this design choice?

* **Alternative Stabilization Techniques (lines 255-256):** While the authors address instability concerns, alternative regularization techniques commonly used in recurrent networks, such as weight norm constraints or gradient clipping, should be considered and discussed.

* **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification evaluation should be conducted separately, excluding neutral class examples during training. Including these examples deviates from the standard experimental protocol and renders the results incomparable to existing literature. This experiment should be redone adhering to the proper methodology.

* **Generalization Beyond Tree Structure:** The analogy to deep recurrent networks is particularly strong when making predictions at each node in the parse tree, as demonstrated by the experiments on the Sentiment Treebank. However, it would be valuable to investigate whether the benefits of depth persist when only a single, root-level label is desired. Specifically, would a deep recursive network still outperform a single-layer recursive network in this scenario? This would strengthen the argument for the general applicability of deep recursive networks.

**Overall Assessment:**

The proposed deep recursive neural network is a promising approach with demonstrated performance on a challenging sentiment analysis task. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns outlined above. I recommend the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to standard protocols, investigate the impact of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include experiments on 1-2 additional datasets employing only global labels. These additional investigations would provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across space and lead to a stronger, more convincing publication.
