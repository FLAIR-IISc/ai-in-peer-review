This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, a concept motivated by the success of similar approaches with recurrent networks. Each layer in the proposed model has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also suggest the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. The experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, albeit an extension of the approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable or not well-justified, and additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is provided for the reader to reproduce the results. However, there are some areas that require clarification and improvement.

Detailed comments and questions:

- Line 138: The variables W^eta and W^xh are not defined. It would be clearer to have two equations, one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. A summary of the parameter space, including the dimensionalities of each matrix, would also be helpful.

- Lines 148-154: The discussion seems speculative, and it is unclear if it is based on empirical results. If so, it should be explicitly stated. The issue raised in this section is not entirely convincing.

- Baseline: The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year for a more comprehensive comparison.

- The decision not to fine-tune the word vectors is puzzling. Fine-tuning them on a sentiment task allows the word embeddings to become reflective of their sentiment, which seems essential for the task at hand. It is possible that the improvements obtained with deep RNNs are due to the lack of fine-tuning, and a single layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform better. This variable needs to be controlled for in the experimentation.

- Line 251: The motivation behind using shared dropout units is unclear and should be explained.

- Lines 255-256: Instead of relying on shared dropout units for stability, the authors could consider constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- Binary classification: The experiment should be performed separately, as the current protocol deviates from the standard approach. The inclusion of neutral class examples during training makes the results not directly comparable to existing approaches.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree. However, it would be useful to investigate whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. Additional experiments on this aspect, as well as the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, would strengthen the paper. I recommend redoing the binary classification experiment according to the proper protocol, studying the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and including 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would provide stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
