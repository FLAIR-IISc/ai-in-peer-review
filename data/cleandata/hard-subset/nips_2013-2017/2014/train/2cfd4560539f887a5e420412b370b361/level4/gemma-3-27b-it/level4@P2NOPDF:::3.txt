This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks, extending the principle of depth—successfully applied in recurrent networks—to a spatial dimension. The core idea is to stack recursive layers, with each layer sharing parameters across its height. The authors further employ dropout and ReLU activations, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to represent input tokens. The method is evaluated on the sentiment treebank dataset.

The approach is novel, effectively generalizing the established techniques used in recurrent neural networks. The reported results demonstrate strong performance on the sentiment treebank, and the observed gains from dropout and ReLU activations are noteworthy. However, several modeling and experimental choices lack sufficient justification, and further investigation is needed before a strong recommendation for acceptance can be made.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the reported results. 

Here are some specific comments and questions:

* **Notation and Clarity (line 138):** The variables W<sup>eta</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup> are undefined. It is unclear if they correspond to W<sup>eta</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>eta</sup><sub>R</sub>, respectively.  For improved clarity, separate equations for inputs and hidden layers would be beneficial.  A summary of the parameter space – {W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c} – would be helpful, alongside the dimensionality of each matrix when initially defined.

* **Speculation (lines 148-154):** The discussion on the interaction between word order and tree structure feels speculative. If these claims are based on empirical observations, they should be explicitly stated as such. The existence of the purported issue isn’t entirely convincing without supporting evidence.

* **Related Work:** The paper should include a comparison to the convolutional neural network approach for sentence modeling presented by Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL, 2014).

* **Word Embedding Fine-tuning:** The decision not to fine-tune the pre-trained word embeddings is questionable. Fine-tuning allows the embeddings to adapt to the specific sentiment task, potentially creating word representations that are more sensitive to sentiment. The current setup results in discriminative phrase and sentence vectors, while the underlying word vectors remain sentiment-agnostic. This seems counterintuitive. Given the success of fine-tuning in deep RNNs, where word vectors are updated through forward propagation, it’s plausible that the observed improvements with deep recursive networks are partially attributable to this lack of fine-tuning. A single-layer RNN *with* fine-tuned embeddings might achieve comparable, or even superior, performance and would serve as an important control.

* **Shared Dropout Motivation (line 251):**  Please explain the rationale behind using shared dropout units. What benefit does this provide?

* **Training Stability (lines 255-256):**  Consider exploring alternative regularization techniques to improve training stability, such as weight norm constraints or gradient clipping—methods commonly employed in recurrent neural network training.

* **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment should be conducted separately from the full-class classification. Including neutral class examples during training introduces a deviation from standard evaluation protocols and renders the results incomparable to existing approaches.

* **Generalization Beyond Treebanks:** While the analogy to deep recurrent networks is well-suited for tasks involving predictions at each node of a tree structure (like the treebank experiments), it would be valuable to investigate whether depth still offers an advantage when only a single, root-level label is required. Would a deep recursive network still outperform a single-layer network in such a scenario? The proposed approach shows promise, but further investigation is needed. 

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following: 1) Re-evaluate the binary classification experiment adhering to the standard protocol; 2) Investigate the impact of fine-tuning the word embeddings; and 3) Include 1-2 additional datasets that utilize only a global label for evaluation. These additional experiments would provide more robust evidence supporting the benefits of depth across space and result in a significantly stronger submission.
