This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation for this approach—adding depth "in space" rather than only "in structure"—is drawn from the recent success of similarly structured deep recurrent neural networks. In the proposed model, each layer has its own set of parameters that are applied recursively across the input tree structure. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, employing modern techniques such as rectifier linear units (ReLUs), dropout, and fixed, pre-trained word embeddings.

The proposed method is a novel extension of recursive networks, and the authors achieve very strong, state-of-the-art performance on the sentiment treebank benchmark. The paper is clearly written for the most part, and it provides sufficient detail for the results to be reproduced. The analysis showing performance gains from architectural choices like using ReLUs and dropout is also a valuable contribution. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are not fully justified, and I believe additional experiments are necessary to solidify the paper's claims before it can be recommended for acceptance.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   **Clarity of Model Definition:** The mathematical description of the model could be improved. In Equation (3) (line 138), the notation `W^{η·}` and `W^{xh·}` is used without explicit definition. It would be clearer to provide separate equations for leaf and internal node computations. Additionally, a summary of all model parameters (e.g., {WL, WR, V, U, b, c}) along with their matrix dimensionalities would greatly aid reader comprehension.

*   **Justification for Untying Leaves and Internals:** The motivation for separating leaf and internal node representations (lines 148-154) seems speculative. The authors argue that a single weight matrix would struggle to handle both sparse (post-ReLU hidden states) and dense (word embeddings) inputs. Is this claim based on empirical results from preliminary experiments? Clarifying this would strengthen the rationale for this design choice.

*   **Lack of Word Vector Fine-tuning:** A significant experimental choice that warrants more investigation is the decision not to fine-tune the word vectors. For a sentiment analysis task, fine-tuning allows word embeddings to adapt and better capture sentiment polarity. It is plausible that the performance gains attributed to depth are, in part, an artifact of using fixed embeddings; the stacked layers may simply be learning a task-specific transformation that a fine-tuned embedding layer would otherwise provide. A crucial control experiment would be to compare the proposed deep RNNs against a single-layer RNN where the word embeddings *are* fine-tuned. This would provide a more direct assessment of the benefits of depth.

*   **Incorrect Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification evaluation does not follow the standard protocol for this dataset, which makes the results not directly comparable to those of previous work. The authors adapt their 5-class model post-hoc instead of training a dedicated binary classifier. Critically, this means that neutral examples were included during training, which deviates from the established experimental setup. To ensure a fair comparison and validate the claimed state-of-the-art performance, this experiment should be re-run by training a new model exclusively on positive and negative examples.

*   **Generalizability to Tasks with Root-Only Supervision:** The paper's evaluation is confined to the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, where supervision is provided at every node of the parse tree. While the deep RNN architecture is well-suited for this scenario, its advantages in more common NLP tasks—where only a single, sentence-level label is available—are not explored. Would the benefits of spatial depth persist in such settings? To strengthen the paper's contribution and demonstrate the broader applicability of the proposed method, it would be highly valuable to include experiments on one or two additional datasets that rely only on a global, root-level label.

*   **Minor Questions:**
    *   What was the motivation for sharing the same dropout units across all nodes within a single sentence and layer (line 251)?
    *   Regarding model stability (lines 255-256), the authors use L2 penalties on weights and activations. Have they considered alternative techniques common in training recurrent networks, such as gradient clipping or constraining the norms of the weight matrices?

In summary, while the paper presents a promising approach, its experimental evaluation needs to be more thorough. I would recommend that the authors conduct the control experiment with fine-tuned embeddings, redo the binary classification experiment using the correct protocol, and test their model on datasets with only sentence-level labels. These additions would provide much stronger evidence for the usefulness of adding spatial depth to recursive networks and would result in a much stronger paper.
