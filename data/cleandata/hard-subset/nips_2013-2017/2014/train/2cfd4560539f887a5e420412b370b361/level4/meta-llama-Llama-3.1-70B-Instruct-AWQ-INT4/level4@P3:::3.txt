This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, which is motivated by the success of doing the same thing with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, although essentially a generalization of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are interesting in themselves. That being said, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable (or not well justified) and I believe some additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper itself is clearly written for the most part and sufficient detail is given for the reader to reproduce the results on their own.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors carefully evaluate the performance of their proposed model and compare it with several baselines. However, some of the model and experimental design choices could be better justified. For instance, the decision to fix the word representations to pre-trained embeddings without fine-tuning them on the sentiment task could be better motivated.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the reader to understand and reproduce the results. However, there are some minor issues with notation and clarity. For example, the definition of W^eta and W^xh could be clearer, and the parameter space could be summarized more explicitly.

Originality: The proposed method is novel, although it is essentially a generalization of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The use of dropout and ReLUs in the context of recursive neural networks is also a new contribution.

Significance: The paper achieves very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the results are likely to be of interest to researchers in natural language processing. However, the significance of the results could be further enhanced by including additional experiments, such as studying the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings and including 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The proposed method is novel and achieves strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the reader to understand and reproduce the results.
* The use of dropout and ReLUs in the context of recursive neural networks is a new contribution.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Some of the model and experimental design choices could be better justified.
* The decision to fix the word representations to pre-trained embeddings without fine-tuning them on the sentiment task could be better motivated.
* Additional experiments, such as studying the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings and including 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label, would strengthen the paper and provide more evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.

Recommendations:

* The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year as a baseline.
* The authors should study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings and include this as an additional experiment.
* The authors should redo the binary classification experiment, as per the proper protocol, and include this as an additional experiment.
* The authors should consider including 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label to further demonstrate the usefulness of depth across space.
