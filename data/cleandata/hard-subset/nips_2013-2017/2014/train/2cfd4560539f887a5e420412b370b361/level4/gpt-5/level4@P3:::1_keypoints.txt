- Proposes stacked recursive neural networks (deep RecNNs) that add "depth-in-space" by stacking recursive layers on parse trees, with untied leaf mappings, ReLUs, and dropout.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (fine-grained and binary), reporting new state-of-the-art results and qualitative layer analyses.

- Architecture and learning procedure are clearly specified and technically sound; supervision connected only to the final layer for interpretability.  
- Untying leaves vs internals, using ReLUs with dropout, and fixed pretrained embeddings are reasonable methodological choices; small L2 on activations prevents ReLU explosion.

- Strong empirical results on SST with parameter-matched comparisons showing gains from depth up to an optimal number of layers.  
- Improvements may partly stem from modern choices (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout) rather than depth alone; more ablations needed to isolate effects.  
- No variance across runs or statistical significance reported; results could be sensitive to initialization.  
- Evaluation is limited to one dataset/task, reducing evidence for generality.  
- Training cost, memory usage, and convergence behavior with increasing depth are not reported.

- Paper is clearly written with helpful figures, but ambiguous use of "RNN" (recursive vs recurrent) may confuse readers.  
- Depth/width/parameter-matching protocol should be summarized in a compact table; clarify output parameter sharing and binary label thresholding.

- Stacking recursive layers is a natural but nontrivial extension; untying leaves and using ReLUs in trees are practical contributions.  
- Novel application of deep recurrent ideas to tree-structured composition with layer-wise analysis in sentiment context.

- Achieves SOTA on SST and shows consistent depth benefits under parameter-matched comparisons.  
- Qualitative analyses suggest different layers capture different compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs polarity), offering useful insights.

- Pros: clear motivation and architecture, strong benchmark performance, practical training recipe, and insightful qualitative analysis.  
- Cons: limited scope (only SST), incomplete ablations, no variance/significance reporting, some unfair baseline comparisons, and minor clarity issues.

- Suggested improvements: add ablations (depth vs width, untying, ReLU vs tanh, dropout vs L2, supervision connections), report mean/std and significance, include training/memory costs, evaluate on additional tasks, and clarify nomenclature.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance—work is well executed with valuable insights—contingent on adding ablations and variance reporting to strengthen causal claims about depth.
