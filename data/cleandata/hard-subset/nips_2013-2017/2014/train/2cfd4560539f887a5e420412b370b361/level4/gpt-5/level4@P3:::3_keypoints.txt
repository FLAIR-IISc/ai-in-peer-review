- Proposes deep recursive neural networks that stack multiple recursive layers over parse-tree computation to add depth “in space.”
- Unties leaf vs. internal parameters in the first layer, uses ReLUs, dropout, fixed pretrained embeddings, and AdaGrad for training.
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank with node-level supervision, outperforming matched-parameter shallow RNNs and achieving state-of-the-art vs. RNTN, MV-RNN, and Paragraph Vectors.
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbations, nearest-neighbor phrases) indicate successive layers capture different compositional aspects.
- Positions the work between tree-structured compositional models and stacked/deep recurrent networks; stacking recursive layers is a natural extension.
- Method is clearly specified at a high level and empirical comparisons control for total parameter count.
- Training recipe is practical and well motivated, improving shallow baselines too.
- Some key design choices lack justification or ablation: no embedding fine-tuning, shared dropout masks, and classifier connected only to final layer.
- Binary classification evaluation deviates from the standard SST protocol, limiting comparability to prior work.
- Notation and initialization details are insufficiently defined; some sentences are incomplete and dimensionalities are unclear.
- Originality is incremental but novel in stacking recursive layers; combination of known techniques in this setting is interesting and effective.
- Significance is supported by improvements on SST, but broader impact would be clearer with root-level supervision experiments or additional datasets.
- Strength: simple, well-motivated architecture with clear ties to existing literature.
- Strength: strong empirical performance with careful parameter-budget matching and modern training practices.
- Strength: thoughtful qualitative analyses revealing layer-specific behaviors.
- Strength: generally reproducible training protocol and practical implementation choices.
- Request ablation: compare shallow RNN with fine-tuned embeddings vs. deep RNN with/without fine-tuning under matched budgets.
- Request proper binary-protocol experiments trained specifically for binary classification (neutral removed).
- Request experiments with only root supervision (or additional sentence-level datasets) to test generalization beyond dense supervision.
- Baselines: include published DCNN numbers on the same split and ensure all baselines use identical partitions or note non-comparability.
- Dropout: justify sharing dropout masks across nodes and report sensitivity to independent masks per node.
- Stability: consider/report gradient clipping or norm-constrained recurrent matrices in addition to L2 penalties on activations.
- Supervision: ablate connecting classifiers at lower layers or using multi-loss setups to assess optimization benefits.
- Notation: clarify Wη, Wxh, WL, WR, U, b, c with explicit dimensions and present separate equations for leaf vs. internal transformations.
- Speculative claims about ReLU sparsity/tied parameters should be ablated or tempered with evidence.
- Initialization and variance: complete the initialization description and report results averaged over multiple runs with standard deviations.
- Pros for acceptance: clear incremental contribution, strong empirical gains, careful parameter control, and useful qualitative insights.
- Cons against acceptance: missing crucial ablations, non-comparable binary evaluation, limited dataset diversity, and unclear technical details.
- Overall: promising and convincing on SST but borderline pending requested ablations and clarifications; would lean toward acceptance if authors address experimental and technical gaps.
