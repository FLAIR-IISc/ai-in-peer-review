### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Recursive Neural Network (Deep RNN), a novel architecture designed to model compositional structures in language. The key innovation is to extend standard Recursive Neural Networks, which are inherently "deep in structure" due to their traversal of a parse tree, by stacking multiple recursive layers to also create "depth in space." This design allows the model to learn a hierarchy of representations, where each subsequent layer can potentially capture more abstract compositional meaning.

The authors also introduce a well-motivated modification to the standard RNN formulation by "untying" the weights for leaf (word) and internal (phrase) nodes in the first layer. This design choice enables the use of large, pre-trained word vectors with smaller hidden phrase representations and facilitates the effective use of non-saturating activation functions like ReLUs.

The proposed model is evaluated on the challenging Stanford Sentiment Treebank for fine-grained sentiment classification. The Deep RNN achieves new state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong baselines including the Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN) and Paragraph Vectors. The paper's claims are supported by quantitative experiments that demonstrate the benefit of depth over width and by insightful qualitative analyses suggesting that different layers of the model learn distinct aspects of syntax and semantics.

### **Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed Deep RNN architecture is a novel, intuitive, and significant extension of recursive models. The explicit distinction between depth-in-structure and depth-in-space is a valuable conceptual contribution that parallels recent successful work in recurrent networks (e.g., stacked LSTMs) and provides a new and powerful way to build more expressive compositional models.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The model establishes a new state-of-the-art on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, a widely-used and difficult benchmark. The experiments clearly show that the proposed deep models consistently outperform shallow counterparts with a similar number of parameters (Table 1a), providing strong evidence for the benefits of the proposed architecture. The improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods are substantial.
3.  **High Clarity and Quality of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivations for the architectural choices—such as untying leaves from internal nodes, using rectified linear units, and the specific form of the deep connections—are clearly explained and well-justified. The figures effectively illustrate the model's structure and information flow.
4.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting performance metrics and provide valuable qualitative analyses to understand what the model has learned. The input perturbation experiment (Figure 3) and the nearest neighbor phrase analysis (Table 2) offer compelling evidence for the central hypothesis that stacked layers learn a hierarchy of representations, progressing from lexical and syntactic features in lower layers to more abstract sentiment-related semantics in the final layer.

### **Weaknesses and Arguments for Rejection**

1.  **Methodological Issue with Hyperparameter Tuning:** The primary concern with the paper is the experimental methodology for model selection. In Table 1a, the authors compare models of varying depth (`l`) and width (`|h|`) to find the best-performing configuration. However, these results are reported on the final test set. This implies that model selection and hyperparameter tuning were performed using the test set, which violates standard machine learning practice and may lead to an overestimation of the model's true generalization performance. The best model configuration should be selected based on performance on the development set, with the test set used only for a single, final evaluation of the selected model. The authors should clarify this procedure or, if necessary, re-evaluate after selecting the best model based on the development set.
2.  **Missing Architectural Ablation:** The proposed architecture in Equation 4 combines recursive information flow within a layer (via the `W` matrices) with feed-forward information flow between layers at each node (via the `V` matrix). While the model is clearly powerful, the contribution of these vertical (`V`) connections at *every* node is not explicitly ablated. It would significantly strengthen the paper to compare this design to a simpler alternative. For example, what would be the performance of a model where a standard shallow RNN is computed first, and then a multi-layer perceptron (with non-shared weights) is applied to each node's final representation? This analysis would help isolate the benefit of the proposed interleaved recursive and feed-forward processing.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   Could the authors elaborate on the decision to use a shared dropout mask for all nodes within a given layer for a sentence? The text states "we drop the same units of the hidden layer at each node." This is a sensible choice, but a brief justification or comparison to alternative dropout strategies (e.g., using a different mask for each node application) could be beneficial for future work in this area.
*   In Section 3.2, the comparison between a 3-layer RNN with L2 regularization (score 46.06) and one with dropout (score 49.5) is very effective at highlighting the importance of the regularization strategy. This is a great piece of analysis.
*   Minor typo/phrasing suggestion: In the abstract, "Recursive neural networks comprise a class of architecture..." is slightly awkward. It could be rephrased to "Recursive neural networks constitute a class of architecture..." or "The class of recursive neural networks comprises...".
