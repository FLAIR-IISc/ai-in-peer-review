This paper introduces the deep recursive neural network (deep RNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation for this approach, which introduces depth "in space" in addition to the inherent depth "in structure," is drawn from analogous successful developments in deep recurrent networks. The authors evaluate this model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, incorporating several modern techniques including the use of pre-trained word vectors, rectifier linear units (ReLUs), and dropout for regularization.

The paper presents a number of strengths. The proposed deep RNN architecture is a novel and logical extension of prior work on both recursive and recurrent models. The experimental results are significant, achieving a new state-of-the-art on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank benchmark. The analysis showing strong gains from using components like ReLUs and dropout is also a valuable contribution. The manuscript is well-written and organized, providing sufficient detail to allow for the reproduction of the reported results.

However, the paper has weaknesses related to its experimental design and the justification for certain modeling choices. These concerns currently temper the otherwise strong results and should be addressed to solidify the paper's contributions.

Detailed Comments:
*   **Clarity in Methodology:** The notation in Section 2.2 is unclear. The terms `W^{η·}` and `W^{xh·}` are used without explicit definition. It would improve clarity to provide separate equations for leaf node and internal node computations, and to include a summary of all model parameters (e.g., {W_L, W_R, V, U, b, c}) along with their matrix dimensionalities.

*   **Justification for Untying:** The justification for untying leaves and internals (lines 148-154) relies on an argument about rectifiers producing sparse, non-negative vectors, which may conflict with dense word embeddings. This claim is presented as speculation; it would be stronger if supported by empirical evidence from an ablative experiment.

*   **Missing Baseline:** A relevant and important baseline from recent literature appears to be missing: "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" by Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL 2014), which also reports strong performance on sentence-level tasks.

*   **Word Vector Fine-tuning:** A significant experimental choice is the decision not to fine-tune word vectors. While this simplifies the model, it introduces a potential confound. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows word embeddings to capture sentiment-specific semantics. It is conceivable that a shallow RNN with fine-tuned embeddings, which can directly adapt the words to the task, could perform as well or better than a deep RNN with fixed embeddings that must learn this adaptation through its layers. This should be investigated as a control condition. The observed benefit of depth might be an artifact of the word vectors remaining fixed.

*   **Shared Dropout:** The motivation for sharing dropout units across all nodes within a layer and sentence (line 251) is not explained. Could the authors clarify the reasoning behind this choice over applying dropout independently at each node?

*   **Model Stability:** Regarding model stability with non-saturating activations, the authors use a small L2 penalty. It would be useful to know if alternative techniques common for deep/recurrent models, such as gradient clipping or weight norm constraints, were considered.

*   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The evaluation protocol for binary classification does not align with prior work. By training on all 5 classes (including neutral) and then decoding for a binary decision, the training conditions differ from baselines that train a classifier specifically for the positive/negative distinction. To ensure a fair comparison, this experiment should be re-run by training a separate binary classifier only on the non-neutral examples.

*   **Generalization to Root-Level Tasks:** The paper's experiments are confined to a dataset with rich, per-node supervision. A key question is whether the benefits of deep RNNs generalize to more common NLP tasks where only a single label is available at the root of the tree. The rich supervision signal may be particularly helpful in mitigating vanishing gradient issues in a deep model. Including experiments on 1-2 sentence-level classification datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the general utility of this architecture.

In summary, while the paper introduces a promising architecture with strong initial results, its claims would be substantially more convincing with additional experiments. I recommend that the authors: (1) conduct a proper binary classification experiment for fair comparison; (2) investigate the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings as a crucial control experiment; and (3) evaluate the model on datasets with only sentence-level labels to demonstrate broader applicability. Addressing these points would provide stronger evidence for the benefits of adding depth to recursive networks and would make this a very compelling paper.
