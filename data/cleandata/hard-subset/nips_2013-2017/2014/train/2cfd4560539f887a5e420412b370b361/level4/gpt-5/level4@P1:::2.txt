Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (deep RNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure” found in tree-structured recursive models. Within each layer, parent representations are composed from children via shared WL/WR, while node-wise connections V pass information vertically from layer i−1 to layer i at the same tree node; the classifier is attached only to the final layer. The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes so that words and phrases live in different spaces, enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs), and they leverage large pretrained word vectors with relatively small hidden dimensions.

The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained (5-way) and binary sentiment classification. Against prior recursive models (RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) and non-tree baselines (Paragraph Vectors, DCNN), deep RNNs achieve new state-of-the-art results. The paper further provides qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

This work builds directly on Socher et al.’s recursive models (RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) and is conceptually related to “deep” recurrent stacking in the time dimension. Here, depth is introduced across layers over a tree, which is a natural extension of stacking to structured inputs.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized exposition; the architectural choices are well-motivated.
- Simple, effective modifications to prior RNNs are demonstrated to work in practice:
  • Untying leaf and nonterminal nodes.
  • Using ReLUs in the recursive setting.
  • Fixing large pretrained word vectors while using smaller hidden states for nonterminals.
  • Stacking recursive layers with node-wise inter-layer connections V, rather than merely replacing a single layer with a deeper MLP.
- Strong empirical results on SST, with consistent gains over shallow counterparts under a fixed parameter budget and new state-of-the-art performance versus RNTN and Paragraph Vectors.
- Useful qualitative analyses that provide insight into what different layers learn.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Evaluation protocol: Table 1(a) appears to report model selection over depth/width on the final test set. Model selection should be performed on the development set; please report those results and reserve the test set for a single final evaluation. As written, this risks tuning on the test set.
- Additional comparison: It would be informative to evaluate a variant that removes the inter-layer node-wise connections V (i.e., have each layer compose only from its within-layer children and pass information upward structurally, without feeding node representations from layer i−1 into layer i). This would clarify the contribution of V versus depth alone.
- Minor writing issues/typos:
  • “comprise a class of architecture” → “comprise a class of architectures.”

Quality:
- Technically sound and carefully implemented. Training details (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad, initialization) are appropriate and justified. The results are strong, but the test-set tuning concern needs to be addressed to solidify the claims.

Clarity:
- Very clearly written and well-organized; figures and explanations make the architecture easy to understand. The qualitative analyses effectively illustrate layer behavior.

Originality:
- Stacking recursive layers over trees with explicit inter-layer connections is a clean and novel extension of prior recursive models. Untying leaf/internal representations to enable ReLUs in tree composition is also a practical and useful design choice.

Significance:
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on a widely used benchmark and demonstrates that added depth (under a parameter budget) improves compositional modeling. The approach is likely to influence future work on structured deep architectures for language.

Pros for acceptance:
- New, well-motivated deep recursive architecture.
- Strong empirical gains and SOTA results on SST.
- Clear presentation and insightful qualitative analyses.
- Demonstrates benefits of depth under a fixed parameter budget.

Cons against acceptance:
- Model selection appears to use the test set (Table 1(a)); results should be revisited using the dev set.
- Missing comparison to a “no-V” variant to isolate the effect of inter-layer node-wise connections.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a simple, compelling idea, clear exposition, and state-of-the-art results. Addressing the evaluation protocol and adding the suggested comparison would further strengthen it.
