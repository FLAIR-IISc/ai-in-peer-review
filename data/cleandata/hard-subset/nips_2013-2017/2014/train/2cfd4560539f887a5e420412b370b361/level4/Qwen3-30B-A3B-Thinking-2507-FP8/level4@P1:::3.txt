

This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs), constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers to add spatial depth while preserving the structural depth inherent in recursive networks. The authors extend Socher et al.’s 2013 NIPS work on recursive neural networks for sentiment analysis by introducing a hierarchical representation via layer stacking, rather than relying solely on structural depth. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), where DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks of equivalent parameter count and achieve new state-of-the-art results on fine-grained sentiment classification. Key innovations include using rectified linear units (ReLUs), pretrained word embeddings (fixed during training), and dropout regularization, with qualitative analyses showing distinct compositional roles across layers.

**Strengths:**  
The core idea of combining spatial and structural depth is well-motivated by recent advances in deep recurrent networks (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen, NIPS 2013). The empirical results are compelling: DRNNs consistently outperform shallow counterparts and prior baselines (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors), with state-of-the-art performance on SST. The qualitative analyses—input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrase examination—effectively demonstrate that deeper layers capture increasingly abstract compositional aspects (e.g., sentiment intensity vs. raw word similarity). The writing is clear, and sufficient implementation details are provided for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
1. **Unjustified claims and missing baselines:** The assertion about rectifier activation’s benefits (lines 148–154) lacks empirical support; the authors should cite Glorot et al. (NIPS 2011) or reference their own experiments. The baseline list omits Kalchbrenner et al.’s 2014 ACL CNN model (a direct competitor for sentence modeling), weakening the comparison.  
2. **Word embedding fine-tuning:** Fixing word vectors (a choice not justified) is problematic. Since the task is sentiment-based, fine-tuning word embeddings would align with established practice (e.g., Socher et al., NIPS 2013), where word vectors adapt to sentiment context. The authors should test whether DRNNs’ gains depend on this choice—e.g., compare shallow RNNs with/without fine-tuned embeddings. Without this, the DRNN advantage may simply reflect the absence of fine-tuning.  
3. **Binary classification protocol:** The binary results (Section 3.2) are invalid due to training on neutral examples (5-class labels), violating standard protocols (e.g., Socher et al., NIPS 2013). A separate binary classifier should be trained to enable fair comparison.  
4. **Shared dropout units:** The rationale for sharing dropout masks across tree nodes (line 251) is unclear. The authors should justify this design choice or explore alternatives (e.g., per-node dropout).  
5. **Depth justification:** The paper claims depth improves performance, but the experiments don’t test whether depth is beneficial *only* when predicting at multiple tree nodes (as in SST). A critical gap: DRNNs’ advantage should be validated on a task with a single root label (e.g., standard sentence sentiment), where structural depth alone suffices. Without this, the spatial depth claim remains unproven.  

**Recommendation:**  
This is a technically sound contribution with strong empirical results, but the current evidence is insufficient to fully validate the core claim about spatial depth. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:  
- Adding Kalchbrenner et al.’s CNN baseline,  
- Comparing fine-tuned vs. fixed word embeddings,  
- Redoing binary classification per standard protocols,  
- Testing DRNNs on a single-label sentiment task (e.g., SST binary).  

Without these, the claim that "depth across space" is the key innovation lacks robust support. The current results suggest DRNNs are effective, but the *reason* for their success (spatial depth vs. other factors like dropout) remains ambiguous. Given the high quality of the core idea and results, I recommend *weak acceptance* pending these experiments. If the authors address these points, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
