This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, a concept motivated by the success of a similar approach with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, albeit an extension of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are interesting in themselves. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable or not well justified, and I believe some additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper itself is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is given for the reader to reproduce the results on their own.

Detailed comments/questions:

- Line 138: The variables W^eta and W^xh are not defined. Are these meant to refer to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to have two equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. It would also be helpful to summarize the parameter space, including the dimensionalities of each matrix when defined. The parameter space could be summarized as {WL, WR, U, b, c}.

- Lines 148-154: This section appears speculative. Is this based on empirical results? If so, it should be mentioned. I am not convinced that this is actually an issue.

- Baseline: The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year for a more comprehensive comparison.

- The decision not to fine-tune the word vectors is puzzling. Fine-tuning them on a sentiment task allows the word embeddings to become reflective of their sentiment. It seems counterintuitive to have phrases and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment but where individual word vectors are not. Although this is acceptable with a deep RNN, as word vectors can be forward-passed across layers, it is possible that the improvements obtained with deep RNNs are due to the lack of fine-tuning. A single layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings may perform better. I think this needs to be controlled for.

- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What was the motivation for this choice?

- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, the authors could try constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- Binary classification: This should be done separately, as the current protocol deviates from the standard experiment protocol by including neutral class examples at training time. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case in the treebank experiments. However, it would have been useful to see whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. Would the authors still expect to see an improvement over a single layer RNN? The use of deep RNNs seems like a promising approach, but I would like to see some additional experiments. I would recommend that the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to the proper protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would result in a much stronger paper and stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
