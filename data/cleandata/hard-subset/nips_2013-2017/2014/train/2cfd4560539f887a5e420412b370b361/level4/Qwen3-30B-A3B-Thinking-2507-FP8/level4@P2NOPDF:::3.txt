

This paper introduces "deep recursive networks" by extending depth across spatial layers, drawing inspiration from deep recurrent networks. Each layer employs layer-specific parameters shared across heights, alongside dropout, ReLUs, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings. Experiments demonstrate strong results on the sentiment treebank (Socher et al.), though the approach constitutes a generalization of techniques applied to recurrent networks rather than a fundamentally novel concept. While the performance gains from dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy, several methodological choices lack sufficient justification, necessitating additional experiments before acceptance can be recommended. The paper is generally well-written with adequate detail for reproducibility.

The primary concerns center on experimental design and interpretation. First, the decision to fix word embeddings (without fine-tuning) is problematic. Fine-tuning embeddings for sentiment tasks typically enhances representation quality, yet the authors use static embeddings. This creates an inconsistency: sentence/phrase vectors become sentiment-discriminative, but individual word vectors do not. Crucially, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might outperform their deep model, as the latter’s gains could stem from embedding rigidity rather than depth. The authors must compare against fine-tuned baselines to isolate the effect of depth.

Second, the binary classification protocol is flawed. Training includes neutral-class examples, deviating from standard protocols where neutral examples are excluded during training. This invalidates direct comparison with prior work. The authors must re-run binary classification per the correct protocol (excluding neutral labels during training).

Third, the analogy to deep recurrent networks assumes node-wise predictions (as in treebank experiments). However, the paper does not test whether depth provides benefits when only a root label exists (e.g., standard sentence classification). The authors should evaluate this scenario to confirm depth’s utility beyond tree-structured prediction.

Additional critical gaps require addressing:
- **Baseline comparison**: The paper omits Kalchbrenner et al.’s (ACL 2014) convolutional neural network, a relevant baseline for sentence modeling.
- **Equation clarity**: Line 138 references undefined matrices $W^{\eta}$ and $W^{xh}$. Please define all parameters explicitly (e.g., $\{W_L, W_R, U, b, c\}$) with dimensionalities, and separate input/hidden layer equations.
- **Speculative claims**: Lines 148–154 assert unverified issues (e.g., "this is a problem"). If empirical, cite results; otherwise, remove or justify.
- **Shared dropout**: The motivation for "shared dropout units" (line 251) is unclear. Please elaborate.
- **Stability**: Gradient clipping or weight norm constraints (common in RNNs) could address stability (lines 255–256) instead of ad-hoc solutions.

The authors should conduct three key experiments to strengthen the paper:  
1. Re-run binary classification under the correct protocol (excluding neutral labels during training).  
2. Compare deep networks with and without fine-tuned embeddings.  
3. Test on datasets with only global labels (e.g., SST-1) to assess depth’s value beyond tree-structured prediction.  

These additions would provide robust evidence for the proposed method’s advantages. Without them, the claim that "depth across space" is beneficial remains unverified, as observed gains could arise from embedding fixation or protocol inconsistencies. The current results, while strong, do not sufficiently isolate depth as the causal factor.

The paper’s clarity and reproducibility are commendable, but the unresolved methodological gaps undermine the contribution. Addressing the above points would transform this into a compelling contribution to structured prediction. As-is, it lacks sufficient evidence to justify its core claim.
