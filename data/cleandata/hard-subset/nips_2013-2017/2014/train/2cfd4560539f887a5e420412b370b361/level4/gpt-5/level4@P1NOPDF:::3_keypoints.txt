- Paper proposes “deep” recursive networks with layer-wise parameter sharing across tree heights, uses dropout/ReLUs, and fixes pre-trained word embeddings; evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).  
- Method is novel but essentially a generalization of deep recurrent nets.  
- Reported performance on SST is strong, with notable gains from dropout and ReLUs.  
- Several modeling and experimental choices are questionable or insufficiently justified; additional experiments required before acceptance.  
- Paper is generally clear and provides sufficient replication detail.  
- Notation issue: W^η and W^xh are undefined; authors should present separate equations for inputs vs hidden layers and summarize the parameter set and matrix dimensionalities.  
- Lines 148–154 read speculative; clarify whether statements are based on empirical observations or theory.  
- Missing baseline: include results from Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) CNN for sentence modeling.  
- Word embeddings: authors do not fine-tune pretrained vectors; need to justify or control for this since fine-tuning could account for improvements.  
- Question whether gains attributed to deep RNNs might instead come from fixing embeddings; compare to a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings.  
- Shared dropout units (line 251): rationale for this choice is not provided and should be explained.  
- Stability suggestions (lines 255–256): consider weight-norm constraints or gradient truncation/clipping as alternatives.  
- Binary classification protocol is improper: training included neutral examples, so binary results aren’t comparable to standard benchmarks and should be redone separately.  
- Scope of depth: test whether depth helps when only a single root/global label exists; recommend additional experiments on 1–2 datasets with only global labels.  
- Overall recommendation: redo binary experiment with correct protocol, study embedding fine-tuning effects, add additional global-label datasets, and include the CNN baseline to strengthen claims about depth across space.
