This paper introduces “deep” recursive networks by adding depth across spatial structure, inspired by the success of adding depth in recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters, shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also employ dropout and ReLUs, and they keep word representations fixed to pre-trained embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Socher et al. sentiment treebank.

The method is novel, though it is essentially a generalization of the approach used in recurrent networks. The authors report very strong performance on the sentiment treebank, and the gains from using dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy in their own right. That said, several modeling and experimental design choices are questionable or insufficiently justified, and I believe additional experiments are needed before I can fully recommend acceptance.

The paper is largely clear, and it includes sufficient detail for readers to reproduce the results.

Detailed comments/questions:
- line 138: What are W^eta and W^xh? These are not defined. Are they meant to denote W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to present two separate equations: one for the inputs and one for the hidden layers. It would also help to summarize the parameter set, e.g., {WL, WR, U, b, c}, and to specify the dimensionality of each matrix at definition time.
- lines 148–154: This section reads as speculative. Is it supported by your own empirical findings? If so, please state that explicitly. As written, I am not convinced this is actually a concern.
- baseline: Please include results from “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL this year).
- Why did you not fine-tune the word vectors? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows embeddings to encode sentiment information. It is odd to have phrase and sentence vectors that are sentiment-discriminative while individual word vectors are not. (I recognize this can be acceptable with a deep RNN if you rely on forward propagation across layers.) Is it possible that the improvements you observe with deep RNNs arise because the word vectors are not fine-tuned? Conceivably, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings could perform better. This needs to be controlled.
- line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this design choice?
- lines 255–256 (stability): As an alternative, consider constraining weight norms or truncating/clipping gradients, as is common with recurrent networks.
- binary classification: This should be evaluated separately. Your training includes neutral examples, which deviates from the established protocol, making your results not directly comparable to prior work.
- The analogy to deep recurrent networks is appropriate when predictions are made at every tree node, as in the treebank experiments. It would also be helpful to test whether deep RNNs offer an advantage when only a single root label is available. Would you still expect improvements over a single-layer RNN in that setting? The approach appears promising, but additional experiments are needed. I recommend: (1) redoing the binary classification experiment according to the standard protocol; (2) analyzing the impact of fine-tuning the word embeddings; and (3) adding 1–2 datasets with only a global label. These additions would significantly strengthen the paper and the evidence for the utility of depth across space.
