Paper introduces deep recursive neural networks stacking recursive layers for hierarchical compositionality and reports state-of-the-art results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).

Technical soundness and clear experimental design; depth empirically improves fine-grained accuracy (49.5% vs. 48.1% for shallow RNNs).

Use of dropout, ReLUs, and fixed pretrained word vectors follows established practices and is well-motivated in text.

Paper is well organized and detailed; equations and figures (3–5, Fig.2) make architecture and SST setup reproducible.

Stacking recursive layers is a novel extension bridging structural depth (recursive nets) and spatial depth (deep recurrent nets).

Qualitative analyses show layer-specific compositionality, supporting claims about hierarchical representations.

Claim that rectifiers (ReLUs) are beneficial lacks empirical support — no comparison to tanh on identical architectures.

Keeping word vectors fixed is unjustified; no ablation comparing fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings, risking confounding depth benefits with representation adaptability.

Binary classification protocol deviates from SST standard by decoding binary labels from the fine-grained model and excluding neutrals, undermining comparability to baselines.

Notation ambiguity in Equation (3) (e.g., "W^eta" vs. "W^xh") and unspecified parameter dimensionalities reduce clarity.

Stability argument for L2 regularization is speculative without empirical comparison to common alternatives like gradient clipping.

Pro: strong technical contribution, rigorous SST evaluation, clear architecture, and SOTA empirical results.

Con: critical experimental omissions (embedding fine-tuning, binary protocol), ambiguous notation, and insufficient justification for key design choices.

Required revisions: (1) re-run binary classification under SST’s standard protocol, (2) add ablation on embedding fine-tuning vs. fixed, and (3) include Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) as a baseline.

Recommendation: Accept contingent on the above experiments; without them the claimed general benefits of depth remain insufficiently supported.
