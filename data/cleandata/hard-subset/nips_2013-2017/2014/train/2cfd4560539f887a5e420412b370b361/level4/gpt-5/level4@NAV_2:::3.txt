This paper introduces “deep” recursive neural networks by adding depth across space, inspired by analogous gains in deep recurrent networks. Each recursive layer has its own parameters shared across all tree heights within that layer. The authors also employ dropout and ReLU activations while keeping word embeddings fixed to pretrained vectors. Experiments are conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (Socher et al.).

The approach is novel as an extension of ideas from deep recurrent networks to the recursive/tree-structured setting. The method delivers strong results on the sentiment treebank, and the improvements attributable to dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, several modeling and experimental choices are insufficiently motivated, and additional controls would strengthen the empirical case before I can enthusiastically recommend acceptance.

The manuscript is generally clear and contains enough detail for reproduction in its current form.

Detailed comments / questions:
- Line 138: Please clarify the notation W^η and W^xh, which are not previously defined. Are these intended to indicate W_L^η and W_R^η? It would be clearer to present two separate equations—one for leaf-to-hidden (input) transformations and another for hidden-to-hidden (internal) composition—and to explicitly enumerate the parameter set (e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}). Including the dimensionality of each matrix at first mention would further improve clarity.
- Lines 148–154: The claims here read as speculative. Are they supported by your own empirical findings? If so, please state this explicitly; as written, the issue is not convincingly established.
- Baselines: Please include results for “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) for completeness and comparability.
- Word embedding fine-tuning: Why did you choose not to fine-tune embeddings? Fine-tuning on sentiment often yields word vectors that more directly encode polarity. Given that your phrase/sentence representations are optimized for sentiment while word vectors remain fixed, it is possible that the observed gains from deeper RNNs are compensating for not adapting the embeddings. A fair control would compare against a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings; such a model might close part of the gap.
- Line 251 (shared dropout masks): What motivated using the same dropout mask across all nodes within a layer for a given sentence? Please provide justification or an ablation comparing shared vs. independent masks across nodes.
- Lines 255–256 (stability): In addition to the small L2 penalty on weights and activations, consider alternative stabilization strategies commonly used in recurrent settings, such as weight norm constraints or gradient clipping/truncation.
- Binary classification: This should be trained as a separate model to adhere to the established evaluation protocol (which excludes neutral examples). Using a 5-way model and post-hoc mapping is not directly comparable to prior binary results.
- Scope of benefit for depth: The analogy to deep recurrent nets is most direct when supervision is available at every node, as in SST. It would be informative to test whether deep RNNs still outperform single-layer RNNs when only a root label is available. I recommend: (i) re-running the binary classification with the standard protocol, (ii) studying the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and (iii) adding 1–2 datasets with only sentence-level labels. These additions would significantly strengthen the evidence for the benefits of depth across space.
