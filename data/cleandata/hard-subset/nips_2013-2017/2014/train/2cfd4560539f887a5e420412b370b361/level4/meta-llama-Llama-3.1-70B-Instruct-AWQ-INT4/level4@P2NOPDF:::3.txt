This paper proposes a novel approach to recursive neural networks by adding depth across space, a concept inspired by the success of deepening recurrent networks. Each layer in the proposed architecture has its own set of parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also incorporate dropout and ReLUs into their model, while utilizing pre-trained word embeddings with fixed representations. The experimental evaluation is conducted on the sentiment treebank dataset introduced by Socher et al.

The proposed method is innovative, building upon the established approach of deepening recurrent networks. The authors achieve impressive performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The performance gains obtained through the use of dropout and ReLUs are particularly noteworthy. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable or lack sufficient justification, and I believe that additional experimentation is necessary to fully support the claims made in the paper.

The paper is well-written, providing sufficient detail for the reader to reproduce the results. However, there are several aspects that require clarification or additional experimentation.

Detailed comments and questions:

- Line 138: The variables W^eta and W^xh are not defined. It is unclear whether these refer to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R. To improve clarity, it would be beneficial to provide two separate equations for the inputs and hidden layers. Additionally, summarizing the parameter space, including the dimensionalities of each matrix, would be helpful.

- Lines 148-154: The discussion in this section appears speculative, and it is unclear whether it is based on empirical results. If this is the case, it would be beneficial to provide evidence to support the claims made. Without further justification, it is difficult to assess the validity of this argument.

- Baseline: The authors should include a comparison with the convolutional neural network for modeling sentences proposed by Kalchbrenner et al. in their ACL paper this year.

- The decision not to fine-tune the word vectors is surprising, as fine-tuning them for a sentiment task allows the word embeddings to capture sentiment information. This seems counterintuitive, as the model is designed to capture sentiment information at the phrase and sentence levels, but not at the individual word level. It is possible that the improvements obtained with deep RNNs are due to the lack of fine-tuning, and a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings may perform equally well. This aspect needs to be controlled for in the experiments.

- Line 251: The motivation behind using shared dropout units is unclear. Providing a clear justification for this design choice would strengthen the paper.

- Lines 255-256: Instead of using shared dropout units, the authors could consider constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- Binary classification: The current experimental setup deviates from the standard protocol, as it includes neutral class examples during training. To address this, the authors should conduct the binary classification experiment separately, following the standard protocol.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case in the treebank experiments. However, it would be beneficial to investigate whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. The authors should redo the binary classification experiment, study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would significantly strengthen the paper and provide more robust evidence for the effectiveness of depth across space.
