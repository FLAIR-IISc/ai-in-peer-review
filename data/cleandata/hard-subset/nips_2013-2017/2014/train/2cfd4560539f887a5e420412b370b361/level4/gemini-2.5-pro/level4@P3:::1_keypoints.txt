- Introduces Deep Recursive Neural Network: stacks multiple recursive layers to add "depth in space" for modeling compositionality.  
- Hypothesis: stacked recursive layers learn hierarchical representations, with higher layers capturing more abstract compositional features.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank; deep RNNs outperform shallow counterparts with similar parameters and achieve new state-of-the-art vs RNTN and Paragraph Vectors.  
- High technical quality: well-motivated architecture and sound experimental methodology.  
- Rigorous evaluation: standard benchmark, comprehensive baselines, and ablation comparing depth vs width under fixed parameter budgets.  
- Uses best practices (pretrained word vectors, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) contributing to strong results.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) provide evidence that different layers capture different compositional phenomena.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, organization, and effective figures illustrating model structure and data flow.  
- Architectural details (e.g., untying weights for leaves vs internal nodes) are well explained and justified.  
- Original contribution: novel application of stacked/deep layers to tree-structured recursive networks, extending prior recursive models.  
- High significance: introduces a generalizable architectural paradigm for structured prediction and achieves impactful empirical gains.  
- Key insight: depth is more effective than width for a fixed parameter budget in this setting.  
- Suggestion: strengthen linguistic motivation by explicitly hypothesizing which phenomena each layer captures (e.g., local composition, negation, clause-level integration).  
- Suggestion: explore/report architectural variants (e.g., connecting output to all hidden layers) and analyze trade-offs.  
- Overall assessment: excellent, novel, and influential work with strong empirical and qualitative support; minor improvement needed in articulating linguistic intuitions.
