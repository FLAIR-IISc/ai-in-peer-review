This paper proposes “deep” recursive networks by adding depth across space (i.e., across tree structure), motivated by analogous gains observed with deep recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters, shared across tree heights within that layer. The model also incorporates ReLUs and dropout, and uses fixed, pre-trained word embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (Socher et al.).

The approach is novel, albeit a natural generalization of techniques used in recurrent nets. Results on the sentiment treebank are very strong, and the observed gains from using dropout and ReLUs are interesting in their own right. However, several modeling and experimental design choices are insufficiently justified, and I believe additional experiments are needed before I can recommend acceptance with confidence.

The paper is generally clear and contains enough detail for reproduction.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Line 138 (notation): W^η and W^xh are not defined. Are these intended to denote W^η_L and W^η_R? It would be clearer to provide two separate equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. Please also summarize the full parameter set, e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}, and include dimensionalities for all matrices when first introduced.
- Lines 148–154 (speculation): The discussion here seems speculative. Is it supported by your own empirical observations? If so, please state this explicitly. As written, I am not convinced that the stated issue actually arises.
- Baselines: Please include results from “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014).
- Word embeddings: Why did you not fine-tune the word vectors? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task typically makes embeddings more sentiment-aware. It is odd to have phrase/sentence vectors that are sentiment-discriminative while individual word vectors remain fixed. While a deep RNN might propagate transformations across layers, it is still possible that your deep-RNN gains stem from not fine-tuning the words. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings could plausibly perform better. This needs to be controlled for experimentally.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this choice? Please explain the rationale.
- Lines 255–256 (stability): As an alternative to your stabilization approach, you might also consider constraining weight norms or truncating gradients, as is commonly done with recurrent networks.
- Binary classification protocol: This experiment should be conducted separately and in accordance with the established protocol. Including neutral examples during training deviates from prior setups, making your results not directly comparable to existing approaches.
- Scope of evaluation: The analogy to deep recurrent nets is compelling when predictions are made at every node (as in the treebank). It would be informative to test whether deep recursive models retain an advantage when only a single root label is available. Would you still expect improvements over a single-layer RNN? The approach appears promising, but additional experiments would substantially strengthen the paper.

Recommended additional experiments:
- Redo the binary classification experiment using the standard protocol (i.e., excluding neutral examples appropriately).
- Study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings versus keeping them fixed.
- Include 1–2 additional datasets with only a global (root) label to test whether depth across space provides benefits in that setting.

These additions would yield stronger evidence for the utility of depth across space and significantly improve the paper.
