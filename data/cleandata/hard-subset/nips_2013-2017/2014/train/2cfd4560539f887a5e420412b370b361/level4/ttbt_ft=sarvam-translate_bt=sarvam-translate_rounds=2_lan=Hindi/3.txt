This paper proposes the use of a 'deep' recursive network, which adds depth to the space, inspired by the success of doing so with recurrent nets. Each layer has its own parameters shared at different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLU, while fine-tuning word representations in pre-trained embeddings. Experiments on Socher et al.'s sentiment treebank have been applied.

The proposed method is novel, although essentially a generalization of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieved very strong performance on sentiment treebank datasets. The performance gains achieved with dropout and ReLU are interesting in themselves. Having said that, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable (or not well-justified), and I believe some additional experiments are necessary before fully recommending acceptance.

The paper itself is clearly written for the most part, and the reader is given enough detail to reproduce their own results.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Line 138: What are W^eta and W^xh? These are not defined. Are these only to refer to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clear to have two equations: one for the input and another for the hidden layers. It would also be helpful to briefly present the parameter space, i.e., {WL, WR, U, b, c}. It would also be helpful to include the dimensionalities of each matrix when you define them.
- Lines 148-154: This seems very speculative. Is this based on your own empirical results? If so, you should mention it. I'm not sure if this is really an issue.
- Baseline: The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modeling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al.) from ACL this year.
- Why didn't you fine-tune the word vectors properly? Fine-tuning them on the sentiment task allows the word embedding to reflect the sentiment. It seems odd to have phrase and sentence vectors that are derivatives of the sentiment but where the individual word vectors are not. (I realized this is fine with a deep RNN because you can only pass the word vectors to the layers). Is it possible that the improvements you are achieving with a deep RNN are happening because you are not fine-tuning the words properly? Perhaps, a single-layer RNN with properly fine-tuned embeddings would perform better. I think this needs to be controlled.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What was the motivation for this option?
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, you could try limiting the criteria for weights or trunking the gradient, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.
- Binary classification: This should be done separately. The issue is that during training, you are also including examples of the neutral class that deviate from the experiment protocol. As a result, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.
- Analogies with deep recurrent nets are appropriate when you are making predictions at each node in the case of treebank experiments. It would be useful to see if there is any advantage to using a deep RNN when only one, original label is present. Would you still expect to see improvements on a single-layer RNN? Using a deep RNN seems like a promising approach, but I would like to see some additional experiments. I would recommend the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to the appropriate protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings properly, and finally include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would result in a much stronger paper as well as strong evidence for the usefulness of depth in space.
