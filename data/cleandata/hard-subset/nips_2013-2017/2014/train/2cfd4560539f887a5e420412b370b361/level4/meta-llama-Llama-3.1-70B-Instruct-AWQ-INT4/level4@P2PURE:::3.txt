This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, drawing inspiration from the success of a similar approach with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, albeit an extension of the approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable and not well-justified, and I believe additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is provided for the reader to reproduce the results.

Detailed comments and questions:

- Line 138: The definitions of W^eta and W^xh are unclear. Are these equivalent to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to have two separate equations, one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. A summary of the parameter space, including the dimensionalities of each matrix, would also be helpful.

- Lines 148-154: This discussion appears speculative. Is it based on empirical results? If so, this should be explicitly stated. The argument is not entirely convincing, and further evidence is needed to support this claim.

- Baseline: The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year for a more comprehensive comparison.

- The decision not to fine-tune the word vectors is puzzling. Fine-tuning word vectors on a sentiment task allows them to capture sentiment information. It seems counterintuitive to have phrases and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment while individual word vectors are not. It is possible that the improvements obtained with deep RNNs are due to the lack of fine-tuning. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings may perform better. This variable needs to be controlled for.

- Line 251: The motivation behind using shared dropout units is unclear.

- Lines 255-256: Instead of using shared dropout units, the authors could consider constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- Binary classification: This experiment should be performed separately, as the inclusion of neutral class examples during training deviates from the standard protocol. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case in the treebank experiments. However, it would be useful to investigate whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. Would the authors still expect to see an improvement over a single-layer RNN? The use of deep RNNs seems promising, but additional experiments are needed. I recommend that the authors redo the binary classification experiment following the proper protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would result in a stronger paper and provide stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
