- Proposes a deep recursive neural network (deep RNN) that stacks multiple recursive layers to combine depth in structure and depth in space.  
- Builds on Socher et al. (2013) and clarifies the distinction between "depth in structure" (trees) and "depth in space" (stacked layers).  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) and achieves state-of-the-art fine-grained sentiment classification.  
- Technically sound with thorough experimental validation and clear implementation details (rectifier activations, pretrained word vectors, dropout).  
- Quantitative results show deeper architectures outperform shallower ones with comparable parameter counts, supporting the core hypothesis.  
- Lacks sufficient ablation studies separating effects of layer depth versus width and more justification for specific architectural choices (e.g., untied leaves vs internals).  
- Well-written and organized; figures effectively illustrate differences between standard recursive nets and the proposed deep RNN.  
- Methodology and experimental setup are detailed and reproducible, though descriptions of weight sharing across layers could be clearer.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbor phrases) provide insight into layer-wise compositionality.  
- Novelty is the systematic stacking of recursive layers and exploration of depth’s impact on compositionality, despite conceptual similarity to deep recurrent networks.  
- Should more explicitly contrast with related architectures (e.g., deep feedforward on trees) and analyze compositional differences from existing methods.  
- Significant contribution: achieves SOTA on SST and offers meaningful insights into hierarchical representations with potential to generalize beyond sentiment tasks.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of why depth helps specifically for sentiment, incomplete exploration of depth–width trade-offs, and underdeveloped links to broader applications.  
- Overall: strong, well-executed contribution that fills a meaningful gap in recursive neural networks and merits acceptance for presentation.
