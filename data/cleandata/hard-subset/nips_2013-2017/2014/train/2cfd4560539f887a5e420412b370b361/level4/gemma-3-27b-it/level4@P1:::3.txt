This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, motivated by the success of doing the same with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method is novel, though essentially a generalization of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are interesting in themselves. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable (or not well justified), and I believe some additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is given for the reader to reproduce the results.

Detailed comments / questions:

- Line 138: What are W<sup>η</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup>? These haven't been defined. Are these simply references to W<sup>η</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>η</sup><sub>R</sub>? It would be clearer to have two equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. It would also help to summarize the parameter space, i.e., {W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c}. Including the dimensionalities of each of your matrices when you define them would also be helpful.
- Lines 148-154: This seems speculative. Is this based on your own empirical results? If so, you should mention this. I'm not convinced this is actually an issue.
- Baseline: The authors should include the results of “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al.) from ACL 2014.
- Why didn't you fine-tune the word vectors? Fine-tuning them on a sentiment task allows the word embeddings to become reflective of sentiment. It seems strange to have phrase and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment while the individual word vectors are not. (I realize this is OK with a deep RNN since you could just forward-pass the word vectors across layers). Is it possible that the improvements you’re getting with deep RNNs are happening *because* you are not fine-tuning the words? Conceivably, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings should perform better. I think this needs to be controlled for.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What was the motivation for this choice?
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, you could try constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.
- Binary classification: This should be done separately. The issue is that at training time, you are also including the neutral class examples, which deviates from the standard experiment protocol. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.
- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case in the treebank experiments. It would have been useful to see whether there is an advantage to using deep RNNs when only a single, root label exists. Would you still expect to see an improvement over a single-layer RNN? The use of deep RNNs seems promising, but I would like to see some additional experiments. I recommend that the authors redo the binary classification experiment following the proper protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and finally include one or two additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments would result in a much stronger paper and stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of deep learning principles to recursive neural networks.
*   State-of-the-art results on a challenging sentiment analysis task.
*   Clear writing and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   Demonstrated benefit of depth in recursive networks, at least in this specific setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some experimental design choices lack clear justification (e.g., not fine-tuning word vectors).
*   Missing comparison to relevant baseline work (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014).
*   Limited scope of experiments – only one dataset and a specific task.
*   Speculative claims regarding the role of different layers without strong empirical backing.
