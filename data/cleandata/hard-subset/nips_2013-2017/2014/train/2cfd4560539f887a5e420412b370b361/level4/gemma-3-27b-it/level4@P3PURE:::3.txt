This paper proposes the use of ‘deep’ recursive networks by adding depth across space, motivated by the success of similar approaches with recurrent networks. Each layer incorporates its own parameters, shared across different heights within that layer. The authors further employ dropout and ReLUs, while utilizing pre-trained word embeddings to fix word representations. Experiments are conducted on the sentiment treebank dataset of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, effectively generalizing the approach commonly used with recurrent nets. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the sentiment treebank, and the performance gains achieved with dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some model and experimental design choices are questionable, or at least lack sufficient justification, and I believe further experimentation is necessary before I can fully recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results.

Detailed comments / questions:

- line 138: The definitions of W<sup>eta</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup> are unclear. Are these intended to represent W<sup>eta</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>eta</sup><sub>R</sub>? Presenting two separate equations – one for inputs and one for hidden layers – would improve clarity. Additionally, summarizing the parameter space (i.e., {W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c}) and including the dimensionalities of each matrix upon definition would be beneficial.
- lines 148-154: The claims made in this section appear speculative. If based on empirical results, this should be explicitly stated. I am not convinced this represents a genuine issue.
- baseline: The authors should include results from “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) as a baseline.
- Word embeddings: Why weren’t the word vectors fine-tuned? Finetuning them on a sentiment task allows the embeddings to become sentiment-specific. It seems incongruous to have discriminative phrase and sentence vectors while retaining non-sentiment-aware individual word vectors. (I acknowledge this is less of a concern with deep RNNs due to the forward-propagation of word vectors across layers.) It’s possible the improvements observed with deep RNNs stem from *not* fine-tuning the word vectors. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform comparably or better. This requires investigation and control.
- line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this design choice?
- lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, consider constraining weight norms or truncating gradients, techniques commonly used in recurrent neural networks to address stability issues.
- binary classification: This evaluation should be performed separately. Including neutral class examples during training deviates from established experimental protocols and renders the results incomparable to existing approaches.
- Tree structure and depth: The analogy with deep recurrent nets is well-reasoned when predictions are made at each node in the tree, as in the treebank experiments. However, it would be valuable to assess whether a benefit remains when only a single, root label is used. Would improvements still be observed over a single-layer RNN in this scenario? While the use of deep RNNs appears promising, additional experiments would strengthen the paper. I recommend the authors redo the binary classification experiment according to the correct protocol, investigate the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets that utilize only a global label. These experiments would significantly enhance the paper and provide more compelling evidence for the utility of depth across space.
