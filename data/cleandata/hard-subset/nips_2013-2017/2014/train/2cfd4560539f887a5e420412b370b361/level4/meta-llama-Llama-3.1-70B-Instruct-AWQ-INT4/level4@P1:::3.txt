This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, which is motivated by the success of doing the same thing with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the sentiment treebank of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel although essentially a generalization of the same approach used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are interesting in itself. That being said, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable (or not well justified) and I believe some additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper itself is clearly written for the most part and sufficient detail is given for the reader to reproduce the results on their own.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, there are some aspects of the model and experimental design that could be improved. For example, the authors do not provide a clear justification for their choice of not fine-tuning the word vectors, and the use of shared dropout units could be better motivated. Additionally, the binary classification experiment is not conducted according to the standard protocol, which makes it difficult to compare the results to existing approaches.

Originality: The proposed method is a novel extension of recursive neural networks, and the use of dropout and ReLUs is a new contribution. However, the idea of adding depth across space is not entirely new, as it has been explored in the context of recurrent neural networks.

Significance: The paper demonstrates strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing. However, the results could be more convincing if the authors were to conduct additional experiments, such as fine-tuning the word embeddings and including more datasets with global labels.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the reader to reproduce the results. However, there are some minor issues with notation and terminology that could be improved.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel extension of recursive neural networks that achieves strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset.
* The use of dropout and ReLUs is a new contribution that could be useful in other applications.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the reader to reproduce the results.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The model and experimental design choices could be better justified, particularly with regards to the choice of not fine-tuning the word vectors and the use of shared dropout units.
* The binary classification experiment is not conducted according to the standard protocol, which makes it difficult to compare the results to existing approaches.
* Additional experiments, such as fine-tuning the word embeddings and including more datasets with global labels, could strengthen the paper and provide more convincing evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.

Recommendations:

* The authors should provide a clearer justification for their choice of not fine-tuning the word vectors and the use of shared dropout units.
* The authors should redo the binary classification experiment according to the standard protocol.
* The authors should consider conducting additional experiments, such as fine-tuning the word embeddings and including more datasets with global labels, to strengthen the paper and provide more convincing evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
