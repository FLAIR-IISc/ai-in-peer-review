Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) that introduce depth “in space” by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of a parse-tree-structured computation. Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up as in standard tree-structured RNNs, with shared weights across the tree; across layers, each node passes its hidden state to its counterpart in the next layer, enabling hierarchical abstraction. The authors also “untie” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes in the first layer, use rectified linear units and dropout, keep word embeddings fixed to pretrained vectors, and train with AdaGrad. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with node-level supervision, yielding improvements over shallow recursive nets with matched parameter budgets and achieving state-of-the-art results over RNTN, MV-RNN, and Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases by layer) suggest that successive layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The work situates itself between earlier tree-structured models for compositional semantics (e.g., Socher et al.’s RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) and the line of research on deep/stacked recurrent networks that distinguish depth in time vs. depth in space (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). The idea of stacking recursive layers appears to be a natural extension of stacked RNNs to tree-structured computation.

Evaluation
- Quality: The method is clearly specified at a high level, and the empirical comparison controls for total parameter count, which strengthens claims about depth vs. width. The training recipe is practical and well motivated. However, some design choices (no fine-tuning of embeddings; shared dropout masks across tree nodes; output connected only to the final layer) are not fully justified or ablated. The binary classification evaluation deviates from the standard SST protocol, limiting comparability. A few notation/initialization details need clarification.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful figures and training details. Some notation (e.g., Wη, Wxh) and dimensionalities are insufficiently defined, and there is at least one incomplete sentence regarding initialization. Providing a compact parameter summary with dimensions would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward but novel architectural extension for tree models. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and the use of ReLUs and dropout are known techniques, but their combination in this setting is interesting and empirically impactful.
- Significance: Improvements over strong baselines on SST are valuable, and the qualitative analyses are insightful. Demonstrating benefits on settings with only root-level labels (no dense supervision) would broaden significance and clarify where depth in space helps most.

Strengths
- Simple and well-motivated architectural idea; clear connection to deep/stacked RNN literature.
- Strong empirical performance with careful parameter-budget matching; new state of the art on SST at the time of writing.
- Practical training choices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) that improve shallow baselines as well.
- Thoughtful qualitative analyses showing layer-specific behaviors.
- Generally reproducible training protocol.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fine-tuning embeddings: All experiments keep word embeddings fixed. This could bias the comparison in favor of deeper models that can compensate for unadapted inputs. Please add an ablation comparing (i) shallow RNN with fine-tuned embeddings, (ii) deep RNN with and without fine-tuning, while keeping parameter budgets matched.
- Binary classification protocol: Current binary results are derived from the 5-way model, which is not directly comparable to methods trained specifically for binary classification (neutral removed). Please report a model trained per the standard binary protocol.
- Generalization beyond dense supervision: It is unclear whether benefits of depth persist when only root labels are available. Please include experiments on SST using only root supervision and/or 1–2 additional datasets with sentence-level labels only (e.g., movie reviews without phrase annotations).
- Baselines: The text mentions DCNN. Please include the published DCNN numbers on the same split and ensure all reported baselines use the identical train/dev/test partition. If any results are not directly comparable, state this explicitly.
- Dropout choice: You share dropout masks across all nodes within a layer-sentence (line 251). Please motivate this design (e.g., variance reduction, stability) and report sensitivity to using independent masks per node.
- Stability: Instead of (or in addition to) L2 penalties on activations, consider and/or report results with gradient clipping and/or norm-constrained recurrent matrices, common in recurrent/recursive training.
- Supervision connection: You connect the classifier only to the last layer. While this simplifies analysis, a multi-loss setup (auxiliary predictions at lower layers) could improve optimization. A small ablation would clarify trade-offs.
- Notation and dimensions: Clarify Wη and Wxh (line 138) and explicitly define WL, WR, U, b, c with dimensions for leaf vs. internal cases. Consider presenting two equations: one for leaf-to-parent and one for internal-to-parent transformations, plus a succinct parameter summary.
- Speculative claims (lines 148–154): The argument about mismatched sparsity/density with ReLUs when tying leaf/internal parameters reads speculative. If based on experiments, please cite them; otherwise temper the claim and/or provide an ablation comparing tied vs. untied with ReLUs.
- Initialization details: The sentence “All other weights are initialized as …” appears incomplete. Please specify the distribution and scale. Also report whether results are averaged over multiple runs with different seeds (and give standard deviations), as variance can be nontrivial in deep models.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, incremental architectural contribution with solid empirical gains.
- Careful control of parameter count; strong, modern training practices.
- Insightful qualitative analysis supporting the compositional interpretation of layers.
- Potentially useful template for other structured domains beyond sentiment.

Cons against acceptance
- Key ablations missing (embedding fine-tuning; dropout mask choice; supervision placement).
- Binary evaluation not directly comparable to prior work; limited dataset diversity.
- Some claims insufficiently justified; several notation/initialization details unclear.

Overall assessment
I find the idea of adding depth in space to tree-structured models natural and the empirical improvements convincing on SST. With the requested ablations (especially on embedding fine-tuning and proper binary protocol) and at least one experiment with only root supervision, this would be a strong paper. As it stands, I am borderline positive: I would lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the experimental gaps and clarify the noted technical details.
