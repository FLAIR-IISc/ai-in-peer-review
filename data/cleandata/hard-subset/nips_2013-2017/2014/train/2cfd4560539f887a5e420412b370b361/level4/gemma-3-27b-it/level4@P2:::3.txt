This paper introduces a ‘deep’ recursive neural network by extending the concept of depth, successfully applied to recurrent networks, to the spatial dimension of recursive networks. The architecture stacks recursive layers, each sharing parameters within a given layer but learning distinct transformations. The authors also incorporate dropout and ReLU activations, utilizing pre-trained word embeddings. Evaluation is performed on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).

The proposed approach represents a novel and logical generalization of existing recursive network techniques, drawing a clear parallel to deep recurrent networks. The reported performance on the SST is strong, achieving state-of-the-art results. The observed gains from dropout and ReLU activations are noteworthy and contribute to the paper’s value. However, several modeling and experimental choices require further justification, and additional experimentation would strengthen the paper’s conclusions.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The notation is mostly clear, though some clarifications would enhance readability (see detailed comments below).

**Detailed Comments/Questions:**

*   **Notation (lines 138):** The notation W<sup>eta</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup> is unclear. It would be more precise to define separate equations for transformations applied to inputs (leaf nodes) and hidden layer nodes, specifying W<sup>eta</sup><sub>L</sub> and W<sup>eta</sup><sub>R</sub>. A summary of the parameter space, including the dimensionality of each matrix (e.g., W<sub>L</sub>: |h| x |x|), would also improve clarity.
*   **Untying Leaves and Internals Justification (lines 148-154):** The rationale for untying leaves and internals and the claim about rectifier activation units is somewhat speculative. If based on empirical observations, this should be explicitly stated and supported with results. The assertion that this is necessarily an issue requires stronger justification.
*   **Missing Baseline:** The authors should include a comparison with Kalchbrenner et al. (2014), "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences," from ACL. This represents a relevant and competitive baseline.
*   **Word Vector Fine-tuning:** The decision not to fine-tune word vectors is questionable. Fine-tuning allows embeddings to become sentiment-specific, potentially improving performance. The authors acknowledge the possibility of learning sentiment through deeper layers but haven't addressed whether this compensates for the lack of sentiment-aware initial embeddings. An experiment controlling for this (e.g., comparing a single-layer RNN with and without fine-tuning) is crucial. It's possible the gains observed with deep RNNs are partly attributable to this factor.
*   **Shared Dropout Units (line 251):** What motivated the choice to share dropout units across nodes within a layer? Providing a rationale would be helpful.
*   **Stability (lines 255-256):** While L2 regularization is a reasonable approach, alternative techniques like weight norm constraints or gradient clipping (common in recurrent networks) could also be considered and might offer advantages.
*   **Binary Classification Protocol (Binary classification):** The binary classification evaluation is flawed. Training on data including neutral examples introduces a discrepancy with standard protocols and makes comparison with existing approaches invalid. A separate binary classification model should be trained without neutral examples.
*   **Generalizability Beyond Treebank Structure:** The analogy with deep recurrent networks is strongest when predictions are made at each node in the tree, as in the SST experiments. Exploring the benefits of deep RNNs when only a single, root-level label is available would strengthen the argument for their generalizability. Would improvements still be observed compared to a single-layer RNN in this scenario?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The concept of stacking recursive layers is a natural and logical extension of existing work.
*   Strong Performance: The method achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark dataset.
*   Clear Presentation: The paper is well-written and relatively easy to follow.
*   Interesting Ablation: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dropout and ReLU activation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Justification of Design Choices: Some modeling decisions (e.g., not fine-tuning word vectors, shared dropout) lack strong justification.
*   Incomplete Experimental Evaluation: Missing baselines and a flawed binary classification experiment weaken the conclusions.
*   Limited Generalizability: The benefits of deep RNNs haven’t been demonstrated beyond the specific structure of the SST.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel contribution. However, the concerns regarding experimental design and justification of choices prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance. I believe that addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding word vector fine-tuning, a corrected binary classification experiment, and exploration of scenarios beyond tree-structured data, would significantly strengthen the paper and make a compelling case for acceptance. I lean towards a **weak accept** pending revisions that address these concerns.
