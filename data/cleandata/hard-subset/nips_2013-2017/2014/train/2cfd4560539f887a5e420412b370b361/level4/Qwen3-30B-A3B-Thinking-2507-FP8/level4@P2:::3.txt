

This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) as a generalization of conventional recursive neural networks, achieved by stacking multiple recursive layers to incorporate "depth in space" alongside the inherent "depth in structure" of recursive architectures. The authors draw inspiration from the success of deep recurrent networks (Schmidhuber, 2010; Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013) and apply their approach to fine-grained sentiment analysis using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST). The paper makes two main contributions: a novel architecture that stacks recursive layers and empirical evidence demonstrating that deeper networks outperform shallow counterparts with comparable parameter counts, achieving new state-of-the-art results on SST.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. First, the technical contribution is well-motivated and builds logically upon prior work on recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2013) and deep recurrent networks. Second, the experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies showing consistent improvements with increased depth, and qualitative analyses demonstrating that different layers capture distinct aspects of compositional meaning. Third, the methodology is clearly explained, with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the writing is generally clear and well-organized.

However, several concerns regarding experimental design and justification need addressing before the paper can be recommended for acceptance. The most significant issue concerns the decision not to fine-tune word embeddings. The authors fix pretrained word vectors (Mikolov et al., 2013) while learning phrase and sentence representations, which creates an inconsistency: the model's sentiment representations depend on non-sentiment-aware word vectors. This is particularly problematic since fine-tuning word vectors on sentiment data is standard practice (Socher et al., 2013) and could potentially yield substantial improvements. The authors should provide a rigorous comparison between their approach and a shallow RNN with fine-tuned embeddings to determine whether their depth-based improvements are genuine or merely a consequence of their embedding strategy.

The paper also makes an important methodological oversight in the binary sentiment classification experiment. The authors use the same model trained for fine-grained (5-class) prediction to make binary decisions, which includes neutral examples in the training process. This deviates from standard practice where binary sentiment classification typically excludes neutral examples. The results should be re-evaluated using a proper binary classification protocol for fair comparison with previous work.

Additionally, the baseline comparison is incomplete. The authors should include results from Kalchbrenner et al.'s convolutional neural network for sentence modeling (ACL 2014), which represents a strong recent baseline for sentence-level sentiment analysis. The current baseline comparison omits this relevant work.

Regarding notation, the paper would benefit from greater clarity in defining parameters. For instance, lines 138-139 reference W^eta and W^xh without clear definitions, and it would be helpful to explicitly summarize all parameters ({WL, WR, U, b, c}) with their dimensionalities when first introduced.

The paper's claim about the relationship between depth in structure and depth in space is reasonable for the SST task, but the authors should validate this claim in a more general setting. It would be valuable to test whether deep RNNs provide advantages when only root-level labels exist (rather than per-node labels), which is the typical scenario in sentiment analysis. The authors should conduct experiments on a dataset with only global sentiment labels to determine whether the depth-based improvements generalize beyond the treebank setting.

The decision to use shared dropout units across nodes (line 251) lacks justification. The authors should clarify why this approach was chosen over alternative regularization methods, such as weight norm constraints or gradient clipping, which are common in deep learning.

Despite these concerns, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that depth across space can improve recursive neural networks, and the qualitative analyses showing how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality are particularly compelling. The use of ReLUs and dropout is well-justified by the empirical results, and the paper's overall quality is strong.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and well-executed extension of recursive neural networks with promising empirical results. However, the methodological issues surrounding word embedding fine-tuning, binary classification protocol, and baseline comparisons require resolution before the paper can be accepted. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the contribution and provide more convincing evidence for the value of depth in recursive networks.

Pro: Novel architecture extending recursive networks with depth in space; strong empirical results on SST; clear qualitative analyses of layer-specific behavior; well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Con: Inconsistent embedding strategy (no fine-tuning) potentially confounding results; binary classification protocol deviates from standard practice; baseline comparison incomplete (missing Kalchbrenner et al.); need for additional experiments on global-label datasets to validate generality.
