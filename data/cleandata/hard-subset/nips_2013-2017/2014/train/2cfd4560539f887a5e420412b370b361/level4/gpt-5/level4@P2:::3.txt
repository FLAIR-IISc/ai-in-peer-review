Summary
The paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers, thereby adding “depth in space” in addition to the usual “depth in structure” from tree recursion. Each layer has its own parameters, shared across all tree positions within that layer, and passes its hidden states upward to the next layer. The model distinguishes leaf and internal nodes in the first layer (untied parameters), enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and pretrained word embeddings, which are kept fixed. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all tree nodes. Empirically, deeper (narrower) models outperform shallow models with a matched parameter budget and achieve state-of-the-art results against RNTN, MV-RNN, and Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest neighbors) suggest that layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The architecture parallels deep recurrent networks that stack recurrent layers to separate “depth in time” from “depth in space.” It builds on recursive models used for parsing and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al.’s RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) and compares to document/sentence encoders such as Paragraph Vectors and dynamic CNNs.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension: stacking recursive layers to introduce representational hierarchy in tree-structured models.
- Strong empirical results on SST with a careful parameter-matched comparison to shallow baselines.
- Practical training choices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) that likely generalize to other structured tasks.
- Qualitative analyses that provide insight into what different layers encode.
- Generally clear exposition, with sufficient detail to reimplement the method.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Some modeling and experimental choices are insufficiently justified, and key ablations are missing (e.g., effect of fine-tuning embeddings).
- The binary sentiment evaluation deviates from the common SST protocol, making comparisons less direct.
- A few notational and clarity issues impede reproducibility (e.g., undefined symbols and missing dimensions).
- The case where only root-level supervision is available is not evaluated, limiting claims about the broader utility of depth across space.

Detailed comments and questions
- Clarity/notation (around line 138): W^η and W^xh are undefined. Please clarify whether these refer to W_L^η and W_R^η, and separate equations for leaf-to-hidden vs hidden-to-hidden. A concise parameter summary {W_L, W_R, U, b, c, V} with dimensions would aid reproducibility.
- Rectifiers and space separation (lines 148–154): The argument that separating leaf and internal spaces “enables” rectifiers reads speculative. If based on observations, please cite your own ablations; otherwise, consider tempering the claim or adding evidence.
- Baselines: Ensure inclusion of the dynamic CNN results (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) under the same train/dev/test split. If numbers are taken from different splits, report that explicitly.
- Embedding fine-tuning: Why keep word vectors fixed? Fine-tuning typically improves sentiment tasks by aligning word embeddings with label semantics. It is important to control for this: compare shallow vs deep RNNs both with fixed and fine-tuned embeddings to confirm that depth, rather than frozen embeddings, drives the gain.
- Shared dropout mask (line 251): Please justify using a shared dropout pattern across all nodes within a sentence and layer. Did you compare to independent node-wise dropout, variational-style dropout, or DropConnect?
- Stability (lines 255–256): Beyond L2 on activations and weights, consider mentioning alternative stabilizers commonly used in recurrent/recursive setups: gradient clipping, spectral/orthogonal initialization, norm constraints, or layer normalization.
- Binary classification: Training only the fine-grained model and mapping its outputs to binary deviates from the standard SST protocol (which excludes neutral). For comparability, please train and report a binary model following the standard split and label selection.
- Scope of supervision: Results are shown where labels are available at all nodes. What happens when supervision is available only at the root? Do deep RNNs still outperform shallow ones with matched parameters? An experiment on root-only labels (on SST or an additional dataset with global labels) would strengthen the generality claim.
- Additional datasets: To support broader significance, consider 1–2 more datasets with only sentence-level labels (e.g., other sentiment corpora or textual entailment), to test whether depth across space is beneficial beyond full-tree supervision.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually sound and timely extension of recursive models with a clear analogy to deep recurrent networks.
- Strong, state-of-the-art empirical results on a standard benchmark.
- Insightful qualitative analyses suggesting layer-wise specialization in compositionality.
- Well-written and reproducible overall.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing key controls (notably embedding fine-tuning) and protocol-aligned binary results.
- Limited evaluation beyond SST and full-tree supervision.
- Some unclear notation and under-justified design choices (shared dropout mask, stability method).

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-executed paper that advances recursive modeling by adding depth across space and demonstrates solid gains. Addressing the experimental gaps—proper binary evaluation, embedding fine-tuning, and at least one root-only dataset—would considerably strengthen the case for acceptance and the paper’s broader impact.
