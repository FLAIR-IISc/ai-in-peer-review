

This paper introduces "deep recursive networks" by extending the depth concept from recurrent networks to spatial hierarchies (e.g., tree structures), where each layer has shared parameters across its vertical extent. The authors employ dropout, ReLUs, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings, demonstrating strong results on the sentiment treebank. While the method constitutes a meaningful generalization of recurrent depth to recursive architectures and yields compelling performance gains—particularly from dropout and ReLUs—the experimental design contains critical gaps that require resolution before acceptance. The paper is well-written and sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, but the following issues must be addressed.

**Major Concerns Requiring Revision:**  
1. **Unjustified Experimental Choices:** The decision to fix word embeddings (rather than fine-tune them) is unexplained. Fine-tuning embeddings on sentiment tasks is standard practice to align word representations with sentiment semantics. The authors claim their phrase/sentence vectors are sentiment-discriminative while word vectors are not—a contradiction that undermines the model’s interpretability. Crucially, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might outperform their deep model; this must be tested as a baseline. Without this control, the claimed advantage of depth may stem from embedding rigidity rather than architectural innovation.  

2. **Binary Classification Protocol Violation:** The binary classification experiment (Section 5.2) incorrectly includes neutral-class examples during training, deviating from the standard protocol where neutral examples are excluded. This invalidates comparisons with prior work. The authors must re-run this experiment per protocol and report results separately for binary vs. ternary settings.  

3. **Missing Baselines and Context:** The omission of Kalchbrenner et al.’s convolutional sentence model (ACL 2014) is a significant oversight. This strong baseline is directly comparable to the authors’ recursive approach and must be included. Additionally, the paper should test the model on datasets with *only* global (root) labels (e.g., IMDB), not just tree-structured sentiment treebank data. The value of "depth across space" must be validated beyond node-level prediction tasks.  

**Minor Technical Clarifications:**  
- **Equation Ambiguity (Lines 138, 148–154):** The notation $W^{\eta}$ and $W^{xh}$ is undefined. Clarify whether these denote left/right weight matrices ($W_L, W_R$) or hidden-layer weights. Provide explicit equations for input and hidden-layer transformations, and specify all parameter dimensions (e.g., "W_L ∈ ℝ^{d×d}", "U ∈ ℝ^{d×2d}").  
- **Shared Dropout (Line 251):** Justify why dropout units are shared across layers. Is this to prevent overfitting to the tree structure? If empirical, cite supporting results.  
- **Stability (Lines 255–256):** The claim that "stability is not an issue" lacks evidence. Suggest comparing against weight-norm constraints or gradient clipping—common practices for recurrent networks—to strengthen the argument.  

**Recommendation:**  
The core idea is promising and well-motivated by the success of depth in recurrent networks. However, the current evidence is insufficient to support the claim that depth *across space* is the key driver of performance. The unaddressed issues—particularly the embedding fine-tuning control, binary classification protocol, and missing baselines—undermine the paper’s central contribution. Without these experiments, the paper risks overattributing gains to depth rather than other design choices.  

**Required Revisions:**  
1. Re-run binary classification *exclusively* on non-neutral examples.  
2. Add Kalchbrenner et al.’s CNN baseline and report results.  
3. Test the model on a root-label-only dataset (e.g., IMDB) to assess depth’s utility beyond tree-structured prediction.  
4. Include a fine-tuned embedding baseline (single-layer RNN) to isolate the depth effect.  
5. Clarify all technical notation (equations, parameters) with explicit dimensions.  

These experiments would not only address critical weaknesses but also significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution by rigorously validating the role of spatial depth. The current work shows promise but requires these additions to be accepted as a substantial contribution to the field. As it stands, the evidence for the proposed method’s superiority is incomplete.
