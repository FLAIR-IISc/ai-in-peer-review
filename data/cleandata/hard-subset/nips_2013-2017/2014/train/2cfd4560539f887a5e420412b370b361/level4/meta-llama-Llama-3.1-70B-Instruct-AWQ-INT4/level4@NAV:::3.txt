This paper introduces the concept of 'deep' recursive networks by incorporating depth across space, drawing inspiration from the success of a similar approach in recurrent networks. Each layer in the proposed architecture has its own set of parameters that are shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also explore the use of dropout and ReLUs, while utilizing pre-trained embeddings for word representations. The experimentation is conducted on the sentiment treebank dataset of Socher et al.

The proposed method is novel, albeit an extension of the approach employed with recurrent networks. The authors achieve impressive performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the performance gains obtained through the use of dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some of the model and experimental design choices are debatable and require further justification. Additional experimentation is necessary to fully endorse the acceptance of this paper.

The paper is well-written, providing sufficient detail for readers to replicate the results.

Detailed comments and questions:

- line 138: The variables W^eta and W^xh are not defined. Are these equivalent to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to have separate equations for inputs and hidden layers. A summary of the parameter space, including dimensionalities of each matrix, would be beneficial.

- lines 148-154: This section appears speculative. Is this based on empirical results? If so, it should be mentioned. The issue raised here is not entirely convincing.

- baseline: The authors should include the results of "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al) from ACL this year for a more comprehensive comparison.

- The decision not to fine-tune word vectors is puzzling. Fine-tuning word vectors on a sentiment task allows them to capture sentiment information. It seems counterintuitive to have phrase and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment while individual word vectors are not. This aspect needs to be controlled for, as it may impact the observed improvements with deep RNNs.

- line 251 (shared dropout units): The motivation behind this choice is unclear.

- lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, the authors could consider constraining the norms of the weights or truncating the gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- binary classification: This should be performed separately, as the current approach deviates from the experiment protocol by including neutral class examples during training.

- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as in the treebank experiments. However, it would be useful to investigate whether deep RNNs offer an advantage when only a single, root label exists. Additional experiments, including redoing the binary classification experiment, studying the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and incorporating 1-2 additional datasets with global labels, would significantly strengthen the paper and provide more robust evidence for the effectiveness of depth across space.
