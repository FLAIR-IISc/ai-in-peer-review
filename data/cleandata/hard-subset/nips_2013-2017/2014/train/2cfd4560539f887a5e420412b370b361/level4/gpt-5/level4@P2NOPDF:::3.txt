This paper proposes “deep” recursive networks by adding depth across tree structure, inspired by analogous gains in deep recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters shared across all positions (heights) within that layer. The model uses dropout and ReLUs, and fixes word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (Socher et al.).

The method is novel, though largely a natural extension of depth in recurrent nets. The reported results on the sentiment treebank are strong, and the gains from dropout and ReLUs are interesting on their own. However, several modeling and experimental choices are weakly justified, and additional experiments are needed before I can fully recommend acceptance.

The paper is generally well written, and it provides enough detail for independent reproduction.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Line 138: What are W^η and W^xh? These symbols are not defined. Do they stand for W^{ηL} and W^{ηR}? It would be clearer to present two separate equations—one for inputs and one for hidden states—and to summarize the full parameter set (e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}). Please also specify matrix dimensions when introducing each parameter.
- Lines 148–154: The claims here read as speculative. Are they supported by your own empirical observations? If so, please say so and provide evidence. As written, I am not convinced the stated issue actually arises.
- Baselines: Please include results from “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014).
- Word embeddings: Why did you not fine-tune the embeddings? Fine-tuning on sentiment typically allows word vectors to capture sentiment polarity. It seems odd for phrase/sentence vectors to be sentiment-discriminative while word vectors remain static. It is also possible that your deep RNN gains partially stem from not fine-tuning words; a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform as well or better. This needs to be controlled for with a direct comparison.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated sharing dropout masks? Please justify or compare against independent dropout across positions/layers.
- Lines 255–256 (stability): In addition to your approach, you could constrain weight norms or clip gradients, as is common with recurrent networks. Have you tried these alternatives?
- Binary classification: This experiment should follow the standard protocol and be trained without neutral examples when reporting binary results. Including neutral examples during training deviates from prior work and makes your results not directly comparable.
- Scope of “depth across space”: The analogy to deep recurrent nets is compelling when making predictions at each tree node, as in the treebank. It would be informative to test whether deep recursive models still help when only a single root label is available. Would you still expect gains over a single-layer recursive model in that setting? Please consider:
  - Re-running the binary classification experiment using the accepted protocol.
  - Studying the effect of fine-tuning embeddings versus keeping them fixed.
  - Adding 1–2 datasets with only global (root) labels to assess whether depth across space remains beneficial.

Overall, the approach is promising and the results are strong, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by clearer definitions, better-justified design choices, inclusion of key baselines, and the additional control experiments outlined above.
