This paper introduces a novel deep recursive network architecture, extending the successful concept of depth found in recurrent neural networks to a spatial dimension. The model employs shared parameters within each layer across different tree heights, and incorporates dropout and ReLU activations with fixed, pre-trained word embeddings. The method is evaluated on the sentiment treebank dataset.

The approach is a logical generalization of existing recurrent network techniques and demonstrates strong performance on the chosen dataset. The observed benefits of dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, several modeling and experimental decisions lack sufficient justification, and further investigation is needed before I can fully support acceptance.

The paper is generally well-written and provides adequate detail for reproducibility.

Detailed comments / questions:

- Line 138: The notation W^eta and W^xh is undefined. Clarification is needed, potentially linking these to W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R. Presenting separate equations for input and hidden layer calculations, along with a clear summary of the parameter space (e.g., {WL, WR, U, b, c}) including the dimensionality of each matrix, would improve clarity.
- Lines 148-154: The claims made in this section appear speculative. If based on empirical findings, these should be explicitly stated. The presented issue is not entirely convincing as stated.
- Baseline: Inclusion of results from Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL, 2014) – “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” – is recommended for a more comprehensive comparison.
- Word Embeddings: The decision to not fine-tune the word embeddings is unusual, given that sentiment-specific adaptation can improve performance. The discrepancy between discriminative phrase/sentence vectors and non-sentiment-aware word vectors is concerning. It is possible that the observed improvements are a consequence of *not* fine-tuning, and a comparison with a single-layer RNN employing fine-tuned embeddings is necessary to control for this effect.
- Line 251: The motivation behind shared dropout units should be explained.
- Lines 255-256: Consider alternative stabilization techniques such as weight norm constraints or gradient clipping, commonly used in recurrent networks.
- Binary Classification: The binary classification experiment should be conducted separately, excluding neutral class examples during training to align with standard evaluation protocols. The current results are therefore not directly comparable to existing work.
- Generalization: While the analogy to deep recurrent networks is valid for node-wise predictions in tree structures, it would be beneficial to assess the performance of the deep recursive network when predicting only a single, root-level label. Investigating whether depth still provides an advantage in this scenario would strengthen the argument for the approach. 

I recommend the authors address the binary classification experiment, investigate the impact of fine-tuning word embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets with global labels. These additions would significantly strengthen the paper and provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across space.
