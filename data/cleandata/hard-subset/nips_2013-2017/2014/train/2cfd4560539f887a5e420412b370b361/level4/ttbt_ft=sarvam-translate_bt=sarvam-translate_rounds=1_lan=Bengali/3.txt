This paper proposes the use of a 'deep' recursive network, adding depth across space, inspired by the success of doing the same thing with recursive networks. Each layer has its own parameters, which are divided into different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLU, where word representations are specified with pre-trained embeddings. Experiments have been conducted on Socher and others' sentiment tribanks.

The proposed method is new, although essentially a generalization of the same method used with recursive networks. The authors have achieved very strong performance on sentiment tribanks datasets. The performance improvements achieved with dropout and ReLU are interesting in themselves. However, some choices in the model and experimental design are questionable (or not well-justified), and I believe more experimentation is needed before fully recommending adoption.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and provides enough detail for the reader to reproduce the results themselves.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Line 138: What are W^eta and W^xh? These are not defined. Are they only to mean W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer if there were two equations: one for the input and one for the hidden layer. It would also be helpful to summarize the parameter space, i.e., {WL, WR, U, b, c}. It would also be helpful to include them when you define the dimensions of your matrices.
- Lines 148-154: This seems very speculative. Is it based on your own empirical results? If so, you should mention it. I am not sure if this is actually a problem.
- Baseline: The authors should include the results of this year's "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (Kalchbrenner et al.) from ACL.
- Why didn't you fine-tune the word vectors? If you fine-tune them in a sentiment task, the word embeddings may reflect their sentiment. It is strange to have some phrase and sentence vectors that can distinguish sentiment, but where individual word vectors do not. (I understand that this is fine with a deep RNN because you can only forward-pass word vectors across layers). Is it possible that the improvements you are getting with a deep RNN are due to not fine-tuning the words? Perhaps, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might do better. I think this needs to be controlled.
- Line 251 (shared dropout unit): What was the purpose behind this choice?
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, you could limit the norm of the weights or prune the gradients, as is sometimes done with recursive networks.
- Binary classification: This should be done separately. The problem is that during training, you are also including examples of neutral classes that deviate from the test protocol. As a result, these results are not directly comparable to the existing method.
- Similarity with deep recursive networks is reasonable when you are predicting at each node of the tree, as seen in the tribank experiment. It was necessary to see if there is any advantage to using a deep RNN if only a root label exists. Would you still expect to see improvements over a single-layer RNN? The use of deep RNN seems like a promising method, but I would like to see some additional experiments. I would recommend that the authors redo the binary classification test according to the correct protocol, study the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and finally include 1-2 additional datasets that only use a global label. These additional experiments will provide stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space as well as a stronger research paper.
