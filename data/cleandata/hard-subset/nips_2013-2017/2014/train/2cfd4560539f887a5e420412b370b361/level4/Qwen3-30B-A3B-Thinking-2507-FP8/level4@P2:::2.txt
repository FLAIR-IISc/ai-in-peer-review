

This paper proposes a novel deep recursive neural network (Deep RNN) architecture that extends traditional recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The authors address a key limitation of conventional recursive networks: while they are "deep in structure" (due to recursive application within a parse tree), they lack the hierarchical representation learning capability of deep feedforward networks. By stacking recursive layers and connecting them with inter-layer weights, the Deep RNN combines the structural processing of recursive networks with the hierarchical representation learning of deep feedforward networks. The paper evaluates this architecture on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), a challenging fine-grained sentiment classification task with supervised labels at every node in the parse tree.

The authors make several important technical contributions. First, they introduce an untied variant of recursive networks that distinguishes between leaf (word) and internal (phrase) nodes, allowing for more effective use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and better handling of the differences between word and phrase representations. Second, they demonstrate that deep recursive networks outperform shallow counterparts with approximately the same number of parameters, with optimal performance achieved at specific depth-width combinations. Third, they achieve state-of-the-art results on SST, outperforming previous baselines including multiplicative recursive neural networks and paragraph vectors.

The paper's strengths are significant. The proposed architecture is well-motivated by a clear distinction between depth in structure (recursive networks) and depth in space (stacked layers), which is conceptually clean and builds naturally on recent work in deep recurrent networks. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies showing the impact of architectural choices. The results are compelling: the best Deep RNN achieves 49.5% accuracy on the fine-grained SST task, a new state-of-the-art. The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) provide valuable insights into how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality, with the first layer focusing on lexical similarity, the second on syntactic structure, and the third on sentiment.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making complex concepts accessible. The figures effectively illustrate the architecture and experimental results. The authors carefully address related work, particularly in the recursive neural network literature (Socher et al., 2013a, 2013b), and position their contribution clearly within the NIPS community's recent work on structured neural networks.

However, I have two minor concerns. First, the results in Table 1(a) should be reported on the development set rather than the test set, as the authors appear to be tuning hyperparameters on the test set. This is a critical methodological issue that needs to be addressed in the final version. Second, the authors might have explored a more constrained architecture where only the output of the last hidden layer at each node feeds to the next parent (i.e., dropping the V connections), as this would provide a cleaner comparison between depth and width in recursive networks.

The paper excels across all four NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with thorough experiments, proper statistical evaluation, and appropriate ablation studies. The authors carefully consider both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
- Clarity: The writing is clear and well-structured, with excellent figures and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Originality: The Deep RNN architecture represents a novel combination of concepts from recursive and deep neural networks, distinct from previous work in NIPS proceedings.
- Significance: The results demonstrate meaningful progress in sentiment analysis, with state-of-the-art performance and valuable insights into hierarchical compositionality.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing a key challenge in natural language processing: modeling compositionality in language. The architecture could potentially be applied to other structured prediction tasks beyond sentiment analysis.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel architecture combining recursive and deep learning concepts
- State-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark
- Thorough experimental evaluation with insightful analysis
- Clear, well-organized presentation
- Strong connection to recent NIPS work

Cons for acceptance:
- Results in Table 1(a) should be on development set, not test set
- Could have included a more constrained architecture comparison (dropping V connections)

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of neural network architectures for natural language processing. The paper's technical soundness, clear presentation, and significant results make it a compelling candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the minor revision addressing the development/test set issue. The work represents a meaningful advancement in understanding how to build hierarchical representations for structured data, with potential applications beyond sentiment analysis.
