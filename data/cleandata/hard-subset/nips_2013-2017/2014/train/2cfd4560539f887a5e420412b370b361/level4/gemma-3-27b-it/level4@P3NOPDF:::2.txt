This paper presents a novel and exciting deep recursive neural network architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging sentiment classification dataset. The results are impressive, and the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear in its exposition. The authors propose several clever and well-motivated modifications to existing recursive neural network (RNN) models, which are convincingly demonstrated to improve performance. These contributions include:

*   **Untying Leaf and Nonterminal Nodes:** Allowing separate parameterizations for leaf and nonterminal nodes offers increased flexibility and representational power.
*   **Rectified Linear Units in a Recursive Setting:** Successfully adapting rectified linear units (ReLUs) to the recursive framework is a significant improvement, leveraging their known benefits for training deep networks.
*   **Leveraging Pre-trained Word Vectors with Reduced Dimensionality:** The authors wisely utilize large, unsupervised word vectors while employing smaller hidden representations for nonterminal nodes, likely preventing overfitting and improving generalization.
*   **Deep Recursive Architecture with Inter-Layer Connections:**  Perhaps the most significant contribution is the introduction of a truly *deep* recursive architecture. This goes beyond simply replacing a single RNN layer with a deeper network; it establishes connections *between* layers and tree nodes, facilitating richer feature interactions. While the proposed architecture is effective, it would be insightful to see a comparison where the input to each parent node is solely the output of the last hidden layer of its children – effectively removing the direct connection through the matrix V. This would help isolate the benefit of the full inter-layer connectivity.

However, there is a critical concern regarding experimental methodology. The results presented in Table 1(a) appear to be reported on the final test set. This is problematic, as the model is effectively being tuned *on* the test set, leading to an overly optimistic performance estimate. These results should instead be reported on a dedicated development set to ensure a fair and unbiased evaluation.

Finally, I noted a few minor typographical errors: 

*   “comprise a class of architecture” should likely be “constitute a class of architectures” or “form a class of architectures.”
*   The word "classificationd" in the first sentence appears to be a typo and should be "classification".

Despite these minor issues, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of recursive neural networks. The proposed architecture is innovative and demonstrably effective. Addressing the test set reporting issue is crucial, but I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
