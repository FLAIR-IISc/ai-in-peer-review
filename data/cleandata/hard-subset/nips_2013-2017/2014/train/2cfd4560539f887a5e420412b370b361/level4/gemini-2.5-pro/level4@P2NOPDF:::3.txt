This paper introduces a 'deep' recursive neural network architecture. The central idea, motivated by the success of deep recurrent networks, is to add depth 'spatially' by stacking multiple recursive layers. Each layer possesses its own set of parameters, which are shared across all nodes at that depth. The authors combine this architecture with modern techniques such as ReLU activations and dropout, and utilize pre-trained word embeddings without fine-tuning. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The paper is well-written and presents a novel extension of recursive networks, achieving impressive results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank. The performance gains attributed to dropout and ReLU activations are particularly noteworthy. However, the paper's primary weakness lies in several questionable or poorly justified modeling and experimental design choices. While the core idea is promising, substantial additional experimentation is required to validate the proposed architecture's effectiveness and generalize its contributions. Without these experiments, a strong recommendation for acceptance is difficult.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   **Clarity of Notation (line 138):** The notation `W^eta` and `W^xh` is used without prior definition. It is unclear if these are meant to be general placeholders for the left and right weight matrices (`W^{eta}L`, `W^{eta}R`). For improved clarity, I suggest providing separate equations for the input-to-hidden and hidden-to-hidden transformations. Furthermore, explicitly defining the full parameter space (e.g., `{WL, WR, U, b, c}`) and including the dimensionalities of all matrices and vectors upon their introduction would significantly aid reproducibility.

*   **Unsupported Claims (lines 148-154):** The discussion in this section reads as speculative. If these claims are based on empirical observations from your experiments, this should be stated explicitly. Otherwise, the argument that this is a significant issue for standard recursive models is unconvincing without supporting evidence.

*   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing from the comparison. The authors should include and discuss the results from Kalchbrenner et al.'s "A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences" (ACL 2014), which represents a key contemporary approach to this task.

*   **Lack of Word Vector Fine-tuning:** The decision not to fine-tune the word embeddings is a major concern. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows the embeddings themselves to capture sentiment polarity, which is a standard and often beneficial practice. It seems counter-intuitive to have sentiment-aware phrase representations built from sentiment-agnostic word vectors. A critical unanswered question is whether the performance gains from the deep architecture are simply compensating for the lack of fine-tuning. It is plausible that a shallow, single-layer recursive network with fine-tuned embeddings could match or even outperform the proposed deep model. This is a crucial ablation study that must be included to isolate the true contribution of architectural depth.

*   **Motivation for Shared Dropout (line 251):** The paper mentions using shared dropout units. Could the authors please elaborate on the motivation behind this specific design choice?

*   **Alternative Stabilization Methods (lines 255-256):** Regarding training stability, the authors could also explore alternative techniques commonly used in recurrent networks, such as constraining the L2 norm of the weight matrices or applying gradient clipping. These might offer other ways to manage the training dynamics.

*   **Flawed Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment does not follow the standard protocol for this task. By including neutral examples during training, the model is trained on a different data distribution than the models it is being compared against. This deviation makes the reported results incomparable to prior work. The experiment should be re-run following the established protocol (i.e., removing neutral examples entirely before training and testing).

*   **Generalization to Sentence-Level Tasks and Required Revisions:** The analogy to deep recurrent networks is most compelling on the Sentiment Treebank, where predictions are made at every node. However, a key test of the architecture's general utility would be its performance on tasks with only a single label at the root of the tree (i.e., standard sentence classification). It is not obvious that the benefits of depth would persist in this setting.

To strengthen the paper and provide more convincing evidence for the value of spatial depth, I strongly recommend the following revisions:
1.  Re-run the binary classification experiment using the correct protocol.
2.  Conduct an ablation study comparing the proposed model with a single-layer baseline where word embeddings are fine-tuned.
3.  Evaluate the model on 1-2 additional sentence-level classification datasets where only the root label is used for supervision.

These experiments are essential for properly contextualizing the paper's contributions and would transform it into a much stronger submission.
