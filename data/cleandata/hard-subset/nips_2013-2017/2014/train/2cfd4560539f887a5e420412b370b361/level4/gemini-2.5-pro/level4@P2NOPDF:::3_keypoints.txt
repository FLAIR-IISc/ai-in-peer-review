- Introduces a novel "deep" recursive neural network that stacks multiple recursive layers with depth-specific shared parameters.  
- Uses ReLU, dropout, and pre-trained (non-fine-tuned) word embeddings; evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.  
- Paper is well-written and achieves strong results; performance gains from dropout and ReLU are noteworthy.  
- Main weakness: several modeling and experimental choices are questionable or poorly justified, requiring more experiments.  
- Notation clarity issue: undefined symbols (e.g., W^eta, W^xh) and missing explicit parameter/dimensionality definitions hurt reproducibility.  
- Some claims are speculative or unsupported by empirical evidence and should be labeled or backed by results.  
- Missing important baseline: results from Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL 2014) should be included for comparison.  
- Major concern: word embeddings are not fine-tuned; need ablation to test whether depth benefits compensate for fixed embeddings.  
- Motivation for using shared dropout units is not explained and should be elaborated.  
- Suggest exploring alternative stabilization methods (e.g., weight norm constraints, gradient clipping) used in recurrent nets.  
- Binary classification protocol is flawed: neutral examples were included, making results incomparable to prior work—must follow standard protocol.  
- Unclear if benefits generalize to sentence-level tasks with only root supervision; evaluate on additional sentence-level datasets.  
- Recommended revisions: re-run binary experiment correctly, add ablation with fine-tuned embeddings vs single-layer baseline, and test on 1–2 sentence-level datasets.
