- Proposes a deep recursive neural network (stacking recursive layers) to improve modeling of compositionality in language.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) and reports state-of-the-art performance.  
- Methodology is technically sound, building on prior recursive RNN work and using techniques like dropout and pre-trained embeddings.  
- Experimental setup is comprehensive, with thorough baselines and ablation studies exploring depth and width.  
- Results consistently show improvements with increasing depth, validating the core hypothesis.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) indicate different layers capture distinct aspects of sentiment and similarity.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, with clear notation and illustrative figures that aid reproducibility.  
- The idea is novel and a reasonable extension of existing work, though relatively simple in concept.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for specific architectural choices (e.g., range of layer depths explored).  
- Weakness: lacks deeper discussion of why deep RNNs are particularly effective for compositionality and connections to deep feedforward nets.  
- Significance: meaningful contribution to NLP and compositional semantics; approach likely applicable to other structured tasks.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution with compelling results; minor clarifications needed; recommendation: Accept.
