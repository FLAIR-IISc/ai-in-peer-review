This paper proposes “deep” recursive networks by adding depth across space, inspired by similar successes with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters, shared across different tree heights within that layer. The authors also use dropout and ReLUs, while fixing word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank of Socher et al.

The method is novel, though essentially a generalization of ideas from deep recurrent nets. The reported performance on the sentiment treebank is very strong, and the gains from dropout and ReLUs are interesting in their own right. That said, several modeling and experimental choices are questionable or insufficiently justified. I believe additional experiments are necessary before I can fully recommend acceptance.

The paper is generally clear, and sufficient detail is provided for replication.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Line 138: What are W^η and W^xh? These have not been defined. Do you mean W^{η}_L and W^{η}_R? It would be clearer to present two equations: one for the inputs and another for the hidden layers. It would also help to summarize the parameter space, e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}, and include the dimensionalities of each matrix when first introduced.

- Lines 148–154: This discussion seems speculative. Is it based on your own empirical observations? If so, please say so. I am not convinced this is actually an issue.

- Baseline: Please include the results of “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014).

- Word vectors: Why didn’t you fine-tune the word embeddings? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows word embeddings to reflect sentiment. It seems strange to have phrase and sentence vectors that are sentiment-discriminative while the individual word vectors are not (I realize this is less problematic with a deep RNN since you can transform fixed embeddings across layers). Is it possible that the improvements you attribute to deep RNNs arise because you are not fine-tuning the words? Conceivably, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings could perform better. This needs to be controlled for.

- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this choice?

- Lines 255–256 (stability): Alternatively, consider constraining weight norms or truncating gradients, as is sometimes done with recurrent nets.

- Binary classification: This should be done separately. During training you include neutral examples, which deviates from the standard protocol. Consequently, these results are not directly comparable to existing approaches.

- Scope of depth: The analogy with deep recurrent nets is reasonable when making predictions at each node in the tree, as in the treebank experiments. It would be useful to test whether there is an advantage to deep RNNs when only a single root label exists. Would you still expect improvements over a single-layer RNN? I recommend redoing the binary classification experiment following the proper protocol, studying the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and including 1–2 additional datasets that have only a global label. These additional experiments would substantially strengthen the paper and provide stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space.
