This paper proposes a 'deep' recursive neural network, extending the concept of depth from recurrent networks to recursive structures. The model introduces depth "spatially," with each layer having its own set of parameters that are shared across all nodes within that layer. The authors also incorporate dropout and ReLU activations, while keeping the input word representations fixed to pre-trained embeddings. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method is novel, though it can be seen as a natural generalization of adding depth to recurrent networks. The authors achieve very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the analysis of performance gains from dropout and ReLUs is an interesting contribution in its own right. That being said, some of the model and experimental design choices are questionable or not sufficiently justified. I believe additional experimentation is necessary to fully substantiate the paper's claims before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance. The paper is, for the most part, clearly written, providing sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   **Notation (line 138):** The terms W^eta and W^xh are used without prior definition. For clarity, please specify if these are simply placeholders for W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R. It would be more explicit to provide two separate equations: one for the input-to-hidden transformation and another for the hidden-to-hidden transformations in deeper layers. It would also be helpful to summarize the full parameter space (e.g., {WL, WR, U, b, c}) and include the dimensionality of each matrix when it is first defined.

*   **Speculative Argument (lines 148-154):** The argument presented in these lines appears highly speculative. Is this claim based on your own empirical results? If so, this should be mentioned. As written, the argument that this is a significant issue is not convincing.

*   **Missing Baseline:** A relevant and recent baseline is missing from the comparison: "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" by Kalchbrenner et al. (ACL 2014). The authors should include the results from this work.

*   **Fine-Tuning of Word Vectors:** The decision to not fine-tune the word vectors is not well-justified. Fine-tuning embeddings on a sentiment task typically allows them to capture sentiment-specific semantics. It seems counter-intuitive for the model to learn phrase and sentence vectors that are discriminative of sentiment while the underlying word vectors are not. Is it possible that the performance improvements attributed to the deep recursive model are an artifact of *not* fine-tuning the embeddings? A single-layer recursive network with fine-tuned embeddings might conceivably perform better. This should be investigated as a control experiment to properly isolate the benefits of depth.

*   **Shared Dropout Units (line 251):** What was the motivation for sharing dropout units across nodes? Please elaborate on this design choice.

*   **Model Stability (lines 255-256):** Regarding model stability, have the authors considered alternative techniques common in training recurrent networks, such as constraining the norms of the weight matrices or truncating gradients?

*   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment deviates from the standard evaluation protocol for this task. By including neutral class examples during training, the reported results are not directly comparable to those of existing approaches. This experiment should be redone following the established protocol.

*   **Generalizability and Required Experiments:** The analogy with deep recurrent networks is reasonable for tasks requiring predictions at each node, as is the case in the treebank experiments. However, it would be valuable to see if the advantage of using deep recursive networks persists when only a single, root-level label is predicted. Would the deep model still be expected to outperform a single-layer one in that setting?

The use of deep recursive networks is a promising approach, but the paper would be much stronger with additional experiments. I would recommend that the authors:
1.  Redo the binary classification experiment using the proper protocol for comparability.
2.  Study the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings.
3.  Include 1-2 additional datasets that use only a single, global label.

These experiments would provide much stronger evidence for the usefulness of depth across space and result in a significantly more impactful paper.
