This paper introduces the Deep Recursive Neural Network (Deep RNN), a novel architecture constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers. This approach is inspired by recent advancements in deep recurrent neural networks, drawing a parallel between depth in structure (from recursion over a parse tree) and depth in space (from the stacked layers). The central hypothesis is that this stacking enables a hierarchy of representations, allowing each successive layer to learn more abstract compositional semantics. The authors evaluate this model on the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, demonstrating its effectiveness against shallow counterparts and previous state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a well-executed paper that presents a logical and effective extension to an important class of models for natural language processing. The arguments are supported by strong empirical results and insightful analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed architecture is clearly formulated, and the experimental setup is thorough. The authors provide a solid evaluation that includes quantitative comparisons across models of varying depth and width, as well as valuable qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor comparisons) that lend credence to the claim that deeper layers learn more abstract representations.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for introducing depth in space to recursive models is clearly articulated by drawing an analogy to the successful deep recurrent architectures. The model description and figures are easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The idea of stacking recursive layers is a novel and intuitive contribution. While depth is a central concept in neural networks, this work is the first to systematically explore its application to recursive models for linguistic structures and to demonstrate its benefits over simply increasing the width of a shallow model.
*   **Significance:** The paper presents new state-of-the-art results on a challenging and widely-used benchmark. The finding that depth is more beneficial than width for a fixed parameter budget is an important result that is likely to be impactful for researchers working on compositional models and structured data. The analyses provide useful insights into what these deeper hierarchical models are learning.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The paper could be strengthened with a more detailed discussion regarding the specific architectural choices. For example, the rationale for connecting the output layer only to the final hidden layer is to encourage high-level representations. An empirical comparison against an architecture where all layers connect to the output would provide stronger evidence for this design decision.
*   The authors observe that performance degrades beyond a certain depth. While they hypothesize this is due to the corresponding decrease in layer width, it would be beneficial to discuss other potential factors. Could optimization difficulties or vanishing gradients play a role, despite the dense supervision provided by the treebank? A deeper exploration of this trade-off would add value for future work in this direction.

In summary, this paper presents a clear and significant contribution. The proposed deep recursive architecture is well-motivated, and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated through rigorous experiments and analysis. The strengths of the paper are substantial, and the suggestions for improvement are minor points that could be addressed in future work.
