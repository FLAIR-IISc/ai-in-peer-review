Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers to introduce “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure” found in tree-structured models. The model distinguishes leaves from internal nodes (untied parameters), uses rectified linear units, leverages large pretrained word vectors without fine-tuning, and regularizes with dropout. Each layer processes the tree structure and also passes node representations upward across layers via learned inter-layer connections. The approach is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained and binary sentiment classification. Deep RNNs outperform comparable shallow recursive baselines with a matched parameter budget and achieve state-of-the-art results relative to prior recursive models (e.g., MV-RNN, RNTN) and paragraph vectors. Exploratory analyses (input perturbations; nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest successive layers capture different aspects of compositionality and sentiment intensity.

Relation to prior work
The model builds on recursive neural networks for parsing and sentiment (Socher et al.) and on the notion of adding “depth in space” to recurrent models (e.g., stacked RNNs). The novelty lies in bringing layered depth to tree-structured composition with explicit across-layer connections at each node, while employing ReLUs and dropout in this setting, and in untying parameters for leaves versus internals to better exploit pretrained word vectors.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation with precise equations and helpful figures.
- Conceptually clean extension of recursive nets to stacked depth; the across-layer connection V is well-motivated.
- Solid empirical gains over strong baselines on SST, while controlling total parameter count to isolate depth effects.
- Sensible training recipe (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad, identity-based initialization) that stabilizes learning in deep trees.
- Qualitative analyses provide insight: lower layers reflect lexical/syntactic composition; higher layers focus on sentiment polarity/intensity.

Weaknesses and questions
- Potential test-set tuning: Table 1(a) appears to report model-selection results (varying depth/width, dropout) on the test set. Model selection should be done on the development set; please clarify and, if applicable, re-report Table 1(a) using dev results and reserve the test set for the final chosen configuration.
- Ablations on architecture coupling: A direct comparison to (i) stacking per-node MLP depth without across-layer V connections, and (ii) removing V to test whether inter-layer shortcuts are essential, would sharpen the claim that depth-in-space with V drives the gains rather than simply more nonlinearities.
- Output connections: You choose to predict only from the final layer. An ablation that predicts from all layers (e.g., auxiliary losses) or compares with predicting from an intermediate layer would test the necessity of the deepest layer and might further improve performance.
- Effect of untying and ReLUs: Please add ablations isolating (a) untying leaves vs. internals, (b) ReLUs vs. tanh in the same architecture, and (c) dropout vs. L2 only, to quantify each design choice.
- Fixed word vectors: Since embeddings are not fine-tuned, a brief experiment assessing the impact (and possible overfitting) of fine-tuning would be informative, given SST’s rich supervision at all nodes.
- Notation: “RNN” is used to mean recursive neural network; consider “RecNN” to avoid confusion with recurrent nets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Generally strong. Training details are careful (dropout sharing across nodes, small L2 on activations to prevent explosions, identity+noise initialization). The main gap is the lack of ablations to attribute gains and the possible test-set model selection.
- Empirical support: Convincing, with matched-parameter comparisons and state-of-the-art results. Please ensure dev/test hygiene and include the proposed ablations.
- Analysis: The qualitative studies are thoughtful and consistent with the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is clear and well-structured. Figures aid understanding. Minor notation cleanup (recursive vs. recurrent naming) and a clearer statement of the data split usage for Table 1(a) would help.

Originality
- The idea of stacking recursive layers with explicit inter-layer node connections is a meaningful and novel extension of prior tree models. Use of ReLUs and dropout in trees is incremental but effective. The qualitative analyses add insight.

Significance
- Results advance the state of the art on SST and demonstrate that added depth in tree composition is beneficial under a fixed parameter budget. The approach is likely to influence future tree-structured modeling beyond sentiment.

Pros
- Well-motivated architecture that cleanly extends recursive composition with depth.
- Strong empirical results and insightful qualitative analyses.
- Careful consideration of training stability in deep tree structures.

Cons
- Possible test-set leakage in Table 1(a); needs clarification/correction.
- Missing ablations to disentangle the contributions of V connections, untying, ReLUs, and dropout.
- No exploration of embedding fine-tuning, which could further strengthen the results or yield useful insights.

Minor comments and typos
- “comprise a class of architecture” → “comprise a class of architectures” or “constitute a class of architectures.”
- “classificationd dataset” → “classification dataset.”
- Please ensure that all hyperparameter choices (e.g., dropout rates) are selected on the development set, not the test set.

Overall
A strong and clearly presented contribution. With proper dev/test separation and the suggested ablations, this work would be a solid addition to NIPS, both for its empirical gains and for clarifying how depth benefits compositional modeling in trees.
