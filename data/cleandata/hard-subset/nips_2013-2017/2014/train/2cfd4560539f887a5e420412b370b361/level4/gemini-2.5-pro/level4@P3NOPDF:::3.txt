### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a "deep" Recursive Neural Network (RNN) architecture, extending the standard RNN model by adding multiple layers. The motivation for this spatial depth is drawn from the success of temporally deep recurrent networks. In the proposed model, each layer has its own set of parameters that are shared across all nodes at that layer. The authors also incorporate modern techniques such as dropout and Rectified Linear Units (ReLUs) and initialize the model with pre-trained word embeddings, which are held fixed during training. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, where it achieves state-of-the-art results.

### Overall Assessment

The proposed deep RNN is a novel and logical extension of the standard recursive model, and the authors demonstrate very strong performance on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank. The empirical gains from incorporating dropout and ReLUs are also noteworthy and of independent interest. The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and provides sufficient detail for the results to be reproduced.

However, several aspects of the model and experimental design are not fully justified, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by additional experiments to validate the central claims. These concerns, detailed below, currently prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

**Model Clarity and Notation:**

*   **Equation Clarity (line 138):** The notation in the equations is unclear. The terms `W^eta` and `W^xh` are used without prior definition. It is unclear if these are meant to be general placeholders for the left and right weight matrices (e.g., `W^{eta}L` and `W^{eta}R`). A clearer presentation would involve separate equations for the input layer and subsequent hidden layers. Furthermore, summarizing the full parameter space (e.g., `{WL, WR, U, b, c}`) and specifying the dimensionality of all matrices and vectors upon their introduction would greatly improve the clarity of the model description.

*   **Speculative Claims (lines 148-154):** The discussion regarding the vanishing gradient problem in this section appears to be speculative. Is this reasoning based on empirical observation from your experiments? If so, this should be stated explicitly. Without empirical evidence, the claim that this is a significant issue for single-layer RNNs is not fully convincing.

**Experimental Design and Baselines:**

*   **Missing Baseline:** The experimental comparison is missing a key and highly relevant baseline: the convolutional neural network model for sentences by Kalchbrenner et al. ("A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences," ACL 2014). Given its strong performance and conceptual relevance, this comparison should be included.

*   **Word Vector Fine-tuning:** A significant experimental choice that requires justification is the decision not to fine-tune the pre-trained word embeddings. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task often allows word vectors to capture sentiment-specific semantics. It is plausible that the performance gains attributed to the deep architecture are, in part, a compensation for the representational limitations of fixed embeddings. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might perform substantially better than the single-layer baseline presented. To isolate the contribution of depth, the experiments should be repeated with fine-tuning enabled for both the single-layer and deep models.

*   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment does not appear to follow the standard protocol for this task. By including neutral examples during training, the model is trained on a different data distribution, which makes the results incomparable to prior work. This experiment should be re-run using only the positive and negative classes, following the established protocol.

**Model Design Choices and Suggestions:**

*   **Shared Dropout (line 251):** Could the authors provide the motivation for using shared dropout units across children nodes? What is the intuition behind this design choice?

*   **Model Stability (lines 255-256):** Regarding model stability, the authors might also consider alternative or complementary techniques commonly used in recurrent networks, such as constraining the norms of the weight matrices or applying gradient clipping. Did the authors experiment with these methods?

**Generalization and Required Experiments:**

The paper's central motivation rests on an analogy to deep recurrent networks. This analogy holds well for the treebank experiments where predictions are made at every node. However, a crucial test of the general utility of spatial depth would be on tasks where only a single label at the root of the tree is predicted. It is not immediately obvious that a deep architecture would still provide a significant advantage over a single-layer model in such a setting.

To create a more compelling case for the general applicability of deep recursive networks, I would strongly recommend the following additional experiments:

1.  Re-run the binary classification experiment according to the correct protocol to ensure fair comparison.
2.  Conduct an ablation study on the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings for both the baseline and the proposed deep model.
3.  Evaluate the model on 1-2 additional sentence classification datasets that involve only a single root-level label.

These experiments would provide much stronger evidence for the paper's claims regarding the usefulness of depth and would significantly strengthen the overall contribution.
