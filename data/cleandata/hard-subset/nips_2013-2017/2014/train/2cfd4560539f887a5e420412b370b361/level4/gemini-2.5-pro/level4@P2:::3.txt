### Summary

This paper introduces the Deep Recursive Neural Network (Deep RNN), a novel architecture for modeling compositional structure in language. The primary contribution is extending standard Recursive Neural Networks by stacking multiple recursive layers, creating a model that is "deep in space" in addition to being "deep in structure." This idea is motivated by similar successful extensions of recurrent networks to deep recurrent networks. The model unties the parameters for leaf (word) and internal (phrase) nodes in the first layer, utilizes rectifier linear units (ReLUs) and dropout for regularization, and leverages fixed, pre-trained word embeddings.

The authors evaluate the Deep RNN on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, demonstrating that deeper models outperform shallower counterparts with a similar number of parameters. Their best model achieves state-of-the-art results on both the fine-grained and binary sentiment classification tasks, outperforming previous methods like the Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN) and Paragraph Vectors. The paper concludes with qualitative analyses suggesting that different layers in the deep architecture learn to capture different aspects of compositionality, from lexical and syntactic features in lower layers to more abstract semantic (sentiment) features in the final layer.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and provides sufficient detail for the main ideas to be understood and likely reproduced. The proposed model is technically sound, and the connection to deep recurrent networks provides a strong motivation for the architecture. The quantitative and qualitative results are presented clearly.

However, several aspects of the experimental design and justification could be strengthened to make the claims more robust.

**Strengths:**
*   The model achieves very strong empirical results, setting a new state-of-the-art on a challenging and widely used benchmark.
*   The qualitative analyses in sections 3.2 (Input Perturbation and Nearest Neighbor Phrases) are insightful and provide compelling evidence that the stacked layers learn a hierarchy of representations.
*   The gains from using modern deep learning techniques (pre-trained vectors, ReLUs, dropout) on a recursive architecture are interesting in their own right.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
1.  **Justification for Untying Nodes:** The reasoning in lines 148-154 for separating leaf and internal node representations to better accommodate ReLU activations seems speculative. Is this hypothesis supported by preliminary experiments? A brief mention of empirical evidence would strengthen this design choice.
2.  **Clarity of Notation:** The notation in Equation 3 could be clarified. The terms `W_l(η)` and `W_r(η)` are not explicitly defined. It would be clearer to provide separate equations for leaf-level composition (from word space to phrase space) and internal node composition (from phrase space to phrase space). Additionally, summarizing the model's parameters and their dimensionalities would improve readability.
3.  **Experimental Protocol for Binary Classification:** The binary classification experiment deviates from the standard protocol. By training on all 5 classes and then decoding a binary decision, the model is exposed to neutral examples during training, which are normally discarded. This makes the results not directly comparable to prior work. To ensure a fair comparison, a separate model should be trained specifically for the binary task on the positive/negative data.
4.  **Confounding Factor of Word Vector Fine-tuning:** A crucial experimental detail is the decision not to fine-tune the word vectors. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows word embeddings to capture sentiment-specific semantics. It is plausible that a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned word vectors could perform much better, potentially closing the gap with the deep model. The performance gains attributed to depth may, in part, be due to the multiple layers learning to transform the general-purpose embeddings into task-specific representations—a role that fine-tuning would otherwise play. An ablation study comparing shallow and deep models *with* fine-tuning is necessary to isolate the true benefit of architectural depth.

### Originality and Significance

The proposed Deep RNN is a novel contribution. While the idea of stacking layers is not new in deep learning, its application to recursive networks is a logical and well-motivated extension. The work successfully demonstrates that the benefits of spatial depth, previously shown in feed-forward and recurrent models, also apply to tree-structured models.

The significance lies in the state-of-the-art performance and the demonstration that deeper compositional models can learn hierarchical representations. This opens up a promising direction for future research in natural language understanding. However, the paper's impact could be greater if its central claims were supported by a more rigorous experimental setup. The current experiments are confined to a single dataset where dense supervision is available at every node of the parse tree. It remains unclear whether the advantages of a Deep RNN would persist in the more common scenario where supervision is only available at the root (sentence) level.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and intuitive deep architecture for recursive networks.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.
*   Provides strong qualitative analysis that offers insights into the model's inner workings.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison to shallow models is potentially confounded by the lack of word vector fine-tuning.
*   The binary classification results are not directly comparable to previous work due to a flawed protocol.
*   The evaluation is limited to a single, densely-labeled dataset, leaving the model's utility on more typical root-only labeled datasets unevaluated.
*   The paper omits comparison to a relevant and strong baseline from concurrent work ("A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences," Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014), which also reports results on this dataset.

### Detailed Questions and Suggestions

*   **Shared Dropout Units (line 251):** What was the motivation for sharing the same dropout mask across all nodes within a layer for a given sentence?
*   **Stability Regularization (lines 255-256):** As an alternative to L2 penalty on activations for stability, have you considered other common techniques from RNN literature, such as gradient clipping or constraining the norms of the weight matrices?
*   **Generalization:** The Stanford Sentiment Treebank provides a rich, per-node supervisory signal that may be particularly well-suited for deep models. To strengthen the paper's claim about the general utility of Deep RNNs, it would be highly beneficial to include experiments on 1-2 additional datasets that only have sentence-level labels. This would demonstrate whether the architecture is effective without dense, intermediate supervision.

### Conclusion

This paper introduces a promising architectural extension for recursive neural networks and demonstrates its potential with impressive results on a popular sentiment analysis task. However, the experimental evaluation contains several weaknesses—most notably the lack of control for word vector fine-tuning and an incorrect protocol for binary classification—that temper the main conclusions about the benefits of depth.

While the idea is strong, I believe the paper requires additional experiments to be recommended for acceptance. Specifically, the authors should:
1.  Re-run the binary classification experiment using the standard protocol.
2.  Conduct an ablation study on the effect of fine-tuning word vectors for both shallow and deep models.
3.  Ideally, demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a task with only sentence-level supervision.

Addressing these points would result in a much stronger paper and provide more conclusive evidence for the value of adding spatial depth to recursive networks.
