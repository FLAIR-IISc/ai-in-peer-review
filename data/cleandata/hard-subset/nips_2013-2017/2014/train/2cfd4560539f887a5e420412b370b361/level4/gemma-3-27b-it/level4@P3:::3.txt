This paper proposes the use of ‘deep’ recursive networks by adding depth across space, motivated by the success of doing the same with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose using dropout and ReLUs, while fixing word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method is novel, representing a logical generalization of approaches used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some model and experimental design choices are questionable or lack sufficient justification, and I believe additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written for the most part, and sufficient detail is provided for reproduction of the results. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of deep learning and NLP, clearly articulating the motivation for exploring depth in recursive networks. The methodology section is generally well-explained, although some notation could be clarified (see detailed comments below). The experimental setup is reasonable, and the results are presented clearly in tables and accompanied by insightful qualitative analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of deep recursive neural networks, stacking recursive layers to achieve depth in space, is a valuable contribution.
*   **Strong Performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, making the complex ideas accessible.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase experiments provide valuable insights into how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Justification of Design Choices:** Some design choices, such as not fine-tuning word embeddings, are not adequately justified and may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is primarily focused on a single dataset (SST) and task (sentiment analysis). Additional experiments on diverse datasets and tasks would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The current binary classification setup deviates from standard practice by including neutral examples during training, making comparisons to existing approaches less direct.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper omits a comparison to a relevant recent work ("A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" by Kalchbrenner et al.).
*   **Speculative Claims:** Certain statements about the behavior of different layers (lines 148-154) are presented as factual without sufficient empirical support.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful architecture (deep RNNs).
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods on a challenging benchmark.
*   The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into the internal workings of the model.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of justification for certain design choices weakens the robustness of the findings.
*   The limited scope of experiments raises concerns about generalizability.
*   The binary classification protocol is flawed and affects comparability.
*   The omission of a relevant recent baseline limits the completeness of the evaluation.
*   Some claims are presented without adequate empirical evidence.

**Specific Comments/Questions:**

*   **Line 138:** Clarify the meaning of  W<sup>η</sup> and W<sup>xh</sup>. It would be clearer to present two separate equations – one for inputs and another for hidden layers.  Also, include a summary of the parameter space (e.g., {W<sub>L</sub>, W<sub>R</sub>, U, b, c}) along with the dimensionality of each matrix when defined.
*   **Lines 148-154:** The claim that a potential issue exists with shallow networks representing both sparse and dense data is speculative. If based on empirical results, please cite or present those results.
*   **Baseline:** Include results from Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) ("A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences").
*   **Word Embeddings:** Explain the rationale for not fine-tuning word embeddings. Consider exploring the impact of fine-tuning on performance. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings should be included for a fair comparison.
*   **Line 251 (Shared Dropout Units):**  Explain the motivation for sharing dropout units across nodes within a layer.
*   **Lines 255-256 (Stability):** Consider alternative stabilization techniques like weight norm constraints or gradient clipping, as used in recurrent networks.
*   **Binary Classification:** Redo the binary classification experiment using a proper protocol – train a separate binary classifier or decode the 5-dimensional posterior probabilities from the fine-grained model *only* on the binary classification test set, excluding neutral examples during training.
*   **Root Label Experiment:** Investigate the performance of deep RNNs when only a single root label is available, similar to traditional sentiment analysis settings. Would the improvements still hold?



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the core idea is promising and the results are encouraging, the identified weaknesses and unanswered questions prevent me from recommending acceptance. Addressing the specific comments above, particularly regarding experimental design, justification of choices, and inclusion of relevant baselines, would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit.
