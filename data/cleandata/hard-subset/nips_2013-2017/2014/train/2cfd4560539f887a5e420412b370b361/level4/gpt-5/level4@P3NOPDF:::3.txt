Summary
The paper introduces “deep” recursive neural networks by increasing depth across tree structure (space) rather than time, with layer-specific parameters shared across tree heights. The model uses ReLUs, dropout, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings. Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank show strong performance. The approach is a natural extension of adding depth to recurrent networks and yields noteworthy gains.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and an intuitive architectural generalization of deep recurrent nets to trees.
- Strong empirical results on the sentiment treebank; gains from ReLUs and dropout are compelling.
- Generally clear writing, with enough procedural detail to facilitate reproduction.

Main concerns
- Conceptual novelty is incremental; stronger empirical validation would solidify the contribution.
- Several modeling and experimental choices are insufficiently justified (e.g., fixed embeddings, shared dropout units).
- Some notation is unclear, and protocol deviations undermine comparability for the binary setting.

Detailed comments and questions
- Notation and parameterization (around line 138): W^η and W^xh are undefined. Please clarify whether these denote W^η_L and W^η_R, and separate equations for (i) the input-to-hidden transforms at the bottom layer and (ii) inter-layer transforms for higher layers. A concise summary of the parameter set (e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}) with dimensionalities for each matrix/vector would improve clarity and reproducibility.
- Speculation (lines 148–154): The claims here read as speculative. Are they supported by empirical observations in your experiments? If so, cite the evidence; if not, consider softening or removing these statements.
- Missing baseline: Please include results from “A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL 2014) to situate your method among contemporary strong baselines.
- Embedding fine-tuning: You fix word vectors to pre-trained embeddings. On a sentiment task, fine-tuning often yields sentiment-aware embeddings and can materially affect results. This raises two issues:
  - Could your gains from depth be partly compensating for not fine-tuning embeddings?
  - A fairer comparison would include: (a) single-layer RNN/RecNN with fine-tuned embeddings; (b) your deep model with fine-tuned embeddings; and (c) your current setup. This control would help isolate the contribution of depth across space.
- Shared dropout units (around line 251): What motivates sharing dropout units? Please explain whether masks are shared across tree nodes, across layers, or both, and provide an ablation comparing shared vs. independent dropout. Report dropout rates per layer and how dropout is applied at leaves vs. internal nodes.
- Training stability (lines 255–256): In addition to your approach, consider and/or report on standard techniques used with recurrent/recursive nets: constraining weight norms and gradient clipping. These can materially affect optimization and would make your training setup more robust and comparable.
- Binary classification protocol: Your training includes neutral examples, which deviates from the standard SST binary protocol. Results are therefore not directly comparable. Please retrain strictly on binary labels (train/dev/test as defined for the binary task) and report those results separately from the fine-grained setting.
- Predictions at all nodes vs. root-only supervision: The analogy to deep recurrent nets is strongest when supervision exists at every node (as in SST). It would be valuable to test whether depth across space helps when only a root label is available (e.g., a root-only variant of SST or other sentence-level datasets with global labels). Do you still observe gains over a single-layer recursive model under sparse supervision?
- Model capacity and efficiency: Please report parameter counts per depth and training/inference time. Do improvements scale linearly with added layers, or is there an optimal depth? Some discussion of overfitting vs. capacity would help interpret the role of depth.
- Ablations: Since you highlight the benefits of ReLUs and dropout, a small ablation table quantifying each component’s contribution (activation choice, with/without dropout, with/without depth) would strengthen the empirical story.
- Implementation details: Document optimizer, learning-rate schedule, initialization, gradient clipping threshold (if used), early stopping criterion, L2 regularization (if any), and development set usage for hyperparameter selection. This will further improve reproducibility. Code or config release would also be welcome.
- Error analysis: A brief qualitative analysis showing examples where deeper layers correct shallower errors would make the case for what “depth across space” captures.

Presentation
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Addressing the notation gaps, adding dimensionalities when parameters are introduced, and more carefully motivating unusual choices (e.g., shared dropout) will improve readability.

Recommendation
The idea is promising, and the empirical results on SST are strong. However, missing baselines, protocol deviations for binary classification, and the lack of controls around embedding fine-tuning limit the conclusiveness of the evidence. I view the paper as borderline at this stage. If the authors can:
- add the Kalchbrenner et al. baseline,
- redo the binary experiments with the standard protocol,
- study the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings (including a strong single-layer baseline),
- and provide at least one root-only/global-label evaluation (or a root-only SST setting),

then the work would be considerably stronger and its contribution clearer.
