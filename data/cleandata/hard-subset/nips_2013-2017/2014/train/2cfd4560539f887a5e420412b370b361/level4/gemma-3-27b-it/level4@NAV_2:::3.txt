This paper proposes the use of 'deep' recursive networks by adding depth across space, motivated by the success of this approach with recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters shared across different heights within that layer. The authors also propose the use of dropout and ReLUs, while fixing the word representations to pre-trained embeddings. Experimentation is performed on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method is novel, effectively generalizing approaches used with recurrent nets. The authors achieve strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset. The performance gains obtained with dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, some model and experimental design choices are questionable or lack sufficient justification, and I believe additional experimentation is necessary before I can wholeheartedly recommend acceptance.

The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducing the results.

Detailed comments / questions:
- Line 138: The notation W^eta and W^xh is unclear. Are these intended to represent W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? Two equations – one for inputs and another for hidden layers – would improve clarity. A summary of the parameter space (e.g., {WL, WR, U, b, c}) including the dimensionality of each matrix would also be helpful.
- Lines 148-154: This claim seems speculative. If based on empirical results, this should be explicitly stated. I'm not convinced this is a significant issue.
- Baseline: The authors should include results from Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) ("A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences") from ACL.
- The decision not to fine-tune word vectors is surprising. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows embeddings to reflect sentiment, creating a mismatch between discriminative phrase/sentence vectors and non-sentiment-aware word vectors. Although a deep RNN *can* address this through forward-passing, it’s unclear if the observed improvements are independent of this choice. A single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings should be used as a control.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What was the motivation for this design choice?
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternatively, consider weight norm constraint or gradient clipping, techniques common in recurrent neural network training.
- Binary classification: This should be performed separately. Including neutral class examples during training, while evaluating on a binary classification task, deviates from standard experimental protocols and makes comparisons to existing approaches invalid.
- The analogy with deep recurrent nets is most apt given predictions at each node in the tree, as is the case on the treebank. It would be useful to explore whether deep RNNs offer an advantage when only a single root label exists. Does improvement still occur over a single-layer RNN? The approach is promising, but additional experiments are desirable. I recommend re-performing the binary classification experiment following proper protocol, investigating the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and including 1-2 additional datasets employing only global labels. These additions would strengthen the paper and provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across space.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel architecture extending successful ideas from recurrent networks.
*   Strong empirical results achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   Clear writing and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   Interesting findings regarding the effect of dropout and ReLUs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Some model design choices lack clear justification.
*   Missing comparison to recent and relevant baselines.
*   Potential confounding factor of not fine-tuning word embeddings.
*   Limited scope of experiments (single dataset, single task).
*   Binary classification experiment is not performed according to standard protocols.
