This paper introduces deep recursive networks, extending the concept of depth successfully applied to recurrent networks to a spatial dimension. The approach utilizes layers with shared parameters across different levels within each layer, combined with dropout and ReLU activations, while leveraging pre-trained word embeddings. The method is evaluated on the sentiment treebank dataset.

The proposed method is novel, representing a logical generalization of existing recurrent neural network techniques. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the sentiment treebank, and the observed gains from employing dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, several modeling and experimental choices lack sufficient justification, and I believe further investigation is required before a confident acceptance can be recommended.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail to enable reproduction of the reported results.

Detailed comments / questions:

- Line 138: The notation W^eta and W^xh is undefined. Are these intended to represent W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? Clarity would be improved by presenting separate equations for input and hidden layer calculations. A summary of the parameter space – {WL, WR, U, b, c} – would also be beneficial, as would including the dimensionality of each matrix upon its definition.
- Lines 148-154: This discussion appears speculative. If based on empirical observations, this should be stated explicitly. The asserted issue is not fully convincing as presented.
- Baseline: The results of Kalchbrenner et al. (“A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences,” ACL 2013) should be included for comparison.
- Word Vectors: Why were the word vectors not fine-tuned? Allowing the embeddings to adapt to the sentiment task could enhance their sentiment-specific representation. The current setup creates a discrepancy between discriminative phrase/sentence vectors and non-discriminative word vectors, which seems counterintuitive, despite the ability of deep RNNs to propagate information across layers. It is possible that the observed improvements from deep RNNs are partially attributable to the lack of word vector fine-tuning; a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings might achieve comparable or superior performance. This requires further investigation.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated the decision to share dropout units?
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Consider alternative stabilization techniques such as weight norm regularization or gradient clipping, common practices in recurrent neural network training.
- Binary Classification: The binary classification experiment should be conducted separately to avoid the inclusion of neutral class examples during training, which deviates from established experimental protocols and renders the results incomparable to existing approaches.
- Depth Analogy & Root Labels: The analogy to deep recurrent networks is appropriate in the treebank experiments where predictions are made at each node. However, it would be valuable to assess the benefit of deep recursive networks when only a single, root-level label is available. Would an improvement over a single-layer RNN still be observed in this scenario? While the deep recursive network approach appears promising, additional experiments are necessary to solidify these findings. I recommend re-performing the binary classification experiment following the proper protocol, investigating the effect of fine-tuning the word embeddings, and including one or two additional datasets featuring only global labels. These experiments would strengthen the paper and provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across space.
