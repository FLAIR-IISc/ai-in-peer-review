- Proposes “deep” recursive networks adding depth across space with per-layer parameter sharing; uses dropout and ReLUs; fixes pre-trained word embeddings; evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST).
- Novelty: effectively generalizes techniques from recurrent nets to recursive spatial depth.
- Strong empirical performance on SST and notable gains from dropout and ReLUs.
- Paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Notation unclear (W^eta, W^xh); needs separate equations for inputs vs hidden layers and a parameter/dimensionality summary.
- Claim in lines 148–154 is speculative and should be supported by empirical evidence or reworded.
- Missing baseline: should include Kalchbrenner et al. (2014) convolutional sentence model from ACL.
- Decision not to fine-tune word embeddings is surprising and may confound results; include a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings as a control.
- Motivation for shared dropout units (line 251) is not explained.
- Suggest considering alternative stabilization techniques (weight norm constraints or gradient clipping) instead of the current approach (lines 255–256).
- Binary classification experiment is flawed: training with neutral examples but evaluating binary makes comparisons invalid—re-run with standard binary protocol.
- Unclear whether deep recursive nets help when only a single root label exists; test on additional datasets with only global labels.
- Recommended experiments to strengthen the paper: redo binary classification properly, test fine-tuning embeddings, and add 1–2 additional global-label datasets.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel architecture, state-of-the-art results, clear writing, and interesting findings about dropout/ReLUs.
- Arguments against acceptance: some model choices lack justification, missing relevant baselines, potential confound from frozen embeddings, limited experimental scope, and improper binary evaluation.
