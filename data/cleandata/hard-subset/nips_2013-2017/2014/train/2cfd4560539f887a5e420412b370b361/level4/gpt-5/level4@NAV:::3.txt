Review- This paper introduces “deep” recursive networks by increasing depth across the tree structure, motivated by the success of doing the same in recurrent networks. Each layer has its own parameters, tied across different heights within that layer. The authors also employ dropout and ReLU activations, while keeping word vectors fixed to pretrained embeddings. Experiments are conducted on the Socher et al. sentiment treebank.

The method is new, though largely a generalization of the approach used with recurrent nets. The results on the sentiment treebank are very strong. The gains attributable to dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy on their own. However, several modeling and experimental choices are questionable or insufficiently justified, and I believe further experimentation is needed before I can fully endorse acceptance.

The paper is mostly clearly written, with enough detail for readers to reproduce the results.

Detailed comments / questions:
- line 138: What are W^eta and W^xh? These are not defined. Do you mean W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R? It would be clearer to present two separate equations: one for inputs and one for hidden states. It would also help to summarize the parameter set, e.g., {W_L, W_R, U, b, c}, and include the dimensionality of each matrix when first introduced.
- lines 148-154: This reads as speculative. Is it based on your empirical observations? If so, please state that. I’m not convinced this is actually a concern.
- baseline: Please include results from “A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences” (Kalchbrenner et al., ACL this year).
- Why did you not fine-tune the word embeddings? Fine-tuning on a sentiment task typically makes word vectors reflect sentiment. It is odd to have phrase/sentence vectors that are sentiment-discriminative while individual word vectors are not. (Granted, a deep RNN could propagate transformations across layers.) Is it possible the observed improvements with deep RNNs arise because you did not fine-tune the words? Conceivably, a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings could outperform your model. This needs to be controlled.
- line 251 (shared dropout units): What motivated this design choice?
- lines 255-256 (stability): As an alternative, consider constraining weight norms or truncating/ clipping gradients, as is common with recurrent nets.
- binary classification: This should be conducted separately. Including neutral examples at training time deviates from the standard protocol, making your results not directly comparable to prior work.
- The analogy to deep recurrent nets is appropriate when predicting at every tree node, as in the treebank setup. It would be valuable to test whether deep RNNs still help when only a single root label is available. Would you still expect improvements over a single-layer RNN? The approach is promising, but additional experiments would strengthen the case: redo the binary classification under the proper protocol, study the impact of fine-tuning embeddings, and include 1–2 more datasets with only a global label. These additions would make for a stronger paper and provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth across space.
