This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks, extending standard recursive models by adding depth spatially. This approach is well-motivated by the success of similar deep architectures in recurrent neural networks. In the proposed model, each layer has its own set of parameters, which are shared across all nodes in the tree. The authors also incorporate dropout and Rectified Linear Units (ReLUs) and use fixed, pre-trained word embeddings for the input layer. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The proposed method is novel, representing a natural generalization of the deep stacking approach that has proven successful for recurrent networks. The authors demonstrate very strong performance on the sentiment treebank dataset, and the performance gains attributed to dropout and ReLUs are interesting in their own right. However, I have reservations about certain aspects of the model and experimental design that are not sufficiently justified. Consequently, I believe additional experimentation is required before I can confidently recommend this paper for acceptance.

The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail for the reader to reproduce the results.

**Detailed comments / questions:**

-   **line 138:** The notation W^eta and W^xh is used without prior definition. If these are meant to be general placeholders for the left and right weight matrices (e.g., W^{eta}L and W^{eta}R), this should be clarified. For greater clarity, I would suggest using separate equations for the input-to-hidden transformation and the hidden-to-hidden transformations in subsequent layers. It would also be beneficial to explicitly summarize the full set of model parameters (e.g., {WL, WR, U, b, c}) and to specify the dimensionality of each matrix upon its introduction.

-   **lines 148-154:** The argument presented here seems speculative. Is this claim supported by empirical results from your experiments? If so, please state this explicitly. As written, I am not convinced that this is a significant issue without further evidence.

-   **Missing Baseline:** The authors should compare their results to the recent work of Kalchbrenner et al. ("A Convolutional Neural Network for Modelling Sentences," ACL 2014), which represents a key related baseline.

-   **Lack of Word Vector Fine-tuning:** The decision not to fine-tune the word vectors is a critical design choice that requires more justification. Fine-tuning on a sentiment task allows the embeddings to capture sentiment-specific semantics. It seems counter-intuitive for the model to learn sentiment-discriminative phrase and sentence representations while the underlying word vectors remain fixed and sentiment-agnostic. A key concern is that the performance gains from the deep architecture might be an artifact of this choice. It is conceivable that a single-layer recursive network with fine-tuned word embeddings could perform as well or better than the proposed deep model with fixed embeddings. This is a crucial baseline that should be included to properly isolate the contribution of depth.

-   **line 251 (shared dropout units):** Could the authors elaborate on the motivation for sharing the same dropout mask for the child vectors?

-   **lines 255-256 (stability):** Regarding stability, have the authors considered alternative techniques common in recurrent networks, such as constraining the norms of the weight matrices or using gradient clipping?

-   **Binary Classification Protocol:** The binary classification experiment does not appear to follow the standard protocol. By including neutral examples during training, the model is trained on a different data distribution than previous work, making the results not directly comparable. This experiment should be re-run, excluding neutral examples from the training process, to allow for a fair comparison.

The analogy to deep recurrent networks is compelling for the treebank task, where predictions are made at every node. However, a key unanswered question is whether the benefits of depth extend to tasks where only a single label is predicted at the root of the tree. Would a deep model still outperform a shallow one in that setting?

The proposed architecture is a promising direction, but its general utility is not yet fully established. To make a stronger case for the value of spatial depth, I would recommend the following additional experiments:
1.  Re-run the binary classification experiment using the standard protocol for fair comparison.
2.  Conduct an ablation study on the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, including a comparison to a single-layer model with fine-tuned vectors.
3.  Evaluate the model on 1-2 additional datasets that involve sentence-level classification (i.e., only a root label), to test the approach's generalizability.

Incorporating these experiments would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and provide more conclusive evidence for the usefulness of deep recursive networks.
