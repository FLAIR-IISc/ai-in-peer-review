

This paper introduces 'deep' recursive networks by introducing spatial depth, drawing inspiration from the success of depth in recurrent networks. Each layer employs its own parameters, shared across different vertical positions within that layer. The authors incorporate dropout and ReLUs while fixing word representations to pre-trained embeddings, evaluating their approach on Socher et al.'s sentiment treebank. The method is novel as a generalization of depth techniques applied to recurrent networks, achieving strong performance on the sentiment treebank. The observed gains from dropout and ReLUs are noteworthy. However, certain model and experimental design choices lack sufficient justification, and additional experiments are required before I can recommend acceptance. The paper is generally well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Detailed comments:
- Line 138: Define $W^\eta$ and $W^{xh}$. Are these intended to represent $W^{\eta}_L$ and $W^{\eta}_R$? Clarify by presenting separate equations for input and hidden layers, and explicitly summarize the parameter set $\{W_L, W_R, U, b, c\}$ with dimensionalities.
- Lines 148-154: This claim appears speculative. If based on empirical results, state this explicitly; otherwise, the argument lacks support.
- Baseline: Include results from Kalchbrenner et al.'s "A convolutional neural network for modelling sentences" (ACL 2013).
- Word vectors: Explain why word embeddings are fixed rather than fine-tuned. Fine-tuning is standard for sentiment tasks to make embeddings sentiment-sensitive. The current design—where phrase/sentence vectors are sentiment-discriminative but word vectors are not—raises concern. It is possible that performance gains stem from not fine-tuning; a single-layer RNN with fine-tuned embeddings should be compared.
- Line 251 (shared dropout units): Clarify the rationale for this design choice.
- Lines 255-256 (stability): Alternative approaches like weight norm constraints or gradient clipping (common in recurrent networks) could be explored.
- Binary classification: This experiment must be conducted separately. Training includes neutral-class examples, deviating from standard protocol, making results incomparable to existing methods.
- Depth utility: The analogy to deep recurrent networks holds for node-wise predictions (as in the treebank). Demonstrate whether depth provides an advantage for single-root-label tasks (e.g., standard sentence classification). The authors should re-conduct the binary classification experiment per standard protocol, test the effect of fine-tuning embeddings, and include 1-2 additional datasets using only global labels. These experiments would significantly strengthen the paper and provide clearer evidence for spatial depth.
