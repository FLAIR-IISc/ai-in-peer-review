Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs) that combine depth in structure (tree recursion) with depth in space (stacked layers). Concretely, the authors stack multiple recursive layers; within each layer, node representations are computed bottom-up along a binary parse tree, and each node passes its hidden state to the corresponding node in the next layer. The paper also “unties” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer so that word vectors (X) and phrase vectors (H) live in different spaces, enabling rectified linear activations (ReLUs). Training uses pretrained 300d word2vec embeddings (frozen), dropout with shared masks across all nodes in a layer for a sentence, AdaGrad, early stopping, and a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations.

Evaluation is on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), predicting 5-way fine-grained sentiment (and binary via mapping 5-way outputs). DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets of comparable parameter count, and the best model exceeds previously reported results for RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN on the fine-grained task. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases per layer) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (lexical dominance at layer 1, syntactic/strength at layer 2, sentiment polarity at layer 3).

This work relates to recursive networks on trees for parsing and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al., 2011–2013) and to deep/stacked recurrent networks for sequences (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). Its contribution is the explicit stacking of recursive layers on trees plus practical choices (untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLU, dropout) to make training effective.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and architecture: distinguishing depth in structure vs. depth in space is well articulated; the stacked formulation is simple and implementable.
- Strong empirical results on SST with new state-of-the-art reported for fine-grained classification.
- Sensible engineering: use of ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad, identity-like initialization, and shared dropout across tree nodes is well justified.
- Ablation in spirit: comparisons between shallow and deeper models under roughly fixed parameter budgets support the claim that depth helps.
- Qualitative analyses are insightful and align with the compositionality motivation.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Attribution of gains is unclear. Multiple changes (pretrained embeddings, untied leaf/internal, ReLU, dropout, activation/weight penalties) are introduced; only limited ablation is shown. It is difficult to isolate the incremental benefit of stacking beyond these factors.
- Fairness of comparisons: best baselines (e.g., RNTN) were trained with randomly initialized embeddings fine-tuned on SST, while the proposed method uses large pretrained embeddings kept fixed. This is not apples-to-apples and may explain part of the gains. Re-running baselines with the same embeddings/regimen or fine-tuning embeddings in DRNN would clarify.
- No statistical significance or variance across runs is reported. SST is small; different random seeds and early stopping can materially affect results.
- Only one dataset/task (SST). Generality beyond sentiment on parse trees remains unverified.
- The paper reports that connecting the classifier only to the top layer simplifies analysis, but does not empirically compare against deep supervision (outputs at all layers).

Clarity
- Overall clear, but some details are underspecified: exact parameter counts and per-configuration widths; computational complexity and training time; whether V matrices above layer 1 are split for leaves vs. internals in practice; specifics of dropout mask sharing and its implications for gradient estimates.
- The activation L2 penalty is nonstandard; its role and sensitivity could be elaborated.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers is a relatively straightforward extension of stacked RNNs; untying leaves/internals and using ReLU are incremental. The qualitative layer analyses are a nice touch, but the core technical novelty is moderate.

Significance
- Achieves strong results on a canonical benchmark; the layer-wise findings may inform future compositional models. However, the impact is tempered by limited scope (single dataset) and uncertain attribution of improvements.

Detailed suggestions
- Provide ablations to isolate contributions:
  - Depth vs. width at constant parameters (done partially); also show a fixed-width comparison.
  - ReLU vs. tanh; with/without untying leaf/internal; with/without dropout; pretrained vs. randomly initialized embeddings; with/without activation L2 penalty.
  - Connect outputs to all layers (deep supervision) vs. only top.
- Ensure fair baselines:
  - Re-train RNTN/MV-RNN with the same pretrained embeddings (frozen and fine-tuned) and regularization, or fine-tune embeddings in DRNN and report both.
  - Report standard deviations over multiple random seeds and significance tests.
- Extend evaluation:
  - Additional datasets or tasks involving compositionality (e.g., paraphrase detection as in Socher et al. 2011, question classification, phrase-level entailment if available at submission time).
  - Binary sentiment trained directly to test whether depth still helps in an “easier” regime.
- Report efficiency metrics: parameter counts per configuration, training time/epoch, memory footprint, and asymptotic complexity with depth and tree size.
- Analyze optimization: gradient norms across layers/depth, effect of 0.5I initialization vs. identity/orthogonal, sensitivity to learning rate and dropout rate.
- Clarify architectural details: whether V matrices are split for leaves/internals above layer 1; precise masking scheme for dropout across nodes/layers.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the improvement over RNTN remains if both models use the same pretrained embeddings (frozen or fine-tuned) and the same regularization?
2) Did you try fine-tuning word vectors? If so, how did it affect performance and the layer-wise qualitative behavior?
3) What is the variance across random seeds? Are improvements over shallow RNNs statistically significant?
4) How does connecting the output to all layers affect performance and interpretability?
5) What are training time and parameter counts for your best models?

Pro/Con list for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, motivated extension of recursive models with practical training improvements
- Strong empirical performance (SST SOTA) and informative qualitative analyses
- Evidence that depth in space adds value beyond depth in structure

Cons:
- Modest novelty; stacking is expected
- Attribution unclear due to multiple concurrent changes and lack of controlled ablations
- Comparisons not strictly fair; no significance/variance
- Limited scope (single dataset)

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is sound and results are promising, but stronger, fairer empirical evidence and clearer attribution of what causes the gains are needed for a NeurIPS standard. The paper could become a solid contribution with the suggested ablations, fair baselines, and broader evaluation.
