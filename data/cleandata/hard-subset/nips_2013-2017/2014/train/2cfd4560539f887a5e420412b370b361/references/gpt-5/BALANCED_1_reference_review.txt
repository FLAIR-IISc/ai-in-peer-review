Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for compositional semantics: instead of applying a single recursive composition function over a parse tree, the authors stack multiple recursive layers, adding “depth in space” to the usual “depth in structure.” The architecture introduces skip-like connections between corresponding nodes across layers (V(i)), uses ReLU activations, dropout regularization, and unties parameters for leaf-to-parent versus internal-to-parent compositions in the first layer. The model is trained end-to-end with backpropagation through structure and AdaGrad, and evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way and binary sentiment classification. The paper also includes qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) to suggest layer specialization.

The work builds on and clearly relates to: recursive nets over trees (Goller & Küchler; Socher et al.), deep recurrent networks that increase depth “in time” (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), and advances in deep learning training (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad). It compares against RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN (Socher et al.), Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN, reporting improved fine-grained and binary accuracies on SST. The approach is conceptually close to stacking recurrent layers (as in deep RNNs) but adapted to tree structures, which appears to be novel.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea (stacked recursive layers) with a plausible motivation connecting depth to hierarchical abstraction in composition.
- Solid engineering choices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad, pretrained embeddings) and a fair parameter-matched comparison across different depths.
- Empirical gains over baselines on a widely used dataset; state-of-the-art at the time on fine-grained SST.
- Useful qualitative analyses hinting at layer specialization (intensity vs polarity) and shifts in neighborhood structure across layers.
- The decision to connect supervision only to the top layer aids interpretability of what each layer may be learning.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of evaluation: only SST is used. No tests on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment, relation classification) or other languages/domains; thus external validity is unclear.
- Ablations are insufficient to isolate contributions. Improvements could stem from pretrained embeddings, ReLU, dropout, and untying leaves/internal nodes, rather than depth per se. While parameter-matched depth comparisons are provided, there is little disentangling of these factors.
- Baseline fairness: prior RNTN/MV-RNN results typically did not use the same pretrained embeddings, dropout, or rectifiers. Re-running strong baselines with these improvements would clarify the incremental value of stacking versus better regularization/initialization.
- Statistical rigor: no variance across runs, confidence intervals, or significance tests. Given modest improvements, robustness to random seeds/hyperparameters should be reported.
- Some notational and methodological clarity issues: occasional confusion between WL/WR vs Whh; initialization “0.5 I + ε” for tree composition (two children) merits more explanation; details on parsing and implementation choices (parser, binarization) and computational costs are sparse.
- Regularization details (activation L2 penalty, shared dropout masks across all nodes of a sentence) are somewhat ad hoc; justification or ablation would help.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sensible and the training details are mostly thorough, but the experimental evidence is narrow and lacks strong ablations/statistical analysis. Claims about depth enabling layer specialization are suggestive but not firmly established.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with useful figures and methodological detail. Some notation/inits and regularization choices could be clarified.
- Originality: Stacking recursive layers for trees appears novel, distinct from multiplicative composition (RNTN) and deep recurrent nets in time. Untying leaves vs internals for ReLUs is a reasonable, if incremental, design choice.
- Significance: Potentially useful for a class of tree-structured NLP problems. However, with evidence limited to SST and with confounding improvements from standard deep-learning tricks, the broader impact is uncertain.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea that likely transfers to other structured tasks.
- Empirical improvements over published baselines; parameter-matched comparisons across depth.
- Interesting qualitative analyses pointing to meaningful layer behaviors.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow empirical scope; lack of strong ablations to isolate the effect of depth versus other factors.
- Baselines not re-tuned with comparable modern training tricks, leaving fairness concerns.
- No statistical significance or variance reporting; unclear robustness.
- Some clarity gaps and missing details (initialization rationale, computational costs).

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a plausible, well-motivated architectural extension with promising results, but the current evidence does not convincingly establish that depth in space—rather than improved regularization/initialization—drives the gains. A stronger empirical study across tasks, tighter baselines, and more thorough ablations would raise confidence in the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled ablations: (a) shallow vs deep with identical ReLU+dropout+pretrained settings; (b) effect of untying leaves/internals; (c) connecting outputs to all layers; (d) dropout mask sharing vs independent masks; (e) with/without activation L2 penalty.
- Re-run strong baselines (RNTN, MV-RNN, DCNN) with pretrained embeddings and dropout to ensure fairness; report parameter counts and training budgets.
- Report averages and standard deviations over multiple seeds; include significance testing; provide training curves and wall-clock analysis.
- Expand evaluation to at least one more structured NLP task (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment) to demonstrate generality.
- Clarify notation and initialization schemes (how “0.5 I” maps to two-child composition), parser/binarization details, and provide code for reproducibility.
- Explore depth-width trade-offs more fully (including equal-width settings) and analyze failure modes when going deeper.
- Consider investigating layer-wise supervision or multi-task auxiliary losses to probe and solidify layer specialization claims.
