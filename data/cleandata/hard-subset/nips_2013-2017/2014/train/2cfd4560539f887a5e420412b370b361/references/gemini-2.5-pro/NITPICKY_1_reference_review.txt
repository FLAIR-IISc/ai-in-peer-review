**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Recursive Neural Network (Deep RNN), an extension of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) that incorporates depth by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation draws an analogy to deep Recurrent Neural Networks, distinguishing between depth in *structure* (the depth of the input parse tree) and depth in *space* (the number of stacked layers). The authors propose that this stacking allows for a hierarchy of representations, where each layer can learn more abstract compositions. A key methodological detail is the "untying" of parameters for leaf nodes (words) and internal nodes (phrases) in the first layer, which the authors argue allows for more flexible model design and better use of rectifier activation functions. The proposed architecture is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, where it is shown to outperform shallow counterparts with a similar parameter count and achieve state-of-the-art results on the fine-grained sentiment classification task compared to prior work, including RNTNs and Paragraph Vectors. The paper concludes with qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor queries) to provide intuition about the different roles played by each layer in the deep architecture.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The core idea of stacking recursive layers is a logical and novel extension of the existing recursive network framework. The motivation, which differentiates depth in structure from depth in space, is clear and provides a solid conceptual foundation for the proposed model.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, a widely used and competitive benchmark. The performance improvement over strong baselines like the RNTN is significant.
3.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The comparison between deep/narrow and shallow/wide models with a constrained parameter budget is a well-designed experiment that directly supports the central claim that depth, not just parameter count, is beneficial.
4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The inclusion of input perturbation and nearest neighbor analyses is commendable. These experiments provide valuable, albeit preliminary, insights into the model's inner workings and support the hypothesis that different layers learn distinct aspects of compositionality.
5.  **Methodological Contribution:** The "untying" of leaf and internal node transformations in Section 2.2 is a simple but effective architectural choice. The justification regarding dimensionality and the use of rectifier units is plausible and seems to be validated by the strong performance gain over previous shallow RNNs.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a promising research direction, it is undermined by a significant number of flaws ranging from major presentation issues to a lack of technical and experimental rigor.

1.  **Major Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The manuscript text refers to Figure 1 (RNN variants), Figure 2 (Deep RNN architecture), Figure 3 (input perturbation analysis), Table 1 (quantitative results), and Table 2 (nearest neighbor examples). None of these essential components are present in the submitted paper. This is a critical omission that makes it impossible to fully understand the architecture, verify the results, or appreciate the qualitative analyses. The paper is fundamentally incomplete without them.
    *   **Incomplete and Inconsistent Bibliography:** The reference section is in a state of disarray. A large number of citations (e.g., [1], [2], [4]) are incomplete, containing placeholder commas (`,, , ,`) where year, volume, or page numbers should be. Author names in reference [2] appear to contain typos ("Rjean", "Jauvin"). This level of carelessness is concerning and falls far below the publication standards for NIPS.

2.  **Lack of Technical Clarity and Detail:**
    *   **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation used in Equation (3), `W l(η) L h l(η)`, is non-standard and poorly explained. It is unclear if `W l(η)` implies that the weight matrix `W` is a function of the node `l(η)`, or if it refers to a specific weight matrix chosen based on the type of node `l(η)`. This ambiguity hinders a precise understanding of the model.
    *   **Superficial Justifications:** The argument in Section 2.2 that separating leaf/internal nodes allows for a more "natural" use of rectifiers because one weight matrix would otherwise have to handle both sparse and dense cases is weak. This is stated as a possibility ("might be difficult") rather than a rigorously demonstrated fact. An ablation study would have been more convincing.
    *   **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** The description of backpropagation is extremely high-level. Key details about hyperparameter selection are missing; for instance, the learning rate is fixed at .01 and the L2 penalty at 10⁻⁵ without justification or sensitivity analysis. While AdaGrad is used, the initial learning rate can still have a significant impact.

3.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **No Significance Testing:** The paper claims state-of-the-art results, but provides no statistical significance tests or confidence intervals. Given the small margins often seen on this benchmark, it is crucial to demonstrate that the observed improvements over baselines are statistically significant and not due to random initialization or training dynamics.
    *   **Potentially Cherry-Picked Qualitative Evidence:** The qualitative analyses in Section 3.2 are based on one or two selected examples. While interesting, the strong conclusions drawn (e.g., that the second layer captures sentiment intensity but not direction) are speculative and could be artifacts of the specific examples chosen. A more systematic analysis is needed to validate these claims.
    *   **Weakened Binary Classification Claim:** The binary classification results are obtained by decoding from the 5-class model, not by training a dedicated binary classifier. The authors correctly admit this "solves a harder problem" and that "there might be room for improvement." While this is honest, it weakens the claim of achieving SOTA on the binary task, as it is not an apples-to-apples comparison with methods optimized specifically for that task.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**.

The core idea of this paper is interesting and the headline results are strong. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. The complete absence of figures and tables is a fatal flaw that makes a proper review impossible. Furthermore, the paper is marred by a lack of attention to detail, evident in the deeply flawed bibliography, ambiguous mathematical notation, and insufficient experimental rigor. While many of these are fixable, the sheer number of issues, from major to minor, suggests a rushed preparation. The work shows promise, but it requires a very substantial revision to meet the quality standards of NIPS.
