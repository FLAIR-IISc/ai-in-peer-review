

# Peer Review: Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language

## Summary

This paper introduces "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (DRNNs), which stack multiple recursive neural network layers to combine "depth in structure" (recursive processing within a layer) with "depth in space" (stacking layers). The authors argue that standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) are deep in structure but lack hierarchical representation spaces across layers, unlike standard deep feedforward networks. The DRNN architecture extends RNNs by adding connections between layers, allowing each layer to learn different aspects of compositionality. The authors evaluate DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in fine-grained sentiment analysis and providing qualitative evidence that different layers capture different compositional aspects.

The work builds on recursive neural networks (e.g., Socher et al., 2013) and deep recurrent networks (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013), but extends them by combining structural depth with spatial depth. The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community's interest in compositional modeling and deep learning architectures.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Methodological Contribution**: The DRNN architecture is well-defined with precise equations (Section 2.3) and visualizations (Figures 1-2). The distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" is conceptually useful and clarifies the relationship between RNNs and deep feedforward networks.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over shallow RNNs and previous state-of-the-art methods (Table 1). The 49.5% fine-grained accuracy on SST represents a meaningful improvement over prior work (48.1% for shallow RNNs with similar parameters).

3. **Insightful Qualitative Analysis**: The input perturbation (Figure 3) and nearest neighbor analysis (Table 2) provide compelling evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality. This is a significant contribution beyond just reporting accuracy gains.

4. **Careful Experimental Design**: The paper properly addresses potential challenges with deep networks (vanishing gradients via fine-grained labels), uses appropriate baselines, and includes ablation studies (e.g., the impact of dropout).

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Novelty**: While the DRNN architecture is well-executed, the core idea of stacking recursive layers is not entirely novel. Tai et al. (2015) proposed similar architectures for parsing, and the distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" is somewhat analogous to the distinction between recurrent and deep feedforward networks. The paper should more explicitly differentiate its contribution from these prior works.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper demonstrates empirically that deeper networks outperform shallower ones, but doesn't provide theoretical analysis to explain *why* this is the case. Why do DRNNs outperform shallow RNNs with the same number of parameters? The paper's explanation ("decrease in width dominating gains from increased depth") is somewhat hand-wavy and lacks empirical support.

3. **Limited Generalizability**: The results are confined to sentiment analysis on SST. The paper doesn't explore whether DRNNs would benefit other compositional tasks (e.g., semantic parsing, question answering), limiting the significance of the findings. The claim that "different layers capture different aspects of compositionality" is compelling for sentiment, but it's unclear how generalizable this insight is.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of DRNNs compared to shallow RNNs or other architectures. With deeper networks, the forward and backward passes would be more expensive, especially with the recursive structure within each layer. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper compares to RNTN and MV-RNN but doesn't address more recent approaches like Tree-LSTM (Tai et al., 2015) or dynamic convolutional networks (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014), which have achieved strong results on SST. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of being state-of-the-art.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of compositional modeling in NLP. The DRNN architecture is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over baselines on a standard benchmark. The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into how different layers process compositional information.

However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the paper could have made a stronger case for the significance of its contribution by addressing the limitations mentioned above. The empirical results are strong, but the theoretical justification and broader applicability are weaker.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The main areas for improvement are:

1. More clearly articulating the novelty relative to prior work on deep recursive architectures (e.g., Tai et al., 2015)
2. Providing more theoretical justification for why depth in this architecture improves performance
3. Discussing computational complexity and comparing to more recent architectures on SST

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential directions for future work beyond sentiment analysis.

This work aligns well with NIPS' focus on deep learning and compositional modeling, and the results represent a meaningful advancement in the field. The qualitative analysis is particularly valuable for understanding how deep compositional models process language, which is a significant contribution beyond just achieving better accuracy.
