

# Review

This paper proposes "deep recursive neural networks" (DRNNs) as a combination of depth in structure (from recursive neural networks) and depth in space (from stacking layers). The authors claim DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks and previous state-of-the-art methods on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment analysis.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear motivation for combining structural and spatial depth in neural networks. The experimental results are well-organized, with comprehensive comparisons to multiple baselines. The qualitative analysis using input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases provides interesting insights into layer-wise compositionality.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Terminology Error**: The paper consistently uses "RNN" to refer to "Recursive Neural Networks" (e.g., "Recursive neural networks (e.g. [6]) (RNNs)"). This is a fundamental error that misrepresents the field. In the neural network community, "RNN" specifically refers to Recurrent Neural Networks, not Recursive Neural Networks. This confusion will mislead readers and is unacceptable in a technical paper.

2. **Inconsistent and Unclear Notation**: In Equation (3), the authors write "W η· = W xh· if η is a leaf and W η· =W hh · otherwise." The dot notation is ambiguous (does it represent a matrix row or a typo?), and the inconsistent formatting of W^xh vs W^hh creates confusion. The paper should use standard matrix notation throughout.

3. **Reproducibility Issues**: The paper states "we use the publicly available 300 dimensional word vectors by [19]" but fails to specify which exact vectors (e.g., Google News vectors, dimensionality, training corpus). This is critical for reproducibility. Additionally, the paper mentions tuning dropout rate over {0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5} but doesn't report which value was used for the best results.

4. **Methodological Omission**: The paper claims "we do not finetune for simplicity of our models" but provides no justification for this decision. Given that word vector finetuning is standard practice and could significantly impact performance, this omission is substantial. The authors should at least discuss the implications of this choice.

5. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: References contain formatting errors (e.g., "Learning deep architectures for AI, Yoshua Bengio, Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning,, , ,"), suggesting poor attention to detail. The paper also inconsistently uses "RNN" for both recurrent and recursive networks.

6. **Theoretical Justification Gap**: The paper claims "the benefits of depth" but provides no theoretical analysis to support why depth would help in this architecture. The empirical results are presented, but without theoretical grounding, it's difficult to understand why depth improves performance.

7. **Missing Qualitative Evidence**: The paper references "Figure 3" for input perturbation results but provides no actual data or figures in the text. Without visual evidence, the qualitative claims about layer behavior cannot be properly evaluated.

8. **Prior Work Coverage**: The paper fails to cite or discuss relevant prior work on deep recursive networks. For example, Chen et al.'s "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (2015) appears to be directly related but is not mentioned, suggesting inadequate literature review.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a clear experimental comparison and some interesting qualitative insights, it contains multiple fundamental errors and omissions that undermine its technical credibility. The critical terminology error (misusing "RNN" for recursive networks) is particularly problematic as it misrepresents the field. The formatting inconsistencies, reproducibility issues, and omission of key details (like exact hyperparameters and finetuning discussion) indicate a lack of meticulous attention to detail expected at NIPS.

The paper's contributions are modest relative to the errors present. While the experimental results are promising, the fundamental terminology confusion and methodological omissions prevent the paper from meeting NIPS standards for technical rigor and reproducibility.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical and methodological flaws, despite the paper's potential value in the area. The errors are not minor oversights but fundamental issues that would mislead readers and hinder reproducibility.
