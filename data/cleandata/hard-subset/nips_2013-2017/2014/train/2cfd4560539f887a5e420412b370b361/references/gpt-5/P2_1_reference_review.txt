Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures. Each layer performs standard bottom-up composition over a parse tree (with shared WL/WR per layer), while also receiving a vertical input from the corresponding node representation in the previous layer via a V matrix, yielding “depth in space” in addition to the usual “depth in structure.” The first layer “unties” leaves and internal nodes (separate Wxh, Whh) to enable rectified linear units (ReLUs) and the use of large, fixed, pretrained word vectors without quadratic parameter blow-up. Training uses dropout (with shared masks per sentence per layer), AdaGrad, early stopping, and a small additional L2 penalty on activations and weights to stabilize ReLUs. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all nodes. Deep RNNs consistently outperform shallow RNNs of approximately equal parameter count, and the best model surpasses previously published results (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors) on fine-grained and binary sentiment. The authors include qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of composition (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is a clear and well-motivated extension of recursive nets. The vertical connections and per-layer parameters are standard mechanisms for stacking. The learning setup and regularization choices are reasonable for ReLUs on trees. The training protocol (AdaGrad, early stopping, shared dropout masks) is plausible; initialization of Whh near identity is sensible for stability in trees.
- Empirical support: The central claim—depth in space improves over a shallow recursive model at comparable parameter budgets—is directly tested by holding total parameters roughly constant across depth/width configurations. This is a strong design choice and supports the main quantitative claim. However, statistical significance is not reported, and the absolute gains over the strongest baselines are not shown here (tables referenced but not included), limiting certainty about the magnitude and robustness of improvements.
- Ablations: The paper attributes gains to depth, pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout. While the shallow-vs-deep comparison isolates the benefit of depth under the same ingredients, the effect sizes of untying leaves vs internals, ReLUs vs tanh, pretrained vs learned embeddings, and dropout vs L2 are not disentangled systematically (beyond one preliminary note on dropout). More thorough ablations would strengthen causal claims.
- Correctness of qualitative analyses: The input-perturbation and nearest-neighbor probes are sensible and align with the stated hypothesis, though they remain anecdotal; more systematic probing (e.g., controlled linguistic phenomena across many examples) would be stronger.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is clearly explained, and the equations are straightforward. The training details are mostly sufficient to reproduce results (optimizer, rates, epochs, batch size, dropout rates, initialization strategy). A few details are missing or garbled (e.g., “All other weights are initialized as .” seems truncated; precise initialization distribution is unclear; exact parameter counts per model configuration are not reported in the text provided). The motivation for shared dropout masks is explained. The choice to attach supervision only to the final layer is justified for interpretability.

Originality
- The idea of stacking recurrent layers (depth in time) is known; this paper brings an analogous stacking to recursive networks (depth in structure plus depth in space), with a clean formulation, and demonstrates empirical value. The untying of leaves vs internals to enable ReLUs with large fixed embeddings is a practical twist. While the conceptual novelty is incremental (a natural extension), the combination and the empirical demonstration on SST appear to be new and useful at the time of writing. Related work on multiplicative and matrix-vector recursive models is discussed; the relation to gated tree architectures (e.g., TreeLSTM) is not covered, but may postdate or be contemporaneous.

Significance
- The work addresses a central problem in NLP—compositionality—and advances the state of the art on a widely used benchmark, which many will find valuable. Beyond numbers, the paper offers insight into how depth might disentangle different aspects of composition, which can influence future model design. A limitation is evaluation on a single dataset with gold parse trees; broader validation (other tree-structured tasks, robustness to automatic parsing, or non-sentiment tasks) would increase impact. Still, the demonstrated gains and the clarity of the architectural idea suggest that other researchers can build on this.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and well-motivated architectural extension; easy to implement.
- Fair shallow-vs-deep comparisons at fixed parameter budgets support the main claim.
- Strong empirical results on SST; state-of-the-art reported.
- Practical training recipe for tree ReLUs (shared dropout, activation penalty, identity-leaning init).
- Qualitative analyses offer plausible evidence that different layers capture complementary phenomena.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablation to isolate contributions of untying, ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, and vertical connections beyond depth alone.
- No statistical significance or variance reporting; single split only.
- Comparisons to prior baselines may not be fully controlled (e.g., do RNTN/DCNN baselines also use pretrained embeddings and comparable regularization?).
- Evaluation confined to a single dataset and task with gold parses; no analysis of computational cost or training time.
- Minor clarity issues in initialization details and missing table context in the text provided.

Questions/requests for authors
- Please report parameter counts per configuration and training/inference time.
- Provide ablations: (a) tanh vs ReLU, (b) tied vs untied leaves/internals, (c) with/without vertical V connections, (d) with/without dropout, (e) fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings.
- Clarify weight initialization for “other weights” (distribution and scale).
- Report confidence intervals or significance tests on test accuracy.
- Discuss how performance degrades with automatic parses vs gold.

Reproducibility
- Mostly adequate details (optimizer, LR, batch size, epochs, dropout grid, early stopping, embedding source). Missing exact initialization for non-Whh weights, exact parameter counts, and the precise dev-tuned hyperparameters for the best model. Code release or a more detailed supplement would help.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Rationale: The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates that stacking recursive layers yields consistent improvements over strong shallow baselines at fixed parameter budgets, reaching state-of-the-art on SST. The novelty is incremental but well-executed, and the qualitative analysis is insightful. Broader evaluation and more thorough ablations would strengthen the case, but overall the contribution is likely to be useful to the community.

Confidence: Medium (further details on missing tables and ablations could shift the assessment).
