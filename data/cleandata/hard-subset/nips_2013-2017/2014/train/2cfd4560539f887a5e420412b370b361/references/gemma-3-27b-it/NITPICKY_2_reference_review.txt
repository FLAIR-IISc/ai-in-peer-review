The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), extending recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation stems from analogous advancements in recurrent neural networks, where distinguishing between depth in time and space has proven beneficial. The authors apply DRNNs to fine-grained sentiment detection on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improved performance over shallow RNNs and existing state-of-the-art methods. Qualitative analysis suggests distinct layers capture different aspects of compositional semantics.

While the core idea of extending recursive networks in a “deep” manner is conceptually sound and the empirical results are positive, the paper suffers from a surprisingly large number of minor flaws and inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality. The presentation, while generally understandable, lacks meticulousness, and several methodological choices are not adequately justified or thoroughly explored. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The DRNN architecture represents a reasonable extension of existing recursive neural network models, offering a potentially more powerful framework for compositional representation learning.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The paper demonstrates improved performance on the SST dataset, achieving new state-of-the-art results.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase experiments offer some insight into the learned representations, suggesting different layers capture different aspects of meaning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Methodological Details – Untying Leaves and Internals:** The justification for "untying" leaves and internals feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the benefits regarding rectifier activations and dimensionality are mentioned, the claim that it allows the use of large pretrained word vectors *without* a quadratic dependence is incorrect. The weight matrices remain |h|x|x| and |h|x|h|, still scaling with the word vector dimensionality. The explanation is unclear and lacks a proper derivation.
* **Regularization:** The regularization strategy is described as an *additional* L2 penalty *in addition to* dropout. This is unusual and not clearly explained why both are necessary. Furthermore, the L2 penalty is described as "small and fixed" without any mention of hyperparameter tuning.
* **Network Training – Learning Rate:** A fixed learning rate of 0.01 is used. While AdaGrad is employed, the sensitivity of the model to different learning rates isn't explored, which is crucial for deep networks.
* **Network Training – Initialization:** The initialization of recursive weights (0.5I + noise) is described, but the rationale is weak. It aims for an initial "mean of children" representation, but why this is preferred over other initialization schemes isn’t justified.
* **Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies. For instance, the impact of the untying mechanism alone is not evaluated. It's not clear whether the performance gains are solely attributable to depth or a combination of depth and untying.
* **Presentation & Clarity:** Several sentences are awkwardly phrased and could benefit from rewriting. For instance, “This is especially important for deep learning, since it allows a richer supervised error signal…” feels redundant. The formatting in Table 1a is strange - the depth and width are denoted by ` and |h|, but the table header uses different symbols.
* **Related Work:** While related work is mentioned, the discussion feels cursory. Specifically, the connection to hierarchical attention networks, which also aim to capture hierarchical representations, is missing.
* **Minor Details:** The phrase "vanishing gradients associated with deep neural networks" in the introduction is a bit oversimplified. Modern techniques like ReLU and batch normalization address this issue, rendering it less of a primary concern. There are also minor grammatical errors throughout the text.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

* The sheer number of minor flaws, while individually not devastating, collectively suggest a lack of meticulousness in the experimental setup and presentation.
* The justification for several methodological choices is weak or absent.
* The lack of comprehensive ablation studies hinders understanding the true contribution of depth.

**For:**

* The DRNN architecture is a novel and potentially valuable extension to recursive neural networks.
* The empirical results are positive, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the SST dataset.
* The qualitative analysis provides some initial insights into the learned representations.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the proposed architecture and the promising empirical results, I am leaning towards **rejection**. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The numerous minor flaws and insufficiently justified methodological choices raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the findings. While the idea is interesting, the execution needs significant refinement before it can be considered a substantial contribution to the field. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly through more rigorous experimentation and clearer explanations, would substantially improve the paper's quality.
