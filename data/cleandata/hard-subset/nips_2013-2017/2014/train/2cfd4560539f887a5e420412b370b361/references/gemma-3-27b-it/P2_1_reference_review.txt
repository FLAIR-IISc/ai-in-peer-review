## Review of Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), extending traditional Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers. The core idea is to model hierarchical compositionality in language more effectively by allowing different layers to learn different aspects of phrase structure and semantics. The authors evaluate their model on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improved performance over shallow RNNs and existing state-of-the-art methods. They further provide qualitative analyses showing that different layers capture different notions of similarity and handle different aspects of compositionality.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundation of RNNs for NLP (Socher et al., 2013) and connects it to recent advancements in deep learning, particularly the distinction between depth in time (RNNs) and depth in space (deep feedforward networks) (El Hihi & Bengio, 2014).  It addresses a key limitation of standard RNNs – their inability to fully capture hierarchical compositional structure – by drawing inspiration from deep recurrent networks (Schmidhuber, 1992). The work also relates to prior work on compositional semantics using tensor networks (Socher et al., 2012) and distributional semantics (Mikolov et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of stacking recursive layers to create DRNNs is a reasonable and interesting extension of existing RNN architectures. It addresses a potential limitation of shallow RNNs in modeling complex compositional semantics.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates statistically significant performance improvements on the SST dataset, achieving new state-of-the-art results. The ablation studies clearly show the benefits of increasing depth.
* **Detailed Qualitative Analysis:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase experiments provide valuable insights into how different layers of the DRNN learn and represent information. These analyses help to support the claim that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
* **Thorough Experimentation:**  The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons to several strong baselines, careful parameter tuning (dropout, learning rate, regularization), and consideration of word vector initialization.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed architecture and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the SST dataset is well-suited for evaluating compositional semantics, it is a relatively specific task.  It would be beneficial to see the DRNN evaluated on other tasks that require compositional reasoning, such as question answering or textual entailment.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of deep learning models is often sensitive to hyperparameter choices. The paper reports some tuning, but a more systematic exploration of the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit size) would strengthen the results.  The optimal depth seems to be quite sensitive, and more exploration of why performance degrades with too much depth would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Deep models are typically more computationally expensive to train than shallow models. The paper does not discuss the computational resources required to train the DRNNs, which could be a barrier to adoption for researchers with limited resources.
* **Untying Leaves & Internals Justification:** While the "untied" approach is presented as beneficial, the rationale given (rectifier activation units and managing dimensionality) feels slightly weak. A more thorough explanation or additional experiments comparing tied vs. untied configurations could strengthen this aspect.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension of RNNs.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the DRNNs.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* The computational cost of DRNNs is not discussed.
* Some aspects of the design choices (e.g., untying leaves and internals) lack strong justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The proposed DRNN architecture is a promising approach to modeling hierarchical structure in language, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor points:

1.  Briefly discuss the computational cost of training the DRNNs.
2.  Consider adding a sentence or two to the discussion of the "untied" configuration to strengthen the justification.
3.  Acknowledge the limitation of evaluating on a single dataset and suggest potential directions for future work to address this.
