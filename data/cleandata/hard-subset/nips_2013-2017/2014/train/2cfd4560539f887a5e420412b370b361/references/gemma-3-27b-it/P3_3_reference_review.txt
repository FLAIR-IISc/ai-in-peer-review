## Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling extension to recursive neural networks (RNNs) by introducing depth through stacked layers, creating Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs). The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), achieving state-of-the-art performance. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. While the core idea isn’t radically new – it’s a natural progression from deep learning in other domains – the application to recursive structures and the insightful analysis of layer-wise representations make this a valuable contribution. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on the foundation of RNNs, which recursively combine structured inputs (like parse trees) to generate representations. It notes the success of deep learning with stacked layers in other areas (e.g., deep recurrent networks) and proposes applying this concept to RNNs, resulting in DRNNs. The key idea is to learn compositional representations hierarchically, with each layer building upon the abstractions learned by the previous layer.  This is framed as combining “depth in structure” (RNN recursion) and “depth in space” (stacked layers).  The paper appropriately cites relevant work in RNNs ([6, 7, 8]), deep recurrent networks ([10, 11, 12]), and related compositional models ([16, 17, 18]). It positions itself within this landscape by offering a specific approach to combining hierarchical structure with depth.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNNs consistently outperform shallow RNNs and previous state-of-the-art methods (RNTN, Paragraph Vectors) on the SST dataset. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Motivation and Methodology:** The paper effectively articulates the rationale behind DRNNs and explains the architecture in a clear and concise manner. The diagrams (Figures 1 & 2) are helpful.
* **Layer-wise Analysis:** The qualitative experiments (input perturbation and nearest neighbor analysis) are particularly insightful. These analyses provide evidence that different layers indeed capture different aspects of compositionality and meaning. The observation that the second layer focuses on sentiment intensity while the final layer captures sentiment direction is a key finding.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address important practical aspects of training deep networks, such as the use of pre-trained word vectors, dropout, and an L2 regularizer to prevent exploding gradients. The discussion of rectifier activation units and the untying of leaves and internals is also well-justified.
* **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of the standard SST benchmark and comparison to strong baselines strengthens the validity of the results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely focused on the SST dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see results on other tasks or datasets to assess the generalizability of the approach.  The tree structure reliance might be a limitation for languages with freer word order.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters like dropout rate, but a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter search process and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of *why* DRNNs perform better. Exploring the representational capacity of deeper vs. shallower networks could provide further insight.
* **Computational Cost:** Deep networks are often more computationally expensive to train. The paper doesn’t adequately address the computational cost of DRNNs compared to shallow RNNs.  Discussing training time and memory requirements would be helpful.



**4. Specific Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.3 (Equation 4):** The role of  V(i) is somewhat glossed over.  Is this a shared weight matrix across all nodes in layer *i*?  Clarifying this would be helpful.
* **Input Perturbation (Section 3.2):** The choice of words for perturbation ("coolest", "good", "average", "bad", "worst") feels somewhat arbitrary.  A more systematic approach to selecting perturbation words, perhaps based on a sentiment lexicon or word embeddings, could strengthen this analysis.
* **Nearest Neighbor Phrases (Section 3.2):** While the observations are insightful, it might be helpful to quantify these findings. For example, could you measure the sentiment similarity between the query phrase and its nearest neighbors?




**5. Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical performance on a challenging benchmark.
* Clear and well-motivated methodology.
* Insightful qualitative analysis that supports the core claim.
* Addresses practical challenges of training deep networks.
* A logical and valuable extension of existing work on RNNs.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of evaluation.
* Lack of detailed hyperparameter analysis.
* Limited theoretical justification.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

*   Briefly discuss the computational cost of DRNNs.
*   Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process.
*   Consider adding a discussion about the limitations of the tree structure dependency.
*   Clarify the role of V(i) in Equation 4.




This paper is a significant contribution to the field of compositional semantics and has the potential to inspire further research in deep learning for natural language processing.
