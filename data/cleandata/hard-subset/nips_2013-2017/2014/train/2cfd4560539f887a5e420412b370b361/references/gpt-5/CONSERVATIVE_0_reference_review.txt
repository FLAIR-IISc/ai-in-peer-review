Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of parse-tree structures. The key ideas are: (i) distinguishing leaves from internal nodes (“untied” weights) to map words into a phrase space, (ii) using ReLU activations, dropout, and AdaGrad for training stability and regularization, and (iii) feeding the representation of each node at layer i to its counterpart at layer i+1. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all tree nodes, and claims state-of-the-art performance on fine-grained sentiment, modestly exceeding prior recursive models (MV-RNN, RNTN) and DCNN/Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on classic recursive neural networks and the RNTN line (Socher et al.), and adapts the “depth in space” idea from stacked recurrent nets to tree-structured computation. The “untie leaves vs. internals” design reduces parameter blow-up with large pretrained embeddings and enables ReLUs, which are established techniques in deep feed-forward/conv nets. The novelty is largely architectural combination and training choices rather than a new composition operator.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for separating word and phrase spaces and for adding depth across layers.
- Thorough description of training details (dropout, AdaGrad, initialization, early stopping), which aids reproducibility.
- Sensible parameter budgeting: when increasing depth, width is reduced to keep parameter count roughly constant.
- Empirical performance is competitive; reported fine-grained accuracy slightly improves over prior published baselines.
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) are interesting and consistent with the design intuition.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: Only SST is used; no corroboration on other tree-structured or sentence-level tasks. Claims about “depth” generality rest on a single dataset.
- Attribution of gains is unclear: several confounded changes (pretrained word vectors, ReLU, dropout, leaf/internal untying, activation L2) are introduced, yet ablations are insufficient to isolate depth as the cause. The observed improvements over the authors’ shallow RNN are small.
- Baseline mismatch: Comparisons rely on previously published numbers with different training setups (e.g., embeddings, fine-tuning, regularization). RNTN/DCNN may not be using the same pretrained vectors, ReLU, or dropout, making fairness questionable.
- No statistical significance or variance across runs; improvements of ~1–2 points may fall within run-to-run variability.
- Computational aspects are omitted: no wall-clock, FLOPs, or memory comparison; deeper stacks on trees may be considerably slower/inflexible than strong sequence/CNN alternatives.
- Some design choices are asserted but weakly justified: output connected only to the final layer (vs. multi-layer supervision), unusual L2 penalty on activations, and “shared dropout masks per sentence/layer” without comparison to standard node-wise dropout.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the evidence that stacking recursive layers (as opposed to better initialization/regularization) is the primary driver is inconclusive. The training details are helpful, yet key controls are missing:
- No ablation for tied vs. untied leaves/internals.
- No comparison of ReLU vs. tanh holding everything else fixed.
- No study of fixed vs. fine-tuned word vectors.
- No depth ablation under identical conditions with significance testing and multiple random seeds.
- No reimplementation of key baselines under the same setting (same word embeddings, dropout, optimizer).

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The methodology is described clearly, and relations to recurrent nets are articulated. However:
- Precise parameter counts per configuration are not reported; “same number of parameters” is claimed but not quantified.
- It is ambiguous whether supervision is applied at all nodes only in the top layer or also in lower layers for auxiliary losses (the text suggests top-layer-only).
- Rationale for activation L2 and the specific choice of shared dropout masks would benefit from justification or empirical comparison.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking recursive layers is a natural extension of stacked recurrent nets; untying leaves vs. internals and using ReLUs/dropout are established tools. The qualitative analyses are nice but not novel. The contribution is an incremental architectural variant.

Significance
Modest. The reported gains on SST are small and not backed by significance tests or broader validation. Practical impact is unclear given reliance on parse trees and potential computational cost. Without stronger, controlled evidence, it is hard to conclude that “depth in space” for trees is the key ingredient.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Provide controlled ablations: (a) depth 1 vs. 2–4 with identical settings; (b) tied vs. untied; (c) ReLU vs. tanh; (d) with/without dropout; (e) fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings. Report mean±std over multiple seeds; run significance tests.
- Re-run a strong recursive baseline (e.g., RNTN) under your training recipe (same pretrained vectors, optimizer, dropout) to ensure fair comparisons.
- Report exact parameter counts, training/inference time per sentence, and memory footprint for each depth.
- Evaluate on at least one additional task/dataset (e.g., SST-2 sentence-level, another sentiment dataset, or a paraphrase/entailment task) to assess generality.
- Analyze sensitivity to sentence/tree depth and to parse errors; consider dependency trees.
- Justify the activation L2 penalty and shared dropout mask; compare to standard choices.

Pros
- Clear, coherent presentation and reasonable engineering.
- Competitive performance with small improvements over prior results.
- Interesting qualitative layer analyses.

Cons
- Evidence that depth is the main source of improvement is weak; ablations and fair baselines are lacking.
- Limited experimental validation (single dataset; no significance; no variance).
- Computational and practical considerations not addressed.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is plausible and the paper is well-written, but the empirical validation does not convincingly isolate the benefit of stacking recursive layers, and the scope is too narrow for publication at NIPS without stronger, controlled experiments and broader evaluation.
