Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep-RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of a given tree structure, distinguishing “depth in structure” (from tree recursion) from “depth in space” (from stacking). Each layer performs standard tree composition within the layer, and nodes also pass their hidden states vertically to their counterparts in the next layer. The first layer “unties” leaves and internal nodes with separate transformations from word space to phrase space and between phrase representations, enabling the use of rectified linear units. The output layer connects only to the top recursive layer to encourage higher-level abstractions. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks per sentence per layer, and small L2 penalties on weights and activations for stability.

The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with 5-way sentiment classification and binary sentiment derived from the 5-way model’s posteriors. The authors compare shallow vs deep recursive architectures under a fixed parameter budget, report that depth improves performance up to a point, and achieve state-of-the-art results compared to baselines including RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN [8,16], Paragraph Vectors [17], and DCNN [18]. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path from a modified leaf to the root, and layerwise nearest-neighbor phrase retrieval) suggest different layers capture distinct aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on recursive/structured neural models [6–9,14,16] and “deep in time” stacked recurrent networks [10–12]. The contribution is to bring stacking to tree-structured recursion, with design choices (untied leaf/internal parameters, rectifiers, dropout) that differ from earlier tree models such as MV-RNN and RNTN.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and standard for tree composition, extended with inter-layer connections. The untying between leaves and internals is well-motivated (enabling large fixed word vectors and rectifiers). Training details (AdaGrad, dropout, initialization with 0.5I to average children) are reasonable and likely to stabilize learning. The evaluation uses a single dataset but includes multiple architecture depths at matched parameter counts. However:
  - The reported gains combine several changes (pretrained 300d word embeddings, rectifiers, dropout, untied weights, stacking). Ablations isolating the effect of stacking vs other choices are limited. The single reference to a shallow RNN improvement suggests some factors (pretraining/rectifiers/dropout) may account for a large fraction of gains over [8].
  - No variance across runs or statistical significance is reported.
  - Fairness of baseline comparison is not fully controlled (e.g., whether RNTN or MV-RNN are also allowed pretrained embeddings; binary results are derived from the 5-way model, which is conservative but complicates comparisons to models trained directly for binary).
  - The addition of an L2 penalty on activations is a nonstandard regularizer that should be further justified or compared against alternatives (e.g., gradient clipping).
- Experiments are otherwise carefully set up (fixed parameter budget across depth, early stopping on dev, shared dropout masks per sentence/layer). The qualitative analyses are thoughtful and align with the architectural intuition.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. The model equations are straightforward, and training details are explicit. The intuition for depth vs width and for layer-specific roles is well conveyed through qualitative probes.
- Minor issues: some notation (e.g., superscripts/subscripts in Eq. 3–4) could be streamlined; some references lack complete bibliographic details; it would help to explicitly tabulate parameter counts when comparing depths and to include run-time or complexity considerations. Statistical reporting (means/std over seeds) would strengthen conclusions.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers over trees to introduce “depth in space” appears to be a novel and natural extension relative to prior tree models (RAE, MV-RNN, RNTN) and to deep recurrent models in time [10–12]. The untying of leaves/internals with rectifiers in tree composition is also a useful design. The qualitative diagnostics are insightful. Overall, the contribution is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
- The model achieves new state-of-the-art on SST at the time of the compared baselines and shows that depth (at fixed parameter budget) can help on tree-structured composition. The approach is likely to be adopted in other structured NLP tasks. However, the empirical scope is limited to a single benchmark, and stronger ablations are needed to clearly attribute gains to stacking rather than to other training choices.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of stacked tree-structured networks; well-motivated untying with rectifiers.
- Parameter-matched comparisons showing depth helps.
- State-of-the-art results on SST against strong baselines.
- Practical training recipe (AdaGrad, dropout, initialization) with details enabling reproduction.
- Qualitative analyses that support the layered-composition hypothesis.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations to disentangle contributions of depth vs pretrained embeddings, rectifiers, dropout, and untying.
- Single-dataset evaluation; no cross-task validation of compositional benefits.
- No reporting of variance/significance across seeds.
- Potential fairness issues versus prior baselines regarding pretrained embeddings and binary evaluation protocol.
- Incomplete references and minor notation/clarity issues.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide ablations isolating stacking (e.g., shallow vs deep with identical activation/regularization/embeddings), and variance over multiple seeds?
- How do results change when fine-tuning word embeddings? And when training a binary classifier directly?
- How sensitive are results to the shared vs unshared dropout masks across tree nodes?
- Can you compare the L2-activation penalty to alternatives such as gradient clipping or layer norm?
- Any results on additional datasets (e.g., other tree-structured classification tasks) to support generality?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated extension of recursive models with demonstrated empirical gains.
- Solid engineering and training insights; reproducible details.
- Useful qualitative analyses of layer behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; improvements may partly stem from training choices rather than stacking per se.
- Limited experimental breadth and lack of statistical robustness.
- Some baselines may not be directly comparable in terms of external data and training protocol.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The paper presents a clear, technically sound extension with empirically supported benefits and informative analyses, though broader evaluation and stronger ablations would strengthen the case.
