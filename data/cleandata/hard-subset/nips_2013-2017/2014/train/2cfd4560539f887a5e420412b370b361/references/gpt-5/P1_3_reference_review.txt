Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other to separate “depth in structure” (tree recursion) from “depth in space” (stacked layers). Within each layer, composition over a binary parse tree is performed with shared left/right matrices, and across layers, each node passes its representation vertically via a learned V matrix. The first layer “unties” leaves from internal nodes so that word vectors (x) live in a different space from phrase vectors (h), enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and large, fixed, pretrained word embeddings without quadratic parameter growth. The output layer connects only to the top recursive layer to encourage high-level abstraction. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout (with masks shared across nodes in a layer/sentence), small L2 on weights and activations for stability, and early stopping. Evaluation on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) shows that, for matched parameter budgets, deeper-and-narrower DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets, and that the best DRNN achieves new state-of-the-art accuracy on fine-grained (5-way) and binary (pos/neg) sentiment classification compared to previously reported results for RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a root path; nearest-neighbor phrases per layer) suggest layers capture different aspects of compositionality (lexical dominance vs syntactic type vs sentiment polarity/intensity). This work is well-situated with respect to recursive models [6–9,16] and deep/stacked recurrent models [10–12], and leverages advances in embeddings [2,19] and regularization [20–22].

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea: introduce vertical connections across stacked recursive layers to realize depth in space in tree-structured models.
- Thorough practical recipe: untie leaves vs internals, use ReLUs, fixed high-dimensional pretrained word vectors, dropout with shared masks, AdaGrad, and careful initialization.
- Solid empirical evidence on SST with a fair protocol (phrase-level supervision, standard split), showing consistent gains of deep over shallow for fixed parameter budgets and new SOTA at the time reported.
- Useful qualitative analyses that illuminate what different layers capture.
- Well written and generally easy to follow; training details are specified.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited ablations: Gains likely come from multiple choices (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, untying, stacking). The paper isolates dropout’s contribution in one case, but does not disentangle the effect of untying, ReLU vs tanh, fixing vs fine-tuning word vectors, or output-only-top-layer vs multi-head supervision.
- Statistical robustness: No variance across random seeds or significance testing; early stopping across 200 epochs may yield variance that matters at the 1–2% level.
- Fairness of comparisons: Some baselines (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) may not exploit phrase-level supervision; RNTN/MV-RNN do. Clarify which baselines are trained with phrase-level labels and whether they used pretrained embeddings. Also clarify that parameter budgets are matched when comparing shallow vs deep, but not necessarily between your best DRNN and other external baselines.
- Generality: Results are only on SST. The motivation (compositionality) suggests applicability to other structured NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, entailment), but no evidence is provided.
- Optimization/stability: The model uses L2 on activations to prevent explosions; common alternatives (gradient clipping, norm constraints, normalization) aren’t explored. The choice to connect supervision only to the final layer may hamper gradient flow without phrase-level labels in other settings.

Quality
Technically sound and complete. The training protocol is appropriate and sufficiently detailed to reproduce the main results. Claims about performance improvements are supported; the qualitative analyses bolster the interpretation that different layers learn different functions. The main gap is a more systematic ablation to substantiate which design choices are essential.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Figures help. Suggestions:
- Unify notation: x vs h is sometimes overloaded across sections; keep consistent symbols for leaf/phrase spaces across layers.
- Provide explicit complexity analysis: cost per sentence vs number of layers, hidden size, and tree nodes; report training time.
- Brief pseudocode or a schematic of “backprop-through-structure-and-space” would aid reproducibility.
- Specify parameter counts for configurations in Table 1a, and list exact best hyperparameters (dropout rates per layer if different).

Originality
Moderately novel. Stacking to create depth in space is well known for sequential RNNs; applying the same idea to recursive (tree-structured) nets with vertical per-node connections, together with the practical untying and ReLU recipe, is a natural but useful extension. The qualitative analyses are insightful. Related work is adequately cited for the era.

Significance
Empirically meaningful: consistent gains over shallow recursive nets for matched parameter budgets and improved SOTA on a widely used benchmark. Methodologically, this paper will likely influence practitioners building tree-structured models by demonstrating that “deep-in-space” helps beyond “deep-in-structure,” and by providing a training recipe that works without pretraining. Broader impact is limited by the focus on a single dataset; still, the approach is general.

Questions for authors
- Please provide ablations isolating the effects of: (a) stacking without untying/relu; (b) untying with tanh vs ReLU; (c) fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings; (d) with vs without dropout; (e) output connected to all layers vs last only.
- How sensitive are results to the shared dropout mask per layer/sentence? Did you try independent masks per node?
- Do you observe high variance across runs? Please report mean±std over multiple seeds.
- Are parameter counts matched against RNTN/MV-RNN in your best DRNN comparison? Did those baselines use pretrained embeddings?
- Could you report root-only (sentence-level) results for parity with methods that do not use phrase labels?
- Any experiments on other datasets or tasks to demonstrate generality?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural extension with consistent empirical gains.
- State-of-the-art results on SST and informative qualitative analyses.
- Clear exposition and practical training guidance.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete ablation and limited robustness analysis.
- Single-task evaluation; unclear generality beyond sentiment.
- Some fairness ambiguities in baseline comparisons.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: High
