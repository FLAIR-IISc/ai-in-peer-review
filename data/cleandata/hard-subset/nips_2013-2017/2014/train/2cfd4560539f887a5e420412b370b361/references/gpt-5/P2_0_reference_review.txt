Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (Deep-RNNs) that stack multiple tree-structured recursive layers (“depth in space”) on top of the usual depth in structure induced by parse trees. The authors also “untie” parameters between leaves and internal nodes to map word vectors (X) into a separate phrase space (H), enabling the use of rectified linear units and large fixed pre-trained embeddings without quadratic parameter growth. Training uses Adagrad, dropout (shared across nodes within a layer for a sentence), and a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations. Evaluation on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) shows that: (i) a shallow RNN with these engineering choices improves substantially over prior shallow baselines; (ii) stacking recursive layers improves performance at fixed parameter budgets, up to an optimal depth; (iii) the best model achieves state-of-the-art on fine-grained and binary sentiment at the time of writing. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path and nearest neighbor phrases per layer) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Quality
- Soundness: The model is a straightforward, well-motivated extension of tree-structured RNNs, with clear recurrence equations and training regime. The untied leaf/internal parametrization is sensible and its benefits (parameter efficiency with large embeddings, compatibility with ReLUs) are explained.
- Empirics: The SST setup is standard and strong baselines are included (RNN, MV-RNN/RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN). The authors hold total parameter counts approximately fixed when varying depth, supporting the claim that depth (not just capacity) helps. Hyperparameters and optimization choices are described in useful detail (pretrained word vectors, dropout grid, learning rate, optimizer, epochs, minibatch, initialization).
- Missing pieces: Ablations are partial. While the paper shows a shallow RNN gains from pretrained word vectors + ReLUs + dropout, it does not fully disentangle contributions of untying vs ReLUs vs dropout vs embeddings in the deep setting. No statistical significance tests are reported. Results are limited to a single dataset/task. Quantitative details (exact scores for all configurations) are referenced but not fully shown here; please ensure Table 1 reports these comprehensively.
- Analysis: The qualitative analyses are insightful but anecdotal (one path, a few neighbor lists). Stronger evidence (e.g., layer-wise probing, correlation with sentiment intensity) would bolster claims about roles of different layers.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is articulated cleanly, equations are precise, and training choices are justified. The rationale for untying and for connecting supervision only to the top layer is clear. Visuals (Figures 1–2) help. Minor suggestions: explicitly state the source of parse trees in SST; include parameter counts per configuration; report variance over multiple runs or seeds; clarify the exact dropout masking scheme and whether it differs across layers.

Originality
- Stacking tree-structured recursive layers appears novel and is a natural extension of “deep RNNs” in time to tree structures. The untied leaf/internal parametrization is a useful design decision in this context, though related ideas exist in recurrent nets and prior tree models. Using ReLUs and dropout in tree RNNs is incremental but meaningful. The qualitative layer-wise analyses are a nice contribution. Related work is appropriately cited, and the differences are explained.

Significance
- The paper advances state-of-the-art performance on a widely used benchmark with a conceptually simple, general architecture likely to be adopted by practitioners. The idea of depth across space for trees is general and may transfer to other structured NLP tasks (parsing, entailment, semantic relations). That said, the empirical scope is narrow (one dataset), and the analysis does not yet establish broader benefits beyond sentiment. Showing gains on additional tree-structured tasks would substantially increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architectural contribution (stacked tree-RNNs).
- Strong empirical results with careful training and regularization; SOTA on SST.
- Sensible parameter budgeting when comparing depths.
- Practical design (fixed pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout), likely to be useful for others.
- Initial qualitative evidence that layers capture distinct aspects of composition.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset/task; no cross-domain or non-sentiment results.
- Incomplete ablations; contributions of components not fully disentangled.
- No statistical significance testing or multiple-run variability.
- Qualitative analyses are limited in scope; more systematic probing would help.
- The decision to supervise only the top layer is reasonable, but an experiment with intermediate losses could test the claimed advantages and might improve optimization.

Questions for authors
- Please report mean ± std over multiple seeds and perform significance testing against RNTN/DCNN.
- Can you provide a full ablation (untied vs tied, ReLU vs tanh, dropout on/off, fixed vs finetuned embeddings) for both shallow and deep models?
- How sensitive are results to the shared-dropout masking choice? Did you try per-node dropout or variational/dropconnect variants?
- What is the computational overhead (training time) versus a shallow RNN or RNTN?
- Any preliminary results on other tree-structured tasks?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with clear motivation; improves SOTA on a canonical benchmark.
- Good clarity and reasonable experimental rigor for NeurIPS standards at submission time.
- Likely to influence follow-up work on tree-structured deep models.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow empirical scope; limited ablations and statistical rigor.
- Evidence for layer-wise roles is mostly anecdotal.

Recommendation
- Verdict: Weak accept.
- Rationale: The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates meaningful improvements with a broadly applicable architectural idea. Expanding experiments and ablations would strengthen it further, but the current contribution meets NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
