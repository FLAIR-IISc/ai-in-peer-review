Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal node parameters, use rectified linear units and dropout, and train with AdaGrad while keeping pretrained word vectors fixed. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), they report that deeper (but narrower) recursive models outperform shallow counterparts under a roughly fixed parameter budget and achieve new state-of-the-art results on fine-grained sentiment. They include qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggesting different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive nets over trees (Goller & Küchler, Socher et al.) and the notion of “depth in time vs. space” explored in deep recurrent networks (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). Compared to prior recursive models like MV-RNN and RNTN, the main novelty is stacking recursive layers and untying leaf/internal parameters with ReLU and dropout. Empirically, the evaluation focuses on SST, comparing to previously reported baselines (BINB, RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN).

Strengths
- Clear idea and architecture: stacking recursive layers is a natural extension; the untying of leaves vs. internals is well motivated.
- Solid engineering choices: ReLU, dropout with shared masks across tree nodes, AdaGrad, stability via small activation penalty and identity-initialized structural weights.
- Fair attempt to control capacity: deeper models are narrower to approximately fix total parameters; early stopping on dev set.
- Empirical gains on SST, including a consistent (if modest) boost from increasing depth; qualitative analyses are thoughtful and align with intuition about hierarchical composition.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Insufficient ablations to isolate contributions. The largest jump comes from using pretrained embeddings + ReLU + dropout in a shallow RNN (48.1 vs. 43.2 in prior tanh RNN). The incremental gain attributable to depth appears small (e.g., ~1–1.5 points). A thorough ablation is missing:
  - depth vs. width under fixed parameters (beyond a few points on Table 1);
  - effect of untying leaves/internals;
  - ReLU vs. tanh with identical regularization;
  - dropout vs. L2/none;
  - fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings.
- Statistical significance is not reported. Given small margins between models, significance tests are needed.
- Evaluation on a single dataset and single split. Claims about compositionality and general benefit of depth would be stronger with additional datasets (e.g., other sentiment datasets, paraphrase, entailment) and/or cross-validation.
- Fairness of baselines: Many older baselines did not use dropout/ReLU/pretrained embeddings. It is unclear whether a strong shallow RNN with the same modern training recipe would close most of the gap.
- Computational cost is not discussed. Stacking recursive layers increases training/inference cost; no analysis of time, memory, or convergence behavior vs. depth.
- The decision to connect supervision only to the top layer is not empirically justified; multi-layer supervision could alter conclusions about the benefits of depth.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; however, some important details are missing:
  - Exact parameter counts for the compared models;
  - Initialization details for all weights (only Whh initialization described);
  - Choice of parser and handling of parse errors;
  - Hyperparameter search ranges beyond dropout rate.
- Tables referred to in text are critical to understanding results; ensure they are self-contained with parameter counts and standard deviations.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward extension of deep RNN ideas to tree structures. The untying of leaves/internals and use of ReLU/dropout are not novel per se. The combination is reasonable, but the conceptual novelty is modest. The qualitative probes are interesting but not methodologically new.

Significance
- The reported SOTA on SST is potentially useful, but the practical impact is limited by:
  - narrow empirical scope (single dataset);
  - small gains attributable to depth after modernizing the baseline;
  - reliance on gold/binary parse trees (applicability depends on parser quality and domain).
- Stronger significance would come from demonstrating that depth yields robust improvements across tasks and under matched training recipes.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide ablations quantifying the marginal contribution of:
   - depth (e.g., 1–5 layers) with identical width and training recipe;
   - untying leaves vs. tied parameters;
   - ReLU vs. tanh; dropout on/off; fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings.
2. Are improvements statistically significant over the best shallow RNN with pretrained embeddings + ReLU + dropout?
3. What are the exact parameter counts and training/inference times for each depth?
4. How does performance change when supervising intermediate layers, or when using auxiliary losses?
5. Do results hold with automatically produced (non-binarized) parses and in out-of-domain settings?
6. Can you report results on at least one additional structured NLP task (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) to support claims about compositionality?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-executed extension with decent engineering and training stability.
- Empirical improvement over published baselines on a prominent benchmark.
- Qualitative analyses provide insight into layer-wise behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; stacking is expected and not deeply analyzed.
- Main gains appear due to modern training choices rather than depth.
- Single-dataset evaluation without significance tests; weak evidence for generality.
- Missing ablations and computational analysis.

Overall rating: Weak reject
Justification: The paper is well written and the idea is reasonable, but the evidence that “depth in space” for recursive nets is the key driver of improvement is not convincingly isolated from other factors. The empirical scope is narrow and lacks significance testing. With a more thorough ablation study, additional datasets, and analysis of cost/performance tradeoffs, I would be inclined toward acceptance.
