- Paper proposes stacked "deep" recursive neural networks that add spatial depth on top of tree-structured depth, with inter-layer connections, untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLUs, fixed pretrained embeddings, shared-mask dropout, Adagrad, and activation L2 regularization.  
- On Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) the Deep RNNs outperform similarly sized shallow RNNs and prior published systems, with qualitative analyses suggesting layer-wise specialization (lexico-syntactic lower, sentiment semantic upper).  
- Novelty is incremental: stacking recursive layers is a natural extension of tree models, with main contributions being empirical demonstration, untied leaf/internal parameterization, and a practical training recipe.  
- Strength: clear architectural idea disentangling structural vs. spatial depth with a concrete stacked formulation and inter-layer connections.  
- Strength: strong empirical results on SST with parameter-budget controls indicating real gains from depth.  
- Strength: useful practical training insights (ReLUs, shared-mask dropout, Adagrad, identity-biased Whh initialization).  
- Strength: qualitative analyses that provide intuition about layer specialization.  
- Strength: untied leaf/internal parameters are sensible and practically beneficial, especially with ReLUs and fixed embeddings.  
- Weakness: attribution of gains is unclear because many changes (untied parameters, ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, Adagrad, activation L2) are conflated; ablations are missing.  
- Weakness: comparisons to RNTN and DCNN may be unfair due to differing parameterizations, embedding fine-tuning, and supervision regimes; report parameter counts and run variance.  
- Weakness: using L2 on activations to prevent explosion is unconventional; gradient clipping or norm constraints would be a cleaner alternative and require justification/sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: supervision strategy is unclear—are losses applied at all nodes on the top layer or only at roots—and effects of supervising intermediate layers are not reported.  
- Weakness: binary sentiment evaluation is derived from 5-way posteriors (not optimized for binary); a binary-optimized model or calibrated mapping is needed for fair comparison.  
- Weakness: statistical rigor lacking—no multiple seeds, confidence intervals, or significance testing for close comparisons.  
- Clarity issue: missing initialization details in text and lack of explicit loss aggregation, class-imbalance handling, exact parameter counts, and training time/memory reporting.  
- Originality: approach is analogous to deep recurrent networks in time and is not conceptually novel, though practically useful.  
- Significance: demonstrates that added spatial depth benefits tree models and achieves SOTA on SST, but scope is limited to one dataset and task; broader validation is needed.  
- Key questions for authors: provide ablations for tied vs. untied leaves, tanh vs. ReLU, dropout vs. no dropout, fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings, activation L2 vs. gradient clipping.  
- Key questions for authors: clarify whether supervision is applied at all tree nodes on the top layer, how losses are weighted, and effects of supervising lower layers.  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to shared vs. independent dropout masks across nodes?  
- Key questions for authors: report total parameter counts, training time, and comparisons to RNTN/DCNN.  
- Key questions for authors: how stable are results across random seeds/initializations?  
- Suggestions: add comprehensive ablations and variance reporting, include binary-optimized results, and provide fair parameter-matched comparisons to baselines.  
- Suggestions: explore gradient clipping instead of activation L2 and report stability/sensitivity.  
- Suggestions: evaluate robustness to parsing errors, alternative structures (e.g., dependency trees), and extend evaluation to other compositional tasks with systematic error analysis.  
- Pros: clear, well-motivated architecture; good engineering; demonstrated improvements with parameter-budget controls; insightful qualitative analyses.  
- Cons: limited novelty; unclear attribution of gains due to missing ablations and variance; comparisons not fully controlled; scope limited to one dataset/task.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on adding ablations, clearer supervision details, and more controlled comparisons to strengthen experimental rigor.
