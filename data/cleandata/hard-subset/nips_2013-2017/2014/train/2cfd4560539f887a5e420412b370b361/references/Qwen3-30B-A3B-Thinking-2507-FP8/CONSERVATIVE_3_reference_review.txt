

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper proposes "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (DRNNs) by stacking multiple recursive neural network (RNN) layers, claiming this approach better captures compositionality in language than shallow recursive networks. The authors apply their architecture to the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) task, reporting modest improvements over shallow RNNs and other baselines, with qualitative evidence suggesting different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear presentation of the architecture and thorough experimental validation. The methodology section is well-structured, with precise mathematical formulations of the proposed DRNN and its variants. The experimental setup is appropriate, with careful comparison against multiple relevant baselines including shallow RNNs, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. The use of pretrained word vectors, rectifier activations, and dropout is well-justified and aligns with established practices in the field.

However, the paper's main weakness is its lack of sufficient justification for the novelty and significance of the proposed approach. The core idea of stacking layers is not new to deep learning; it's a standard technique used in feedforward networks, deep RNNs, and other architectures. The authors acknowledge this by referencing "deep recurrent networks" [10-12], yet fail to convincingly demonstrate why stacking layers specifically for recursive networks constitutes a significant advancement over existing deep learning techniques. The paper doesn't adequately address whether the observed improvements stem from the stacking mechanism itself or from standard deep learning practices like dropout regularization (which the authors themselves note was critical to their success).

The paper's claimed "state-of-the-art" performance (49.5% accuracy) is only marginally better than their own shallow RNN baseline (48.1%), and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that this small gain is due to the DRNN architecture rather than better regularization. The improvement over previous work [8] is also modest (49.5% vs. 48.1% for their shallow RNN), with no clear evidence that the DRNN structure is responsible for this small gain.

The qualitative analysis (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) is interesting but insufficient to support the paper's main claim. The observation that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality is consistent with what one would expect from any deep network, not a specific characteristic of DRNNs. The authors don't provide evidence that these layers are capturing compositionality in a fundamentally new or superior way compared to standard deep networks.

Furthermore, the paper lacks crucial comparisons: Why not compare with deep versions of the RNTN or MV-RNN baselines? The paper claims their approach is "constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers" but fails to demonstrate that this stacking provides advantages over simply increasing the width of a single layer (which would be a more straightforward architectural choice). The paper also doesn't adequately address why the observed degradation in performance with excessive depth (beyond 2-4 layers) isn't simply due to the width reduction (as the authors themselves suggest) rather than any fundamental limitation of the stacking approach.

The paper's significance claim is overstated. While the SST task is important, the paper doesn't demonstrate broader applicability beyond sentiment analysis, nor does it provide evidence that DRNNs would outperform standard deep learning approaches on other compositional language tasks. The authors' suggestion that "adding depth to a recursive net is different from adding width" isn't substantiated by comparative experiments that isolate these factors.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a well-executed experiment with clear methodology, it fails to demonstrate a significant methodological advance. The proposed DRNN architecture appears to be a minor variation of standard deep learning techniques, with performance gains that could be attributed to established practices like dropout regularization rather than the stacking mechanism itself. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from existing deep learning approaches, nor does it provide compelling evidence that the claimed "different aspects of compositionality" are uniquely captured by DRNNs.

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it doesn't meet the novelty and significance standards required for NIPS, particularly given the modest performance improvements and lack of clear justification for the architectural innovation. The authors would need to provide substantially stronger evidence that their stacking approach offers unique advantages beyond standard deep learning techniques to warrant publication at NIPS.
