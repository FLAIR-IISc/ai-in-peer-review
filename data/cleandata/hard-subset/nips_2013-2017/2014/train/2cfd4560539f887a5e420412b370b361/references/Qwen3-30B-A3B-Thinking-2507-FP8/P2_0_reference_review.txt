

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

## Summary

This paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture that stacks multiple recursive layers on top of each other to create a network that is deep both in structure (through recursive connections within each layer) and in space (through layer stacking). The authors argue that while traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) are deep in structure (as deep as the parse tree), they lack a hierarchical representation across layers, which is a key benefit of deep feedforward networks. By stacking multiple recursive layers, DRNNs allow each layer to handle different aspects of compositionality, with lower layers capturing more basic aspects and higher layers capturing more abstract representations.

The authors evaluate DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), where each node in the parse tree has a sentiment label, not just the root. They demonstrate that DRNNs outperform shallow RNNs of comparable size and outperform previous state-of-the-art methods on both fine-grained and binary sentiment classification tasks. The paper also provides qualitative analysis through input perturbation experiments and nearest neighbor phrase analysis, suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated extension of recursive neural networks with clear mathematical formulations (Equations 4-5). The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is conceptually valuable and well-explained.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper provides thorough experimental results on a standard benchmark (SST), with multiple baselines including shallow RNNs, multiplicative RNN variants (RNTN), and state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors. The results consistently show DRNNs outperforming baselines, achieving new state-of-the-art performance.

3. **Insightful Qualitative Analysis**: The input perturbation experiments (Figure 3) and nearest neighbor analysis (Table 2) provide valuable insights into how different layers process information, demonstrating that "each layer handles a different aspect of compositionality." This is a significant contribution beyond just performance metrics.

4. **Clear Writing and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology, experiments, and analysis. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate key concepts.

5. **Careful Evaluation**: The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the degradation in performance when networks become too deep (due to decreased width), and they provide a reasonable explanation for this phenomenon.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide clear ablation studies to isolate the specific contribution of the depth aspect versus other factors. For example, it's not clear whether the improvements come from the depth itself or from other changes (like using rectifier activations, which the authors note is enabled by their "untied leaves and internals" approach).

2. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The experiments are conducted only on the SST dataset. While SST is a standard benchmark for this task, the paper would be stronger with results on additional NLP tasks to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. **Computational Cost Analysis Missing**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of deeper networks compared to shallower ones. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

4. **Vague Explanation of Performance Degradation**: The authors note that "increasing the depth further starts to cause a degrade" but don't provide a detailed explanation. While they mention "decrease in width dominating the gains from increased depth," this is somewhat superficial and would benefit from more analysis.

5. **Limited Comparison to Deep Recurrent Networks**: The paper distinguishes between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" and mentions deep recurrent networks as inspiration. However, it doesn't directly compare to deep recurrent networks (which stack recurrent layers) or explain why DRNNs would be preferable for structured inputs.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with clear contributions to the field of neural network architectures for structured inputs. The proposed DRNN architecture is well-motivated, and the experimental results are strong, demonstrating clear improvements over previous methods. The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into how compositionality is processed at different layers, which is a significant contribution beyond just quantitative performance.

The main weaknesses are the lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of depth, limited dataset scope, and missing computational cost analysis. However, these are not fatal flaws for a NIPS paper, especially given the paper's strong technical content and clear contributions to understanding compositionality in neural networks.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of neural network architectures for structured inputs. It provides a novel architecture with clear experimental validation and insightful qualitative analysis. While some additional experiments would strengthen the paper, the current results are compelling and meet NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

The paper advances the state of the art on a standard benchmark, provides new theoretical insights into compositionality, and offers a promising direction for future work on structured neural networks. The work is well-executed, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field of NLP and deep learning.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add an ablation study to isolate the contribution of depth versus other factors (like rectifier activations enabled by untied leaves).

2. Include results on at least one additional NLP task to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. Discuss the computational cost of deeper networks and compare training/inference times to shallower networks.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of why performance degrades with excessive depth.

5. Include a more direct comparison to deep recurrent networks (stacked recurrent layers) to clarify the relationship between the proposed architecture and existing approaches.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field, and I recommend it for acceptance at NIPS.
