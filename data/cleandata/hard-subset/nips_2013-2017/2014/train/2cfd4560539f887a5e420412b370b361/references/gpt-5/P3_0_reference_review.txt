Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs): stacked layers of tree-structured recursive networks to introduce “depth in space” on top of the usual “depth in structure.” Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up along a binary parse tree with shared weights; across layers, node representations are fed forward via inter-layer connections. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters in the first layer to map from word space X to phrase space H, enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and large, fixed pretrained word vectors. Training uses backpropagation through structure and depth, AdaGrad, dropout (with a sentence-wise shared mask per layer), and a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations.

Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show: (i) shallow recursive nets benefit from the combination of pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout; (ii) under a fixed parameter budget, deeper and narrower DRNNs outperform shallow counterparts; (iii) the best DRNN outperforms prior work including RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN on fine-grained sentiment, with competitive binary results. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases at different layers) suggest progressively more abstract representations across layers (lexical similarity in layer 1, syntactic patterning in layer 2, sentiment focus in layer 3).

This work is positioned as the structural analogue of stacked recurrent networks [Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio], and extends prior tree-structured models such as RAE/RNN [Socher et al.], MV-RNN, and RNTN by adding stacked depth and using ReLUs with an untied leaf/internal mapping.

Strengths
- Clear and simple architectural idea: stacking recursive layers with inter-layer connections to separate “depth in space” from “depth in structure.”
- Thoughtful engineering choices that matter in practice for trees: untie leaf/internal transforms, fix large pretrained word vectors, use ReLUs and dropout, sensible initialization (0.5I+ε), AdaGrad to handle scale differences.
- Empirical evidence under a controlled parameter budget demonstrating gains from depth, not just capacity. This is a valuable result for the community.
- New state-of-the-art on SST at the time, with both quantitative improvements and qualitative analyses supporting the interpretation that layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
- The qualitative probes (perturbations and nearest neighbors by layer) are insightful and align with the training objective.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Originality is moderate. Stacking tree-structured composition functions is a fairly direct extension of stacked RNNs; the novelty lies more in the particular combination (untied leaf/internal + ReLUs + dropout) and empirical study than in a fundamentally new modeling principle.
- Fairness of comparisons: the DRNN uses fixed pretrained word2vec embeddings; several baselines (e.g., RNTN/MV-RNN) in the cited works learned embeddings from scratch or fine-tuned them. The paper does not report ablations that isolate how much of the gain over prior published numbers comes from pretrained embeddings and dropout, as opposed to depth. The intra-paper comparison to a shallow RNN helps, but a side-by-side reimplementation of RNTN (or at least of a strong shallow TreeRNN) with the same training recipe would strengthen claims.
- Limited ablation coverage. Important design choices are not individually evaluated: ReLU vs tanh; untied vs tied leaves/internals; dropout vs L2; output connected only to the top layer vs to all layers; L2 penalty on activations (nonstandard) vs gradient clipping. The single reported preliminary L2-only number is insufficient.
- Reporting/variance: it appears results are from single runs with early stopping on a single dev split. No standard deviations or significance tests are provided. SST is small; variance can be nontrivial.
- Some training choices merit further justification. The sentence-wise shared dropout mask per layer may bias regularization toward node-invariant features; why not independent masks per node? The use of L2 on activations addresses exploding activations with ReLU, but the interaction with dropout and AdaGrad is not analyzed; alternative stabilizers (e.g., norm clipping) are not compared.
- Depth-width tradeoff: While the fixed-parameter-budget protocol is good, deeper models are necessarily narrower, and the paper reports that performance degrades past 2–4 layers. More analysis of this tradeoff (e.g., capacity vs effective depth) would be useful to generalize beyond SST.
- Scope: Evaluation is restricted to SST; conclusions about “compositionality” could be bolstered by tasks emphasizing logical/negation phenomena or different structures (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) and by testing on non-binary parses or DAGs.

Quality
Technically sound and clearly defined. The learning setup is standard and appropriate. The main claims (depth helps under fixed budget; SOTA on SST) are empirically supported on one dataset. The lack of broader ablations/variance and fairness controls weakens the strength of the comparative SOTA claim.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well organized. The distinction between depth in structure vs space is well motivated. Some implementation details need clarification to ensure reproducibility: exact parameter counts for each configuration, the selected dropout rates per model, the exact depths/widths of best models, and whether internal node labels in SST supervise all nodes at the final layer (it seems so) should be explicit. Consider reporting training time and early stopping epochs.

Originality
Incremental but meaningful: stacking tree-structured layers with untied leaf/internal transforms and ReLUs appears novel in this combination, though closely related to prior deep recurrent work and tree models.

Significance
Moderate to high for NLP/representation learning: SST is a standard benchmark; showing clear gains from stacking tree layers under a budget is useful guidance. The architectural template is simple and likely to be adopted.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain over prior RNTN/MV-RNN numbers remains if you use the same pretrained embeddings and dropout in a strong shallow baseline? Can you provide a matched reimplementation?
- What are the exact parameter counts, widths, and depths for the models in Table 1, and which dropout rates were selected?
- Did you try connecting the classifier to all layers (multi-level supervision)? How did that affect accuracy and the interpretability analyses?
- How sensitive are results to fixing vs fine-tuning embeddings?
- Why a sentence-wise shared dropout mask rather than node-wise? Any empirical comparison?
- Did you try gradient norm clipping instead of L2 on activations?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with clear empirical gains under a controlled parameter budget.
- New SOTA on SST and informative qualitative probes.
- Useful practical recipe for tree models (ReLU, untied leaf/internal, dropout, AdaGrad).

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty is incremental; fairness of comparisons to prior SOTA unclear without matched training.
- Missing ablations and variance estimates; single small dataset.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The work offers a clean and practically impactful extension to tree-structured models with solid evidence on SST, but would benefit from stronger ablations and matched comparisons to solidify the source of gains.
