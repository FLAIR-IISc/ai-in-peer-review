Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs) that combine “depth in structure” (from tree recursion) with “depth in space” (from stacking multiple recursive layers). Each layer performs standard tree-based composition over binary parse trees, and the layers are connected vertically via learned transformations. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters in the first layer, allowing a mapping from pretrained word vectors to a hidden phrase space and enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs). The model is trained end-to-end with AdaGrad, dropout regularization, and early stopping, while keeping word embeddings fixed.

Empirically, the work targets the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with fine-grained (5-way) and derived binary evaluations. DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets of comparable parameter count, and the best DRNN achieves state-of-the-art on the fine-grained task compared to prior systems including RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases across layers) suggest layers specialize: lower layers preserve lexical content, intermediate layers capture syntactic patterns and sentiment intensity, and the final layer aligns with sentiment polarity.

Relative to prior work, the paper builds on Recursive Neural Networks over trees [Goller & Küchler; Socher et al.], multiplicative composition variants (MV-RNN, RNTN), and deep/stacked recurrent networks in time [Schmidhuber; El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen]. The main claimed novelty is stacking recursive layers to introduce a representational hierarchy over tree compositions, coupled with practical choices (ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, untied leaf/internal mapping).

Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Model: The recursive composition and vertical inter-layer connections are clearly specified (Eq. 4–5). Untying leaves vs. internals in the first layer is well-motivated for using ReLUs with fixed dense embeddings. The training protocol (AdaGrad, minibatching, dropout with shared masks per layer/sentence, small L2 on weights and activations) is reasonable; initializing intra-layer recursive weights near identity is a sensible choice.
- Experiments: The study includes comparisons across depths with roughly constant parameter budgets, which isolates depth vs. width to some extent. However, ablations are limited. The improved shallow RNN baseline differs from prior RNN baselines by using pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout; thus comparisons to older baselines (e.g., RNTN) may conflate the benefits of regularization/initialization with architecture. The paper provides a preliminary note on dropout gains but lacks systematic ablations for each design choice (pretrained vs. learned embeddings, ReLU vs. tanh, untied vs. tied leaf/internal parameters, output connection to last vs. all layers, activation L2 penalty).
- Scope and robustness: Results are reported on a single dataset (SST). There are no statistical significance tests, variance across runs, or training-time/memory reports. Still, the quantitative trend (deep > shallow under fixed parameter budget) appears consistent across sizes, and the qualitative analyses are thoughtful.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The conceptual distinction between structural and spatial depth is articulated, with helpful figures. Training details (optimizer, learning rate, batch size, epochs, initialization) are provided. Some clarifications would improve reproducibility: exact hidden sizes and depths for best models; full hyperparameter grids; precise handling of tree binarization; dropout mask sharing rationale; and reporting of multiple random seeds. Including or referencing full tables with numbers and standard deviations would be helpful.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers for tree-structured composition is a natural but nontrivial extension of prior TreeRNNs; to the best of the cited literature, multi-layer TreeRNNs have not been comprehensively explored. The combination with untied leaf/internal mappings and ReLUs is practical rather than conceptually novel. Overall, the architectural contribution is incremental yet distinct.

Significance
Achieving state-of-the-art on fine-grained SST and demonstrating consistent gains from depth under matched parameter budgets are valuable. The qualitative analyses provide insight into layer roles. That said, the impact is tempered by: (i) reliance on a single benchmark; (ii) limited ablations disentangling the contributions of depth vs. other training choices; and (iii) unclear fairness vs. strong baselines enhanced with the same regularization and embeddings. If corroborated across tasks and with stronger ablations, the approach could influence tree-structured modeling in NLP.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear and well-motivated architecture that extends TreeRNNs with depth in space.
- Consistent empirical gains of deep over shallow models under fixed parameter budgets.
- State-of-the-art on fine-grained SST at time of writing.
- Sensible training recipe (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout) and informative qualitative analyses suggesting complementary layer behaviors.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited ablations; improvements may partially stem from pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout rather than depth alone.
- Comparisons to prior baselines may be unfair without reimplementing them with the same training enhancements.
- Evaluation confined to SST; no tests on other tasks/datasets (e.g., parsing, entailment, other sentiment corpora).
- No statistical significance, multiple runs, or runtime/complexity analysis; some reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of: depth (with and without keeping parameters constant), pretrained vs. randomly initialized embeddings, ReLU vs. tanh, tied vs. untied leaf/internal parameters, dropout vs. L2, and output connected to last layer vs. all layers.
- Report results over multiple random seeds with means and standard deviations; include significance tests for key comparisons.
- Evaluate on additional tree-structured tasks or datasets to demonstrate generality.
- Discuss computational cost (training time, memory) as depth increases; compare to multiplicative models (MV-RNN/RNTN).
- Clarify details: exact best-performing configurations; parser/binarization; whether V matrices are separate for leaves vs. internals beyond the first layer; effect of the activation L2 penalty vs. gradient clipping.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a reasonably novel and technically sound extension of TreeRNNs with stacked layers, supported by clear exposition and encouraging empirical results on SST. The main weaknesses are the limited ablations and scope of evaluation, and potential confounds in baseline comparisons. With stronger experimental controls and broader validation, the contribution would be more compelling.
