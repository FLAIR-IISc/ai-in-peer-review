

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (DRNNs), which stack multiple recursive layers on top of each other to combine "depth in structure" (from recursive networks) and "depth in space" (from stacked layers). The authors evaluate DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), claiming state-of-the-art performance over shallow recursive networks and previous baselines. The paper makes an interesting attempt to extend recursive networks with depth in space, but contains several significant technical and conceptual issues that undermine its contribution.

## Strengths
- The paper identifies an interesting conceptual distinction between depth in structure and depth in space, which is well-motivated.
- The experimental evaluation on SST is thorough and includes both quantitative results and qualitative analysis through input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases.
- The paper makes a clear distinction between "untied" and "tied" weight matrices for leaves and internal nodes, which is a reasonable technical choice.

## Weaknesses

### Major Terminology Confusion
The paper consistently uses "RNN" to refer to both recursive neural networks and recurrent neural networks, creating significant confusion throughout the paper. For example, the paper states "a recursive neural network (RNNs)" in the introduction, while "RNN" standardly refers to recurrent neural networks. This is a critical error that undermines the paper's technical precision. The authors should have used "recursive neural network (RNN)" for the former and "recurrent neural network (RNN)" for the latter, or simply used "recursive network" and "recurrent network" to avoid confusion. This confusion appears in the abstract, introduction, and throughout the paper.

### Technical Errors
1. **Incorrect claim about quadratic dependence**: The paper claims "we can use large pretrained word vectors and a small number of hidden units without a quadratic dependence on the word vector dimensionality |x|." This is factually incorrect. The weight matrix dimensions are |h| × |x| and |h| × |h|, so the dependence on |x| is linear, not quadratic. Quadratic dependence would occur if the matrices were |x| × |x|, which they are not.

2. **Misapplication of L2 regularization**: The paper states "a small fixed additional L2 penalty (10^-5) over both the connection weights and the unit activations." L2 regularization is applied to weights, not activations. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of regularization techniques.

3. **Confusion between "keep fixed" and "do not finetune"**: The paper states "we keep the word vectors fixed and do not finetune for simplicity of our models," but these are not equivalent terms. "Keep fixed" means no updates at all, while "do not finetune" implies they could have been updated but weren't. This confusion is pedantic but undermines the paper's technical credibility.

### Significant Omission of Prior Work
The paper fails to cite El Hihi & Bengio's "Hierarchical recurrent neural networks for long-term dependencies" (1995), which proposed stacking recurrent layers on top of each other. This work directly precedes the authors' approach and represents a clear precedent for their "depth in space" concept. The omission is particularly egregious given the paper's emphasis on the novelty of combining depth in structure with depth in space.

### Marginal Performance Gains
The paper claims state-of-the-art results, but the improvement over the previous best (RNTN at 49.2% vs. DRNN at 49.5%) is marginal. The paper attributes this improvement to dropout, but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify the claim that the marginal gain is due to the DRNN architecture rather than regularization techniques. The paper also doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these results.

### Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies
- Equation (4) contains a typo: "h(i)l(η)" should be "h(i)l(η)" (with the l as a subscript).
- The paper claims "every node is represented in the same space at layers above the first, regardless of their 'leafness'." This is confusing because leafness is a property of the node in the parse tree, not of the layer. A node is a leaf in the parse tree regardless of which layer of the DRNN it's in.
- The paper uses "rectifier activation" and "rectifier linear activation" interchangeably, but the correct term is "rectified linear unit" (ReLU).

## Recommendation

While the paper makes an interesting conceptual attempt to combine depth in structure with depth in space, the numerous technical errors, significant terminology confusion, and omission of key prior work undermine its contribution. The marginal performance gain over state-of-the-art methods further diminishes the significance of the work. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, particularly the critical RNN terminology confusion and the technical errors regarding weight dimensions and regularization.

Given the stringent standards for NIPS papers and the fact that these issues represent fundamental misunderstandings of core concepts in neural network architecture, I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel or technically sound to warrant acceptance at NIPS, especially when compared to the quality of other papers in the field.
