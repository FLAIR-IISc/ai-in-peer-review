Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of parse-tree structure. Within each layer, composition is performed bottom-up over the tree with shared parameters; across layers, each node passes its hidden state to its counterpart in the next layer via feedforward connections. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters to allow mapping from word space to phrase space, and they adopt rectified linear units and dropout. Training uses SST with supervision at all tree nodes and output attached only to the top recursive layer. Experiments on fine-grained (5-class) and binary sentiment show that deeper (narrower) models outperform shallow ones with similar parameter counts and achieve state-of-the-art results against several published baselines (BINB, RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different aspects of composition.

Relation to prior work
The idea of increasing “depth in space” for recurrent models parallels deep recurrent networks in time. For recursive nets, prior work includes standard tree RNNs, MV-RNN and tensor-based RNTN. The proposed architecture appears to be a straightforward stacking of recursive layers with cross-layer connections, plus engineering choices (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad). The paper positions the work as bringing the deep-in-space notion to tree-structured models.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the architectural distinction between depth in structure and depth in space for tree models.
- Empirical finding that, under a fixed parameter budget, modest depth can outperform pure width on SST.
- Use of strong regularization (dropout) and simple training with AdaGrad; no pretraining needed.
- Qualitative analyses are informative and consistent with the claim that layers capture different aspects (although anecdotal).
- Reproducibility is aided by many training details (optimizer, learning rate, batch size, initialization).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Limited validation: all quantitative results are on a single dataset (SST). No tests on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., other sentiment datasets, paraphrase, NLI, or parsing) to evaluate generality.
- Ablations insufficient to isolate where gains come from. Multiple changes relative to prior RNN baselines are introduced simultaneously: untying leaves/internals, use of pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, and depth. While there is a shallow-vs-deep comparison within the proposed framework, there is no controlled ablation disentangling these factors. The improvement over prior “RNN” may be largely due to embeddings/regularization rather than depth.
- Statistical robustness is not addressed. No multiple runs or variance/confidence intervals; no statistical significance tests for the reported improvements.
- Fairness of baseline comparisons is unclear. Some baselines (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) may use different supervision regimes (sentence vs phrase level) or different embedding treatment. Using published numbers rather than re-running under matched conditions complicates claims of SOTA.
- The choice to connect supervision only to the last layer is motivated qualitatively but lacks quantitative comparison to multi-layer supervision, which might provide stronger gradients and improved training.
- No analysis of computational cost, training time, or memory, which matters for deeper models.

Clarity
- Generally well written. However, some essential details are missing or scattered: exact parameter counts per depth/width configuration; which hyperparameters were tuned per model; dropout rate used in the best models; details on binarization and parse source; and whether class weighting was used for the 5-way task.

Originality
- The core novelty is stacking recursive layers with per-node skip connections across layers. This is a natural extension of deep RNNs and not a fundamentally new composition operator. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and use of ReLUs/dropout are standard engineering choices. The qualitative analyses are nice but not methodologically novel.

Significance
- On SST, the reported gains over strong tree-structured baselines (RNTN) are potentially interesting. However, without broader validation, ablations, and controlled baselines, it is difficult to assess whether this will influence practice beyond this dataset. The contribution feels incremental.

Questions for authors
- Please provide ablations quantifying the contributions of: depth, untying leaves/internals, pretrained embeddings, ReLUs vs tanh, and dropout (ideally in a factorial design).
- How sensitive are results to depth when the width is held fixed (i.e., increasing total parameters), and vice versa?
- What is the variance across random seeds? Are improvements statistically significant?
- Did you try attaching supervision to all layers? If so, how do results and training stability compare?
- What is the computational overhead (train/inference time) as a function of depth?
- Are the published baselines re-run under matched preprocessing and supervision (phrase-level vs sentence-level)? If not, can you provide matched comparisons for at least the key baselines?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on at least one additional dataset that uses tree structure and phrase-level supervision, or report sentence-level-only training to match baselines.
- Run controlled ablations to isolate the effect of depth vs other design choices.
- Report multiple runs with mean/std and significance tests.
- Provide parameter counts, runtime, and memory usage per configuration.
- Consider a comparison where total parameters are matched not only across your depth variants but also approximately to the strongest baselines.

Pros
- Clear motivation and architecture for depth in space on trees.
- Simple training without pretraining; practical.
- Empirical improvements on SST and informative qualitative analyses.

Cons
- Limited novelty; stacking is incremental.
- Evidence confined to a single dataset; no statistical robustness.
- Baseline comparability not fully controlled.
- Insufficient ablation to attribute gains to depth.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (borderline reject)
Confidence: High

Rationale: While the idea is reasonable and results are promising, the methodological evidence is not yet rigorous enough to support a strong claim that depth-in-space for recursive nets is the key driver of improvements. With broader evaluation, controlled ablations, and matched baselines, this could be a solid contribution. As is, I remain cautious about endorsing a new architectural variant based on a single-dataset study with confounded factors.
