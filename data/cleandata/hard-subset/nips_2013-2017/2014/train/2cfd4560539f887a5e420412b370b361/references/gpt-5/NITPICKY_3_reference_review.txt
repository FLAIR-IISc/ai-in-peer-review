Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep-RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of binary parse trees. The authors also “untie” parameters between leaves and internal nodes to allow different transformations (word-to-phrase vs phrase-to-phrase). They use ReLU activations, AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks across tree nodes, and fixed pre-trained word embeddings. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all nodes but an output head only on the final layer. The authors report that deeper (narrower) models outperform shallow (wider) models with a comparable parameter budget, achieving state-of-the-art performance on fine-grained and binary sentiment. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned as an extension of recursive neural networks (Socher et al.) by adding depth “in space,” analogous to stacked RNNs for sequences. It compares to RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. However, related work is incompletely discussed and the reference list is severely malformed. The idea of stacking tree-recursive layers is reasonable and plausibly novel relative to [6–9,16], though the paper does not thoroughly clarify differences to multiplicative or tensor-augmented models beyond depth.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: separating leaf/internal transformations plus stacking recursive layers is intuitive and well-motivated.
- Practical recipe: ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad, fixed pre-trained embeddings, shared dropout across tree nodes—these details are useful for practitioners.
- Empirical improvements on SST, including fine-grained classification, with qualitative analyses that attempt to interpret what layers learn.
- Sensible observation that connecting the classifier only to the final layer simplifies qualitative analysis.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Reproducibility gaps and missing details:
  - Tables and figures (Table 1a/b, Table 2, Figures 1–3) are referenced but not included. Without exact numbers, widths, depths, and parameter counts, claims are not verifiable.
  - The thresholds used to map SST real-valued labels to five classes are unspecified.
  - Parsing details are missing: which parser, binarization scheme, handling of unary chains, unknown tokens/OOV handling.
  - Initialization is incomplete: “Recursive weights initialized as 0.5I + … All other weights are initialized as …” Values are missing. The variance/range of the uniform noise and bias initialization are not given.
  - Dropout specifics: which layers get dropout, rate per layer, applied before/after nonlinearity, test-time scaling—only a coarse dev-set sweep is reported. The “shared mask across nodes” choice is nonstandard and needs justification and ablation.
  - AdaGrad hyperparameters (epsilon), gradient clipping (absent), batch composition (balanced? random?), and early stopping criterion (metric, patience) are not fully specified.
- Methodological ablations insufficient:
  - No ablation on the untying of leaf vs internal parameters.
  - No ablation on ReLU vs tanh, or fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings.
  - No ablation on connecting outputs at intermediate layers vs only the final layer.
  - No ablation removing the inter-layer V(i) connections or testing residual/skip variants.
- Stability claim and regularization:
  - The use of an L2 penalty on activations (10^-5) to “prevent explosion” is ad hoc; standard gradient clipping is not attempted or reported.
  - The claim that ReLU leads to exploding activations is not quantified; no gradient/activation statistics are shown.
- Comparisons and fairness:
  - It’s unclear whether baseline numbers are strictly comparable (e.g., did DCNN/Paragraph Vectors use the same data splits and evaluation protocol? Were they trained specifically for binary vs fine-grained?).
  - The paper asserts state-of-the-art but does not include the exact numbers in-text; without the tables, it’s impossible to assess significance or error bars.
  - Controlling parameter count across depth is commendable, but runtime complexity increases with depth; no training/inference time or memory usage are reported.
- Conceptual clarity:
  - The statement that “depth in structure is unlikely to involve a hierarchical interpretation” is speculative and unsubstantiated; some prior tree models do yield hierarchical abstractions even within a single layer.
  - Supervision is at all nodes but the classifier only attaches to the top layer; a discussion of gradient propagation/vanishing and supervision strength across layers is missing.
- Notation and minor technical issues:
  - Softmax definition g(x) = e^{x_i}/sum_j e^{x_j} is improperly defined without vector context.
  - Section 2.2 notational overload (x, h) vs Section 2.3 (h^(i)) is inconsistent; the handling of leaf vs internal V^(2) (V^xh(2), V^hh(2)) is mentioned but not formalized in equations.
  - Shapes/dimensions of matrices/tensors are not always specified; “square matrices” in Eq. (1) conflicts with later untying.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking recursive layers appears to be a straightforward extension; untying leaves and internals plus ReLU/dropout are known techniques. The contribution is an engineering combination yielding better SST performance and some interpretability analysis. The paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical or empirical dissection of why depth helps in trees beyond capacity.

Significance
Potentially useful if results are solid, but current presentation lacks the necessary details, tables, and ablations to convince. The empirical gain may largely stem from pre-trained embeddings, dropout, and ReLU rather than architectural novelty; without ablations, the specific contribution of stacking remains unclear.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but missing figures/tables, incomplete equations, inconsistent notation, and an error-ridden bibliography severely hinder clarity and reproducibility.

Pros
- Simple, plausible architectural idea with practical training recipe.
- Reported gains on a standard benchmark; qualitative analyses are interesting.
- Encourages further work on deep compositional structures.

Cons
- Missing tables/figures and critical training details; incomplete initialization description.
- Insufficient ablations to isolate contributions.
- Questionable regularization choice (activation L2) without alternatives.
- Incomplete/incorrect references and several notational/formatting issues.

Questions for authors
1. Provide full tables with exact numbers, parameter counts, depths, widths, and runtime/memory.
2. Clarify parsing/binarization pipeline and label thresholding.
3. Give complete initialization details and AdaGrad hyperparameters; did you try gradient clipping?
4. Ablate: depth vs width, untying, ReLU vs tanh, dropout mask sharing, fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings, output on all layers.
5. Justify shared dropout masks; how sensitive are results to mask sharing?
6. Are the SOTA claims statistically significant?

Typos/formatting
- Many references lack years/venues/pages; several author lists are corrupted.
- “Jaz K, Thomas Hofmann, Tomaso Poggio, John Shawe-taylor” erroneously appended in [2].
- Missing values in initialization text; softmax formula misuse; minor grammar issues.
