Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of parse trees. At each layer, node representations are computed by recursively composing children (shared weights within a layer), and each node also feeds its hidden state vertically to its counterpart in the next layer. The output layer reads only from the topmost recursive layer. The authors also “untie” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer, mapping words (X) to phrases (H) with separate parameters and using rectified linear units (ReLUs), dropout, AdaGrad, and pretrained (fixed) word embeddings. Training uses backpropagation both through structure (within a layer) and through space (across layers).

Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that: (a) even shallow recursive models benefit from untied leaf/internal parameters, pretrained embeddings, and ReLUs+dropout; (b) deeper stacked recursive models outperform shallow recursive models with roughly the same number of parameters; and (c) the best deep model achieves state-of-the-art fine-grained and competitive binary sentiment results compared to RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN on the same splits. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path from leaf to root, and layer-wise nearest neighbor phrase retrieval) suggest layer specialization, with lower layers capturing lexical/syntactic similarity and upper layers capturing sentiment polarity/intensity.

This work builds on recursive neural networks for parsing and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al. 2011–2013) and on the idea of depth in recurrent models (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), extending “depth in space” to recursive/tree-structured models. It also brings practices effective in feedforward/sequential deep learning (ReLUs, dropout, pretrained embeddings, AdaGrad) to tree models.

Strengths
- Clear idea and motivation: separates depth in structure from depth in space for recursive models, with a straightforward, well-motivated architecture.
- Empirical value: consistent improvements of deep over shallow recursive baselines under controlled parameter budgets; new SOTA on SST fine-grained at the time of the cited baselines.
- Sound engineering: untying leaves/internal nodes to allow ReLUs, leveraging pretrained embeddings, using dropout with shared masks per sentence/layer, stable initialization for Whh, and AdaGrad.
- Qualitative analyses provide insight into layer behavior (intensity vs polarity, lexical vs sentiment similarity).
- Good experimental controls: when varying depth, keep total parameters roughly constant, reducing width accordingly.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Limited evaluation breadth: only SST is used. Results on additional tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, or other sentiment datasets) would substantiate generality.
- Ablation depth: while the shallow untied+ReLU+pretrained baseline is compared against prior tanh/learned-embedding RNNs, the paper does not fully disentangle which components (untying, ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, output from only final layer) contribute how much to improvements. More ablations would strengthen causal claims.
- Statistical rigor: no variance across runs or statistical significance testing is reported; SST has known variability across seeds and training choices.
- Regularization choice: “small fixed L2 penalty on activations” is used to prevent explosion with ReLUs; gradient clipping or normalization could be alternatives. Some details are unclear (see missing initialization details below).
- Binary results are derived from a 5-way model; a dedicated binary model might be a fairer comparator to prior binary baselines.

Clarity
- Generally well written with clear equations. However:
  - Some initialization details appear truncated (“All other weights are initialized as .”). Please clarify initialization scale/distribution.
  - Explicitly define whether V(i) for leaves vs internals above the first layer are distinct (you mention Vxh(2) and Vhh(2) but not for higher layers).
  - Provide exact parameter counts per configuration in Table 1a to verify the constant-parameter claim.
  - Clarify the effect and rationale of sharing dropout masks across tree nodes within a sentence; report if node-wise independent dropout was tried.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers for depth in space is a natural but nontrivial extension of prior tree RNNs; to my knowledge it has not been thoroughly evaluated with modern training tricks on SST at the time of the cited baselines. Untying leaves/internals plus ReLUs/dropout for trees is also a useful design. The qualitative layer analyses are a nice addition.
- That said, the architectural novelty is incremental: no new composition function per se, but a stacking of standard ones. Positioning relative to multiplicative/tensor models (RNTN) and to deeper but gated models (if contemporaneous) could be discussed.

Significance
- Achieving SOTA on a widely used benchmark and demonstrating consistent depth benefits under parameter control is impactful for the NLP/tree-structured modeling community.
- The ideas (stacking, untying, ReLUs, dropout) are likely to be adopted by practitioners.
- Broader significance would be higher with evidence beyond SST and with stronger ablations.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report multiple runs with mean/std and significance tests versus RNTN and DCNN?
- How much does each component contribute? Ablate untying, ReLU vs tanh, pretrained vs learned embeddings, dropout on/off, and output from last layer only vs from all layers.
- Did you try gradient clipping instead of L2 on activations? Any stability issues with deeper stacks without activation penalties?
- How sensitive are results to sharing the dropout mask across nodes? What happens with independent masks?
- Why freeze embeddings? Does fine-tuning help or hurt with ReLUs and dropout?
- Can you include results on at least one additional tree task to support generality?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Provide exact hyperparameters (widths per depth, parameter counts, dropout rates used by the best models).
- Consider reporting training time/complexity and memory usage versus shallow models.
- Explore skip/residual connections between nonadjacent layers to ease optimization in deeper stacks.
- The qualitative section would benefit from more examples and quantitative probes (e.g., linear probes for polarity/intensity at each layer).

Reproducibility
- Uses standard dataset/split and public embeddings; training details are mostly given. Missing initialization details and lack of code release reduce reproducibility; please include these.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated extension of recursive models with demonstrated empirical gains.
- State-of-the-art fine-grained sentiment on SST with controlled comparisons.
- Practical training recipe for deep tree models and insightful qualitative analyses.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scope (single dataset), incomplete ablations, and no significance analysis.
- Incremental novelty; primarily an engineering advance rather than a fundamentally new composition mechanism.
- Some missing details hinder full reproducibility.

Overall rating: Weak accept
