- Proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs): stacked tree-structured recursive layers with inter-layer connections, untied leaf/internal transforms, ReLUs, and fixed large pretrained word vectors.
- Training uses backpropagation through structure and depth, AdaGrad, sentence-wise shared-mask dropout, and small L2 penalties on weights and activations.
- Experiments on SST show shallow recursive nets benefit from pretrained embeddings+ReLU+dropout, and under a fixed-parameter budget deeper-narrower DRNNs outperform shallow counterparts.
- Best DRNN achieves new state-of-the-art on fine-grained SST and competitive binary results, with qualitative analyses indicating progressively more abstract representations across layers.
- Positions DRNNs as the structural analogue of stacked RNNs and an extension of prior tree models (RAE/RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) via stacked depth and untied leaf mapping.
- Strength: Clear, simple architectural idea separating “depth in space” from “depth in structure.”
- Strength: Strong engineering choices (untied leaf/internal, fixed pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, sensible init, AdaGrad) that matter in practice.
- Strength: Controlled parameter-budget experiments provide empirical evidence that depth (not just capacity) yields gains.
- Strength: Informative qualitative probes (perturbations, nearest neighbors by layer) that support the compositionality interpretation.
- Weakness: Originality is moderate—stacking tree compositions is a direct extension of stacked RNNs; novelty is mainly the combination and empirical study.
- Weakness: Fairness of comparisons is unclear because DRNNs use fixed pretrained embeddings while many baselines learned/fine-tuned embeddings; no matched reimplementation of strong baselines.
- Weakness: Limited ablation studies—key choices (ReLU vs tanh, untied vs tied, dropout vs L2, classifier connection to layers, L2 on activations vs clipping) are not individually evaluated.
- Weakness: Results appear from single runs with early stopping on one dev split; no standard deviations or significance testing reported.
- Weakness: Some training choices lack justification or comparative analysis (sentence-wise shared dropout mask; L2 on activations vs norm clipping; interaction with AdaGrad).
- Weakness: Depth-width tradeoff under fixed-parameter protocol needs deeper analysis—performance degrades past 2–4 layers and narrower widths may limit generalization.
- Weakness: Scope limited to SST; claims about compositionality would be stronger with other tasks (negation, entailment, paraphrase) and non-binary/DAG structures.
- Quality: Technically sound and clear; main claims supported on SST but weakened by missing ablations and fairness controls.
- Clarity: Well written and organized, but missing implementation details needed for reproducibility (exact parameter counts, dropout rates, model widths/depths, supervision details).
- Originality: Incremental but meaningful combination of ideas that is likely to be adopted.
- Significance: Moderate-to-high for representation learning in NLP—simple, practical template with demonstrated gains on a standard benchmark.
- Questions for authors: quantify gains with matched pretrained embeddings in shallow baselines; provide exact parameter counts/widths/depths and dropout rates; report experiments connecting classifier to all layers; sensitivity to fixing vs fine-tuning embeddings; rationale/empirical comparison for sentence-wise dropout mask; comparison of L2-on-activations vs gradient clipping.
- Pros for acceptance: Simple well-motivated architecture, controlled evidence that depth helps, new SOTA on SST, and useful practical recipe for tree models.
- Cons for acceptance: Incremental novelty, unclear fairness of comparisons, insufficient ablations/variance estimates, and evaluation on a single dataset.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — clean, practically impactful work that would benefit from stronger ablations and matched comparisons to solidify the source of gains.
