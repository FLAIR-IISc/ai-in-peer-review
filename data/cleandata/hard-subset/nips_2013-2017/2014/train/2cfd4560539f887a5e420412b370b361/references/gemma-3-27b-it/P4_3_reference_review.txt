This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs), extending traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation stems from the success of deep architectures in other domains, aiming to capture a hierarchy of compositionality in language. The authors apply this architecture to fine-grained sentiment detection on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improvements over shallow RNNs and existing state-of-the-art methods. Qualitative analysis suggests that different layers learn different aspects of compositionality.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The methodology is clearly presented, building upon established RNN foundations. The extension to deep RNNs is logically motivated and well-defined. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to a strong set of baselines and ablation studies (varying depth and width). The use of dropout and L2 regularization addresses potential training instability, and the justification for these choices is provided. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed DRNNs on the SST dataset. However, the improvements, while statistically significant, are not dramatic, and the ablation study is limited to varying depth and width while keeping the total number of parameters constant. It would be valuable to see results with different parameter budgets. Further, the discussion of potential failure cases or limitations is minimal.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work and positions it within the broader context of deep learning and NLP. The methodology section is detailed and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the mathematical formulations. Figures 1 and 2 are helpful in visualizing the architecture. However, the notation could be slightly more consistent (e.g., the subscripting of variables). The qualitative analysis section is well-structured and the examples provided are insightful, illustrating how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality. The writing is generally concise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The idea of stacking recursive layers is a natural extension of deep learning principles to the domain of recursive neural networks. While not a revolutionary breakthrough, it represents a novel contribution. The untying of leaves and internals within the recursive layers is also a worthwhile addition. The combination of depth in structure and depth in space, inspired by deep recurrent neural networks, is a solid conceptual link. The authors adequately reference relevant prior work, including foundational papers on RNNs, deep learning, and sentiment analysis. The connection to recent work on deep recurrent networks is explicitly highlighted.

**Significance:** The results are significant in demonstrating the potential benefits of deep architectures for capturing compositional semantics in language. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SST dataset is a notable accomplishment. The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the DRNNs, suggesting that different layers learn different representations. This understanding could inform the design of future architectures for compositional reasoning. While the application is currently limited to sentiment analysis, the underlying principles could be applied to other NLP tasks involving hierarchical structures. However, the practical impact might be limited if the gains are not substantial across diverse tasks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated extension of existing RNN architectures.
*   Empirically demonstrates improvements over strong baselines on a challenging dataset.
*   Provides insightful qualitative analysis supporting the claim that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Improvements, while statistically significant, are not dramatic.
*   Ablation study is limited.
*   Minimal discussion of potential failure cases or limitations.
*   Application is currently limited to a single task.
