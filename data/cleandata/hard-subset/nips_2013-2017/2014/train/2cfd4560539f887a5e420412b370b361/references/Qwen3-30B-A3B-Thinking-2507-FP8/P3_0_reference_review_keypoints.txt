- Proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs): stacks multiple recursive layers to add "depth in space" to tree-structured models.  
- Introduces an "untied" variant separating leaf and internal node vector spaces, enabling rectifier activations.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), showing deeper DRNNs outperform shallower RNNs and prior baselines (RNTN, Paragraph Vectors).  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture different compositional aspects.  
- Technically sound with clear mathematical formulation and strong experimental design.  
- Novel conceptual distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" applied to recursive networks.  
- Achieves new state-of-the-art results on SST fine-grained sentiment classification.  
- Limited qualitative analysis: only two examples each for key experiments, reducing generalizability of interpretive claims.  
- Lacks comparisons with other modern deep architectures (LSTMs, transformers, contextual embeddings like ELMo/BERT).  
- Insufficient analysis of why performance degrades at greater depth and the depth-width trade-off.  
- No discussion of computational cost (training time, memory) for deeper DRNNs.  
- Limited theoretical justification linking stacked recursive layers to linguistic theories of compositionality.  
- Recommendation: accept — contribution is significant, well-written, and empirically validated.  
- Suggested improvements: expand qualitative examples (negation, idioms, complex syntax), compare to modern architectures, discuss limitations/trade-offs, clarify relation to deep recurrent networks, and include computational cost analysis.  
- Overall pros: novel extension, strong empirical gains, insightful qualitative work, clear presentation; cons: limited qualitative scope, missing modern baselines, weaker analysis of limitations and efficiency.
