Summary
The paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures. The key idea is to distinguish “depth in structure” (recursion over the parse tree) from “depth in space” (stacking layers), and to inject vertical connections that pass a node’s representation from layer i−1 to its counterpart at layer i. The model also “unties” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer, mapping dense word vectors to a separate phrase space, enabling the use of rectified linear units. Training uses backpropagation through structure and space, pretrained word embeddings kept fixed, dropout with a shared mask per sentence and layer, AdaGrad, and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations to prevent explosion. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), the best deep model outperforms shallow recursive nets with similar parameter budgets and achieves state-of-the-art performance against published baselines (RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN). Qualitative analyses suggest different layers specialize (e.g., sentiment intensity vs. polarity).

Relation to prior work
- Extends classic recursive nets over trees (Goller and Küchler; Socher et al.) by introducing stacked layers (akin to stacked recurrent nets in time).
- Contrasts with prior compositional models that increase expressivity via multiplicative interactions (MV-RNN, RNTN) rather than depth.
- Uses pretrained word vectors (Mikolov et al.) and modern training practices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad).
- The distinction between leaf and internal transformations relates recursive nets closer to Elman RNNs and addresses practical issues with activation distributions.

Quality
- Methodologically sound: the formulation h(i)η = f(W(i)L h(i)l + W(i)R h(i)r + V(i)h(i−1)η + b(i)) is clear, trains via standard backprop through structure, and design choices (ReLU, dropout, initialization Whh ≈ 0.5I) are sensible for stability.
- The parameter-budget control (deeper means narrower to hold total params fixed) is thoughtful, though it confounds isolating the effect of depth vs. width.
- Empirically, the paper demonstrates consistent gains from depth up to a point, and overall SOTA on SST. However, statistical significance, variance across runs, and ablations are limited.
- Qualitative analyses are insightful but anecdotal; stronger aggregate probing would bolster claims about layer specialization.

Clarity
- Well-written and organized. The architectural diagrams referenced (Fig. 1–2) and equations are clear.
- Training details are sufficiently specified to reproduce (optimizer, learning rate, dropout rates, initialization, epochs, early stopping).
- Some implementation choices merit further elaboration (e.g., choice of shared dropout mask per sentence/layer; effect of not fine-tuning embeddings).

Originality
- The main novelty is stacking recursive layers with vertical connections for tree-structured networks, and the leaf/internal untying to leverage ReLUs. While stacking is a familiar idea in sequence RNNs, its adaptation to tree structures with careful parameterization and training is nontrivial. The qualitative analyses of layer behavior add value.
- The work is a novel combination of established components rather than a fundamentally new model class, but the combination is original and useful for tree composition.

Significance
- Demonstrates that “depth in space” benefits tree compositional models beyond “depth in structure,” with new SOTA on SST at the time of comparison.
- Practical impact: simple architecture, no need for expensive multiplicative tensors; uses fixed pretrained embeddings yet competitive/strong results.
- The broader promise is a general recipe to scale tree models by depth rather than width or more complex bilinear forms. However, generalization beyond sentiment is not empirically established in this paper.

Strengths
- Clear, principled architecture that is easy to implement and train.
- Strong empirical results on a widely used benchmark; careful control for parameter count.
- Sensible engineering choices (ReLU, untying leaves/internals, identity-initialized recursive matrices).
- Qualitative analyses that attempt to interpret what each layer learns.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: single dataset/task (SST). Claims about compositionality would be stronger with additional tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, semantic relation classification).
- Ablations are incomplete: the contribution of each choice (untied leaves vs tied; ReLU vs tanh; dropout vs none; fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings; output on last layer vs all layers) is not systematically quantified. One preliminary dropout comparison is mentioned but not comprehensive.
- No variance or significance testing; results appear single-run.
- The fairness of comparisons: some baselines fine-tune embeddings and/or use different optimization; only published numbers are used. A reimplementation baseline under the same training regime would clarify.
- Stability: reliance on an L2 penalty on activations suggests potential exploding activations; the paper does not explore gradient clipping or normalization, and does not analyze training dynamics in depth.
- The shared dropout mask per layer/sentence is an unusual choice; rationale and sensitivity are not explored.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide results with constant width across depths (i.e., allowing parameter count to grow) and/or residual/skip connections across layers to disentangle depth benefits from narrowness?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of ReLU vs leaky ReLU/tanh, and to fine-tuning word embeddings?
- What is the effect of tying leaves and internals in the first layer? Does untying drive most of the gains seen for the shallow RNN baseline?
- Do you observe consistent improvements across multiple random seeds? Please report mean ± std.
- Why use a shared dropout mask across nodes within a layer? How do results change with independent masks?
- Could you add a small ablation on connecting the classifier to intermediate layers as well as the last?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea with clear empirical gains.
- Advances SOTA on a canonical compositional benchmark.
- Good clarity and reproducibility; insightful qualitative analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental scope (single dataset) and limited ablations/statistical rigor.
- Novelty is incremental (stacking) relative to prior recursive models.
- Some training choices and comparisons could be better justified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clean and useful extension of recursive nets with demonstrated benefits and clear exposition. Strengthening the experimental evidence (ablations, variance, possibly an additional task) would elevate it further.
