Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of binary parse trees. The authors distinguish depth in structure (as in standard recursive networks) from depth in space (stacking), and argue that stacking enables a hierarchy of representations, analogous to deep feedforward/recurrent nets. They also “untie” leaves from internal nodes to allow separate mappings from word to phrase space and use rectified linear units, dropout (with shared masks across tree nodes within a layer), AdaGrad, and small L2 penalties on both weights and activations. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), they report that deeper, narrower models (with constant parameter budget) outperform shallow counterparts, and that their best DRNN beats prior work (RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN) on fine-grained and binary sentiment. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest layers capture different aspects (intensity vs polarity).

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive extension: stacking recursive layers is a natural and potentially useful way to add representational hierarchy in tree-structured models.
- Empirical gains on a widely used benchmark (SST), with practical training choices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) that simplify optimization without pretraining/fine-tuning.
- Sensible design of “untied” leaf/internal parameters enabling large pretrained word vectors plus compact hidden layers; the rectifier motivation is reasonable.
- Useful qualitative probes (input perturbation along a path; layerwise nearest neighbors) that align with the “different layers capture different aspects” hypothesis.
- Attempts to control parameter count when comparing depths.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Ablations are insufficient to isolate the source of gains. Depth, untied leaves/internals, pretrained word vectors, rectifiers, and dropout are all changed vs older RNN baselines. The text attributes a large boost to pretrained vectors + rectifiers + dropout (48.1 vs 43.2) even for shallow models, and to dropout for deeper ones (46.06→49.5), but depth’s marginal contribution remains under-quantified. Please provide a systematic ablation: {tanh vs ReLU, tied vs untied, with/without pretrained vectors, with/without dropout} across depths.
- Fairness of comparisons: RNTN and MV-RNN typically fine-tune word vectors; you keep vectors fixed. Conversely, you use large external pretrained vectors; not all baselines do. Report versions with (a) fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings for your models and (b) comparable pretraining settings for baselines where possible; otherwise, clearly discuss the confound.
- Training/objective unspecified details: it is not clear whether loss is summed over all nodes, how node losses are weighted (root vs leaves), and how class imbalance is handled. This matters for SST where many node labels are near-neutral. Also, no details on test-time dropout scaling, gradient clipping, or per-layer norm constraints (you mention exploding activations but solve it via small L2 on activations; why not clipping?).
- Variability/significance: No multiple runs or variance/confidence intervals are shown. Given small absolute gains (e.g., 1–2 points), please report mean±std over several seeds and significance tests.
- Model specification is incomplete: exact architectures for each depth/width setting, parameter counts, and hyperparameters are not tabulated. The text mentions “200 units each” in one setting, but Table 1a (referred to) needs to include layer widths per depth to enable reproducibility.
- Initialization: Whh initialized as 0.5I+ε, but WL and WR are separate—are both set to 0.5I? How is “left/right” asymmetry handled at init? Also, the text literally says “All other weights are initialized as .” (missing value).
- Regularization: L2 on activations is unusual; please justify and quantify its impact vs gradient clipping or max-norm. The shared dropout mask across all tree nodes per sentence per layer is a design choice; motivate it (e.g., as a structured regularizer) and compare to independent masks.
- Qualitative analyses are anecdotal: single sentence for perturbation; a couple of phrases for nearest neighbors; no quantitative measure of layer specialization. Consider probing with more sentences and reporting aggregate statistics (e.g., correlation with polarity/intensity labels).

Clarity
- Notational drift and inconsistencies: xη vs hη, switching between tied and untied variants; superscripts (i) vs superscripts for leaf/internal mappings; sometimes W^xh_L, sometimes Wη·. Equation (3) is hard to parse; please unify notation. Explain clearly which layers use untied leaf/internal parameters (first only) and how V matrices are organized (Vxh vs Vhh).
- Loss/optimization details, hyperparameters, and evaluation protocol are under-specified (see Quality).
- Several grammar issues and awkward phrasing; a careful copy-edit is needed.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward extension of stacking in RNNs; the conceptual novelty is incremental. Prior work on deep recurrent nets (temporal) is acknowledged; please more explicitly position vs existing tree models that increase capacity via multiplicative interactions (MV-RNN, RNTN) and discuss trade-offs (depth vs multiplicative composition). If similar stacked tree models exist (e.g., multi-layer tree encoders), cite and differentiate.

Significance
- If the reported SOTA holds under fair and statistically sound comparisons, the contribution is practically relevant. However, given the confounds and missing ablations/variances, it is difficult to attribute the improvement to depth per se. The qualitative insights are interesting but not conclusive.

Questions for the authors
- What exact loss aggregation over tree nodes is used? Any reweighting?
- Exact architectures for each row in Table 1a, with parameter counts?
- Do you fine-tune embeddings anywhere? If not, why not show an ablation?
- Test-time dropout scaling? Any gradient clipping?
- Why L1 distance for nearest neighbors; are vectors normalized? Results with cosine?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of shared vs independent dropout masks?

Typos/formatting
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues), inconsistent, and contain typos (e.g., “Jeffrey Pennin”).
- “All other weights are initialized as .” incomplete.
- Inconsistent notation (e.g., W hh vs Whh), backtick for `, spacing/superscripts.
- Multiple minor grammar issues; e.g., “Recursive neural networks, comprise,” “which potentially alleviating,” etc.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea; easy to implement.
- Empirical gains on SST; nice qualitative layer analyses.
- Practical training recipe that avoids pretraining.

Cons
- Incomplete ablations; unclear attribution of gains to depth.
- Missing/reproducibility details, no variance/significance.
- Several notational/formatting errors and reference issues.
- Comparisons may be confounded by differing embedding pretraining/fine-tuning.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is reasonable and likely useful, but the current submission has too many methodological and presentation issues to assess the core contribution rigorously. With thorough ablations, clearer/fairer comparisons, statistical rigor, and cleaned-up writing/references, this could be a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
