Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of a tree-structured neural network. The authors distinguish “depth in structure” (from tree recursion) from “depth in space” (from stacking), and argue that stacking should enable hierarchical abstractions across layers, analogously to deep feedforward/recurrent nets. They also “untie” leaf and internal node parameters to allow different transformations (including word-to-phrase mappings), use rectified linear units, dropout, and pretrained word vectors. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all tree nodes. Results indicate (i) deeper/narrower models outperform shallow/wider models under a fixed parameter budget, and (ii) the best DRNN achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on fine-grained sentiment compared to prior tree models (e.g., RNTN) and non-tree baselines (Paragraph Vectors, DCNN). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive neural networks for trees (e.g., Socher et al.) and on deep stacked recurrent architectures (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen) by transferring the stacking idea to tree structures. Prior multiplicative/tree-tensor models (MV-RNN, RNTN) offered richer compositions but at higher parameter cost. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and the use of ReLUs/dropout seem incremental adaptations of established techniques to recursive settings.

Quality
- Positives: The model is clearly defined, training details are given (AdaGrad, initialization, dropout scheme), and the parameter budget is (mostly) controlled when varying depth. The qualitative analyses are thoughtful. On SST, deeper models outperform shallow ones with similar parameter counts.
- Concerns:
  - Attribution of gains is unclear. Several changes are introduced simultaneously (stacking, leaf/internal untying, ReLUs, dropout, pretrained embeddings). Apart from a brief note on dropout, there is no systematic ablation to isolate the contribution of stacking per se. It remains plausible that most of the gains over prior RNN baselines come from better regularization/activations/embeddings rather than depth.
  - Baseline fairness: Comparisons use previously published results with different training setups. The paper’s “shallow RNN” baseline uses pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout, whereas the RNN from [8] used tanh and learned embeddings. Likewise, RNTN/MV-RNN numbers are taken as-is, without matching word vectors or regularization. A re-implementation or stronger controlled baselines are necessary to substantiate SOTA claims.
  - Statistical rigor: No variance over multiple runs or significance tests are reported. Given the modest absolute differences typically observed on SST, this is important.
  - Scope: Evaluation is limited to a single dataset and task. Claims about compositionality and layer specialization would be more convincing with additional tasks (e.g., phrase similarity/entailment, other treebanked sentiment datasets) or at least multiple SST settings (e.g., binary model trained explicitly for binary).
  - Design choices need more justification: sharing dropped units across all nodes within a layer, adding L2 penalty on activations for stability, and connecting the classifier only to the top layer are reasonable but unexplored choices (no alternatives or sensitivity analyses).
  - Efficiency/complexity: Deeper stacked trees increase computational cost; there is no discussion of training/inference time or memory overhead relative to shallow models and multiplicative baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear equations and a sensible exposition. However, the manuscript as provided references tables/figures without showing all numbers; final camera-ready should include complete results, hyperparameter grids, and training times. Some claims (e.g., “state-of-the-art”) should be qualified with exact figures and significance.

Originality
The main idea—stacking recursive layers—is a relatively straightforward extension of known stacking in RNNs to trees. The untying of leaf vs. internal parameters and use of ReLUs/dropout/pretrained embeddings are standard techniques adapted to the recursive setting. The qualitative probes are nice but not methodologically novel. Overall originality is moderate.

Significance
Demonstrating that depth-in-space helps in tree models could be meaningful if gains are robust and attributable. However, with a single dataset, limited ablations, and potentially unfair baseline comparisons, the evidence is not yet compelling. If validated more rigorously and across tasks, the approach could be adopted by practitioners working with tree structures.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide ablations isolating: stacking (depth) vs. single-layer with identical ReLU/dropout/pretrained embeddings; effect of leaf/internal untying; effect of dropout scheme (node-wise vs. shared), and the activation L2 penalty?
- Can you re-run strong baselines (RNN, RNTN) under matched settings (same fixed pretrained embeddings, same dropout, same optimization), or at least report additional controlled comparisons?
- What is the variance across 5–10 random seeds? Are the improvements significant?
- What are the training/inference time and memory costs vs. shallow RNN and RNTN?
- How does performance change if embeddings are fine-tuned? If outputs are connected to all layers?
- Any results on additional datasets or tasks to support claims about compositionality beyond sentiment?

Pros
- Clear problem setup and method; sensible training recipe.
- Parameter-budget-controlled comparison indicating benefits of stacking.
- Qualitative analyses suggest layer specialization.
- Potentially stronger regularization for recursive models (dropout with ReLUs).

Cons
- Limited novelty; stacking is expected.
- Insufficient controlled ablations to attribute gains to depth.
- Single-dataset evaluation; no statistical tests.
- Baseline fairness concerns; SOTA claim not fully substantiated.
- Missing analysis of computational cost and sensitivity to design choices.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well written and the idea is reasonable, but the empirical evidence is not yet rigorous enough for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to add thorough ablations, matched baselines, significance analyses, and broader evaluation; with those, this could become a solid contribution.
