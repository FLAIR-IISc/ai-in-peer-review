- Proposes Deep RNNs that stack multiple recursive layers ("depth in space") on binary parse trees with shared weights and feedforward connections across layers.  
- Unties leaves from internal nodes in the first layer to map word embeddings to phrase space, enabling ReLU use and preventing dense/sparse mixing.  
- Employs ReLU, shared dropout masks across nodes, Adagrad, and small L2 penalties on weights/activations for training stability.  
- Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (5-way and binary), supervising every node but attaching the output layer to the top recursive layer.  
- Deep RNNs beat shallow recursive baselines at fixed parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art results on SST versus RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN.  
- Best performance typically occurs at 2–4 layers depending on model width; qualitative probes indicate deeper layers capture more sentiment-oriented abstractions.  
- Novelty is moderate: stacking depth from recurrent nets applied to tree-structured recursion plus untied leaves/ReLU/dropout is a sensible, original combination.  
- Technical soundness is good: architecture and backprop are well-defined and stabilization choices are reasonable.  
- Empirical limitations: single dataset evaluation, no significance testing, and limited ablations to separate effects of depth, untying, activations, and regularization.  
- Lacks runtime, memory, and quantitative training-stability comparisons against RNTN/DCNN and other baselines.  
- Paper is clear and well organized with helpful notation and figures, but should clarify supervision/loss details and report exact parameter counts and variance.  
- Strengths: clear motivation and architecture, solid engineering (untied leaves, ReLU, dropout), strong empirical gains at fixed budgets, and informative qualitative analyses.  
- Weaknesses: narrow empirical scope, incomplete ablations, missing comparisons to gated/tree-LSTM and strong sequential baselines, and anecdotal interpretability analyses.  
- Key questions for authors: impact of auxiliary losses on intermediate layers, shared vs independent dropout masks, effect of fine-tuning embeddings, and generalization to other compositional tasks/trees.  
- Suggestions: perform ablations (ReLU vs tanh, untying, dropout, output at all layers), report statistical significance and variance across seeds, include runtime/memory comparisons, and evaluate on additional datasets/tasks.  
- Overall recommendation: lean accept — useful, well-executed architectural extension with convincing SST gains, but would be stronger with broader validation and deeper ablations.
