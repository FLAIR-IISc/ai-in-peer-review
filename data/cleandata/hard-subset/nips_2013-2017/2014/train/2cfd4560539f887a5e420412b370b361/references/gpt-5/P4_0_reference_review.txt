Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers (“depth in space”) on top of tree-structured composition (“depth in structure”). Within each layer, composition is standard tree-structured recurrence over binary parse trees; across layers, each node passes its representation upward via inter-layer connections, with supervision applied only from the last recursive layer. The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes, mapping pre-trained word vectors (X) to a hidden phrase space (H) via separate parameters, enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) for internal nodes.

The work builds on recursive nets over trees [Goller & Kuchler 1996; Socher et al. 2011–2013], distinguishes structural depth from network depth analogously to deep recurrent nets [Schmidhuber 1992; El Hihi & Bengio 1995; Hermans & Schrauwen 2013], and contrasts with multiplicative composition (MV-RNN, RNTN) and CNN-based sentence models (DCNN). Empirically, on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with phrase-level supervision [Socher et al. 2013], deep stacked recursive layers outperform shallow TreeRNNs with matched parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art results among published systems at the time, while using fixed 300d word2vec embeddings and dropout regularization. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a parse path; layer-wise nearest neighbors) suggest layers capture progressively different aspects of compositionality (lexical similarity at lower layers, syntactic patterns in the middle, and sentiment polarity/intensity at the top).

Quality:
- Method: The architecture is well-defined. Layer i computes h(i) via standard binary-tree composition plus a linear skip from h(i−1) at the same node; outputs y are predicted from the final layer, at all nodes with labels. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and the use of ReLUs with activation regularization are sensible and technically sound. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks per sentence/layer, small L2 on weights and activations for stability, and early stopping.
- Empirics: The study controls for total parameter count when varying depth, showing consistent gains for moderate depth (2–4 layers) on SST fine-grained and binary settings. The paper reports new SOTA versus RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. The qualitative analyses are informative. However, ablations are limited: the contribution attributed to dropout is supported by a single preliminary comparison; the effect of untying leaves, ReLUs vs tanh, and pre-trained vs learned embeddings are not isolated. No statistical significance tests or variance across runs are provided. Runtime or memory overhead from stacking layers is not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with precise mathematical definitions and thorough training details. Figures and tables (as described) appear to convey the architecture and analyses well. Minor notational shifts (x vs h; tying vs untying across sections) could be tightened. The description of dropout (sentence-level shared masks) is clear but could specify whether masks vary across epochs.

Originality:
Stacking recursive layers to introduce depth in space over tree structures is a natural but nontrivial extension of prior recursive nets. The explicit untying of leaf and internal node parameters to enable ReLU activations for phrase representations is a useful design. While deep recurrence has precedents in sequence models, its application to tree-structured composition with these training choices and analyses appears novel relative to contemporaneous recursive models (MV-RNN, RNTN). The qualitative diagnostics are also a valuable addition.

Significance:
On a standard benchmark (SST), the approach yields improved accuracy versus strong baselines and prior SOTA, indicating practical value. The demonstration that depth (under a fixed parameter budget) helps over width in tree composition is an important empirical takeaway. That said, evaluation is restricted to one dataset/task and one structural formalism (constituency trees), limiting claims of generality. The paper relies on external pre-trained embeddings; comparability to baselines that do/don’t use such embeddings should be discussed more explicitly.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of deep stacking for tree-structured composition with principled training choices (ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad).
- Controlled comparisons showing depth helps over width under fixed parameters.
- Strong results on SST; uses only supervised error without pretraining and keeps embeddings fixed.
- Informative qualitative experiments that shed light on what layers learn.

Weaknesses:
- Limited ablation: no systematic study of (i) untying leaves vs tying, (ii) ReLU vs tanh, (iii) fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings, (iv) outputs attached to all layers vs last layer only.
- Single dataset; no statistical significance or variability across runs; no computational cost analysis.
- Fairness/comparability to baselines regarding use of large external pretraining (Google News word2vec) is not fully addressed.
- Dependence on parse trees is not analyzed for robustness to parser errors or alternative tree formalisms.

Questions for authors:
- How much of the gain over shallow TreeRNNs remains if leaves/internal parameters are tied, or if tanh activations are used? Please provide ablations.
- Do results change when fine-tuning embeddings? Conversely, how do baselines perform with the same fixed 300d word2vec initialization?
- What is the effect of attaching supervision to all hidden layers (deeply-supervised variant)?
- What are training time, memory, and inference costs as depth increases?
- How sensitive are results to the dropout rate and to using node-specific vs sentence-shared dropout masks?

Suggestions:
- Add ablations for untying, activation choice, embedding tuning, and output connectivity.
- Report mean/std over multiple runs and simple significance tests.
- Provide a brief complexity/runtime analysis.
- Evaluate on an additional dataset (e.g., binary sentiment without phrase labels) or a dependency-tree variant to test generality and robustness.
- Clarify notations and reference formatting; consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, technically sound extension introducing depth in space for tree-structured models.
- Strong empirical results and a well-controlled depth-vs-width study.
- Useful qualitative analyses elucidating layer roles.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Evidence is limited to a single dataset; ablations and statistical analyses are insufficient to isolate contributions.
- Comparability to baselines regarding external pretrained embeddings is not fully controlled.
- No analysis of computational trade-offs or robustness to parsing quality.

Overall recommendation:
A solid empirical contribution with a clear architectural idea and promising results on a canonical benchmark. Strengthened ablations and broader evaluation would solidify the case, but the current submission meets NeurIPS standards for clarity and technical soundness and offers a meaningful step forward in compositional modeling.
