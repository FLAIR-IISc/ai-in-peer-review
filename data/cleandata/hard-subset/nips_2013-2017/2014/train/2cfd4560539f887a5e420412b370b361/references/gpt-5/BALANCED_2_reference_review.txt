Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers over tree structures to separate “depth in structure” from “depth in space.” Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up along a given binary parse tree with shared parameters, and across layers, node representations are passed to corresponding nodes in higher layers via feedforward connections. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal node parameters in the first layer, enabling different transformations for words vs. phrases, and adopt rectified linear units and dropout.

The work is positioned as the structural analogue of stacked recurrent networks for sequences [10–12], and builds on recursive neural networks over trees for parsing and semantics [6, 7, 8, 14]. It contrasts with prior compositional models such as MV-RNN [16] and RNTN [8], which increase expressivity of the local composition function via matrices/tensors, by instead deepening the architecture via multiple stacked recursive layers. The empirical evaluation is on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) [8], reporting improved fine-grained (5-class) and binary sentiment accuracy relative to previously published results, including RNTN, Paragraph Vectors [17], and DCNN [18]. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest neighbors by layer) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: clean extension of “stacked RNNs” to tree structures, with a straightforward learning procedure (backprop through space and through structure).
- Solid engineering choices (rectifiers, dropout, AdaGrad, pretrained embeddings, untied leaf/internal parameters) that yield strong performance without pretraining or finetuning embeddings.
- Empirically demonstrates that depth (with parameter count held roughly constant) can outperform width for tree-structured composition; achieves SOTA on SST at time of writing.
- Useful qualitative analyses that probe what each layer encodes.
- Good discussion of training stability and regularization choices (dropout mask sharing across nodes, small L2 on activations).

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of evaluation: all results are on a single dataset/task (SST). No tests on other tree-structured NLP tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) or parsing to demonstrate generality.
- Attribution of gains is under-explored. Depth, rectifiers, dropout, pretrained vectors, and untying are changed simultaneously; ablations are partial. It remains unclear how much of the gain over RNTN/DCNN is due to depth vs. other factors.
- No statistical significance tests or variance over multiple runs; hyperparameter tuning protocol is not fully specified for all baselines.
- Some clarity issues: a missing detail in weight initialization (“All other weights are initialized as .”), and notational switches between x/h that may confuse readers. Complexity/runtime and memory implications of deeper stacks are not discussed.
- Output supervision is only on the top layer; alternative connections (e.g., auxiliary losses at lower layers) are not empirically compared.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is well-defined, training procedure is standard and appropriate. The shared dropout mask per layer per sentence for trees is a reasonable design choice. However, deeper analysis of optimization (e.g., gradient norms across layers and depth) would strengthen claims about mitigating vanishing gradients via tree-level supervision.
- Empirical support: The SST results are promising and consistent with the hypothesis that stacked recursive layers help. Nevertheless, stronger ablation studies (e.g., removing untying, switching to tanh, removing dropout, finetuning embeddings) are needed to isolate contributions. Reporting means/stdev over seeds and significance would improve confidence.
- Completeness: The work is more than a position paper and provides both quantitative and qualitative evidence, but remains narrow in task diversity.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. Equations (1)–(5) are clear and the architectural figure (referenced) is helpful.
- Please fix the incomplete weight initialization sentence and clarify initialization ranges. Also clarify whether the same dropout mask is shared across layers or only across nodes within a layer, and whether masks differ across training epochs.

Originality
- Moderate. Stacking is a known strategy in sequence RNNs; applying it to tree-structured recursive nets with careful engineering is a natural but meaningful extension. Untying leaves/internals and using rectifiers are reasonable, partly known ideas adapted thoughtfully to trees.

Significance
- Measurable improvement on a canonical benchmark with a simple, reproducible architecture is valuable. If shown to transfer beyond SST, this could influence future compositional models by encouraging “depth-in-space” for tree architectures rather than increasingly complex local composition functions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablation studies: (a) shallow vs deep with identical rectifier/dropout/pretrained settings; (b) tied vs untied leaf/internal weights; (c) rectifier vs tanh; (d) with/without dropout; (e) with/without embedding finetuning. Report means/std over ≥3 seeds and significance tests.
- Evaluate on at least one additional tree-structured task (e.g., paraphrase detection [9], entailment, or phrase-level sentiment on another dataset) to demonstrate generality.
- Compare variants that connect output heads to intermediate layers (multi-task or deep supervision) to test the design choice of supervising only the final layer.
- Report training time, parameter counts, and memory usage as depth increases; include a brief complexity analysis.
- Add robustness analyses: performance vs. sentence length, depth of parse, and accuracy with predicted vs. gold parses.
- Clarify implementation details: exact initialization distribution, regularization strengths, and whether the shared dropout mask is per-layer per-sentence only.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea with empirical SOTA on SST.
- Demonstrates that depth-in-space benefits tree composition beyond depth-in-structure.
- Useful qualitative insights into layer roles.
- Likely to be adopted due to implementation simplicity and compatibility with standard toolchains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to stacked RNNs and prior tree models; limited theoretical insight.
- Narrow empirical scope (single dataset); insufficient ablations hinder attribution of gains.
- Missing significance/variance and some clarity issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a straightforward and effective extension of recursive nets that achieves strong results and offers insight into compositional representations. With additional ablations, broader evaluation, and clarity fixes, it would be a solid NeurIPS contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
