- Proposal: Deep-RNNs stack multiple tree-structured recursive layers and untie leaf/internal parameters to map word vectors into a phrase space, enabling ReLUs and large fixed embeddings.
- Training: Uses Adagrad, shared dropout across nodes within a layer, and small L2 penalties on weights and activations.
- Main empirical claim: On SST, engineering choices boost shallow RNNs, stacking improves performance at fixed parameter budgets up to an optimal depth, and the best model is state-of-the-art.
- Qualitative claim: Layer-wise analyses suggest different layers capture different compositional aspects (e.g., intensity vs polarity), but evidence is anecdotal.
- Soundness: Model is a straightforward, well-motivated extension with clear equations; untied leaf/internal parametrization is sensible and justified.
- Empirics quality: Standard SST setup with strong baselines; depth comparisons hold parameter counts approximately fixed; hyperparameters and training choices are described.
- Missing/weak experimental pieces: Ablations are partial (untangling untying vs ReLU vs dropout vs embeddings is incomplete); no statistical significance tests; single-dataset evaluation.
- Analysis limitations: Qualitative analyses are insightful but limited; stronger systematic probing (layer-wise tests, correlation with sentiment intensity) is needed.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized; minor clarifications requested (parse tree source, parameter counts, variance over runs, exact dropout masking).
- Originality: Stacking tree-RNN layers is a novel extension; untying parameters is useful though related to prior ideas; ReLUs/dropout are incremental but practical.
- Significance: Advances SOTA and presents a general idea likely useful for other structured NLP tasks, but narrow empirical scope reduces broader impact.
- Strengths: Clear architectural contribution, strong results, careful parameter budgeting, practical design choices, and initial qualitative insights.
- Weaknesses: Evaluation limited to one task, incomplete ablations, lack of significance/variance reporting, limited qualitative scope, no experiments with intermediate supervision.
- Questions for authors: Provide mean±std and significance tests; full ablation (untied vs tied, ReLU vs tanh, dropout, embeddings finetune); sensitivity to shared-dropout; computational overhead; results on other tree tasks.
- Pros for acceptance: Simple, general, well-motivated idea with SOTA results and clear presentation likely to influence follow-up work.
- Cons for acceptance: Narrow empirical validation, limited ablations, and anecdotal evidence for layer-wise roles.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—technically sound and useful but would benefit from expanded experiments and ablations.
- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
