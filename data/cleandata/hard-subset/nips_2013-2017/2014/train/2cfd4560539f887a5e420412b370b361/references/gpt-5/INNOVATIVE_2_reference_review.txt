Summary
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), a stacked architecture for tree-structured inputs that explicitly separates “depth in structure” (the usual recursion over parse trees) from “depth in space” (stacking multiple recursive layers). Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up along the tree with shared parameters; across layers, each node passes its hidden state vertically via learned inter-layer connections, enabling successive layers to refine compositional representations. The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes, allowing different transformations for word-to-phrase and phrase-to-phrase mappings, and they adopt ReLU activations with dropout for stability and regularization. They evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), keeping pre-trained word embeddings fixed, and report improved fine-grained and binary sentiment accuracy compared to shallow recursive nets and prior tensor/matrix-vector variants (RNTN, MV-RNN) and Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and layer-wise nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture distinct aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work: Recursive nets over trees (e.g., RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN) have been widely used for compositional semantics; stacked depth has been explored for recurrent nets in time. This paper brings the “depth in space” idea to tree-structured models, with practical training choices (ReLU + dropout, AdaGrad), and demonstrates state-of-the-art SST performance.

Strengths
- Novel direction: A clear and simple but nontrivial extension—stacking recursive layers with explicit inter-layer connections—to bring hierarchical abstraction to tree models. This is a natural and potentially general pattern beyond sentiment.
- Empirical gains: New state-of-the-art on SST fine-grained and competitive binary results, while fixing word embeddings and avoiding heavy tensor compositions. The parameter-budget-controlled comparisons show depth (up to a point) beats width.
- Practical training recipe: Untying leaves from internals to enable ReLU, sharing dropout masks across tree nodes, L2 on activations for stability, and AdaGrad. These design choices are justified and reproducible.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: Input perturbation traces and layer-wise nearest neighbors provide evidence that layers specialize (e.g., sentiment intensity vs polarity), supporting the conceptual motivation.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Only SST is used. The claims about general compositionality would be stronger with additional datasets/tasks (e.g., other sentiment corpora, semantic relatedness, paraphrase, NLI).
- Insufficient ablations: Improvements confound multiple changes (pre-trained embeddings, ReLU, leaf/internal untying, dropout, depth). The paper acknowledges dropout’s contribution but does not systematically isolate the effect of each factor (e.g., shallow vs deep with/without dropout, with/without untying).
- Missing comparisons/variants: It would be informative to try connecting outputs from all layers, residual/skip connections, or gating mechanisms to alleviate the degradation at larger depths. Training a binary-specific model could clarify the headroom in that setting.
- Statistical rigor: No significance testing, and little discussion of variance across runs given known sensitivity in deep recursive models.
- Efficiency/complexity: No training/inference cost analysis vs baselines (particularly RNTN), despite stacking multiple layers. Practical cost matters for wider adoption.
- Interpretability claims are preliminary: Qualitative analyses are interesting but small-scale; more systematic probing would strengthen the “different layers capture different aspects” claim.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The model is well specified; training details are clear. The parameter-controlled comparison is thoughtful. However, the experimental validation is narrow (single dataset) and lacks comprehensive ablations and statistical tests. The qualitative analyses are valuable but limited in scope.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The distinction between depth in structure vs depth in space is well motivated. Training choices are explicit. Suggestions:
- Clarify and justify the shared dropout mask across all nodes within a layer for a sentence (why this choice vs independent dropout?).
- Provide a brief complexity analysis (parameters, time per sentence) and training curves or variance across seeds.
- Consider adding an ablation table in the appendix to disentangle the role of untying, ReLU, dropout, and depth.

Originality
Moderately strong. Stacking recursive layers with vertical connections to bring “deep in space” to tree models is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior tree RNN variants. Using ReLU and dropout in recursive nets and untying leaves are sensible but incremental. The conceptual framing of hierarchical abstraction across layers for compositionality is the most original contribution.

Significance
Moderate to strong for NLP/ML practitioners working with structured inputs. Demonstrating that deep-in-space helps in tree models and achieving SOTA on SST (while fixing embeddings) are meaningful. The architectural idea is simple enough to be adopted broadly. Broader significance would increase with evidence on more tasks and stronger ablations.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from depth vs untying vs ReLU vs dropout? Can you include a small ablation grid?
- Does connecting the output to all layers help or hurt performance?
- How sensitive are results to the shared dropout mask choice?
- What are training/inference times compared to RNTN and shallow RNNs?
- Does fine-tuning word embeddings further improve DRNN?
- Can you report variance across runs and significance of gains?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new and promising architectural direction (stacked recursive layers) for compositionality.
- Achieves state-of-the-art on SST with a comparatively simple model.
- Clear exposition; practical, reproducible training details.
- Qualitative analyses support the core hypothesis about layer specialization.

Cons for acceptance
- Single-dataset evaluation; limited ablations and no statistical tests.
- Performance degrades at larger depth; limited analysis of why and how to fix (e.g., residual/gating).
- Missing efficiency analysis and broader comparisons to alternative deepening strategies.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper advances a novel architectural idea for tree-structured models and demonstrates clear empirical benefits. While the evaluation is narrower than ideal, the conceptual contribution is likely to stimulate follow-on work and is worth inclusion.

Confidence
Medium.
