Summary
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), which stack multiple recursive (tree-structured) layers to introduce “depth in space” on top of “depth in structure.” The model also “unties” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes to map word embeddings to a distinct phrase space and enable rectified linear units (ReLUs). Training uses dropout, AdaGrad, and small L2 penalties on weights and activations. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets with matched parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art results over RNTN and other baselines in fine-grained and binary sentiment. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and layer-wise nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality (lexical dominance, syntactic patterning/intensity, sentiment polarity).

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned as an analogue of stacked recurrent nets (depth in time vs. depth in space) and extends single-layer recursive models used in parsing, sentiment, and paraphrase detection. It compares against MV-RNN, RNTN, DCNN, and Paragraph Vectors, and shows gains despite using fixed pretrained word vectors and a relatively simple composition function. To my knowledge, explicitly stacking multiple tree-structured layers with shared structure within each layer is a novel and clean generalization beyond single-layer recursive nets and multiplicative variants.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The “depth in space for trees” idea is simple, orthogonal to composition choice, and broadly applicable to structured inputs. The untying of leaf/internal parameters to allow ReLUs is a practical, well-motivated tweak.
- Empirical impact: The model achieves new SOTA on SST (fine-grained) with a relatively simple architecture, improving over tensor/multiplicative models and sophisticated CNN/Paragraph Vector baselines.
- Fair comparisons w.r.t. capacity: Depth vs. width trade-offs are explored under roughly fixed parameter budgets, demonstrating consistent benefits from added depth up to a point.
- Training pragmatics: Clear recipe (dropout, AdaGrad, ReLUs, simple initialization) and early stopping; this is valuable to practitioners.
- Qualitative insights: The layer-wise perturbation and nearest-neighbor analyses compellingly support the claim that layers specialize (lexical, syntactic/intensity, sentiment polarity).

Weaknesses
- Narrow empirical scope: All results are on a single dataset/task (SST). Given the generality of the proposal, evaluation on additional tree-structured tasks (e.g., phrase/sentence entailment, paraphrase detection, parsing/fine-grained constituency labels) would strengthen the case for broad utility.
- Limited ablations: The paper attributes gains to depth and dropout, but does not systematically ablate key design choices: untying leaves vs. tied, ReLU vs. tanh, activation L2 penalty, tying vs. untying V across layers, output connection only to the top vs. all layers. A small ablation table would clarify which ingredients matter most.
- Statistical rigor: No report of variance across runs or significance testing. Depth-related improvements could be sensitive to initialization/training noise; error bars would help.
- Computational analysis: Deeper models are narrower to keep parameters constant, but training/inference time and memory vs. depth are not discussed. A brief cost analysis would aid adoption.
- Qualitative analyses are anecdotal: Insightful but limited to a couple of examples. A more systematic probe (e.g., layer-wise linear probes for sentiment/intensity/negation) would be stronger.
- Clarity details: While training details are mostly clear, some specifics could be expanded (e.g., exact architectures per result row, depth at which degradation begins, justification for sharing dropout masks across tree nodes within a layer, and rationale for L2 penalty on activations).

Quality
Technically sound with a straightforward extension of backprop through structure to stacked layers. Training choices are reasonable and practical. Claims are supported by quantitative gains and informative qualitative analyses, though stronger ablations and broader tasks would improve completeness.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The conceptual framing (depth in structure vs. depth in space) is crisp. Adding a concise schematic of gradients flowing “through structure” and “through space” would help. A compact ablation table and clearer reporting of hyperparameters per result would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately high. Prior work made recursive composition richer via multiplicative/tensor operators; this work introduces stacking across layers in a tree setting, which is a distinct and underexplored axis of depth at the time. The leaf/internal untying to enable ReLUs is also a neat contribution.

Significance
High for NLP/representation learning. The idea is simple, extensible, and shows state-of-the-art results with good training practice. It opens a line of work on deep tree architectures later expanded by gated/residual variants.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: tied vs. untied leaves, ReLU vs. tanh, with/without dropout, with/without activation L2, top-only vs. all-layers output connection.
- Report mean/std over multiple seeds and basic significance tests.
- Evaluate on at least one additional structured task (e.g., paraphrase detection as in prior RAEs, or a tree-structured entailment dataset).
- Provide a brief runtime/memory analysis vs. depth and discuss potential mitigations (e.g., residual/skip connections, layer normalization).
- Consider probing tasks to quantify what each layer captures (negation, intensity, syntax).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general, and novel architectural idea (stacked tree layers).
- Strong empirical improvements on a standard benchmark with a simpler operator than prior SOTA.
- Insightful qualitative analyses supporting the compositional interpretation of layers.
- Practical training recipe likely to influence follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Single-task evaluation and limited ablations leave open how much credit goes to depth vs. other choices (ReLU, dropout, untying).
- Lack of variance/significance reporting.
- Limited discussion of computational costs and scalability.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some experimental incompleteness, the core idea is novel, the empirical gains are solid, and the qualitative evidence is compelling. The paper is likely to spur follow-up work on deep, layered tree architectures and compositional representation learning.
