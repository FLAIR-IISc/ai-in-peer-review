

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper proposes a novel architecture called Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), which combines depth in structure (from recursive neural networks) with depth in space (from stacking layers in feedforward networks). The authors argue that recursive neural networks are inherently deep in structure but lack hierarchical representation learning across layers, so they propose stacking multiple recursive layers to create a hierarchy of representations. The paper demonstrates that DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) task, achieving new state-of-the-art results, and provides qualitative evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The paper builds on the work of Socher et al. on recursive neural networks and recent advances in deep feedforward networks, but makes a novel conceptual leap by combining these two notions of depth. While deep recurrent networks have been explored previously, extending this concept to recursive networks represents a fresh perspective on compositionality in language processing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong novelty:** The core idea of stacking recursive layers to create depth in space while maintaining structural processing is genuinely novel. The paper clearly articulates the distinction between depth in structure (from recursion) and depth in space (from stacking layers), which is a conceptual contribution beyond incremental improvements.

2. **Significant empirical results:** The DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance on the SST fine-grained sentiment task, outperforming previous recursive networks and other baselines like Paragraph Vectors. The improvements are consistent across multiple experimental setups.

3. **Compelling qualitative analysis:** The input perturbation experiments and nearest neighbor analysis provide strong evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality, supporting the paper's central claim about hierarchical representation learning.

4. **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear figures and explanations that make the architecture accessible. The methodology is described thoroughly enough to be reproducible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The paper only evaluates on the SST sentiment analysis task, which limits the generalizability of the findings. While the authors mention future work on broader tasks, the current results don't demonstrate the approach's applicability to other NLP problems.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide adequate ablation experiments to isolate the contribution of depth specifically. It's unclear whether the improvements come from the depth itself, the untying of leaves and internals, or other architectural choices.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis to explain why the depth in space would improve compositionality, beyond the intuitive argument.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core contribution—stacking recursive layers to create depth in space while maintaining structural processing—is a bold and novel idea that significantly advances the field of compositional neural networks. While the evaluation is limited to sentiment analysis, the qualitative evidence supporting the hierarchical representation learning is compelling, and the paper clearly articulates the conceptual novelty.

The paper's strengths in novelty and empirical validation outweigh the weaknesses in scope and theoretical justification. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find the paper's core idea sufficiently groundbreaking to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel architecture combining two notions of depth (structure and space)
- State-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark
- Strong qualitative evidence of hierarchical compositionality
- Clear conceptual contribution to understanding compositionality in neural networks

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited to a single NLP task (sentiment analysis)
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate depth as the key factor
- Lack of theoretical justification for the approach

The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of compositionality in neural networks by introducing a novel architectural approach that combines two complementary notions of depth. While future work should extend this to broader tasks and provide more theoretical analysis, the current work presents a compelling new direction for research on compositional language understanding. The paper's novelty and empirical validation make it a strong candidate for NIPS.
