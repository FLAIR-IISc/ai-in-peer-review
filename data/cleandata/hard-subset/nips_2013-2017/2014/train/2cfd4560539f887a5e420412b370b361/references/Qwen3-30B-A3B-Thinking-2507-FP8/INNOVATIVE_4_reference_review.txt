

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper introduces a novel architecture called "deep recursive neural networks" (DRNNs) that combines depth in structure (from recursive neural networks) with depth in space (from stacked feedforward layers). The authors make a clear conceptual distinction between these two types of depth, which they argue has been overlooked in previous work on recursive neural networks (RNNs) and deep recurrent networks. DRNNs are constructed by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other, with each layer processing the structured input (e.g., parse trees) and passing representations to the next layer. This approach allows different layers to capture different aspects of compositionality, as demonstrated through qualitative analysis on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank.

The paper makes a strong conceptual contribution by explicitly addressing the distinction between depth in structure (which depends on the depth of the input tree) and depth in space (which depends on the number of stacked layers). This is a novel perspective that goes beyond simply stacking recursive layers, as previous work on "deep" RNNs has primarily focused on temporal depth. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their architecture, distinguishing it from prior work such as the recursive neural tensor network (RNTN) and shallow recursive networks.

The experimental results are compelling, showing that DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks of comparable size on the fine-grained sentiment analysis task (49.5% accuracy vs. 48.1% for shallow RNNs), achieving new state-of-the-art results on the SST dataset. The qualitative analysis is particularly strong, with the input perturbation experiments suggesting that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., first layer captures word-level features, second layer captures intensity, and third layer captures sentiment). The nearest neighbor analysis across layers further supports this interpretation, showing how phrase representations evolve from word-based similarity to sentiment-based similarity across layers.

The paper's novelty is significant in the context of NIPS proceedings. While recursive neural networks have been studied extensively (e.g., Socher et al., 2013), the specific approach of stacking recursive layers to create "depth in space" while maintaining "depth in structure" represents a fresh conceptual direction. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior deep recurrent networks (which operate on temporal sequences) and standard recursive networks (which operate on a single layer of structure).

The paper has several strengths that align with NIPS priorities:
1. **Strong conceptual novelty**: The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is a bold new perspective.
2. **Clear empirical validation**: The experiments provide convincing evidence that the architecture works better than alternatives.
3. **Meaningful qualitative analysis**: The input perturbation and nearest neighbor analyses offer valuable insights into how compositionality is processed across layers.
4. **Potential for broader impact**: The architecture could be applied to other structured prediction tasks beyond sentiment analysis.

Minor weaknesses include:
- Limited ablation studies to isolate the contribution of depth versus the specific weight-sharing scheme.
- The paper doesn't fully explain why deeper networks with the same number of parameters perform better beyond the qualitative analysis.
- The connection to broader NLP applications beyond sentiment analysis is somewhat limited.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions over methodological perfection, I believe these weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The paper makes a clear, novel distinction that has the potential to reshape how we think about depth in structured neural networks. The qualitative analysis provides compelling evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality, which is a valuable insight for understanding how neural networks process language.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The experimental setup is appropriate for the task, and the results are convincing. While the improvements over previous work on SST are incremental in terms of absolute accuracy, the conceptual contribution is substantial and represents a new direction for research on recursive neural networks.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by introducing a novel architecture that combines depth in structure with depth in space. The qualitative analysis provides compelling evidence that the architecture captures different aspects of compositionality across layers. Despite minor methodological limitations, the paper's bold conceptual contribution and clear validation make it a strong candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance as it represents a meaningful new direction in the field of compositional neural architectures for language processing.
