This paper introduces Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), extending recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The motivation stems from the success of deep recurrent networks, which combine depth in time and space, and the authors aim to achieve a similar benefit for tree-structured data. They apply their DRNNs to fine-grained sentiment detection on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improved performance over shallow recursive networks and existing state-of-the-art methods. The paper also includes insightful qualitative analyses examining how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of stacking recursive layers to create depth beyond the tree structure is a natural and valuable extension to existing recursive neural network models. This hasn’t been extensively explored previously, particularly in the context of combining structural and spatial depth.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance on the SST dataset, demonstrating a clear benefit from increasing depth. The ablation studies and comparisons to strong baselines are well-conducted and convincing.
* **Thorough Analysis:** The qualitative analyses, including input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase examination, provide valuable insights into the behavior of the different layers and support the claim that each layer learns different aspects of compositionality.  These analyses go beyond simply reporting performance numbers and help to build intuition about *why* the models work.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. Figures are helpful for visualizing the architecture and experimental setup.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of pretrained word vectors, dropout regularization, and AdaGrad optimization are all sensible choices and contribute to the robustness of the results. The untying of leaves and internals is a useful modification to standard RNNs.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (SST) and a single task (sentiment analysis). While the results are strong on SST, it’s unclear how well the DRNNs generalize to other tasks or datasets with different structures.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The details of hyperparameter tuning (beyond dropout rates) are not fully specified.  A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter search process would strengthen the credibility of the results.  For instance, how was the number of layers chosen, and what were the results of different depths?
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of training DRNNs compared to shallower models. Deeper networks are likely to be more computationally expensive, and this trade-off should be acknowledged.
* **Discussion of Vanishing Gradients:** While the authors mention alleviating vanishing gradients via the richer supervision in SST, a more detailed discussion of strategies used to mitigate vanishing gradients (beyond dropout and L2 regularization) would be beneficial, especially given the depth of the models.
* **Untying Justification:** While the untying of leaves and internals is empirically effective, a stronger theoretical justification for this design choice would be appreciated.  Why is it important to represent words and phrases in different spaces?




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to existing recursive neural network models.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear benefit from the proposed approach.
* The qualitative analyses provide valuable insights into the behavior of the models.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset and task.
* The discussion of computational cost and hyperparameter tuning is incomplete.
* The theoretical justification for certain design choices (e.g., untying leaves and internals) could be stronger.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of recursive neural networks and compositional semantics. The proposed DRNNs are a promising new architecture that achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task. While the evaluation is limited and some aspects of the methodology could be further elaborated upon, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Expand the evaluation to include other datasets or tasks to assess the generalizability of the DRNNs.
*   Provide a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning, including the search space and the criteria used to select the best hyperparameters.
*   Discuss the computational cost of training DRNNs and compare it to shallower models.
*   Elaborate on the strategies used to mitigate vanishing gradients, particularly in the context of deep architectures.
*   Strengthen the theoretical justification for the untying of leaves and internals.
* Consider adding an ablation study of the V matrix in equation (4) to show its importance.
