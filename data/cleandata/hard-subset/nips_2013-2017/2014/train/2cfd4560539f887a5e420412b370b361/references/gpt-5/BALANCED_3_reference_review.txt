Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs) for compositional semantics over tree structures. Building on standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) applied to parse trees [Goller & Kuchler, Socher et al.], the authors introduce “depth in space” by stacking multiple recursive layers, analogous to stacking recurrent layers in time for deep RNNs [Schmidhuber; El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen]. They also “untie” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes, enabling distinct transformations from word to phrase space and phrase-to-phrase composition, and employ rectified linear units and dropout. Training uses AdaGrad and phrase-level supervision on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST). Empirically, DRNNs outperform single-layer tree RNNs of similar parameter counts and achieve new state-of-the-art results on fine-grained sentiment; qualitative analyses (input perturbations; nearest neighbors across layers) suggest different layers capture complementary aspects of compositionality.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for distinguishing depth in structure vs. depth in space and for stacking recursive layers. The architecture is simple, principled, and easily reproducible.
- Solid engineering choices (ReLUs, pretrained word embeddings, dropout, AdaGrad) adapted to tree-structured computation, with useful practical details (e.g., shared dropout masks per sentence/layer; activation L2 penalty).
- Quantitative improvements over strong baselines (RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN) on SST fine-grained and binary tasks; consistent depth-vs-width trends under roughly fixed parameter budgets.
- Qualitative analyses are thoughtful and illustrate the claimed layer-wise specialization.
- Writing is generally clear and the paper situates itself with respect to prior recursive and deep recurrent work.

Weaknesses
- Scope: All quantitative results are on a single dataset (SST). Claims about compositionality and generality would be stronger with additional tasks (e.g., paraphrase, textual entailment/SICK, sentiment on other corpora) or with dependency trees.
- Ablations: Improvements may conflate multiple factors. The paper reports that a shallow RNN improves significantly simply by untying leaves/internals, using pretrained vectors, ReLUs, and dropout. A systematic ablation is needed to quantify each contribution (tied vs. untied; ReLU vs. tanh; dropout on/off; pretrained vs. random; activation L2 vs. gradient clipping).
- Fairness of comparisons: Some baselines (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) are sentence-level models trained without phrase-level supervision, whereas DRNNs use phrase-level supervision. This advantage should be explicitly discussed, and if possible, matched training regimes should be compared (e.g., DRNN trained only on sentence labels; or re-running baselines with phrase labels).
- Evaluation details: It is not fully clear whether training uses all node labels (as implied) while evaluation is at the sentence (root) level, which is the standard SST setup; please clarify the loss (sum over nodes?), class weighting, and whether any label pruning is applied. Report variance over multiple runs and statistical significance.
- Depth/width control: Keeping parameter count fixed while increasing depth is reasonable, but a complementary experiment holding width fixed would help isolate optimization vs. capacity effects. Also, consider connecting outputs to all layers to test whether representational synergy improves performance.
- Limited analysis of tree properties: It would be valuable to analyze performance as a function of sentence length, tree depth, or specific compositional phenomena (e.g., negation, intensification).
- Related work: While prior recursive models are well covered, additional references on tree-structured dropout/regularization and alternative gating mechanisms (e.g., later Tree-LSTM) could be acknowledged to contextualize choices; although Tree-LSTM is contemporaneous/after, discussing gating vs. stacking as design axes would be helpful.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The model and training procedure are standard and carefully described; the qualitative analyses are insightful. However, lack of thorough ablations and single-dataset evaluation limit confidence in the source of gains and generality. Statistical testing and run-to-run variance are missing.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable. Suggestions:
- Specify the exact training objective (cross-entropy summed over which nodes?), class weighting, and whether dev/test evaluation is at the root only.
- Report parameter counts per configuration; give exact numbers in Table 1 for reproducibility.
- Clarify the shared dropout mask (per sentence and layer) and whether dropout is applied inter-layer as well.
- Release code or pseudo-code for backpropagation through structure across stacked layers.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking recursive layers is a natural extension of deep RNNs to tree structures; untying leaves vs. internals for ReLU compatibility is a useful design detail. The qualitative layer-probing analyses are a nice contribution. The novelty is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
Moderate. Achieving SOTA on SST with a simple architecture is valuable, and the results may encourage broader use of deeper tree models. Broader impact would be clearer with additional datasets and ablations isolating why depth helps.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Simple, well-motivated extension that improves over strong tree baselines.
- Careful engineering with practical guidance; reproducible.
- Insightful qualitative analyses supporting the compositionality claims.
- New SOTA on a widely used benchmark.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Evidence limited to one dataset; unclear generality.
- Missing ablations disentangling contributions of architecture vs. training tricks.
- Some baseline comparisons are not strictly apples-to-apples due to supervision differences.
- No variance/significance reporting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: tied vs. untied leaves/internals; tanh vs. ReLU; with/without dropout; pretrained vs. finetuned vs. random embeddings; output attached to last vs. all layers.
- Report multiple runs with mean±std and significance tests; analyze performance by sentence length/tree depth and specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., negation).
- Evaluate on at least one additional task/dataset (e.g., SICK, MR, SST-2 with sentence-only training) and, if possible, on dependency trees.
- Ensure comparable supervision when claiming SOTA; alternatively, present two settings (phrase-supervised vs. sentence-only) across methods.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a clean, well-executed idea with empirical gains and useful analyses, but would benefit from broader evaluation and ablation to solidify its contributions.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
