Proposes stacking multiple recursive layers on the same parse tree to distinguish structural depth (tree recursion) from spatial depth (stacked nonlinear layers).  
Unties leaf and internal node transformations, enabling rectifiers, and uses dropout, AdaGrad, and fixed pretrained word embeddings.  
Evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank with supervision at all tree nodes and root prediction for 5-way and binary sentiment.  
Deeper/narrower models outperform shallower/wider ones at comparable parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art vs. RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN.  
Qualitative analyses indicate different layers capture lexical, syntactic, and sentiment intensity/direction information.  
Novelty lies in bringing stacked (spatial) depth to tree-structured RNNs and analyzing its role in compositionality.  
Technical soundness: natural, coherent extension of recursive nets using standard backprop-through-structure and controlled depth-vs-width comparisons.  
Empirical support: consistent gains from added depth up to an optimal number of layers and improved stability with ReLUs, dropout, and pretrained embeddings.  
Missing ablation: effect of untying leaves vs. tying not evaluated.  
Missing ablation: ReLU vs. tanh comparison under the same training recipe is absent.  
Missing ablation: dropout vs. L2-only regularization comparison is not provided.  
Missing ablation: impact of connecting outputs from all layers or using only the last layer is not tested.  
Statistical significance and runtime/memory (efficiency) analyses are not reported.  
Clarity: well-written with clear motivation (structural vs. spatial depth) and reproducible training details.  
Suggested clarifications: specify exact layer widths per parameter budget, training time/compute, and whether full tree supervision is crucial.  
Originality: stacking tree composition layers and untying leaves are straightforward but meaningful innovations in recursive nets.  
Significance: advances SOTA on a prominent benchmark and shows spatial depth benefits tree-structured compositional models.  
Scope limitation: experiments are confined to a single dataset (SST) and binary constituency trees, limiting generality.  
Strengths: clear motivation, fair comparisons, SOTA gains without embedding finetuning, informative qualitative analyses, and practical training recipe.  
Weaknesses: limited evaluation breadth, missing key ablations, unclear attribution of gains to stacking vs. improved regularization/embeddings, and no efficiency analysis.  
Key questions for authors: effect of finetuning embeddings, impact of removing node-level supervision, results from connecting intermediate outputs or auxiliary losses, and sensitivity to shared dropout masks.  
Pros for acceptance: conceptually clean, timely extension with careful experiments and useful qualitative insights likely to spur follow-ups.  
Cons for acceptance: limited ablations and broader evaluations, some methodological choices not isolated, and no scalability/efficiency analysis.  
Overall assessment: reviewer leans toward acceptance—technically sound, clearly presented, and empirically effective despite missing analyses and broader validation.
