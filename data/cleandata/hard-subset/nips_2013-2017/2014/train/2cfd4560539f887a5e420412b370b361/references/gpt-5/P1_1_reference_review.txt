Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (Deep RNNs) that introduce “depth in space” by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other, in addition to the usual “depth in structure” that arises from tree composition. Within each layer, recursive composition proceeds bottom-up along a binary parse tree with shared weights; across layers, each node’s hidden state is passed to its counterpart in the next layer via a feedforward connection. The authors also “untie” leaves from internal nodes in the first layer, mapping word embeddings to a phrase space with separate parameters, which enables the use of rectified linear units (ReLU) and mitigates issues arising from mixing dense word vectors and sparse hidden states. Training uses Adagrad, dropout (with shared masks across all nodes in a layer for a sentence), and small L2 penalties on weights and activations to control explosions.

Experiments focus on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for 5-way fine-grained and binary sentiment classification, training with supervision at every node but attaching the output layer only to the top recursive layer. Deep RNNs with fixed parameter budgets outperform shallow recursive baselines with similar parameter counts, with best performance at 2–4 layers depending on width, and obtain state-of-the-art results on SST versus published RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN baselines at the time. Qualitative analyses include input perturbations to probe layer behavior and nearest-neighbor phrase searches to illustrate that deeper layers capture more sentiment-oriented abstractions.

Relation to prior work
The work is a natural extension of (i) tree-structured recursive nets in NLP (Socher et al.), and (ii) stacked recurrent nets that separate “depth in time” from “depth in space” (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). The novelty here is transplanting the notion of stacked depth to tree-structured recursion and showing empirical gains on a prominent compositionality benchmark, using ReLU and dropout with an untied leaf/internal design. Multiplicative composition models (MV-RNN, RNTN) and convolutional sentence models (DCNN) are included as baselines. Compared to earlier recursive models, the paper is clear about design choices (rectifiers, shared dropout, initialization) and offers qualitative diagnostics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is well-defined; backpropagation through both structure and layers is standard. Stabilization choices (ReLU, dropout, small L2 on activations) are reasonable. The leave/internal untying is carefully motivated.
- Empirical support: The main claims—stacking helps at fixed parameter budgets and achieves SOTA on SST—are supported. The comparison to a strong shallow baseline that also uses pretrained embeddings, ReLU, and dropout isolates depth’s effect to some degree. However:
  - Results are reported on a single dataset/task; no significance testing is provided.
  - Ablations are limited: the paper does not quantify the incremental contributions of untying, ReLU vs tanh, dropout, supervision at all nodes, or output connections to earlier layers.
  - No runtime/parameter efficiency comparisons vs RNTN and DCNN beyond parameter counts; training stability (e.g., gradient norms) is discussed qualitatively but not analyzed.
- Completeness: For the stated scope (SST), the study is self-contained, with reasonable training details. Some hyperparameter details (exact layer widths per parameter budget) are given, though code release is not mentioned.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and readable. The notation distinguishing x (word space) and h (phrase spaces across layers) is helpful. Figures effectively illustrate the model. Minor suggestions:
- Clarify whether supervision is applied at all nodes at the final layer only (as implied by Eq. 5 and text) and whether the same loss is used at all nodes.
- Report exact parameter counts per configuration in the results table for reproducibility, and include standard deviations/variance across runs.
- Explain the choice of shared dropout masks across nodes and whether independent masks were tried.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers for tree-structured models, combined with untying leaves, ReLU, and dropout, is a sensible and, to my knowledge at the time, novel combination for compositional semantics. The conceptual distinction between structural depth and spatial depth in trees parallels known ideas in recurrent nets, and applying it to tree nets is a meaningful step. The qualitative probes are simple but useful. The method itself is not a radical departure (no new objective or theoretical analysis), so the originality is moderate but legitimate.

Significance
SST is a widely used benchmark for compositional sentiment; achieving SOTA with a relatively simple extension is valuable. Demonstrating that stacked depth helps even at constant parameter budgets is a useful empirical result that others can build on. That said, the impact would be stronger with broader validations (e.g., paraphrase detection, entailment, semantic relation classification), comparisons to sequence LSTM/TreeLSTM baselines, and more thorough ablations. Still, practitioners interested in tree-based composition will likely find this architecture and training recipe useful.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that extends recursive nets with stacked depth.
- Solid engineering choices (untied leaf/internal mapping, ReLU, dropout, Adagrad) resulting in strong baselines and stable training.
- Demonstrated improvements over shallow counterparts at fixed parameter budgets and SOTA on SST.
- Qualitative analyses that provide insight into layer roles and compositionality.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset/task; no significance testing.
- Ablations incomplete; hard to disentangle contributions of depth vs regularization/activations and untying.
- No comparison to gated/tree LSTM-style models or strong sequential baselines; reliance on a single parser/tree family.
- Limited analysis of training efficiency and scaling; no discussion of inference speed or memory vs prior recursive/multiplicative models.
- Interpretability analyses are anecdotal; no quantitative probing.

Questions for the authors
- Did you try attaching auxiliary losses to intermediate layers? How did that affect performance and interpretability?
- How sensitive are results to using independent vs shared dropout masks across nodes?
- What is the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings, and do gains from depth persist?
- Do results hold on other compositional tasks (e.g., paraphrase detection, SNLI/RTE-style entailment) or on dependency trees?

Suggestions
- Add ablations for: ReLU vs tanh, with/without untying, with/without dropout, and output attached to all layers vs final only.
- Report statistical significance and variance across multiple seeds.
- Include a runtime/complexity comparison and memory footprint vs RNTN and DCNN.
- Evaluate on at least one additional dataset or task to bolster generality.
- Consider gradient clipping and report gradient norm dynamics to complement the L2-on-activations choice.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and timely idea; simple extension with measurable gains.
- New SOTA on a popular benchmark with a transparent, reproducible setup.
- Insightful qualitative analyses; good clarity and presentation.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow empirical scope; limited ablations and statistical rigor.
- Moderate novelty; architecture is an incremental extension.
- Missing comparisons to strong gated tree/sequence baselines.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a clear, useful architectural advance for tree-structured composition with solid empirical gains and good exposition. Broadening the experimental scope and deepening ablations would strengthen the contribution, but the current results are sufficiently convincing and of interest to the NeurIPS community.
