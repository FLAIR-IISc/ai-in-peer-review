- Introduces deep recursive neural nets that stack multiple recursive layers over tree structures, with vertical connections passing representations between layers.  
- Distinguishes “depth in structure” (tree recursion) from “depth in space” (stacked layers) and unties leaves vs. internal nodes to map word vectors into a phrase space.  
- Uses ReLUs, pretrained fixed word embeddings, backpropagation through structure and space, dropout with a shared mask per sentence/layer, AdaGrad, and small L2 penalties for stability.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, with deeper models outperforming shallow ones under similar parameter budgets.  
- Qualitative analyses suggest different layers specialize (e.g., intensity vs. polarity) though evidence is anecdotal.  
- Extends classic recursive nets by adapting the familiar idea of stacking from sequential RNNs to tree structures with careful parameterization.  
- Contrasts with prior multiplicative/compositional models (RNTN, MV-RNN) by increasing expressivity via depth rather than bilinear interactions.  
- Methodologically sound formulation and sensible design choices (ReLU, initialization, etc.), but parameter-budget control (narrower networks when deeper) confounds depth vs. width effects.  
- Empirically shows consistent gains from depth up to a point, but lacks statistical significance testing, variance across runs, and comprehensive ablations.  
- Training and architectural details are clearly described and largely reproducible, though some implementation choices need more justification (shared dropout mask, fixed embeddings).  
- Main novelty is the adaptation of stacking to tree-structured models and leaf/internal untying to enable ReLUs; overall an original combination of known components rather than a fundamentally new model class.  
- Significant because it provides a simple, practical way to scale tree models and attains strong results without expensive multiplicative tensors.  
- Limited scope: evaluation is confined to a single dataset/task (SST), so broader generalization is not demonstrated.  
- Incomplete ablations: effects of untying leaves, activation choices, dropout strategy, embedding fine-tuning, and layer-wise classifiers are not systematically quantified.  
- No reported variance or multi-seed experiments; comparisons rely on published baseline numbers that may use different training regimes.  
- Potential stability concerns: reliance on L2 penalty on activations and lack of analysis on gradient clipping or training dynamics.  
- The shared dropout mask per sentence/layer is unusual and unexplored for sensitivity or rationale.  
- Reviewer questions request experiments disentangling depth vs. width, sensitivity to activations and embedding tuning, impact of tying leaves, multi-seed results, dropout mask choice, and classifier connections to intermediate layers.  
- Pros: simple, well-motivated architecture with clear empirical gains, good clarity/reproducibility, and insightful qualitative analysis.  
- Cons: limited experiments/ablations, incremental novelty, and some training/comparison justifications lacking.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on stronger experimental evidence (ablations, variance reporting, and ideally an additional task).
