- Proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers to better capture hierarchical compositionality.  
- Applies DRNNs to Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment and reports new state-of-the-art performance.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) indicate different layers capture different aspects of compositional meaning.  
- Positions work within prior RNN/deep recurrent and sentiment analysis literature, citing relevant embedding and deep-architecture work.  
- Novelty: stacking recursive layers is a reasonable and potentially impactful extension of RNN architectures.  
- Strong empirical results: DRNNs convincingly outperform shallow and multiplicative RNN baselines on SST.  
- Evaluation includes both quantitative and qualitative analyses, offering insight into model behavior.  
- Clear methodology with detailed architecture, training procedure, hyperparameters, and a sensible untying of leaf/internal node parameters.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- Limited scope: experiments are restricted to a single dataset (SST) and task, raising concerns about generalizability.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is under-analyzed beyond dropout; more thorough tuning details are needed (learning rate, layer sizes, etc.).  
- Qualitative analysis is somewhat superficial and lacks deeper explanations for why layers respond differently.  
- Unexplored causes for performance degradation at greater depth (e.g., vanishing gradients, overfitting, architectural limits).  
- Missing discussion of computational/training cost compared to shallower models.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, requesting added computational cost discussion, broader hyperparameter analysis, deeper qualitative explanation of layer behaviors and depth effects, and brief discussion of future extensions to other datasets/tasks.
