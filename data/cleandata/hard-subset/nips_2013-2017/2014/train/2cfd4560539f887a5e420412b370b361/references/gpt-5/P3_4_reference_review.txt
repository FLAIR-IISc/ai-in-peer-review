Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (Deep RNNs) for compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers “in space” on top of the usual tree-structured depth “in structure.” Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up over binary parse trees with shared parameters. Between layers, each node passes its representation upwards via inter-layer connections V(i), with supervision applied only at the topmost layer. The authors also “untie” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes in the first layer (separate Wxh and Whh), use rectified linear units, pretrained word embeddings (fixed), dropout (with shared masks across nodes within a sentence), Adagrad, and a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations.

On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, the proposed Deep RNNs outperform similarly sized shallow RNNs, and the best configuration improves over prior published systems including Socher et al.’s RNTN and Paragraph Vectors. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest specialization across layers: lower layers reflect lexical and syntactic similarity, upper layers align more closely with sentiment semantics.

This work is closely related to prior tree-structured models (recursive autoencoders, MV-RNN, RNTN) and to deep (stacked) recurrent networks in time. The central novelty here is stacking recursive layers across space, along with the leaf/internal untying and the practical training recipe.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: disentangling depth in structure from depth in space for tree models, with a concrete stacked formulation and inter-layer connections.
- Strong empirical results on SST, with controls for parameter budget when varying depth, suggesting genuine gains from going deep.
- Practical training insights: rectifiers, shared-mask dropout on trees, Adagrad, identity-biased Whh initialization to stabilize ReLU trees.
- Useful qualitative analyses that provide intuition about layer specialization.
- The untied leaf/internal parameterization is sensible and yields practical benefits, especially with ReLUs and large fixed embeddings.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Attribution of gains: The improvements conflate several changes (untied leaf/internal, ReLUs, dropout, pretrained embeddings, Adagrad, activation L2). While the paper shows depth helps under a fixed parameter budget, it lacks ablations isolating each factor. In particular, how much of the gain over prior RNN baselines comes from ReLU+dropout+pretrained embeddings vs. stacking?
- Fairness of comparisons: The RNTN baseline and DCNN differ in parameterization, fine-tuning choices, and supervision regimes. You keep embeddings fixed; prior work often fine-tunes. It would be informative to (a) fine-tune embeddings for your model, (b) report parameter counts for all baselines, and (c) provide multiple runs with variance.
- Regularization and stability: Using L2 on activations to prevent explosion is unusual; gradient clipping or norm constraints are standard and would be a cleaner, well-justified choice. Please justify the choice and show sensitivity.
- Supervision strategy: You state the output layer is connected only to the final hidden layer but that labels exist at all tree nodes. Do you use losses at all nodes (on the final layer) or only at roots? Clarify and report the effect of supervising intermediate nodes and/or attaching classifiers to intermediate layers.
- Binary classification: You evaluate binary sentiment by mapping the 5-class posterior, which you note is not optimized for binary. For fair comparison to DCNN and prior binary results, train a binary version (or at least report a calibrated mapping) and compare.
- Statistical rigor: Report multiple random seeds, confidence intervals, and significance tests, especially for close comparisons.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear equations and motivation. However:
  - Some initialization details are missing in the extracted text (“All other weights are initialized as .”). Please specify the distribution and scale.
  - Be explicit about loss aggregation over tree nodes and any class-imbalance handling.
  - Provide exact parameter counts per configuration in Table 1, and training time/memory footprints.

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers is a natural and relatively incremental extension of tree-structured models, analogous to deep RNNs in time. The untied leaf/internal parameterization and use of ReLUs on trees are reasonable but not conceptually novel. The main contribution is demonstrating that stacking helps on SST with a careful training setup, plus qualitative evidence of layer specialization.

Significance
- Given the centrality of compositional modeling in NLP, showing that added depth in space benefits tree models is useful. Achieving new SOTA on SST (at the time) increases the paper’s practical impact. That said, the scope is limited to one dataset and task; broader validation (e.g., entailment, paraphrase, semantic role labeling), robustness to parser errors, and comparisons to alternative compositional architectures would strengthen the case for general applicability.

Questions for the authors
- How much improvement comes from each component? Please provide ablations: tied vs. untied leaves, tanh vs. ReLU, no dropout vs. dropout, fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings, activation L2 vs. gradient clipping.
- Do you supervise at all tree nodes on the top layer? If so, how are losses weighted? What happens if you also attach supervision to lower layers?
- How sensitive are results to using shared vs. independent dropout masks across nodes?
- What is the total parameter count and training time for your best model compared to RNTN and DCNN?
- How stable are results across random initializations?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comprehensive ablations and variance reporting to isolate architectural vs. training-trick contributions.
- Provide binary-optimized results and fair parameter-matched comparisons to prior baselines.
- Explore gradient clipping instead of activation L2, and report stability/sensitivity.
- Evaluate robustness to parsing errors or on alternative structures (e.g., dependency trees).
- Extend evaluation to other compositional tasks and provide a more systematic error analysis (negation, intensifiers, contrastive conjunctions).

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated architecture; good engineering choices.
- Demonstrated improvements over strong baselines with parameter-budget controls.
- Insightful qualitative analyses.

Cons
- Limited novelty; stacking is incremental.
- Attribution of gains unclear; missing ablations and variance.
- Comparisons to baselines not fully controlled; binary evaluation not directly optimized.
- Scope limited to one dataset/task.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding ablations, clearer supervision details, and more controlled comparisons. The empirical gains and practical recipe are valuable; strengthening experimental rigor will make the contribution more compelling.
