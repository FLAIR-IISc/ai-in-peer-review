Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other to separate depth in structure (tree recursions) from depth in space (stacked layers). Within each layer, a standard binary TreeRNN composition function combines left/right child vectors to form parent representations; across layers, each node’s hidden state feeds forward vertically to its counterpart in the next layer via layer-to-layer weights. The first layer “unties” leaves and internal nodes (different x→h vs h→h parameters) to enable the use of large, fixed pretrained word embeddings and rectified linear units (ReLUs). The model applies dropout (with shared masks per sentence/layer), AdaGrad, and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations to stabilize ReLU dynamics. Supervision is applied to every node’s representation at the final stacked layer. Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that DRNNs outperform shallow TreeRNNs with the same parameter budget and achieve state of the art vs MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN on fine-grained and binary sentiment. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path, and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

This work builds on recursive neural networks for parsing, sentiment, and paraphrase detection (Socher et al.), deep stacked recurrent nets in time (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen), and modern training practices (ReLUs, dropout, pretrained word vectors). The novelty is combining structural recursion with stacked depth, plus an untied leaf/internal parametrization to accommodate ReLUs and fixed embeddings.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea that is a natural yet nontrivial extension: decoupling depth in structure from depth in space for TreeRNNs.
- Sound engineering: untying leaves vs internals, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad, careful initialization, and stability regularization.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over matched-parameter shallow baselines; new SOTA on SST (as reported).
- Good qualitative analysis indicating layer specialization (intensity vs polarity) and different similarity notions across layers.
- Sensible training design: supervision at all tree nodes; parameter-budget control when varying depth.

Weaknesses
- Attribution of gains to depth vs other factors is under-explored; ablations isolating depth, ReLU, dropout, and pretraining are limited.
- Evaluation is on a single dataset/task (SST). Generality to other tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) is not demonstrated.
- Fairness and statistical rigor: results reference tables but lack variance across seeds and significance tests; parameter counts vs external baselines are not matched/normalized.
- Limited comparison to stronger recurrent/gated baselines (e.g., LSTM-based trees or residual/skip connections) that address vanishing gradients.
- Some training choices are ad hoc (shared dropout masks per layer/sentence; L2 on activations) and need justification/ablation.
- Clarity gaps: precise binarization/parsing details, computational complexity, and resource usage are not reported.

Quality
Technically sound: the formulation of stacked recursive layers is well defined, backprop through structure and across layers is standard, and training choices are reasonable. The optimization stability workaround (small L2 on activations) is pragmatic, though a comparison to gradient clipping would help. Claims of superiority over shallow models are supported under matched parameter budgets. However, the state-of-the-art claim would be stronger with multiple seeds, confidence intervals, and a tighter baseline protocol (e.g., ensuring the same embeddings/fine-tuning regime across methods). An ablation showing the effect of (i) untying leaves/internals, (ii) ReLU vs tanh, (iii) dropout vs none, (iv) depth with identical nonlinearity/regularization, would better quantify contributions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The architectural equations are clear, and the rationale for untying and for using ReLUs is persuasive. The qualitative analyses are informative. To improve reproducibility, please:
- Include full quantitative tables with parameter counts, training times, and standard deviations over multiple runs.
- Detail parser and binarization settings, vocabulary handling (OOV, casing), and whether class weighting is used given SST imbalance.
- Justify and specify the shared-dropout scheme, and report results with conventional node-wise dropout as a control.
- Report complexity: O(N L d^2) estimate, and wall-clock/epoch times.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers to achieve depth in space for tree-structured processing appears novel relative to prior TreeRNN, MV-RNN, and RNTN formulations, and is a natural analog to stacked recurrent nets in time. Untying leaves/internals for ReLU compatibility is a useful design tweak, though conceptually straightforward. The qualitative analyses on layer roles are insightful even if exploratory.

Significance
If the reported gains hold under stronger evaluation, the work is a meaningful step for compositional representation learning: it shows that structural recursion benefits from added depth, and it achieves SOTA on a widely used benchmark with a relatively simple architecture. The idea is likely to influence follow-on work (e.g., integrating stacking with gating or residual connections) and to be adopted in other tree-structured tasks.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Do you supervise at every node using labels from SST but only via the final layer (Eq. 5)? Please confirm and quantify whether intermediate-layer supervision (auxiliary losses) changes results.
- How sensitive are results to the shared vs independent dropout masks across nodes? Any effect on invariance across isomorphic subtrees?
- Did you try gradient clipping instead of L2 on activations? How do training dynamics and performance compare?
- How does performance change if embeddings are fine-tuned, and if so, does depth help more/less?
- Are improvements consistent across sentence length buckets and tree depth? A breakdown would be valuable.

Suggestions
- Add ablations disentangling depth from other factors (untied vs tied; ReLU vs tanh; dropout on/off; pretrained vs random/fine-tuned).
- Explore lightweight skip/residual connections between layers to enable deeper stacks without reducing width.
- Evaluate on at least one additional tree-structured task to support generality.
- Provide multiple-run averages with confidence intervals; include a matched-parameter RNTN or a reimplementation with your training protocol for fairness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea with solid motivation and implementation.
- Demonstrated improvements over strong baselines; likely to be used by the community.
- Insightful qualitative analyses suggesting layer-wise specialization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation scope and incomplete ablations make it hard to attribute the gains specifically to added depth.
- Lack of statistical rigor (no variance/significance), and baseline mismatches could overstate SOTA.
- Missing comparisons to alternative depth-enhancement mechanisms (gating/residuals).

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper introduces a compelling architectural extension with practical gains and good insights. With strengthened experimental rigor and broader evaluation, it would be a strong NeurIPS contribution.
