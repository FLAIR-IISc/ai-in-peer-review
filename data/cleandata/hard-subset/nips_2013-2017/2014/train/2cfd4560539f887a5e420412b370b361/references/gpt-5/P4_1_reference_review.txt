Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of parse trees. Within each layer, composition proceeds bottom-up over a binary tree via shared parameters; across layers, each node’s hidden state is passed to its counterpart in the next layer, adding “depth in space” to the usual “depth in structure.” The authors also “untie” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes in the first layer and employ rectified linear units and dropout.

The approach is positioned as the tree-structured analogue of deep recurrent networks that stack recurrent layers in time [10–12], distinguishing depth in structure from depth in space. It builds on recursive neural networks applied to parsing and sentiment [6–9] and compares against matrix-vector and tensor variants (MV-RNN [16], RNTN [8]), as well as Paragraph Vectors [17] and DCNN [18]. The evaluation is on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) [8], using phrase-level supervision.

Empirically, DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets with similar parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art results on both fine-grained (5-way) and binary sentiment classification on SST, using fixed pre-trained word2vec embeddings [19], ReLU activations [15], dropout [20], and AdaGrad [23]. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is a straightforward and coherent extension of tree-structured RNNs: Eq. (4) defines per-layer tree composition with residual-like cross-layer connections V(i). The untying of leaf vs. internal composition in the first layer is well-motivated (dimensionality and activation distribution). Training details (initialization, optimizer, regularization, early stopping) are explicitly specified. The use of a small L2 penalty on activations to curb unbounded ReLUs is reasonable.
- Empirical support: Results indicate (i) gains from pre-trained embeddings + ReLU + dropout even for a shallow recursive baseline; and (ii) additional gains from stacking layers under fixed parameter budgets, with best performance at moderate depth (2–4 layers). The comparisons use the standard SST split and published baselines.
- Limitations: The evaluation is confined to a single dataset and task. Ablations do not isolate the contribution of each design choice (depth vs. untying vs. ReLU vs. dropout vs. pre-trained embeddings). It is unclear whether competing recursive baselines would also benefit from the same modern training practices (e.g., dropout, pre-trained embeddings), which affects fairness. Variance across random seeds and statistical significance are not reported. There is no analysis of computational cost or memory with increasing depth. The approach does not incorporate gating mechanisms that are known to help gradient flow in deep/structured settings (e.g., LSTM/GRU), and no comparison to gated tree models is provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The distinction between depth in structure and depth in space is explained, and equations and training protocols are given with sufficient detail to reproduce the models. The qualitative analyses are informative and link to the compositional goals. Suggested improvements:
- Include a complete ablation table quantifying the effect of (a) depth, (b) untying leaves vs. internals, (c) ReLU vs. tanh, (d) dropout, and (e) pre-trained vs. learned embeddings.
- Report mean ± std over multiple runs and conduct significance testing against strong baselines.
- Clarify the exact parameter budgets for each depth configuration and provide training time/memory comparisons.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers over trees to introduce depth in space appears to be a novel and natural extension of prior recursive models and of deep recurrent networks in time [10–12]. The untying of leaves vs. internal nodes in a tree setting, the use of ReLU and dropout for recursive models, and the qualitative layer analyses are incremental but well-executed. The contribution is primarily architectural and empirical rather than introducing a new composition operator.

Significance
On SST, the method achieves new state of the art at the time of writing, which is meaningful given the prominence of this benchmark. The idea of separating and combining depth in structure and depth in space is likely to influence future tree-structured models. However, the broader impact would be strengthened by (i) demonstrating on additional structured NLP tasks (e.g., semantic relation classification, paraphrase detection, semantic parsing), and (ii) comparing to or incorporating gating mechanisms to address gradient flow in deep trees.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Fair comparison: Re-run strong recursive baselines (e.g., RNTN) with pre-trained embeddings, dropout, and similar regularization to assess whether DRNN’s gains stem from depth rather than training practice.
- Ablations: Provide a depth sweep with fixed width (not only fixed total parameters) to decouple the depth–width trade-off; include a “output attached to all layers” variant to empirically validate the design choice of supervising only the top layer.
- Stability: Consider and/or discuss gradient clipping and gating (e.g., Tree-LSTM; Tai et al., 2015; LSTM [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997]) as alternatives to L2 on activations for ReLU stability in deep trees.
- Generalization: Evaluate on at least one additional tree-structured dataset (e.g., TREC, SNLI parse-level tasks, or paraphrase detection [9]) to support generality.
- Analysis: Report per-phrase-length performance and phrase-level vs. root-level metrics; include parameter counts and training curves; provide runtime analysis.
- Reproducibility: Release code or pseudocode for the backprop-through-structure-and-space schedule; specify dropout mask sharing more precisely and any differences between train/test inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and simple architectural contribution: stacking tree-structured layers to add depth in space.
- Strong empirical results with state-of-the-art accuracy on SST.
- Careful training setup (pre-trained embeddings, ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad) and thorough hyperparameter notes.
- Insightful qualitative analyses indicating different roles for different layers.

Cons against acceptance
- Evidence limited to a single dataset/task; generality not demonstrated.
- Lack of comprehensive ablation to isolate the effect of depth vs. other factors.
- Potential unfairness in comparisons if baselines do not benefit from the same training improvements.
- No consideration of gated/tree-LSTM baselines that may be competitive and address vanishing gradients.
- Missing variance/significance analyses and computational trade-off discussion.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and clearly described architectural extension with promising results and reasonable insights, though additional experiments and ablations would strengthen claims about the unique contribution of depth in space.
