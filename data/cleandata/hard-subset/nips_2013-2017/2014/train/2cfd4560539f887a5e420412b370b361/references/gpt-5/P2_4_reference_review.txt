Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for tree-structured compositionality. The key idea is to distinguish two notions of depth—depth in structure (from applying the same composition recursively up a parse tree) and depth in space (stacking multiple nonlinear layers)—and to combine them by stacking multiple recursive layers, each performing bottom-up composition on the same tree and passing intermediate representations vertically to the next layer. The authors also (i) “untie” leaves from internal nodes so word vectors and phrase vectors can live in different spaces, enabling the use of rectifiers, and (ii) employ dropout, AdaGrad, and pretrained word vectors (kept fixed). They evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), training with supervision at all tree nodes and predicting at the root for 5-way and binary sentiment. They report that deeper/narrower models outperform shallow/wider ones with comparable parameter budgets, and achieve state-of-the-art results against RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different aspects of composition (lexical, syntactic, sentiment intensity/direction).

This work is related to recursive nets for parsing and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al.), and to deep/stacked recurrent networks that distinguish depth in time vs. space (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). The novelty is bringing “stacked” depth to tree-structured RNNs and analyzing its role in compositionality.

Evaluation against review criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is a natural and coherent extension of tree-structured RNNs with an added vertical (layer) dimension and untied leaf/internal transformations. Training uses standard backprop-through-structure across stacked layers. The authors control for parameter count when comparing depth vs. width, which strengthens the claim that depth helps.
- Empirical support: Results on SST show consistent gains from depth up to an optimal number of layers, and overall SOTA at the time. The use of pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout is justified by improved shallow baselines and qualitative training stability.
- Missing analyses: Some ablations would strengthen causal claims: (1) effect of untying leaves vs. tied, (2) ReLU vs. tanh with the same training recipe, (3) dropout vs. L2 only, (4) connecting outputs from all layers vs. last layer only. Statistical significance is not reported. Runtime/memory costs are not discussed.
- Overall: Solid, careful experimental setup with a fair depth-vs-width comparison, but limited to a single dataset and missing a few key ablations.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized. The motivation for distinguishing depth in structure vs. space is well articulated, and the training details (optimizer, rates, dropout tuning, initialization) are presented at a level that aids reproducibility.
- The qualitative analyses are informative and easy to follow. Figures/tables (referenced but not included here) likely help.
- Minor suggestions: More detail on the exact layer widths per parameter budget, training time/compute, and whether full tree supervision is crucial would improve clarity. Explicitly listing the final test accuracies in the text (in addition to tables) would help.

Originality
- Stacking multiple tree-structured composition layers with vertical connections is a straightforward but nontrivial extension of prior recursive nets. To my knowledge at the time, tree models typically used a single composition layer (with variants like tensors or matrix-vector parameters). The untying of leaves/internals and use of rectifiers in tree models are also reasonable innovations.
- While the engineering choices (dropout, AdaGrad, pretrained embeddings) are standard, the combination and the explicit depth-vs-width study in tree-structured models is novel and useful.

Significance
- The paper advances state of the art on a prominent benchmark and provides evidence that depth in space benefits tree-structured compositional models beyond what is achieved by stronger composition functions (e.g., tensors).
- The qualitative evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality is valuable and may encourage further work on interpretability and design of multi-layer composition.
- The scope is limited to one dataset/task (SST) and one structure type (binary constituency trees). Demonstrations on other tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, parsing) or other structures (e.g., dependency trees) would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and design: distinguishing and combining two kinds of depth.
- Fair comparisons: depth vs. width at fixed parameter budgets; improved shallow baseline with same modern training choices.
- Empirical gains to SOTA on SST with simple architecture and no embedding finetuning.
- Qualitative analyses that align with claims about layered compositionality.
- Practical training recipe (ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad) with sensible initialization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited evaluation breadth: only SST; no additional tasks or structures.
- Missing ablations: effect of untying, activation choice, supervision at all nodes, last-layer-only outputs.
- Lack of statistical significance and computational cost analysis.
- It remains somewhat unclear how much of the gain over prior work is due to stacking vs. better regularization and pretrained embeddings (though the depth-vs-width comparison helps).

Questions for authors
- How does performance change if embeddings are finetuned? Does depth still help?
- What is the impact of removing node-level supervision (training only at the root)?
- Have you tried connecting outputs from intermediate layers, or auxiliary losses, and does that help/hurt?
- How sensitive are results to the shared dropout mask choice across all nodes in a layer?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and timely extension of recursive nets; demonstrates that “deep” helps beyond structural depth.
- Strong empirical result on a widely used benchmark with careful comparisons.
- Useful qualitative insights; likely to spur follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation breadth and ablations are limited; significance beyond SST is not demonstrated.
- Some methodological choices (untied leaves, ReLU) not isolated via ablation.
- No analysis of efficiency or scalability.

Overall assessment
I lean toward acceptance. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, introduces a simple but meaningful architectural idea, and demonstrates measurable, state-of-the-art improvements with thoughtful analysis. While broader evaluations and ablations would strengthen the case, the contribution is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on structured deep learning and NLP compositionality.
