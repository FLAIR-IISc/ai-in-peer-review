

# Peer Review: "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel architecture called Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), which combines the concept of "depth in structure" (from recursive neural networks) with "depth in space" (from stacked layers in deep feedforward networks). The authors argue that while recursive neural networks (RNNs) are "deep in structure" due to their recursive application within a parse tree, they don't provide the hierarchical representation learning benefits of traditional deep feedforward networks. Their DRNN architecture stacks multiple recursive layers on top of each other, where each layer processes the structure (e.g., parse tree) and passes information to the next layer. The paper evaluates DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on fine-grained sentiment analysis and providing qualitative evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

The work builds upon Socher et al.'s recursive neural networks for sentiment analysis (2013) and extends it by adding depth through layer stacking, similar to how deep feedforward networks stack layers. The paper also introduces an "untied" variant that distinguishes between leaves (words) and internal nodes (phrases), which the authors claim allows for more efficient use of rectifier activations.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper is technically sound with clear architecture definitions, supported by equations (4) and (5) that precisely define the forward propagation.
- The experimental setup is well-defined and uses the standard SST benchmark with proper train/dev/test splits.
- The paper provides thorough comparison against multiple strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art work.
- The qualitative analysis (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) is insightful and provides valuable evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
- The authors are careful to note the limitations of their approach, such as the modest improvement over previous work and the need for future work on unsupervised tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper fails to clearly distinguish their approach from previous work on deep variants of recursive neural networks. For example, they reference "deep recurrent networks" but don't clarify how their DRNN differs from those.
- The theoretical justification for why stacking recursive layers would improve performance over shallow recursive networks is weak. The paper attributes improvements to "depth" but doesn't provide a compelling theoretical explanation.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of training DRNNs compared to shallow RNNs, which is an important consideration for practical applications.
- The distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" is confusingly presented, making it difficult to understand what the authors are claiming is novel.
- The paper uses pretrained word vectors without fine-tuning, which is a common practice in NLP but isn't justified in the paper, and the impact of fine-tuning on performance isn't explored.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is generally well-written with a clear organization and logical flow.
- The figures (Figures 1, 2, 3) are helpful for visualizing the architecture and experimental results.
- The methodology section provides sufficient detail for reproduction, including specific implementation details like the use of rectifier activations and dropout.

**Weaknesses:**
- The explanation of "depth in structure" vs. "depth in space" is unnecessarily confusing and doesn't clearly establish the novelty of the work.
- The paper could better clarify how their DRNN differs from previous deep variants of recursive networks (e.g., the deep recurrent networks mentioned in the introduction).
- The section on "Untying Leaves and Internals" (2.2) is somewhat confusing in its explanation of benefits. The authors state benefits (1) and (2) but don't clearly connect them to the sentiment analysis task.
- The input perturbation experiment (Section 3.2) would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the authors chose specific sentiment-shifted words rather than adding noise to the word vectors.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new architecture that combines recursive processing with stacking of layers, which is a novel extension to recursive neural networks.
- The untied variant that distinguishes between leaves and internal nodes is a useful modification to the standard recursive neural network architecture.

**Weaknesses:**
- The core idea of stacking layers to create deeper networks is not new (it's the standard approach to creating deep networks), so the novelty is limited to applying this stacking to recursive neural networks.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their work from previous deep variants of recursive neural networks. A thorough literature review of related work on deep recursive networks would strengthen the novelty claim.
- The paper doesn't cite or discuss work on deep recursive networks that might have already explored similar ideas (e.g., the deep recurrent networks referenced in the introduction).

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the SST sentiment analysis task, which is a standard benchmark in NLP.
- The qualitative analysis provides valuable insights about how different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.
- The work contributes to understanding compositionality in language, which is a fundamental problem in NLP.

**Weaknesses:**
- The improvement over previous state-of-the-art is modest (from 49.0% to 49.5% on the fine-grained task), which might not be significant enough to justify the novelty claim.
- The paper is limited to sentiment analysis on a specific dataset (SST), so the broader impact is unclear. The authors suggest future work on unsupervised tasks, but this isn't demonstrated in the current paper.
- The paper doesn't clearly explain why the DRNN approach is better than simply using a deeper feedforward network with the same number of parameters, which would be a more straightforward approach.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable extension to recursive neural networks by stacking layers to create "depth in space" in addition to the existing "depth in structure." The empirical results show modest improvements over previous work, and the qualitative analysis provides interesting insights about how different layers operate.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses in terms of clarity, originality, and significance. The distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" is confusingly presented, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from previous work on deep variants of recursive neural networks. The improvements over previous work are modest, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why the DRNN architecture is better than simply using a deeper feedforward network.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While the work is technically sound and the results are promising, the paper doesn't make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field. The improvements are modest, and the novelty is limited to applying a standard deep learning technique (stacking layers) to an existing architecture (recursive neural networks) without providing a compelling theoretical justification for why this should work better.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Clarify the novelty**: The paper needs to better distinguish between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" and clearly explain how the DRNN architecture differs from previous deep variants of recursive neural networks. A thorough literature review of related work would be helpful.

2. **Strengthen the theoretical justification**: The paper attributes improvements to "depth" but doesn't provide a compelling theoretical explanation. Consider adding a brief theoretical analysis of why stacking recursive layers might lead to better performance.

3. **Address the computational cost**: Discuss the computational cost of training DRNNs compared to shallow RNNs, as this is an important consideration for practical applications.

4. **Justify the use of pretrained word vectors without fine-tuning**: Explain why the authors chose not to fine-tune word vectors, and consider including experiments with fine-tuned vectors to show the impact on performance.

5. **Improve the qualitative analysis**: The nearest neighbor phrases examples in Table 2 are interesting but could be more clearly connected to the main thesis about different layers capturing different aspects of compositionality.

6. **Address the modest improvement**: The paper should acknowledge that the improvement over previous work is modest and discuss whether this is due to the specific architecture or the limitations of the SST dataset.

This paper has potential, but it needs to address these weaknesses to make a stronger contribution to the field.
