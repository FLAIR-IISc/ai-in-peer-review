Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language. Building on recursive neural networks over parse trees, the authors distinguish two notions of depth: depth in structure (due to tree recursion) and depth in space (via stacking layers). They introduce stacked recursive layers where each layer performs a standard tree composition and passes node representations upward through “space” connections to the next layer. They further “untie” parameters for leaves versus internal nodes in the first layer (separate Wx→h and Wh→h) to enable the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs) and to better accommodate dense word vectors versus sparse phrase vectors. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout on hidden states shared across tree positions, pretrained 300d word2vec embeddings (fixed), and small L2 on weights and activations for stability.

Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that: (i) a strong shallow baseline using pretrained word vectors, ReLUs, dropout, and untying outperforms past shallow RNNs; (ii) deeper, narrower stacked recursive models outperform shallow models with approximately matched parameter budgets, up to an optimal depth (2–4 layers depending on width); and (iii) the best Deep RNN surpasses previously reported results for SST fine-grained and binary sentiment, including RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN (using published numbers). Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases by layer) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work: The approach combines ideas from deep feedforward/recurrent learning (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio) with tree-structured compositional models (Socher et al.: RAE/MV-RNN/RNTN) and modern training practices (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout). The key novelty is stacking tree composition layers (depth in space) and untying leaf/internal parameters to facilitate ReLUs.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated distinction between depth in structure vs depth in space; simple architecture that generalizes conventional recursive nets.
- Sound training setup: AdaGrad, dropout over trees, stability tricks, and frozen word2vec provide robust optimization without pretraining.
- Parameter-controlled comparisons: depth vs width trade-off under matched parameter budgets offers credible evidence that depth (in space) helps.
- Empirical results are competitive and appear to set a new state of the art on SST at the time of writing.
- Qualitative probes (perturbation and nearest neighbors) provide insight into what different layers learn; claims are consistent with observed behavior.
- Clarity: equations and design choices are explained; the untying rationale (dense leaves vs sparse internal representations with ReLUs) is thoughtful.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: all quantitative evidence is on a single dataset (SST). This constrains claims about generality to compositional tasks beyond sentiment.
- Attribution of gains: several changes are introduced together (pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, dropout, untying, stacking). While there is some comparison of shallow vs deep under similar budgets, a systematic ablation (e.g., shallow with/without untying, ReLUs, dropout; deep without stacking but equal parameters; deep with tanh) is missing to isolate the contribution of depth versus other factors.
- Baseline comparability: comparisons rely on published numbers. Some baselines (e.g., RNTN, MV-RNN) may use different embedding initialization/fine-tuning regimes, making SOTA claims potentially confounded by pretrained embeddings and dropout. An apples-to-apples reimplementation or re-run with the same embeddings and regularization would strengthen conclusions.
- Statistical rigor: no multiple runs or standard deviations; no statistical significance tests. Given known variance in deep NLP models, reporting variability is important.
- Design choice of supervising only the top layer is motivated by interpretability, but the paper does not compare to alternatives (e.g., auxiliary losses at intermediate layers, skip/shortcut connections), which could affect both performance and the claimed layer specialization.
- Analysis remains mostly anecdotal; more quantitative probes (e.g., linear probes for polarity/intensity at each layer, layerwise contribution to accuracy) would substantiate the qualitative claims.
- No discussion of computational cost and memory as depth increases, or of training dynamics (e.g., gradient clipping vs the chosen activation regularization).

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound and well-implemented. However, the empirical evaluation lacks ablations, variability estimates, and broader datasets, limiting the strength of causal claims.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Minor citation formatting issues aside, the presentation should be readily reproducible by an expert.
- Originality: Moderate. Stacking tree-structured composition layers and untying leaves/internals are natural extensions of known ideas; combining them with ReLUs and dropout in recursive settings is a reasonable, if incremental, contribution.
- Significance: Moderate. Improvements on a prominent benchmark are valuable, and the design may influence subsequent compositional models. Broader impact would be clearer with results on additional tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, semantic relatedness) and a more thorough analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations to disentangle the effects of: depth (stacking), untying leaves/internals, ReLU vs tanh, dropout, and pretrained embeddings (including fine-tuning vs freezing).
- Re-run key baselines (e.g., RNTN, shallow RNN) with the same pretrained embeddings, dropout, and training protocol to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons; report multiple seeds and confidence intervals.
- Add experiments on at least one other tree-structured task (e.g., paraphrase detection, semantic relatedness) to demonstrate generality; even a small-scale parsing or entailment experiment would help.
- Include quantitative layerwise probes (e.g., linear classifiers for polarity/intensity/syntax over frozen layer representations) and possibly layer-drop experiments to validate the claimed division of labor across layers.
- Report training time, parameter counts, and memory footprint; discuss stability (e.g., role of activation L2 vs gradient clipping) and the effect of supervising intermediate layers.
- Explore fine-tuning embeddings and/or gating mechanisms (e.g., compare to a tree-structured gated unit) to test whether depth in space continues to help under stronger baselines.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea that is well-motivated and practical.
- Strong empirical performance on SST with careful parameter budgeting across depths.
- Useful qualitative insights and training practices for recursive models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evidence limited to a single dataset; generality not demonstrated.
- SOTA claims may conflate benefits of pretraining/dropout with stacking; missing ablations and controlled baseline re-runs.
- Lack of statistical rigor (no multiple seeds/significance), and limited quantitative analysis of layer roles.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is well written and proposes a reasonable and potentially impactful variant of recursive models, but the empirical evidence is not yet thorough enough for NeurIPS. Addressing the ablation, comparability, and generalization concerns would likely elevate this to a strong submission.
