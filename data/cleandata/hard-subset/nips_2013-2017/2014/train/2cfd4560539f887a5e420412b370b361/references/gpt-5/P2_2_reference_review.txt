Summary
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (deep RNNs) for tree-structured inputs by stacking multiple recursive layers “in space” on top of the standard depth “in structure.” The model computes node representations bottom-up within each layer (shared WL/WR), and passes each node’s hidden state to the corresponding node in the next layer via V connections. The first layer unties leaf and internal transformations and uses rectified linear units. Training uses SST with supervision at every tree node, fixed pretrained word vectors, dropout (shared per layer across tree nodes), AdaGrad, and an additional small L2 penalty on weights and activations. With constant parameter budget, deeper-and-narrower models outperform shallower-and-wider ones up to an optimal depth (2–4 layers), and the best configuration achieves state-of-the-art on SST fine-grained and binary sentiment (according to the paper). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path; nearest-neighbor phrases across layers) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work
The approach extends recursive neural networks for trees (Goller & Küchler; Socher et al.) by adding stacked layers, analogous to deep recurrent networks for sequences. It contrasts with MV-RNN and RNTN (multiplicative composition) and with non-tree sentence models (DCNN, Paragraph Vectors). The untying of leaf/internal parameters with ReLUs and dropout is a practical twist not emphasized in prior tree RNN work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is well specified (equations for within-layer recursion and cross-layer connections, output only from the top layer). Training details (optimizer, initialization, regularization) are explicit. The choice to share dropout masks across all nodes in a layer for a given sentence is reasonable for stability. The small L2 penalty on activations addresses ReLU explosion and is justified empirically.
- Empirical support: The central claim—depth in space helps beyond depth in structure at fixed parameter count—is supported by comparisons across depths and widths, with best results at intermediate depth and degradation when too deep and too narrow. The paper also shows improvements from using pretrained embeddings, ReLUs, and dropout compared to older shallow tree RNN baselines.
- Missing or weak evidence: The attribution of gains to depth vs other changes is not cleanly disentangled. Although a stronger shallow baseline with pretrained embeddings, rectifiers, and dropout is included, a thorough ablation (toggling untying, ReLUs vs tanh, dropout on/off, L2 on activations, output attached to all layers vs last only) is not presented. Results are on a single dataset (SST); no replication on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, or other sentiment datasets). No statistical significance tests or multiple random seeds are reported, which is important given potential variance in deep models. Detailed numbers for the comparisons (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) and parameter counts per configuration should be clearly tabulated for reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The modeling choices are motivated (depth in structure vs depth in space), and training details are sufficiently described for reproduction by an expert. The qualitative analyses are easy to follow and add intuition. A few places to improve clarity: explicitly list the exact parameter counts per configuration; report full tables with means/variances; specify whether parses are gold/provided vs re-parsed; clarify whether word vectors are frozen for all models and whether competitor baselines fine-tune embeddings; and briefly discuss computational cost.

Originality
The idea of stacking recursive layers for trees is a natural and relatively straightforward extension of deep recurrent networks to tree structures. The novelty is therefore moderate but nontrivial: to my knowledge, explicit stacking of tree-structured recursive layers with cross-layer node connections and analysis of their roles had not been thoroughly explored in prior work cited here. The untying of leaf/internal transforms with ReLU and dropout is more of an engineering refinement than a conceptual leap. The qualitative evidence that different layers capture different aspects of composition is interesting, albeit anecdotal.

Significance
Achieving state-of-the-art on SST at the time is valuable. The finding that depth-in-space helps even when controlling parameter count is a useful empirical result that others can build on for tree models. However, significance is tempered by the limited scope of evaluation (one dataset, one task) and the lack of ablations to isolate which ingredients are essential. Broader impact would be clearer with results on additional structured NLP tasks and comparisons to gated tree models (if available) and alternative ways to deepen trees (e.g., highway/gating across layers).

Questions for the authors
- Please provide ablations isolating: depth vs width; ReLU vs tanh; with/without dropout; tied vs untied leaf/internal; output connected to last vs all layers.
- How sensitive are results to freezing vs fine-tuning word embeddings?
- Do results hold across multiple random seeds? Any statistical significance tests?
- What parses were used (gold, given by SST) and how robust is performance to parsing errors?
- What is the computational overhead (training/inference time) as depth increases?

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated extension of recursive tree models to deep stacks; sound formulation.
- Strong empirical results on SST; controls parameter count to fairly test depth.
- Practical training recipe (ReLU, dropout with shared masks, AdaGrad) and explicit details.
- Useful qualitative analyses suggesting different layers capture distinct compositional aspects.

Cons
- Limited novelty; largely a straightforward deepening of known architectures.
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset/task; no multi-dataset validation.
- Missing ablations to attribute gains to depth vs other design choices.
- No variance/significance reporting; unclear robustness.
- Lacks discussion of computational efficiency and memory footprint.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides credible evidence that stacking recursive layers improves tree-based compositional models at fixed parameter budgets, achieving strong results on a standard benchmark. While the conceptual novelty is modest and the empirical scope is narrow, the study should interest the community working on structured deep models and compositionality. Strengthening the ablation analysis and adding an additional dataset would move this to a solid accept.

Confidence
High for assessment of technical correctness and clarity; medium for claims of significance due to missing ablations and single-dataset evaluation.
