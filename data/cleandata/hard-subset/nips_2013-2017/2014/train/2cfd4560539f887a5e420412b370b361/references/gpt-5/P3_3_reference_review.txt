Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of a parse-tree-structured composition function. The key ideas are: (i) distinguish leaf and internal nodes (untied parameters for word-to-phrase and phrase-to-phrase transformations), enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLU); (ii) add “depth in space” via vertical connections between recursive layers (in addition to the inherent “depth in structure” of trees); (iii) regularize with dropout where dropped units are shared across all nodes within a layer for a sentence; (iv) train with AdaGrad and modest activity regularization to avoid exploding activations. The model is evaluated on fine-grained and binary sentiment classification on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), reporting improvements over shallow recursive models and previous state of the art (including RNTN and Paragraph Vectors). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest that different stacked layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned relative to: (a) recursive neural networks over parse trees for semantics and sentiment (RAE/RNN/MV-RNN/RNTN), and (b) deep/stacked recurrent networks in time (Elman-type deep RNNs). The contribution is to introduce stacking across tree-structured composition, bridging “depth in structure” with “depth in space.” The design choice of untying leaf and internal transformations, using ReLU and dropout, draws from advances in deep feedforward/conv nets and brings them to tree models. Comparisons are made to RNTN, MV-RNN, DCNN, and Paragraph Vectors.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and simple architectural extension: stacking recursive layers with vertical connections, with a cogent motivation about representational hierarchy beyond uniform composition in a single tree layer.
- Sensible engineering choices (pretrained word vectors, ReLU, AdaGrad, dropout with shared masks across the tree) that improve trainability and performance of tree models.
- Careful within-family comparisons: depth is varied while roughly controlling parameter budget, and the paper shows that deeper-but-narrower tree models can outperform shallow ones of similar size, with an optimal depth that depends on capacity.
- Qualitative analyses are insightful and consistent with the hypothesis that different layers capture different aspects (e.g., intensity vs polarity).
- Reported results are strong on SST, establishing a new SOTA at the time among published baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness of external baselines: The strongest gains over prior work may conflate architecture with training regime. RNTN/MV-RNN baselines did not use pretrained word embeddings, ReLU, or dropout. The paper shows that even a shallow RNN gains substantially from these modern choices (48.1 vs. 43.2), so it is important to also report RNTN/MV-RNN reimplementations under the same regimen (pretrained embeddings fixed or fine-tuned, ReLU where applicable, dropout) to quantify the gain attributable to depth versus better inputs/regularization.
- Limited ablations. Multiple changes are introduced simultaneously: untying leaves vs internals, ReLU vs tanh, dropout vs L2, using pretrained embeddings vs learning from scratch, and vertical stacking. Only a partial ablation (dropout vs L2) is provided. A more complete study (toggle each factor on/off in the shallow model; then add stacking) would substantiate the causal contribution of depth.
- Statistical robustness. Results are reported for single runs with early stopping; no variances/confidence intervals across random initializations, especially pertinent for deep, rectified models with dropout. This is important given relatively small absolute gains across depth settings.
- Depth/width trade-off confounding. Keeping parameter count fixed makes deeper models narrower, confounding the interpretation of “depth helps.” It would be useful to include: (i) a curve at fixed width showing absolute performance as depth increases (even if parameter count grows), and (ii) a parameter-efficiency plot (accuracy vs parameters) to demonstrate that deeper models are more parameter-efficient than shallow ones.
- Scope and generality. Evaluation is solely on SST. The claim that layers capture different compositional phenomena would be strengthened by results on at least one additional compositional task (e.g., paraphrase detection on MSRP, sentiment on MR/CR, or question classification), or on a version without gold phrase-level supervision to test whether the approach helps when the per-node signal is absent.
- Model design choices need more justification or comparison:
  - Output only from the top layer: the paper argues for interpretability, but an experiment connecting classifiers to all layers (deep supervision) could test performance trade-offs.
  - Activity L2 penalty to curb exploding activations is unusual; gradient clipping is a common alternative. A brief comparison would clarify stability choices.
  - The shared-dropout mask per layer per sentence is a key design. Please provide an ablation vs independent-node dropout.
- Computational considerations. The paper lacks training time/complexity comparisons. Stacking recursive layers increases compute per sentence; practical runtimes and memory use compared to shallow trees and tensor models would be helpful.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a well-specified architecture and training details sufficient for reproduction. Experimental methodology needs stronger ablations and matched baselines to isolate the effect of depth, and statistical reporting across seeds.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Suggestions:
- Tighten notation in Section 2.2/2.3 (the overloading of h/x across leaf/internal and layers can be confusing). Summarize parameter tying/sharing in a compact diagram or table.
- Quantify parameter counts in Table 1a to make “constant budget” concrete.

Originality
Stacking recursive layers over trees, with vertical connections and ReLU-enabled leaf/internal untying, is a novel and timely combination. The qualitative analyses for layer roles are also a nice addition. While components are known, their integration in tree-structured models appears original.

Significance
Moderate to high. Improving compositional modeling in tree RNNs is important, and the results on a widely used benchmark will be of interest. The approach is simple enough to be adopted broadly. Strengthening the fairness of baselines and ablations would solidify the contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled architectural idea with empirical gains.
- Strong practical training recipe for tree models (pretrained embeddings, ReLU, dropout).
- Insightful qualitative analyses supporting the layer-wise compositional interpretation.

Cons for acceptance
- Baseline fairness and missing ablations leave ambiguity about how much of the gain is due to depth.
- Single dataset; limited statistical robustness reporting.
- Lacking runtime/complexity analysis.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a useful and likely impactful architectural refinement with solid results and good intuitions. I encourage the authors to (i) add matched-regimen RNTN/MV-RNN baselines or a careful ablation isolating depth, (ii) report variance across runs, and (iii) include a second dataset or additional ablations to strengthen the case.
