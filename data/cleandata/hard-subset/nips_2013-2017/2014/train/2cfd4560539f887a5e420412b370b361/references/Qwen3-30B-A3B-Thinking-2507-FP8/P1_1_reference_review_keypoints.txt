- Proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers to add "depth in space" to recursive architectures.  
- Introduces an untied variant distinguishing leaf (word-level) and internal (phrase-level) representations and connects corresponding nodes across layers.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) showing DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks and prior state-of-the-art.  
- Provides qualitative analysis suggesting different layers capture different compositional aspects (word similarity, intensity, sentiment).  

- Strength: Technically sound architecture with clear equations, figures, and ablation studies.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments against strong baselines with proper evaluation metrics (5-class and binary).  
- Strength: Leverages fine-grained supervision in SST to mitigate vanishing gradients at every parse node.  
- Strength: Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) give insights into hierarchical layer behavior.  
- Strength: Uses dropout and L2 regularization appropriately to stabilize rectifier activations.  

- Weakness: No theoretical justification for why stacking recursive layers improves performance beyond empirical evidence.  
- Weakness: Motivation for stacking recursive layers vs. using a deeper feedforward network with same constraints is not fully clear.  
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to sentiment analysis (SST) — unclear generalization to other NLP tasks.  
- Weakness: Superficial discussion of relation to deep recurrent networks (Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013) and prior work.  

- Clarity strength: Well-written, well-organized paper with helpful figures and detailed methodology.  
- Clarity strength: Experimental setup, preprocessing, hyperparameters, and results are clearly presented.  
- Clarity weakness: Occasional confusion between "recursive" and "recurrent" neural network terminology.  
- Clarity weakness: The distinction between "depth in structure" and "depth in space" could be more precisely explained.  
- Clarity weakness: Qualitative results (Figure 3, Table 2) need clearer explanations (e.g., why layer 2 captures intensity).  

- Originality strength: Novel extension of stacking in recursive networks and a well-motivated untied variant for leaf/internal nodes.  
- Originality weakness: Limited discussion of whether similar stacking has been explored in other domains or structured tasks.  
- Originality weakness: Could better contextualize novelty against recent deep NLP architectures (e.g., transformers, hierarchical models).  

- Significance strength: Achieves new state-of-the-art results on SST and offers insights into layer-wise compositionality.  
- Significance weakness: Impact is somewhat narrow due to single-task evaluation and lack of statistical significance testing.  

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel architecture, strong empirical improvements on SST, insightful qualitative analysis, and solid presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited theoretical grounding, narrow evaluation scope, terminology confusion, and unclear distinction from prior deep recurrent work.  

- Overall recommendation: Accept — paper is a meaningful, well-validated contribution that advances compositional modeling in NLP.  

- Suggestion 1: Clarify and consistently distinguish "recursive" vs. "recurrent" neural networks throughout the paper.  
- Suggestion 2: Add a brief theoretical discussion or intuition for why stacking recursive layers helps, drawing parallels to depth in feedforward nets.  
- Suggestion 3: Expand the related work section to more explicitly contrast with Hermans & Schrauwen (2013) and other deep recurrent approaches.  
- Suggestion 4: Provide statistical significance tests for reported improvements over baselines.  
- Suggestion 5: Discuss potential applications to other NLP tasks and how the architecture might generalize beyond sentiment analysis.  
- Suggestion 6: In Figure 3, better explain why the second layer captures "intensity" versus "direction" of sentiment, and compare layer behaviors more thoroughly.  
- Suggestion 7: Consider comparisons with more recent deep NLP approaches (e.g., transformers) to better position the contribution.
