Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs) that add “depth in space” to tree-structured recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. Within each layer, node representations are computed bottom-up via shared structural weights (as in standard tree RNNs), while across layers, each node passes its hidden state vertically via inter-layer connections. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters, enabling the use of large, fixed pretrained word vectors and rectified linear units. The model is trained end-to-end with dropout and AdaGrad, with supervision only at the final layer. Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that deep variants outperform shallow recursive nets with comparable parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art results compared to RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbations and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (e.g., sentiment intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive neural networks for compositional semantics and prior enhancements such as matrix-vector and tensor-based composition (MV-RNN, RNTN). It parallels developments in “deep recurrent” models that distinguish depth in time vs space by stacking layers. To my knowledge, explicitly stacking multiple tree-structured recursive layers with shared structural weights within each layer and vertical transformations across layers has not been explored in earlier sentiment work; the paper cites deep RNNs in time (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen) and positions DRNNs as the tree analogue. The untying of leaves vs internals to better exploit ReLUs and pretrained embeddings is also a useful practical refinement.

Quality
- Methodology: The architecture is clearly specified with equations, including the interleaved backpropagation through structure and layers. The untying of leaves/internals and the ReLU/dropout recipe are sensible and well motivated.
- Empirics: Results on SST indicate consistent gains from depth under a fixed parameter budget, and state-of-the-art performance over strong baselines. The qualitative probes are thoughtful and consistent with the architectural hypothesis.
- Caveats: Evaluation is limited to one dataset/task. There is no variance reporting across random seeds. Some implementation choices (shared dropout mask per layer/tree, L2 on activations to prevent explosion) are not deeply justified or ablated. Fairness of some baseline comparisons (e.g., pretraining, regularization, and search budgets) could be made clearer.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The distinction between structural depth and spatial depth is articulated nicely. The figures (as described) appear helpful. Training details are mostly sufficient to reproduce, though a few items could be clarified (see questions).

Originality
The central idea—introducing spatial depth for tree-structured composition by stacking recursive layers—is a simple but novel and compelling architectural direction distinct from the multiplicative/tensor approaches. The untying and ReLU-based design adapted to trees adds further originality in practical modeling choices. The qualitative layer-wise analyses are also insightful.

Significance
If the reported SOTA holds, the contribution is meaningful for compositional NLP and structured deep architectures. The idea that different layers in a tree stack capture different aspects of semantics is intriguing and likely to spur follow-up work. That said, the empirical scope is narrow (SST only), and the gap to prior work should be contextualized with stronger ablations and fairer apples-to-apples baselines. Still, as a concept, deepening recursive models is a promising direction beyond incremental tweaks.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and novel architectural extension: stacking recursive layers with per-node vertical connections.
- Practical design choices (untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLU, dropout) that make tree models trainable and performant without pretraining or finetuning the word vectors.
- Strong empirical results on a widely used benchmark; consistent gains from depth under fixed parameter budgets.
- Thoughtful qualitative diagnostics that support the claim of layer specialization.

Weaknesses
- Narrow evaluation: single dataset and task; no tests on other tree-structured tasks (e.g., parsing, entailment) to establish generality.
- Baseline parity potentially uneven: some baselines may not use the same pretrained embeddings, dropout, or hyperparameter tuning. It is unclear whether RNTN/DCNN were retrained under the same recipe vs using published numbers.
- Limited ablations: no direct ablation of the untying, ReLU vs tanh, dropout vs L2, output attached to intermediate layers, or finetuning embeddings. The observed benefit may be partially attributable to these choices rather than depth alone.
- No runtime/memory analysis; deeper tree models could be more expensive, and training stability is handled via ad hoc L2 on activations rather than e.g. gradient clipping.
- No uncertainty estimates or multiple random seeds; improvements could be within variance.

Questions/clarifications for the authors
- Did you re-run baselines (RNTN, DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) under your training setup (dropout, AdaGrad, same word vectors) or report published numbers? If the latter, can you provide a controlled comparison with matched pretraining/regularization?
- How sensitive are the results to fixing vs finetuning the word embeddings? Given you already use pretrained vectors, finetuning could be a strong baseline.
- Please report variance over multiple runs and any early stopping criteria used across models.
- Why share the dropout mask across nodes within a layer/tree? Have you compared to independent masks per node?
- Did you try connecting the classifier to all layers (with or without auxiliary losses)? A small ablation would strengthen the “layer specialization” claim.

Suggestions
- Add ablations: (i) depth vs width without fixing parameter budget (to probe peak performance), (ii) untying leaves vs tying, (iii) ReLU vs tanh, (iv) with/without dropout, (v) with/without activation L2 vs gradient clipping.
- Evaluate on at least one additional structured task or dataset to demonstrate generality.
- Provide training cost and parameter counts, and report mean/std across seeds.

Reasons to accept
- Introduces a clear, novel architectural dimension for tree models (stacked recursive layers) with promising empirical and qualitative evidence.
- Achieves new SOTA on SST with a relatively simple, general approach.
- Insightful analysis of how depth shapes compositional representations.

Reasons to reject
- Limited scope (single benchmark), incomplete ablations, and potential baseline unfairness make it hard to attribute gains solely to the proposed depth.
- Conceptually incremental to “stacking” deep RNNs; novelty may be viewed as modest by some.

Recommendation
Score: 7 (accept). The idea of adding spatial depth to tree-structured composition is a meaningful, forward-looking contribution with solid empirical support. While the evaluation could be broader and ablations deeper, the conceptual advance and encouraging results justify acceptance at NeurIPS.
