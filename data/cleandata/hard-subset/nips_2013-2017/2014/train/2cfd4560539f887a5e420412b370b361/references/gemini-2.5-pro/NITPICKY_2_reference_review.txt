**Summary**

This paper proposes the Deep Recursive Neural Network, an architecture constructed by stacking multiple layers of recursive neural networks. The motivation is to introduce a hierarchy of representations, analogous to what has been shown to be effective in deep recurrent and feedforward networks, by creating depth "in space" in addition to the depth "in structure" inherent to a single recursive net. The authors introduce a variant that unties the parameters for leaf (word) and internal (phrase) nodes, which they argue allows for more flexible model design and better use of rectifier activation units. The proposed models are evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank for fine-grained sentiment classification. The results show that deeper, narrower models outperform their shallower, wider counterparts with a similar number of parameters. The best deep model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on this benchmark. The paper concludes with qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor queries) suggesting that different layers in the deep architecture learn to represent different aspects of compositionality.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of stacking recursive layers is a logical and novel extension of the existing recursive network framework. It draws a clear and well-motivated parallel to the successful development of deep recurrent neural networks. The presented state-of-the-art results on a widely used benchmark (SST) demonstrate the potential significance and practical utility of the proposed architecture.

2.  **Controlled Experiments:** The primary quantitative comparison (Table 1a) is well-designed. By keeping the total number of model parameters approximately constant while varying depth and width, the authors provide convincing evidence that the performance gains are attributable to the increased depth rather than simply a larger model.

3.  **Interesting Qualitative Insights:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor analyses, while preliminary, offer intriguing hypotheses about the function of each layer in the hierarchy. The suggestion that lower layers focus on lexical or syntactic features while higher layers capture more abstract semantic properties (like sentiment) is plausible and points toward interesting directions for future analysis.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising and the headline results are strong, the paper is undermined by a significant number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a general lack of rigor in its presentation and methodology.

1.  **Clarity and Notation:**
    *   The use of the abbreviation "RNN" for *Recursive* Neural Network throughout the paper is highly problematic and confusing, given that "RNN" is the near-universal acronym for *Recurrent* Neural Network. This choice significantly impairs readability for anyone in the field.
    *   The methodology for untying the `V` matrices that connect layers is confusingly described. Section 2.3 states that the leaf/internal untying from Section 2.2 is "only necessary for the first layer," but then immediately follows by stating that for the second layer, one uses "separate V(2) for leaves and internals (Vxh(2) and Vhh(2))". This is a direct contradiction that makes the exact architecture unclear.

2.  **Methodological Justification:** Several key methodological choices are either unusual or poorly justified.
    *   **Regularization:** The paper uses a small L2 penalty on *unit activations* in addition to weights. This is a non-standard technique, and the justification provided—that it "resolves the explosion problem"—is insufficient. A more detailed explanation or empirical justification for this choice over more standard methods like gradient clipping is needed.
    *   **Initialization:** The initialization of recursive weights as `0.5I + ε` is justified by the claim that the parent representation is "approximately the mean of its two children." This interpretation holds for a linear activation function but is not accurate for the rectified linear unit used in the experiments. This reflects a lack of precision in the technical description.
    *   **Dropout:** A non-standard dropout scheme is employed where the same units are dropped for all nodes within a given layer for a sentence. This is an important implementation detail that is mentioned only in passing, without any justification for why this was chosen over a more standard node-independent dropout.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **Binary Classification:** The results for binary classification are derived from the 5-class model rather than a dedicated, retrained binary classifier. The argument that this "solves a harder problem" is unconvincing and sidesteps a more direct and rigorous comparison with baseline methods.
    *   **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative experiments are based on a single example sentence (Figure 3) and two example phrases (Table 2). While interesting, these anecdotal results are not sufficient to draw firm conclusions about the model's systematic behavior. The choice of one-norm for nearest neighbor search is also not justified against other standard metrics like cosine similarity.
    *   **Reproducibility:** Details regarding hyperparameter tuning are sparse. The learning rate is fixed, but the process for selecting this rate and other parameters (e.g., AdaGrad's epsilon, minibatch size) is not described, hindering reproducibility.

4.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The quality of the manuscript's presentation is far below the expected standard for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted, with missing author names (e.g., [2]), jumbled metadata, and inconsistent styling (e.g., "[1]...Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning,, , ,"). This lack of attention to detail reflects poorly on the overall care taken in preparing the submission.

**Conclusion**

The paper introduces a novel and effective architecture, supported by strong empirical results. However, the work in its current form is marred by a substantial number of small but collectively significant issues. These include major clarity problems (the "RNN" acronym), methodological inconsistencies (the untying of V), poorly justified technical choices (L2 on activations, initialization), and a profound lack of care in the paper's presentation, most notably the reference list. While the core contribution is valuable, the execution and write-up do not meet the high standards of technical soundness and clarity required for publication at NIPS. Therefore, I recommend rejection, but I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these numerous issues for a future submission.
