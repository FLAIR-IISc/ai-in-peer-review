Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for compositionality over parse trees by stacking multiple recursive layers (“depth in space”) on top of the structural recursion (“depth in structure”). The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters to map word embeddings into a phrase space, enabling ReLU activations and sparsity. They train with AdaGrad, dropout (with shared masks across a tree), and a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations, using fixed 300D pretrained word vectors. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), they report that deeper (narrower) stacked recursive models outperform single-layer recursive models with roughly equal parameter budgets, and achieve state-of-the-art results over prior RNTN/MV-RNN/DCNN/Paragraph Vector baselines. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and layerwise nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different aspects of composition (e.g., intensity vs polarity).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive neural networks for parsing/sentiment (Socher et al., 2011–2013) and the distinction between “depth in time” and “depth in space” for RNNs (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio). The idea of stacking recursive layers is a natural extension analogous to stacked recurrent networks. The paper positions itself against RNTN/MV-RNN and DCNNs on SST.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension: stacking recursive layers is a reasonable and underexplored design at the time, and untying leaf/internal parameters is sensible.
- Empirical gains over shallow recursive baselines with roughly matched parameter counts are interesting.
- Qualitative analyses provide some intuition for layer roles in compositionality.
- Practical training details (shared dropout over nodes; ReLU + small activation penalty; identity-biased initialization) are useful.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Methodology and reporting
- Critical missing training detail: “All other weights are initialized as .” is incomplete; without this, the experiments are not reproducible.
- Parameter budgets are said to be “kept constant within each group,” but exact parameter counts per configuration (depth/width) are not reported. Without a table of sizes and counts, the fairness of deep vs shallow comparisons is unverifiable.
- Loss function and supervision: it is implied that all nodes are supervised (as in SST), but the paper does not explicitly specify the loss (presumably summed cross-entropy) nor whether nodes are weighted (tree depth bias) or whether sentence/root performance is separately optimized. This is important because SST is dominated by short phrases; class imbalance and node-type imbalance are not discussed.
- Binary classification protocol: the binary performance is derived from the 5-way model without a dedicated binary model; several published baselines train binary models directly. The comparison may be unfair; at minimum report both settings or clearly separate evaluation regimes.
- Variance/significance: no standard deviations or multiple runs are reported. The gains over prior work could be within variance given small absolute improvements; statistical testing is absent.
- Ablations are incomplete. The paper attributes improvements to dropout and to untying/rectifiers/pretrained embeddings, but does not disentangle these effects systematically. Only one preliminary number is provided for L2 vs dropout. Missing ablations:
  - untied vs tied leaves/internals
  - ReLU vs tanh
  - with/without pretrained embeddings (and with fine-tuning)
  - dropout placement (which layers; input vs hidden; rate sensitivity)
  - activation L2 penalty vs gradient clipping (none used?)
  - output connected to final layer only vs deep supervision
- Stability: The paper notes exploding activations and introduces an ad hoc L2 penalty on activations (10^-5), but provides no analysis of gradient norms, the role of AdaGrad, or comparisons to clipping. Sharing dropout masks across nodes is asserted but not justified or compared to independent masks.
- Notation/initialization ambiguity: The text refers to WL/WR, Wxh· and Whh·; then states “Recursive weights within a layer (Whh) are initialized as 0.5I + ε.” It is unclear whether both WL and WR (square) get identity-biased initializations, how this applies to the first layer where Wxh are rectangular (cannot use I), and how V(i) (inter-layer) are initialized.
- Nearest neighbor and perturbation analysis use L1 distances without normalization. Given ReLU layers have scale changes (the authors note higher-layer activations have larger scale), cross-layer comparisons of distances are questionable without normalization or whitening. It is also unclear whether dropout is disabled during these analyses.

Baselines and scope
- Missing comparisons to gated composition models. While Tree-LSTM/GRU variants (e.g., later work) are not included, even sequential LSTM baselines on SST are absent. Given vanishing gradient concerns and the rise of gating mechanisms, an apples-to-apples baseline with a strong sequence LSTM would be informative.
- Fairness with DCNN/Paragraph Vectors is hard to assess; differences in supervision granularity and training protocols are not discussed.

Clarity/Presentation
- Several references are malformed or incomplete (many missing years/venues/pages; incorrect authors/editors; inconsistent formatting).
- Multiple typos and minor grammar issues (“evaulate,” inconsistent spacing in equations, etc.).
- Figures and tables are central to the claims (especially Table 1), but precise numbers/standard errors and parameter counts are not in the text; reproducibility suffers.
- The paper does not specify OOV handling, vocabulary size, or whether embedding fine-tuning was tried (they “keep word vectors fixed for simplicity,” but at least a discussion of trade-offs would help).

Quality: Technically plausible but undermined by missing initialization details, incomplete ablations, lack of variance/significance, and some ambiguity in the training objective and supervision weighting.

Clarity: Generally understandable but marred by missing details, ambiguous notation/initialization, and poor references.

Originality: Moderate. Stacking in recursive architectures is a natural extension; untying leaf/internal parameters and using ReLU/dropout are known techniques. The qualitative layer analyses are a nice touch.

Significance: Potentially useful if robust and reproducible; however, limited to SST and without stronger baselines and rigorous analysis, impact is uncertain.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea (stacked recursive layers) with encouraging empirical gains.
- Practical training recipe for deep tree models.
- Qualitative evidence that different layers capture complementary compositional aspects.

Arguments for rejection
- Reproducibility gap: incomplete initialization specification; no parameter counts; no variance/significance.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline comparisons; no gated models; no sequential LSTM baseline; binary evaluation mismatch.
- Insufficient ablations to support causal claims (dropout, untying, ReLU).
- Methodological ambiguities (loss aggregation/weighting; dropout scheme justification; stability remedies).
- Numerous reference and formatting errors; several typos.

Questions for authors
1. Provide exact parameter counts, depth/width per model, and complete initialization details (including V and non-square matrices).
2. Specify the loss and supervision scheme (node weighting, class imbalance handling).
3. Report mean±std over multiple runs and significance tests.
4. Add ablations isolating untying, ReLU, dropout placement, activation penalty, and embedding fine-tuning; consider deep supervision.
5. Include stronger baselines (sequence LSTM) and clarify fairness for binary tasks.
6. Normalize or otherwise justify the distance metrics in qualitative analyses.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and likely valuable, but the cumulative methodological and presentation issues prevent confident assessment and reproduction. With tighter experiments, clearer reporting, and stronger baselines, this could be a solid contribution.
