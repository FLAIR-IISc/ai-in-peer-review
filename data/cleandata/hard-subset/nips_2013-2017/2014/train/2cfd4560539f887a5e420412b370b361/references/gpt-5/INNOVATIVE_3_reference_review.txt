Summary
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for compositional modeling over tree structures. The core idea is to distinguish “depth in structure” (the height of the parse tree) from “depth in space” (stacking multiple recursive layers), and to combine them by stacking multiple recursive layers with inter-layer connections. The model also “unties” leaves from internal nodes, enabling separate mappings from word space to phrase space and the use of rectified linear units. The authors train with AdaGrad, dropout (with shared masks per sentence/layer), and a small L2 regularization on both weights and activations for stability. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), the deep variants outperform shallow recursive nets with comparable parameter counts and achieve new state of the art compared to MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses suggest distinct roles for layers (e.g., an intermediate layer capturing sentiment intensity), supported by input perturbation experiments and nearest-neighbor phrase analyses.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive architectures for NLP (RAE/RNN/MV-RNN/RNTN) and on stacking in recurrent networks (deep RNNs in time). The distinction between structural depth vs. representational depth is articulated clearly here and parallels recent stacking in sequential RNNs. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and the use of ReLUs and dropout are aligned with broader deep learning practice but, to my knowledge, are less explored in tree-structured nets at the time of the cited work. The empirical comparison to RNTN and Paragraph Vectors is appropriate; however, there is limited discussion of contemporaneous or closely related “deep” tree models (e.g., stacked tree autoencoders or gated tree architectures), and the novelty primarily lies in combining known components in a tree setting and analyzing their layer-wise effects.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: clear articulation of depth-in-structure vs depth-in-space and a straightforward architecture to realize it.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over shallow recursive baselines under a fixed parameter budget; claimed SOTA on SST in both 5-way and binary settings.
- Practical training recipe: rectifiers in tree nets, untied leaf/internal parameters enabling large fixed embeddings with smaller hidden layers, shared dropout mask across nodes, and a sensible initialization for Whh.
- Qualitative insights: the input perturbation and nearest-neighbor analyses are thoughtful and provide evidence that different layers capture complementary aspects of composition (lexical dominance, syntactic patterns, sentiment/intensity).
- Simplicity: no pretraining, fixed word vectors, and a clean design that many practitioners can reproduce.

Weaknesses
- Novelty feels incremental: stacking recursive layers is a natural extension of stacking recurrent layers, and most gains seem to come from a combination of standard techniques (pretrained embeddings, ReLU, dropout, better initialization), not a fundamentally new compositional operator.
- Limited scope: results are confined to a single dataset/task (SST). Generalization to other tree-structured tasks (e.g., entailment, semantic relatedness, parsing) is not demonstrated.
- Comparisons and fairness: DCNN and Paragraph Vectors typically do not leverage phrase-level supervision; deep recursive models trained with labels at all nodes have an advantage on SST. This should be discussed more explicitly, or additional “sentence-only supervision” comparisons should be reported.
- Ablation clarity: while there are some comparisons (shallow vs. deep, influence of dropout), a more systematic ablation disentangling the effects of (i) untying leaves/internals, (ii) ReLU vs. tanh, (iii) stacking depth, (iv) dropout/shared mask, and (v) fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings would strengthen causal claims.
- Stability and reproducibility: using an L2 penalty on activations and a shared dropout mask are sensible, but these choices are not deeply justified or compared against alternatives (e.g., layer norm, gradient clipping, independent dropout masks). No variance/error bars over multiple runs are provided.
- Clarity/formatting: some references are incomplete, and details like exact parameter counts per configuration and the full numeric tables (beyond selected values) are essential for reproduction.

Quality
Technically sound, with reasonable design choices and training heuristics. Claims are supported by quantitative and qualitative evidence on SST. The lack of broader evaluation and deeper ablations makes it harder to isolate the core architectural contribution.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The conceptual narrative is strong, and the qualitative sections are insightful. The paper would benefit from:
- Explicitly stating whether the loss is computed at all tree nodes (it appears so) and how class thresholds are applied.
- Reporting full tables with parameter counts and multiple runs.
- Clarifying the fairness of comparisons to models not using phrase-level supervision.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is the explicit introduction of representational depth in tree-structured nets via stacking, plus thoughtful training and analysis. The individual components are known; the combination and qualitative insights are the most original aspects.

Significance
Moderate to good. If the reported SOTA holds and the findings about layer roles are robust, this can influence how the community designs tree-based models. The approach is simple enough to be adopted. Broader impact would be stronger with demonstrations beyond SST.

Questions for the authors
- Do you train with supervision at all tree nodes or only at the root? Please clarify the loss aggregation.
- How sensitive are results to untying leaves vs. using a single shared composition space? Ablation?
- Did you try fine-tuning word embeddings? If so, how does that interact with depth?
- Does sharing the dropout mask across nodes materially help versus independent dropout per node? Please provide a comparison.
- Are the gains over RNTN preserved when matching the supervision regime (sentence-only vs. full-tree)?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, compelling conceptual framing and simple, general architecture.
- Empirical improvements with a fixed parameter budget.
- Useful qualitative analyses revealing different roles for layers.
- Practical training recipe likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; relies on known components.
- Limited evaluation scope; missing ablations and fairness discussion.
- Some reproducibility details are missing; no variance reporting.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. While not a radical departure, the paper introduces a clean and potentially impactful direction—adding representational depth to tree-structured models—backed by solid empirical gains and insightful analyses. With stronger ablations and broader validation, this could be a clear accept; as is, it still offers a useful contribution likely to influence practitioners.
