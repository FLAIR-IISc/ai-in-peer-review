- Proposal: stack multiple recursive layers over parse trees (deep RNNs) with vertical connections and output read from topmost layer.  
- Experiments (SST): deeper stacked recursive models outperform shallow ones under controlled parameter budgets and achieve SOTA fine-grained sentiment.  
- Qualitative analyses indicate layer specialization: lower layers capture lexical/syntactic similarity, upper layers capture sentiment polarity/intensity.  
- Builds on prior tree RNN and depth-in-recurrent work; applies modern training tricks (ReLUs, dropout, pretrained embeddings, AdaGrad) to tree models.  

Strengths:
- Clear idea and motivation separating depth in structure from depth in space for recursive models.  
- Empirical value: consistent depth benefits and competitive/SOTA results on SST.  
- Sound engineering choices: untied leaf/internal parameters, ReLUs, dropout with shared masks, stable Whh init, AdaGrad.  
- Useful qualitative analyses giving insight into layer behaviors.  
- Good experimental control: kept total parameters roughly constant when varying depth.  

Weaknesses / Concerns:
- Limited evaluation: only evaluated on SST; generality across other tree tasks is untested.  
- Incomplete ablations: unclear how much each component (untying, ReLU, dropout, pretrained embeddings, final-layer output) contributes.  
- No statistical rigor: no variance across runs or significance testing reported.  
- Regularization/initialization issues: reliance on a small L2 penalty on activations to prevent ReLU explosion; some initialization details missing/truncated.  
- Binary results derived from a 5-way model rather than a dedicated binary model, possibly unfair to baselines.  
- Clarity gaps: missing initialization scale, unclear parameterization of V matrices across higher layers, and missing exact parameter counts per configuration.  
- Dropout question: rationale and effects of sharing dropout masks across tree nodes per sentence need clarification and comparison to independent masks.  
- Originality is incremental: stacking is natural extension rather than novel composition function.  
- Significance limited by single-dataset evidence and incomplete ablations.  

Questions for authors (requested analyses):
- Report multiple runs with mean/std and significance tests versus RNTN and DCNN.  
- Ablate untying, ReLU vs tanh, pretrained vs learned embeddings, dropout on/off, and using only final-layer outputs.  
- Did you try gradient clipping instead of L2 on activations and were there stability issues for deeper stacks?  
- How sensitive are results to sharing the dropout mask across nodes vs independent masks?  
- Why freeze embeddings—does fine-tuning help or hurt with ReLUs and dropout?  
- Can you include results on at least one additional tree-structured task to support generality?  

Minor suggestions:
- Provide exact hyperparameters (widths per depth), parameter counts, dropout rates, and training time/memory.  
- Explore skip/residual connections between nonadjacent layers to ease deeper optimization.  
- Expand qualitative analyses with more examples and quantitative probes (e.g., linear probes for polarity/intensity).  

Reproducibility:
- Uses standard dataset and public embeddings, but missing initialization details and lack of code release reduce reproducibility.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated extension with demonstrated empirical gains and practical training recipe; SOTA on fine-grained SST at the time.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Single-dataset evaluation, incomplete ablations and significance testing, incremental novelty, and missing details hinder full assessment.  

Overall rating: Weak accept.  
Reviewer confidence: High.
