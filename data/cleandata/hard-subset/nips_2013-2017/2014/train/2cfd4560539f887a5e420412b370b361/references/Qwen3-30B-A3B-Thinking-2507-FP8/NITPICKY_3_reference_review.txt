

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

This paper proposes "deep recursive neural networks" (DRNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers, claiming this architecture outperforms shallow recursive networks on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment analysis. The authors distinguish between "depth in structure" (recursive networks) and "depth in space" (stacked layers), arguing their DRNNs combine both concepts. While the paper presents an interesting architectural extension to recursive neural networks (RNNs), it suffers from multiple critical terminology inconsistencies and methodological ambiguities that undermine its technical rigor.

The most severe issue is the paper's inconsistent use of "RNN" to refer to both Recurrent Neural Networks and Recursive Neural Networks. In standard NLP terminology, RNNs are recurrent networks, not recursive ones. The paper states: "a recurrent neural network is simply a recursive neural network with a particular structure" (Section 1), but then uses "RNN" as the standard abbreviation for both, creating immediate confusion. This is particularly problematic since the paper's core contribution is about recursive networks, not recurrent ones. The paper's Figure 1c caption incorrectly identifies a recurrent network as "RNN," further muddying the terminology. This is a fundamental error in technical writing that would confuse readers and reviewers alike.

The paper also fails to clarify key methodological details. In Equation (1), the authors write "xη = f(WLxl(η) +WRxr(η) + b)" but omit dimensions of the weight matrices, which is critical for reproducibility. In Section 2.2, the authors claim that untying leaves and internals "allows us to use large pretrained word vectors and a small number of hidden units without a quadratic dependence on the word vector dimensionality |x|." This is misleading—while the weight matrices have different dimensions, the computational complexity still depends on the word vector dimensionality, just in a different way.

The paper's experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. It states "We use the publicly available 300 dimensional word vectors by [19]" but fails to specify whether these are GloVe, word2vec, or another embedding, and doesn't provide the exact source or preprocessing steps. The paper also mentions using "the single training-validation-test set partitioning provided by the authors" but doesn't specify which exact partition was used from SST's multiple available splits.

Several technical inconsistencies further weaken the paper. The authors claim "deep RNNs outperform shallow RNNs of the same size" but later refer to "shallow RNN" when they mean "shallow recursive neural network," creating confusion. The paper's Figure 2 description (regarding weight sharing) is impossible to verify without the figure, and the paper references Figure 3 without providing the actual figure or sufficient detail about the perturbation experiment.

The paper also misrepresents prior work in its comparison. It claims to outperform "Paragraph Vectors" but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the exact implementation details used in the original Paragraph Vectors paper. The paper's Table 1b references "previous work" but fails to clarify whether these are the exact numbers from the cited papers or re-implemented results.

The paper's claim that "each layer handles a different aspect of compositionality" is supported only by qualitative analysis of nearest neighbors and input perturbations, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about how these analyses were conducted or how the "different aspects" were quantified. The qualitative results in Table 2 are presented without statistical significance tests, making it difficult to assess whether the observed differences are meaningful.

The paper also contains minor formatting issues: inconsistent use of "RNN" (for both recurrent and recursive networks), incorrect citation format for the Bengio et al. (2009) paper, and inconsistent equation numbering (e.g., Equation (4) references "h(i) l(η)" which should be "h(i)l(η)" for clarity).

While the paper's core idea is conceptually interesting and the experimental results appear promising, the numerous technical inconsistencies, terminology confusion, and lack of reproducibility details are unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify terminology (using "recursive neural network" consistently and avoiding the misleading abbreviation "RNN"), provide missing methodological details, and properly contextualize the work within the existing literature on recursive networks.

In summary, the paper makes a potentially valuable contribution to recursive neural network architectures, but its technical presentation is marred by fundamental terminology errors and insufficient methodological detail. These issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility and reproducibility, which are essential for a top-tier machine learning conference like NIPS.

Pro: Interesting architectural extension of recursive neural networks, promising empirical results on SST, qualitative analysis suggesting layer specialization.

Con: Severe terminology confusion between RNNs and recursive neural networks, lack of methodological detail for reproducibility, inconsistent notation, insufficient comparison to prior work.
