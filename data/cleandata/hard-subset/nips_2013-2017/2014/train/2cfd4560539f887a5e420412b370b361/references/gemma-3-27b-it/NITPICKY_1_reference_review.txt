This paper introduces deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs), extending recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The core idea is to combine depth in structure (from the recursive application within each layer) with depth in space (from stacking layers), drawing parallels to recent advances in deep recurrent neural networks. The authors evaluate DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating improved performance over shallow RNNs and existing state-of-the-art methods. Qualitative analyses suggest that different layers capture varying aspects of compositionality.

While the idea of extending recursive networks to multiple layers is intuitively appealing and the reported results are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, issues that detract from its overall quality. The presentation is often imprecise, lacking rigorous mathematical formulation in several key areas, and the experimental details, while extensive, are inconsistently reported.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of stacking recursive layers is a logical extension of existing work and offers a potentially valuable architectural improvement.
* **Empirical Results:** The DRNNs demonstrably outperform several baselines on the SST dataset, including achieving new state-of-the-art performance.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor experiments provide some initial insights into the behavior of different layers.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigor:** Equation (4) defining the deep RNN layer is poorly motivated. Why is V(i) applied *additively* to the hidden state? What is the theoretical justification for this? The intuition given is weak. Similarly, the explanation for why untying leaves and internals allows for the use of rectifiers is hand-wavy and lacks a clear connection to the underlying principles of activation functions and representation learning.
* **Inconsistent Notation:** The notation is occasionally ambiguous and inconsistent. For example, 'hη' is sometimes used to represent the hidden state *and* the overall hidden representation of a node, leading to potential confusion. The use of subscripts for layers (e.g., W(i)L) is good, but inconsistent; sometimes weights are indexed by layer, sometimes they are not.
* **Overly Detailed Methodology:** While detailed methodology is generally positive, here it borders on excessive and detracts from the clarity of the core ideas. The lengthy descriptions of regularization, training procedures, and initialization techniques feel more like a recipe than a rigorous explanation. Furthermore, the justification for the specific values chosen (e.g., the L2 penalty of 10^-5) is lacking.
* **Redundancy and Repetition:** The introduction repeats information unnecessarily. The discussion of depth in time vs. space, while relevant, feels somewhat forced and doesn’t substantially contribute to the core argument.
* **Minor Errors and Imprecisions:** Several minor errors and imprecisions are present throughout the paper. For instance, the statement that "With this untying, a recursive network becomes a generalization of the Elman type recurrent neural network" is a stretch. While there's a conceptual similarity, it's not a direct generalization. Furthermore, the claim that the network *forces* high-level information into the final layer by connecting the output layer only to it is questionable; this is a design choice, not an inherent property.
* **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The ablation study regarding dropout is limited. A more thorough analysis of the impact of different regularization techniques (e.g., dropout rates, L2 penalties) would be valuable.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the qualitative analyses are a good start, a more detailed error analysis identifying specific cases where the DRNNs fail would provide further insights.
* **Reference Formatting:** The reference list has inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., some entries include issue numbers, others do not).

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:** The paper, despite its promising results, feels unfinished and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively undermine the credibility of the work. The lack of theoretical justification for key design choices and the inconsistent presentation detract from the clarity of the ideas.

**For:** The idea is novel and the experimental results are encouraging. The qualitative analyses offer some initial insights into the behavior of the network. The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP and contributes to the growing body of work on compositional semantics.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the authors have presented an interesting idea and achieved promising empirical results, the paper’s numerous flaws – particularly the lack of rigor, inconsistent notation, and excessive methodological detail – prevent it from meeting the quality standards expected of a NIPS publication. The authors would significantly benefit from addressing these issues and resubmitting a revised version with a stronger theoretical foundation and a more polished presentation. A stronger emphasis on the mathematical underpinnings of the proposed architecture and a more careful attention to detail are crucial for elevating this work to a publishable level.
