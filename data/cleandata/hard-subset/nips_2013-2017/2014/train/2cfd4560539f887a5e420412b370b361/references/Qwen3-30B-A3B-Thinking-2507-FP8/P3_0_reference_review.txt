

# Peer Review: "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

## Summary

This paper proposes "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (DRNNs) as an extension to traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of each other. The key insight is that while traditional recursive networks are "deep in structure" (due to their recursive application on parse trees), they lack "depth in space" (i.e., hierarchical processing across multiple layers). The authors argue that combining these two notions of depth can lead to better compositional representations. They introduce an "untied" variant where word representations (leaves) and phrase representations (internal nodes) occupy different vector spaces, enabling the use of rectifier activation units. The paper evaluates DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment analysis, showing that deeper DRNNs outperform shallower recursive networks and previous baselines, including the multiplicative RNN (RNTN) and Paragraph Vectors. The authors further provide qualitative evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

This work builds on the seminal recursive neural network work by Socher et al. (2013) at NIPS, which introduced recursive networks for sentiment analysis using the SST dataset. It also relates to recent developments in deep learning, particularly the distinction between "depth in time" (recurrent networks) and "depth in space" (stacked layers) that has been explored in deep recurrent networks (Hermans & Schrauwen, 2013; Schmidhuber, 1997). However, the paper makes a novel contribution by applying this distinction specifically to recursive networks for language compositionality.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Quality**: The paper presents a well-motivated extension to recursive neural networks with clear mathematical formulations. The DRNN architecture is logically derived from the limitations of traditional RNNs. The experimental design is strong, using the SST dataset with appropriate baselines and thorough quantitative evaluation. The qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrases) provide compelling evidence for the claim that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

2. **Novelty**: The paper makes a clear conceptual distinction between "depth in structure" (recursive networks) and "depth in space" (stacked layers), which is novel in the context of recursive neural networks. The untied variant of leaves and internals is also a meaningful contribution that enables the use of rectifier units and addresses a limitation of previous recursive network formulations.

3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The DRNN models achieve new state-of-the-art results on the SST fine-grained sentiment classification task, outperforming previous recursive network variants and competing methods like Paragraph Vectors. The consistent improvement with depth (up to a point) provides strong empirical validation for the proposed architecture.

4. **Qualitative Analysis**: The qualitative analysis is one of the paper's strongest points. The input perturbation experiment demonstrates how sentiment intensity is processed across layers, and the nearest neighbor analysis shows how semantic similarity evolves across layers. These analyses provide valuable insights into how the model processes language compositionality.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Qualitative Analysis**: The qualitative analysis is limited to just two examples for input perturbation and two for nearest neighbor phrases. While these examples are illustrative, more comprehensive qualitative analysis would strengthen the claims about different layers capturing different aspects of compositionality. For instance, showing how different layers process negation or complex syntactic structures would be more convincing.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Other Deep Architectures**: The paper doesn't compare to other deep architectures that might handle compositionality differently, such as LSTMs, transformers, or deep contextualized word representations (e.g., ELMo, BERT). This is particularly notable given the recent success of transformer-based models in NLP. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the gains are specific to the recursive structure or would be achievable with other deep architectures.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper notes that deeper networks eventually start to degrade in performance but doesn't thoroughly investigate why. The authors mention "decrease in width dominating gains from increased depth," but don't explore this in depth or discuss potential solutions. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between depth and width would be valuable.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of deeper networks compared to shallower ones or other architectures. For practical applications, this is an important consideration that would strengthen the paper's significance.

5. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a theoretical justification for why stacking recursive layers would improve compositional representations. A brief discussion of how the hierarchical representation might relate to linguistic theories of compositionality would add depth to the argument.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of compositionality in language through the introduction of deep recursive neural networks. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides both strong empirical results and insightful qualitative analyses. While there are some limitations in the scope of the analysis, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is significant for the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand Qualitative Analysis**: Include more examples in the qualitative analysis, particularly focusing on challenging linguistic phenomena like negation, idioms, and complex syntactic structures.

2. **Add Comparison to Modern Architectures**: Include comparisons with at least one modern deep architecture (e.g., transformer-based models) to better position the contribution within the current NLP landscape.

3. **Discuss Limitations More Thoroughly**: Add a subsection discussing the limitations of the current approach, particularly the trade-off between depth and width, and potential solutions.

4. **Clarify Relationship to Deep Recurrent Networks**: The paper mentions "deep recurrent networks" in the introduction but doesn't clearly distinguish how DRNNs differ from those. Clarifying this would strengthen the paper's positioning in the literature.

5. **Include Computational Cost Analysis**: Add a brief discussion of the computational cost of deeper networks and how it compares to other architectures.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel extension to recursive neural networks with clear conceptual distinction between depth in structure and depth in space
- Strong empirical results showing state-of-the-art performance on SST
- Insightful qualitative analysis demonstrating different layers capture different aspects of compositionality
- Well-organized and clearly written paper with solid technical foundation

**Con:**
- Limited qualitative analysis (only two examples for key analyses)
- No comparison to modern deep architectures (e.g., transformers)
- Insufficient discussion of limitations, particularly the degradation with excessive depth
- No computational cost analysis

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional language modeling and provides compelling evidence for the benefits of depth in recursive networks. The weaknesses are notable but do not undermine the significance of the main contribution. The paper would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
