Summary:
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers over tree structures, aiming to disentangle depth-in-structure from depth-in-space. The model also “unties” leaves and internal nodes (mapping word-space X to phrase-space H at the first layer) and uses rectified linear units, dropout, and AdaGrad. The authors evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), reporting improvements over shallow RNNs and several published baselines (MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work:
The work connects to recursive neural networks for parsing and sentiment (Socher et al.), multiplicative/tensor composition (RNTN), matrix–vector composition (MV-RNN), and stacked recurrent nets in time (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). The idea of stacking tree-structured composition layers is a natural extension of “deep” notions from sequential RNNs to recursive nets.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for separating depth-in-structure vs depth-in-space; stacking is conceptually sensible.
- The leaf/internal untying with ReLU is a pragmatic and potentially impactful design; it also enables use of large pretrained embeddings with smaller hidden states.
- Empirical improvements over a shallow RNN with similar parameter budgets; exploration of depth/width trade-offs.
- Sensible engineering: dropout, AdaGrad, identity-biased initialization for WL/WR.
- Qualitative analyses are interesting and pedagogical, hinting at layerwise specialization.

Weaknesses (technical and methodological):
- Critical missing content: Figures (1–3) and Tables (1–2) are referenced but not included. This prevents verification of architectures, results, and qualitative claims.
- Incomplete specification: “All other weights are initialized as .” appears to be truncated; initializations for V(i), U, Wxh are not provided. Dimensions of V(i) for leaves vs internals (Vxh(2), Vhh(2)) are not fully specified beyond a brief mention.
- Supervision scope unclear in the deep setting: SST provides node-level labels, but it is ambiguous whether the loss is applied at all nodes or only roots in Eq. (5) (it says “connect the output layer to only the final hidden layer,” but not whether to all nodes at that layer). This affects training signal and fairness vs prior work.
- Comparison fairness: Many baselines fine-tune word embeddings; authors freeze embeddings. The shallow RNN they implement uses ReLU and dropout, whereas RNTN/MV-RNN results are from prior work with different regularization and possibly different preprocessing. No controlled re-implementations with matched conditions are provided.
- No variance reporting: Results appear to be single runs with early stopping, no multiple seeds, no standard deviations or significance tests. The claimed SOTA and dropout attribution (46.06→49.5) are not statistically supported.
- Hyperparameter tuning is under-specified: Only dropout rate is tuned; learning rate, hidden sizes, and depth selection strategy are not sufficiently detailed. The “constant parameter budget” claim is not backed with actual parameter counts per configuration.
- Regularization/stability: The paper adds an L2 penalty on activations (10−5) to avoid exploding activations but does not justify this choice vs standard practices (gradient clipping, layer norm). No details on test-time scaling for dropout, nor the effect of “locked dropout” across nodes (used here) vs independent dropout.
- Input perturbation analysis and nearest-neighbor evaluations rely on unavailable figures/tables; additionally, nearest neighbors use L1 distance on ReLU activations without normalization, which confounds magnitude with similarity; cosine similarity or normalized distances would be more appropriate.
- Computational aspects are missing: no training time, memory, batch sizing in terms of nodes, or complexity analysis, despite a deeper architecture and per-layer structured propagation.
- Notation/definitions are occasionally confusing: “positional DAG” is not defined; switching between xη and hη with hη = xη at leaves is hard to follow; Eq. (3) superscripts/subscripts are dense and could be clarified; whether WL/WR at higher layers are shared across positions is implied but not crisply stated.

Clarity and presentation issues:
- Numerous typographical and grammatical glitches (e.g., “Recursive neural networks, comprise…”, inconsistent spacing/commas).
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/pages, author misspellings, venues not standardized).
- Several sentences are awkward or incomplete (e.g., the truncated weight initialization line).

Originality:
- Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward but nontrivial extension; the leaf/internal untying with ReLU is a useful design. However, the conceptual novelty is incremental relative to prior recursive architectures and deep RNNs in time. The paper does not position itself against contemporaneous or near-contemporaneous tree variants (e.g., better gating like Tree-LSTM), nor does it explore multi-task/auxiliary losses across layers.

Significance:
- If substantiated, the empirical gains on SST would be of interest. However, absent figures/tables, matched baselines, and variance analysis, the significance is difficult to assess. Practical adoption is impeded by reproducibility gaps.

Quality:
- The core idea is sound, but the paper falls short on experimental rigor, clarity, and completeness of specification. Several key claims cannot be evaluated due to missing content.

Reproducibility:
- Currently low: missing tables/figures, incomplete hyperparameters and initializations, unclear loss application, and no code or detailed preprocessing description (OOV handling, tokenization, capitalization).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, well-motivated architectural idea; empirical indications that adding depth-in-space to trees helps.
- Pragmatic engineering choices (ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad) that may benefit the community working on tree models.
- Qualitative analyses suggest interpretable layer behavior.

Arguments for rejection:
- Missing figures/tables and truncated methods text; key results and visuals cannot be assessed.
- Insufficiently controlled and reported comparisons; no variance/significance.
- Ambiguities in supervision setup and parameter counting undermine core claims.
- Numerous presentation and reference-quality issues below NeurIPS standards.

Questions for authors:
- Please include all figures/tables and provide exact parameter counts per configuration. Clarify whether the loss is applied at all tree nodes on the final layer or only at roots.
- Specify all initializations (including V(i), U, Wxh), dropout scaling at test time, and whether gradients are clipped.
- Report mean±std over multiple seeds and perform matched re-implementations of baselines (or release code).
- Justify locked dropout and L1 neighbor distance; consider cosine similarity with normalization.

Typos/formatting (non-exhaustive):
- Truncated sentence: “All other weights are initialized as .”
- Multiple reference entries lack years/venues; author names misspelled (e.g., “Jeffrey Pennin”, “Jaz K”).
- Inconsistent punctuation and spacing in several places.

Recommendation: Reject (due to missing content, insufficient experimental rigor, and clarity/presentation issues), with encouragement to resubmit after fixing the omissions, tightening comparisons, and improving reproducibility.

Confidence: High regarding presentation/methodological issues; medium regarding empirical claims (cannot verify without the missing tables/figures).
