Summary
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RNNs) for compositionality in language by stacking multiple tree-structured recursive layers. Each layer performs bottom-up composition along a binary parse tree with shared structural weights, and each node also feeds its hidden state vertically to its counterpart in the next layer. The model “unties” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer to map from word space to phrase space, enabling the use of rectified linear units (ReLUs). Training uses fixed pretrained word vectors, dropout (with shared masks across tree nodes per sentence/layer), AdaGrad, and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations. The output layer is connected only to the final recursive layer. Experiments on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) show that deeper, narrower stacks outperform parameter-matched shallow TreeRNNs, and the best model achieves state-of-the-art results on 5-way and binary sentiment classification versus RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrase comparisons) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on TreeRNNs (Goller & Küchler; Socher et al.), distinguishing depth in structure (tree depth) from depth in space (stacked layers), analogous to stacked recurrent networks (El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen). It compares against multiplicative/tensor composition (RNTN) and MV-RNN, as well as non-tree models (DCNN, Paragraph Vectors). The untying of leaf/internal parameters and the use of ReLUs with dropout are consistent with advances in deep nets (Glorot et al.; Srivastava et al.). To my knowledge, stacking multiple recursive layers over trees with vertical connections V(i) and output only on the top layer is not standard in prior TreeRNN literature and is a clear incremental novelty.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture: combining structural recursion with vertical depth, and untying leaves to leverage ReLU activations.
- Strong empirical results: parameter-controlled comparisons show gains from depth; new SOTA on SST fine-grained and binary (using the same 5-way-trained model), despite not fine-tuning embeddings.
- Solid training recipe: ReLU + dropout + AdaGrad + careful initialization; practical stabilization via small L2 on activations.
- Useful qualitative analyses that illuminate layer roles (intensity vs polarity).
- Simplicity and reproducibility: public embeddings, standard optimizer, and no pretraining.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: stacking TreeRNN layers is a natural extension of stacked RNNs; no new composition operator or theoretical advances.
- Fairness of comparisons: the model exploits phrase-level supervision at all tree nodes; some baselines (e.g., DCNN, Paragraph Vectors) may not, which could advantage the proposed method. This should be discussed and, if possible, controlled.
- Limited ablations: while there is one comparison highlighting dropout’s impact, the paper does not disentangle the contributions of untying, ReLU, dropout, and depth. A shallow TreeRNN with all modern tricks is partially reported, but a more systematic ablation is needed.
- No statistical significance or variance across random seeds; results appear single-run with early stopping. SST is known to have variance across inits.
- Missing details: exact parameter counts, per-model sizes (|h|, number of layers) and training time are not fully specified in the text; clarity on where dropout is applied (pre/post nonlinearity), inference scaling, and whether inverted-dropout is used at test.
- Generalization: reliance on phrase-level labels may limit applicability; the method’s performance without internal-node supervision is not evaluated.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully engineered. The training stability considerations (ReLU, shared dropout, L2 on activations, identity-ish initialization of recursive weights) are appropriate. Claims about outperforming shallow TreeRNNs are supported by parameter-matched comparisons; the SOTA claim relies on previously published numbers but may be confounded by supervision granularity and embedding fine-tuning differences. The qualitative analyses are insightful but limited in scope (a few examples).

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The distinction between depth in structure vs space is clear. Figures (as described) help. A more explicit listing of hyperparameters, parameter counts per model, and precise dropout application would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately novel. The key idea—stacked recursive layers with vertical connections—is a straightforward but meaningful extension to TreeRNNs. The untying of leaves/internal nodes and ReLU use are sensible adaptations rather than novel contributions.

Significance
The method advances SOTA on a prominent benchmark and provides a stronger baseline for tree-structured composition. The idea is likely to be adopted by practitioners working on tree models. Broader impact is somewhat limited by dependence on parse trees and phrase-level supervision, but the approach is conceptually general.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations isolating the effects of untying, ReLU, dropout, and depth; include a shallow model with exactly the same training recipe and embeddings as the deep model.
- Report averages and standard deviations over multiple random seeds; include statistical significance tests.
- Clarify the supervision used by each baseline (sentence-only vs phrase-level) and, if feasible, run a version of your model trained with sentence-level supervision only for fairness.
- Detail model sizes, parameter counts, training time, and inference cost per sentence; include learning-rate and dropout specifics (mask location, test-time scaling).
- Explore embedding fine-tuning and/or compare to RNTN/DCNN variants using fixed vs tuned embeddings.
- Consider connecting outputs to intermediate layers as an ablation to support claims about layer roles.

Questions for authors
1) Do you apply dropout before or after the ReLU? Is inverted-dropout used at test time?
2) Are your reported baselines trained with phrase-level supervision? If not, how might this affect the comparison?
3) What are the exact parameter counts and |h| for each depth in Table 1a? How sensitive are results to |h| at fixed depth?
4) What is the variance across 5–10 random seeds?
5) How does the model perform if trained with only sentence-level labels?

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural extension with clear empirical gains.
- New SOTA on a widely used benchmark with a clean training setup.
- Insightful qualitative analysis of compositional layers.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; limited theoretical insight.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons need strengthening (phrase-level supervision; variance; ablations).
- Limited generalization evidence beyond SST.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
