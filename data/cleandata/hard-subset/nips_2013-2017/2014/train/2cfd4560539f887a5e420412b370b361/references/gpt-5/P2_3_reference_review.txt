Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (Deep RvNNs) that stack multiple recursive layers on top of a parse-tree structure, distinguishing “depth in structure” (from tree recursion) and “depth in space” (from stacking). Within each layer, parent representations are computed recursively from child nodes; across layers, node-wise feedforward connections pass intermediate representations upward in depth. The authors also “untie” leaf and internal-node parameters, enabling different transforms from words to phrase space, and use rectified linear activations with dropout and AdaGrad. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with labels at all tree nodes. Empirically, deeper (but narrower) stacks outperform shallow RvNNs with a matched parameter budget and achieve reported state-of-the-art results on fine-grained sentiment. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation and nearest-neighbor phrases) suggest layers capture different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive neural networks over parse trees (e.g., Socher et al., 2011–2013) and on the notion of adding “depth in space” to recurrent networks (e.g., Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio). Compared to RNTN/MV-RNN, the novelty is not a new composition operator but stacking multiple recursive layers with node-wise vertical connections, paired with rectifiers, dropout, and untied leaf/internal parameters. Comparisons are provided to RNN, MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN on SST.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea: stacking recursive layers to separate levels of abstraction, with a well-motivated untying of leaf/internal transforms that enables ReLUs.
- Solid empirical signal: with matched parameter count, deeper models consistently outperform shallow RvNNs; best model outperforms prior published baselines on SST fine-grained and binary tasks (using the same split).
- Training choices are sensible and reproducible: fixed pre-trained word vectors, AdaGrad, dropout (shared across tree nodes), early stopping, and initialization details.
- Qualitative analyses are insightful and consistent with the intended role of depth; they suggest layer-wise specialization (e.g., intensity vs polarity).
- Paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: stacking is a straightforward extension of recursive nets, and the empirical gains may partially stem from training refinements (ReLU, dropout, leaf/internal untying) rather than depth per se; the ablations do not fully disentangle these factors.
- Limited scope: evaluation is on a single dataset (SST). Claims about compositionality and generality would be stronger with additional tasks (e.g., other sentiment datasets, entailment, paraphrase).
- Statistical rigor: no report of variance across random seeds or statistical significance; best-dev model selection without confidence intervals makes the SOTA claim less robust.
- Baseline fairness: authors keep word embeddings fixed, which is fine, but prior baselines may fine-tune embeddings; a reimplementation of strong baselines under the same training protocol (ReLU/dropout, fixed vs tuned embeddings) would better isolate architectural contributions.
- Design choices under-explored: output connected only to the last layer is justified for interpretability, but empirical trade-offs versus multi-layer supervision are not shown. Similarly, the impact of the unusual L2 penalty on activations, gradient clipping, and depth/width trade-offs are only partially explored.
- Practicality: reliance on gold-quality binarized parse trees is assumed; sensitivity to parse errors or parser type isn’t evaluated. Computational cost and training time versus shallow or CNN baselines are not reported.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the model and training are coherent and the empirical work is careful within the chosen setting. However, the causal attribution of gains to depth would benefit from stronger ablations (e.g., shallow+ReLU+dropout+untied vs deep, matched params; with and without fixed embeddings). Lack of variance/significance reporting weakens the strength of the SOTA claim.

Clarity
Clear and well organized. Most implementation details are provided, though exact parameter counts per configuration, training time, and random-seed variance are missing. Minor typos exist (“evaulate”, etc.).

Originality
Moderate. The central idea—stacking recursive layers with vertical connections—is a natural extension of deep RNNs to tree-structured computation. The untying of leaf/internal parameters and systematic use of ReLU/dropout in RvNNs are practical refinements rather than fundamentally new concepts. The qualitative layer-probing is a nice contribution.

Significance
Moderate. Achieving improved performance on SST is useful, and the idea may be broadly applicable to other tree/DAG-structured tasks. Nonetheless, without broader validation and stronger ablations, the impact is more incremental than transformative.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please provide ablations isolating the effect of depth from ReLU, dropout, and leaf/internal untying (e.g., shallow vs deep under identical components).
- Report mean/std over multiple seeds and significance tests on SST.
- Evaluate on at least one additional compositionality-sensitive dataset to support generality.
- Compare connecting supervision at all layers vs only the last layer.
- Report parameter counts, training time, and memory usage across depths.
- Analyze sensitivity to parse errors and to the choice of parser/binarization.
- Clarify initialization scales for non-recursive weights and whether gradient clipping was used.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with consistent empirical gains under a fixed parameter budget.
- Clear write-up and insightful qualitative analyses.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; depth vs other training improvements not fully disentangled.
- Single-dataset evaluation; limited statistical rigor.
- Missing analyses on computational cost and robustness to parsing.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a clean and useful extension of recursive nets with convincing (if narrow) empirical improvements and good qualitative evidence for layer specialization. Strengthening ablations and statistical reporting would further justify acceptance.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
