Summary
The paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking multiple recursive layers on top of a tree-structured composition function. The authors also “untie” word-to-phrase and phrase-to-phrase composition at the first layer, enabling the use of pretrained word embeddings and rectified linear units. Within each layer, composition is performed bottom-up along a binary parse tree with shared weights; across layers, each node passes its hidden state to its counterpart at the next depth via a feedforward connection. Supervision is applied only at the top layer. Training uses AdaGrad, dropout (with shared masks per sentence and layer), and a small L2 penalty on weights and activations. On the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, DRNNs outperform shallow tree RNNs with comparable parameter budgets and achieve state-of-the-art fine-grained sentiment accuracy compared to RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative probes (word replacement along a path, nearest neighbors across layers) suggest that different layers learn different aspects of compositionality.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on tree-structured neural models (Socher et al.) and stacked recurrent networks (Hermans & Schrauwen; El Hihi & Bengio), transferring the notion of “depth in space” to recursive architectures. It contrasts with multiplicative/tensor composition (RNTN) and matrix-vector composition (MV-RNN) by relying on simple linear-recurrent composition with ReLUs and depth. The use of pretrained word2vec embeddings and dropout are standard, but their integration into tree architectures and the untying of leaf/internal parameters are sensible and practically impactful. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to gated composition functions (e.g., LSTM-style or gated recursive/Tree-LSTM variants), which are natural alternatives to address vanishing/exploding gradients in deep structures.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural idea: stacking tree composition layers with shared within-layer recursive weights and cross-layer feedforward connections.
- Sound empirical finding that depth (with fixed parameter budget) improves over width for tree models on SST.
- New state-of-the-art on fine-grained sentiment with a relatively simple model, without fine-tuning word embeddings.
- Careful regularization (dropout and small activation penalty) and training setup yields stable optimization with ReLUs.
- Useful qualitative analyses that plausibly support the claim that layers specialize (lexical/syntactic vs. sentiment-oriented).
- Reproducibility is facilitated by many stated training details (optimizer, minibatch, initialization, early stopping).

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: only a single dataset (SST) and task (sentiment). Claims about compositionality would be stronger with other tree-structured tasks (semantic relatedness, paraphrase, entailment) or non-sentiment datasets.
- Fairness of comparisons: DRNNs use fixed pretrained 300d embeddings; several baselines (e.g., RNTN, MV-RNN) in their original reports fine-tune embeddings and/or differ in parameter counts. The paper controls parameter budgets within its own models but does not report matched parameter and embedding settings vs. baselines.
- Ablation is incomplete: no systematic study of (i) tied vs. untied leaf/internal parameters, (ii) ReLU vs. tanh, (iii) dropout vs. L2/none, (iv) supervision at all nodes vs. only roots (SST enables both), and (v) connecting outputs to all layers vs. only the last. Reported improvements could be primarily due to pretrained embeddings and dropout rather than depth.
- Stability choices (L2 penalty on activations, shared dropout masks) are somewhat ad hoc and under-justified. No gradient clipping is used; a comparison could clarify whether clipping or gating obviates the need for activation penalties.
- The qualitative analyses are insightful but anecdotal; no aggregate statistics across many paths/phrases or human evaluation are provided.
- Notation clarity: Section 2.3 mentions separate V for leaves vs. internals above the first layer but Eq. (4) does not reflect this; details on dimensionalities and parameter counts per setting are missing.
- No runtime/memory analysis; scaling with tree size and depth, and training time vs. shallow/tensor baselines, are not discussed.
- No variance estimates or statistical significance on the reported gains.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the model is well-specified and training choices are reasonable. The experimental methodology demonstrates the main claim (depth helps under a fixed parameter budget) on SST, but stronger ablations and broader benchmarks are needed to fully isolate where the gains come from. The qualitative evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The conceptual distinction between depth in structure vs. depth in space is explained well. Some notational inconsistencies and missing hyperparameter/parameter-count details should be fixed. Including the exact numbers of parameters for each configuration and clarifying the shared/dropout scheme would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking is a natural extension of recursive models, analogous to stacked RNNs; untying leaves/internals and using ReLUs with pretrained embeddings are incremental but useful. The main novelty lies in showing that such stacking is effective in tree-structured composition with empirical and qualitative support.

Significance
Empirically significant on SST with a new SOTA at the time, and practically relevant because the approach is simple and complementary to more complex composition functions. Potential to influence subsequent tree architectures. However, broader significance is tempered by the narrow evaluation and incomplete ablations.

Questions for the authors
1. How much of the gain comes from depth vs. pretrained embeddings vs. dropout vs. ReLUs? Please provide ablations.
2. Did you try fine-tuning embeddings and/or training without pretrained embeddings for a matched comparison to RNTN?
3. Can you report parameter counts and wall-clock training time for each configuration?
4. What happens if you connect outputs to all layers or supervise only at the root?
5. Did you try gradient clipping instead of (or in addition to) the L2 activation penalty?
6. How sensitive are results to using shared vs. independent dropout masks across tree nodes?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural extension that improves a strong baseline.
- New state-of-the-art on a widely used benchmark with careful training.
- Useful qualitative insights into layer specialization.

Cons for acceptance
- Single-task evaluation; limited ablations; fairness to baselines uncertain.
- Missing comparisons to gated tree models; stability choices not fully justified.
- Lacks statistical significance and runtime analysis.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clear and effective idea with solid empirical improvements on SST and insightful analysis, but it would benefit from broader evaluation and stronger ablations to firmly establish the source of gains and generality.

Reviewer confidence
High.
