Summary
This paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (Deep RNNs) for modeling compositionality in language by stacking multiple recursive (tree-structured) layers. The key idea is to disentangle “depth in structure” (induced by the parse tree) from “depth in space” (stacked layers), analogous to deep recurrent networks that stack layers over time. The model also “unties” parameters for leaves vs. internal nodes, enabling the use of large fixed pretrained word vectors and rectified linear units. The authors evaluate on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all nodes, show that deeper/narrower models outperform shallow/wider ones under a fixed parameter budget, and report new state-of-the-art results at the time against RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation along a path; nearest neighbors across layers) suggest different layers encode different aspects of compositionality (e.g., intensity vs. polarity).

Relation to prior work
The work extends recursive neural networks over trees (Socher et al.) by adding stacked layers (akin to El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen for recurrent nets) and contrasts with multiplicative/tensor composition (RNTN) and MV-RNN. Compared to prior “deep” structures that are deep only in tree depth or time, the contribution is to introduce depth across layers in tree-structured compositional models. The untying of leaf/internal parameters is related to standard practices of separating input-to-hidden and hidden-to-hidden transformations in RNNs, but is less common in tree models.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean, general mechanism to add spatial depth to tree-structured models, with an explicit discussion of depth in structure vs. depth in space. This feels like a meaningful direction beyond simply making the local composition operator more complex.
- Empirical validation under parameter control: Systematic comparison of shallow vs. deep models with matched parameter budgets; consistent gains up to a modest depth; new SOTA on SST fine-grained and binary settings.
- Practical design: Use of ReLUs, dropout (shared masks within a sentence/layer), AdaGrad, and fixed pretrained embeddings; training is stable without pretraining and only supervision at the top layer.
- Qualitative insight: Layer-wise analyses provide plausible evidence that different layers capture distinct facets (e.g., first layer lexical dominance, second layer syntactic adjacency/intensity, last layer sentiment polarity).

Weaknesses
- Scope/validation: All quantitative results are on a single dataset and task (SST sentiment). Claims about general compositionality would be stronger with evaluations on additional structured tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment, semantic relation classification) or robustness to parse noise.
- Attribution of gains: While parameter-matched depth comparisons are provided, a clearer ablation disentangling the contributions of (i) untying leaves/internals, (ii) ReLUs, (iii) dropout, (iv) pretrained embeddings, and (v) stacking, would strengthen the case that depth per se is the main driver.
- Limited depth scalability: Performance degrades beyond 2–4 layers in the controlled budget setting. This suggests optimization/expressivity limitations (narrowing layers) and raises the question of whether gating/residual connections would yield more robust depth benefits.
- Comparisons and significance: It is unclear if baselines (e.g., RNTN, DCNN) were augmented with the same engineering (pretrained embeddings, dropout). No statistical significance tests are reported.
- Analysis breadth: The qualitative analyses are insightful but anecdotal (single sentence perturbation, few neighbor examples). More systematic probing would help support the interpretability claims.
- Minor clarity points: Some initialization details are underspecified in text (e.g., missing symbol in “0.5I + ...”), and the decision to connect supervision only at the final layer could be contrasted with deep supervision variants.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate training details and regularization for non-saturating activations. The evaluation is careful within the SST setting and includes parameter controls. Some missing ablations and broader validation limit the strength of the empirical claims.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. The motivation for depth-in-space vs. depth-in-structure is clear, and figures help. A bit more precision in a few training details and expanded related work on tree models that separate input vs. hidden transformations would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderately novel. Stacking recurrent layers is known, but applying this idea to tree-structured recursive nets and articulating the benefits (including untying to enable ReLUs with large fixed embeddings) is a useful and, to my knowledge, previously unexplored combination in this context.

Significance
Likely to influence subsequent work on deep compositional architectures. The idea is simple, general, and practical, and achieves SOTA on a widely used benchmark. With stronger validation across tasks, the impact could be substantial.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations isolating the effects of untying, ReLU, dropout, and pretrained embeddings on shallow and deep variants.
- Report statistical significance and variance across runs.
- Evaluate on additional tasks (e.g., paraphrase, entailment) and/or different tree structures; analyze robustness to parser errors.
- Explore architectural enhancements to enable deeper stacks (residual/skip connections, gating as in Tree-LSTM).
- Compare against RNTN and DCNN with matched pretrained embeddings and regularization.
- Consider auxiliary supervision at intermediate layers and analyze its effect on performance and layer semantics.

Recommendation
Accept. While the idea is simple, it is a meaningful and novel direction for tree-structured models, with clear empirical gains and insightful analysis. The work opens a pathway to deeper compositional architectures and is likely to spur follow-up research.

Confidence: High.
