Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes deep recursive neural networks (DRNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recursive (tree-structured) layers, with lateral “through-space” connections V(i) between corresponding nodes across layers. Two additional design choices are (i) “untying” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer so words and phrases live in different spaces, and (ii) using rectified linear units and dropout. The model is evaluated on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) with supervision at all tree nodes and reports improvements over shallow recursive nets and previously published baselines (MV-RNN, RNTN, Paragraph Vectors, DCNN).

Conceptually, this is an extension of established recursive nets [Goller & Küchler 1996; Socher et al. 2011–2013] and stacked recurrent nets in time [El Hihi & Bengio; Hermans & Schrauwen] to the tree-structured setting. The architectural components (stacking, ReLU, dropout, AdaGrad, pre-trained word vectors) are all established; the novelty is primarily in combining them for tree-structured composition and showing empirical gains on SST.

Strengths
- Clear and generally well-written description of the architecture; equations are explicit and training details are reasonably specified (AdaGrad, dropout schedule, initialization).
- Sensible design choices (untie leaves/internals; ReLU; dropout) that align with known best practices for deep nets.
- Careful control of parameter budget when varying depth vs width; identity-biased initialization for stability is thoughtful.
- Empirical results indicate that adding depth (at fixed total parameters) yields modest but consistent gains over a shallow recursive baseline on SST. The reported best model achieves state-of-the-art on the SST fine-grained task at the time of writing.
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation; nearest neighbors across layers) are interesting and suggest different layers capture different aspects (intensity vs polarity vs syntax).

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Limited evaluation: all quantitative results are on a single dataset (SST). Claims about depth helping compositionality would be stronger with corroboration on additional tasks/datasets (e.g., other sentiment datasets without full phrase labels, entailment/semantic relatedness such as SICK, paraphrase detection, or dependency-tree variants).
- Lack of statistical rigor: results are reported from a single run with early stopping. No variance across random seeds, no confidence intervals, and no statistical significance tests. Given that the absolute gains over a strong shallow baseline appear modest (~1–1.5 points), this is important.
- Incomplete ablations: several changes are introduced simultaneously (untied leaves/internals, ReLU, dropout, pre-trained 300d vectors, identity-biased init, L2 on activations). The paper attributes a large part of the gain to dropout with one anecdotal comparison, but there is no systematic breakdown quantifying how much of the improvement comes from depth per se versus these other factors. In particular:
  - ReLU vs tanh with/without dropout, holding all else fixed.
  - Untying leaves vs tied model with the same embedding and activations.
  - Finetuning embeddings vs freezing (authors freeze; many baselines finetune).
  - Output at last layer only vs outputs at all layers.
- Fairness of baselines: the paper compares to published numbers, but the proposed model uses large 300d pre-trained word2vec vectors (frozen); prior baselines may have used different embeddings or embedding sizes and different regularization. It’s unclear whether the comparison is apples-to-apples. A reimplementation or at least controlled re-runs of key baselines with the same embeddings and regularization would strengthen claims.
- Efficiency/memory: no discussion of training/inference cost as depth increases, convergence speed, or resource usage. Practical impact depends on these factors, especially since tree models are already more expensive than sequential models.

Clarity
- While the core architecture is described, some implementation details are missing for reproducibility: exact parameter counts per configuration, full list of tuned hyperparameters and chosen values (beyond dropout), gradient clipping (used or not), stopping criterion, and whether development set decisions were made once or per seed.
- The description of higher-layer handling of leaves vs internals (Vxh(2) and Vhh(2)) could be clarified with explicit equations.
- References appear incomplete in the provided text (may be a formatting artifact), but please ensure proper citation formatting.

Originality
- The main idea—stacking recursive layers with across-layer connections—is a natural extension of known architectures. The untying, ReLU, and dropout are established modifications. Novelty is therefore incremental. This is acceptable if the empirical evidence is strong, but currently it rests on one dataset with modest gains.

Significance
- Incremental SOTA on SST is useful but limited in scope. Without broader validation and stronger evidence that depth (not other choices) is the driver, the impact is modest.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide results averaged over multiple random seeds with standard deviations? Are improvements over the best shallow model statistically significant?
2. Ablations: quantify the individual contributions of depth, untying, ReLU, dropout, initialization, and frozen vs finetuned embeddings.
3. Baseline fairness: can you re-run RNN/RNTN with the same 300d embeddings and dropout to control for embeddings/regularization?
4. How does the method perform when only sentence-level labels are available (no phrase supervision)? Does depth still help?
5. What are training times and memory usage across depths? Any gradient clipping used?
6. Have you tried connecting outputs at all layers; does it change accuracy or interpretability?

Pros
- Clear architecture; principled extension of recursive nets.
- Parameter-controlled depth vs width comparison.
- State-of-the-art numbers reported on SST (fine-grained).
- Interesting qualitative analyses.

Cons
- Single-dataset evaluation; no multiple seeds/significance.
- Missing ablations to isolate the effect of depth.
- Potentially unfair baseline comparisons due to differing embeddings/regularization.
- Incremental novelty; modest empirical gains.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is reasonable and the paper is well written, but the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough: limited to a single dataset, modest gains without significance analysis, and confounded by multiple concurrent changes and potentially unfair baseline comparisons. With thorough ablations, multiple-seed reporting, stronger baseline controls, and additional tasks, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
