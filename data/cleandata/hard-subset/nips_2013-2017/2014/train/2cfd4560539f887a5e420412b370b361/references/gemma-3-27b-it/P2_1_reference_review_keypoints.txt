- Proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs): stack multiple recursive layers to better model hierarchical compositionality in language.  
- Evaluated on Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) and achieves statistically significant state-of-the-art improvements over shallow RNNs.  
- Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest-neighbor phrases) show different layers capture different notions of similarity and compositional aspects.  
- Builds on prior RNN and compositional semantics work (Socher et al., El Hihi & Bengio, Schmidhuber) and connects depth-in-space ideas to recursive models.  
- Novelty: stacking recursive layers is a reasonable and interesting extension addressing shallow RNN limitations.  
- Strong empirical results with ablation studies showing benefits of increased depth.  
- Thorough experimentation: comparisons to strong baselines, careful tuning (dropout, LR, regularization), and attention to word vector initialization.  
- Clear writing and organization, making architecture and experiments easy to follow.  
- Limited scope: evaluation confined to SST; needs testing on other compositional tasks (QA, entailment).  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: optimal depth and architectural choices (layers, hidden size) appear sensitive and underexplored.  
- Computational cost not discussed; training expense could hinder adoption.  
- Untying leaves vs. internals lacks strong justification; rationale (rectifiers, dimensionality) feels weak and needs more evidence.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Required minor revisions: discuss computational cost, strengthen justification of "untied" configuration, and acknowledge single-dataset evaluation with future work directions.
