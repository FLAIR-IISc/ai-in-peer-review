### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Deep Recursive Neural Network (Deep RNN), a novel architecture for learning compositional representations of language. The central idea is to extend standard Recursive Neural Networks, which are "deep in structure" due to their recursive application over a parse tree, by also making them "deep in space" by stacking multiple recursive layers. In this proposed architecture, the representation of a node at a given layer `i` is computed based on the representations of its children at the same layer `i`, as well as its own representation from the layer below, `i-1`. This approach is inspired by recent work on Deep Recurrent Neural Networks that demonstrated the benefits of stacking layers for processing sequential data.

The authors also propose a modification to the base recursive layer, "untying" the weights used for composing word vectors (leaves) from those used for composing intermediate phrase vectors (internal nodes). This allows for the use of large, pre-trained word vectors with smaller hidden state sizes and facilitates the use of non-saturating activation functions like rectified linear units (ReLUs).

The Deep RNN is evaluated on the task of fine-grained sentiment classification using the Stanford Sentiment Treebank. The experiments demonstrate that Deep RNNs outperform their shallow counterparts with a similar number of parameters. The best-performing model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on this benchmark, surpassing previous recursive models such as the RNTN, as well as other strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors and a Dynamic Convolutional Neural Network. Qualitative analyses are presented to suggest that the stacked layers learn a hierarchy of representations, with lower layers capturing more lexical/syntactic features and higher layers capturing more abstract semantic (sentiment) features.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is a well-motivated and sound extension of existing recursive architectures. The experimental setup is rigorous, using a standard benchmark dataset and evaluation protocol, and comparing against a comprehensive set of relevant and strong baselines. The claims are well-supported by the empirical results presented in Table 1. For instance, the systematic comparison of models with varying depth and width clearly supports the central claim that adding depth is more beneficial than adding width for a fixed parameter budget. The authors are careful in their methodology, employing modern techniques like pre-trained word vectors, dropout, and AdaGrad, and they thoughtfully address practical issues like activation explosion by adding a small L2 penalty. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a new model, thorough quantitative evaluation, and insightful qualitative analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The core motivation—distinguishing "depth in structure" from "depth in space"—is a powerful and clear framing for the proposed model. The architecture is described precisely with mathematical formulations (Eq. 4) and is effectively illustrated in Figure 2. The distinction between the standard RNN, the untied variant, and the deep model is explained clearly with the help of Figure 1. The experimental section is detailed and provides sufficient information for an expert to understand the setup. The qualitative analyses, while based on a few examples, are presented in a way that makes the authors' interpretation clear and understandable.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the Deep Recursive Neural Network—is novel. While the idea of stacking layers is well-established in feedforward and recurrent networks, its application to recursive neural networks operating on tree structures is a new and logical contribution. This work successfully imports a successful concept from the sequential domain to the structural domain. The "untying" of leaf and internal node composition is a simple but clever modification that, as the results show, provides a substantial performance boost and is a novel contribution in its own right. The paper correctly positions its contributions with respect to prior work on recursive models [6, 8] and deep recurrent models [12].

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant. By demonstrating a clear method for improving recursive models and achieving state-of-the-art performance on a widely used benchmark, the work provides a valuable direction for future research in compositional semantics. The results strongly suggest that hierarchical representation learning is as beneficial for tree-structured data as it is for sequential or unstructured data. The qualitative analysis, which suggests that different layers capture different aspects of composition (from lexical to semantic), offers valuable insights and is likely to inspire more detailed investigations into the representations learned by deep compositional models. Researchers and practitioners working on tasks requiring compositional understanding of language are likely to build upon these ideas. A minor limitation on the model's broader significance is its reliance on pre-computed parse trees, which may not be available or optimal for all tasks.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Novelty and Soundness:** Proposes a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound architecture (Deep RNN) that logically extends recursive networks with spatial depth.
*   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a competitive and standard NLP benchmark (Stanford Sentiment Treebank), empirically validating the proposed architecture.
*   **Strong Empirical Analysis:** Provides compelling evidence that stacking layers is more effective than widening a single layer for recursive models, offering a clear architectural guideline.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a complex model and its motivation easy to understand.
*   **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The paper includes intriguing qualitative experiments that provide initial evidence for hierarchical feature learning in the model's layers.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
*   **Incremental Nature:** While novel in the context of recursive models, the core idea of stacking layers is borrowed from deep recurrent networks. Thus, the contribution could be viewed as an effective, but incremental, combination of existing ideas.
*   **Limited Qualitative Evidence:** The qualitative analyses, while insightful, are based on a very small number of examples. More extensive analysis would be needed to make definitive claims about the function of each layer.
*   **Dependency on Parse Trees:** Like other models in this family, the proposed method relies on external parsers, which limits its applicability and ties its performance to parser quality.
