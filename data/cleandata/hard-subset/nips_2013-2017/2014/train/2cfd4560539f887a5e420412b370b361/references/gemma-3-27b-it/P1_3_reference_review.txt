## Peer Review: Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a natural and logical extension of recursive neural networks (RNNs) by introducing depth in a manner analogous to deep recurrent networks. The motivation is clear, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) are convincing, achieving state-of-the-art performance. While not revolutionary, the paper represents a solid contribution to the field of NLP, particularly regarding compositional semantics. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors address the challenge of capturing compositional semantics in language. RNNs, while effective at processing structured inputs like parse trees, traditionally operate with a single layer of recursion. This paper proposes "Deep Recursive Neural Networks" (DRNNs) which stack multiple recursive layers, aiming to learn hierarchical representations where each layer captures different aspects of compositionality. This mirrors the development of deep recurrent networks (DRNNs), which stack recurrent layers for improved temporal modeling.  The paper builds directly upon previous work in RNNs [6, 7, 8], deep recurrent networks [10, 11, 12], and compositional semantics [16, 17].  The connection to deep feedforward networks is also clearly articulated.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Intuition:** The analogy to deep recurrent networks is well-explained and provides a strong rationale for the proposed architecture. The idea of layers handling different aspects of compositionality is intuitive and potentially powerful.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNNs consistently outperform shallow RNNs and achieve state-of-the-art results on the SST dataset. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Thorough Experiments:** The authors perform extensive experiments, varying depth and width while keeping the total number of parameters constant, and compare against a strong set of baselines.  The ablation studies regarding dropout and activation functions are also valuable.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The input perturbation and nearest neighbor phrase experiments provide insights into how different layers learn and represent information. These analyses are insightful and help to validate the intuition about hierarchical compositionality.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The methodology is explained in detail, and the experiments are described thoroughly.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the DRNN is clear and consistent. The use of untying leaves and internals is well justified and shown to improve performance.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are limited to the SST dataset. While SST is a valuable resource, it would be beneficial to see the DRNNs evaluated on other tasks and datasets to assess their generalizability.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal depth appears to depend on the width, and there is a trade-off between the two. More discussion of this trade-off and potentially a more systematic hyperparameter search could be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Stacking recursive layers may increase computational cost, especially for large parse trees. This aspect is not explicitly discussed.
* **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the qualitative analysis is helpful, a more detailed error analysis (e.g., examining specific examples where the model fails) could provide further insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.



**4. Detailed Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 4 (Introduction):**  Clarify the benefit of the richer supervised error signal in SST. While mentioned, explicitly state *how* this alleviates vanishing gradients.
* **Page 6 (Untying Leaves and Internals):** Mention the potential impact of this untying on the interpretability of the learned representations. Does it make it harder to understand what individual layers are learning?
* **Page 8 (Network Training):**  The justification for the fixed L2 penalty feels somewhat ad-hoc. Could the authors elaborate on why a small, fixed penalty is effective in preventing explosions?
* **Page 9 (Quantitative Evaluation):**  Consider including a table summarizing the computational cost (e.g., training time, memory usage) for different network configurations.
* **Page 11 (Input Perturbation):** The explanation of the observed trend in Figure 3 could be more detailed. Specifically, why does the second layer focus on intensity rather than direction?
* **Page 12 (Nearest Neighbor Phrases):**  Expand on the limitations of using one-norm distance. Could other distance metrics reveal different patterns?



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark.
* Presents a logically sound and well-motivated extension of existing RNN architectures.
* Provides insightful qualitative analyses that support the core ideas.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost are not fully addressed.
* More detailed error analysis could be beneficial.


**6. Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the comments outlined above.  The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics and has the potential to inspire further research in deep recursive neural networks. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.
