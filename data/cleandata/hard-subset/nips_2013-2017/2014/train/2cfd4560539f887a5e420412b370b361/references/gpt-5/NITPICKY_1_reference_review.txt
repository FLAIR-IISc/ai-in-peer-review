Summary
This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs) for compositional semantics by stacking multiple tree-structured (recursive) layers on top of each other. The model distinguishes “depth in structure” (tree depth) from “depth in space” (stacked layers) and aims to realize hierarchical abstraction across layers, analogous to stacked recurrent networks in sequence models. A further contribution is “untying” leaves and internal nodes in the first layer so words (dense vectors) can be mapped into a different space than phrases and enable the use of ReLU activations. The authors train on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank with supervision at all nodes (implied), keep pre-trained word vectors fixed, and regularize with dropout (shared across nodes per layer) plus a small L2 penalty on both weights and activations. Quantitatively, deeper-but-narrower DRNNs outperform shallow recursive nets of comparable parameter count and reportedly achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on fine-grained sentiment, surpassing RNTN, MV-RNN, Paragraph Vectors, and DCNN. Qualitative analyses (input perturbation; layer-wise nearest neighbors) suggest different layers capture different aspects of compositionality (lexical content vs syntactic patterns vs sentiment polarity/intensity).

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive architectural extension of recursive nets to stacked depth; the distinction between leaf/internal parameterization is sensible and practical with ReLUs.
- Empirical gains on SST over strong tree-based baselines (RNTN, MV-RNN) and non-tree models (DCNN, Paragraph Vectors), with consistent benefit of added depth until a point.
- Training recipe is simple (no pretraining beyond word vectors, AdaGrad, dropout) and potentially useful to practitioners.
- Interesting qualitative explorations that align with the stated intuition about layer roles.

Weaknesses and concerns

Quality
- Missing/ambiguous training details threaten reproducibility:
  - Objective: It is not explicitly stated whether the loss is summed over all nodes (phrases) or only at the root; weighting of node-level losses (root vs internal) is not specified.
  - Binary evaluation: You “decode” the 5-way posterior and discard neutral at test time; please clarify whether you use the standard SST sentence-level split and metrics used in prior work (Socher et al., 2013).
  - Initialization: “All other weights are initialized as .” is incomplete; provide the exact scheme (distribution, scale) and bias initialization (important for ReLUs).
  - Dropout: Where exactly applied (before/after composition, per-layer/per-connection), scaling at test time, and whether applied to inputs/embeddings are not stated. The “shared mask across nodes” design is interesting but not justified via ablation.
  - Optimization: No mention of gradient clipping, which is standard for deep (tree) ReLU models to prevent explosion; the paper instead adds L2 on activations (unusual) with no ablation. Please justify and compare to clipping (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2013).
- Evaluation rigor:
  - Results appear from single runs; no variance or significance tests are reported. Given sensitivity to initialization and tree structures, statistical significance should be reported.
  - Fairness: You use fixed pre-trained embeddings, while several baselines fine-tune embeddings; the converse also matters (how much DRNN improves with fine-tuning). Using only published numbers makes the comparison less controlled.
  - Parameter/control ablations are insufficient. The claim that “depth helps” is confounded by several simultaneous changes (untied leaf/internal, ReLUs, dropout, AdaGrad, fixed embeddings). Stronger ablations are needed: (i) tied vs untied leaves/internals; (ii) tanh vs ReLU; (iii) with/without dropout; (iv) equal-width vs constant-parameter regimes; (v) output attached to last layer vs all layers; (vi) effect of training with only root supervision.
- Limited analysis of computational cost: No training time, memory, or asymptotic complexity, which are relevant when stacking multiple tree layers.

Clarity
- Notation is inconsistent and sometimes confusing: x vs h, switching spaces X/H/H(i); Eq. (4) suggests a single V(i) but text says separate Vxh(2)/Vhh(2) for leaves/internals; please write the exact equations used.
- Terminology: “RNN” alternates between “recursive” and “recurrent”; disambiguate throughout (e.g., use RecNN vs RNN).
- Several grammatical issues and typos; references are poorly formatted, include misspelled author names, and omit years/venues (e.g., “All other weights are initialized as .”; “Jeffrey Pennin”; many entries missing bibliographic fields).

Originality
- Stacking recursive layers is a straightforward extension of stacking recurrent layers; the novelty is moderate. The untying of leaf/internal transformations with ReLUs is a practical tweak. The qualitative layer-behavior findings are interesting but anecdotal.
- Related work could be expanded: prior deep/stacked tree models and alternative regularization techniques for tree-structured ReLU networks; discuss multi-task/auxiliary losses attached to intermediate layers.

Significance
- Achieving SOTA on SST would be meaningful; however, the experimental confounds, missing details, and lack of significance tests weaken the strength of the claim.
- The idea is likely to be used by practitioners if clearly specified and reproducible; as written, important implementation choices are underspecified.

Requested clarifications/experiments
- Precisely define the training objective (node-level vs root-only; class weighting; loss aggregation).
- Complete initialization details and dropout specification; compare activation L2 vs gradient clipping.
- Report results over multiple random seeds with mean/std; run significance tests vs RNTN and DCNN.
- Provide ablations: (a) tied vs untied leaves; (b) tanh vs ReLU; (c) with/without dropout; (d) output connected to last vs all layers; (e) constant width vs constant parameter count.
- Evaluate with and without embedding fine-tuning for fairness and to understand headroom.
- Report computational costs.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Simple, intuitive extension of recursive nets that improves performance on a canonical benchmark.
- Practical training/regularization insights for tree-structured ReLU models.
- Qualitative analyses support the compositionality narrative.

Cons (for rejection)
- Incomplete methodological specification (initialization, objective, dropout, evaluation protocol).
- Limited and potentially confounded ablations; no variance/significance reporting.
- Modest conceptual novelty; several contributions are incremental tweaks.
- Reference formatting and numerous minor errors reduce polish and trustworthiness.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper has promising ideas and empirically strong results, but the accumulation of missing methodological details, insufficiently controlled experiments, lack of statistical rigor, and presentation issues make it hard to assess and reproduce. With clearer specifications, stronger ablations, and tightened writing, this work could be a solid contribution.
