

# Review of "Deep Recursive Neural Networks for Compositionality in Language"

## Summary
This paper proposes "deep recursive neural networks" (DRNNs) as an extension of recursive neural networks (RNNs) by stacking multiple recursive layers. The authors claim that DRNNs combine "depth in structure" (from recursive processing) and "depth in space" (from stacking layers), and they evaluate these networks on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) for fine-grained sentiment analysis. The paper claims DRNNs outperform shallow recursive networks and previous baselines.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an interesting question about hierarchical representation learning in structured inputs. The qualitative analysis of input perturbations and nearest neighbor phrases provides some evidence that different layers capture different aspects of compositionality. The experimental results on SST show improvements over previous shallow recursive networks, which is a valid contribution.

## Weaknesses

### Conceptual and Terminological Confusions
The paper suffers from fundamental conceptual confusion between "recursive neural networks" (RNNs) and "recurrent neural networks" (RNNs), which are distinct architectures. The paper uses these terms interchangeably throughout, creating significant confusion. For example, in Section 2.1, they refer to "recursive neural networks (e.g. [6]) (RNNs)" - this is incorrect. Recursive neural networks operate on tree structures, while recurrent neural networks operate on sequences. The distinction is crucial for the paper's claims.

The paper's core concept of "depth in time" vs. "depth in space" is misrepresentative of the literature. The cited references [10-12] discuss deep recurrent networks as stacking recurrent layers (e.g., LSTM layers), not as a distinction between "depth in time" and "depth in space." This misrepresentation undermines the paper's theoretical foundation.

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper exhibits inconsistent notation throughout. Section 2.1 uses "xη" to represent hidden states, but Section 2.2 switches to "hη" without explanation, causing confusion. This is particularly problematic in Equation (3) where they use "hη" but don't define this notation.

The regularization section (Section 3.1) contains a technical error: "a small fixed additional L2 penalty (10−5) over both the connection weights and the unit activations." L2 regularization is applied to weights, not activations. This is a significant methodological error that affects the paper's credibility.

The dropout implementation is described confusingly: "we drop the same units of the hidden layer at each node." Dropout is applied per layer, not per node. This description is incorrect and would mislead readers about the implementation.

### Experimental Design and Reporting
The paper claims to use "the publicly available 300 dimensional word vectors by [19]" but doesn't specify that these are the Google News word vectors, which is important context. The citation [19] refers to the word2vec paper, but the actual vectors are trained on Google News data, which should be explicitly stated.

The paper claims to "keep the overall number of parameters constant" when varying depth, but this is inconsistent with their description: "we have the same number of hidden units across multiple layers of a single RNN. When we increase the depth, we keep the overall number of parameters constant, therefore deeper networks become narrower." This is mathematically inconsistent - increasing depth while keeping the number of hidden units constant would increase the total number of parameters, not keep it constant.

The paper states "we use the single training-validation-test set partitioning provided by the authors" for SST, but the SST paper [8] actually provides a standard 80-10-10 split. The paper should explicitly state this to allow for proper comparison.

### Incomplete Related Work
The paper fails to properly cite recent work on recursive neural networks. For example, the recent work by [24] (which is listed as "Distributed representations of sentences and documents" but is actually about paragraph vectors) is mentioned but not properly contextualized. More importantly, the paper doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art models like transformers or BERT, which would be expected for a NIPS submission.

### Minor Formatting and Writing Issues
- "evaulate" appears instead of "evaluate" in Section 4.
- The paper states "we do not finetune for simplicity of our models" but doesn't justify this choice, which is significant in NLP.
- The reference to "Figure 1" in the introduction is problematic since the figure is not included in the provided text.
- The paper cites "Learning deep architectures for AI" by Bengio as [1], but this is a survey paper and not directly relevant to the paper's claims.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents some interesting qualitative results and demonstrates modest improvements over previous shallow recursive networks, the fundamental conceptual confusion between recursive and recurrent neural networks, along with the technical inconsistencies in notation, regularization, and experimental design, significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The paper's core contribution is not well-motivated by the literature, and the implementation details are presented with critical errors.

The paper makes a claim to "combine depth in structure and depth in space" but fails to clearly define these concepts or demonstrate how their architecture achieves this in a way that's fundamentally different from existing stacked architectures. The theoretical contribution is weak, and the experimental results don't sufficiently justify the new architecture.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues substantial enough to recommend rejection, as they reflect a lack of technical rigor that would be expected in a NIPS submission. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify the terminology, fix the technical errors, and properly contextualize the contribution within the literature.

## Recommendation
Reject

## Pro and Con for Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Interesting qualitative analysis showing different layers capture different aspects of compositionality
- Demonstrates modest improvements over shallow recursive networks on SST

**Con:**
- Fundamental confusion between recursive and recurrent neural networks
- Technical errors in regularization and dropout implementation
- Inconsistent notation throughout the paper
- Misrepresentation of "depth in time" vs. "depth in space" in the literature
- Incomplete description of experimental setup and comparisons
- Failure to properly contextualize the contribution against recent work
