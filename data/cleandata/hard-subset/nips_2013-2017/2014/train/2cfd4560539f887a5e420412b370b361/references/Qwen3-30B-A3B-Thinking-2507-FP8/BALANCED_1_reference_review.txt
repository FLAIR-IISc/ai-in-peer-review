

This paper proposes "deep recursive neural networks" (DRNNs), which stack multiple recursive layers to combine "depth in structure" (from recursive processing of tree structures) with "depth in space" (from stacked layers). The authors argue that this allows each layer to handle distinct aspects of linguistic compositionality, as opposed to shallow recursive networks that handle all composition in a single layer. They evaluate DRNNs on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance over shallow recursive nets and prior baselines (e.g., RNTN, Paragraph Vectors). Qualitative analyses (input perturbation, nearest neighbors) support their claim that layers capture different compositional aspects.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novel architecture:** The distinction between depth in structure (tree depth) and depth in space (stacked layers) is well-motivated and addresses a gap in recursive networks. The DRNN design (Equation 4) elegantly integrates recursive and deep learning concepts.  
2. **Strong empirical results:** DRNNs consistently outperform shallow counterparts (Table 1a) and previous work (Table 1b), achieving new SOTA on SST fine-grained (49.5% vs. prior 47.4%) and binary sentiment tasks. The ablation study (3.2) convincingly shows depth improves performance over width when parameters are fixed.  
3. **Insightful analysis:** The input perturbation (Figure 3) and nearest-neighbor experiments (Table 2) provide compelling evidence that layers specialize: Layer 1 captures word-level similarity, Layer 2 handles intensity/syntax, and Layer 3 focuses on sentiment. This advances understanding of compositional processing in neural networks.  
4. **Technical rigor:** The methodology (Section 2.2–2.3) clearly justifies key design choices (untied leaves/internals, rectifiers, dropout), and experiments are well-executed with appropriate baselines and hyperparameter tuning.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited scope:** The work is evaluated *only* on SST sentiment analysis. While SST is a standard benchmark, the lack of evaluation on other structured NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, machine translation) or unsupervised compositionality (as noted in the conclusion) weakens claims about generalizability. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a significant limitation for a NIPS submission.  
2. **Depth vs. width trade-off:** The paper keeps total parameters constant when varying depth/width, but does not rigorously isolate whether gains stem from depth or simply a reparameterization (e.g., a wider shallow network might match depth gains). An ablation comparing depth vs. width *with identical parameter counts* would strengthen the argument.  
3. **Binary classification oversight:** Using the fine-grained model for binary classification (Section 3.2) is suboptimal; a dedicated binary model could yield higher results (e.g., 88.9% vs. 87.4% in Table 1b). This minor oversight weakens the binary results.  
4. **Comparison gaps:** No comparison to recent deep tree-structured models (e.g., tree-LSTMs, Tai et al. 2015) or other deep recursive variants (e.g., dynamic tree RNNs, Chen et al. 2015). While RNTN and Paragraph Vectors are included, these are less directly comparable to DRNNs.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a technically sound, original contribution to recursive neural networks with clear empirical validation and insightful analysis. The core idea—stacking recursive layers to enable hierarchical compositional processing—is novel and well-justified, and the results on SST are compelling. However, the narrow scope (one dataset, one task) limits the perceived significance for NIPS, where broader impact is expected. The weaknesses are not fatal but reduce the paper’s potential to influence wider NLP research.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (borderline for NIPS). The work is strong in quality and originality but lacks sufficient breadth to justify acceptance over more generalizable contributions. To improve:  
- Add experiments on a second task (e.g., dependency parsing or paraphrase detection) to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Include a deeper analysis of depth vs. width trade-offs (e.g., fixed width, varying depth; or fixed depth, varying width).  
- Report binary results from a dedicated binary classifier.  
- Compare against tree-LSTMs or similar deep tree-structured models.  

The paper is well-written and technically rigorous, and with these additions, it would be a strong contribution. As-is, it is a solid but incremental advance on a specific task.
