This paper proposes Deep Recursive Neural Networks (DRNNs), extending recursive neural networks by stacking multiple recursive layers. The authors motivate this by drawing parallels to the distinction between depth in time (RNNs) and depth in space (deep feedforward networks) and apply the resulting architecture to fine-grained sentiment analysis on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST). While the core idea of combining structural and spatial depth is conceptually sound, the presentation and execution reveal numerous deficiencies that, despite achieving state-of-the-art results, warrant a rejection.

The introduction, while establishing context, is overly verbose and repetitive. The analogies to recurrent networks, while intended to be illustrative, feel strained and detract from the novelty of the proposed architecture. The statement regarding the “hierarchy” resulting from deep recurrent networks [10, 11, 12] feels unsupported and used primarily as justification for the DRNN’s design. The concluding sentence regarding each layer handling “different aspects of compositionality” is a strong claim that isn’t rigorously substantiated within the paper itself – it reads as a hopeful observation rather than a demonstrated fact.

The methodology section suffers from several issues. While Equation (1) is standard for RNNs, the subsequent discussion about the weight matrices being square is trivial and adds little value. The “untied” variant (Section 2.2) is a reasonable modification, but the justification – preventing handling of both sparse and dense cases – is weak. Separating spaces avoids the issue, but the authors don’t demonstrate this is actually a problem with the tied variant. The claim of enabling large pretrained word vectors is also misleading; the dimensionality of *Wxh* and *Whh* is still dependent on the hidden layer size, not the word vector dimensionality. More importantly, the discussion lacks mathematical rigor; the connection to Elman-type RNNs is stated but not clearly explained. 

Equation (4), defining the DRNN, is presented without sufficient explanation. The role of *V(i)*, connecting layers, is glossed over. The authors claim untying is only necessary for the first layer, which is not logically explained. Why can’t the leaf/internal distinction provide benefits in subsequent layers? The reasoning feels ad-hoc.  The explanation of connecting the output layer only to the final hidden layer, while attempting to provide a rationale for interpretability, feels contrived. A more detailed ablation study exploring different connectivity options would have been beneficial.

The experimental setup is acceptable, but suffers from minor inconsistencies. The activation function choice is justified post-hoc (“experimentally rectifier gives better performance”), rather than with a principled explanation. The use of dropout and L2 regularization is standard, but the description of the L2 penalty as resolving “explosion problems” is concerning. This suggests instability, and the authors should have investigated the cause more thoroughly instead of applying a fix. The training details (learning rate, optimizer, batch size) are adequate, but the initialization strategy is oddly specific ("0.5I + small noise"), and the justification for this is missing. The choice to keep word vectors fixed is reasonable, but not explicitly motivated.

The results, while demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, are presented with insufficient analysis. Table 1a shows improvements with depth, but the degradation beyond a certain point is dismissed with a vague explanation about width.  The input perturbation experiment (Figure 3) is poorly explained and difficult to interpret. The one-norm distance is an arbitrary metric, and the qualitative observations (“coolest” and “worst” converging) lack statistical significance.  The nearest neighbor analysis (Table 2) is more insightful, but the conclusions about layer-specific representations are again presented as observations rather than rigorously demonstrated findings. The analysis lacks quantitative support.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on SST.
*   The idea of combining structural and spatial depth is conceptually interesting.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor methodological and presentation flaws.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for design choices.
*   Weak theoretical underpinnings.
*   Qualitative analysis is superficial and lacks statistical support.
*   Overly verbose and repetitive writing.
*   The paper feels more like an engineering effort than a significant theoretical contribution.



**Overall:** Despite achieving impressive empirical results, the numerous shortcomings in methodology, analysis, and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper lacks the rigor and clarity expected of a NIPS publication. The authors should address the concerns raised above, providing more detailed justifications, rigorous analysis, and a more concise and well-structured presentation.  I recommend **rejection**.
