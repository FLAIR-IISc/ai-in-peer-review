The authors propose a deep convolutional neural network (dasNet) incorporating top-down processing via an attention-based feedback mechanism. The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

This paper is generally interesting and demonstrates novelty in both model design and its application to the vision problem. It is forward-looking to consider both bottom-up and top-down signals for CNN training. I particularly appreciate the aspect of evaluating the model over time and using gated signals to constrain the representation. Given that the quality of learned representations depends heavily on the attention policy, it would be valuable to understand potential failure cases or limitations of the algorithm – exploring scenarios where it might struggle could strengthen the work.

The experiments are well-presented, with visualizations aiding in understanding the model's behavior. However, given the increased complexity compared to traditional feedforward CNNs, a key area for improvement would be demonstrating performance on a larger-scale dataset like ImageNet (ILSVRC). This would convincingly demonstrate the scalability of the approach to larger images and more complex scenes and address concerns about computational cost. This remains my primary concern. The combination of attention-based feedback with a CNN creates an interesting model worthy of further investigation. Practical insights gained from experiments on larger datasets would be particularly beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating top-down feedback into CNNs.
*   State-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
*   Clear explanation of the method and insightful visualizations.
*   Addresses an important question of how to incorporate recurrent processing in deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of evaluation on a large-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet) limits assessment of scalability.
*   Limited discussion of potential failure modes or limitations.
