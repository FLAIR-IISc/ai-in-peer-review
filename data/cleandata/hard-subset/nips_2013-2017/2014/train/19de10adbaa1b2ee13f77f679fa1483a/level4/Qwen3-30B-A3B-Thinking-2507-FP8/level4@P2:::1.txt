

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that integrates feedback connections into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to enable dynamic, internally focused attention during inference. DasNet extends standard feedforward CNNs by introducing a reinforcement learning (RL)-learned policy that modulates filter activations across multiple passes through the network. The policy, evolved via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle the million-dimensional parameter space, selectively enhances or suppresses feature maps to correct misclassifications on difficult examples—without altering the pre-trained CNN weights. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving a 6% relative improvement over baseline CNNs by leveraging iterative attention refinement. This work directly addresses the biological inspiration from visual cortex architecture (Felleman & Essen, 1991), where top-down feedback modulates feature processing, and advances prior computer vision approaches that used RL for simplified tasks (e.g., saccade learning in Bar et al., 2002; Schmidhuber, 1991).

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical execution is robust. The SNES-based policy evolution is well-motivated for high-dimensional control (vs. standard RL), with clear ablation studies (Fig. 3) showing how filter emphasis changes across layers to correct misclassifications (e.g., disambiguating "cat" from "dog"). The experimental design rigorously isolates the attention mechanism’s impact: dasNet improves accuracy on *ambiguous* cases without degrading performance on easy examples (Table 1). The regularization and boosting terms (Eqs. 8–11) are appropriately tuned, and the computational analysis (4-day training on GTX 560 Ti) establishes feasibility.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized. Section 4 clearly formalizes the dasNet mechanism (Eq. 5), while Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 provide intuitive walkthroughs of the iterative attention process. Key results (e.g., layer-specific filter dynamics in Fig. 3) are visually and textually well-explained. The writing avoids excessive jargon, making the core innovation accessible.  
*Originality:* The paper makes a significant contribution by applying SNES to learn *filter-level attention policies* in pre-trained CNNs—a novel combination of evolutionary RL and attention mechanisms. While feedback connections (Felleman & Essen, 1991) and RL for vision (Schmidhuber, 1991) are established, dasNet’s *implementation* (using SNES for scalability) and *focus on correcting misclassifications* (not just learning attention from scratch) are distinct from prior work (e.g., Ciresan et al., 2011; Bar et al., 2002). The approach directly addresses a key gap: how to make CNNs *adaptive* post-training.  
*Significance:* This work demonstrates that feedback-driven attention is not merely biologically plausible but *practically effective* for state-of-the-art vision tasks. The SOTA results on CIFAR-10/100 (Table 1) validate the approach’s utility, suggesting a promising path for improving robustness in real-world systems. The method’s generality—applicable to any pre-trained CNN—makes it broadly relevant for practitioners seeking to enhance existing models.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation is scope: experiments are confined to CIFAR-10/100, which are relatively small datasets. While the authors acknowledge this (Section 6), the significance would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating scalability to ImageNet or more complex tasks (e.g., object detection). The paper notes that "future work will consider more complex actions," but this gap should be addressed explicitly in the revision.  
Additionally, the claim that "no part is particularly novel" (in the draft) is inaccurate. The SNES-based policy for *filter modulation* in a pre-trained CNN is original; the related work (Section 5) adequately contextualizes this. The paper could better emphasize *why* SNES was chosen over standard RL (e.g., computational efficiency for high-dimensional policies), though the SNES advantages are implied in Section 3.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a compelling contribution to the intersection of attention mechanisms and deep learning. The core idea—using RL to dynamically refine CNN attention post-training—is both novel and impactful, with strong empirical validation. The clarity of presentation and technical soundness meet NIPS standards. While expanding to ImageNet would be ideal, the current results on CIFAR-10/100 are sufficient for acceptance as a significant step toward more adaptive vision systems. I recommend *accept* with the minor suggestion that authors address scalability in the revision (e.g., "Future work will evaluate dasNet on ImageNet, where the scalability of SNES will be critical"). The paper’s strengths in quality, clarity, originality, and significance outweigh its current scope limitations.
