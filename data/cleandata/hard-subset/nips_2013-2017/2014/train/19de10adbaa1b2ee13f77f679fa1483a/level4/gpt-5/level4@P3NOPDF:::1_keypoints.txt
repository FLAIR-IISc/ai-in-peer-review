- Proposal: convnet with learned top-down feedback that gates filter activations to implement attention, trained with reinforcement learning.
- Integration: components (convnets, gating, RL) are not novel individually, but their integration is compelling and empirically effective.
- Empirical claim: strong, reportedly state-of-the-art results on CIFAR demonstrating learned feedback improves feedforward convnets.
- Motivation strength: principled and timely direction to incorporate feedback for attention in vision models.
- Method strength: gating filter activations is simple, interpretable, and an effective top-down modulation mechanism.
- Clarity: paper is generally well written; approach and training scheme are understandable.
- Novelty concern: contribution is mainly integration and empirical validation rather than new fundamental components.
- Evaluation scope: experiments are limited to CIFAR; lack of larger-scale or more challenging benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) weakens generality claims.
- Baselines/ablations missing: need capacity-matched feedforward comparisons and alternatives (soft/spatial gates, non-RL gating); require ablations (no-feedback, random/supervised gates, gate placement).
- Training stability: RL can add variance and instability; paper should report training cost, convergence, hyperparameter sensitivity, and run-to-run variance.
- Computational overhead: feedback steps likely add latency/compute; need test-time cost vs. accuracy analysis.
- Clarification needed on policy design: what are the action/state spaces, parameterization, and whether gates are continuous or discrete with corresponding credit assignment.
- Clarification on feedback steps: how many steps during training vs. inference and performance sensitivity to step count.
- Gate constraints and regularization: whether gates are constrained (nonnegativity, sparsity) or regularized to encourage parsimony/stability.
- RL training details: what variance-reduction or exploration strategies are used and how sensitive results are to these choices.
- Baseline matching: how baseline convnets are matched in depth/width and training budget to ensure fair comparison.
- Suggested experiments: test on larger datasets, provide comprehensive ablations and alternative attention comparisons, and include cost analyses (FLOPs, time, memory).
- Suggested analyses: visualize learned attention/gates, show qualitative cases and failures, run robustness tests (occlusion, noise, distribution shift), and discuss extensions/limitations.
- Significance: meaningful contribution by empirically showing feedback-driven attention can yield tangible gains despite incremental novelty.
- Overall recommendation: solid and timely work with acceptance recommended contingent on clarifications and, if possible, larger-scale experiments or more extensive ablations.
