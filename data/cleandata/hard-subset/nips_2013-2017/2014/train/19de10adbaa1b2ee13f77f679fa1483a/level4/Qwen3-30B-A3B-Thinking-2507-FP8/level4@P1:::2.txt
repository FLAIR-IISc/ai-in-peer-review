

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable internal selective attention during classification. dasNet dynamically adjusts convolutional filter sensitivities across multiple passes through the network via a policy learned through Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), rather than fixed feedforward processing. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs), showing that iterative attention refinement corrects misclassifications by emphasizing/de-emphasizing specific filters. The work aligns with neuroscience insights on top-down processing and addresses a key limitation of current CNNs: their inability to adaptively reconfigure internal representations after training.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling technical contribution by demonstrating that even simple feedback mechanisms can significantly boost performance on challenging classification tasks. The SOTA results on standard benchmarks are robust and well-validated. The exposition is generally clear, with well-structured sections explaining the Maxout backbone, SNES-based policy learning, and dasNet’s training dynamics. The method’s feasibility is convincingly shown through practical implementation details (e.g., T=5 steps, 4-day training time on GTX 560 Ti).

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of interpretability:** The core innovation—dynamic filter weighting—remains a "black box." While the paper shows *that* attention improves accuracy, it fails to explain *why* or *how* specific filter adjustments correct errors. The analysis of the cat/dog example (Fig. 3) is superficial: it shows activation changes but does not link them to the classification outcome. For instance, it is unclear why layer 1 activations universally increase in the first iteration, or whether this scaling affects the loss (Eq. 8), as the objective is invariant to global filter scaling. A deeper analysis of *misclassified examples* where dasNet succeeds (vs. standard CNNs) would better demonstrate the mechanism’s value.  
2. **Misalignment with reinforcement learning:** The method is framed as RL, but the setup diverges significantly from standard RL. Rewards are immediate (no temporal discounting), policies are deterministic, and the "environment" is static (fixed image). This resembles recurrent network training more than RL. The authors should clarify this and consider a dynamical systems perspective (e.g., characterizing the policy as a state-dependent dynamical system), which would better contextualize the work within prior recurrent architectures (e.g., [48, 49, 50]).  
3. **Technical ambiguities:**  
   - Algorithm 1: The observation function `h(M)` is undefined. What exactly is concatenated (e.g., layer-wise activation statistics)?  
   - Eq. 11: The summation index is unclear. Is `Li` computed per image (requiring indexing over `j`)?  
   - Theta regularization: The necessity of `λ_L2` is unexplained, as SNES’s Gaussian prior already encourages small weights.  
   - The final optimization step (updating `µ` and `Σ`) is described without theoretical justification—how are natural gradients computed from sampled fitness values?  
4. **Limited analysis of learned parameters:** The paper reports mean/covariance of the policy distribution but does not interpret what these values encode (e.g., do high-variance weights correspond to filters critical for ambiguous cases?). Constraining these parameters (e.g., sparsity) could reveal meaningful structure.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**  
- **Prioritize interpretability:** Analyze *specific misclassified examples* where dasNet succeeds. Visualize gating patterns (e.g., "which filters are suppressed for 'cat' vs. 'dog'") and correlate them with class boundaries.  
- **Reframe the RL connection:** Explicitly state that this is a *non-standard* RL application and discuss how the method relates to recurrent networks or dynamical systems.  
- **Clarify technical details:** Define `h(M)`, resolve indexing in Eq. 8/11, and justify theta regularization.  
- **Simplify the reward:** The `λ_boost` mechanism (Eq. 10) is ad hoc; a standard cross-entropy loss might suffice.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
dasNet is a valuable step toward more adaptive deep networks, with strong empirical results that advance the state-of-the-art. However, its interpretability gap and misaligned RL framing limit its scientific contribution. While the method is practical and effective, the paper does not sufficiently explain *how* or *why* the attention mechanism works—critical for broader adoption in vision research. The clarity of exposition and technical execution are strong, but the lack of mechanistic insight prevents it from being a transformative contribution.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* SOTA results on CIFAR; novel application of SNES to policy learning; clear implementation details; addresses a meaningful limitation of feedforward CNNs.  
*Con:* Poor interpretability; RL framing misaligned with methodology; unresolved technical ambiguities; superficial analysis of attention dynamics.  

The work is technically sound and significant for its empirical impact but lacks the theoretical depth or mechanistic insight to fully justify its novelty. It is a strong candidate for acceptance *only* if the authors address the interpretability and framing gaps in revision. As submitted, it leans toward borderline acceptance due to the compelling results but falls short of the "unique theoretical or pragmatic approach" criterion.
