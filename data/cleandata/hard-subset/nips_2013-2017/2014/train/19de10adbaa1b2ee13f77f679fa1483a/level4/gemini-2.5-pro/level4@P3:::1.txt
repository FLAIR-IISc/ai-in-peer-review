### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is to move beyond the static, one-pass nature of traditional CNNs by allowing the network to iteratively refine its classification of an image over multiple passes. This is achieved by introducing modulatory gates that can enhance or suppress the activations of convolutional filters.

The training process is a two-stage hybrid approach. First, a base CNN model (a Maxout network in this case) is trained using standard supervised learning. Subsequently, a control policy, implemented as a neural network, is trained using reinforcement learning to operate the modulatory gates. This policy takes the internal state of the CNN as input (e.g., average map activations, class probabilities) and outputs a set of weights for the filters for the next pass. Due to the high dimensionality of the action space (over 1 million parameters for the policy), the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for policy search. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, particularly by correcting initial misclassifications on difficult examples.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This is a well-executed and interesting paper that addresses a fundamental limitation of feedforward vision models. The approach is well-motivated by the recurrent and modulatory nature of the human visual cortex.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper's primary strength is its contribution to a more dynamic and biologically plausible model of vision. By demonstrating that an iterative, attention-driven refinement process can improve upon a strong feedforward baseline, the work provides compelling evidence for a promising research direction. The state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 are significant and validate that feedback and attention are not just theoretically appealing but practically effective.

2.  **Originality:** While the constituent components—CNNs, feedback connections, and reinforcement learning—are not new in isolation, their synthesis in this context is novel and clever. Using a black-box optimizer like SNES to train a high-dimensional policy that directly modulates the internal feature maps of a modern deep network is an original and effective approach to a challenging problem.

3.  **Quality:** The technical execution is sound. The methodology is clearly described, and the choice of SNES for the high-dimensional parameter space is well-justified. The experiments are conducted on standard benchmarks, and the results are convincingly presented. The authors include useful analyses, such as visualizing the changes in filter activations and verifying that the learned gate values contain significant class-relevant information, which strengthens their claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the architecture is explained with sufficient detail, and Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the training procedure. The connection to related work in both neuroscience and computer vision is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** A key question is the scalability of this approach. The experiments are confined to the relatively small CIFAR datasets. Training the attentional policy took four days on a GPU, on top of the initial training time for the base network. It is unclear how this method would scale to larger and higher-resolution datasets like ImageNet, where the number of filters, and thus the dimensionality of the policy's action space, would increase dramatically. A discussion of the computational challenges and potential avenues for scaling would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study. For example:
    *   How was the observation vector `o_t` (constructed by `h(·)`) chosen? How sensitive is performance to the inclusion or exclusion of its components (average activations, intermediate activations, class probabilities)?
    *   The dynamics analysis for different values of `T` is interesting, but it would be valuable to see a direct comparison of models trained with `T=1, 2, 3...` to understand the trade-off between performance gain and computational overhead.
    *   How crucial is the boosting scheme (`λ_boost`) to the final performance?

3.  **Interpretability:** The authors acknowledge that visualizing the function of high-level filters is an open problem. While their visualization of activation changes (Figure 3) is insightful, the mechanism remains opaque. It is difficult to develop a semantic understanding of *why* the policy chooses to emphasize certain filters for a given ambiguous image (e.g., distinguishing a cat from a dog). While this is a very hard problem, any further insight would be valuable.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective method for incorporating feedback and attention into deep networks. The combination of a strong baseline CNN with an RL-trained policy for iterative refinement is a compelling and well-motivated idea. The state-of-the-art results on established benchmarks provide solid empirical support for the approach. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope of the experiments and avenues for future exploration, rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method. The paper makes a significant contribution and is likely to inspire future work on dynamic and recurrent vision models. I recommend acceptance.

**Suggestion for Authors:**
If possible, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the potential scalability of dasNet to larger-scale problems like ImageNet, even if only to outline the expected challenges and potential solutions (e.g., parameter sharing in the policy, attention over spatial regions instead of entire feature maps).
