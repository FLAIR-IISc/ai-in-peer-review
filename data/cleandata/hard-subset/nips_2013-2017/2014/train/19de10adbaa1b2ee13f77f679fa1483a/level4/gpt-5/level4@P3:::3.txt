Summary and contributions
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture with learned feedback that modulates internal feature maps across multiple evaluation passes. Starting from a trained Maxout CNN, the authors add multiplicative gates on each feature map in each layer. At test time, the network processes an input for T sequential passes. After each pass, an observation vector is constructed (comprising average feature-map activations per layer, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities). A learned policy maps this observation to the next set of gates, thereby selectively amplifying or suppressing filters on the next pass. The policy has close to a million parameters and is trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimizer using a diagonal Gaussian search distribution. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (unaugmented), dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and reports state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data. The paper also provides visualizations, analyses of the dynamics across passes, and evidence that the learned gates carry class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work
The work is motivated by extensive neuroscience evidence for top-down and recurrent processing in vision, and it relates to earlier machine learning ideas on recurrent and attention-based perception, reinforcement learning for selective sensing, and feedback in neural nets. Within the NIPS community, the paper builds on feedforward CNN advances (e.g., Maxout, dropout) and adds a learned, global channel-wise attention mechanism trained using evolutionary strategies rather than gradient-based RL. Compared to previous selective attention or foveation models, dasNet is applied to modern CNNs and evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, aiming at improving state-of-the-art performance rather than only demonstrating proof-of-concept.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural modification (multiplicative gating of feature maps) is simple and well-defined. The training protocol—pretrain the base CNN with supervision, then learn the attention policy via SNES using a loss that upweights misclassified samples—is clearly described. Using a scaled softmax to keep gates positive and mean-one is a sensible stability constraint.
- Empirical support: On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), dasNet yields consistent improvements over the baseline Maxout CNN. Visualizations illustrate how gates evolve across passes and how decisions can flip on ambiguous images (e.g., dog vs. cat). The “dynamics” experiment shows performance peaking near the trained number of passes and remaining stable with additional passes.
- Missing comparisons/ablations: Several important baselines are absent, such as: (1) a multi-pass feedforward model without feedback (e.g., re-run the same network T times and average logits); (2) supervised end-to-end training of the gates via backpropagation through time; (3) restricting gating to the last convolutional layer; (4) other black-box optimizers or (stochastic) policy gradient methods. The paper would be stronger with per-class error analysis to show where improvements concentrate, and with quantitative reporting of how many test examples change decision due to feedback.
- Scalability/cost: Policy learning required days on a single GPU with population-based SNES. The approach multiplies inference cost by T. While the authors motivate SNES by the policy dimensionality, additional evidence on sample efficiency, number of generations, and wall-clock cost compared to gradient-based alternatives would help assess practicality.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The algorithm is specified, and the role of observations/actions is explained. A few points that could improve clarity:
- Explicitly state the dimensions of the observation and action vectors for the reported model, and per-layer gating granularity.
- Provide the number of SNES generations and convergence criteria.
- Clarify whether the policy network is linear or has nonlinear hidden layers (equation suggests a linear map plus softmax).
- Summarize computational overhead at inference (e.g., T=5 multiplies runtime by 5).

Originality
The idea of learned, global channel-wise attention applied recurrently within a CNN, trained via a black-box evolutionary optimizer, is novel in the context of mainstream CNNs used for vision benchmarks at the time. The specific combination of (i) policy-driven internal gating, (ii) recurrent multi-pass inference over the same image, and (iii) SNES for million-parameter policies is distinctive. The paper also provides new empirical evidence that such feedback can correct ambiguous cases beyond a strong feedforward baseline.

Significance
The work pushes beyond purely feedforward CNNs and shows that learned internal attention can improve accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented). This is a timely and relevant direction for NIPS. However, the scope is limited to small 32×32 images; there are no results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ILSVRC). Demonstrating scalability to higher-resolution images and larger class sets would strengthen the claim of general significance. The approach also introduces nontrivial training and inference overhead, and the reliance on black-box optimization may impede adoption unless justified by clear advantages over gradient-based training.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architectural contribution that operationalizes top-down attention in CNNs.
- Empirical gains over a strong baseline, with thoughtful visualizations and dynamics analyses.
- Sensible design choices to stabilize gating and focus training on hard examples.
- Evidence that gate activations themselves contain discriminative information.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited evaluation scope: add experiments on larger datasets or at least higher-resolution benchmarks to demonstrate scalability.
- Baselines and ablations: compare to multi-pass averaging, end-to-end BPTT with gated layers, and gating limited to specific layers; report per-class improvements and number of corrected decisions.
- Policy learning choices: justify SNES versus policy gradients or deterministic policy optimization; report sample efficiency, generations, and compute costs; discuss overfitting risks from evolving on the training set.
- Action/observation design: current gates are global per feature map and lose spatial specificity; discuss or test spatial attention variants. Similarly, the observation uses average activations; consider richer summaries and analyze sensitivity to these design choices.
- Practicality: quantify inference-time cost versus accuracy gains, and discuss deployment trade-offs.

Reasons to accept
- Novel, well-motivated integration of feedback attention into CNNs with demonstrated gains.
- Clear formulation and interesting analyses that will stimulate follow-up work on recurrent/attention mechanisms in deep vision models.
- Empirical results that, even on CIFAR, improve over strong state-of-the-art feedforward baselines for unaugmented data.

Reasons to reject
- Evaluation limited to small datasets; no evidence of scalability to larger images/classes.
- Missing key baselines and ablations to isolate where the gains come from and whether simpler methods suffice.
- Reliance on compute-intensive black-box optimization without a thorough comparison to gradient-based alternatives.

Overall, this is a solid and forward-looking paper that meaningfully advances the conversation on feedback and attention in deep vision. With stronger baselines and broader evaluation, it would be an easy accept; as is, it is still a valuable contribution.
