This paper presents an innovative approach to incorporating selective attention into a convolutional neural network, enabling the optimization of classification performance through the training of gating parameters for attention. By tackling the critical challenge of adaptively reconfiguring complex, deep processing stages under limited resources and diverse task requirements, this work addresses a significant problem in computer vision. The classification results are indeed impressive; however, the underlying mechanisms remain obscure, offering valuable directions for future research without providing insight into the method's effectiveness. The analysis of image representation falls short of being revelatory, and the role of the learned parameters (mean and variance of the theta distribution) is not clearly elucidated. Nevertheless, this work represents a significant step forward from the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with fixed parameters post-learning. The exposition is clear, although some equations require correction or further clarification.

Major Comments:
I recommend that the authors explore the connection to dynamical systems in greater depth. The work appears to be more closely related to recurrent networks than reinforcement learning, given the immediate observation of rewards and the deterministic policy. Training involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.

I found the analysis of the network processing the cat image to be less instructive in understanding the benefits of the new model features for recognition. It would be beneficial to investigate why layer 1 activations increase at the first iteration and whether this affects the objective function or if the output is invariant to global scalings. Focusing on a smaller region of the image and analyzing the evolution of the gating variables in that region could provide valuable insights. Additionally, examining challenging cases, such as those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model, or teasing out the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by constraining them further, would be an interesting direction to pursue.

Minor Comments:
In Algorithm 1, the definition of h_M is unclear. Is this the step where observations are collected? Furthermore, F[i] and \Theta[i] are inside the loop over images (j); are the image fitness values collected in an array or overwritten on each iteration?

Equation 11 lacks clarity regarding the sum. Is Equation 8 specific to a single image and should be indexed j? The text following Equation 11 is unclear, as the variables 'x' and 'd' are not defined.

The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, given that theta is already sampled from the prior and tends towards small values. The final step of the optimization (gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas) is not well-explained (how are the gradients computed) or theoretically justified (are hyper-parameters of the posterior over policies being adjusted).

This approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition is intriguing. The high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation aids in extracting or focusing on more complex image features, but the lack of transparency in the method's workings hinders understanding of its effectiveness.
