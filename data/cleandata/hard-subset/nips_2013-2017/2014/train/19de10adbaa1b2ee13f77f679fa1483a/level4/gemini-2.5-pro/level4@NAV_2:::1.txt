This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. These connections allow the network to dynamically modulate the activity of its convolutional filters over multiple sequential passes for a single input image. This process is framed as an internal selective attention mechanism, where a control policy decides which features to enhance or suppress to improve classification accuracy. The base network, a Maxout CNN, is trained conventionally, after which the high-dimensional attention policy is trained using reinforcement learning, specifically with Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to overcome the challenges of the large parameter space. This work is situated within a broader effort to move beyond purely feedforward vision models, drawing inspiration from the recurrent and top-down processing observed in the primate visual cortex.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a key limitation of standard CNNs: their static, single-pass nature. By incorporating a dynamic, iterative refinement mechanism, the work explores a compelling and biologically-plausible direction for improving model robustness. The success of this approach provides strong evidence that integrating feedback and attention mechanisms is a fruitful avenue for future research in deep learning for vision.
*   **Performance:** The method demonstrates a clear empirical benefit, achieving state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (without data augmentation). This is a significant result that validates the utility of the proposed attention mechanism in improving classification on challenging benchmarks.
*   **Approach:** The combination of a pre-trained, high-performing CNN with a reinforcement learning-based policy for attention is a novel and well-executed synthesis of established techniques. The choice of SNES to train a policy with over a million parameters is a practical solution to a difficult large-scale RL problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and lucidly explaining the proposed architecture and training procedure. The connection to concepts from neuroscience is well-argued and provides a strong foundation for the work.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Computational Cost:** The iterative processing and the separate RL training phase introduce significant computational overhead. While justified by the performance gains on CIFAR, it remains an open question how this approach would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet, where both the training time and the complexity of the policy search space would increase substantially.
*   **Limited Interpretability:** While the paper includes an interesting qualitative analysis of one classification example, the work could be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention policies. For instance, a quantitative study of which layers or filter types are most frequently modulated for certain classes could provide deeper insights into the mechanism's operation.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a well-motivated and effective approach for incorporating feedback-driven attention into deep networks. The strong empirical results confirm that this is a promising research direction. A valuable next step would be to investigate the scalability of this method to larger and more complex vision problems.
