- Paper introduces dasNet: a CNN architecture with learned feedback connections that iteratively modulate lower-layer filter activations for selective attention.  
- Policy for attention modulation is learned with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle a million-parameter policy space.  
- Approach is neuroscience-motivated (bidirectional cortical connections) and builds on prior RL/top-down vision work.  
- dasNet achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100, improving prior CNNs by ~6% on un-augmented data.  
- Visualizations show attention adjustments can correct initial misclassifications (e.g., cat vs. dog).  
- Technical execution is rigorous: SNES effectively scales, and experiments include sensible ablations and dynamics analysis.  
- The method improves classification without retraining the base CNN, offering a pragmatic post-training refinement.  
- Clarity is high: algorithm and ablation figures clearly illustrate attention evolution and impact.  
- Originality arises from the principled integration of feedback, RL control, and iterative post-training refinement rather than any single component.  
- Scope is limited to CIFAR-10/100, raising questions about generalization to larger-scale datasets like ImageNet.  
- Comparison to related work is incomplete—lacks contrasts with recurrent CNNs and recent attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation).  
- Interpretability is limited: paper provides visual dynamics but not quantitative analysis of why specific filters are emphasized.  
- Recommendation: strong accept—method is significant, well-executed, and advances feedback-driven attention in CNNs despite scope/comparison gaps.  
- Suggested revisions: explicitly compare to modern attention modules (e.g., SE blocks), include or discuss ImageNet scalability, and analyze semantic relevance of emphasized filters.
