

This paper introduces selective attention into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by developing a method to train gating parameters that optimize classification performance. It tackles a critical challenge in computer vision: adaptively reconfiguring deep processing stages under resource constraints for diverse tasks. While the classification results are compelling, the approach remains largely a black box—offering valuable directions but failing to elucidate *why* it works. The analysis of learned image representations is superficial, and the roles of key parameters (mean/variance of the theta distribution) remain unclear. Despite this, the work represents a meaningful advancement beyond fixed-parameter, feedforward networks. The exposition is generally clear, though some equations contain typos or require better explanation.

**Major Comments**  
The authors should substantially deepen the connection to dynamical systems. The method resembles recurrent networks more than reinforcement learning (RL), as the reward is immediate and the policy is deterministic. Training effectively traverses the space of recurrent "policy" parameters θ to maximize the objective, warranting a dynamical systems perspective rather than an RL framing.  

The analysis of the cat image (Section 4.1) does little to clarify how the model’s attention mechanism improves recognition. Why are layer 1 activations uniformly increased at the first iteration? Is this scaling invariant to the objective function? A more focused analysis—e.g., tracking gating variable evolution in a small image region—would be instructive. Similarly, examining misclassified examples (where standard Maxout fails but the proposed model succeeds) or probing the impact of constraining θ’s mean/variance would reveal mechanistic insights.  

**Minor Comments**  
- **Algorithm 1**: Define \(h_M\) (likely a hidden state) and clarify if \(F[i]\) and \(\Theta[i]\) are accumulated per image or overwritten.  
- **Equation 11**: Specify the summation index (e.g., over policy samples \(i\)). If Equation 8 applies to a single image, it should be indexed \(j\).  
- **Text after Equation 11**: Clarify that \(x\) denotes input and \(d\) denotes target labels.  
- **Regularization of θ**: The necessity is unclear, as θ is already sampled from a prior that encourages small values. The final gradient update step (post-sampling) lacks theoretical justification and explanation of gradient computation.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The work presents a promising approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition, with high classification accuracy suggesting recurrent computation extracts or focuses on richer image features. However, the lack of mechanistic understanding—particularly regarding parameter roles and the method’s efficacy—limits its impact. While the results are compelling, the black-box nature prevents definitive conclusions about *why* the model succeeds. Future work should prioritize interpretability through dynamical systems analysis, targeted parameter studies, and case-specific visualizations. The core idea is significant, but the current implementation requires deeper theoretical grounding to advance the field beyond empirical validation.
