## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper presents Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to implement internal selective attention. The core idea is to move beyond purely feedforward processing, mimicking aspects of biological vision by allowing layers to influence each other across successive passes over an image. The authors train this attentional mechanism via direct policy search using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. 

This work builds upon the established success of deep convolutional neural networks [1, 7] for image recognition, particularly Maxout networks [7] which serve as the base model. The motivation to incorporate feedback connections is well-articulated, drawing parallels to human visual processing where complex classification tasks often require iterative analysis [16, 17]. The approach aligns with, and extends, recent work exploring recurrent processing in computer vision [48, 49, 50] and reinforcement learning for attention mechanisms [40, 41, 42]. The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a practical contribution, addressing a key challenge in applying reinforcement learning to large-scale neural networks [19].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed dasNet architecture introduces a unique approach to integrating feedback connections and attention mechanisms within deep CNNs. This departs from traditional purely feedforward designs and offers a potentially powerful method for improving classification performance.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly on challenging classification instances.
* **Clear Motivation & Biological Inspiration:** The authors effectively connect their work to principles of human visual processing, providing a compelling rationale for the design choices.
* **Scalable Optimization:** The use of SNES allows for the training of a high-dimensional policy, overcoming a significant hurdle in applying reinforcement learning to complex neural networks.
* **Insightful Visualization:** The visualization of filter activations and changes across iterations (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the network’s internal decision-making process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** While the results on CIFAR are promising, the paper lacks evaluation on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet (ILSVRC).  Demonstrating performance on a larger scale would significantly strengthen the claim of practical impact and validate the scalability of the approach.
* **Lack of Failure Mode Analysis:**  The paper focuses primarily on successes. A discussion of potential failure modes or scenarios where dasNet might struggle (e.g., specific image characteristics, adversarial examples) would provide a more balanced and comprehensive evaluation. Exploring the limitations of the approach would be valuable.
* **Complexity & Computational Cost:** The addition of feedback connections and the reinforcement learning training process introduces significant complexity. While the authors mention the training time (4 days), a more detailed analysis of the computational cost compared to standard CNN training would be beneficial.
* **Information Content of Gates:** While the authors assess the information content of the gates, a more rigorous analysis of *what* information the gates are encoding and *how* it contributes to improved performance could further strengthen the findings.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving CNN performance.
* The empirical results on CIFAR datasets are compelling and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.
* The use of SNES provides a practical solution to training the high-dimensional attention policy.
* The visualization provides valuable insights into the network's behavior.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of evaluation on a large-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet) limits the assessment of the approach's scalability and practical impact.
* The absence of a detailed discussion of potential failure modes or limitations weakens the overall evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces an innovative architecture with promising results. The combination of attention mechanisms, feedback connections, and reinforcement learning is a compelling contribution.  However, the lack of scalability demonstration on a larger dataset and the limited analysis of failure modes are significant concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** the paper with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concern by including results on a larger dataset in the camera-ready version, or provide a strong justification for why such an evaluation is not feasible. Addressing the failure mode analysis would also significantly improve the paper.
