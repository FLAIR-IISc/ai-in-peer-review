This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections. The core idea is that after an initial, rapid, feedforward pass, the network can iteratively refine its prediction by modulating the activity of its own convolutional filters over several subsequent passes. This internal selective attention mechanism is implemented as a policy, which is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, scalable natural evolution strategies (SNES)—to handle the high-dimensional parameter space. This approach is motivated by the recurrent and attentional processing found in the human visual cortex, which is hypothesized to aid in disambiguating complex scenes. The authors apply this method to a strong Maxout network baseline and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and conceptually appealing approach. Moving beyond purely feedforward architectures by incorporating a learned, top-down, attentional feedback loop is a significant and forward-looking contribution. This work successfully operationalizes the idea of a network performing sequential "internal thoughts" to correct or confirm an initial guess, which could inspire further research into dynamic and recurrent deep learning models for vision.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The decision to use an evolution strategy like SNES to train the control policy is well-justified given the challenge of applying traditional RL methods to a parameter space of over a million dimensions. The experiments are thorough, and the results convincingly demonstrate that dasNet improves upon a very strong baseline (Maxout networks), achieving a new state-of-the-art result.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the architecture and training process, aided by the clear pseudocode in Algorithm 1. The qualitative analysis, particularly the visualization of filter activation changes in Figure 3, provides valuable insight into how the attentional mechanism operates to correct a misclassification. The analysis of the information content in the gate values further strengthens the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful strategy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The primary weakness of the work is the lack of experiments on a large-scale, high-resolution dataset such as ImageNet. While achieving state-of-the-art on CIFAR is a notable accomplishment, it is crucial to demonstrate that the approach is computationally feasible and effective on larger, more complex images to truly prove its significance and practicality. The current training time (4 days on a GPU for CIFAR) suggests that scaling this method, which involves multiple passes and a black-box optimizer, might be a major challenge.
*   **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The paper effectively illustrates a success story where the network corrects itself. However, a more complete evaluation would include an analysis of failure cases. It would be insightful to understand when the attention mechanism fails to correct an initial error, or more critically, when it might turn a correct initial prediction into an incorrect one. This would provide a more balanced view of the model's robustness and limitations.
*   **Ablation Study on Policy Input:** The observation vector provided to the policy is a concatenation of average map activations, intermediate layer outputs, and class probabilities. While this seems reasonable, an ablation study investigating the relative importance of each of these components would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does the performance change if the policy only observes the final class probabilities? This would provide better justification for the chosen design.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and significant architectural idea that integrates feedback and attention into modern CNNs. It is technically sound, well-executed, and achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks, supported by strong qualitative and quantitative analysis.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The main concern is the demonstrated scalability. Without results on a large-scale benchmark like ImageNet, the broader impact and applicability of the method remain in question.

Overall, the combination of a CNN with a reinforcement-learned attentional feedback mechanism makes for a fascinating and valuable contribution. Despite concerns about scalability, the novelty of the idea and the quality of the results on CIFAR make this a strong paper worthy of acceptance.
