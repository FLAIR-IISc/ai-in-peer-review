This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to dynamically modulate filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its focus on relevant features, mirroring aspects of human visual processing. This is achieved by learning a policy, via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to gate the activation of convolutional filters on successive passes through the network. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, suggesting the effectiveness of their approach.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of established techniques in deep learning, including convolutional neural networks [1, 7], maxout networks [7], and reinforcement learning [23, 24]. The concept of incorporating feedback connections and attention mechanisms in neural networks is not entirely new [42, 43, 44, 45], and the biological inspiration from recurrent processing in the visual cortex is well-established [28, 29, 30]. However, the specific combination of SNES for policy optimization and its application to modulate filter activations within a deep convolutional network represents a noteworthy contribution. Related work exploring iterative processing and recurrent connections [48, 49, 50] is appropriately cited, and the authors acknowledge relevant findings on human visual perception [16, 17, 31, 32, 33, 37].

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, establishing the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Novel Combination of Techniques:** Combining reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) with deep convolutional networks to learn filter-level attention is a compelling and well-executed idea.
*   **Clear Motivation and Biological Plausibility:** The authors effectively motivate their work by drawing parallels to human visual processing, highlighting the potential benefits of iterative refinement and selective attention.
*   **Good Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core concepts accessible. The inclusion of Figure 3, visualizing the filter activations and changes, is particularly helpful.
* **Scalability:** Using SNES to train the policy is a good choice given the large number of parameters involved, addressing a potential bottleneck for reinforcement learning in this context.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are currently limited to the CIFAR datasets. While these provide a solid proof-of-concept, the generalizability of the approach to more complex datasets like ImageNet remains unknown. As suggested by the authors, extending the experiments to larger datasets would be a valuable next step.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** While the visualization of filter activations is insightful, a more in-depth analysis of *what* the network is learning to attend to would be beneficial. Understanding the specific features being emphasized or suppressed could provide further insights into the learned policy.
* **Dependence on Maxout Networks:** The authors specifically chose Maxout networks as the base architecture. While the authors mention its benefits, it's unclear how the performance would change with other CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet).
* **SNES Parameters:** The choice of SNES parameters (population size, learning rate, etc.) could benefit from further justification or ablation studies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep convolutional networks.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmark datasets.
*   The work is well-motivated and grounded in relevant prior research.
*   The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited scope of experiments raises questions about the generalizability of the approach.
*   A deeper analysis of the learned attention mechanisms would strengthen the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The authors successfully demonstrate the potential of using reinforcement learning to learn filter-level attention in deep convolutional networks. While the experiments are currently limited to CIFAR datasets, the promising results and well-motivated approach justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with the encouragement to expand the experiments to larger, more complex datasets and to provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention mechanisms.
