This paper introduces a method for incorporating selective attention into a convolutional neural network, developing a technique to train gating parameters that optimize the network's classification performance. The work addresses a significant and timely problem in computer vision: how to adaptively reconfigure complex, deep processing architectures, particularly when computational resources are limited or when a single model must be applied to varied tasks.

The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating a notable improvement in classification accuracy. The paper represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, static, feed-forward networks whose parameters are fixed after training. The exposition is generally clear and easy to follow.

However, the primary weakness of the work is that the model largely operates as a "black box." While the paper successfully demonstrates *that* the method works, it offers limited insight into the underlying mechanisms driving its success. The analysis of the learned image representations is not particularly revealing, and it remains unclear what role the learned distribution parameters (i.e., the mean and variance of theta) play in the attention process. Despite these shortcomings, the paper presents a promising direction for future research.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Conceptual Framing: Reinforcement Learning vs. Dynamical Systems:** I would encourage the authors to explore the connection between their model and dynamical systems. The current framing in the context of Reinforcement Learning seems tenuous. Since the reward (classification loss) is observed immediately after a forward pass and the policy is deterministic, the training process is more analogous to optimizing the parameters of a recurrent neural network. The training effectively involves a deterministic traversal through the space of recurrent "policy" parameters (theta) to directly maximize the objective function. Reframing the work in this context could provide a clearer theoretical foundation and better connect it to related literature on recurrent networks for vision.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Model Behavior:** The analysis of the network processing the cat image was not sufficient to build an intuition for how the proposed attention mechanism aids recognition. For instance, why do the activations in layer 1 uniformly increase during the first iteration? Does this have a meaningful impact on the objective function, or is the network's output simply invariant to such global scalings? A more insightful analysis could be achieved through several avenues:
    *   Focus on a smaller, specific region of an image and visualize the evolution of the gating variables over successive iterations. This could reveal how the network learns to attend to salient features.
    *   Analyze difficult classification cases. A comparison of images misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model could highlight the specific advantages of the recurrent attention mechanism.
    *   Isolate the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta). Conducting experiments where these parameters are constrained or ablated could clarify their respective contributions to the model's performance.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The description is ambiguous in places. The variable `h_M` is used without a clear definition; is this meant to denote the step of collecting observations? Furthermore, `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` are updated within the inner loop over images (`j`). It is unclear if these arrays are meant to collect values across the dataset or are simply overwritten in each iteration.
*   **Equation 11:** The summation lacks an explicit index. Please clarify what the sum is over.
*   **Equation 8:** This equation appears to be defined for a single image. To be consistent with the algorithm's notation, should it be indexed by `j`?
*   **Clarity on Variables:** In the text following Equation 11, the variables 'x' and 'd' are used without being defined in that context.
*   **Motivation for Regularization:** The rationale for regularizing theta is not well-justified. Since theta is already sampled from a prior distribution, which would naturally encourage its values to remain small, the purpose of an additional regularization term is unclear.
*   **Gradient Computation:** The final optimization step—updating the distribution parameters via gradients after sampling and evaluating theta values—is not explained in sufficient detail. A clearer description of how these gradients are computed is needed, along with a theoretical justification for this procedure. For example, is the objective to adjust the hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies? This is a critical component of the proposed method and warrants a more thorough explanation.
