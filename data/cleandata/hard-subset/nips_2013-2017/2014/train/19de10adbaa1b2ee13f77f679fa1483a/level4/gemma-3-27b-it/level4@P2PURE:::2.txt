This paper introduces selective attention into a convolutional neural network and presents a method for learning the gating parameters of this attention mechanism to optimize classification performance. It addresses a crucial challenge in computer vision: adaptively reconfiguring complex, deep processing pipelines under resource constraints and for diverse tasks. The reported classification results are strong; however, the method remains largely opaque – while it points to promising research directions, it lacks mechanistic insight into *why* it works. The analysis of image representations is not particularly informative, and the meaning of the learned parameters (specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution) is unclear. Despite this, the work represents an important advance beyond the current paradigm of large, fixed-parameter feed-forward networks. The exposition is generally clear, although some equations contain errors or require further clarification.

**Major Comments:**

The authors should consider exploring the connection to dynamical systems theory. The proposed approach appears more aligned with recurrent networks than with Reinforcement Learning. The reward signal is observed immediately, and the policy is deterministic, framing the training process as a traversal through the space of recurrent “policy” parameters, theta, to maximize the observed objective function. 

I found the analysis of the network processing the cat image unconvincing as it currently stands, and it doesn’t effectively demonstrate how the novel features of the model contribute to improved recognition. For example, why do activations in layer 1 universally increase during the initial iteration? Does this influence the objective function, or is the output insensitive to global scalings? A more focused analysis, perhaps examining a smaller region of the image and tracking the evolution of the gating variables within that region, could prove more insightful. Furthermore, investigating difficult cases – those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model – or systematically probing the impact of the learned model parameters (the mean and variance of theta) through further constrained experimentation would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

Algorithm 1 description: The definition of h_M is missing. Is this the step involving data collection? Regarding F[i] and \Theta[i] within the loop over images (j), is the image fitness value accumulated in an array, or overwritten with each iteration?

Eqn 11: The summation is undefined. Is Eqn 8 defined for a single image and thus should be indexed by j?

Text after eqn 11: Please clarify the meaning of ‘x’ and ‘d’.

The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, given that it is already sampled from a prior distribution and is therefore naturally biased towards smaller values. Moreover, the final optimization step (gradient updates on the parameters following theta sampling and fitness evaluation) is insufficiently explained—specifically, how these gradients are computed—and lacks theoretical justification (are these updates adjusting hyperparameters of the posterior distribution over policies?).

In summary, this is an interesting approach to constructing deep recurrent networks for image recognition. The substantial classification accuracy achieved, relative to standard methods, suggests that the recurrent computation facilitates the extraction or focusing on more complex image features. However, the method’s efficacy remains unexplained due to its black-box nature.
