This paper proposes the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a convolutional neural network (CNN) that incorporates top-down processing through attention-based feedback connections. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can iteratively refine its predictions by dynamically modulating the activity of its convolutional filters. This internal attention mechanism is governed by a policy trained with reinforcement learning (specifically, Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies) to correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets without data augmentation.

This is a well-written and interesting paper that introduces a novel architecture for computer vision. The work is forward-looking in its attempt to bridge the gap between purely feedforward models and the recurrent, top-down processing observed in biological vision systems. The combination of a powerful base model (Maxout CNN) with an RL-trained attention policy is a creative and significant contribution.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The model design is conceptually novel and represents a promising direction for research. I particularly appreciate the dynamic, time-dependent nature of the model, where gated signals are used to constrain and refine representations over multiple processing steps. The experiments are well-conducted, and the authors provide insightful analyses and visualizations that help build an intuition for the model's behavior. The example of the network correcting its "dog" classification to "cat" over several steps, along with the visualization of filter activations, is particularly effective.

Despite these strengths, there are areas where the paper could be improved.

First, while the paper effectively demonstrates successful applications of the attention policy, a more complete evaluation would include an analysis of its failure modes. Since the quality of the learned representations hinges on the policy, it would be highly informative to understand the circumstances under which the iterative refinement process fails. For instance, are there cases where the policy reinforces an incorrect initial guess or becomes unstable? A discussion of such limitations would provide deeper insight into the method's robustness.

My main concern, however, is the scalability of the proposed approach. While achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR is commendable, these are relatively small-scale datasets. The added complexity of dasNet, which requires multiple forward passes per image and a computationally intensive evolutionary strategy to train the million-parameter policy, raises questions about its feasibility for larger, higher-resolution datasets such as ILSVRC ImageNet. Demonstrating that this method is tractable and effective on a large-scale benchmark is crucial for establishing its practical significance beyond the CIFAR datasets. Without such results, it is difficult to assess whether this is a generally applicable technique or one that is only effective in a limited domain.

In summary, the paper presents an exciting and novel model that is worthy of further study. The integration of attention-based feedback with modern CNNs is a valuable contribution. However, to be a truly compelling contribution, the paper would need to provide more evidence of the method's scalability and practical utility through experiments on larger, more challenging datasets.
