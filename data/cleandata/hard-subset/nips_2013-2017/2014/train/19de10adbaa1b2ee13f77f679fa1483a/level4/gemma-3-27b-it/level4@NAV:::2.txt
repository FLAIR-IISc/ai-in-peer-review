This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism integrated into a convolutional neural network, alongside a novel training procedure for the attention gating parameters aimed at enhancing classification performance. It tackles a critical challenge in computer vision: adaptively configuring deep processing stages under resource constraints and varying task demands. The reported classification results are compelling; however, the underlying mechanisms remain largely opaque – the work points to promising research avenues without fully elucidating *why* the method achieves its success. The analysis of image representations lacks depth, and the roles of the learned parameters (specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution) are unclear. Despite this, the work represents a significant advancement beyond conventional, static, feed-forward network architectures. The presentation is generally well-written, although several equations contain typographical errors or require further clarification.

**Major Comments:**

The authors should thoroughly investigate the relationship between this work and dynamical systems theory. The approach appears more aligned with recurrent networks than with Reinforcement Learning, given the immediate reward signal and deterministic policy. Training can be viewed as navigating the space of recurrent “policy” parameters (theta) to maximize the observed objective function. 

The analysis of network processing applied to the cat image did not provide significant insight into how the model’s novel features contribute to recognition. For instance, the initial, uniform increase in layer 1 activations warrants further investigation: does this impact the objective function, or is the output insensitive to global scaling? A more focused analysis, perhaps examining a smaller image region and tracking the evolution of gating variables within that region, could be highly informative. Furthermore, examining challenging cases – those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model – or systematically evaluating the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) through controlled constraints, would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

Algorithm 1 Description: The definition of h_M is missing. Does this represent the observation collection step? Additionally, F[i] and Θ[i] appear within the loop iterating over images (j); clarification is needed regarding whether image fitness values are accumulated in an array or overwritten with each iteration.

Equation 11: The summation’s scope is unclear. Furthermore, Equation 8 appears to be formulated for a single image and should likely be indexed by *j*.

Text Following Equation 11: The referents of ‘x’ and ‘d’ are undefined.

The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, given that it is already sampled from a prior distribution that naturally biases towards smaller values. Moreover, the final optimization step – gradient updates on parameters *after* sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas – lacks a clear explanation of the gradient computation method or a theoretical justification (i.e., are these adjustments to hyperparameters governing the posterior over policies?).

Overall, this presents an intriguing approach to constructing deep recurrent networks for image recognition. The improved classification accuracy compared to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation facilitates the extraction or focusing on more complex image features, but the lack of interpretability limits our understanding of *why* this approach proves effective.
