Summary
This paper augments a feedforward Maxout CNN with internal, selective attention controlled by feedback connections. A policy network produces multiplicative gates on conv feature maps across multiple passes over the same image, allowing the model to iteratively reweight filters and refine predictions. The policy is trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) after standard supervised training of the base CNN. On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented test sets), the approach improves over a strong Maxout baseline. This work builds on prior CNN advances (e.g., Maxout, dropout) and connects to recurrent processing and attention mechanisms studied in neuroscience and machine learning, but uses black-box policy search rather than gradient-based RL.

Quality
- The method is technically sound and empirically effective on CIFAR-10/100, with a clear training protocol: supervised pretraining of the base CNN, followed by SNES to optimize a high-dimensional deterministic policy.
- However, the approach is largely a black box. The paper provides limited insight into why sequential gating helps, what the learned gates encode, or how the policy’s mean and variance evolve. The qualitative analysis (e.g., the cat example) is not very informative.
- The RL framing is somewhat superficial: the reward is immediate, the policy is deterministic, and the objective effectively optimizes a recurrent parameterization by direct search, closer to training a small recurrent controller than to classical RL with delayed credit assignment.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but some equations and algorithmic details are ambiguous or contain typos. In particular, Algorithm 1 and the loss/fitness definitions need more precise indexing and variable definitions.
- The rationale for L2 regularization on θ, given a distributional prior in SNES, should be clarified.

Originality
- The idea of modulating internal CNN activations via learned top-down signals is timely and differentiates the work from purely feedforward architectures. Using SNES to optimize a million-parameter policy for attention within a CNN is an original combination at the time of writing.
- Conceptually, the paper positions attention as iterative internal reweighting, bridging deep CNNs and recurrent/attentional processing inspired by neuroscience.

Significance
- The performance gains on CIFAR-10/100 suggest that iterative feedback can improve classification in ambiguous cases.
- If made more interpretable and less black-box, this approach could influence how feedback and attention are integrated into modern CNNs.

Major comments
- Relationship to dynamical systems and recurrence: The method appears closer to training a recurrent network than to RL per se. The reward is obtained at the final pass over the same input, and the policy is deterministic. Please discuss the dynamical systems view: the controller defines a closed-loop recurrent system Mt+1 = f(Mt, a(ot)). Can training be framed as optimizing recurrent parameters for multi-step inference? How does this relate to unrolled backprop through time versus black-box search?
- Interpretability of gates and dynamics: The cat example does not clarify why gating improves recognition. Why are layer-1 activations globally increased at the first iteration? Is the output invariant to global scaling of gates? Consider:
  - Analyzing localized regions (e.g., patches or CAM-like maps) and tracking gate evolution spatially and across layers for hard cases.
  - Systematically studying images misclassified by the base Maxout model but corrected by dasNet, to identify consistent gating patterns.
  - Probing the role of the policy distribution by constraining or ablating the mean/variance of θ, or freezing one while learning the other.
  - Reporting how performance changes beyond the trained horizon T, and whether the system converges or oscillates.
- Objective and SNES update: Provide a concise derivation or reference for how SNES updates µ and Σ from sampled fitnesses. Clarify whether the L2 penalty acts as a prior term on θ in the fitness and how it interacts with the search distribution. Explain why additional L2 regularization is needed if θ is sampled from a Gaussian with adaptive variance.

Minor comments
- Algorithm 1:
  - Define h(M) explicitly as the mechanism for collecting observations (averaged map activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities).
  - The placement of F[i] and Θ[i] within the loop is unclear. Are fitness values accumulated per image and then aggregated per θi, or overwritten? Please index the loss per image Lj,i and define Li = sum over images in the batch for θi.
- Equation 8/11:
  - Index Eq. 8 by image j if it is a per-image loss. In Eq. 11, specify the sum over the n images in the batch: fi(θi) = sum_{j=1..n} Lj,i + λL2||θi||^2.
  - After Eq. 11, define all symbols (x is the input image, d the correct class label).
- Regularization: Justify the L2 penalty on θ given that SNES maintains a Gaussian search distribution. Is the penalty acting as a MAP term, or primarily for numerical stability?
- Typos and notation: There are scattered typographical issues in equations; a careful pass would help (e.g., consistent layer/map indices, softmax scaling factor, clear dimensionalities for O and A).

Pros
- Clear, simple mechanism to add learned feedback to CNNs.
- Strong empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 without test-time augmentation.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired top-down modulation with deep CNNs.
- Scalable black-box optimization (SNES) enables training very high-dimensional controllers.

Cons
- Limited interpretability and analysis of learned dynamics; largely a black box.
- The RL framing is weak; the method is effectively direct search over recurrent parameters.
- Algorithmic details and equations need clearer definitions and indexing.
- Computational cost and practical guidance for stability/generalization are under-discussed.

Overall assessment
An interesting and promising approach to incorporating feedback and selective attention in deep CNNs, with compelling empirical results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a clearer dynamical interpretation, deeper analysis of the learned gates and corrected cases, and tighter, unambiguous presentation of the algorithm and objective.
