

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to enable dynamic selective attention during classification. DasNet extends standard feedforward CNNs by allowing higher layers to modulate lower-layer filter sensitivities through a policy learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), rather than conventional reinforcement learning. The approach dynamically refines classification by iteratively emphasizing or suppressing convolutional filters across multiple passes over an image, without altering the underlying CNN weights. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art results on un-augmented data, outperforming prior methods by 6%. This work builds upon NIPS contributions like Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) and recent efforts in feedback-driven vision (e.g., Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000; Bar et al., 2006), but uniquely applies scalable evolutionary optimization to learn attention policies in high-dimensional parameter spaces.  

**Strengths**:  
- **Significance**: Demonstrates a compelling method to enhance classification accuracy in challenging cases without data augmentation, advancing the state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks.  
- **Quality**: Experiments are rigorous, with clear ablation (e.g., T=5 steps), and results are well-supported by quantitative comparisons to strong baselines.  
- **Clarity**: The architecture and training procedure are well-explained, though minor notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., in Equations 1, 8–11).  

**Weaknesses**:  
- **Originality**: While the feedback mechanism is novel, the reliance on SNES (rather than RL) is under-theorized, and the connection to dynamical systems (e.g., recurrent networks) is not sufficiently explored.  
- **Quality**: The "black box" nature of the policy limits interpretability. The analysis of the cat/dog example (Figure 3) offers minimal insights into *why* specific filter changes improve recognition—e.g., why layer 1 activations uniformly increase in the first iteration, or how gating adjustments affect the loss function.  
- **Clarity**: Key elements require clarification: (1) The meaning of "d" in Equation 10 (correct class label), (2) the summation index in Equation 11 (over images in the batch), and (3) the necessity of L2 regularization for θ (since θ is sampled from a prior distribution).  

**Major Comments**:  
1. The method resembles recurrent processing more than standard RL, as the policy is deterministic and the reward (Equation 8) is a cumulative loss over fixed steps, not a sequential reward signal. The authors should explicitly discuss this in relation to dynamical systems theory and prior work on recurrent CNNs (e.g., Wyatte et al., 2012).  
2. The cat image analysis fails to link gating changes to classification improvements. The authors should: (a) quantify how layer-specific gating variations correlate with accuracy gains, (b) investigate whether global scaling of activations (e.g., layer 1) is loss-invariant, and (c) analyze misclassified examples where dasNet succeeds to isolate the mechanism of correction.  
3. The role of the policy distribution’s mean/variance parameters (θ) remains opaque. A constrained analysis (e.g., fixing variance to zero) would clarify if these parameters encode meaningful attention patterns or merely stabilize optimization.  

**Minor Comments**:  
- Algorithm 1: The observation function h(·) (line 9) is undefined; clarify its components (e.g., Avg(y_j), ȳ_j, v_t as in Section 4).  
- Equation 11: The sum over "i" should index images (j), not policy samples (i).  
- Equation 8: The "d" in λ_boost is the correct class label (not "d" as written).  
- The L2 regularization term (Equation 11) is not theoretically justified given θ is sampled from a prior; explain why it is necessary to prevent overfitting to the policy space.  

**Overall**: dasNet presents a significant empirical advance by demonstrating that iterative attention via feedback connections improves classification accuracy in a scalable manner. However, the lack of mechanistic insights into *how* the policy enhances recognition limits the paper’s theoretical contribution. The work is well-executed and impactful for practitioners, but deeper analysis of the attention dynamics would elevate it to a stronger theoretical contribution.
