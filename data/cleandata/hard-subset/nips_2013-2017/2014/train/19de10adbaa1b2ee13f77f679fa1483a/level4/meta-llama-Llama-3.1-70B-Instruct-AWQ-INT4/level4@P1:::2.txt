This paper introduces a novel approach to selective attention in deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by allowing each layer to influence all other layers on successive passes over an image through special connections. The authors propose Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which model selective attention by modulating the activity of convolutional filters through a learned control policy. This policy is learned through reinforcement learning using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a variant of Natural Evolution Strategies.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the limitations of traditional CNNs and the potential benefits of incorporating selective attention. The use of SNES to learn the control policy is an interesting approach, especially given the large number of parameters required to control CNNs of typical size.

The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets are impressive, with dasNet outperforming the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets. The visualization of the network's processing of a cat image is also helpful in understanding how the new features of the model help recognition.

However, the analysis of image representation is not very revealing, and there is no sense of what the learned parameters (mean and variance of the theta distribution) are doing. The authors could benefit from exploring the connection to dynamical systems, as the work seems closer to recurrent networks than to Reinforcement Learning. The reward is observed immediately, and the policy is deterministic, so training involves a traversal through the space of recurrent "policy" parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.

To better understand how the new features of the model help recognition, the authors could focus on a smaller region of the image and analyze the evolution of the gating variables in that region. Looking at difficult cases -- those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model -- or teasing out the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by constraining them further would also be interesting.

There are a few minor issues with the paper. Algorithm 1 description is unclear, and it would be helpful to define h_M and clarify the role of F[i] and \Theta[i] in the loop over images. Equation 11 is also unclear, and it would be helpful to specify the sum over and clarify whether Equation 8 is for a single image and should be indexed j. Additionally, the text after Equation 11 is unclear, and it would be helpful to define 'x' and 'd'. Finally, the regularization of theta is not well-explained, and it would be helpful to justify the need for regularization and explain how the gradients are computed.

Overall, this is an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition, and the high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation helps extract or focus on more complex features of the images. However, the black box approach does not explain why it's effective, and further analysis is needed to understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to selective attention in deep CNNs, which is a timely and important contribution to the field.
* The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets are impressive, with dasNet outperforming the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets.
* The visualization of the network's processing of a cat image is helpful in understanding how the new features of the model help recognition.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The analysis of image representation is not very revealing, and there is no sense of what the learned parameters (mean and variance of the theta distribution) are doing.
* The connection to dynamical systems is not explored, and the work seems closer to recurrent networks than to Reinforcement Learning.
* The paper could benefit from further analysis to understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.
