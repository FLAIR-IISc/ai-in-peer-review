This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), an architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can iteratively re-process an image by modulating the activity of its convolutional filters. This sequential "re-thinking" allows the model to refine its initial prediction, which is particularly useful for ambiguous or difficult classification cases.

The proposed system is built on a pre-trained, state-of-the-art Maxout network. A control policy, which determines how to gate the filter activations at each step, is then trained on top of the frozen network using reinforcement learning (RL). Given the massive, million-dimensional parameter space of this policy, the authors effectively employ Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization algorithm, for training. This approach is validated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where dasNet is shown to correct initial misclassifications and achieve a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented versions of these benchmarks. The work successfully bridges concepts from neuroscience (recurrent processing), reinforcement learning, and deep learning.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper's most significant contribution is providing strong empirical evidence that moving beyond purely feedforward architectures can lead to tangible performance gains in challenging image recognition tasks. By demonstrating state-of-the-art results, the work validates the hypothesis that biologically-inspired recurrent processing and attention are not just conceptually appealing but are a practical and promising direction for the field. This paper is likely to influence future research into dynamic and attentional deep learning models.
*   **Quality:** The technical execution is sound. The two-stage training process—first training the base CNN with supervised learning, then training the attention policy with RL—is a pragmatic and sensible approach. The choice of SNES to handle the high-dimensional, non-differentiable optimization problem is well-justified and effective. The claims are well-supported by the experimental results, and the authors include insightful analyses, such as visualizing filter modulations and verifying the information content of the learned policy's actions, which adds confidence in the method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background on the base Maxout network and the RL framework before detailing their integration in the dasNet architecture. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a clear and concise summary of the training procedure.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Originality:** While the overall system is novel and effective, the constituent parts (Maxout networks, feedback connections, RL for attention, and evolution strategies) are not new in themselves. The main contribution is the specific, non-trivial synthesis of these ideas into a coherent system that successfully advances the state of the art. This is a minor point, as the novelty of the combination and the resulting performance are significant.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on the CIFAR datasets. A key question is how this approach scales to larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet. The training time (four days for the policy on CIFAR) and the added computational cost of multiple passes during inference might become prohibitive. A brief discussion on the potential scalability challenges and possible directions to address them would strengthen the paper's impact.
*   **Further Analysis:** The analysis of the learned behavior is good but could be deepened. For instance, while the paper shows dynamics for `T=1` and `T=2`, a more comprehensive study on how performance scales with the number of attention steps (`T`) would be valuable. Additionally, an ablation study isolating the effect of the boosting scheme (i.e., weighting misclassified samples more heavily) would help quantify its importance to the overall success of the training procedure.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a very good paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective architecture. It tackles the important challenge of incorporating dynamic, attentional processing into deep vision models and provides compelling, state-of-the-art results that validate its approach. The strengths in significance, quality, and clarity far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper represents a solid contribution to the field and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
