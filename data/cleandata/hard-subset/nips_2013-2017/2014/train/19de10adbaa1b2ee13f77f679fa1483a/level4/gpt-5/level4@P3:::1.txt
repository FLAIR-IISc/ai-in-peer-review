Paper summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture that augments a strong feedforward Maxout CNN with learned feedback connections. The feedback acts as multiplicative gates on feature maps across multiple passes over the same image, allowing the network to iteratively emphasize or suppress internal features before producing a final prediction. The gating policy is learned via direct policy search with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for efficiency in a million-parameter policy space. The observation to the policy comprises averaged map activations in each Maxout layer, the pre-softmax activations, and the class probability vector; the policy outputs per-feature-map gains constrained to be positive via a scaled softmax. A boosting-style loss upweights misclassified examples during policy learning. On CIFAR-10/100 the method improves over a strong Maxout baseline and reports state-of-the-art results among single models on “unaugmented” datasets. The paper also presents qualitative analyses and stability experiments across different numbers of recurrent steps.

This work builds on a large body of research on deep CNNs for recognition (e.g., Krizhevsky et al. 2012; Goodfellow et al. on Maxout), neuroscience-inspired feedback and attention mechanisms, and RL-based selective attention (e.g., Denil et al., Larochelle & Hinton, Schmidhuber & Huber). Unlike spatial “glimpse” models, dasNet performs global, per-feature-map modulation and targets error correction on ambiguous inputs. Relative to recurrent or top-down CNNs trained by backpropagation, the novelty lies in the use of high-dimensional black-box policy search (SNES) to learn internal feedback that improves classification.

Quality:
- The method is soundly described: gating is well integrated into Maxout layers; observations/actions are defined; the policy uses a simple feedforward mapping; SNES is an appropriate choice given dimensionality. The boosting-weighted loss focuses learning on hard cases.
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, and the qualitative analyses are informative (e.g., per-layer changes, stability as steps increase).
- However, some aspects need clarification or stronger evidence:
  - The claim of “unaugmented datasets” conflicts with the statement that the Maxout net was trained with data augmentation. Please clarify whether “unaugmented” refers to not using test-time augmentation or external data/ensembles.
  - Baselines are limited. A recurrent/gated CNN trained end-to-end with backpropagation through time (without SNES), a simpler learned linear gating function, or policy gradient methods would strengthen the case that evolutionary search, rather than simply recurrence, is key to the gains.
  - Statistical significance, per-class improvements, and error analysis (e.g., confusion matrices before/after gating) would help quantify when and why the feedback helps.
  - Computational cost is material: T repeated passes and a million-parameter policy imply overhead. Reporting training and inference compute budgets and wall-clock comparisons would contextualize the improvements.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The algorithm is clear, and implementation choices are stated (e.g., T=5, dropout rates). A few suggestions:
- Resolve the “unaugmented” terminology and clearly distinguish between training-time augmentation, test-time augmentation, and use of ensembles.
- Provide exact observation/action dimensionalities and a brief description of SNES update steps (even if details are in the supplement).
- Tighten notation in Eq. (5)–(7) and ensure consistency for indices and activation functions.

Originality:
The combination of per-feature-map feedback gating with high-dimensional policy search and its application to strong CNN baselines is a novel and interesting take on attention/feedback. While feedback and RL-based attention have prior art, demonstrating measurable gains on CIFAR with a modern CNN is a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
The work supports the hypothesis that learned feedback improves recognition, particularly on ambiguous cases, and suggests a promising direction beyond purely feedforward pipelines. The demonstrated gains on CIFAR are modest but credible. The approach’s scalability to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and tasks (detection/segmentation) remains to be shown; nonetheless, the paper is likely to influence further research on recurrent/feedback mechanisms in deep vision.

Pros:
- Clear formulation of internal selective attention via per-feature-map gating across recurrent passes.
- Pragmatic learning scheme (SNES) for million-parameter policies; thoughtful observation design.
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines; qualitative analyses and stability results.
- Connects neuroscience-inspired feedback with modern deep learning practice.

Cons:
- Baseline comparisons are limited; no backprop-trained recurrent/gated counterpart.
- Ambiguity around “unaugmented” claim; need for clearer experimental protocol and statistical reporting.
- Compute and parameter overhead not fully quantified; scalability to larger datasets untested.
- NES/SNES choice is justified by dimensionality, but alternatives (e.g., differentiable gating with BPTT, deterministic policy gradients) are not empirically assessed.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Clarify the data augmentation setup and the “unaugmented” claim. Provide direct comparisons with/without train/test augmentation and with ensembles.
- Include a backprop-trained recurrent/gated baseline (e.g., learned gains via BPTT) and/or simpler linear gating to isolate the benefit of SNES.
- Report compute: training time, FLOPs/latency overhead per additional step, and policy size relative to the base network.
- Provide per-class improvements and confusion matrices to illustrate error correction patterns.
- Explore scaling to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and to spatially selective actions (attention maps).
- Consider ablations on observation components and policy capacity (e.g., diagonal vs. full covariance in NES, smaller policies).

Recommendation: Accept. While the individual components are not entirely new, the paper presents a well-executed and practically effective integration of feedback attention with deep CNNs, delivering state-of-the-art single-model results on CIFAR and offering a compelling case for learned internal feedback in vision.
