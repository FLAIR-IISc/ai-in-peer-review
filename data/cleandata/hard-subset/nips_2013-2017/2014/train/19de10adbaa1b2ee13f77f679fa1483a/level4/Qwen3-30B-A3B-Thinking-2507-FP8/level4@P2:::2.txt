

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which enhance convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by incorporating selective attention through feedback connections. The authors propose a novel framework where a trained CNN can iteratively refine its classification by dynamically adjusting filter sensitivities across multiple passes over the same image. The key innovation lies in using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to train a policy that modulates convolutional filter weights, allowing the network to "rethink" its initial classification. The approach demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs without data augmentation.

dasNet makes an important contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental limitation of conventional CNNs: their fixed parameterization after training. While recent work has explored recurrent connections in neural networks (e.g., [48, 49]), dasNet distinguishes itself by applying this concept directly to established state-of-the-art architectures rather than creating simplified models for demonstration purposes. The work builds upon Maxout networks [7] and leverages reinforcement learning principles to develop a policy that enhances classification performance through sequential processing. This approach aligns with biological observations of human visual processing, where multiple feedforward passes with top-down modulation improve recognition of ambiguous stimuli.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, establishing a new state-of-the-art for CIFAR-10/100 with unaugmented data. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including ablation studies on the number of passes (T), analysis of classification dynamics, and verification of the policy's information content through downstream classification tasks. The exposition is generally clear, with well-organized sections and helpful figures illustrating the network's behavior across iterations.

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant limitations in its theoretical and analytical depth. The most critical weakness is the lack of interpretability: dasNet functions as a powerful black box, offering impressive results without explaining *why* or *how* the attention mechanism improves classification. The analysis of the cat-dog misclassification example (Figure 3) fails to provide meaningful insights into the mechanism, leaving readers to speculate about the functional significance of the observed activation patterns. Specifically, the paper does not address why layer 1 activations increase uniformly in the first iteration, whether global scaling affects the objective function, or how the gating variables evolve meaningfully in spatial regions of interest.

The technical description contains several ambiguities that undermine the paper's credibility. Algorithm 1 lacks definition of the observation extraction function h(M), and the treatment of fitness values (F[i] and Θ[i]) appears inconsistent with the described loop structure. Equation 11 is unclear about the summation index, and the text following it does not define variables x and d. The regularization term for θ (λL2∥θi∥2) is introduced without justification, particularly since θ is already sampled from a distribution that would naturally encourage small values. The authors also fail to explain the theoretical basis for their SNES implementation or how the natural gradient updates are computed.

The reviewer strongly recommends that the authors reframe their approach as a dynamical system rather than strictly as reinforcement learning. The immediate reward structure and deterministic policy make the connection to standard RL tenuous; the work is more accurately characterized as a recurrent processing framework where the policy parameters define a sequence of network states. A clearer theoretical foundation would significantly strengthen the paper.

To address these limitations, the authors should:
1. Provide a more rigorous analysis of the attention mechanism, including spatially localized activation changes and their relationship to classification outcomes
2. Clarify the relationship between the policy parameters (θ), their distribution (µ, Σ), and the network's behavior
3. Better explain the observation function h(M) and the fitness calculation
4. Justify the regularization term and clarify the optimization procedure
5. Explore the connection to dynamical systems theory in the main text

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that recurrent processing can significantly improve classification performance in established CNN architectures. However, the current version fails to sufficiently explain *how* this improvement is achieved, limiting its scientific contribution. The results are compelling, but the lack of interpretability means the findings cannot be easily incorporated into future work or provide theoretical insights about visual processing. The authors have presented a promising technique that needs deeper theoretical analysis to realize its full potential as a scientific contribution.

Overall, dasNet represents an important step toward more flexible, adaptive deep learning architectures, but the current version is not sufficiently complete for acceptance at NIPS. With substantial revisions addressing the theoretical and analytical shortcomings, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. The authors should focus on providing a clearer explanation of their method's inner workings and its relationship to established theoretical frameworks.
