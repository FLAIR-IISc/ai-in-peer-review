

This paper introduces selective attention into convolutional neural networks and proposes a method for training gating parameters to optimize classification performance. It tackles a significant challenge in computer vision: adaptively reconfiguring complex, deep processing stages under resource constraints for diverse tasks. While the classification results are impressive, the method remains largely opaque—it suggests promising directions but fails to explain why it works. The analysis of image representations is unilluminating, and the role of learned parameters (mean and variance of the theta distribution) is not clarified. Nevertheless, this work represents an important advancement beyond the current paradigm of large, fixed-parameter feed-forward networks. The exposition is clear, though some equations contain typographical errors or require better explanation.

Major comments:  
The authors should rigorously explore the connection to dynamical systems. The methodology aligns more closely with recurrent network architectures than with reinforcement learning, as the reward is immediately observed and the policy is deterministic. Training thus involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters θ to maximize the objective function.  
The analysis of the cat image processing offers little insight into how the model's new features aid recognition. Specifically: Why are layer 1 activations uniformly increased at the first iteration? Does this affect the objective function, or is the output invariant to global scalings? Focusing on a smaller image region and analyzing the evolution of gating variables there could be instructive. Additionally, examining difficult cases—those misclassified by standard Maxout networks but correctly classified by the proposed model—and isolating the effect of learned parameters (mean/variance of θ) by further constraining them would be valuable.

Minor comments:  
Algorithm 1: Define h_M. Clarify whether it denotes the step of collecting observations. F[i] and Θ[i] are looped over images (j); are image fitness values stored in an array or overwritten per iteration?  
Equation 11: Specify the summation index. If Equation 8 applies to a single image, it should be indexed by j.  
Text after Equation 11: Define 'x' and 'd'.  
The necessity of theta regularization is unclear, as theta is already sampled from a prior and tends toward small values. Furthermore, the final optimization step (gradient updates on parameters after sampling/evaluating theta) lacks clear explanation (how gradients are computed) and theoretical justification (whether hyper-parameters of the posterior over policies are being adjusted).  

This approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition demonstrates high classification accuracy relative to standard methods, suggesting recurrent computation enhances the extraction or focusing of complex image features. However, the opaque nature of the method prevents understanding why it is effective.
