This paper introduces a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to learn visual attention. The core idea is to use feedback to gate the activations of convolutional filters, effectively allowing the network to dynamically focus on relevant image regions. This attention mechanism is trained using reinforcement learning, building upon a standard convolutional network foundation. 

While no single component of this work represents a radical departure from existing research, the combination and application of these techniques is compelling and represents a strong direction for future work in visual attention. The authors effectively demonstrate the efficacy of their approach, achieving state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR datasets. This performance provides significant validation for the broader concept of utilizing feedback and attention mechanisms to improve convolutional network performance. The empirical results convincingly support the hypothesis that feedback-driven attention is a promising avenue for advancing the state-of-the-art in computer vision.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed architecture and training procedure. The quality of the presentation is high, making it easy to understand the key contributions and experimental setup. The results are presented in a concise and informative manner, with appropriate comparisons to existing methods. 

The strength of this work lies in its pragmatic and well-executed approach. The authors have successfully integrated reinforcement learning with a convolutional network in a way that yields demonstrable improvements in image classification. This is not merely a theoretical exercise; the achieved performance gains on CIFAR are noteworthy.

However, a potential area for future exploration – and a suggestion for the authors – is scaling this approach to more challenging and complex datasets, such as ImageNet. While the CIFAR results are strong, demonstrating performance on a larger, more realistic dataset would further solidify the impact and generalizability of the proposed method. It would be particularly interesting to see how the attention mechanism behaves when applied to images with greater visual complexity and a wider range of object categories. Investigating the computational cost associated with the feedback connections and exploring strategies for efficient implementation would also be valuable. 

Overall, this is a well-conceived and effectively implemented work that offers a valuable contribution to the field of visual attention. The approach of using reinforcement learning to guide feedback within a convolutional network is both intuitive and powerful. I believe this paper warrants acceptance and represents a significant step forward in the development of more intelligent and efficient visual systems.
