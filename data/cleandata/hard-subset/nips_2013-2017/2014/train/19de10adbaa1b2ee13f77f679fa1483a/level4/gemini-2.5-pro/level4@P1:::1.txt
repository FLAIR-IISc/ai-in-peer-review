This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. This feedback mechanism allows the network to modulate the activations of its convolutional filters over multiple processing passes on a single input image, effectively implementing a form of internal selective attention. After an initial supervised training phase for the base CNN (a Maxout network), the control policy for this attentional mechanism is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to optimize classification performance on difficult or ambiguous images. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The core idea of integrating iterative, top-down attentional processing into deep CNNs is well-motivated by the human visual system and represents a promising direction for the field. By demonstrating a concrete performance improvement on standard benchmarks, this work provides strong evidence for the value of moving beyond purely feed-forward architectures. The results suggest that such recurrent "internal thought" processes can help resolve ambiguities that a single feed-forward pass cannot, which is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Originality and Technical Approach:** While the constituent components—CNNs, reinforcement learning, and attention mechanisms—are individually well-established, their synthesis into a coherent and effective model is novel and well-executed. Using reinforcement learning to train a modulatory policy on top of a pre-trained network is an elegant solution. The choice of SNES to tackle the high-dimensional policy space (over 1 million parameters) is a practical and effective engineering decision that makes the approach viable.

3.  **Quality of Experiments:** The paper is technically sound. The experiments are well-designed, using a strong baseline (a state-of-the-art Maxout network) and showing a clear performance gain. The authors provide valuable analysis beyond simple accuracy metrics, including an investigation of the policy's dynamics over multiple time steps and an experiment confirming that the learned gate activations contain significant class-relevant information. The inclusion of a visualization (Figure 3) that attempts to illustrate the filter modulation, while hard to interpret definitively, is a welcome effort toward understanding the model's behavior.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating their work, explaining the architecture and training procedure (Algorithm 1 is very helpful), and contextualizing their contribution within both computer vision and neuroscience literature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability to Larger Datasets:** A primary limitation of the current work is that the evaluation is confined to the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. While these are challenging benchmarks, the images are small and the number of classes is limited compared to datasets like ImageNet. Demonstrating that the dasNet approach scales effectively and provides similar benefits on a large-scale recognition task would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and impact. I would strongly encourage the authors to pursue this direction in future work.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The reinforcement learning stage of training is computationally intensive, reportedly taking around four days on a GPU for the CIFAR experiments. This cost could be a barrier to scalability and wider adoption. It would be beneficial for the authors to include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and training time, and perhaps comment on potential avenues for improving the efficiency of the policy search.

3.  **Interpretability of the Learned Policy:** The authors rightly note that qualitatively analyzing the effect of the filter modulations is difficult. The current analysis is a good start, but providing more insight into the learned attentional strategies would be highly valuable. For instance, does the policy learn consistent patterns for specific inter-class confusions (e.g., the "cat" vs. "dog" example)? Could the learned gates corresponding to specific misclassifications be clustered or analyzed to reveal common corrective strategies? Further work in this area could provide deeper insights into what high-level features the network learns to focus on.

**Summary:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating feedback and attention into deep networks. The combination of a powerful CNN architecture with a reinforcement-learned control policy is elegant, and the state-of-the-art results on CIFAR provide strong validation. The work is significant in that it successfully demonstrates that going beyond simple feed-forward processing can yield tangible benefits, paving the way for more complex and dynamic vision models. While questions about scalability and the interpretability of the learned policy remain, the contribution is solid and is likely to inspire future research in this direction.
