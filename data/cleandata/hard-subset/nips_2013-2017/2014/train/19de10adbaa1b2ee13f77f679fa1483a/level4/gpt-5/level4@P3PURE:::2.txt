This paper augments a convolutional neural network with selective attention and proposes a method for training gating parameters to optimize classification performance. It addresses an important set of problems in computer vision: how to adaptively reconfigure deep, complex processing stages when resources are limited and multiple tasks must be solved. The reported classification results are impressive. However, the approach functions largely as a black box—it points to promising directions but offers little insight into why it works. The analysis of the learned image representations is not very informative, and there is no clear interpretation of what the learned parameters (the mean and variance of the theta distribution) are doing. Even so, the work represents a meaningful step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with parameters fixed after training. The exposition is generally clear, though several equations contain typos or require clearer explanations.

Major comments
- Please explore the connection to dynamical systems and recurrent computation more seriously. The framework appears closer to recurrent networks than to reinforcement learning: the reward is observed immediately, and the policy is deterministic. Training thus resembles a traversal through the space of recurrent “policy” parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.
- The analysis of the network processing the “cat” image does not clarify how the new model components aid recognition. Why are all layer-1 activations increased in the first iteration? Does such a change affect the objective, or is the output invariant to global scalings? A more focused analysis on a smaller image region—tracking the evolution of gating variables within that region—could be more revealing. It would also be useful to examine difficult cases (e.g., examples misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model) and to tease apart the contribution of the learned parameters (mean and variance of theta) by imposing additional constraints or ablations.

Minor comments
- Algorithm 1: Where is h_M defined? Is this the step that collects observations? Also, F[i] and Θ[i] appear inside the loop over images (j). Are the image-specific fitness values stored in an array or overwritten at each iteration?
- Equation 11: What is the domain of the summation? Is Equation 8 meant for a single image and, if so, should it be indexed by j?
- Text following Equation 11: What do “x” and “d” denote?
- The need for regularizing theta is unclear, given that theta is sampled from a prior and should already be biased toward small values. Moreover, the final optimization step—gradient updates of parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas—is not well explained (how are the gradients computed?) and lacks theoretical justification (are you adjusting hyperparameters of the posterior over policies?).

Overall assessment
- This is an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The notably high accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation can help extract or focus on more complex image features. However, the current black-box treatment does not explain why the method is effective. Clarifying the learning dynamics, the role of the gating parameters (including the mean and variance of theta), and the connections to recurrent/dynamical systems would significantly strengthen the paper.
