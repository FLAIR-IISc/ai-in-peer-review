This paper proposes a novel deep neural network architecture called Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections to improve image classification performance. The authors draw inspiration from the human visual cortex, where top-down connections play a crucial role in modulating the activity of neurons in lower layers. The dasNet architecture is designed to mimic this process by allowing each layer to influence all other layers on successive passes over an image, thereby enabling the network to iteratively focus its attention on the most discriminative features.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction provides a thorough background on the limitations of traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and the potential benefits of incorporating top-down processing. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work in the field, highlighting the importance of recurrent connections and attention-based mechanisms in visual processing.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a detailed description of the dasNet architecture and its components. The use of reinforcement learning to train the attention policy is a novel aspect of the paper, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how this is achieved using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES).

The experimental evaluation of dasNet is thorough, with results presented on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. The authors demonstrate that dasNet outperforms the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets, and provide visualizations to help understand how the attention mechanism works. However, I would have liked to see results on larger datasets, such as ILSVRC, to truly demonstrate the scalability of the approach.

One potential concern is that the dasNet architecture may be more prone to overfitting due to the increased number of parameters and the complexity of the attention mechanism. However, the authors provide some evidence that the attention policy is learning meaningful representations, and the use of regularization techniques such as dropout may help to mitigate this issue.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, and the dasNet architecture is a promising approach for improving image classification performance. The authors have demonstrated the potential of their approach on smaller datasets, and I would encourage them to explore its scalability to larger datasets in future work.

Strengths:

* Novel architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections
* Sound technical content and clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components
* Thorough experimental evaluation on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets
* Promising results that demonstrate the potential of the approach

Weaknesses:

* Limited evaluation on smaller datasets; results on larger datasets such as ILSVRC would be beneficial
* Potential concern about overfitting due to increased number of parameters and complexity of attention mechanism

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and promising approach for improving image classification performance
* The technical content is sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components
* The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate the potential of the approach

Arguments for rejection:

* Limited evaluation on smaller datasets; results on larger datasets such as ILSVRC would be beneficial
* Potential concern about overfitting due to increased number of parameters and complexity of attention mechanism

Recommendation:

I would recommend accepting this paper, but encouraging the authors to explore its scalability to larger datasets in future work. The dasNet architecture is a promising approach, and the authors have demonstrated its potential on smaller datasets. However, further evaluation on larger datasets would be beneficial to fully understand its capabilities and limitations.
