

The authors propose Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel CNN architecture that incorporates top-down attentional feedback to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification. Unlike standard feedforward CNNs, dasNet uses reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to train feedback connections that modulate internal filter activations across multiple passes over an image. This iterative process allows the network to correct initial misclassifications, as demonstrated by state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation. The paper makes a compelling case for integrating bidirectional processing inspired by biological visual systems, with clear experimental validation through visualizations of filter activation changes and dynamics analysis across iterative passes.

The work offers several notable strengths. First, it presents a genuinely novel architectural approach that bridges recurrent processing and attention mechanisms within a deep learning framework—addressing a significant gap in current CNN design. The use of SNES to handle the million-dimensional policy space is well-justified and technically sound, avoiding the pitfalls of conventional RL methods. The experimental design is thorough: the authors systematically analyze how attention weights evolve (e.g., layer-specific filter emphasis changes in the cat/dog example), validate the policy's information content via gate-value classification, and demonstrate stable dynamics beyond the training steps. The visualizations of activation shifts across iterations are particularly insightful, making the internal "thought process" of the network tangible. The paper also thoughtfully contextualizes its contribution within both neuroscience (cortical feedback mechanisms) and computer vision (RL for attention), strengthening its significance.

However, the primary limitation lies in the scope of empirical validation. While CIFAR-10/100 results are impressive, the absence of experiments on larger-scale benchmarks like ImageNet (ILSVRC) leaves the scalability question unresolved. Given the paper's claim of applying "this perspective directly to the known state-of-the-art neural networks," the lack of results on a more challenging, high-resolution dataset weakens the argument for broad applicability. The authors acknowledge the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), but without evidence of scalability to ImageNet, it remains unclear whether dasNet’s complexity is feasible for modern vision tasks. Additionally, while the paper notes the model corrects misclassifications, it does not explore failure cases where the attention mechanism might misdirect or fail to improve performance—this would strengthen the critical evaluation.

**Pro Acceptance:**  
- Novel architecture bridging biological attention and deep learning  
- Rigorous validation of iterative attention dynamics via visualizations and gate analysis  
- Demonstrated state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks without data augmentation  
- Strong theoretical grounding in reinforcement learning and cortical processing  

**Con Acceptance:**  
- Limited scalability evidence beyond CIFAR (no ImageNet/ILSVRC results)  
- Insufficient exploration of failure modes or limitations of the attention mechanism  
- Computational cost not benchmarked against modern architectures (e.g., ResNet)  

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to attention mechanisms in deep learning. While the scalability concern is significant, the core innovation is compelling and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that future work should address scalability and failure analysis. The work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on advancing deep learning through principled, biologically inspired approaches.
