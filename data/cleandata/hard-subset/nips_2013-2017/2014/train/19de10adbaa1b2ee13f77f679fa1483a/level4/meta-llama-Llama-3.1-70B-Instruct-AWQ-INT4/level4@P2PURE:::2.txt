This paper proposes a novel approach to incorporating selective attention into a convolutional neural network, enabling the optimization of the network's classification performance through the training of gating parameters for attention. By tackling the challenge of adaptively reconfiguring complex, deep processing stages under limited resources and varying tasks, this work addresses a crucial problem in computer vision. The reported classification results are impressive, yet the underlying mechanisms of the method remain unclear, rendering the approach somewhat of a black box. While the method suggests promising directions for future research, it fails to provide insight into why it is effective. The analysis of image representation is not particularly revealing, and the role of the learned parameters (mean and variance of the theta distribution) is not well understood. Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with fixed parameters after learning. The exposition is generally clear, although some equations contain typos or could benefit from further explanation.

Major Comments:
A more in-depth exploration of the connection to dynamical systems would be beneficial. The proposed approach appears to be more closely related to recurrent networks than reinforcement learning, as the reward is immediately observable and the policy is deterministic. As a result, training involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.

The analysis of the network processing the cat image did not provide significant insight into how the new features of the model contribute to recognition. It would be helpful to understand why the layer 1 activations are all increased at the first iteration and whether this affects the objective function or if the output is invariant to global scalings. A more instructive approach might involve focusing on a smaller region of the image and analyzing the evolution of the gating variables within that region. Additionally, examining difficult cases, such as those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model, or investigating the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by imposing further constraints, could be enlightening.

Minor Comments:
In Algorithm 1, the definition of h_M is unclear; is it the step of collecting observations? Furthermore, it is not evident whether F[i] and \Theta[i] are collected in an array or overwritten on each iteration, as they are defined inside the loop over images (j).

Equation 11 lacks clarity regarding the sum; is Equation 8 specific to a single image and should be indexed by j? The text following Equation 11 is unclear, as the variables 'x' and 'd' are not defined.

The necessity of regularizing theta is not apparent, as theta is already sampled from the prior and tends towards small values. The final step of the optimization, involving gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas, is not well explained (including how gradients are computed) or theoretically justified (specifically, whether hyper-parameters of the posterior over policies are being adjusted).

The proposed approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition is intriguing. The high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation aids in extracting or focusing on more complex features of the images. However, the lack of transparency in the approach makes it difficult to understand why it is effective.
