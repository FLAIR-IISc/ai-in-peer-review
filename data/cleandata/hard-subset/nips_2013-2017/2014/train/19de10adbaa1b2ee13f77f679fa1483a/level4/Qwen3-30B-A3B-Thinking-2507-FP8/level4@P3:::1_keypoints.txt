- Introduces dasNet: adds feedback connections to CNNs to enable internal selective attention during inference.  
- Core innovation: dynamically modulates convolutional filter sensitivities via an RL policy learned with SNES.  
- Iterative refinement across multiple passes corrects misclassifications by emphasizing discriminative filters and suppressing irrelevant ones.  
- Achieves strong CIFAR results: 7.0% error on CIFAR-10 and 29.8% on CIFAR-100 without data augmentation.  
- Biologically plausible framing (top-down cortical feedback) strengthens conceptual motivation.  
- SNES is well-justified as a practical choice for million-dimensional policy learning.  
- Experiments are thorough: isolate ambiguous cases, validate dynamics, and show gate-value classification performance.  
- Architecture cleanly integrates with Maxout networks while preserving original structure.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized, with detailed algorithmic presentation and effective figures.  
- Original contribution: scalable RL-driven feedback for post-training refinement of SOTA CNNs, distinct from prior simpler feedback works.  
- Significance: empirically validates that iterative attention improves classification without retraining and is applicable to other pre-trained CNNs.  
- Scope limited to CIFAR-10/100; no ImageNet-scale validation to support broader state-of-the-art claims.  
- Computational cost is nontrivial (four days on GTX 560 Ti), raising deployment concerns.  
- Limited ablation: fixed T=5 without analysis of performance vs. T or detailed per-filter impact studies.  
- RL choice not compared to alternative scalable methods (e.g., PPO), weakening the SNES justification.  
- Minimal discussion of failure cases or when dasNet fails to correct errors.  
- Pros summarized: first scalable RL-driven feedback for post-training refinement, strong empirical and methodological grounding.  
- Cons summarized: limited dataset scope, computational burden, and missing comparisons/ablation analyses.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant, and well-executed work with clear acceptance recommendation.
