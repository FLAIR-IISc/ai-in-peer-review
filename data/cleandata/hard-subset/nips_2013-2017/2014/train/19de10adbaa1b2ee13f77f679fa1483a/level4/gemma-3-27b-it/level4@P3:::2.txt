This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), augmenting convolutional neural networks with feedback connections and employing reinforcement learning to learn a policy for dynamically adjusting filter sensitivities during classification. The work addresses the important problem of adapting deep networks post-training to improve performance on challenging instances, drawing inspiration from the recurrent processing observed in biological visual systems. The reported classification results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are compelling, exceeding the performance of previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets. While the approach remains largely a “black box,” it represents a significant step beyond the conventional paradigm of fixed-parameter, feedforward networks and opens promising avenues for future research. The exposition is generally clear, though some equations could benefit from clarification and correction.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper effectively contextualizes its contribution within the broader landscape of computer vision and neuroscience. It cites relevant work on CNNs, max-pooling, backpropagation, and biologically-inspired visual processing, including the role of feedback connections and recurrent processing. The authors appropriately reference key papers in reinforcement learning and natural evolution strategies, establishing the foundations of their methodology.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections within a deep CNN architecture and the use of reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) to learn an attentional policy is a novel contribution. This moves beyond purely feedforward architectures and explores dynamic adaptation during inference.
*   **Performance:** The reported performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the motivation behind the work, drawing parallels to human visual processing and highlighting the limitations of static, feedforward networks.
*   **Well-Written (Generally):** The paper is largely well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Interpretability:** The “black box” nature of the learned attentional policy is a significant limitation. The authors provide a limited analysis of *why* the network is making specific adjustments, and what the learned parameters represent. Visualizing the effect of the gating variables on smaller image regions could prove insightful.
*   **Connection to Dynamical Systems:** The authors’ approach exhibits strong similarities to recurrent neural networks. The connection to dynamical systems should be explored more thoroughly. The problem formulation—maximizing an immediate reward with a deterministic policy via traversal of a parameter space—suggests an optimization perspective rather than traditional reinforcement learning.
*   **Limited Analysis:** The analysis of the cat image classification, while illustrative, is not particularly convincing. The initial increase in layer 1 activations warrants further investigation (e.g., examining sensitivity to global scalings). Exploring misclassified examples and analyzing the effect of constraining the policy parameters (mean and variance of θ) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Technical Details:** Several minor technical details require clarification (see specific comments below).

**Specific Comments:**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The definition of `h_M` is missing. Is it the step of collecting observations? Also, clarify if `F[i]` and `\Theta[i]` store cumulative fitness values or are overwritten in each iteration of the inner loop (over images `j`).
*   **Equation 11:** What is the summation over? Clarify the scope of the summation.
*   **Equation 8:** Is this equation for a single image? If so, it should be indexed by `j`.
*   **Text after Equation 11:** Define `x` and `d`.
*   **Regularization:** Justification for regularizing `θ` is weak. Given the sampling from a prior, the parameter values will naturally tend towards small values. Further, the process of gradient updates on `θ` after sampling and evaluation is not well-explained or theoretically grounded.
*   **Equation 1:** Consider better explaining the variables in this equation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep CNNs.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements on standard benchmarks.
*   The work opens up a new direction for research in adaptive and dynamic deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of interpretability and limited analysis make it difficult to fully understand *why* the proposed method works.
*   The connection to dynamical systems and the theoretical justification for the optimization process are not fully explored.
*   Minor technical details require clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty, performance improvements, and clear motivation outweigh the limitations. While more analysis and theoretical grounding would be beneficial, the work represents a valuable step forward in the development of more adaptive and intelligent deep learning systems. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the technical comments and to expand the analysis. The authors should prioritize providing more insight into the learned attentional policies and exploring the connection to dynamical systems.
