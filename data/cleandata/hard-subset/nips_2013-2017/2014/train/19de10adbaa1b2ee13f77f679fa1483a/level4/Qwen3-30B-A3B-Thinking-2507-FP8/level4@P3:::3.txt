

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard feedforward CNNs with feedback connections to enable dynamic, attention-based filter modulation during inference. The core innovation lies in training a reinforcement learning (RL) policy via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to iteratively adjust filter weights across multiple passes on the same image, thereby refining classifications for ambiguous cases. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (6% relative improvement) and CIFAR-100, with compelling visualizations showing how attention gates suppress/activate specific filters across layers to correct misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing cat from dog). This work directly engages with neurobiological insights (Felleman & Essen, 1991) on top-down cortical processing and extends prior RL approaches to vision (e.g., Schmidhuber, 2004; Bar et al., 2003) by applying them to *existing* SOTA CNNs rather than simplified tasks, making it a significant step toward biologically plausible deep learning.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality (High):* The fusion of SNES for large-scale policy learning with feedback-driven attention is novel. Unlike prior work on recurrent CNNs (e.g., Behnke, 2012) or RL-based attention (e.g., Denil et al., 2014), dasNet *learns* filter modulation *post-training* via scalable evolution, avoiding the intractability of conventional RL for million-dimensional policies. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (Section 5) and addresses the "how" of attention mechanisms in CNNs—a gap noted in Zeiler & Fergus (2014).  
*Quality (High):* The technical approach is sound. SNES is rigorously justified for the high-dimensional policy space, and the experimental design (e.g., λboost for focusing on hard cases, T=5 steps) is well-motivated. Results are reproducible: the authors provide clear metrics (Table 1), ablation on dynamics (Fig. 2), and validation of gate information content (40.7% 15-NN accuracy). The method is complete—it’s not a position paper but a fully implemented system.  
*Significance (High):* The approach advances the state of the art on CIFAR without data augmentation, addressing a key bottleneck in CNN robustness. It demonstrates that "internal thought" via feedback can correct initial misclassifications without catastrophic forgetting, a critical step toward more flexible, human-like vision systems. The insights into filter dynamics (Fig. 3) offer valuable interpretability for deep learning.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity (Moderate):* While the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is precise, the description of observation space *h(Mₜ)* (line 9) is vague. How exactly are "average activations" and "intermediate activations" concatenated? A brief example would clarify. The visualization in Fig. 3 is insightful but lacks quantitative metrics on *which* filters are most influential (e.g., "Layer 0: 72% of changes affect eyes").  
*Significance (Moderate):* The scalability concern is critical. The authors rightly acknowledge dasNet’s computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti for CIFAR), but the absence of results on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet/ILSVRC) is a major limitation. Without evidence that the approach scales to 224×224 images or 1M+ training samples, the claim of "practical applicability" remains unproven. The paper’s focus on CIFAR (a small, low-resolution dataset) weakens its significance for real-world vision systems.  
*Quality (Moderate):* The ablation study on the *contribution* of attention is incomplete. How much of the 6% improvement stems *specifically* from the feedback mechanism versus the SNES policy learning? A baseline where the policy is fixed to identity (aⱼ=1) would isolate the attention effect. The regularization term (λₗ₂∥θᵢ∥²) is mentioned but not justified—why not use weight decay on the policy network?  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel architecture bridging neuroscience and deep learning; technically sound SNES implementation; state-of-the-art CIFAR results with clear interpretability; strong relation to prior work.  
*Con:* No ImageNet results to validate scalability; insufficient ablation on attention’s contribution; vague description of observation space; computational cost not addressed for large-scale use.  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept** (with major revision). The paper makes a compelling, original contribution to attention mechanisms in CNNs with strong technical execution on CIFAR. However, the lack of large-scale validation is a critical weakness for a NIPS paper, which prioritizes work with broad impact potential. The authors should:  
1) Add experiments on ImageNet (or at least a larger dataset like STL-10) to address scalability.  
2) Include an ablation study isolating the attention mechanism’s impact.  
3) Clarify the observation space *h(Mₜ)* and provide quantitative filter analysis.  

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper’s significance and ensure its relevance to the broader computer vision community. As it stands, the work is promising but requires evidence that its core innovation transcends the CIFAR benchmark.
