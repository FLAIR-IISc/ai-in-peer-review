Summary
The paper augments a convolutional network with selective attention via learned gating and proposes a training procedure for the gating parameters to improve classification. The work targets an important problem in vision: how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing under resource constraints and task demands. Empirical results are strong, and the exposition is generally clear, though several equations and algorithmic details need clarification. Conceptually, the approach pushes beyond fixed, feed-forward architectures toward adaptive, recurrent computation; however, the current presentation remains a black box and provides limited insight into why the method works.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely question of adaptive computation in deep networks.
- Demonstrates impressive classification results over strong feed-forward baselines.
- Clear high-level narrative and connection to selective attention.
- Potentially impactful step toward deep recurrent networks for recognition.

Weaknesses
- Limited interpretability: little understanding of what the learned gating parameters do or why performance improves.
- The theoretical framing as reinforcement learning seems misaligned; the setup appears closer to a deterministic recurrent dynamical system with immediate reward.
- Important training details (gradient estimation, role of regularization, objective over datasets) are underspecified.
- Analysis (e.g., the cat image example) is not convincing and does not reveal the contribution of attention.

Major comments
1) Framing and connection to dynamical systems/recurrent networks
- The method appears to instantiate a deterministic recurrent computation with attention gates as state/control variables. The reward is observed immediately, and the “policy” seems deterministic at test time. Please clarify why an RL framing is necessary and whether the core is better seen as optimizing parameters of a recurrent dynamical system.
- Training appears to involve traversing the space of recurrent parameters θ to maximize a supervised objective. Provide a precise optimization view: is this backpropagation through time (with reparameterization for any stochasticity), a score-function/REINFORCE estimator, or an evolution-strategy–style gradient-free update?

2) Gradient estimation and the final optimization step
- The “last step” where gradients update parameters after sampling/evaluating θ is under-specified. How are gradients with respect to θ’s distribution parameters computed? If you use a stochastic policy over θ, is there a reparameterization trick or a likelihood-ratio gradient? If the policy is deterministic, what stochasticity remains to justify a policy-gradient view?
- Please justify theoretically what is being optimized: are you updating hyperparameters of a posterior over policies, or directly optimizing an expected supervised loss under a parametric distribution over θ?

3) Role of regularization and priors on θ
- If θ is sampled from a prior with small mean/variance, why is an additional regularization on θ necessary? Clarify the motivation (e.g., stabilizing recurrent dynamics, preventing exploding activations, encouraging sparsity) and show sensitivity to the regularization strength.
- An ablation removing this regularizer and varying the prior mean/variance would help disentangle their effects.

4) Interpretability and analysis of the learned attention
- The cat image analysis is not informative. Why are layer-1 activations globally increased in the first iteration? Is the classifier invariant to uniform scalings at early layers, or does this materially affect the objective?
- Please provide localized analyses: select a smaller region and plot the evolution of its gating variables across iterations; visualize spatial/feature-wise gates and their trajectories.
- Examine challenging cases: images misclassified by the Maxout baseline but corrected by your model. Contrast their gating trajectories and intermediate representations.
- Probe the contribution of learned distribution parameters (mean/variance of θ): constrain or fix them and report how accuracy and attention patterns change.

5) Experimental ablations and compute
- Report performance vs. number of recurrent/attention steps and vs. compute budget (FLOPs or wall-clock). Does accuracy saturate, and what is the marginal gain per step?
- Compare deterministic vs. stochastic gating during training and testing.
- Clarify parameter counts and inference time overhead relative to baselines to ensure fairness.

Minor comments and clarity issues
- Algorithm 1: Define h_M explicitly. Is this the step where observations are aggregated? F[i] and Θ[i] are inside the loop over images j; are fitness values stored per image or overwritten each iteration? Please fix indexing and clarify storage.
- Equation 8/11: If Eq. 8 is per-image, index it by j. In Eq. 11, specify the index of summation (e.g., over images j and/or time steps t). State whether you optimize the empirical sum over the dataset or a minibatch expectation.
- Text after Eq. 11: Define x and d when first introduced (e.g., x for inputs/images, d for desired labels).
- Regularization: Provide the exact penalty term and its coefficient, and explain how it interacts with the prior over θ.
- Notation/typos: Some equations appear to have missing indices or mismatched dimensions; a careful pass aligning symbols in text, pseudocode, and equations would improve readability.

Overall assessment
This is a compelling approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition via learned attention gates, and the strong empirical gains suggest that recurrent computation can extract or emphasize more discriminative features. To strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to (i) adopt a clearer dynamical-systems/recurrent framing (or justify the RL view), (ii) precisely document the gradient estimation and the role of regularization, and (iii) provide targeted analyses and ablations that reveal how the attention mechanism improves recognition. With these clarifications, the work would make a strong and informative contribution beyond being an effective black box.
