This paper introduces selective attention to a convolutional neural network, and develops a method for learning gating parameters to optimize classification performance. It addresses a significant challenge in computer vision: how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing stages when computational resources are limited or when tackling diverse tasks. The reported classification results are strong, and the work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, fixed-parameter feed-forward networks. However, the method largely functions as a “black box” – while it points towards promising research directions, it offers limited insight into *why* it works. The analysis of image representations is not particularly revealing, and the roles of the learned parameters (specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution) remain unclear. Despite these limitations, the work is a valuable contribution. The exposition is generally clear, though several equations contain typos or require further clarification.

**Major Comments:**

The authors should seriously explore the connection between their approach and dynamical systems. The proposed method appears closer in spirit to recurrent neural networks than to reinforcement learning. The reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, suggesting that training can be framed as a traversal through the space of recurrent “policy” parameters (theta) to maximize the observed objective function. This perspective might offer a more principled understanding of the optimization process. 

The analysis of the cat image provided is not sufficiently informative to elucidate how the introduced attention mechanism improves recognition performance. For example, the initial increase in layer 1 activations at the first iteration requires explanation. Does this scaling affect the objective function, or is the output invariant to such global changes? A more focused analysis – perhaps restricting attention to a small region of the image and monitoring the evolution of the gating variables within that region – could be more instructive. Further investigation of challenging cases would also be beneficial; specifically, analyzing examples misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model. Finally, directly probing the impact of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by introducing controlled constraints on their values could offer valuable insights.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The definition of *h<sub>M</sub>* is missing. Is this the step corresponding to observation collection? The loop over images is indexed by *j*, but *F[i]* and *Θ[i]* appear within this loop. Clarify whether image fitness values are accumulated in an array or overwritten with each iteration.
*   **Equation 11:** The summation is not clearly defined. Is Equation 8 defined for a single image and thus should be indexed by *j*?
*   **Text following Equation 11:** The variables 'x' and 'd' are not defined. Please clarify their meaning.
*   **Regularization of Theta:** The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, given that it is already sampled from a prior distribution that inherently biases towards smaller values.
*   **Optimization Step:** The final optimization step – gradient updates on the network parameters following sampling and fitness evaluation of thetas – lacks sufficient explanation. How are these gradients computed? Furthermore, the theoretical justification for this step is weak. Are these updates adjusting hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies? A more detailed explanation is required.



In summary, this work presents an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for image recognition. The significant improvement in classification accuracy compared to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation facilitates the extraction or focusing on more complex image features. However, the lack of interpretability hinders a deeper understanding of *why* this approach is effective. Addressing the points raised above would substantially strengthen the paper and further illuminate the potential of this promising line of research.
