The authors propose a novel convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that incorporates a form of top-down processing through attention-based feedback, dubbed Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet). This approach enables the network to dynamically alter its convolutional filter sensitivities during classification, allowing it to iteratively focus its internal attention on the most discriminative features in an image.

The paper presents a clear and well-organized discussion of the dasNet architecture, its components, and the reinforcement learning method used to train the attention policy. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work in the field, highlighting the importance of top-down processing in human vision and the potential benefits of incorporating similar mechanisms in CNNs.

The experimental evaluation of dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, with a relative improvement of 6% over the vanilla CNN. The authors also provide insightful visualizations of the network's behavior, showcasing the dynamic changes in filter activations and attention patterns over time.

The paper's strengths include its novelty in terms of model design and application, as well as its forward-looking approach to incorporating both bottom-up and top-down signals for training a CNN. The evaluation of the model across time and the use of gated signals to constrain the representation are also notable aspects of the paper.

However, the paper's main limitation is the lack of experiments on larger datasets, such as ILSVRC, to truly demonstrate the scalability of dasNet to more complex and realistic scenarios. While the results on CIFAR are promising, it is unclear how the model will perform on larger images and more challenging datasets.

To further improve the paper, the authors could consider discussing potential failure cases or limitations of the dasNet algorithm, as well as providing more practical insights from experiments on larger datasets. Nevertheless, the combination of attention-based feedback and CNNs makes for an interesting model, which is worth further study.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novelty in terms of model design and application
* State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR datasets
* Insightful visualizations of the network's behavior
* Forward-looking approach to incorporating both bottom-up and top-down signals

Arguments against acceptance:

* Limited experimental evaluation on small datasets
* Lack of scalability demonstrations on larger datasets
* Unclear performance on more complex and realistic scenarios

Overall, the paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea, with promising results on small datasets. However, further experimentation and evaluation on larger datasets are necessary to fully demonstrate the potential of dasNet.
