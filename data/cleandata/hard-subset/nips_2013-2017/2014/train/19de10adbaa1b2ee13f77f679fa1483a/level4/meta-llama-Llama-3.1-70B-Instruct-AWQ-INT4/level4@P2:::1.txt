This paper introduces a novel approach to deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by incorporating internal selective attention through feedback connections. The proposed Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet) architecture allows the network to dynamically alter its convolutional filter sensitivities during classification, enabling it to iteratively focus its internal attention on the most discriminative features in an image. This is achieved through reinforcement learning, specifically using scalable natural evolution strategies (SNES), to train the feedback structure.

The authors provide a thorough overview of the dasNet architecture, including its components, such as the Maxout network, convolutional and pooling layers, and the reinforcement learning framework used to train the attentional policy. The paper also presents a detailed analysis of the experimental results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of dasNet in improving classification performance, particularly on difficult cases.

Strengths of the paper:

1. Novel approach: The incorporation of internal selective attention through feedback connections is a fresh perspective on deep CNNs, and the use of reinforcement learning to train the attentional policy is a significant contribution.
2. State-of-the-art results: The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet, achieving a relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN.
3. Clarity and quality: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components.

Weaknesses of the paper:

1. Limited scope: The paper focuses primarily on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, and it would be beneficial to see the approach applied to more challenging problems, such as ImageNet.
2. Complexity: The dasNet architecture and the reinforcement learning framework used to train the attentional policy may be complex and difficult to implement for some readers.
