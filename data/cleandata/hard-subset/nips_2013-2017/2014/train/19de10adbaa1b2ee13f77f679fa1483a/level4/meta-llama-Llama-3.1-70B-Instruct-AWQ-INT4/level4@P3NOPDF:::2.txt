This paper presents an innovative approach to incorporating selective attention into a convolutional neural network, enabling the adaptive reconfiguration of complex processing stages under limited resources. The authors develop a method for training gating parameters to optimize the network's classification performance, addressing a crucial problem in computer vision. The proposed approach yields impressive classification results, demonstrating the potential of this method in image recognition tasks.

However, the current implementation can be seen as a "black box," providing limited insight into why the method is effective. The analysis of image representation does not offer a clear understanding of the learned parameters, specifically the mean and variance of the theta distribution. Despite this limitation, the work represents a significant step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with fixed parameters after learning. The exposition is generally clear, although some equations require clarification or correction to avoid typos.

Major Comments:

1. The authors should explore the connection to dynamical systems more thoroughly. The proposed approach appears to be more closely related to recurrent networks than reinforcement learning, as the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic. Training involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.

2. The analysis of the network processing the cat image does not effectively demonstrate how the new features of the model contribute to improved recognition. It would be beneficial to investigate why layer 1 activations increase at the first iteration and whether this affects the objective function or is invariant to global scalings. Focusing on a smaller region of the image and analyzing the evolution of the gating variables in that region could provide valuable insights. Additionally, examining difficult cases, such as those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model, or constraining the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) to understand their impact, would be instructive.

Minor Comments:

1. In Algorithm 1, the definition of h_M is missing. It is unclear whether this refers to the step of collecting observations. Furthermore, the variables F[i] and \Theta[i] are inside the loop over images (j), but it is not specified whether the image fitness values are collected in an array or overwritten on each iteration.

2. Equation 11 lacks clarity regarding the sum. It is unclear whether Equation 8 refers to a single image and should be indexed j.

3. The text following Equation 11 uses the variables 'x' and 'd' without clear definitions. Providing explicit definitions or explanations for these variables would improve understanding.

4. The regularization of theta appears unnecessary, as theta is already sampled from the prior and tends to small values. The final step of the optimization, involving gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas, is not well-explained and lacks theoretical justification. Specifically, it is unclear how the gradients are computed and whether this step is equivalent to adjusting the hyper-parameters of the posterior over policies.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition tasks. The high classification accuracy achieved by the proposed method suggests that recurrent computation can help extract or focus on more complex features of images. However, the "black box" approach does not provide a clear understanding of why this method is effective, and further analysis is necessary to fully appreciate its potential.
