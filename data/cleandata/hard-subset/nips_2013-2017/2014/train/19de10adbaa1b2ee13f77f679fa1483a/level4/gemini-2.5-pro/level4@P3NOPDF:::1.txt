This paper presents a compelling approach for incorporating visual attention into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through feedback connections. The core contribution is a model that learns to selectively gate the activations of convolutional filters, effectively directing computational resources to the most salient features of an image. This attention mechanism is trained using a reinforcement learning framework, where the network learns a policy for attending to specific visual cues.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Soundness and Execution:** While the individual components of the model—convolutional networks, feedback mechanisms, and reinforcement learning—are not new in isolation, their synthesis in this work is both elegant and highly effective. The authors' contribution lies not in inventing a new primitive, but in demonstrating a principled and successful integration of these ideas to tackle the problem of visual attention. This paper provides a strong argument that this combination is a promising direction for the field.

2.  **Empirical Validation:** The strength of this approach is convincingly demonstrated by the empirical results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR benchmark is a significant accomplishment and provides strong validation for the proposed model. More importantly, these results lend substantial weight to the hypothesis that incorporating top-down, attentional feedback is a crucial step forward for computer vision models, potentially moving beyond purely feed-forward architectures.

3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors effectively articulate the motivation for their work, the details of their model, and the interpretation of their results.

**Areas for Improvement:**

The primary area for future work lies in scaling the proposed method. While the results on CIFAR are impressive, the true test for many modern vision architectures is performance on larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet. Demonstrating the efficacy and scalability of this RL-driven attention mechanism on such a benchmark would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and showcase its broader applicability. I would encourage the authors to discuss the potential challenges and computational considerations of applying their model to ImageNet-scale problems, even if full experiments are not yet complete.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a well-executed and conceptually appealing model for visual attention. By combining convolutional networks with reinforcement learning to control feedback gating, the authors have developed a powerful mechanism that achieves excellent results. The work serves as an important proof of concept for the value of attention and feedback in deep vision models. I recommend acceptance.
