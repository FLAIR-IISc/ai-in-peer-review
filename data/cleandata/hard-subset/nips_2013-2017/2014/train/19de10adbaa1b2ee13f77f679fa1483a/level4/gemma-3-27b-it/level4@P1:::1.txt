This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to learn selective internal attention. The core idea is to modulate the activation of convolutional filters through learned gating mechanisms, effectively allowing the network to refine its processing through iterative passes over an image. This attentional policy is learned using reinforcement learning – specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) – applied on top of a pre-trained convolutional network. While none of the individual components are entirely novel, the combination and application of these techniques represent a sound approach, and the authors demonstrate promising results achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. This work provides further evidence supporting the potential of feedback and attention mechanisms in improving visual recognition systems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing literature on convolutional neural networks, reinforcement learning, and attention mechanisms in both neuroscience and computer vision. They provide sufficient detail to understand the methodology and experimental setup. The organization, referencing, and mathematical notation are all clear and contribute to the overall readability of the manuscript. It provides enough information for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results, although details regarding the SNES implementation are relegated to the supplementary material.

The originality of this work lies primarily in the specific integration of SNES-driven feedback connections with a Maxout network for image classification. While recurrent connections and reinforcement learning have been explored in computer vision previously (e.g., [40, 41, 42, 43]), this work distinguishes itself by focusing on modulating internal filter activations rather than explicitly controlling gaze or performing higher-level actions. The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a pragmatic choice, particularly given the scale of the networks typically used for image classification.  The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches, correctly positioning dasNet within the broader research landscape.

The significance of this work lies in its demonstration that learned feedback connections can effectively improve the performance of deep convolutional networks, even on relatively small datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. The authors' results suggest that allowing a network to iteratively refine its internal representations through attention can lead to more robust and accurate classification, particularly in ambiguous cases. Establishing a new state-of-the-art result, albeit on unaugmented data, is a positive step.  The analysis presented in Figure 3, visualizing the filter activations and changes during the iterative process, offers some preliminary insight into how dasNet operates, although further investigation into the learned attention policies is warranted. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) is also a valuable addition, suggesting that the learned attention patterns generalize beyond the training iterations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
*   A well-motivated and clearly explained approach combining reinforcement learning and feedback connections in a deep convolutional network.
*   Good clarity of writing and organization, making the paper easy to understand.
*   Demonstrates the potential of iterative refinement through internal attention mechanisms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The impact of this work would be significantly strengthened by experiments on larger, more challenging datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
*   The analysis of the learned attention policies is somewhat limited. A deeper understanding of *what* the network is attending to and *why* would be valuable.
*   The connection between the biological inspiration (feedback connections in the visual cortex) and the specific implementation of dasNet could be more explicitly discussed.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning and computer vision.  I recommend acceptance.
