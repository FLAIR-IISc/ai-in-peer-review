This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a convolutional neural network, proposing a method to train the corresponding gating parameters to enhance the network's classification capabilities. The work addresses a crucial set of problems in computer vision, namely how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing architectures under limited resources or for different tasks. While the classification results are impressive, the method's internal mechanics remain opaque; it suggests promising directions for research but does not elucidate why it is effective. The analysis of the learned image representations offers little insight, and the role of the learned parameters for the theta distribution (its mean and variance) is not explained. Nonetheless, this work is a significant step forward from the current paradigm of large, static, feed-forward networks with parameters that are fixed after training. The manuscript is written clearly, although some equations appear to have typographical errors or require better explanations.

**Major Comments:**

*   A more rigorous exploration of the connection to dynamical systems would significantly strengthen the paper. The proposed framework appears more analogous to recurrent networks than to Reinforcement Learning. As the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, the training process is effectively a search through the space of the recurrent "policy" parameters, theta, to maximize the given objective function.
*   The analysis of the network processing the sample cat image was not particularly helpful in understanding how the novel aspects of the model aid recognition. For example, why do all activations in layer 1 increase in the first iteration? Does this change impact the objective function, or is the output invariant to such global scaling? A more instructive analysis could be achieved by focusing on a smaller image region and tracking the evolution of its gating variables. It would also be interesting to analyze difficult classification cases—those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly handled by the proposed model—or to isolate the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by applying further constraints.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the description of Algorithm 1: where is `h_M` defined? Is this the step for collecting observations? Also, `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` are inside the loop over images (j); are these fitness values meant to be collected in an array or are they overwritten with each iteration?
*   Equation 11: The summation index is not specified.
*   Equation 8: Is this equation intended for a single image? If so, it should likely be indexed with `j`.
*   Text following Equation 11: The variables 'x' and 'd' are used without being defined.
*   The necessity for the regularization of theta is unclear, as theta is already sampled from a prior and should thus be biased toward smaller values.
*   The final optimization step (updating parameters via gradients after sampling and evaluating thetas) is not well-explained. The paper does not detail how the gradients are computed, nor does it provide a theoretical justification for this step (e.g., is this an adjustment of hyperparameters of the posterior over policies?).

This is an interesting approach for building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation aids in extracting or focusing on more complex image features, but the black-box presentation fails to explain why the method is effective.
