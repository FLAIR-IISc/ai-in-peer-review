This paper introduces selective attention to a convolutional neural network and develops a method for training gating parameters to optimize classification performance. It addresses an important problem in computer vision: how to adaptively reconfigure complex, deep processing stages when resources are limited and tasks vary. The classification results are impressive, but the method largely remains a black box – it suggests promising directions for future work without revealing *why* it works. The analysis of image representations is not particularly insightful, and the learned parameters (specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution) lack clear interpretation. Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with fixed parameters after training. The exposition is generally clear, though some equations contain typos or require further explanation.

**Major Comments:**

I encourage the authors to seriously explore the connection between their approach and dynamical systems. The work appears closer to recurrent networks than to Reinforcement Learning. The reward is observed immediately, and the policy is deterministic, meaning training involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters, theta, to maximize the observed objective function.

I did not find the analysis of the network processing the cat image particularly helpful in understanding how the novel features of the model contribute to recognition. For example, why do layer 1 activations increase across the board during the first iteration? Does this affect the objective function, or is the output invariant to global scalings? It might be instructive to focus on a smaller region of the image and analyze the evolution of the gating variables within that region. Additionally, examining difficult cases – those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model – or further investigating the effect of the learned model parameters (the mean and variance of theta) by imposing additional constraints on them, could be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

Algorithm 1 description: Where is h<sub>M</sub> defined? Is this the step where observations are collected? Within the loop over images (j), F[i] and Θ[i] are used. Are image fitness values accumulated in an array, or overwritten with each iteration?

Equation 11: What is the summation over? Is Equation 8 defined for a single image, and should it be indexed by *j*?

Text following Equation 11: What do ‘x’ and ‘d’ refer to?

I’m unclear why regularizing theta is necessary, given that it is already sampled from a prior distribution and would naturally tend towards small values. Furthermore, the final optimization step – gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas – is not well explained (how are the gradients computed?) or theoretically justified (are you adjusting hyperparameters of the posterior over policies?).

Overall, this is an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The high classification accuracy, relative to standard methods, suggests that recurrent computation aids in extracting or focusing on more complex image features. However, the black-box nature of the approach prevents a deeper understanding of *why* it is effective.
