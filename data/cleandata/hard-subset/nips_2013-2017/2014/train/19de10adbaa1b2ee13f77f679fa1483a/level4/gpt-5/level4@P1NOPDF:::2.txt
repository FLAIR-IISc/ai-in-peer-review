This paper augments a convolutional neural network with selective attention via learnable gating, and proposes a training method for the gating parameters that optimizes classification performance. It tackles an important problem in computer vision: how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing stages when resources are limited and tasks vary. The classification results are impressive. However, the approach remains largely a black box—it points to promising directions but offers little insight into why it works. The representation analysis is not very informative, and the role of the learned parameters (the mean and variance of the theta distribution) is unclear. Nevertheless, the work is an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, purely feed-forward networks with parameters fixed after training. The exposition is generally clear, though several equations appear to contain typos or need better explanation.

Major comments:
- Please explore more seriously the connection to dynamical systems. The work seems closer to recurrent networks than to reinforcement learning. The reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, so training amounts to traversing the space of recurrent “policy” parameters theta to maximize the observed objective. Framing the method explicitly as a dynamical, recurrent process could clarify both the learning signal and the stability/expressivity of the attention mechanism.
- The analysis using the cat image did not clarify how the new features help recognition. Why are all layer-1 activations increased in the first iteration? Does this change the objective, or is the output invariant to global scaling? Focusing on a smaller image region and analyzing the evolution of the gating variables there could be more instructive. It would also help to examine difficult cases—images misclassified by the standard Maxout network but corrected by the proposed model—and to tease out the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by further constraining or ablating them.

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1: Where is h_M defined? Is that the step where observations are collected? Also, F[i] and Θ[i] appear inside the loop over images (j). Are the fitness values for each image stored in an array, or are they overwritten on each iteration?
- Equation 11: What is the index of the summation? Is Equation 8 defined for a single image and should therefore be indexed by j?
- Text after Equation 11: What do the variables x and d refer to?
- The need for regularizing theta is unclear, since theta is sampled from the prior and should tend toward small values. Moreover, the final optimization step—performing gradient updates on parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas—is not well explained. How are these gradients computed? What is the theoretical justification? Are you effectively adjusting hyperparameters of a posterior over policies?

Overall, this is an interesting approach to constructing deep recurrent networks for recognition. The high accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation helps extract or focus on more complex image features. However, the current black-box presentation does not explain why the method is effective, and stronger analysis would significantly improve the paper.
