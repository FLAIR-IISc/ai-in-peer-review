This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to dynamically modulate filter activations. The core idea – learning to selectively attend to internal features via reinforcement learning – builds upon established concepts in both CNNs and attention mechanisms, but presents a compelling approach to improving classification performance. The authors train a standard CNN with supervised learning, then utilize Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a policy that gates the activations of convolutional filters across successive passes through the network.  This allows the network to refine its classifications by iteratively focusing on salient features. 

The work is situated within a growing body of research inspired by the recurrent processing observed in the biological visual system.  The authors appropriately reference key works on CNNs [1, 7], reinforcement learning [23, 24], and related attention mechanisms [40, 41]. The motivation, drawn from observations of human visual processing [16, 17], is clearly articulated:  humans often require multiple passes over an image to resolve ambiguity, suggesting the potential benefit of iterative refinement in artificial systems. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its elegant approach and strong empirical results. The methodology of applying reinforcement learning *after* supervised pre-training is a pragmatic and scalable solution for navigating the large parameter space associated with controlling a deep CNN. The reported state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging instances, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the dasNet architecture. The clarity of the writing is generally good, with the paper well-organized and the core concepts explained effectively. The inclusion of Figure 3, demonstrating the filter weighting changes, is a valuable addition that helps to visualize the network’s attentional process.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the lack of substantial novelty in individual components, they could more thoroughly discuss the contributions of their specific *combination* of techniques and how it advances the field beyond existing work.  The justification for choosing SNES over other reinforcement learning algorithms is reasonable given the dimensionality of the action space, but a more detailed discussion of potential trade-offs with other methods would be beneficial. The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100; while a strong baseline, expanding the evaluation to larger and more complex datasets like ImageNet would significantly strengthen the claims of significance.  The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) is somewhat limited in scope and could be further explored. The authors mention the difficulty of qualitatively analyzing the effect of the filter changes, and this remains an open question throughout the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100.
*   A compelling and well-motivated approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs.
*   Clear writing and a well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of using reinforcement learning to refine the behavior of deep convolutional networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in individual components; the contribution lies in the combination, which requires more thorough justification.
*   Experiments are limited to relatively small datasets; evaluation on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is needed to assess scalability and generalizability.
*   Qualitative analysis of the learned attention mechanisms is limited.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning, demonstrating a promising approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination is effective and supported by strong experimental results. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider expanding their experiments to larger datasets and providing a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention patterns in future work.
