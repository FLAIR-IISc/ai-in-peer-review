This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network incorporating top-down processing via an attention-based feedback mechanism. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, suggesting a promising approach to improving image classification.

The work is generally compelling and demonstrates novelty in both model architecture and its application to computer vision. The consideration of both bottom-up and top-down signal flow for CNN training is a forward-looking direction, and the use of sequential processing and gated signals to refine representations is particularly noteworthy. The authors effectively visualize the network’s behavior, aiding understanding of the learned attention mechanisms. 

However, while the results are encouraging, a potential weakness lies in the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The experiments are primarily focused on the relatively small CIFAR datasets. To fully assess the scalability and generalizability of dasNet, and to convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing state-of-the-art methods, it would be highly beneficial to see results on a larger, more challenging benchmark such as ImageNet (ILSVRC). This would address concerns about whether the performance gains observed on CIFAR translate to real-world, high-resolution images and more complex datasets. Furthermore, exploring scenarios where the algorithm *fails* – or identifying limitations of the approach – would strengthen the paper by providing a more nuanced understanding of its capabilities. While the authors mention complex scenarios for potential failure, demonstrating this with empirical data would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of feedback connections and reinforcement learning to modulate convolutional filters represents a novel approach within the field of deep learning.
*   **Performance:** The reported state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Clarity of Explanation:** The paper clearly explains the dasNet architecture and the reinforcement learning process used to train the attentional policy.
*   **Visualizations:** The visualizations of filter activations and attention weights provide valuable insights into the network’s internal workings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** The lack of results on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) raises questions about the scalability of the approach.
*   **Failure Modes:** A deeper analysis of potential failure cases and the algorithm's limitations would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Computational Cost:** While not a major weakness, the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) should be acknowledged and discussed in relation to practical applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to improving CNN performance through the incorporation of feedback and attention mechanisms.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on relevant benchmark datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation raises concerns about the scalability and generalizability of the approach.
*   The lack of analysis of failure modes weakens the overall evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed dasNet architecture offers a promising approach to enhancing CNNs by incorporating feedback and attention mechanisms. While the lack of results on larger datasets is a concern, the novelty and potential impact of the work justify consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the scalability concern, either through additional experiments or a thorough discussion of the limitations.
