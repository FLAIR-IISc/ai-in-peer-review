- Proposes dasNet: a Maxout CNN augmented with multiplicative per-feature-map gates and recurrent multi-pass inference controlled by a learned policy.
- Observations (avg feature-map activations, pre-softmax activations, class probabilities) drive a policy that sets gates for the next pass.
- Policy (≈1M params) is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) as a black-box optimizer.
- On unaugmented CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and reports state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.
- Visualizations and dynamics analyses show gates evolve across passes and can flip ambiguous classifications; performance peaks near the trained number of passes.
- Architectural and training design choices are simple, well-defined, and include stability measures (scaled softmax to keep gates positive and mean-one).
- Empirical support is consistent but limited to small 32×32 datasets; no experiments on larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ILSVRC).
- Missing important baselines/ablations: multi-pass feedforward baseline, end-to-end BPTT training of gates, gating restricted to specific layers, and comparisons to policy-gradient or other optimizers.
- Lacks per-class error analysis and quantitative reporting of how many test examples change decision due to feedback.
- Policy learning is compute-intensive (days on a single GPU) and inference cost is multiplied by the number of passes T.
- Paper should report SNES generations, sample efficiency, wall-clock cost, and justify SNES vs gradient-based alternatives.
- Clarity is generally good but needs explicit dimensions for observation/action vectors, per-layer gating granularity, and policy network architecture details.
- Originality: novel combination of global channel-wise recurrent attention in mainstream CNNs trained with evolutionary optimization.
- Significance is promising for advancing feedback/attention in vision but limited by dataset scale and practical overheads.
- Strengths: clear motivation, empirical gains over a strong baseline, informative visualizations, and evidence that gates carry discriminative information.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: expand to larger datasets, add missing baselines/ablations, justify optimizer choice, test spatial attention and richer observations, and quantify inference-time trade-offs.
- Reasons to accept: innovative, well-motivated, empirically effective for CIFAR, and likely to stimulate follow-up work.
- Reasons to reject: limited scalability evidence, missing key comparisons, and reliance on expensive black-box optimization without clear advantage.
