This paper presents a convolutional neural network (convnet) with feedback connections to learn visual attention. The feedback mechanism is implemented by gating the activation of certain filters, allowing the model to selectively focus on relevant features. The approach is novel in its combination of techniques, rather than introducing new individual components. Specifically, the model is learned using reinforcement learning on top of a convnet, which enables it to adaptively allocate attention.

Although the individual components of the model may not be groundbreaking, the overall approach is well-motivated and effective. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their method by achieving state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR dataset. This achievement is significant, as it provides strong evidence that incorporating feedback and attention mechanisms is a promising direction for future research in computer vision.

The quality and clarity of the paper are good, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning and understand the technical details of their approach. The use of reinforcement learning to drive feedback attention in a convnet is a compelling and well-executed idea. The results presented in the paper provide strong support for the authors' claims, and the overall approach is well-suited to tackling complex computer vision tasks.

A potential direction for future work could be to apply this approach to larger and more challenging datasets, such as ImageNet. This would provide further evidence of the method's scalability and effectiveness in real-world applications. Overall, the paper presents a strong contribution to the field of computer vision, and the authors' approach is well worth further exploration and development.
