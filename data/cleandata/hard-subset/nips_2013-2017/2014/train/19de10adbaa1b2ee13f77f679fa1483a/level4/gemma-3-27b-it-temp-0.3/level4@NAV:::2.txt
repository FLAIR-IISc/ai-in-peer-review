This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism integrated into a convolutional neural network, alongside a novel training procedure for the attention gating parameters aimed at enhancing classification performance. It tackles a significant challenge in computer vision: adaptively configuring deep processing stages under resource constraints and varying task demands. The reported classification results are compelling; however, the method largely operates as a “black box,” indicating promising research avenues without elucidating the underlying reasons for its success. The analysis of image representations lacks depth, and the role of the learned parameters – specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution – remains unclear. Despite this, the work represents an important advancement beyond conventional, large, feed-forward networks with fixed post-training parameters. The presentation is generally lucid, although some equations contain errors or require further clarification.

**Major Comments:**

The authors should thoroughly investigate the connection between their work and dynamical systems theory. The approach appears more aligned with recurrent neural networks than with Reinforcement Learning, given the immediate reward signal and deterministic policy. Training can be viewed as navigating the space of recurrent “policy” parameters (theta) to maximize the observed objective function. 

The analysis of the network’s processing of the cat image did not provide substantial insight into how the model’s new features contribute to recognition. The initial increase in layer 1 activations during the first iteration warrants investigation: does this impact the objective function, or is the output insensitive to global scaling? A more focused analysis, perhaps concentrating on a smaller image region and tracking the evolution of the gating variables within that region, could prove informative. Furthermore, examining challenging cases – those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model – or systematically manipulating the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) through further constraints would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

Algorithm 1 Description: The definition of h_M is missing. Does this represent the observation collection step? Regarding F[i] and Θ[i] within the image loop (j), is there an array accumulating image fitness values, or are they overwritten with each iteration?

Equation 11: The summation’s scope is unclear. Is Equation 8 specific to a single image and, if so, should it be indexed by *j*?

Text following Equation 11: The references to ‘x’ and ‘d’ are ambiguous.

The necessity of regularizing theta is not fully justified, as sampling from the prior distribution already encourages small values. Moreover, the final optimization step – gradient updates on the parameters following theta sampling and fitness evaluation – lacks a clear explanation of the gradient computation process and a theoretical justification (e.g., are these adjustments to hyperparameters of the posterior over policies?). 

In summary, this is an interesting approach to constructing deep recurrent networks for image recognition. The superior classification accuracy compared to standard methods suggests that the recurrent computation facilitates the extraction or focusing on more complex image features, but the lack of interpretability hinders a deeper understanding of its effectiveness.
