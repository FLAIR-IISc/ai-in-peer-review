

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections to enable internal selective attention in deep convolutional networks. DasNet extends standard Maxout networks by introducing gating mechanisms (equation 5) that dynamically modulate convolutional filter activations across sequential passes over an image, with the attention policy learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to correct misclassifications. The approach directly addresses a key limitation of stationary feedforward CNNs by modeling recurrent processing inspired by biological visual systems (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme, 2000), where top-down feedback refines initial classifications. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs by selectively enhancing/de-emphasizing features in misclassified cases (e.g., disambiguating cat/dog images as shown in Figure 3), without requiring data augmentation or retraining the base network.  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical execution is rigorous. The SNES-based policy learning effectively handles the million-dimensional parameter space (a significant challenge for conventional RL), and experiments include thorough ablation studies (e.g., dynamics analysis in Figure 2, gate information content validation in Section 6). The method is well-motivated by biological plausibility and demonstrates clear empirical improvements over strong baselines.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with precise mathematical formulations (equations 1–11), clear algorithmic description (Algorithm 1), and insightful visualizations (Figure 3). The connection between biological vision (Section 5) and the technical approach is compellingly articulated.  
*Originality:* While selective attention in vision is not entirely new (e.g., Bar et al., 2003; Mnih et al., 2014), dasNet’s use of *evolutionary policy search* for *filter-level gating* in *pre-trained CNNs*—without architectural changes or data augmentation—represents a novel and practical approach. It significantly differs from prior work that uses RL for saccade learning (e.g., Schmidhuber, 2005) or simplified tasks, as it directly enhances established SOTA models.  
*Significance:* The work provides a compelling demonstration that feedback-driven internal attention is a viable path to improving CNNs, with clear implications for robustness in ambiguous classification scenarios. The 6% improvement on CIFAR-10/100 establishes a new SOTA for unaugmented data, advancing the field beyond incremental gains.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The scope is limited to CIFAR-10/100; extension to larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) would strengthen the claim of broader applicability. While the paper notes this as future work, the current validation on smaller datasets slightly limits the perceived significance for general vision systems.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Strong technical contribution with clear biological motivation, rigorous execution, state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks, and a novel solution to a well-known CNN limitation (non-adaptive feedforward processing).  
*Con:* Limited scalability to larger datasets (though this is a natural extension, not a flaw in the current work).  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to understanding and improving deep vision systems through biologically inspired feedback mechanisms, directly addressing core limitations of current CNNs. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the work aligns with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, scientifically rigorous contributions. The minor scope limitation does not detract from the paper’s substantive value.
