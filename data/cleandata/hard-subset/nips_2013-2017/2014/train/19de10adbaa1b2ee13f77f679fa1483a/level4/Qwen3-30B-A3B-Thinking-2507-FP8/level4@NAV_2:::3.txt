

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates selective attention via feedback connections within convolutional neural networks (CNNs), enabling iterative refinement of filter sensitivities during classification. This work builds upon the observation that biological visual systems employ recurrent processing (e.g., Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000), contrasting with stationary feedforward CNNs. dasNet extends Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) by learning a policy (via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to dynamically weight convolutional filters across multiple passes, correcting misclassifications without retraining the base network. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art results on unaugmented data, demonstrating significant relative improvement (6%) over vanilla CNNs.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** Proposes a genuinely novel mechanism for internal attention in CNNs through feedback-based filter weighting, distinct from prior work on recurrent networks (e.g., Behnke, 2014) or reinforcement learning for saccade control (Schmidhuber, 2002). The integration of SNES for policy evolution addresses scalability challenges in high-dimensional control spaces, a significant technical contribution.  
- **Quality:** The experimental design is rigorous, with thorough ablation studies (e.g., dynamics across T steps, gate information content) and compelling visualizations showing how filter emphasis changes correct misclassifications (e.g., cat vs. dog). Results on ambiguous CIFAR cases are well-supported.  
- **Significance:** Demonstrates tangible performance gains on standard benchmarks without data augmentation, advancing the state-of-the-art. The framework offers a practical path to "corrective thinking" in CNNs, with potential for broader application in vision tasks.  
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear algorithmic descriptions and effective visualizations. The connection between biological inspiration and technical implementation is persuasive.

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Scalability:** The absence of results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet/ILSVRC) limits assessment of the method’s generalizability. While CIFAR-10/100 are challenging, the lack of validation on more complex, real-world data (with higher resolution and class diversity) is a notable gap for significance.  
- **Failure Analysis:** The paper does not systematically investigate cases where dasNet fails (e.g., specific misclassification patterns it cannot correct), which would strengthen the claim about the robustness of the attention mechanism.  
- **Reinforcement Learning Context:** The justification for SNES over standard RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) is adequate but could better contextualize why conventional RL fails at this scale (beyond "million-dimensional parameter space").

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel architecture, strong CIFAR results, rigorous evaluation of attention dynamics, and clear biological motivation.  
*Con:* Limited scalability validation (no ILSVRC results) and insufficient failure analysis.  

The work is technically sound, original, and significant for the vision community, but the lack of larger-scale validation slightly diminishes its broader impact. The authors should address scalability in future work; however, the current results are sufficient for acceptance at NIPS given the methodological novelty and strong CIFAR performance.
