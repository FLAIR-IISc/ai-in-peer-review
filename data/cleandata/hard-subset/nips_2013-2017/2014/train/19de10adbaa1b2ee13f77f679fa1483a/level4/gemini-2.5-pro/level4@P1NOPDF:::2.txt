This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a convolutional neural network, proposing a method for training the attention's gating parameters to optimize the network's classification performance. The work addresses the important problem of how to adaptively reconfigure deep, complex processing stages when computational resources are limited or when different tasks need to be solved. While the classification results are impressive, the method itself remains a black box; it points toward promising research directions but offers little insight into *why* it is effective. The analysis of the learned image representations is superficial, and the paper fails to provide an intuitive understanding of the role of the learned parameters (i.e., the mean and variance of the theta distribution). Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with parameters that are fixed after learning. The exposition is generally clear, though several equations appear to contain typos or require further explanation.

**Major Comments:**

*   I would encourage the authors to explore the connection to dynamical systems. The proposed framework seems more closely related to recurrent neural networks than to reinforcement learning. Given that the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, the training process is effectively a direct optimization of the objective function with respect to the recurrent "policy" parameters, theta.

*   The analysis of the network processing the cat image was not particularly helpful for understanding how the model's novel components contribute to recognition. For instance, why do layer 1 activations all increase during the first iteration? Does this change affect the objective function, or is the output invariant to such global scalings? A more insightful analysis could involve focusing on a smaller image region and examining the evolution of its corresponding gating variables. It would also be compelling to analyze difficult classification cases—for example, images that are misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model. Further experiments that tease out the effect of the learned model parameters (the mean and variance of theta) by constraining them could also provide valuable insights.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The variable h_M is used without being defined. Does this refer to the step of collecting observations? Also, F[i] and Θ[i] are defined inside the loop over images (j). Are these values collected in an array, or are they overwritten in each iteration?

*   **Eq. 11:** It is unclear what the sum is over.

*   **Eq. 8:** Is this equation intended for a single image? If so, perhaps it should be indexed by j.

*   **Text after Eq. 11:** The variables 'x' and 'd' are not defined in this context.

*   **Regularization:** The motivation for regularizing theta is unclear, as it is sampled from a prior and should therefore already tend toward small values.

*   **Optimization:** The final optimization step—updating parameters based on gradients after sampling and evaluating the fitness of different thetas—is not well-explained or theoretically justified. Specifically, how are these gradients computed? Is the procedure effectively adjusting the hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies?

In summary, this paper presents an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The model's high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation is beneficial for extracting or focusing on more complex image features. However, the black-box nature of the model prevents a deeper understanding of why it is so effective.
