This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a model that incorporates feedback connections into a standard convolutional neural network (CNN) to enable a form of selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can sequentially modulate the activity of its own convolutional filters over several iterations to refine its classification. This attentional control policy is trained using reinforcement learning, specifically Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), on top of a pre-trained Maxout network. The work builds upon the extensive literature on deep feedforward CNNs for vision, but distinguishes itself by introducing a dynamic, recurrent processing mechanism, drawing inspiration from theories of attentional phases in human visual processing.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper takes an important and novel step beyond the dominant paradigm of large, static, feedforward networks. The concept of training a policy to dynamically modulate internal feature maps to improve classification is a compelling direction for future research.
*   **Performance:** The method achieves impressive, state-of-the-art classification accuracy on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. This demonstrates that the proposed recurrent attentional mechanism provides a tangible performance benefit over a strong baseline.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the main ideas and architecture of the dasNet model.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Lack of Interpretability:** A significant weakness is that the method operates as a "black box." While the results are strong, the paper offers little insight into *why* the method is effective. The analysis of the cat image, for instance, is not particularly revealing, and there is no clear explanation of what the learned policy parameters (the mean and variance of the SNES distribution) represent or how they influence the attentional process.
*   **Theoretical Framing:** The framing of the problem as reinforcement learning seems somewhat forced. Given that the policy is deterministic and the reward is observed immediately after a fixed number of steps, the approach more closely resembles the training of a recurrent neural network or a dynamical system. Exploring this connection more deeply could provide a stronger theoretical foundation.
*   **Methodological Clarity:** Several key details of the training algorithm and objective function are not fully explained or justified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel architecture that successfully integrates recurrent, attentional feedback into a state-of-the-art CNN.
*   Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (unaugmented CIFAR datasets), demonstrating the practical value of the approach.
*   Addresses the important problem of moving beyond purely feedforward processing, which is a significant research direction for the community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work lacks a deep analysis of *why* the model works, limiting its scientific contribution to a demonstration of performance.
*   The connection to reinforcement learning is not fully developed, and the model could be more accurately framed as a recurrent network.
*   Key details regarding the optimization procedure and certain equations are unclear.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
*   The analysis of the cat image could be more insightful. For example, why do the activations in layer 1 appear to be globally increased? Is the network's output invariant to such global scaling of activations? A more helpful analysis might focus on a small, ambiguous region of an image and track the evolution of the gating variables (`a`) in that specific region over the T steps. It would also be highly beneficial to analyze cases where the baseline Maxout network fails but dasNet succeeds.
*   Could you elaborate on the connection to dynamical systems? The iterative process seems very similar to unrolling a recurrent network. A discussion comparing this approach to traditional RNN training (like BPTT) would be valuable, even if to justify the choice of SNES for this large parameter space.
*   Please clarify the following points in the methodology:
    *   In Algorithm 1, what does `h(Mt)` represent? It appears to be the observation collection step but is not defined.
    *   Also in Algorithm 1, `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` are updated within the inner loop over images (`j`). Does this mean fitness values are overwritten for each image in the batch, or are they accumulated?
    *   In Equation 11, what is the summation over? It seems Equation 8 defines the loss `L` for a single image, so should `Li` in Eq. 11 be indexed by `j` (for images)?
    *   What is the justification for the L2 regularization on `θ`? Since `θ` is sampled from a Gaussian distribution maintained by SNES, the optimization process already inherently penalizes large parameter values.
    *   The SNES update step is cited but not explained. A brief description of how the fitness values `F` are used to update the distribution parameters `µ` and `Σ` would improve the paper's self-containedness.
