

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that endows CNNs with dynamic, feedback-driven internal attention mechanisms. dasNet extends stationary feedforward CNNs by adding trainable feedback connections that modulate convolutional filter sensitivities during classification, enabling iterative refinement of feature representations. The authors train a policy for these attention gates using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), circumventing the intractability of conventional RL in high-dimensional spaces (over 1 million parameters). On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art results for unaugmented datasets, outperforming prior work by 6% relative improvement. This work directly engages with NIPS themes: it builds upon Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013) and leverages reinforcement learning principles, while addressing a core limitation of feedforward CNNs—rigid, unchanging feature processing post-training. It complements recent NIPS work on recurrent processing (e.g., Wyatte et al., NIPS 2013) but distinguishes itself by applying this paradigm to *existing* state-of-the-art CNNs rather than designing new architectures from scratch.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality and Significance (High):* dasNet tackles a fundamental gap in CNN design—static filter weights during inference—by proposing a biologically inspired, feedback-driven attention mechanism. The approach is conceptually novel: instead of modifying network architecture, it dynamically reweights *existing* features via learned policy. The empirical results are compelling: dasNet sets a new SOTA on CIFAR-10/100 without data augmentation, demonstrating tangible significance for the field. This work clearly advances the state of the art by showing that *post-training iterative refinement* can boost performance in a computationally feasible manner.  
*Quality (High):* The technical execution is robust. The SNES approach is well-motivated for the high-dimensional policy space, and experiments are thorough (CIFAR-10/100, ablation studies, dynamics analysis). The training procedure (Algorithm 1) is clearly described, and the focus on *ambiguous cases* (where misclassifications occur) is insightful. The results on the cat/dog example (Fig. 3) effectively illustrate the mechanism.  
*Clarity (Moderate-High):* The paper is well-structured and generally clear. The motivation and methodology are accessible, though some equations (e.g., Eq. 5, 11) need minor clarification. The exposition of the attention mechanism is particularly strong.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Interpretability and Theoretical Grounding (Critical):* The work’s primary limitation is a lack of interpretability. While dasNet achieves strong results, the paper fails to explain *why* the attention mechanism works. Why do certain filters get emphasized? How do gating dynamics relate to classification errors? The analysis of the cat image (Fig. 3) shows activation changes but does not link them to *why* those specific changes improve recognition (e.g., "layer 1 activations increase" without explaining *how this resolves the misclassification"). The claim that "filter activations are stable" (Eq. 7) is insufficient; the paper should quantify how gating parameters (θ) correlate with classification accuracy or feature importance. Crucially, the connection to dynamical systems is underexplored—this is fundamentally a *recurrent* process (iterative refinement), not standard RL (deterministic policy, immediate reward), yet the RL framing is dominant. A brief discussion of how dasNet relates to recurrent neural networks (e.g., as a "policy-driven" recurrence) would strengthen the theoretical basis.  
*Methodological Detail (Moderate):* The regularization in Eq. 11 (λL₂∥θ∥²) is not justified—θ is already sampled from a distribution that should encourage small values. The optimization step (line 17, Algorithm 1) lacks explanation of *how* the natural gradient is computed from fitness values. The text also ambiguously references "x" and "d" in Eq. 11 (should be "x_j" and "d_j" for image j).  
*Related Work (Minor Gap):* While the related work section covers neuroscience well, it overlooks recent NIPS papers on *interpretable* attention (e.g., Zeiler & Fergus, NIPS 2014), which could have framed dasNet’s interpretability challenge more precisely.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking approach to dynamic CNN inference; strong empirical results on standard benchmarks; technically sound implementation; addresses a clear, important limitation of feedforward networks.  
*Con:* Lacks interpretability of *why* the method works; superficial treatment of the dynamical systems perspective; minor technical gaps in methodology description.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**  
This paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that *internal attention*—not just architectural changes—can enhance CNN performance. The 6% improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is substantial and reproducible. While the interpretability gap is notable, the work provides a valuable new direction for future research (e.g., "What features does dasNet attend to?"). The authors should address the points above in revision (particularly the dynamical systems framing and gating parameter analysis), but the core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper’s significance outweighs its current interpretability limitations, as it opens a promising path toward more adaptive, human-like vision systems. It is a clear advancement beyond static CNNs and aligns well with NIPS’ focus on principled, impactful machine learning.
