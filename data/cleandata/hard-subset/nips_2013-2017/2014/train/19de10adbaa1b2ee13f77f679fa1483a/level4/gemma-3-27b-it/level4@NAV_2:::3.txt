The authors propose Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel CNN architecture incorporating top-down processing via attention-based feedback connections. This allows the network to dynamically adjust its filter sensitivities during classification, effectively implementing selective attention. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, outperforming previous models on unaugmented datasets.

This paper presents an interesting and novel approach to CNN design, moving beyond purely feedforward architectures to incorporate biologically-inspired feedback mechanisms. The concept of learning to selectively attend to internal filters is compelling and aligns with observations of human visual processing [16, 17]. The use of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to train the attentional policy is a pragmatic solution to the challenges posed by the large parameter space. The visualization of filter activations and their changes over time (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the network’s behavior. The authors effectively build upon existing work in Maxout networks [7] and reinforcement learning [23, 24].

A key strength of this work is its attempt to bridge the gap between biologically plausible models of attention [28, 29, 30] and practical improvements in CNN performance. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that dasNet can correct misclassifications by emphasizing and de-emphasizing relevant filters. The dynamic analysis (Figure 2) provides further evidence of the network’s adaptive capabilities. 

However, a significant weakness is the limited scale of the experiments. While the results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are promising, demonstrating performance on a larger, more challenging dataset such as ImageNet [6] is crucial to establish the scalability and generalizability of dasNet. Without such a demonstration, it remains unclear whether the benefits observed on smaller datasets will translate to real-world applications. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the complexity introduced by the feedback connections, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements of dasNet compared to standard CNNs would be beneficial. The paper could also benefit from a discussion of potential failure modes or scenarios where the learned attention policy might be detrimental.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture incorporating biologically-inspired feedback mechanisms.
*   State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
*   Convincing demonstration of selective attention through visualization and dynamic analysis.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in deep learning – improving robustness and interpretability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability demonstrated – lack of results on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
*   Missing analysis of computational cost and memory requirements.
*   Insufficient discussion of potential failure modes.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. While the lack of large-scale experiments is a concern, the novelty and potential of the proposed approach warrant consideration. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the scalability concern, perhaps through additional experiments or a more detailed theoretical analysis.
