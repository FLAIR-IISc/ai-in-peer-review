This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a convolutional neural network and presents a method for training the corresponding gating parameters to optimize classification performance. The work addresses the important problem of how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing stages when computational resources are limited or when different tasks need to be solved. While the classification results are impressive, the method itself remains a black box. The paper suggests a promising research direction but provides little insight into *why* the proposed approach is effective. The analysis of the learned image representations is not particularly revealing, and it is unclear how the learned parameters of the policy distribution (i.e., the mean and variance of theta) influence the model's behavior. Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the standard paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with parameters that are fixed after training. The paper is generally well-written, although several equations contain typographical errors or require clearer explanations.

**Major Comments:**

*   The authors should more seriously explore the connection between their model and dynamical systems. The proposed framework appears more closely related to recurrent neural networks (RNNs) than to reinforcement learning. Given that the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, the training procedure can be viewed as a traversal through the space of recurrent policy parameters (theta) to directly maximize the objective function. This framing seems more appropriate than the RL perspective.
*   The analysis of the network processing the cat image was not particularly helpful for understanding how the model's novel components contribute to recognition. For instance, why do all layer 1 activations increase during the first iteration? Does this have a meaningful impact on the objective function, or is the final output simply invariant to such global scalings? A more instructive analysis might involve focusing on a smaller image region and examining the evolution of its corresponding gating variables. Furthermore, it would be insightful to analyze difficult cases, such as images misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model. Another valuable experiment would be to tease out the specific effects of the learned policy parameters (mean and variance of theta) by, for example, constraining them during training.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the description of Algorithm 1: What does `h_M` represent? Is this the step for collecting observations? Also, `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` are updated within the inner loop over images (`j`). Does this mean their values are overwritten on each iteration, or are they collected in an array? The notation is ambiguous.
*   Equation 8: Does this equation apply to a single image? If so, it should probably be indexed by `j`.
*   Equation 11: The summation index is missing. Over what variable is the sum performed?
*   In the text following Equation 11, the variables 'x' and 'd' are used without being defined.
*   The motivation for regularizing theta is unclear. Since theta is sampled from a prior, this should already encourage small values. More importantly, the final optimization step—updating the policy parameters via gradients after sampling and evaluating different thetas—is not well-explained. The paper should clarify how these gradients are computed and provide a theoretical justification for the update rule (e.g., is this equivalent to adjusting the hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies?).

This paper presents an interesting approach for building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The model's high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation is beneficial for extracting or focusing on more complex image features. However, the "black box" nature of the work fails to provide a clear explanation for *why* the method is so effective.
