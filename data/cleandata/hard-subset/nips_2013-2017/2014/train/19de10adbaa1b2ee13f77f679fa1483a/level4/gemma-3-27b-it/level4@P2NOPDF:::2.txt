This paper introduces selective attention within a convolutional neural network framework and presents a novel method for learning gating parameters to optimize classification performance. It tackles a crucial problem in computer vision: adaptively configuring deep processing stages under resource constraints and varying task demands. The reported classification results are compelling; however, the method remains largely opaque, outlining promising avenues for research without providing substantial insight into *why* it functions effectively. While the analysis of image representation is attempted, it proves unrevealing, and the role of the learned parameters – specifically, the mean and variance of the theta distribution – remains unclear. Despite this, the work represents an important progression beyond traditional, large, fixed-parameter feed-forward networks. The exposition is generally clear, although some equations contain errors or require further clarification.

**Major Comments:**

The authors should more deeply investigate the connection between their approach and dynamical systems theory. The proposed method appears more closely aligned with recurrent neural networks than with Reinforcement Learning. The observed reward is immediate, and the policy is deterministic, suggesting that training involves navigating the space of recurrent “policy” parameters (theta) to maximize the observed objective function. 

The analysis of the network processing the cat image did not effectively illuminate how the introduced attention mechanism improves recognition performance. The initial increase in layer 1 activations during the first iteration is unexplained; it's unclear whether this impacts the objective function or if the output is invariant to global scaling. A more focused analysis—perhaps concentrating on a smaller image region and tracking the evolution of the gating variables within that region—could prove more informative. Additionally, examining challenging cases—images misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model—or systematically investigating the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) through further constraints would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The definition of h<sub>M</sub> is missing. Does this represent the observation collection step? Furthermore, the roles of F[i] and Θ[i] within the image loop (indexed by *j*) are unclear. Are image fitness values accumulated in an array, or are they overwritten in each iteration?
*   **Equation 11:** The summation is not defined. Is Equation 8 presented for a single image and should therefore be indexed by *j*?
*   **Text Following Equation 11:** The meaning of ‘x’ and ‘d’ is not explained.
*   **Regularization of Theta:** The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, given that it is already sampled from a prior distribution and therefore naturally tends toward smaller values. The final optimization step—gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas—is poorly explained (the gradient computation method is unspecified) and lacks theoretical justification (is this process adjusting hyperparameters of the posterior over policies?).



In summary, this is an interesting approach to building deep, recurrent networks for image recognition. The achieved classification accuracy, exceeding that of standard methods, suggests that the recurrent computation contributes to the extraction or focusing on more complex image features. However, the lack of interpretability hinders a complete understanding of *why* this approach is successful.
