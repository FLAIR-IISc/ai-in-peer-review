Summary
This paper proposes a convolutional network with feedback connections that implement internal selective attention. The feedback acts by gating the activations of convolutional filters over multiple passes on the same image, allowing the network to emphasize or de-emphasize particular feature maps during inference. The policy that controls these gates is learned with reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—on top of a pretrained Maxout CNN. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, the approach delivers state-of-the-art performance on unaugmented data, improving over a strong baseline. This direction aligns well with both biological evidence for recurrent, top-down processing and prior machine learning work on attention and RL, while applying it in a modern deep CNN setting.

Strengths
- The overall approach—augmenting a strong feedforward CNN with an RL-driven feedback mechanism for internal attention—is compelling and, in my view, the right direction for advancing visual recognition.
- The method is carefully implemented on top of a competitive Maxout architecture and shows clear empirical gains, achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR, which effectively validates the idea.
- The paper is clearly written and technically sound. The design choices (e.g., modulating filter sensitivities via gates and learning the control policy with SNES) are sensible given the large parameter space.

Weaknesses
- No single component is particularly novel in isolation (CNNs, attention/gating, and RL have each been studied), and the contribution is primarily in the combination and successful application.
- The evaluation is limited to CIFAR-scale datasets. It would strengthen the paper to scale the approach to larger, more challenging benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) if possible.

Quality
The work appears technically sound. The feedback mechanism is well integrated into the CNN via multiplicative gating of feature maps, and the use of SNES is justified by the high-dimensional policy space. The empirical evaluation is appropriate for the stated goal and demonstrates consistent improvements over a strong baseline. The claims are supported by quantitative results.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and easy to follow. The problem setup, the gating mechanism, the policy architecture, and the training loop are described clearly. The presentation of results is straightforward and convincing.

Originality
While the individual ingredients (CNNs, attention/feedback, RL) are known, the paper’s value lies in combining them effectively in a modern deep architecture and demonstrating measurable gains on standard benchmarks. In that sense, the conceptual novelty is limited, but the integration and empirical validation are meaningful.

Significance
The results are important as they empirically confirm that feedback/attention mechanisms can improve classification in contemporary CNNs. This supports the broader view that moving beyond purely feedforward inference is a promising path forward. The demonstrated gains on CIFAR make a persuasive case for further exploration and scaling.

Pros
- Strong empirical performance with state-of-the-art results on CIFAR (unaugmented).
- Clear, well-motivated architecture combining CNNs with RL-driven internal attention.
- Good paper quality and readability.

Cons
- Limited novelty in the components; contribution is primarily integrative.
- Evaluation restricted to small-scale datasets; scalability to larger problems remains to be shown.

Recommendation
A very good paper that takes a sensible and promising approach—convnets with RL-driven feedback attention—and validates it convincingly. If possible, extending the evaluation to a larger dataset like ImageNet would further strengthen the contribution.
