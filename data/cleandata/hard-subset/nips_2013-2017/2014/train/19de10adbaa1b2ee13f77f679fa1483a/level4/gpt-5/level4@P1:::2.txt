This paper augments a convolutional neural network (CNN) with selective, internal attention mediated by feedback connections, and trains gating parameters to adapt filter sensitivities across multiple passes over the same image. The CNN’s forward path is a Maxout network; the new component is a deterministic policy that, based on observations derived from intermediate activations and class probabilities, outputs per-map gate values to modulate subsequent passes. Because the policy has close to a million parameters, it is trained by Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) rather than standard RL. On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), the approach improves over a strong Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to several threads: biologically inspired feedback and top-down modulation; recurrent processing for iterative refinement; and attention mechanisms. It builds directly on Maxout CNNs and dropout, and differs from prior selective-attention work by learning feedback that scales internal feature maps within a modern deep CNN, trained end-to-end in two stages (supervised pretraining, then policy evolution). While framed as reinforcement learning, the training essentially performs black-box policy search over a recurrently used control module.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important limitation of purely feedforward CNNs by enabling adaptive, sequential processing.
- Demonstrates notable accuracy gains on CIFAR-10/100 without data augmentation, which suggests practical significance.
- The implementation is conceptually simple (gating feature maps) and is compatible with strong CNN architectures.

Weaknesses:
- The method functions as a black box: it is unclear why or when the learned feedback helps, and what the policy parameters (mean and variance of θ under SNES) are doing.
- The analysis of internal representations (e.g., the cat example) is not very informative; qualitative figures do not elucidate mechanism.
- Several algorithmic and notational details are unclear or contain typos, hindering reproducibility.
- The link to reinforcement learning is somewhat superficial; the immediate reward and deterministic policy make this closer to direct black-box optimization of a recurrent controller than to sequential decision making with delayed rewards.

Major comments:
- Please more seriously connect this work to dynamical systems and recurrent networks. As presented, training performs a traversal through the space of recurrent “policy” parameters θ to maximize an immediate objective; the RL framing is not essential. Clarify why RL terminology is used here and whether any delayed-credit assignment is involved.
- The image analysis (cat vs. dog) does not clarify the mechanism. Why are layer-1 activations globally increased at the first iteration? Is the output invariant to global scaling of gates? A localized analysis—e.g., focusing on a small region and tracking the evolution of the corresponding gates and activations—could be more revealing. More compelling would be a systematic study of hard cases: instances misclassified by the Maxout baseline but corrected by dasNet, with an ablation or constraint study on the learned θ distribution (e.g., limiting variance, fixing mean) to tease out which aspects of the policy matter.
- Please report what the learned SNES mean and variance imply about the policy (e.g., are gates near identity with sparse deviations; are certain layers consistently up/down-weighted?).

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1: h(Mt) is used but not defined. Is this the step that collects observations (average map activations, classification-layer activations, class probabilities)? Please define h explicitly.
- Algorithm 1 indexing: F[i] and Θ[i] appear inside the loop over images j. Are per-image losses accumulated into a single Li per policy, and is F[i] the aggregate fitness after summing over images? As written, it seems these might be overwritten each iteration.
- Equation (11): specify the summation index (over images j in the batch). If Eq. (8) is per-image, it should be indexed accordingly (e.g., Lj).
- Text after Eq. (11): clarify what x and d refer to (input image and its label).
- Regularization of θ: since θ is sampled from a Gaussian search distribution that will concentrate as SNES learns, why is an explicit L2 penalty on θ needed? Please justify its role. Also, the final optimization step—updating the distribution parameters via the natural gradient—needs clearer explanation. How exactly are gradients of expected fitness computed in SNES here, and conceptually, are you adjusting hyperparameters of the search distribution (a posterior over policies) rather than policy parameters directly? A brief derivation or pointer would help.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically plausible and results are strong, but clarity issues (notation/algorithm details) and the lack of diagnostic ablations limit confidence in understanding and reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but equations contain typos/ambiguities, and key functions/indices need clearer definitions. The interpretability analysis is weak.
- Originality: Introducing learned internal attention via feedback gates within a state-of-the-art CNN, trained via SNES, is a novel and timely combination.
- Significance: The performance gains suggest that recurrent/attentive computation can improve recognition. The approach could influence follow-on work on feedback in deep vision models.

Pros for acceptance:
- Demonstrates measurable, state-of-the-art improvements on standard benchmarks with a simple, general mechanism.
- Advances the paradigm from fixed feedforward inference to adaptive, iterative processing.
- Potentially impactful direction for integrating attention and feedback in deep CNNs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited insight into why the method works; analysis is largely qualitative and not convincing.
- Several presentation issues hinder reproducibility and theoretical understanding of the training procedure.
- The RL framing may be overstated relative to what is actually optimized.

Overall: An interesting and promising approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The improved accuracy indicates that iterative, feedback-driven attention can help extract or focus on more complex features. However, the current paper reads as a high-performing black box. Strengthening the technical clarity and providing deeper analysis of the learned dynamics would significantly improve the contribution.
