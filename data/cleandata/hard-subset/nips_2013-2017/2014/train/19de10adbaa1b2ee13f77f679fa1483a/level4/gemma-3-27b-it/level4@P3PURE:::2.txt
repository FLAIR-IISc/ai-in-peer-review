This paper introduces selective attention to a convolutional neural network and develops a method for training gating parameters for this attention mechanism to optimize classification performance. It addresses an important problem in computer vision – how to adaptively reconfigure complex, deep processing stages when resources are limited and different tasks require diverse solutions. The classification results are impressive; however, the method remains largely a black box, suggesting promising directions for future work without fully elucidating *why* it functions effectively. The analysis of image representation is not particularly revealing, and the meaning of the learned parameters (the mean and variance of the theta distribution) remains obscure. Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, fixed-parameter, feed-forward networks. The exposition is generally clear, although some equations contain typos or require further explanation.

**Major Comments:**

I encourage the authors to seriously explore the connection between their work and dynamical systems. The approach appears more closely aligned with recurrent networks than with Reinforcement Learning. The reward is observed immediately, and the policy is deterministic; thus, training involves a traversal through the space of recurrent “policy” parameters, theta, to maximize the observed objective function. 

The analysis of the network processing the cat image did not provide substantial insight into how the novel features of the model contribute to recognition performance. For instance, why are layer 1 activations uniformly increased during the first iteration? Does this influence the objective function, or is the output invariant to global scalings? A more focused analysis, concentrating on a smaller region of the image and tracing the evolution of the gating variables within that region, could be instructive. Furthermore, investigating difficult cases – those misclassified by a standard Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model – or probing the effect of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) by applying further constraints would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

Algorithm 1 description: Where is h_M defined? Does this represent the step of collecting observations? The indices for F[i] and Θ[i] appear within the loop over images (j); are the image fitness values collected in an array or overwritten with each iteration?

Eqn 11: What does the summation represent? Is Eqn 8 defined for a single image and, if so, should it be indexed by j?

Text following Eqn 11: What do 'x' and 'd' refer to?

The necessity of regularizing theta is unclear, as theta is already sampled from a prior distribution and would naturally tend towards small values. Moreover, the final step of the optimization process – the gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas – is not well explained (how are the gradients computed?) or theoretically justified (are these adjustments to hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies?).

In summary, this is an interesting approach to building deep recurrent networks for image recognition. The high classification accuracy compared to standard methods suggests that the recurrent computation facilitates the extraction or focuses attention on more complex image features. However, the method’s effectiveness remains unexplained due to its black-box nature.
