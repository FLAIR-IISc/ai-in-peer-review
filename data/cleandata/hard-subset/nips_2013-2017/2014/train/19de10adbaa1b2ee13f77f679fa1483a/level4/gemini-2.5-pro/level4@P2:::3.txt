This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. The central idea is to mimic the iterative, attentional processing of the human visual system. After an initial feedforward pass, dasNet uses a learned policy to modulate the activity of its convolutional filters over subsequent passes on the same image. This mechanism allows the network to dynamically focus its "internal attention" on discriminative features, with the goal of improving classification accuracy, particularly on ambiguous or difficult examples. The control policy for this attentional mechanism is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to manage the high-dimensional parameter space of the feedback connections. The authors apply this method to a state-of-the-art Maxout network and demonstrate superior performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets for unaugmented data.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual novelty. While most contemporary work focused on deeper and more efficient feedforward architectures, this paper proposes a well-motivated departure by incorporating top-down, recurrent processing. The analogy to human attentive vision is compelling and provides a strong foundation for the model's design. This forward-looking approach of combining bottom-up feature extraction with top-down attentional modulation is a significant contribution.
*   **Novel Technical Approach:** The combination of a deep CNN with reinforcement learning to train an internal attention policy is highly original. The use of SNES to learn a policy in a million-dimensional parameter space is an ambitious and effective solution to a challenging problem that would be intractable for many standard RL algorithms.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 benchmarks (without data augmentation). This demonstrates that the proposed attentional mechanism is not just a theoretical novelty but provides a tangible performance improvement over a very strong baseline (the Maxout network).
*   **Good Qualitative Analysis:** The authors provide valuable experiments to build intuition for the model's behavior. The visualization of how filter activations are emphasized or suppressed over time for a specific image (Figure 3) is particularly insightful. The analysis of the information content in the final gate values further supports the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful, task-relevant strategy.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability and Generalization:** The main concern with this work is the question of scalability. While results on CIFAR-10/100 are impressive, these datasets consist of small (32x32) images. The proposed method is significantly more complex and computationally expensive than a standard feedforward pass, requiring a separate RL training phase and multiple forward passes during inference. To truly establish the significance and practicality of this approach, an evaluation on a large-scale, high-resolution dataset like ILSVRC would be necessary. It is unclear if the benefits observed on CIFAR would translate to more complex visual recognition tasks, or if the computational overhead would become prohibitive.
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper effectively demonstrates cases where the model succeeds in correcting an initial misclassification. However, a more complete analysis would also investigate the model's limitations and failure modes. Are there instances where the iterative "thinking" process actually degrades performance or fixates on incorrect features? Understanding when and why the attention policy fails would provide deeper insight into the method's robustness and could guide future improvements.
*   **Complexity:** The two-stage training process (supervised pre-training of the CNN, followed by RL training of the policy) adds significant complexity. A more detailed discussion of the interplay between these two stages, potential sensitivities to hyperparameters, and the overall training stability would strengthen the paper's contribution from a practical standpoint.

**Summary:**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that introduces a novel and conceptually appealing idea: enabling a CNN to iteratively refine its predictions through an internal, learned attention mechanism. The combination of deep learning with a sophisticated reinforcement learning technique is original, and the state-of-the-art results on CIFAR demonstrate its effectiveness. The primary weakness is the lack of evaluation on larger-scale datasets, which leaves the scalability and general applicability of this more complex model as an open question. Despite this concern, the originality of the approach and the strength of the existing results make this a valuable contribution to the field that is likely to inspire further research into recurrent and attentional mechanisms in deep networks.
