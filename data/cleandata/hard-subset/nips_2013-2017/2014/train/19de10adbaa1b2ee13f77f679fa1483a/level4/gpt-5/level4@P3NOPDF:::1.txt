Summary
The paper proposes a convolutional network augmented with top-down feedback connections that implement visual attention by gating the activations of selected filters. The gating policy is learned with reinforcement learning, enabling the network to adaptively modulate intermediate representations conditioned on the input. While the components themselves are not individually novel, the integration is compelling, and the empirical results on CIFAR are strong, reportedly achieving state-of-the-art performance. The work provides concrete evidence that learned feedback/attention can improve feedforward convnets.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: incorporating feedback for attention is a principled and timely direction for improving vision models.
- Methodological soundness: gating filter activations is a simple, interpretable mechanism for top-down modulation.
- Empirical impact: strong results on CIFAR substantiate the claim that learned attention improves accuracy over comparable feedforward baselines.
- Clarity: the paper is generally well written, and the overall approach and training scheme are understandable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: the individual elements (convnets, attention via gating, reinforcement learning for control) are known; the contribution is primarily in the integration and empirical validation. This is acceptable if the evaluation is thorough.
- Scope of evaluation: results are limited to CIFAR. Given the claim that feedback/attention is a promising next step, testing at larger scale (e.g., ImageNet) or on more challenging settings would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
- Baselines and ablations: it is important to compare against capacity-matched feedforward models (e.g., deeper/wider convnets with similar parameter count and computational budget) and against alternative attention mechanisms (e.g., soft, spatial, or learned gating without RL). Detailed ablations (no-feedback, random or heuristic gates, supervised gates, varying number/placement of gates) would clarify where the gains come from.
- Training complexity and stability: reinforcement learning can introduce variance and training instability. The paper would benefit from reporting training cost, convergence behavior, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and variance across runs.
- Computational overhead: feedback steps may incur additional latency and compute. A careful analysis of test-time cost versus accuracy would help position the approach relative to purely feedforward baselines.

Requests for clarification
- What is the action/state space for the gating policy, and how is it parameterized? Are gates continuous (soft) or discrete (hard), and how is credit assigned through them?
- How many feedback steps are used during training and inference, and are they the same? How does performance change with more or fewer steps?
- Are the gates constrained (e.g., nonnegativity, sparsity) and is there any regularization to encourage parsimony or stability?
- What variance-reduction or exploration strategies are used in RL training, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- How are baseline convnets matched in depth/width and training budget to ensure a fair comparison?

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on at least one larger or more diverse dataset (e.g., ImageNet or a high-resolution benchmark) to demonstrate scalability and generality.
- Provide comprehensive ablations and comparisons to alternative attention mechanisms and capacity-matched baselines.
- Include training and inference cost analyses (FLOPs, wall-clock time, and memory), and discuss trade-offs between feedback depth and performance.
- Visualize the learned attention/gates and provide qualitative analyses showing when and where the model attends; analyze failure cases.
- Report robustness tests (e.g., occlusion, noise, distribution shift) to probe whether feedback attention improves resilience.
- Discuss potential extensions or limitations, such as application to detection/segmentation or compatibility with modern architectures.

Significance
Even with incremental novelty, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by demonstrating that learned feedback-driven attention can deliver tangible gains on standard vision benchmarks. The results and analysis (if expanded as suggested) would help steer the community toward architectures that move beyond purely feedforward processing.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely paper: the idea is well motivated, the method is sensible and interpretable, and the empirical results on CIFAR are strong. The main limitations are the restricted scope of evaluation and the need for deeper ablations and cost analyses. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the above points and, if possible, adding larger-scale experiments or more extensive ablations.
