This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a convolutional neural network and proposes a method for training the attention's gating parameters to optimize classification performance. The work addresses the important problem of how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing stages under resource constraints and for varying tasks. While the classification results are impressive, the model's internal workings remain opaque. The paper points toward promising research directions but provides little insight into the reasons for its effectiveness. The analysis of the learned image representations is not particularly illuminating, and the role of the learned parameters for the `theta` distribution (mean and variance) is unclear. Nevertheless, this work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with parameters that are fixed after training. The paper is generally well-written, although several equations appear to contain typographical errors or require further explanation.

**Major Comments:**

*   A more thorough exploration of the connection to dynamical systems would strengthen the paper. The proposed method appears more closely related to recurrent neural networks (RNNs) than to Reinforcement Learning (RL). Given that the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic, the training procedure can be viewed as a search through the space of recurrent policy parameters, `theta`, to maximize the objective function.
*   The analysis of the network's processing of the example "cat" image does little to clarify how the model's novel components contribute to recognition. For instance, why do all layer 1 activations increase at the first iteration? Does this change affect the objective function, or is the output invariant to such global scalings? A more instructive analysis might involve focusing on a smaller image region and examining the evolution of its gating variables. Additionally, an analysis of difficult classification cases—such as those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model—would be valuable. It would also be interesting to investigate the specific effects of the learned model parameters (mean and variance of `theta`) by constraining them during experiments.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the description of Algorithm 1, `h_M` is used without a definition. Is this the step for collecting observations? Also in Algorithm 1, `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` are updated within the inner loop over images `j`. Are the fitness values for each image collected in an array, or are they overwritten in each iteration?
*   Equation 11: The summation index is missing. What is the sum over? Furthermore, does Equation 8 apply to a single image, and should it therefore be indexed by `j`?
*   Following Equation 11, the variables `x` and `d` are not defined.
*   The motivation for regularizing `theta` is unclear. Since `theta` is sampled from a prior, it should already be biased toward small values. The final optimization step—involving gradient updates on the distribution parameters after sampling and evaluating `theta`—lacks sufficient explanation and theoretical justification. Specifically, it is unclear how these gradients are computed. Is the intention to adjust the hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies?

This paper presents an interesting approach for building deep recurrent networks for recognition. The model's high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation aids in extracting or focusing on more complex image features. However, the "black box" nature of the method is a significant limitation, as the paper does not explain the mechanisms behind its success.
