### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward convolutional neural network (a Maxout network) with feedback connections. These connections allow the network to dynamically modulate the activations of its convolutional filters over multiple processing passes on a single image. This mechanism is framed as a form of internal selective attention, enabling the network to iteratively refine its feature representation to improve classification. The control policy for this attention mechanism, which has over a million parameters, is learned using reinforcement learning—specifically with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—after the base CNN has been trained via standard supervised learning. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art classification performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (without data augmentation), particularly by correcting initial misclassifications on difficult examples.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—adding a dynamic, learned attention mechanism via feedback to a deep CNN—is highly original and significant. It represents an important step beyond the dominant paradigm of large, static, feed-forward networks whose parameters are fixed after training. The work addresses the fundamental question of how a network can adaptively allocate its computational resources during inference, drawing compelling parallels to recurrent processing in the human visual system.

*   **Performance:** The method achieves impressive empirical results, establishing a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data at the time of publication. This provides strong evidence that the proposed recurrent, attentional processing is not just an interesting theoretical idea but also a practically effective technique for improving recognition accuracy.

*   **Problem Formulation:** The application of evolution strategies to train a high-dimensional control policy for internal network states is a novel and powerful formulation. It opens up a new avenue for optimizing the runtime behavior of deep networks.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Lack of Insight / "Black Box" Nature:** While the results are strong, the paper provides limited insight into *why* the method works. The analysis of the learned attention is superficial and does not build a clear understanding of the underlying mechanism.
    *   The visualization of activation changes for the "cat" image (Figure 3) is difficult to interpret. It shows that filter activations are modulated, but it is not clear what specific visual features are being selectively amplified or suppressed, or how these changes directly lead to the corrected classification.
    *   There is no analysis of the learned policy parameters (`theta`). What has the evolution strategy discovered? A characterization of the learned mean and covariance of the policy distribution would be valuable for understanding the solution.

*   **Connection to Recurrent Networks:** The authors frame the approach within reinforcement learning, but the specific implementation seems more closely related to training a recurrent neural network (RNN). The policy is deterministic, the "environment" is the network's own internal state, and the reward is observed immediately after a fixed number of steps (`T`). This setup is equivalent to unrolling an RNN for `T` steps and training the recurrent weights (`theta`) to optimize a final objective function. Discussing this connection would provide a clearer context for the work and relate it more directly to the literature on recurrent models for vision.

*   **Suggestions for Deeper Analysis:** To move beyond a "black box" demonstration, the authors could perform more targeted analyses:
    *   It would be instructive to analyze the model's behavior on simpler, more controlled stimuli where the "correct" attentional strategy is more obvious.
    *   A more focused analysis on difficult cases—those misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but corrected by dasNet—could be very revealing. Tracing the evolution of the gating variables and class probabilities for these specific examples would provide stronger evidence of the model's corrective capabilities.

**Minor Comments and Questions for Clarification**

*   **Algorithm 1:** The function `h(Mt)`, used to construct the observation vector from the network state, is not explicitly defined in the text, though its components are described later. Please provide a clear, consolidated definition for reproducibility.

*   **Algorithm 1:** The updates to `F[i]` and `Θ[i]` (lines 14-15) appear inside the loop over images (`j`). This suggests the fitness is based only on the last image in the batch, which contradicts the cumulative score defined in Equation 11. Please clarify whether the fitness for a policy is accumulated over the entire batch of `n` images.

*   **Equation 11:** The notation in the fitness function is slightly confusing. The sum is indexed by `i`, which is also used to index the policy `θ_i`. Presumably, the sum should be over the image index `j`, and the loss `L` should be indexed by both policy `i` and image `j`. Please correct the notation for clarity.

*   **Regularization:** It is not entirely clear why L2 regularization on each sample `θ_i` (Equation 11) is necessary. Since `θ_i` is sampled from a Gaussian distribution `N(μ, Σ)`, the optimization process of SNES on `μ` already encourages parameters to remain close to a central value. Could the authors explain the motivation for this additional regularization on each sample?

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and exciting approach for incorporating recurrent, attentional processing into deep convolutional networks. The high classification accuracy demonstrates the potential of this direction. While the work is an important contribution, its impact could be significantly strengthened by a deeper analysis that provides more intuition about how and why the learned feedback mechanism improves performance. Despite this limitation, the paper introduces a promising new technique and is a valuable step towards building more dynamic and adaptive recognition systems.
