This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that integrates a form of top-down selective attention into a deep convolutional neural network. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (a Maxout network) with modulatory feedback connections that can dynamically alter the activity of convolutional filters over multiple processing passes on a single input image. This attentional mechanism is implemented as a control policy, parameterized by a neural network, which is trained via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to refine the network's initial classification. Conceptually, this work draws inspiration from the recurrent and top-down pathways in the human visual cortex and aims to scale these principles to state-of-the-art deep learning models. The authors demonstrate that dasNet achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed model is highly original. The combination of a high-performing feedforward CNN with a reinforcement-learning-trained policy for internal, iterative modulation is a novel and creative approach. It represents a significant departure from the purely feedforward architectures that dominate the field and opens up a promising new direction for research into more dynamic and deliberative neural network models.

2.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The concept of allowing a network to perform additional "internal thoughts" to refine an initial guess is powerful. This work provides a concrete and successful implementation of this idea, moving beyond static feature extraction towards a more active and adaptive processing paradigm. The biological plausibility and the potential to solve ambiguous classification cases make this a potentially impactful contribution that other researchers are likely to build upon.

3.  **Quality of Analysis:** The authors provide a strong set of experiments on the CIFAR datasets. Beyond reporting state-of-the-art error rates, they include valuable qualitative analyses that offer insight into the model's behavior. The visualization of filter modulation (Figure 3) is particularly helpful for understanding how the network corrects its initial prediction. Furthermore, the analysis of the system's dynamics over time and the experiment confirming the information content of the gate values add credibility to the claims and demonstrate a thorough investigation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability and Generalization:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental evaluation, which is limited to the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. While the results are impressive, these datasets consist of small 32x32 images. A key question for a novel architecture of this nature is its scalability to larger, more complex, and higher-resolution datasets like ILSVRC. The computational overhead of multiple passes (T=5) and the challenge of learning a million-parameter policy might become prohibitive on such a scale. Without demonstrating effectiveness on a larger benchmark, the significance and practical applicability of dasNet remain somewhat uncertain. A successful demonstration on a larger scale would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and impact.

2.  **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper effectively showcases a successful example of disambiguation (cat vs. dog). To provide a more complete picture, it would be beneficial to include an analysis of the model's failure modes. Are there specific types of images or classes where the attentional policy struggles or even degrades performance compared to the feedforward baseline? Understanding the limitations of the current approach would be as valuable as understanding its successes.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and thought-provoking paper that introduces a highly novel and interesting model. The idea of incorporating dynamic, top-down attention via reinforcement learning is compelling and well-executed. The results on CIFAR are strong and supported by solid analysis.

My main reservation is the question of scalability. The demonstrated performance on CIFAR is a significant achievement, but the true test for a new architectural paradigm in computer vision is often its performance on larger-scale problems like ImageNet. Despite this concern, the novelty of the approach and the potential for future impact are substantial. The paper makes a valuable contribution by showing that such a complex, dynamic system can be successfully trained and can outperform a strong feedforward baseline. I believe the strengths in originality and potential significance outweigh the weakness of the limited experimental scope.
