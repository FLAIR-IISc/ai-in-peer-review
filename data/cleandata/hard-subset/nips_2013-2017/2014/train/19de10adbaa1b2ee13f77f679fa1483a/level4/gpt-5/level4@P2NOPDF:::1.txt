This paper augments a convolutional network with feedback connections that implement visual attention by gating the activations of selected filters. The attention policy is learned via reinforcement learning on top of a standard convnet. While none of the individual components are entirely novel, the integration is well motivated and executed, and the empirical results are strong.

Strengths:
- Clear idea: use feedback-driven gating to focus computation on informative features.
- Sound methodology: reinforcement learning is an appropriate mechanism for learning the attention policy.
- Empirical impact: achieves state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR, providing concrete evidence that feedback/attention can improve convnet accuracy.
- Presentation: the paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty is moderate. The components (convnets, gating, and RL) are known; the main contribution is their combination and validation.
- The demonstrated scale is limited. Results on CIFAR are compelling, but it remains unclear how the approach performs on larger, more complex datasets.
- The computational overhead and training stability introduced by RL-based feedback are not fully discussed in the draft; understanding efficiency and convergence would help assess practicality.

Significance:
The results substantiate the view that learned feedback and attention are promising next steps for vision models. Demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR with this architecture is a meaningful contribution that could influence subsequent work on attention mechanisms.

Suggestions for improvement:
- If not already included, add experiments on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or more challenging benchmarks to test scalability and generalization.
- Provide ablations isolating the contribution of the feedback/gating mechanism versus baseline convnets without feedback.
- Analyze the learned attention patterns (e.g., which filters or layers are most frequently gated) to shed light on the model’s behavior.
- Report computational costs (training time, inference overhead) and discuss RL training stability and variance.

Overall assessment:
A strong and timely paper. Although the novelty lies more in the integration than in any single technical component, the approach is compelling and empirically validated. The work is well executed and clearly presented, and it advances the case for feedback-driven attention in convnets. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to extend the evaluation to larger-scale problems.
