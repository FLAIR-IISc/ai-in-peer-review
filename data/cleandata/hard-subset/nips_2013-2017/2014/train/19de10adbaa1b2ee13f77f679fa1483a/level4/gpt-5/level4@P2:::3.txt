Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture that augments standard feedforward CNNs with learned, top-down feedback to modulate internal feature maps over multiple passes on the same image. A policy network observes summary statistics of intermediate activations and class probabilities and outputs gating actions that scale convolutional filter maps, effectively implementing selective internal attention. The policy is trained post hoc via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), due to the high dimensionality of the action/observation spaces and policy parameters. Built on Maxout networks with dropout, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), particularly improving difficult, commonly confused categories. The paper relates to prior work on recurrent processing and attention in vision, and to Maxout CNNs, but is distinctive in coupling a high-capacity CNN with learned feedback trained through black-box optimization.

Strengths
- Novel architecture: A clear and interesting integration of top-down, attentional feedback with a high-performing CNN, enabling iterative refinement without changing base weights at test time.
- Sound formulation: The action/observation design, gating mechanism, and multi-pass inference are well specified; the softmax-normalized gates maintain activation stability.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with analyses showing the policy can correct initial misclassifications and stable dynamics across steps.
- Interpretability aids: Visualizations of map emphasis/de-emphasis across layers provide insight into how feedback alters internal representations.
- Forward-looking perspective: Embraces biologically inspired bidirectional processing and positions feedback as a general tool to improve CNN robustness.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability and generality: Results are limited to CIFAR-10/100. Demonstrating performance on a larger-scale dataset (e.g., ILSVRC) with higher-resolution images would strengthen claims about practicality and scalability.
- Computational overhead: Multi-pass inference and an additional policy network introduce latency; the paper would benefit from reporting inference-time costs and training compute versus the baseline.
- Limited ablations: It is unclear which observation components (e.g., pooled map activations vs. class probabilities) are most critical, or how sensitive performance is to T, the gating normalization, and policy architecture. Targeted ablations would clarify the design.
- Failure modes: Since performance hinges on the learned attention policy, examples where feedback degrades predictions or fails to help would be informative, along with discussion of robustness to adversarial or out-of-distribution inputs.
- Comparison breadth: While the unaugmented SOTA is notable, many baselines rely on augmentation or committees. A more comprehensive comparison or complementary augmentation experiments would improve fairness and context.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall. The coupling of gates, policy, and SNES is coherent, and the training objective is clearly defined. More rigorous runtime analysis and ablations would strengthen the empirical support.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with helpful algorithmic descriptions and figures. Clarifying the rationale for softmax scaling of gates and the boosting-weighted loss would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The combination of learned, top-down selective attention with Maxout CNNs and SNES policy search is original and differentiates the work from prior recurrent/attention models.
- Significance: Promising and potentially impactful if shown to scale. Demonstrated improvements on CIFAR are meaningful; broader validation on large-scale datasets would elevate significance.

Pros
- Innovative feedback mechanism that adaptively reweights internal features across time.
- Clear gains over a strong CNN baseline on CIFAR without data augmentation.
- Insightful visualizations and stability analysis across multiple passes.

Cons
- No large-scale evaluation to substantiate scalability claims.
- Added computational cost and limited analysis of efficiency.
- Missing ablations and discussion of failure cases.

Overall, this is an interesting and forward-looking paper that thoughtfully integrates top-down attention into CNNs and shows measurable benefits. My main concern is the absence of results on larger datasets to validate scalability and practical utility. Expanding the empirical section with ILSVRC (or a comparably large benchmark), reporting inference/training costs, and adding ablations and failure analyses would substantially strengthen the contribution.
