### Summary

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. This feedback mechanism allows the network to iteratively refine its classification by dynamically modulating the activity of its convolutional filters over multiple passes on a single image. The control policy for this internal selective attention is learned via reinforcement learning—specifically, using scalable natural evolution strategies (SNES)—after the base CNN has been trained with supervised learning. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (for unaugmented data), suggesting that this iterative refinement process can correct initial misclassifications on challenging examples. This work is an interesting step beyond the prevailing paradigm of static, purely feed-forward deep networks.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and compelling problem: how to move beyond static, feed-forward architectures toward more dynamic, adaptive processing, which is a key feature of biological vision. The proposed method of learning a control policy to modulate internal filter activities within a state-of-the-art CNN (a Maxout network) is a highly original approach to this problem.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its performance. Achieving a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets is a notable accomplishment. This provides strong empirical validation that the proposed recurrent, attentional processing can demonstrably improve upon a powerful baseline model.

3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** For the most part, the paper is clearly written and the core idea is well-motivated and easy to grasp.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Insightful Analysis:** Despite the strong results, the paper's main weakness is that the proposed method remains very much a "black box." The analysis provided does not offer a deep understanding of *why* or *how* the attention mechanism works to improve performance.
    *   The analysis of the cat image in Figure 3, for instance, is not very illuminating. While it shows *that* filter activations are being modulated, it provides little intuition into the logic of these changes. Why are nearly all activations in the second layer emphasized? A more insightful analysis could focus on specific, challenging image patches or compare difficult cases that dasNet corrects versus those it still fails on.
    *   Similarly, there is no discussion of what the learned policy parameters (the mean and covariance of the `θ` distribution) represent or how they guide the attention strategy.

2.  **Conceptual Framing:** The framing of the problem as Reinforcement Learning feels somewhat tenuous. Since the reward (classification loss) is observed immediately after a fixed number of steps (`T`) and the policy is deterministic, the system behaves more like a recurrent neural network unrolled for a fixed number of steps, or a discrete-time dynamical system. Exploring this connection more explicitly could provide a stronger theoretical foundation and perhaps motivate alternative training schemes beyond black-box optimization.

3.  **Clarity of Methodological Details:** Several key details of the algorithm and training process are unclear, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The function `h(M)` used to construct the observation vector is not defined in the algorithm block itself. Furthermore, the updates to the fitness array `F[i]` and parameter array `Θ[i]` are placed inside the inner loop over images (`j`), which suggests they are overwritten on each iteration rather than accumulated. This needs clarification.
    *   **Equation 11:** The summation index is missing. It is presumably a sum over the `n` images in the batch, and `L_i` should likely be indexed by `j` to correspond to the `j`-th image.
    *   **Regularization:** The motivation for L2 regularization on `θ` is not well-explained. Since `θ` is sampled from a Gaussian distribution whose parameters are being optimized by SNES, the distribution itself already acts as a form of regularization. Why is an additional explicit regularization term necessary?
    *   **SNES Update:** The paper simply states that SNES updates its distribution without explaining how the natural gradient is calculated from the sampled fitness values. While details can be left to supplementary material, a brief overview of the update mechanism in the main text would improve clarity.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper introduces a novel and interesting architecture that combines state-of-the-art CNNs with feedback and learned attention.
*   It achieves excellent, state-of-the-art results on challenging benchmark datasets, providing strong evidence for the approach's value.
*   The work addresses an important conceptual challenge for the field by exploring a departure from purely feed-forward models.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   The analysis is superficial and fails to provide a meaningful understanding of why the method is effective, reducing its scientific contribution.
*   The connection to reinforcement learning is not fully convincing, and the unexplored relationship to recurrent networks is a missed opportunity for deeper insight.
*   Several ambiguities in the description of the methodology hinder the paper's clarity and potential for reproducibility.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a compelling idea with impressive results. The approach is novel and addresses a significant direction for future research in deep learning. However, the lack of deep analysis and the unclear methodological details detract from its overall quality. While the performance is a strong point, the work would be substantially improved by providing more insight into the learned mechanism. I lean towards acceptance due to the novelty and strong empirical results, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in the final version.
