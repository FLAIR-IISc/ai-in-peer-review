

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that integrates feedback connections into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to enable dynamic, iterative refinement of feature attention during classification. The authors model selective attention by allowing higher layers to modulate lower-layer filter activations through a reinforcement learning (RL)-learned policy, trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize filter weights across multiple passes over an image. The approach is grounded in neuroscience—citing Felleman & Essen’s work on bidirectional cortical connections—and builds on prior RL applications in vision (e.g., Bar et al. on top-down processing for object recognition). dasNet demonstrates state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, outperforming prior CNNs by 6% on un-augmented data, with compelling visualizations showing how attention adjustments correct initial misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing cat/dog images).

**Strengths:**  
The paper excels in *quality* and *significance*. The technical execution is rigorous: SNES effectively handles the million-parameter policy space, avoiding standard RL’s scalability limitations. The experimental design is sound, with clear ablation (e.g., dynamics analysis showing stable performance beyond T=5 steps) and a meaningful boosting mechanism (λ<sub>misclassified</sub> > λ<sub>correct</sub>) that focuses on difficult cases. The results are significant—they empirically validate that feedback-driven attention improves classification *without* retraining the base CNN, advancing the state of the art on benchmark datasets. The *clarity* is excellent: the algorithm (Algorithm 1) and ablation studies (Fig. 3) clearly illustrate how attention weights evolve and impact output. The *originality* lies not in isolated components (feedback connections, RL control) but in the *principled integration* of these elements for iterative, post-training refinement—a compelling direction for attention mechanisms in deep vision.

**Weaknesses:**  
The work’s *scope* is limited to CIFAR-10/100, leaving open whether the approach generalizes to larger-scale tasks like ImageNet (a notable omission given the paper’s claim of "state-of-the-art" results). While the authors justify this with computational constraints, the absence of ImageNet experiments weakens the broader significance argument. Additionally, the *originality* claim could be strengthened by more precisely contrasting dasNet with related work: for instance, how it differs from recurrent CNNs (e.g., *Learning iterative image reconstruction in the Neural Abstraction Pyramid*, Behnke) or RL-based attention (e.g., *Learning to Generate Artificial Fovea Trajectories*, Schmidhuber). The paper also omits a critical comparison to recent attention mechanisms (e.g., *Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks*, Hu et al.), which could contextualize dasNet’s contribution. Finally, while the attention dynamics are visually insightful (Fig. 3), the paper lacks quantitative analysis of *why* specific filters are emphasized (e.g., feature maps’ semantic relevance), limiting interpretability.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in CNN design—leveraging feedback for adaptive attention post-training. The technical execution is robust, the results are compelling, and the approach aligns with both neuroscience and practical needs for robust classification. While the CIFAR-only scope and limited comparison to modern attention models are weaknesses, they do not undermine the core technical merit. The paper makes a clear, evidence-based case for feedback-driven attention as a viable path forward, which is highly significant for the field. I recommend **acceptance**.  

*Constructive suggestions for revision:*  
1. Explicitly compare dasNet to recent attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks) to clarify novelty.  
2. Include ImageNet results (or a brief discussion of feasibility) to address scalability concerns.  
3. Add a sentence on how filter emphasis patterns correlate with known feature detectors (e.g., Gabor-like filters in early layers).  

The paper’s focus on *iterative refinement* of a pre-trained model—rather than end-to-end training—offers a pragmatic, efficient path to improve existing CNNs. This is a valuable direction for the community, and the results convincingly demonstrate its potential.
