- Introduces dasNet: a feedback-augmented CNN that iteratively modulates convolutional filter activations via a learned control policy (trained with SNES) to improve classification.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation, notably correcting hard misclassifications.  
- Original and significant idea: dynamic, learned internal attention via feedback extends static feed-forward paradigms and parallels recurrent human vision.  
- Empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of recurrent attentional processing for recognition.  
- Novel problem formulation: using evolution strategies to train a high-dimensional control policy for runtime network behavior.  
- Main weakness: limited insight into why the method works—the analysis of learned attention is superficial.  
- Visualization (Figure 3) is hard to interpret and doesn’t show which visual features are being amplified or suppressed to cause corrections.  
- No analysis of the learned policy parameters (θ), such as the learned mean and covariance from SNES, to characterize the solution.  
- Method is closely related to unrolled RNNs; authors should discuss connections to recurrent networks and relevant literature.  
- Suggestion: analyze behavior on controlled stimuli and trace gating variables/class probabilities for difficult cases to reveal mechanisms.  
- Algorithm 1 unclear: function h(Mt) used to build observations is not explicitly defined—needs a consolidated definition for reproducibility.  
- Algorithm 1 ambiguity: updates to F[i] and Θ[i] appear inside the image loop—clarify whether fitness is accumulated over the batch.  
- Equation 11 notation is confusing (index i used for both policy and summation); indices should be corrected for clarity.  
- Regularization question: unclear why L2 on each sampled θ_i is needed given SNES’s optimization of μ—motivation should be explained.  
- Conclusion: promising and novel contribution, but impact would be strengthened by deeper analysis explaining how and why feedback improves performance.
