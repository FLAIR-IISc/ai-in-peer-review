Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture augmented with recurrent feedback that modulates internal feature maps during evaluation. After training a standard Maxout CNN, the authors learn a policy that, over multiple passes on a fixed image, gates the activations of feature maps in each layer to emphasize or suppress specific filters. The policy is high-dimensional and is optimized with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimizer well-suited to million-parameter search spaces. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (without data augmentation at test time), dasNet improves over the base CNN and sets a new state of the art for unaugmented data. The work is motivated by biological evidence for top-down feedback and selective attention, and by prior RL-based attention mechanisms; here, the novelty lies in directly modulating internal CNN feature maps via learned feedback in a high-dimensional policy.

Relation to prior work
The approach is consistent with literature on recurrent processing and top-down modulation in vision, and with RL formulations of attention and multi-pass inference. The model differs from glimpse-based attention in that it does not manipulate the input or spatial sampling; rather, it gates internal channels across layers, closer to conditional computation or feature-wise attention. Methodologically, the use of SNES to train a massive deterministic policy over feature-map gates is a distinctive design choice compared to gradient-based RL or backpropagation through time.

Quality
- The method is technically sound: the gating mechanism is straightforward, the observation vector is well-defined (averaged feature-map activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities), and the policy uses a scaled softmax to keep gates positive and near unity.
- Training details are adequate: misclassifications are upweighted to encourage correction, and experiments show stable dynamics beyond the training horizon T.
- Empirical results on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent improvements over the baseline Maxout CNN and prior work on unaugmented data. The qualitative analyses (per-layer gating changes) are informative.
- Missing or weak baselines: it would strengthen the claims to compare against (a) multiple test-time passes without feedback, (b) simple confidence-based ensembling, (c) learned gates trained end-to-end by backprop (e.g., supervised or policy gradient) with the same parameterization, and (d) increasing base CNN capacity or test-time dropout averaging. An ablation isolating which layers benefit most from gating would also help.
- Practicality: the computational overhead (multiple passes and SNES training) is noted but not deeply analyzed. Reporting wall-clock costs, sample efficiency, and latency/accuracy trade-offs would clarify applicability.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The description of the action and observation spaces is clear, and the training algorithm is easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Specify exact dimensionalities of observations and actions for the reported models, and list all hyperparameters for SNES and the policy network.
- Clarify the choice and effect of scaling the softmax by the action dimension, and whether alternative normalizations were tried.
- Provide more explicit error analysis (per-class confusion improvements) and a breakdown of where feedback helps most.

Originality
The components (CNNs, attention, RL, recurrency) are known, but the combination—feature-map gating across the entire network learned via a large-scale NES policy—is a novel and pragmatic variant of top-down attention for CNNs. The conceptual contribution is moderate but meaningful: it demonstrates that internal, iterative attention can correct initial feedforward errors on strong benchmarks.

Significance
The improvements on CIFAR-10/100 establish that learned feedback can yield state-of-the-art results (unaugmented), supporting the case for attention and recurrence in vision systems. The approach is promising and could influence follow-up work on iterative inference and conditional computation. Its broader impact would be clearer with results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and with stronger baselines and ablations.

Pros
- Clear, biologically motivated idea realized in a practical CNN architecture.
- Demonstrated performance gains on CIFAR-10/100 with state-of-the-art unaugmented results.
- Stable multi-pass dynamics and evidence that gate values carry class-relevant information.
- Straightforward interface to existing CNNs; does not require architectural overhauls.

Cons
- Limited novelty in isolation; contribution is mainly the combination and scale.
- Evaluation constrained to CIFAR; no results on larger datasets or detection/segmentation.
- Missing comparisons to simpler or end-to-end differentiable alternatives for gating/policy learning.
- Computational overhead and training efficiency of SNES are not fully quantified.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide ablations: which layers’ gates matter most? What is the gain from gating only the top layer vs. all layers?
- Compare SNES to gradient-based policy learning or supervised end-to-end training of gates.
- Report latency/accuracy trade-offs vs. number of passes, and training/inference compute budgets.
- Analyze failure cases and per-class improvements to substantiate the “disambiguation” claim.
- Explore spatially varying gates or hybrid spatial-channel attention.
- If feasible, evaluate on a larger benchmark (e.g., ImageNet) to test scalability and impact.

Recommendation
A solid and well-executed paper that advances iterative, feedback-driven attention in CNNs with convincing results on CIFAR. While the methodological novelty is incremental, the empirical validation and clarity of the approach merit acceptance.
