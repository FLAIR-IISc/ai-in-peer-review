

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel CNN architecture incorporating top-down feedback connections to enable dynamic, iterative attention modulation during inference. Building on the observation that biological vision employs recurrent processing for difficult classifications (e.g., distinguishing similar bird species), dasNet uses a reinforcement learning policy—evolved via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to adjust filter sensitivities across multiple passes over an image. This allows the network to "correct" initial misclassifications by selectively enhancing or suppressing features, achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation. The approach significantly extends prior work on recurrent CNNs (e.g., [40–45]) by applying iterative attention to *trained* feedforward models (not just training from scratch), leveraging SNES to handle the million-dimensional policy space.

**Quality** is strong: The CIFAR-10/100 results (6% relative improvement over baseline) are well-supported by thorough experiments, including ablation studies on the number of passes (T=5), visualization of attention dynamics, and validation of gate information content (40.70% 15-NN accuracy using only gate activations). The SNES implementation is technically sound, though the computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) is not contextualized against standard CNN training. A notable gap is the absence of failure analysis—demonstrating cases where dasNet fails (e.g., specific misclassifications it cannot correct) would strengthen claims about the policy’s robustness and provide valuable insights for future work.

**Clarity** is generally good, with clear exposition of the dasNet mechanism (Section 4) and effective visualizations (Fig. 3) showing how filter emphasis changes across passes. However, the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) could be more precise—e.g., the role of the observation function *h*(·) and how *ot* is constructed from intermediate layers needs further elaboration to ensure reproducibility. The writing is accessible but occasionally overly technical (e.g., "λboostd log(vT)"), which may hinder broader readability.

**Originality** is a clear strength: The integration of SNES-learned feedback for *post-training* attention modulation represents a novel paradigm distinct from prior recurrent CNNs that optimize weights during training (e.g., [48–50]). The work directly addresses a key biological insight (recurrent processing for difficult classifications) and applies it to modern vision tasks in a way that advances beyond incremental improvements. The related work (Section 5) adequately contextualizes this against cortical hierarchy studies (Felleman & Essen [28]) and prior RL-CNN approaches ([40–45]), confirming novelty relative to recent NIPS literature.

**Significance** is partially demonstrated but requires validation on larger benchmarks. While the CIFAR results are impressive for *unaugmented* data, the lack of ILSVRC 2012/2013 results (a standard NIPS vision benchmark) limits claims about scalability and real-world applicability. The method’s computational overhead (4 days for dasNet vs. standard CNN training) is not compared to alternatives, making it unclear whether the gains justify the cost for larger-scale problems. This is the paper’s most critical weakness—without evidence of efficacy on ImageNet-sized tasks, the significance for practical computer vision remains unproven. The authors’ future work mention (spatial attention) is promising but does not address this gap.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pros*: Novel attention mechanism via SNES-learned feedback; strong CIFAR results; insightful visualizations of attention dynamics; clear biological motivation; technically rigorous RL implementation.  
*Cons*: No ILSVRC validation (weakens significance); insufficient failure analysis; computational cost not benchmarked; algorithm details lack reproducibility clarity.  

The paper is a compelling contribution to the intersection of reinforcement learning and deep vision, with clear potential to influence future work on dynamic neural architectures. However, the absence of large-scale validation prevents it from fully demonstrating the significance expected at NIPS. I recommend **weak acceptance** pending minor revisions: (1) add ILSVRC results (or a clear justification for omitting them), (2) include failure case analysis, and (3) clarify algorithmic details for reproducibility. The core idea is strong, but its broader impact hinges on scalability evidence.
