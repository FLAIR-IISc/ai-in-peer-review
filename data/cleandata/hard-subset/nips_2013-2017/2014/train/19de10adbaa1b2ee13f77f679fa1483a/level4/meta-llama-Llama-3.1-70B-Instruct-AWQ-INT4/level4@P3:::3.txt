This paper proposes a novel deep neural network architecture, Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors demonstrate that dasNet outperforms the state-of-the-art model on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showcasing its potential for image classification tasks.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the proposed architecture, highlighting the limitations of traditional feedforward convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and the benefits of incorporating top-down processing. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work, situating their contribution within the broader context of computer vision and deep learning.

The technical soundness of the paper is evident in the clear and concise explanation of the dasNet architecture, including the use of reinforcement learning to train the attention policy. The authors provide a detailed description of the training algorithm, making it possible for readers to reproduce the results. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and interpretable manner.

One of the strengths of the paper is its novelty, as it introduces a new architecture that combines the benefits of CNNs with the flexibility of top-down processing. The use of attention-based feedback is a promising approach, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in improving image classification performance.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. One concern is the scalability of the proposed architecture to larger datasets, such as ILSVRC. While the results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are impressive, it is unclear whether dasNet would perform equally well on more challenging datasets with larger images. Additional experiments on larger datasets would help to alleviate this concern and demonstrate the practicality of the proposed architecture.

Another potential limitation of the paper is the lack of analysis on cases where the algorithm may fail. As the authors note, the quality of the representations learned by dasNet hinges on the attention policy, and it would be interesting to see how the algorithm performs on more challenging images or in scenarios where the attention policy is suboptimal.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning, and its novelty and technical soundness make it a strong candidate for publication. With some additional experiments and analysis, the paper could be even more compelling and demonstrate the full potential of the proposed architecture.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and promising architecture that combines the benefits of CNNs with top-down processing.
* The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.
* The technical soundness of the paper is evident in the clear and concise explanation of the dasNet architecture and training algorithm.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks experiments on larger datasets, such as ILSVRC, which raises concerns about the scalability of the proposed architecture.
* The authors do not provide an analysis of cases where the algorithm may fail, which could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the proposed architecture.
