This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a deep convolutional neural network (dasNet). The method trains gating parameters that modulate filter activations over successive passes, with the goal of optimizing classification performance. This is accomplished by framing the problem as reinforcement learning, where a policy network learns to control the attention gates based on the CNN's internal state, and is trained using natural evolution strategies. The work addresses the important challenge of how to adaptively reconfigure deep processing architectures, moving beyond the standard paradigm of static, feed-forward networks.

The empirical results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (for unaugmented data). This demonstrates that recurrent, top-down processing can correct initial misclassifications made by a strong baseline model. This is a valuable step beyond conventional feed-forward networks whose parameters are fixed after training.

However, a significant weakness is that the method is presented as a "black box." While the model clearly works, the paper offers little insight into *why* it is effective. The analysis of the network's internal representations (e.g., the cat image visualization) is not particularly revealing, and it remains unclear how the learned policy distribution (parameterized by a mean and covariance) guides the attention mechanism to make better decisions. While the results are strong, the lack of analysis limits the paper's scientific contribution to a proof of concept. The paper is generally well-written, although several equations and algorithmic descriptions could be clarified.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Connection to Recurrent Networks vs. Reinforcement Learning:** The authors frame their method within reinforcement learning, but its connection to dynamical systems and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) seems much stronger and should be explored. Since the reward is observed immediately after a fixed number of steps (T=5) and the policy is deterministic, the training process appears less like a typical RL problem and more like a direct optimization of the parameters of an unrolled RNN. A discussion of this connection would place the work in a more appropriate context and might offer alternative training strategies.

2.  **Analysis of the Attention Mechanism:** The analysis of the network processing the cat image (Figure 3) was not very helpful for understanding how the model improves recognition. For instance, why are many activations in the first layer globally increased? Is the network's output invariant to such global scaling of activations, or does this uniform increase serve a purpose? The analysis could be made far more instructive by:
    *   Focusing on difficult classification cases—images misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but correctly classified by dasNet—to isolate the effect of the attention mechanism.
    *   Analyzing the evolution of gating variables for specific, salient regions of an image across the processing steps.
    *   Investigating the effect of the learned policy distribution parameters (µ, Σ) by constraining them or analyzing their structure. This would help clarify what the policy has actually learned.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Algorithm 1, the function `h(M_t)` used to construct the observation vector is not clearly defined in the main text. Is this simply the collection of activations described in the bulleted list?
*   In Algorithm 1 (lines 12-14), the fitness `F[i]` and policy parameters `Θ[i]` appear to be updated inside the loop over images (`j`), which would cause them to be overwritten for each image in the batch. These assignments should likely occur after the inner loop over `j` is complete.
*   In Equation 11, the sum for `f(θi)` is over `i`, but `i` is the index for the candidate policy. The sum should likely be over the batch of `n` images, indexed by `j`. Furthermore, Equation 8 should probably be indexed by `j` (i.e., `L_i,j`) to denote the loss for policy `i` on image `j`.
*   The procedure for updating the policy distribution via SNES is not well-explained. The paper states that SNES updates its parameters based on the sampled fitness values, but it omits any detail about how the natural gradient is computed or applied. A brief explanation or a clearer reference would be helpful.
*   It is not clear why L2 regularization on the policy parameters `θ` is necessary (Equation 11). Since `θ` is sampled from a Gaussian distribution controlled by SNES, the optimization process already encourages parameters to remain close to the distribution's mean. Could the authors explain the motivation for this additional regularization term?
