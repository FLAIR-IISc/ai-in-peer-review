### Summary

This paper introduces a selective attention mechanism into a convolutional neural network, proposing a method to train the corresponding gating parameters to optimize classification performance. The work addresses the important and challenging problem of how to adaptively configure complex, deep neural networks, particularly when computational resources are limited or tasks require dynamic processing. The classification results presented are impressive and demonstrate the potential of this approach. However, the mechanism by which the model achieves these results remains largely opaque, limiting the paper's explanatory power and making it difficult to understand why the method is effective. While the work is a significant step beyond the prevailing paradigm of large, static feed-forward networks, its contribution would be strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the model's internal dynamics. The exposition is generally clear, though several equations and algorithmic descriptions require clarification.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Importance:** The core idea of incorporating an adaptive, recurrent attention mechanism into a feed-forward CNN is a significant and timely contribution. It represents a promising direction for creating more efficient and flexible vision systems.

2.  **Empirical Performance:** The high classification accuracy relative to standard methods is a strong validation of the proposed approach. It suggests that recurrent computation helps the network focus on or extract more complex and discriminative features from images.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Conceptual Framing (Connection to Recurrent Networks):**
    A central point for consideration is the paper's framing. The proposed method appears more closely related to recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or dynamical systems than to traditional Reinforcement Learning. Given that the "reward" (the classification objective) is observed immediately after a fixed number of iterations and the policy for generating `theta` is deterministic, the training process can be viewed as a gradient-based search through the parameter space of a recurrent policy to maximize an objective function. The authors are encouraged to explore and discuss this connection. Framing the work in the context of RNNs could provide a stronger theoretical grounding and connect it to a broader body of existing literature.

2.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:**
    While the results are strong, the paper offers limited insight into *why* the method works. The analysis of the network's processing of the sample cat image is not particularly revealing. For instance, it is unclear why layer 1 activations globally increase in the first iteration, and whether this has a meaningful impact on the objective or is simply an artifact of scaling. To improve the paper's explanatory power and move beyond a "black box" presentation, I suggest the following analyses:
    *   Analyze the evolution of gating variables over time within a specific, smaller image region to provide a more localized and interpretable view of the attention mechanism.
    *   Conduct a qualitative analysis of difficult classification cases—for example, images that are misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but correctly classified by the proposed model. What does the attention mechanism focus on in these instances?
    *   Investigate the role of the learned distribution parameters (mean and variance of `theta`). This could be done by ablating or constraining these parameters to isolate their effect on both final performance and the resulting attention patterns.

3.  **Clarity of the Training Algorithm and Theoretical Justification:**
    The description of the training procedure and its theoretical underpinnings could be significantly improved. A crucial missing detail is how the gradients with respect to the policy parameters are computed after sampling and evaluating `theta`. A more thorough explanation of this final optimization step is necessary. Furthermore, the motivation for regularizing `theta` is not clear. Since `theta` is sampled from a prior distribution that presumably already favors smaller values, the need for an additional regularization term should be justified. Is the final step adjusting hyperparameters of a posterior distribution over policies? Clarifying the theoretical basis for this update rule would substantially strengthen the paper.

### Minor Points and Typos

*   **Algorithm 1:** The variable `h_M` is used without a prior definition. Does this refer to the step of collecting observations? Additionally, `F[i]` and `\Theta[i]` are updated within the inner loop over images (j). Is the intention to collect fitness values for all images in an array, or are these values overwritten on each iteration?
*   **Equation 11:** The summation lacks an index. Over what variable is the sum performed?
*   **Equation 8:** Does this equation apply to a single image? If so, it might be clearer to index it with `j` to maintain consistency with the surrounding notation.
*   **Text following Eq. 11:** The variables 'x' and 'd' are used without being defined in this context.
