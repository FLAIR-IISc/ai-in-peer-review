

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through feedback connections enabling *internal selective attention*. The core innovation lies in dynamically modulating convolutional filter sensitivities during inference via a reinforcement learning (RL) policy learned with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). Unlike prior work relying on fixed feedforward architectures, dasNet iteratively refines feature activation across multiple passes on the same input, correcting misclassifications by emphasizing discriminative filters while suppressing irrelevant ones. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (7.0% error) and CIFAR-100 (29.8% error) without data augmentation, outperforming prior CNNs by 6% relative improvement.  

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Technical Soundness*: The approach is rigorously grounded in biological plausibility (e.g., top-down cortical feedback) and addresses a critical gap in static CNNs. The SNES-based policy learning is well-justified for handling the million-dimensional parameter space (Section 3), avoiding impractical RL methods. Experiments are thorough: they isolate ambiguous cases (e.g., cat vs. dog misclassifications in Fig. 3), validate dynamics (Fig. 2), and confirm policy efficacy via gate-value classification (40.70% 15-NN accuracy). The architecture cleanly integrates with Maxout networks (Section 2), preserving the original model’s structure while adding feedback gates.  

*Clarity & Organization*: The paper is exceptionally well-structured. The motivation (Section 1) clearly contrasts biological vision with static CNNs, and the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is meticulously detailed. Key innovations (e.g., action representation in Eq. 5, observation construction in Eq. 7) are unambiguous. Figures 1–3 effectively visualize the attention mechanism’s impact on filter activations and classification dynamics.  

*Originality*: While feedback connections exist in other works (e.g., [37, 38, 40]), dasNet’s *scalable RL-driven policy for internal attention* is distinct. It avoids prior limitations—e.g., simplified datasets [40, 41] or non-SOTA applications [48, 49]—by directly enhancing *state-of-the-art* CNNs. The paper explicitly differentiates from related work (Section 5), noting that dasNet’s focus on *post-training refinement* of *pre-trained networks* is novel.  

*Significance*: This work validates a critical hypothesis: iterative attention via feedback improves classification *without retraining*. The CIFAR results are compelling evidence that feedback mechanisms can be practically integrated into modern CNNs. This advances the field beyond purely feedforward models, offering a pathway to more robust, human-like vision systems. The method’s applicability to *any* pre-trained CNN (e.g., ResNet, VGG) suggests broad impact for practitioners.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Scope Limitation*: The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10/100, leaving open whether the approach scales to ImageNet or larger-scale challenges. While the authors acknowledge this (Section 6), the absence of ImageNet results is a notable gap for a "state-of-the-art" claim. The computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti for dasNet training) also warrants discussion for real-world deployment.  

*Limited Ablation*: The paper fixes *T* = 5 steps empirically but does not explore how policy performance varies with *T* (e.g., diminishing returns beyond *T* = 3). A deeper analysis of gate dynamics (e.g., which filters consistently improve classification) would strengthen the "why" behind the results.  

*Reinforcement Learning Choice*: While SNES is pragmatic for high-dimensional policies, the paper does not compare it to alternative scalable RL methods (e.g., proximal policy optimization [51]). This omission slightly weakens the justification for SNES over other approaches.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- First to demonstrate *scalable RL-driven feedback* for *post-training refinement* of SOTA CNNs.  
- Strong empirical validation on challenging ambiguous cases (Fig. 3).  
- Methodologically sound, with clear biological and computational grounding.  
- Directly addresses a key limitation of static CNNs (no internal iterative refinement).  

*Con*:  
- Limited to CIFAR-10/100; no ImageNet or larger-scale validation.  
- Computational cost may hinder adoption without optimization.  
- Minimal discussion of failure cases (e.g., when dasNet *fails* to correct misclassifications).  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, significant contribution that effectively bridges biological insights with practical deep learning. The technical execution is robust, the results are compelling, and the approach is broadly applicable. While the scope is currently limited to CIFAR, the core methodology—using RL to dynamically gate filters post-training—represents a promising direction for future work. The authors’ focus on *practical integration* with existing CNNs (rather than designing new architectures) is particularly valuable. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), conceptually novel (Originality), and advances the field meaningfully (Significance).  

**Recommendation**: Accept. This work provides a rigorous, scalable mechanism for incorporating feedback-driven attention into CNNs, with clear evidence of its efficacy. Future work could extend to ImageNet and explore lightweight policy variants, but the current contribution is substantial and well-executed. The paper’s clarity and methodological precision make it a strong fit for NIPS.
