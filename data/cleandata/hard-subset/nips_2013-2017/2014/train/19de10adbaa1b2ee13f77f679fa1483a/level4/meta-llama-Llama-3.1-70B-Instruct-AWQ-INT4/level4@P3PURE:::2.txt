This paper introduces selective attention to a convolutional neural network and proposes a method for training gating parameters to optimize the network's classification performance. It addresses an important set of problems in computer vision, specifically how to adaptively reconfigure complex, deep processing stages when resources are limited and different tasks need to be solved. The classification results are impressive, but the approach is somewhat of a black box, providing useful directions for future research without revealing the underlying reasons for its success. Although the analysis of image representation is not particularly insightful, the work represents an important step beyond the current paradigm of large, feed-forward networks with fixed parameters after learning. The exposition is generally clear, but some of the equations contain typos or could be explained more thoroughly.

Major Comments:
To further strengthen the paper, I would like to see the authors explore the connection to dynamical systems in more depth. The work appears to be more closely related to recurrent networks than to Reinforcement Learning, as the reward is observed immediately and the policy is deterministic. Training involves traversing the space of recurrent "policy" parameters theta to maximize the observed objective function.

I found the analysis of the network processing the cat image to be less helpful than expected in understanding how the new features of the model contribute to recognition. It would be beneficial to explain why layer 1 activations are all increased at the first iteration and whether this affects the objective function or if the output is invariant to global scalings. A more instructive approach might be to focus on a smaller region of the image and analyze the evolution of the gating variables in that region. Additionally, examining difficult cases, such as those misclassified by the standard Maxout network but correctly labeled by the proposed model, or further constraining the learned model parameters (mean and variance of theta) to tease out their effect, could provide valuable insights.

Minor Comments:
In Algorithm 1, the variable h_M is not defined. It appears to be the step of collecting observations, but clarification is needed. Furthermore, F[i] and \Theta[i] are inside the loop over images (j), so it is unclear whether the image fitness values are collected in an array or overwritten on each iteration.

Equation 11 lacks clarity regarding the sum. Is Equation 8 intended for a single image and should be indexed j? Additionally, the text following Equation 11 refers to 'x' and 'd' without explanation.

The regularization of theta is not well-justified, as theta is already sampled from the prior and tends towards small values. The last step of the optimization, involving gradient updates on the parameters after sampling and evaluating the fitness of thetas, is not well-explained (including how gradients are computed) or theoretically justified (including whether hyper-parameters of the posterior over policies are being adjusted).

The approach to building deep recurrent networks for recognition is intriguing, and the high classification accuracy relative to standard methods suggests that recurrent computation helps extract or focus on more complex features of the images. However, the lack of transparency in the approach makes it difficult to understand why it is effective.
