This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (a Maxout network) with feedback connections. These connections implement an internal selective attention mechanism by gating, or modulating, the activations of convolutional filters. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can iteratively refine its understanding of an image by learning to focus on specific features. This attention policy is not hard-coded but is learned using reinforcement learning (specifically, Natural Evolution Strategies) on top of the pre-trained convnet. The work is well-situated, drawing inspiration from recurrent processing in the human visual system and building upon state-of-the-art CNNs like Maxout networks.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality & Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its high-quality execution and significant empirical results. The authors achieve a new state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10/100 datasets (without data augmentation), providing strong evidence that feedback and attention are promising avenues for improving deep vision models. The approach is technically sound, and the experiments are well-designed to demonstrate the efficacy of the learned attention policy, particularly on ambiguous classification cases. The analysis showing that the final gate activations contain substantial class-relevant information further validates the method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is described with sufficient detail for understanding, and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses & Suggestions:**
*   **Originality:** While the overall system is novel and effective, the individual components—convolutional networks, the concept of feedback, and reinforcement learning for attention—are not new in themselves. The contribution lies in the successful and well-chosen combination of these existing techniques.
*   **Scalability:** The main limitation is the evaluation on relatively small-scale datasets. While achieving SOTA on CIFAR is commendable, the true test for a new architectural paradigm is its performance on larger, more complex problems like ImageNet. The current training time (4 days for CIFAR) suggests that scaling might be a significant challenge. The authors should discuss the computational complexity and potential bottlenecks of their RL-based training strategy.

**Recommendation:**
This is a solid contribution that demonstrates a well-executed and effective idea. The empirical results are strong and provide valuable evidence for the utility of learned internal attention. I recommend acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to scale their approach to a larger dataset like ImageNet in future work to fully establish the significance and generalizability of dasNet.
