This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with attention-based top-down feedback that modulates intermediate feature maps across multiple recurrent passes. A learned policy maps layer summaries and class logits to multiplicative gates over feature maps, trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and reaches state-of-the-art results. The work relates to recurrent and attention mechanisms in vision (e.g., Larochelle & Hinton’s glimpse models; Denil et al. for learned saccades; O’Reilly/Wyatte for feedback in recognition) and to policy-search RL for high-dimensional controllers, but is distinct in directly gating internal CNN representations with policy-learned top-down signals.

Quality: The method is technically sound and complete. The training objective, observation/action design, and SNES optimization are coherent, and visualizations support the claim that feedback alters representations meaningfully. The temporal evaluation (varying T) is welcome. However, scalability is not demonstrated: there are no ILSVRC/ImageNet results, and compute/latency overhead is not quantified. Baselines are limited (no comparisons to other recurrent/feedback CNNs, glimpse/attention models, or simple backprop-trained gating). The dependence on the attention policy is acknowledged but failure cases are not analyzed.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithmic details are clear, though reproducibility would benefit from reporting exact observation dimensionality, gating ranges, SNES hyperparameters, compute budget, and a compute–accuracy trade-off across T.

Originality: The combination of internal selective attention via multiplicative gating, learned through black-box policy search on top of a state-of-the-art CNN, is novel and interesting relative to prior NIPS work on feedforward CNNs and recurrent attention.

Significance: The improvements on CIFAR and the interpretability afforded by visualizations suggest potential impact. Demonstrating scalability to larger datasets and stronger baselines would substantially increase significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, forward-looking integration of bottom-up and top-down signals.
- State-of-the-art CIFAR performance with informative visualizations.
- Careful temporal evaluation and evidence that gates carry semantic information.

Cons against acceptance:
- No ILSVRC or large-scale results; unclear scalability and compute cost.
- Missing comparisons to alternative recurrent/attention mechanisms and supervised gating.
- No systematic failure-case analysis; sensitivity to policy design underexplored.

Suggestions: Add large-scale experiments (even partial), quantify overhead vs accuracy across T, compare to backprop-trained gates and recurrent baselines, and include a taxonomy of failure cases and policy ablations. The paper is worthy of further study.
