This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass through the network, a control policy can iteratively modulate the activations of the network's convolutional filters over several subsequent passes on the same input image. This allows the network to "re-think" its initial prediction by focusing on or suppressing certain learned features, mimicking the attentional and recurrent processing observed in the primate visual cortex.

The model is built upon a state-of-the-art Maxout network. After standard supervised training of the base CNN, the feedback mechanism is implemented as a set of gates that weight the output maps of each convolutional layer. A control policy, implemented as a linear mapping from an observation vector to the gate values, is trained using reinforcement learning to maximize classification accuracy. The observation vector is constructed from intermediate network activations. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over 1 million), the authors cleverly employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a scalable black-box optimization algorithm, for policy search. This approach is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where it achieves a new state-of-the-art result on unaugmented data.

This work relates to a long line of research on CNNs (e.g., Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Goodfellow et al., 2013) but moves beyond the purely feedforward paradigm. It draws strong inspiration from neuroscience, where feedback connections are known to be numerous and play a crucial role in attentional processing and robust object recognition (e.g., Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). While reinforcement learning has been used for visual attention before, often for learning saccades (e.g., Schmidhuber & Huber, 1991) or in simpler settings, this paper's contribution lies in successfully integrating these ideas to modulate the *internal* feature representations of a large-scale, state-of-the-art deep network to demonstrably improve its performance on challenging benchmarks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The combination of a state-of-the-art deep CNN with a reinforcement-learning-trained policy for internal filter attention is highly novel. While the individual components (CNNs, RL, feedback) are not new, their synthesis into the dasNet architecture is a creative and well-motivated contribution.
*   **Significance:** The paper demonstrates a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (without data augmentation). This is a significant empirical result that provides strong evidence for the value of moving beyond purely feedforward architectures. The work opens up a promising research direction in building more dynamic and powerful vision models that can perform iterative refinement of their predictions.
*   **Quality:** The technical execution is solid. The choice of SNES to train a policy in a million-dimensional parameter space is well-justified and effective. The experiments are well-conducted, and the authors include useful analyses, such as an investigation of the network's dynamics over multiple steps and a test confirming that the learned gate values contain significant class-relevant information. The qualitative example of correcting a cat/dog misclassification is illustrative and compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to understand. The motivation is well-established, the model is described precisely (with the help of Algorithm 1), and the experimental setup and results are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability:** The most significant unanswered question is the scalability of this approach. The experiments are performed on the relatively small CIFAR datasets. Training the policy via SNES on top of a pre-trained network already takes 4 days on a GPU. It is unclear how this approach would fare on larger-scale problems like ImageNet, where both the base model and the policy would be significantly larger, and the iterative evaluation would be much more computationally expensive. A discussion of the potential challenges and strategies for scaling would strengthen the paper.
*   **Observation Space Representation:** The observation vector for the policy is constructed by, among other things, averaging the activations of each output map. This collapses all spatial information within a feature map into a single value. While this keeps the observation space manageable, it may be suboptimal. It would be interesting to know if alternative representations (e.g., using spatial pooling or convolutions on the state) were considered and why the current approach was chosen.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies. For example, how does performance change as the number of iterative steps `T` is varied during training? The dynamics analysis is interesting but doesn't fully explore the impact of this key hyperparameter on the final trained model.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that combines CNNs with RL for internal attention.
    *   Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (CIFAR-10), demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
    *   Opens a promising new direction for research into dynamic and recurrent vision models.
    *   The paper is of high quality and is written very clearly.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The experiments are limited to CIFAR, and the scalability to larger datasets like ImageNet is a major open question.
    *   The computational cost is significant.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an original, well-executed, and significant contribution. The weaknesses are primarily related to the scope of the experiments rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The impressive results provide compelling evidence that incorporating feedback and attention is a fruitful direction for deep learning in computer vision. I recommend this paper for acceptance. Scaling this fascinating approach to larger problems is an exciting avenue for future work.
