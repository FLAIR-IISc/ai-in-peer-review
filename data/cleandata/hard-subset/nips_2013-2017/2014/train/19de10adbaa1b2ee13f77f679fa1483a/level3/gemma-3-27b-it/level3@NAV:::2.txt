## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces dasNet, a novel CNN architecture incorporating feedback connections and learned selective attention mechanisms. The core idea – allowing a network to iteratively refine its classification through internal feedback – is a compelling one, drawing inspiration from the iterative processing observed in human vision. The authors address the challenge of adapting CNN behavior post-training, a limitation of standard feedforward networks, and demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. This builds upon prior work in recurrent neural networks and reinforcement learning applied to vision (e.g., [40, 42, 48]), but importantly applies this to state-of-the-art Maxout networks, showcasing feasibility at scale.

The strengths of the paper lie in its demonstration of effective performance gains. The reported results are impressive, establishing a new state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR datasets. The conceptual framework is also promising, offering a potential pathway toward more adaptive and robust image classification models. 

However, a significant weakness is the lack of interpretability. While dasNet *works* well, the mechanisms driving its improvement remain largely opaque. The analysis of image representations and the learned gating parameters (theta) is inadequate. Simply showing activation changes (Figure 3) isn’t sufficient to understand *why* specific filters are emphasized or suppressed. The paper acknowledges this, but it’s a critical shortcoming. Further, the necessity of the theta regularization is unclear, and the gradient updates appear underspecified.

Several technical issues detract from clarity. Algorithm 1 is ambiguous regarding the scope of variable assignments (per image or overwritten), and Equations 11 and 8 contain errors or require further clarification. Undefined symbols (x and d after Eq. 11) also hinder comprehension. The exposition, while generally clear, suffers from these inconsistencies.

**Major Suggestion:** The authors’ framing of the approach as reinforcement learning feels somewhat misplaced. The immediate reward signal and deterministic policy strongly suggest a closer connection to dynamical systems. Exploring this perspective could provide valuable theoretical insights and potentially lead to more efficient learning algorithms.

**Further Analysis Needed:** A deeper dive into the internal dynamics is crucial. Why do layer-1 activations increase during the first iteration? How does global scaling influence the gating behavior? Analyzing misclassified examples and testing constrained theta statistics (e.g., fixed mean/variance) would also be highly informative. 

**Pro Acceptance:**
* Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on challenging datasets.
* Introduces a novel and potentially impactful architectural modification.
* Offers a promising direction for building more adaptive CNNs.

**Con Acceptance:**
* Lacks interpretability and a clear understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
* Contains technical ambiguities and errors in equations/algorithm description.
* The RL framing feels forced; a dynamical systems perspective may be more appropriate.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling idea. Addressing the interpretability concerns and clarifying the technical details would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the reviewer's concerns.
