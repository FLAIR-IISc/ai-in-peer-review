This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable the network to perform iterative, sequential processing on a single input image, allowing it to dynamically modulate the activations of its own convolutional filters. This mechanism is framed as a form of internal selective attention. The model first performs a standard feedforward pass. Based on the internal state of the network (map activations, intermediate layer outputs, and class probabilities), a policy network then outputs a set of modulatory weights, or "gates," for the convolutional filters. These gates are applied, and the same image is passed through the network again. This process can be repeated for several steps, allowing the network to refine its initial prediction.

The underlying CNN is a state-of-the-art Maxout network. While the CNN itself is trained via standard supervised backpropagation, the attention policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL). Specifically, the authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the policy parameters, citing the high dimensionality of the action space (over 1 million parameters) as a challenge for traditional RL methods. The reward function is designed to heavily penalize misclassifications, effectively encouraging the attention policy to focus on correcting the base model's mistakes. This work relates to a rich history of research inspired by the recurrent and feedback-heavy nature of the primate visual cortex, as well as prior work using RL for visual attention (e.g., learning saccades). However, it distinguishes itself by applying this concept to modulate the internal feature maps of a modern, deep CNN to improve classification on a challenging benchmark.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The methodological choice to use SNES for policy search is well-justified given the immense size of the parameter space for the attention controller. The experimental evaluation is solid, demonstrating a notable performance improvement over a strong baseline (the Maxout network). The authors are careful in their analysis, providing not just final accuracy scores but also an investigation into the system's dynamics over multiple time steps and a clever experiment to confirm that the learned attention gates contain significant class-relevant information.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is its compelling empirical demonstration that feedback and iterative refinement can improve the performance of modern, deep vision models. The achievement of a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10/100 for unaugmented data is a significant result and provides strong evidence for the utility of the approach. This work opens up a promising research direction, moving beyond purely feedforward architectures and exploring more dynamic, sequential computation within deep networks. The idea of learning a "refinement" policy on top of a pre-trained expert model is an interesting paradigm that could be influential for other researchers.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the dasNet architecture is explained well (with the aid of Algorithm 1), and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within both neuroscience and prior machine learning literature. The visualizations (Figure 3) are helpful in providing some intuition for what the attention mechanism is doing, even if a full interpretation remains challenging.

**Originality:** While the individual components—CNNs, feedback connections, reinforcement learning, and attention—are not new in isolation, their synthesis in this paper is novel and effective. Applying a policy learned via an evolution strategy to modulate the internal filter maps of a deep CNN in an iterative fashion is a creative and non-trivial approach to improving image classification. This marks a clear and original contribution beyond prior work that used RL for more external forms of attention, such as controlling foveal glimpses.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The main weakness of the work lies in the question of its scalability.
1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The proposed method is computationally intensive. Training the attention policy required 4 days on a GPU, and this is *after* the base Maxout network has already been trained. More importantly, the evaluation was performed on the relatively small CIFAR datasets. A crucial next step is to demonstrate whether this approach can scale to much deeper networks and larger, higher-resolution datasets like ImageNet. As network depth and image size increase, the dimensionality of the observation and action spaces for the RL policy will grow dramatically, potentially making the SNES optimization intractable. The authors should comment on the potential scalability challenges and any ideas for mitigating them.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The number of iterative steps, T, was fixed at 5 for the main experiments. The paper provides a brief analysis of dynamics for models trained with T=1 and T=2, which is good, but a more thorough discussion of how T was chosen and how sensitive the final performance is to this parameter would strengthen the paper. Does performance continue to improve with T > 5, or does it plateau or degrade?

3.  **Ablation Studies:** The choice of SNES is justified by the large parameter space. However, it would be interesting to know if simpler policy representations or other RL algorithms were considered or attempted, even on a smaller-scale version of the problem, to further justify this specific choice.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-executed architecture combining deep CNNs with an RL-trained feedback/attention mechanism.
*   Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation, which is a strong empirical contribution.
*   Provides a compelling demonstration of the value of iterative processing and internal attention, opening up a promising avenue for future research.
*   The paper is clear, technically sound, and well-written.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The primary concern is the scalability of the approach. The high computational cost and demonstration only on CIFAR leave open the question of its applicability to larger-scale problems like ImageNet.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of key hyperparameters, such as the number of time steps T.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—a novel and well-motivated approach backed by state-of-the-art empirical results—outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a significant and interesting contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the scalability question in the final version, even if just in the discussion.
