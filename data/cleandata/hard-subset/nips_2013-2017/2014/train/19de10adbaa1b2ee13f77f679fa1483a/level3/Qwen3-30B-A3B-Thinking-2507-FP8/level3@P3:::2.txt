

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances standard CNNs with selective attention mechanisms through feedback connections. dasNet allows the network to iteratively adjust its filter sensitivities during classification, dynamically focusing on the most discriminative features through a policy evolved via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The authors position this work as a step toward more biologically plausible neural networks that can adapt their behavior post-training, similar to human visual processing with its feedforward and feedback pathways.

The paper builds on Maxout networks [7] and leverages reinforcement learning to train an attention policy that modulates filter weights across multiple passes through the network. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over the baseline Maxout network on unaugmented data, establishing a new state-of-the-art for these datasets. The authors argue that this approach is significant because it moves beyond fixed-parameter feedforward networks, enabling adaptive reconfiguration under resource/task constraints.

**Strengths:**
1. The method demonstrates impressive empirical results, with clear improvements over previous work on challenging datasets.
2. The core idea of adding selective attention through feedback connections is conceptually interesting and addresses a meaningful limitation of standard CNNs.
3. The paper makes a valuable contribution by showing how to apply reinforcement learning to high-dimensional policy spaces (over 1 million parameters) using SNES, which is a practical technical advance.
4. The experimental results show that dasNet successfully corrects misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing cats from dogs) by selectively emphasizing/de-emphasizing specific filters.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of interpretability:** The paper functions as a "black box" with minimal explanation of why the method works. The analysis of image representations and learned theta parameters (mean and variance) is superficial and uninformative. For instance, Figure 3 shows activation changes but provides no insight into why certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized.

2. **Algorithmic and notational issues:** Algorithm 1 is confusing - the meaning of h_M is unclear, and it's ambiguous whether F[i] and Θ[i] are collected per image or overwritten. Equation (11) has an unclear summation index, and Equation (8) may need an image index j. Symbols x and d after Equation (11) are undefined.

3. **Inadequate theoretical analysis:** The paper claims to be closer to recurrent networks than reinforcement learning, but this connection is not explored. A deeper analysis of internal dynamics (why layer-1 activations increase on first iteration, effect of global scaling, evolution of gating variables) is missing. The paper also fails to analyze hard cases where standard Maxout fails but dasNet succeeds.

4. **Questionable regularization:** The necessity and justification of theta regularization are unclear. The paper doesn't explain how final gradient updates are computed or whether they adjust posterior hyperparameters.

**Recommendations:**
1. Explore the connection to dynamical systems more thoroughly. The method appears closer to recurrent networks than reinforcement learning since reward is immediate and the policy is deterministic.
2. Provide deeper analysis of internal dynamics, including why layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration and how gating variables evolve in local image regions.
3. Analyze hard cases where standard Maxout fails but dasNet succeeds, and test the effects of constraining learned theta statistics.
4. Clarify the algorithm and equations, particularly the meaning of h_M and the proper indexing in the equations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and effective recurrent-style approach with strong accuracy gains. The core idea of adding selective attention through feedback connections represents an important step beyond fixed-parameter feedforward networks. However, the lack of interpretability and theoretical clarity significantly limits its scientific contribution.

The paper is technically sound in its implementation and results are well-supported by empirical evidence, but the interpretability shortcomings prevent a deeper understanding of why the method works. The exposition is generally clear but contains several technical issues that would need to be resolved for reproducibility.

The significance of this work is tempered by its black-box nature, though it does open interesting directions for future research on adaptive neural networks. The authors have made a valuable contribution by demonstrating that iterative processing can improve classification, but the paper would benefit greatly from deeper theoretical and interpretive analysis.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper's empirical results and novel approach are compelling, and with the suggested revisions (particularly regarding interpretability and theoretical clarity), it would make a strong contribution to the field. The weaknesses are significant but not fatal to the paper's scientific merit.
