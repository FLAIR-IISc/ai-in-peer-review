This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel CNN architecture that incorporates top-down feedback connections, allowing for dynamic adjustment of convolutional filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to mimic the iterative attentional processes observed in the human visual cortex, where initial assessments are refined through recurrent processing. The attention mechanism, implemented as a control policy, is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the parameter space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that dasNet outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets, establishing a new benchmark.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in CNNs [1, 7, 8], reinforcement learning [23, 24], and biologically inspired visual processing [28, 29, 30]. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in feedforward networks [5, 6], Maxout networks [7], and recurrent approaches to vision [48, 49], positioning their contribution within the broader context of the field. The motivation to explore recurrent connections, drawing parallels to human visual perception [16, 17, 31], is compelling and well-articulated. While existing work has investigated attention mechanisms in vision [40, 41, 44, 45], this paper distinguishes itself by learning the attentional policy through direct policy search (SNES) on a fully trained CNN, rather than integrating attention directly into the training process.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a dynamic attention policy *post-training* via reinforcement learning is a novel and interesting contribution. Integrating feedback connections into a deep CNN framework and evolving them through SNES represents a creative combination of techniques.
*   **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, even without data augmentation, is a significant accomplishment. The 6% relative improvement reported in Table 1 is noteworthy.
*   **Interpretability:** The visualizations of filter activations and changes in emphasis (Figure 3) provide valuable insights into the network's behavior, helping to understand *how* the attention mechanism operates.  The analysis of gate information content provides a quantitative validation of the attention mechanism.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of SNES, while computationally intensive, is justified given the scale of the policy parameter space. The ablation study regarding the number of steps (Figure 2) demonstrates the stability of the learned dynamics.
*   **Biologically Inspired:** The model draws inspiration from the known functionalities of the human visual system and builds a new architecture that aims to mimic these functionalities.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** A primary concern is the lack of results on larger, more challenging datasets, such as ImageNet (ILSVRC). While the authors acknowledge the computational cost, demonstrating scalability is crucial for establishing the practical relevance of dasNet. The CIFAR datasets, though important benchmarks, represent a relatively constrained problem space.
*   **Failure Cases & Robustness:** The paper lacks a thorough investigation of failure cases or potential limitations of the learned attention policy.  Demonstrating where the model struggles and *why* would strengthen the analysis.  Are there specific image characteristics or classes that consistently lead to incorrect classifications, even with the attention mechanism?
*   **Complexity:**  DasNet is considerably more complex than standard feedforward CNNs. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational overhead associated with the iterative processing and SNES training.  
*   **Limited Policy Analysis:** The description of the learned policy is somewhat limited. A deeper analysis of the policy’s structure and the types of features it learns to attend to would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to deep learning. The integration of attention mechanisms inspired by biological vision, combined with the use of SNES for policy learning, is a compelling combination. The achieved performance on CIFAR datasets is impressive, and the visualizations provide valuable insights into the model's behavior. However, the lack of results on larger datasets and a more thorough analysis of failure cases represent significant limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and interesting approach to deep learning.
*   The achieved performance is state-of-the-art on the CIFAR datasets.
*   The visualizations provide valuable insights into the model’s internal workings.
*   The work has the potential to advance the field of biologically inspired deep learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of scalability demonstrated through results on larger datasets.
*   The limited analysis of failure cases and potential limitations.
*   The increased complexity compared to standard CNNs without a corresponding demonstration of significantly improved performance on challenging real-world problems.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should be encouraged to address the scalability concerns by conducting experiments on a larger dataset (even a limited subset of ImageNet would be valuable). They should also investigate and discuss potential failure cases, and provide a more detailed analysis of the learned attention policy. Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
