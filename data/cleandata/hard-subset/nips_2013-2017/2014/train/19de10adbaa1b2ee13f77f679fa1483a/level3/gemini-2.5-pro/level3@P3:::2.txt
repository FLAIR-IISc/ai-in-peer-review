This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable the network to perform multiple processing passes on a single input image, dynamically modulating the activations of its convolutional filters at each step. This internal selective attention mechanism is implemented as a policy that maps the network's internal state (average map activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities) to a set of multiplicative gates applied to the filter outputs. The parameters of this policy are trained using a reinforcement learning approach, specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to minimize classification error after a fixed number of iterations. The work is motivated by recurrent processing in the human visual system and aims to improve performance on difficult classification cases that are not well-handled by a single feed-forward pass. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, outperforming the baseline Maxout network.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and forward-looking problem: how to move beyond static, fixed-parameter deep networks towards more dynamic and adaptive computational models. The idea of learning an internal policy to reconfigure network processing for specific inputs is a significant conceptual step, addressing the limitations of purely feed-forward architectures.
2.  **Performance:** The empirical results are impressive. Achieving a 6% relative improvement over a strong Maxout network baseline on CIFAR-10 is a notable achievement and establishes a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data at the time of publication. This demonstrates that the proposed recurrent, attentional processing is not just conceptually interesting but practically effective.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The high-level motivation and the general description of the dasNet architecture are presented clearly. The connection to attentional mechanisms in biological vision is well-motivated and provides useful context for the work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Interpretability and Analysis:** A major weakness is that the model is presented as a black box. While the performance gains are clear, the paper offers very little insight into *why* the method works. The analysis is superficial and fails to illuminate the learned attentional strategy.
    *   The visualization in Figure 3 is a good start but is insufficient. The analysis of changes in layer activations is vague ("complex suppression and enhancement patterns"). A deeper investigation is needed. For example, why do layer-1 activations seem to increase on the first iteration? What is the effect of the global scaling in the softmax for the action vector (Eq. 7)? How do the gating variables `a` evolve over time for specific, localized regions of an image?
    *   **Suggestion:** A crucial analysis would be to focus on "hard cases"—images that the baseline Maxout network misclassifies but dasNet corrects. Visualizing the sequence of gating actions and resulting feature map activations for these specific examples could provide concrete evidence of a meaningful attentional strategy.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper analyzes the information content of the final gate values but does not explore the learned policy parameters (`theta`). What do the learned mean and variance of the SNES search distribution reveal about the policy? An experiment constraining these statistics (e.g., forcing lower variance) could shed light on the sensitivity of the policy.

2.  **Conceptual Framing (Reinforcement Learning vs. Recurrent Network):** The framing of the problem as reinforcement learning is questionable and potentially misleading. The model appears much closer in spirit to a recurrent neural network (RNN) or a dynamical system unfolded in time.
    *   In this setup, the "reward" is simply the negative log-likelihood at the final time step `T`. It is immediate, not delayed. The policy `pi_theta` is deterministic, and the state transitions are also deterministic. This setup lacks the core challenges of RL, such as temporal credit assignment and the exploration-exploitation trade-off.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by exploring and discussing the connection to RNNs. The policy network `pi_theta` acts as the transition function of an RNN that updates the hidden state (the CNN's internal activations). Framing it this way could connect the work to the vast literature on training and analyzing RNNs and may suggest alternative training methods to SNES (e.g., backpropagation through time), which would be an interesting comparison.

3.  **Technical Clarity and Typos:** Several aspects of the methodology are unclear, hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The function `h(M_t)` used to construct the observation vector `o_t` is not defined. It is also ambiguous whether the fitness values `F[i]` and parameters `Θ[i]` are collected for each image `j` (and then averaged) or if they are overwritten in the inner loop, with only the last image's result being used.
    *   **Equations:**
        *   In Eq. 11, the summation index is `i`, but `i` is also the index for the candidate policy `theta_i`. This should likely be summed over `j` (the image index).
        *   In Eq. 8, the loss `L_i` is defined for a single policy `theta_i`, but it depends on `v_T` and `d`, which are specific to a single image. The equation should probably include an index for the image `j`, e.g., `L_{i,j}`.
        *   The symbols `x` and `d` used in the text immediately following Eq. 11 are not defined in that context.
    *   **Regularization:** The L2 regularization on `theta` (Eq. 11) is introduced without justification. Why is this specific form of regularization necessary or beneficial for the policy parameters? Furthermore, it's unclear how the final SNES updates are computed. The paper states SNES updates its distribution, but does it directly adjust the posterior hyperparameters, and how is the gradient of the regularized fitness function used?

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents an interesting and effective method for incorporating recurrent, attentional processing into deep CNNs. The achievement of state-of-the-art results demonstrates the practical value of the approach. However, the work is significantly hampered by a lack of deep analysis and interpretability, as well as several issues with technical clarity. While the core idea is a valuable contribution, the paper in its current form feels incomplete. A stronger connection to the literature on recurrent networks, a more thorough analysis of the model's internal dynamics, and clarification of the algorithmic details would be necessary to elevate this from an intriguing empirical result to a well-rounded and impactful scientific contribution.
