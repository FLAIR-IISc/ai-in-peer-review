- Introduces dasNet: adds feedback gating to feedforward CNNs to enable selective internal attention during classification.  
- Implements gating by modulating Maxout filter sensitivities and optimizes a million-dimensional policy with SNES.  
- Key innovation: iterative multi-pass refinement over the same image to emphasize/de-emphasize filters, addressing CNNs’ static inference limitation.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains: state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 (6% relative improvement) and CIFAR-100 without augmentation.  
- Positions work relative to neuroscience and prior selective attention and RL literature, while augmenting existing CNNs rather than designing new architectures.  
- Strength: impressive empirical performance on challenging tasks with appropriate baselines and experimental design.  
- Technical choice of SNES is reasonable for high-dimensional policy optimization.  
- Major weakness: poor interpretability — the model is a black box with little explanation of why attention improves classification.  
- Analysis of internal representations is insufficient and fails to link activation changes to classification improvements.  
- Lacks case studies on hard examples that dasNet corrects and comparisons of gating patterns between correct/incorrect cases.  
- Algorithmic/theoretical clarity issues: Algorithm 1 contains undefined terms and ambiguous variables, and several equations have indexing/symbol-definition errors.  
- Regularization term and final gradient/update procedure are unjustified or unclear.  
- The RL framing is questionable given deterministic policy and immediate reward structure; method may align more with dynamical/recurrent systems.  
- Exposition is generally clear but technical details and analysis of gating dynamics (e.g., layer response patterns, global scaling) need substantial improvement.  
- Recommendation: deepen analysis of learned attention (gating statistics, constraints, hard-case behaviors) and clarify theoretical foundations to move beyond empirical novelty.  
- Overall: promising and empirically strong, but scientific impact limited by lack of interpretability, theoretical clarity, and detailed internal analysis.
