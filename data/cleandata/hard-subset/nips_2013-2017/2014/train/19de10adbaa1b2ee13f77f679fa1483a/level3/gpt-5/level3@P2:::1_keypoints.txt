- Proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet): multiplicative, recurrent gating of convolutional feature maps with a policy learned by SNES on top of a Maxout CNN; iterates attention over multiple passes to disambiguate hard examples.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on unaugmented CIFAR-10/100 and provides qualitative analyses of gating dynamics and stability.  

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that operationalizes top-down modulatory feedback in modern CNNs.  
- Simple, differentiable gating mechanism compatible with existing convnets that improves difficult-case predictions.  
- Pragmatic use of SNES to train very high-dimensional policies and boosting to focus learning on misclassified examples.  
- Empirical gains (SOTA on unaugmented CIFARs) with stable dynamics and informative visualizations.  
- Paper is clearly written with sufficient algorithmic detail and hyperparameter transparency.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical scope: only evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, no larger-scale or higher-resolution datasets (e.g., ImageNet).  
- Incomplete baselines and ablations: no comparison to BPTT/gradient-based policy training or simpler policy architectures.  
- No control for extra computation: lacks equal-FLOPs/latency baselines (deeper nets, ensembling, multi-pass without feedback).  
- Observation design under-analyzed: averaging map activations may discard spatial structure; sensitivity to observation features not tested.  
- RL choice (SNES) not fully justified given differentiability of policy and network; end-to-end recurrent training could be competitive.  
- SOTA claim limited to unaugmented protocol; unclear competitiveness under standard augmentation.  
- Computational cost and latency not reported despite multi-pass test-time (T=5).  

Quality:
- Technically sound with coherent learning objective and stable gating parameterization, but missing ablations limit attribution of gains to feedback.  

Clarity:
- Well organized and readable; algorithmic description is clear, though some SNES details are deferred to supplement; needs runtime and parameter-count reporting.  

Originality:
- Components are known, but their integration (feedback-based internal attention learned via evolutionary search producing SOTA) is novel and relevant.  

Significance:
- Moderate to high: provides empirical support for recurrent top-down modulation improving CNNs, but broader impact depends on scalability and standard-protocol comparisons.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or higher-resolution variants and report compute/latency trade-offs per T.  
- Add ablations: linear vs. nonlinear policies; policy trained by BPTT vs. SNES; random/static gates; equal-compute baselines (deeper net, augmentation, ensembles).  
- Report parameter counts and wall-clock costs for base net vs. dasNet; analyze sensitivity to T and early stopping by confidence.  
- Explore richer observations (spatial summaries) and spatially selective actions; test robustness with data augmentation.  
- Provide per-class confusion improvements and calibration metrics to show where feedback helps most.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually compelling, biologically motivated integration of feedback into CNNs.  
- Demonstrated empirical gains on standard benchmarks with clear methodology.  
- General, modular technique likely to stimulate further work on learned feedback in vision models.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation scope and missing strong baselines make it hard to isolate the source of improvement.  
- Unclear scalability and efficiency on larger datasets; reliance on evolutionary search may be unnecessary.  

Overall assessment:
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — well executed with interesting results but would benefit from stronger empirical evaluation and ablations.  

Reviewer confidence:
- High on deep learning and attention; medium on evolutionary optimization specifics.
