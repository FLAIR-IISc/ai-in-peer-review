

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends CNNs with selective attention gating through feedback connections, allowing the network to iteratively focus on relevant features during classification. The authors frame this as a non-stationary CNN that adapts its behavior post-training using reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) to modulate filter sensitivities. The approach represents an important step beyond fixed-parameter feed-forward networks, as it enables the network to "think" about ambiguous classifications through sequential processing.

The paper's main strength lies in its impressive empirical results: dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over state-of-the-art CNNs on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation. This demonstrates the practical value of incorporating adaptive attention mechanisms. The architecture is well-motivated by biological visual processing, and the exposition is generally clear.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in quality and clarity. The method functions as a black box with little explanation of why it works—critical for interpretability in deep learning. The analysis of learned theta parameters (mean and variance) is inadequate and uninformative, failing to explain why certain filter weightings improve classification. Several equations contain typos (e.g., Eq.11's summation index) or require clarification (Eq.8 may need an image index j). Algorithm 1 is unclear: h_M is undefined, and it's ambiguous whether F[i] and Θ[i] are collected per image or overwritten. Notation problems persist with symbols x and d after Eq.11 remaining undefined.

The paper also lacks deeper analysis of internal dynamics—why layer-1 activations increase on first iteration, the effect of global scaling, and evolution of gating variables in local image regions. The authors should analyze hard cases (misclassified by standard Maxout but corrected by dasNet) and test constraints on learned theta statistics. The necessity of theta regularization is questionable, and it's unclear how final gradient updates compute or adjust posterior hyperparameters.

While the approach is interesting and effective with strong accuracy gains, the interpretability shortcomings and theoretical/algorithmic ambiguities prevent it from making a fully compelling contribution. The work is original in its application of SNES to attention gating but falls short in providing the necessary theoretical grounding and analysis expected at NIPS. The paper would benefit substantially from addressing these issues before acceptance.
