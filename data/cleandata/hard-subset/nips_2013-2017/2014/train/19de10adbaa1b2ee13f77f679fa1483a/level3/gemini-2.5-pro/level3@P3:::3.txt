This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with top-down feedback connections. The central idea is to mimic the two-stage process of human vision: a rapid, pre-attentive feedforward pass followed by a slower, attentional phase involving recurrent processing. In dasNet, a pre-trained Maxout CNN performs an initial classification. Subsequently, over several time steps, a policy network modulates the activations of the CNN's convolutional filters via learned gates. This policy, trained using reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies), learns to selectively emphasize or suppress features to refine the network's prediction, effectively creating an internal "spotlight of attention." This approach connects to a rich history of work on the role of feedback in the visual cortex and prior computational models using RL for attention, but its novelty lies in integrating these concepts directly into a modern, state-of-the-art deep learning architecture to improve classification performance on challenging benchmarks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and conceptually interesting model. The integration of a top-down, attention-based feedback loop trained with RL into a powerful feedforward CNN is a significant departure from the purely feedforward models that dominated the field. This forward-looking approach, combining fast bottom-up signals with slower top-down refinement, opens a promising direction for building more sophisticated and biologically plausible vision systems.
*   **Technical Quality and Performance:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (for unaugmented data), outperforming a strong Maxout network baseline. This is a non-trivial achievement and provides strong empirical validation for the utility of the proposed feedback mechanism.
*   **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The inclusion of visualizations (Figure 3) is particularly helpful for interpreting the model's behavior. Showing how the filter activations are modulated over time to correct an initial misclassification provides valuable insight into the learned attention policy. Further experiments analyzing the model's dynamics over time and the information content of the learned gates strengthen the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful strategy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The primary weakness of the work is the lack of evaluation on larger-scale datasets like ILSVRC (ImageNet). While the CIFAR results are strong, the proposed method introduces significant computational complexity due to the iterative processing (T=5 passes) and the separate RL training phase. It is unclear if the performance gains would justify the substantial increase in inference time and training complexity on a dataset with 1000 classes and higher-resolution images. Demonstrating scalability on ILSVRC would be crucial for establishing the practical viability of this approach.
*   **Analysis of the Attention Policy:** The quality of the network's representation and its final prediction hinges critically on the learned attention policy. The paper would be strengthened by a more critical analysis of this component. The authors should consider discussing or demonstrating potential failure cases. For example, can the policy amplify noisy or non-discriminative features, leading to a worse prediction than the initial feedforward pass? Are there mechanisms to prevent the policy from "forgetting" an initially correct classification? A deeper exploration of the policy's limitations and robustness would be valuable.
*   **Choice of RL Algorithm:** The use of SNES is justified by the high dimensionality of the parameter space. However, as a black-box optimization algorithm, it can be sample-inefficient. A brief discussion of why more conventional gradient-based RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) were deemed unsuitable would provide additional context and strengthen the methodological justification.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and thought-provoking architecture. The combination of deep CNNs with an RL-trained attentional feedback mechanism is a compelling idea, backed by strong empirical results on CIFAR. The main concern is the method's scalability and practical utility for larger problems, which is not addressed. Despite this limitation, the novelty of the model and its potential to inspire future research into more dynamic and recurrent deep learning models makes it a valuable contribution. The model is certainly worth further study by the community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Proposes a highly novel and interesting architecture that integrates bottom-up and top-down signals.
2.  Achieves state-of-the-art results on established benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100).
3.  The concept of iterative refinement via an internal attention mechanism is a significant and forward-looking research direction.
4.  The paper includes good analysis and visualizations that aid in model interpretation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  Significant questions remain about the method's scalability and computational cost, with no experiments on large-scale datasets like ImageNet.
2.  The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the potential failure modes of the learned attention policy.

Given the balance of strengths and weaknesses, I recommend acceptance. The originality and significance of the core idea outweigh the concerns about scalability, which can be addressed in future work.
