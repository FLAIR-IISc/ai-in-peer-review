This paper proposes a convolutional network augmented with feedback connections that multiplicatively gate the activations of feature maps across iterations, enabling internal selective attention. The gating policy is learned via reinforcement learning using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) on top of a supervisedly trained Maxout CNN. At test time, the model processes an image for T passes, using top-down signals to enhance or suppress channel activations, achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented). The work is well positioned within a line of research on recurrent processing and attention in vision and on RL-driven attention control (e.g., selective saccades and feedback control cited in the paper), while integrating these ideas into a modern convnet backbone.

Quality: The method is technically sound and clearly described, with a concrete training algorithm and a principled policy-learning objective. Results on CIFAR are strong, and the dynamics analysis is informative. However, the evaluation would benefit from stronger baselines and broader ablations (e.g., backprop-through-time training of the recurrent gates, a purely supervised per-channel gating baseline, multi-pass feedforward ensembling, and curriculum/boosting on hard examples). Reporting compute and latency overhead for T>1, sensitivity to T, and multiple seeds/significance tests would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The interaction between observation features and actions is explained, and design choices (SNES, choice of observation vector) are motivated. A brief diagram clarifying the exact gating granularity (per-channel, spatially uniform) and where gates are injected in each layer would improve accessibility.

Originality: No single component is novel, but the combined architecture—channel-wise feedback attention trained with black-box policy search atop a strong CNN—is well chosen and convincingly executed. The connection to biological feedback is appropriate and supported by citations.

Significance: The empirical gains on CIFAR and the demonstration that feedback/attention can correct ambiguous cases provide valuable evidence that recurrent attention is a promising direction for vision. To solidify impact, scaling to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and assessing robustness under distribution shift would be important next steps.

Pros:
- Simple, coherent integration of feedback attention with strong empirical gains.
- Clear exposition and biologically grounded motivation.
- Reinforcement learning used pragmatically at scale via SNES.

Cons:
- Limited baselines and ablations; unclear trade-offs in compute/latency.
- Evaluation confined to CIFAR; unclear scalability.
- Black-box RL choice vs gradient-based recurrent training not compared.

Recommendation: Promising and well executed; encourage acceptance, with a request to expand baselines and to explore scaling to larger datasets.
