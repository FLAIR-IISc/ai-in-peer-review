This paper introduces dasNet, a convolutional neural network augmented with feedback connections and trained using reinforcement learning to dynamically adjust filter activations for improved classification performance. While no single component is novel – convolutional networks, reinforcement learning, and feedback connections have all been explored extensively – the combination is well-executed and demonstrates a promising direction for attention mechanisms in deep vision models. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100, empirically supporting the hypothesis that feedback and attention can enhance CNN performance, particularly in challenging cases.

The work builds on a strong foundation of prior research. The authors correctly position their contribution within the broader context of both biologically inspired vision (Felleman & Essen, Douglas) and computer vision advancements (LeCun, Krizhevsky et al., Goodfellow et al.). They acknowledge the existing work on recurrent connections and reinforcement learning applied to vision (e.g., Denil, O'Reilly, Stollenga), clearly differentiating their approach by applying this paradigm directly to state-of-the-art networks like Maxout to achieve performance improvements. The related work section is comprehensive and provides necessary context.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Technical Approach:** The integration of reinforcement learning (SNES) with a convolutional network and feedback connections is logically sound and effectively implemented. The use of SNES is a pragmatic choice given the high dimensionality of the policy space.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100, even with unaugmented data, is a significant accomplishment. This provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The explanation of dasNet, the RL framework, and the experimental setup is clear and concise. The inclusion of Figure 3, visualizing the activation changes, is particularly helpful for understanding the model's behavior.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The dynamics study (Figure 2) and the gate information content analysis provide valuable insights into how dasNet learns and utilizes its attentional mechanism.
* **Good Justification:** The motivation for exploring feedback connections, drawing inspiration from human visual perception, is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability Demonstrated:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10/100. While achieving state-of-the-art results on these datasets is commendable, the real test of the approach lies in its scalability to larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet. The computational cost (4 days training on a GTX 560 Ti) raises concerns about feasibility on larger scales.
* **Black Box Nature:** While the authors provide some visualization of activation changes, understanding *why* the network focuses attention on specific filters remains a challenge. Further analysis of the learned policy and the resulting filter activations would be beneficial.
* **SNES Complexity:** While justified, the use of SNES adds complexity. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between SNES and other RL algorithms (e.g., policy gradients) would be helpful.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** Additional ablation studies investigating the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., T, λ values) and architectural choices would strengthen the analysis.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with claims well-supported by experimental results. It presents a complete piece of work that goes beyond a simple position paper. The authors are careful to evaluate the performance and acknowledge the limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are easy to follow.

**Originality:** The originality lies in the *combination* of existing techniques – feedback connections, reinforcement learning, and high-performing convolutional networks – and the successful demonstration of their synergistic effect. While individual components are not new, the combined approach is novel and effective.

**Significance:** The results are significant, providing empirical support for the potential of feedback and attention mechanisms in vision models. The work could inspire further research in this area, potentially leading to more robust and efficient vision systems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100.
* Demonstrates a promising approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs.
* Well-written, clear, and technically sound.
* Provides insightful analysis of the learned behavior.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited scalability demonstrated.
* The "black box" nature of the learned policy hinders interpretability.
* Computational cost may be prohibitive for large-scale applications.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the scalability concerns by providing a discussion of potential strategies for scaling the approach to larger datasets (e.g., distributed training, model compression). They should also consider adding more analysis to shed light on the learned policy and the resulting filter activations. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimizations would also be valuable. Finally, expanding the ablation studies would further strengthen the paper. Scaling to ImageNet is strongly encouraged as future work.
