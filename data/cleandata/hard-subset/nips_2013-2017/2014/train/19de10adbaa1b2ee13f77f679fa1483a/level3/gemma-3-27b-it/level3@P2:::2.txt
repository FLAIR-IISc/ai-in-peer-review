This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into deep convolutional neural networks, enabling dynamic adjustment of filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus its attention on relevant features, inspired by observations of attentional mechanisms in the human visual cortex. This adaptation is achieved through reinforcement learning, specifically using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to learn a control policy governing the feedback connections. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, outperforming previous models on unaugmented data.

This work builds upon the established success of CNNs [1, 7] and the growing interest in incorporating biologically plausible mechanisms, like recurrent connections [28, 29, 30], into deep learning models. It differentiates itself from prior work in attention mechanisms [40, 41, 42, 43, 44, 45] by directly learning to modulate filter activations within a standard CNN architecture, rather than focusing on spatial attention or external feedback loops.  The use of SNES for policy optimization is notable, addressing the challenge of training a high-dimensional control policy.  Related efforts using recurrent processing for image tasks [46, 47, 48, 49, 50] often operate on simplified datasets; this work's application to CIFAR-10/100 is a significant step forward.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of learned feedback connections within a CNN framework is a genuinely novel approach to enhancing classification performance. It moves beyond the limitations of static, feedforward networks.
* **Performance:** The reported state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The 6% relative improvement is substantial.
* **Conceptual Appeal:** The motivation, drawing parallels to the human visual system and the benefits of iterative processing, is well-articulated and provides a strong rationale for the research.
* **Practicality:** While complex, the method is implemented and evaluated on standard datasets, indicating its feasibility.
* **Addressing a Key Limitation:** DasNet directly tackles the limitations of fixed-parameter CNNs, offering a mechanism for adaptive reconfiguration, which is crucial for handling challenging or ambiguous inputs.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Interpretability:** The "black box" nature of the learned attention policy is a significant drawback. While the model *performs* well, understanding *why* it works remains elusive. The analysis of filter activations and gating variables is superficial.
* **Limited Analysis:** The exploration of internal dynamics is insufficient.  The authors briefly mention observed dynamics (e.g., increased layer-1 activations) but lack a deeper investigation into the underlying mechanisms. The effect of global scaling and the evolution of gating variables in specific image regions are unexplored.
* **Algorithmic Clarity:** Algorithm 1 suffers from ambiguities. The function *h<sub>M</sub>* is undefined, and it’s unclear if *F[i]* and *Θ[i]* represent accumulated values or are overwritten in each iteration.
* **Equation Issues:** Several equations contain minor errors or lack sufficient clarity.  Specifically, the summation index in Eq. 11 is ambiguous, and Eq. 8 potentially requires an image index *j*. Notation issues arise after Eq. 11 with undefined symbols *x* and *d*.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The justification for theta regularization is weak, and the details of gradient computation and posterior hyperparameter adjustment are missing.
* **Connections to Dynamical Systems:** The authors miss a crucial opportunity to frame dasNet within the context of dynamical systems.  The architecture, with its recurrent connections and deterministic policy, is arguably closer to a recurrent neural network than a reinforcement learning agent, given the immediate reward structure.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, though the aforementioned algorithmic and equation issues detract from its rigor.  The experimental setup appears well-controlled, and the reported results are statistically significant.
* **Clarity:** The exposition is generally clear, but the ambiguities in Algorithm 1 and the equation errors hinder comprehension. More detailed explanations of the learning process and the role of the different components would be beneficial.
* **Originality:** The approach is highly original, representing a significant departure from traditional CNN architectures.
* **Significance:** The results are important, demonstrating the potential for improving CNN performance through adaptive attention mechanisms. This work could inspire further research into more interpretable and efficient methods for incorporating feedback into deep learning models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks.
* Introduces a novel and promising architecture for adaptive image classification.
* Provides a compelling rationale based on biological plausibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Lack of interpretability and in-depth analysis of the learned attention mechanisms.
* Algorithmic ambiguities and minor equation errors.
* Missed connection to relevant work in dynamical systems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and effective piece of work that advances the field of deep learning by introducing a recurrent-style approach to CNNs. The strong accuracy gains are impressive, but the lack of interpretability and algorithmic clarity are significant weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon addressing the identified ambiguities and providing a more thorough analysis of the learned attention policy and its dynamics. Specifically, the authors should clarify Algorithm 1 and the equations, address the theoretical justification for theta regularization, and explore the connections to dynamical systems. A deeper dive into the internal workings of dasNet is crucial to unlock its full potential and foster further research in this promising direction.
