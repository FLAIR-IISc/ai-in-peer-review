

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment standard CNNs with feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its internal filter sensitivities during classification. The authors propose that by training a policy to selectively enhance or suppress features through these feedback connections, the network can improve its performance on difficult classification cases. The approach builds on Maxout networks and uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the attention policy, as standard RL methods are infeasible for the million-dimensional parameter space. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation, demonstrating the potential of internal selective attention in deep networks.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions such as Goodfellow et al.'s Maxout networks (ICML 2013), Ciresan et al.'s deep networks for image classification (NIPS 2012), and Schmidhuber's work on learning to attend (NIPS 2012). It also connects to neuroscience literature on top-down visual processing (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme, 2000), though the paper could better distinguish its approach from existing recurrent network architectures.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Performance Gains**: The method achieves a 6% relative improvement over the baseline Maxout network on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented datasets. This demonstrates the practical value of the approach.

2. **Novel Architecture**: The idea of adding selective attention through feedback connections to CNNs represents a meaningful departure from traditional stationary feedforward networks. This addresses an important limitation of current CNNs that cannot adapt their behavior post-training.

3. **Feasibility Demonstration**: The paper successfully demonstrates that learning attention policies for large networks is feasible using SNES, making a compelling case for the potential of adaptive neural networks.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Interpretability**: While the method achieves strong results, it functions as a "black box" with minimal explanation of *why* it works. The analysis of internal dynamics (e.g., how gating parameters influence classification) is superficial, and the paper fails to provide meaningful insights into the learned attention mechanisms.

2. **Theoretical and Algorithmic Clarity**: Several issues undermine the paper's technical clarity:
   - Algorithm 1 contains undefined symbols (h_M) and ambiguous implementation details (whether F[i] and Θ[i] are collected per image or overwritten)
   - Equations contain typos and unclear notation (Eq.8 may need image index j, Eq.11's summation index is unclear, symbols x and d after Eq.11 are undefined)
   - The connection to reinforcement learning is tenuous; the dynamics more closely resemble recurrent processing with immediate rewards rather than sequential decision-making

3. **Inadequate Analysis**: The paper lacks deeper analysis of the learned policy parameters (mean and variance of theta), the dynamics of gating variables, and the effect of global scaling. The analysis of "hard cases" (images misclassified by the baseline but corrected by dasNet) is particularly limited.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The method demonstrates significant empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on challenging datasets
- It represents an important conceptual step beyond fixed-parameter feedforward networks
- The approach opens new avenues for research in adaptive neural networks
- The paper's core idea (internal selective attention through feedback) is novel and well-motivated

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The paper fails to adequately explain why the approach works, limiting its scientific contribution
- The theoretical justification for the RL approach is weak, and the connection to reinforcement learning is superficial
- Several technical issues (equation typos, undefined symbols, unclear algorithm description) reduce the paper's credibility
- The analysis of internal dynamics and learned parameters is insufficient to support the claims

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Clarify the connection to reinforcement learning versus recurrent networks. The paper appears closer to recurrent architectures with immediate rewards than to standard RL.

2. Provide deeper analysis of internal dynamics: 
   - Analyze why layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration
   - Investigate the effect of global scaling on gating variables
   - Examine how gating variables evolve across local image regions

3. Address the technical issues:
   - Fix equation typos and clarify notation
   - Define all symbols explicitly
   - Clarify Algorithm 1's implementation details

4. Perform a more thorough analysis of hard cases and the learned policy parameters. Show how the attention mechanism specifically corrects misclassifications.

5. Justify the necessity of theta regularization and clarify how gradient updates are computed.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting and effective approach that demonstrates significant performance gains over state-of-the-art CNNs. While the method is conceptually compelling and achieves strong empirical results, the lack of interpretability and several technical shortcomings prevent it from being a strong scientific contribution. The work represents an important step in the evolution of neural network architectures toward adaptive, attention-based systems, but the current version lacks the theoretical clarity and depth of analysis required for a top-tier NIPS paper. With substantial revisions addressing the interpretability and technical issues raised above, this could become a strong contribution. As is, the paper is borderline for acceptance, leaning toward rejection due to the critical weaknesses in explanation and technical presentation.
