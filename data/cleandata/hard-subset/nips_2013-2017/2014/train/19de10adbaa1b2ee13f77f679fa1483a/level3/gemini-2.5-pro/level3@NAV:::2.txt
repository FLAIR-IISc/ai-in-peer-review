This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that adds recurrent feedback connections and selective attention to traditional feed-forward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Drawing inspiration from the recurrent and attentional processing in the human visual system, the method augments a pre-trained Maxout network with gating parameters that modulate filter activations over successive passes. These parameters are trained via a reinforcement learning approach (specifically, Natural Evolution Strategies) to reconfigure the network's focus adaptively, aiming to correct misclassifications made on the initial feed-forward pass. This work builds upon the large body of literature on deep CNNs and connects to prior work on recurrent models and attention, but is novel in applying this type of iterative, internal modulation to a state-of-the-art classification model.

The primary strength of this paper is its impressive empirical results. The reported accuracy gains on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are significant and demonstrate the potential of moving beyond fixed-parameter, single-pass networks. Conceptually, this is an important contribution that pushes the field toward more dynamic and adaptive models. However, the paper's main weakness is a lack of interpretability and analysis. While the model works well, it functions as a black box with little explanation for *why* it succeeds. The analysis of the learned theta parameters and internal image representations is superficial and does not provide insight into the mechanism of action.

A major suggestion is to explore the model's connections to dynamical systems. The authors frame the approach as reinforcement learning, but given the immediate reward and deterministic policy, it more closely resembles a recurrent neural network. Reframing the work in this context could provide a clearer theoretical grounding. The paper would be substantially improved by a deeper analysis of the model's internal dynamics. For instance, why do layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration? What is the effect of the global scaling in the softmax? A qualitative analysis of "hard cases"—images misclassified by the baseline Maxout but corrected by dasNet—would be highly informative.

The manuscript's clarity also needs improvement. Algorithm 1 is ambiguous: h_M is undefined, and it is unclear if F[i] and Θ[i] are collected per-image or overwritten. Several equations have issues: the summation index in Eq. 11 is not specified, Eq. 8 may require an image index, and the symbols x and d are used after Eq. 11 without definition. The justification for theta regularization is also unclear.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and effective recurrent-style attention mechanism for CNNs that achieves strong performance. However, its acceptance is conditional on a significant revision that addresses the lack of interpretability with deeper analysis and corrects the numerous clarity issues in the equations and algorithm descriptions.
