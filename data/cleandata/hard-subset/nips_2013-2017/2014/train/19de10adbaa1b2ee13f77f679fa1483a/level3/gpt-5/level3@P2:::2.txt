Summary and relation to prior work
This paper augments a strong feedforward Maxout CNN with learned feedback that multiplicatively gates channel activations in every convolutional (and classification) layer over multiple passes on the same image. The action vector a scales feature maps; an observation vector o is constructed from average map activations and top-layer signals; a deterministic policy πθ maps o→a; and the process is repeated for T steps. The policy parameters θ (≈10^6) are optimized by Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to minimize boosted cross-entropy on difficult examples. On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented test), the method improves over a Maxout baseline and prior single-model SOTA.

The approach connects to a long line of work on top-down and recurrent processing in vision (e.g., O’Reilly’s recirculation, feedback in cortex), attention and RL for vision (glimpses/saccades), and recurrent or iterative inference in CNNs. Relative to earlier selective-attention models for vision that often used simplified settings, this work applies feedback to a modern CNN and demonstrates accuracy gains on standard benchmarks.

Quality
- Empirical: The gains on CIFAR-10/100 are noteworthy for a single model without test-time augmentation/ensembles. The dynamics experiment (varying T) suggests stability beyond the training horizon.
- Methodological soundness: The control mechanism is simple and well-motivated (multiplicative gates constrained to be positive and centered around 1). However, key algorithmic and notation details are ambiguous, and the analysis of why/when the feedback helps is limited. The choice of SNES (diagonal covariance) for million-parameter policies is pragmatic but raises questions about sample efficiency and optimization quality vs. alternatives (BPTT, policy gradient).
- Ablations/diagnostics: Currently insufficient. The paper would benefit from systematic ablations (e.g., random or fixed gates, layer-restricted gating, different observation subsets, effect of L2 on θ, alternative normalizations).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but several equations and the training algorithm need clarification:
- Algorithm 1: h(Mt) is not defined; please define hM precisely (inputs, outputs, dimensions). It is also ambiguous whether F[i] and Θ[i] are accumulated per candidate across the entire batch or overwritten per image; the code block suggests f(θi) is computed after summing losses over n images, but lines 14–15 appear redundant unless they store per-candidate results for SNES.
- Eq. 8–11: Indexing is unclear. In Eq. 8, the loss should be Lij or similar to indicate image j; in Eq. 11, the summation index should range over the batch images (j=1..n), not i (already used for candidate index). After Eq. 11, x and d are used without explicit definition in that context; please define them when first used in the loss.
- Notation around vt = Mt(θ, x) conflates the base network parameters with the policy parameters; consider explicit separation: Mφ with fixed φ; policy πθ; Mt denotes the same φ with gates at time t.
- “Gradient updates”: The text occasionally reads as if gradients of θ are computed; with SNES, the natural gradient is over the search distribution parameters (µ,Σ), not θ directly. Please make explicit what is updated each generation (µ and diagonal Σ) and what is held fixed (φ, the Maxout CNN).

Originality
- The idea of recurrent/feedback modulation is not new, but the particular combination—channel-wise multiplicative gating over all layers, deterministic policy driven by internal activations, and large-scale black-box optimization (SNES) on contemporary CNNs—is novel and timely. The contribution is more architectural/pragmatic than theoretical.

Significance
- The demonstrated improvements on CIFAR-10/100 and the demonstration that iterative, internally attentive processing can correct feedforward mistakes make this work significant for the community. If the method scales and the dynamics are better understood, it can influence how we design CNNs with feedback.
- The main limitation is interpretability and incomplete analysis of the learned dynamics, which currently reduces the broader impact.

Strengths
- Clear, biologically inspired motivation; simple mechanism (multiplicative gates) compatible with existing CNNs.
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks for single models without test-time augmentation/ensembles.
- Evidence of stable dynamics beyond the training horizon.
- Practical training recipe with SNES in a very high-dimensional policy space.

Weaknesses
- Limited insight into why the feedback helps; current visualization/analysis is largely anecdotal.
- Algorithmic/notation ambiguities (Algorithm 1; Eq. 8–11; undefined symbols).
- The method is presented as RL, but with deterministic policy and immediate terminal loss it is closer to training a recurrent dynamical system by black-box optimization; the RL framing is not essential and can distract.
- Justification for θ regularization (L2) is not provided; no ablation of regularization strength. The role of global scaling via softmax normalization is not analyzed.
- Lack of fairness checks and ablations (e.g., identity vs. random gating, layer-wise gating, alternative optimizers).

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Clarify Algorithm 1 and notation (hM, indices, storage of F and Θ, definition of x and d at the point of use). State explicitly what SNES updates (µ,Σdiag) and that φ is frozen.
- Provide ablations:
  - T=0/1 baselines, and variable T at test time; early halting when confident.
  - Identity gates vs. random gates vs. learned gates.
  - Gating only top layer vs. all layers; per-layer normalization vs. global softmax; remove/alter L2(θ).
  - Replace SNES with (i) supervised gradient on θ through unrolled T steps (BPTT), (ii) policy gradient, to test whether SNES is necessary.
- Analyze internal dynamics with a dynamical-systems lens:
  - Fixed points and stability (e.g., spectral radius of Jacobian of the gated recurrence).
  - Evolution of layer-wise mean activations across t (why do layer-1 activations initially increase?).
  - Effect of the softmax-normalized, positive gates: disentangle global gain vs. selective modulation; consider per-layer or per-block normalization to control trivial global scaling.
- Interpretability:
  - Statistics of gates (per class, per layer): means, variances, principal components; mutual information between gates and labels.
  - Trajectories of gates on “hard” cases (misclassified by Maxout but corrected by dasNet) with confusion-matrix deltas and qualitative exemplars.
  - Constrain θ (e.g., low variance, sparsity) and report performance to test whether strong selectivity or smooth modulation is key.
- Computational aspects:
  - Report training/evaluation cost relative to baseline; discuss population size vs. performance; sensitivity to diagonal Σ.
  - Evaluate selective multiple passes only on uncertain examples to reduce test-time cost.

Pro/Con arguments
Pros:
- Meaningful accuracy gains on standard datasets with a conceptually simple mechanism.
- Brings feedback/attention to mainstream CNNs in a scalable way.
- Stable dynamics and sensible design choices (positive gates, centered around unity).

Cons:
- Insufficient theoretical/algorithmic clarity and several notation errors.
- Limited analysis of what is learned and why it works; method feels like a black box.
- RL framing is arguably unnecessary; the approach is effectively optimizing a deterministic recurrent controller with terminal loss.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances the state of the art and makes a compelling case for learned internal selective attention via feedback in deep networks, but it should address the clarity issues and provide deeper analysis of the learned dynamics to maximize its impact.
