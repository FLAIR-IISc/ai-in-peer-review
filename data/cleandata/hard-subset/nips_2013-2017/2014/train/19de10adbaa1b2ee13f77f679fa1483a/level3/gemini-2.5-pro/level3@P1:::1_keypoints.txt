- Proposes dasNet: a CNN augmented with feedback connections that iteratively modulate internal convolutional filter activations as an internal selective-attention mechanism.  
- Operates by a feedforward pass followed by a policy network that outputs modulatory gates based on internal states; the gated network reprocesses the same image for multiple refinement steps.  
- Uses a Maxout CNN trained with supervised backprop as the base model, while the attention policy is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the very high-dimensional action space.  
- Reward design strongly penalizes misclassifications, biasing the policy toward correcting base-model errors.  
- Novelty lies in applying RL-learned feedback to internal feature maps of a deep CNN, distinguishing it from prior external-attention/saccade approaches.  

Strengths:
- Technically sound method with well-justified SNES choice given parameter scale.  
- Solid empirical gains: notable improvement over a strong Maxout baseline and new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100 without augmentation.  
- Thorough analysis of system dynamics across time steps and experiments showing attention gates carry class-relevant information.  
- Clear, well-written presentation with helpful visualizations and good contextualization in neuroscience and ML literature.  
- Original synthesis of CNNs, feedback, RL, and attention in an effective and non-trivial way.  

Weaknesses / Suggestions:
- Scalability concern: training the policy is computationally intensive (four days on a GPU post-CNN training) and evaluation is limited to small CIFAR datasets.  
- Unclear how approach scales to deeper networks and larger, higher-resolution datasets (e.g., ImageNet); observation/action dimensionality may render SNES intractable.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: iterative steps T fixed at 5; more analysis needed on choice of T and behavior for T > 5.  
- Ablation studies missing: need experiments with simpler policy representations or alternative RL methods (even at smaller scale) to further justify SNES.  

Summary arguments:
- For accept: novel, well-executed architecture with strong empirical results that open a promising direction beyond feedforward models.  
- For reject: primary concerns are scalability and limited hyperparameter analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, with a strong request to address scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity in the final version.
