## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces dasNet, a novel architecture augmenting CNNs with internal feedback connections and learning to selectively attend to convolutional filters via reinforcement learning using SNES. The core idea – enabling a CNN to iteratively refine its classification through internal feedback – is a compelling step beyond traditional, static feedforward networks, particularly in addressing challenging cases where initial classification is ambiguous. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of biologically inspired vision, referencing the importance of recurrent processing and attention mechanisms in the human visual cortex. The performance gains on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, outperforming previous state-of-the-art results on unaugmented datasets, are impressive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

However, despite these strengths, the paper suffers from a significant lack of interpretability. While the results are strong, the *why* behind dasNet’s success remains largely a black box. The analysis of image representations and the learned theta parameters (mean and variance) is superficial and doesn’t provide meaningful insights into the attention mechanism. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don’t offer substantial exploration. The connection to dynamical systems, as suggested by the recurrent nature of the feedback, is a crucial point that needs further investigation. The model’s behavior feels closer to a recurrent network than a reinforcement learning policy, given the immediate reward and deterministic action selection. 

Several technical issues detract from the clarity of the presentation. Algorithm 1 is ambiguous regarding the scope of variables (per image vs. overwritten), and equations 11 and 8 contain unclear indexing. The necessity of theta regularization is not adequately justified, and the gradient update process is opaque. Furthermore, the notation is inconsistent with undefined symbols (x, d) appearing after Equation 11. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved classification accuracy.
*   Novel approach to adaptive reconfiguration in CNNs, moving beyond static feedforward architectures.
*   Well-motivated by biological principles of visual processing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of interpretability – the mechanism of attention remains a black box.
*   Insufficient analysis of internal dynamics and learned parameters.
*   Technical clarity issues in the algorithm description and equations.
*   Unclear justification for certain design choices (e.g., theta regularization).



**Overall:** This is an interesting and effective approach with significant potential. However, the paper would benefit greatly from a deeper analysis of its internal workings and a clearer, more rigorous presentation of the technical details. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the interpretability concerns and technical issues outlined above. Specifically, exploring the dynamical systems perspective and analyzing hard cases (misclassifications corrected by dasNet) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
