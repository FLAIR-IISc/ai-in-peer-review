This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates selective attention through feedback connections in deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors' main idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on the most discriminative features in an image, improving classification performance. This is achieved by augmenting a standard Maxout network with filters that can be weighted differently on different passes over the same image.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind dasNet, its architecture, and the reinforcement learning approach used to train the attentional policy. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of related work in the field, including human vision, computer vision, and reinforcement learning.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to improve upon the state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, even when using unaugmented data. The authors demonstrate that dasNet can correct misclassifications produced by a fully trained feedforward Maxout network, achieving a relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major concern is the lack of interpretability of the learned attentional policy. The authors acknowledge that the policy functions as a black box, and there is little explanation of why it works. Furthermore, the analysis of image representations and learned theta parameters is inadequate and uninformative. The authors should consider providing more insights into how the attentional policy is learned and how it affects the network's performance.

Another issue is the clarity of some equations and notation. For example, Equation 11's summation index is unclear, and Equation 8 may need an image index j. Additionally, the symbols x and d after Equation 11 are not defined. The authors should ensure that all equations and notation are clearly explained and defined.

The paper also raises some questions about the necessity and justification of theta regularization. The authors should provide more details on how the final gradient updates are computed and whether they adjust posterior hyperparameters.

In terms of originality, dasNet is a novel approach that combines ideas from computer vision and reinforcement learning. However, the authors could have explored connections to dynamical systems, as the model appears closer to recurrent networks than to reinforcement learning. The authors should consider analyzing the internal dynamics of the network, such as why layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration, the effect of global scaling, and the evolution of gating variables in local image regions.

Overall, the paper is an important step beyond fixed-parameter feed-forward networks, despite its interpretability shortcomings. The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of dasNet in improving classification performance, and future research should focus on providing more insights into how the attentional policy is learned and how it affects the network's performance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel architecture that improves upon state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of related work in the field.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind dasNet and its architecture.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of interpretability of the learned attentional policy is a major concern.
* The analysis of image representations and learned theta parameters is inadequate and uninformative.
* Some equations and notation are unclear and require further explanation.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide more insights into how the attentional policy is learned and how it affects the network's performance.
* Analyze the internal dynamics of the network, such as why layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration, the effect of global scaling, and the evolution of gating variables in local image regions.
* Explore connections to dynamical systems and consider analyzing hard cases (images misclassified by standard Maxout but corrected by dasNet).
* Test the effects of constraining learned theta statistics.
* Clarify Equation 11's summation index and Equation 8's image index j.
* Define the symbols x and d after Equation 11.
* Provide more details on how the final gradient updates are computed and whether they adjust posterior hyperparameters.
