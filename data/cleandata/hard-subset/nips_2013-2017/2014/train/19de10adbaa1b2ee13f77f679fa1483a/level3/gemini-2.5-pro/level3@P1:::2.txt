This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feed-forward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal selective attention. The core idea is to augment a pre-trained CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) with modulatory gates that can enhance or suppress the output of convolutional filter maps. Over a fixed number of sequential passes (T=5) on a single input image, a policy network observes the internal state of the CNN and outputs new gate values (`a_t`). This allows the network to iteratively refine its internal representations and final classification. The policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method, to maximize a reward signal that heavily penalizes misclassifications. The authors draw motivation from the human visual system, which combines a rapid feed-forward pass with slower, top-down attentional processing. On the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, dasNet is shown to outperform the baseline Maxout network and achieve state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by moving beyond the paradigm of static, purely feed-forward deep networks for image classification. The concept of using sequential processing and internal feedback to dynamically modulate feature detectors is both novel and compelling. It addresses an important aspect of intelligent systems: the ability to adapt processing based on initial ambiguity or difficulty, akin to "thinking longer" about a problem. This work represents an important and promising direction for future research in deep learning architectures.

2.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 (unaugmented) is a strong demonstration of the method's effectiveness. The ability to improve upon an already powerful baseline (Maxout) by specifically correcting its errors on difficult examples highlights the practical value of the proposed attentional mechanism.

3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and the high-level concept of dasNet is communicated clearly. The motivation from neuroscience is well-argued and provides a useful conceptual anchor for the work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Insight and Interpretability (Quality):** While the results are strong, the paper provides very little insight into *why* the method works. The dasNet currently functions as a black box. The analysis is limited to showing one example of filter modulation (Figure 3) and a basic check that the final gate values contain class-relevant information. This is insufficient for a deep scientific understanding.
    *   **Deeper Analysis Needed:** A more thorough analysis is required. For instance, the authors could analyze a cohort of "hard" cases—images that the baseline Maxout network misclassifies but dasNet correctly classifies. How do the attention gates (`a_t`) evolve over the T steps for these images compared to "easy" images? Are there consistent patterns in which layers or filters are modulated for specific inter-class confusions (e.g., cat vs. dog)?
    *   **Policy Parameter Analysis:** The paper does not analyze the learned policy parameters (`theta`). What does the mean policy `µ` learned by SNES look like? What about the variance `Σ`? Does the variance concentrate on a few key parameters, suggesting only a small subset of connections are important for the policy? Constraining or analyzing these statistics could provide valuable information.
    *   **Internal Dynamics:** The paper reports that performance peaks at step T and then remains stable. What causes this stability? What is the effect of the global scaling in the policy's softmax output (Eq. 7)? A more detailed investigation into the system's dynamics, perhaps by visualizing the trajectory of the state vector `o_t` for different images, would be highly beneficial.

2.  **Conceptual Framing (Originality/Quality):** The authors frame the training of the policy as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem. However, this connection feels tenuous. The environment is static (the image and base CNN do not change), the episodes have a fixed, short horizon (T steps), the policy is deterministic, and the reward is provided only at the end of the episode based on the final state. This setup is much closer to an unrolled Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) or a discrete-time dynamical system than a typical RL problem.
    *   **Connection to RNNs:** The authors should discuss the relationship between dasNet and the literature on RNNs and dynamical systems. Viewing the model this way might suggest alternative training mechanisms (e.g., designing a differentiable policy trainable with BPTT) or provide a stronger theoretical grounding. The choice of SNES seems driven by practicality (large, non-differentiable parameter space) rather than a deep commitment to the RL framework.

3.  **Clarity of Technical Details (Clarity):** Several key technical details are unclear or contain errors, hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The function `h(M)` used to construct the observation vector `o_t` is described in the text but not formally defined in the algorithm, which is confusing. Furthermore, the lines `F[i] <= f(theta_i)` and `Theta[i] <= theta_i` are ambiguous. Given the inner loop over images `j`, it is unclear if `f(theta_i)` is the cumulative score over all `n` images (as suggested by Eq. 11) or if these arrays are being overwritten in the inner loop.
    *   **Equations:**
        *   In Eq. 8 (`L_i = ...`), it seems an index for the image `j` is missing, as the loss is computed per image. It should likely be `L_ij`.
        *   In Eq. 11, the summation index `i` is used for the policy sample, but the summation itself seems to be over the `n` images in the batch. This should be clarified (e.g., `sum_{j=1 to n} L_ij`).
        *   The symbols `d` and `vT` are used in the text right after Eq. 11 (e.g., "...if d = ||vT||_inf"), but `d` here seems to refer to the correct class label as in Eq. 10, not an L-infinity norm. This needs clarification.
    *   **Regularization:** The motivation for the L2 regularization on the policy parameters `theta_i` (Eq. 11) is not discussed. What is its role and sensitivity? How are the final gradient updates in SNES computed with this term?

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and effective method for improving CNN performance through a learned, sequential attention mechanism. The originality of the idea and the strength of the empirical results are major assets. However, the work is significantly hampered by a lack of deep analysis into the learned mechanism, leaving the reader with a powerful result but little understanding. Additionally, the conceptual framing and several key technical details require clarification and refinement. Despite these shortcomings, the paper introduces an important research direction. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the issues of interpretability and technical clarity in the final version to maximize the paper's scientific impact.
