Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional model augmented with recurrent feedback connections that multiplicatively gate the activations of feature maps across layers. At test time the model processes an image for T sequential passes; after each pass it constructs an observation vector (global map activations, classifier pre-activations, and current class probabilities). A learned policy maps this observation to a new set of per-map gates that modulate filter sensitivities on the next pass, implementing a form of internal, feature-level attention. The base vision model is a Maxout CNN trained with dropout; after this supervised training, the attention policy is trained via direct policy search using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the million-plus policy parameters. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves over the base CNN and achieves state-of-the-art results for unaugmented datasets. This work connects longstanding neuroscience ideas on top-down feedback and selective attention to practical deep CNNs, and relates to prior ML work on recurrent/attentional vision, RL-driven saccades/glimpses, and evolutionary strategies for high-dimensional black-box optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mechanism—multiplicative gates per feature map controlled by a policy network—is clearly defined. The observation/action spaces and training objective (boosted log-loss emphasizing misclassifications) are reasonable, and the choice of SNES is well-motivated by scale. The dynamics analysis (varying T, stability past T, and information content in gates) strengthens the claims that the feedback is doing useful work.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent gains over a strong Maxout baseline and competitiveness with the then-current state of the art, with qualitative analyses illustrating how feedback changes internal activations. However, some ablations are missing: e.g., how much do we gain by gating only the top layer vs. all layers; replacing SNES with a simpler learned linear policy; or training the gates by backprop through time with a supervised loss.
- Reproducibility: The paper describes the architecture, training schedule, and key hyperparameters (T, SNES population, λ settings). Still, reporting exact parameter counts for the policy network and compute costs per test image (passes, FLOPs) would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written and well-structured. The core idea, algorithmic loop, and design choices (softmax scaling for gate positivity, boosted loss) are easy to follow. Figures illustrating dynamics and gate effects are helpful. A small clarification is needed on “unaugmented datasets,” given that the base Maxout model uses data augmentation during training—clarify whether the SOTA claim refers to no test-time augmentation/ensembles.

Originality
- No single component is individually novel: CNNs with gating, top-down feedback, and policy-search RL (NES) are all known. The novelty lies in the specific, well-chosen combination—per-map multiplicative gating driven by a high-dimensional policy trained with SNES on top of a competitive Maxout CNN—and in demonstrating that this yields state-of-the-art results on standard vision benchmarks. Related work is appropriately cited and the contribution is clearly distinguished from prior recurrent/attention models.

Significance
- The empirical results provide concrete evidence that internal feedback/attention can improve feedforward CNNs, even with coarse, per-feature-map gates. This is a useful message for the community and may spur further research on more expressive attention mechanisms and learned recurrence. The scope is limited to small-resolution datasets; the next step is to test scalability to larger, more diverse datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and tasks (detection/segmentation) where attention and iterative refinement may yield larger benefits.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of feedback as learned gating over feature maps.
- Sensible use of SNES for million-parameter policies; practical and scalable in this context.
- Strong empirical gains and, reportedly, SOTA on CIFAR without test-time augmentation/ensembles.
- Informative diagnostics on dynamics and gate information content.
- Good writing and positioning relative to neuroscience and ML literature.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: only CIFAR-10/100 at 32×32 resolution.
- Missing ablations and comparisons to simpler alternatives (e.g., supervised gating via BPTT, gating only top layers, linear vs. nonlinear policies).
- Coarse attention: gates are global per map (no spatial selectivity); unclear how much is gained versus gating only the classifier layer.
- Compute overhead and latency of multiple passes not quantified; lack of adaptive stopping criteria.
- The boosted loss and SNES hyperparameters could be analyzed more systematically for sensitivity.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Clarify the SOTA claim: do you compare to single-model baselines with/without test-time augmentation? Provide exact numbers versus the most competitive contemporaries.
- Report parameter counts and FLOPs for the base CNN, the policy, and the per-image test-time cost for T passes. How does accuracy vary with T and with a learned adaptive T?
- Provide ablations: (1) gating only last conv layer; (2) linear policy; (3) supervised BPTT training of the policy; (4) policy-free iterative refinement baseline.
- Analyze per-class improvements and error modes; show whether feedback helps mostly in visually confusable classes.
- Explore spatially structured gates (e.g., per-channel-per-spatial-region) and/or attention over feature groups.

Arguments for acceptance
- Demonstrates that feedback/attention improves strong CNNs and reaches SOTA on CIFAR.
- Method is conceptually simple, generally applicable, and well-executed.
- Good clarity and thoughtful analysis of dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evidence is confined to small datasets; lack of larger-scale validation.
- Missing ablations leave uncertainty about where the gains come from and whether simpler methods suffice.
- Additional compute/latency trade-offs are not quantified.

Recommendation
- Leaning accept. The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and advances the empirical case for internal feedback/attention in convnets. To strengthen the camera-ready version and future work, I recommend scaling the approach to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet), adding key ablations and compute trade-off analyses, and exploring more expressive gating (including spatial attention) and adaptive stopping.
