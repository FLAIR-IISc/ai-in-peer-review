This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network architecture augmented with feedback connections to dynamically modulate filter activations, effectively implementing an internal attention mechanism. The authors hypothesize that, similar to human vision, incorporating feedback can improve classification performance, particularly in challenging cases. A policy governing these feedback connections is learned via reinforcement learning, specifically using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the action space. The approach demonstrates state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, outperforming previous models on unaugmented datasets.

This work builds upon a long line of research in deep convolutional neural networks [1, 4, 6, 7] and draws inspiration from neurobiological observations of recurrent processing in the visual cortex [28, 29, 30]. While individual components – convolutional networks, reinforcement learning, and attention mechanisms – are well-established, the combination presented here is a significant contribution.  Recent work has explored attention in vision through various techniques [40, 41, 44, 45], often focusing on spatial attention or selective saccades. This paper distinguishes itself by focusing on *internal* attention – modulating the activity of convolutional filters themselves – and by employing a policy learned through RL to achieve this modulation. The use of SNES to tackle the high-dimensional policy space is a practical and well-justified choice.  Relatedly, iterative refinement and recurrent processing have also been explored [48, 49, 50], but this paper’s integration with a learned attentional policy presents a novel angle.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper clearly demonstrates improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.  This is a crucial strength, providing concrete evidence supporting the proposed approach.
* **Novel Combination:** While not inventing entirely new components, the combination of feedback connections, a learned attentional policy, and SNES provides a compelling and effective system.
* **Clear Motivation and Biological Plausibility:** The authors convincingly motivate their work by drawing parallels to human visual processing, highlighting the role of feedback and attention in complex visual tasks [16, 17, 31, 37].
* **Good Presentation and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the methodology and results. The visualization of filter activations and changes in Figure 3 is particularly insightful.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately positions the contribution within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While demonstrating success on CIFAR, the paper lacks a detailed discussion about the scalability of the approach to larger and more complex datasets like ImageNet. The computational cost of training with SNES is likely to be significant, and a preliminary analysis of this would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effect of different hyperparameters, particularly those related to the reinforcement learning process (e.g., λcorrect, λmisclassified, SNES population size), could be further investigated. A more thorough ablation study might reveal optimal settings and robustness of the method.
* **Interpretability of Learned Policy:**  While Figure 3 provides some insight into filter activations, a deeper analysis of the learned policy and its behavior would be valuable. Understanding *what* the network is attending to and *why* could provide further insights and guide future development.
* **Maxout Dependence:** The choice of Maxout networks [7] as the base architecture might limit the generalizability of the approach. While justified, the performance with other popular architectures (e.g., ResNet) should be considered.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to incorporating attention into convolutional neural networks.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets.
* The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by a thorough review of related work.
* The use of SNES is a practical solution to the challenges of training a high-dimensional policy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the approach to larger datasets is not fully addressed.
* The interpretability of the learned policy remains limited.
* The dependence on Maxout networks may restrict generalizability.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. The authors have successfully demonstrated the potential of feedback connections and reinforcement learning for improving the performance of convolutional neural networks.  While some limitations exist regarding scalability and interpretability, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and presents a novel and significant contribution. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the suggestion that the authors address the scalability concerns and explore more in-depth analysis of the learned policy in future work.  It would also be valuable to explore the performance of dasNet with different base architectures.
