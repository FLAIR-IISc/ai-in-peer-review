

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends standard feedforward CNNs with feedback connections enabling iterative internal attention refinement during classification. The authors propose that dynamic filter weighting—learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to modulate convolutional filter sensitivities across multiple passes—can improve performance on ambiguous cases, mirroring human cognitive processes where experts require "multiple passes" to resolve difficult classifications. dasNet augments a pre-trained Maxout CNN with a policy that adjusts filter weights (via gating vectors) based on internal observations, with the policy evolved to maximize classification accuracy through SNES. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.

**Originality & Significance**  
The work is highly original in its fusion of reinforcement learning (RL) for policy learning and feedback connections in deep networks, directly addressing a key biological observation: cortical hierarchies feature extensive bidirectional connections (Felleman & Essen, 1991), yet most CNNs ignore this. While prior work (e.g., Schmidhuber on saccade learning; Bar et al. on top-down expectations) explored RL for attention, dasNet is the first to apply this *directly to state-of-the-art CNNs* for classification, without simplifying datasets. The significance is reinforced by its strong CIFAR results (Table 1), demonstrating that even small improvements on benchmark datasets can be impactful for the field. The method’s ability to correct misclassifications (Fig. 3) via selective filter emphasis—e.g., disambiguating "cat" from "dog" through layer-specific gate adjustments—provides compelling evidence of its utility.

**Strengths**  
1. **Technical Quality & Clarity**: The SNES-based policy learning elegantly handles the million-dimensional parameter space (a key limitation of conventional RL), with clear ablation studies (e.g., Fig. 2 showing stable dynamics for T=5 steps). Experiments are well-designed: the gating mechanism’s information content is validated via downstream classification (40.7% 15-NN accuracy), and visualizations (Fig. 3) effectively illustrate how attention shifts across layers.  
2. **Novelty**: The "internal selective attention" concept—using feedback to *refine* a pre-trained network rather than retraining from scratch—is distinct from prior work on recurrent networks (e.g., Wyatte et al. for object recognition) or attention mechanisms (e.g., Xu et al.’s visual attention). The integration of Maxout networks with iterative gating is particularly well-motivated.  
3. **Significance for NIPS**: The paper bridges neuroscience (cortical feedback loops) and deep learning, advancing the state-of-the-art on a widely used benchmark. It demonstrates that "thinking" via feedback can improve performance without data augmentation, a valuable insight for the field.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Scalability Gap**: The most critical limitation is the lack of results on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet/ILSVRC). While CIFAR-10/100 are challenging, state-of-the-art CNNs (e.g., ResNet) dominate ImageNet, and dasNet’s 4-day training time (on CIFAR) on a single GPU raises scalability concerns. Without ImageNet validation, the practical impact is unclear.  
2. **Failure Analysis Missing**: The paper lacks examples where dasNet *fails* (e.g., cases where feedback exacerbates errors), which is crucial for evaluating robustness. The claim that "difficult cases" are corrected (Sec. 6) needs counterexamples to avoid overstatement.  
3. **Complexity vs. Benefit**: The added complexity (SNES training, gated layers) must be justified by significant gains. While 6% is notable on CIFAR, the marginal benefit per compute unit is unquantified. A comparison with simpler iterative methods (e.g., ensembles) would strengthen the case.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments**  
*Pro*:  
- First to apply RL to learn feedback policies for *directly improving* state-of-the-art CNNs.  
- Strong empirical results on CIFAR with clear visualization of attention dynamics.  
- Addresses a biologically plausible mechanism (cortical feedback) in a technically sound way.  

*Con*:  
- No validation on ImageNet or larger-scale tasks, limiting perceived impact.  
- Absence of failure cases and scalability analysis weakens the claim of general applicability.  
- Added training complexity (4 days vs. standard CNNs) without clear cost-benefit analysis.  

**Recommendation**  
This is a technically sound, novel, and well-executed paper that advances our understanding of attention in deep networks. Its CIFAR results are compelling, and the method is clearly articulated. However, the lack of ImageNet results and failure analysis prevents it from being a *strong* candidate for acceptance at NIPS, where scalability and broader impact are highly valued. **Accept with reservations**—the work is promising but requires validation on larger datasets to demonstrate its significance beyond CIFAR. The authors should address scalability in a future revision (e.g., by adding ImageNet experiments or a theoretical analysis of computational complexity). As-is, it is a solid contribution to the *methodology* of attention but not yet a transformative one for large-scale vision.
