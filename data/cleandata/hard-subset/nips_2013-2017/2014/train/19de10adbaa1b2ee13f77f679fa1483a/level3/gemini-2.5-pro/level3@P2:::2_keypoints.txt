- Proposes dasNet: a CNN augmented with feedback that dynamically modulates convolutional filters over multiple processing steps for internal selective attention.  
- Builds on a pre-trained Maxout network; a linear control policy maps network state to filter modulation weights and is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) as a black-box RL problem.  
- Demonstrates improved classification on CIFAR-10/100 and achieves state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10.  
- Strength: technically interesting approach with strong empirical results.  
- Weakness: lacks scientific insight—model treated as a black box and visualizations are uninformative.  
- Missing analysis of “hard cases” (images corrected by dasNet): unclear which features are emphasized or suppressed to fix errors.  
- Internal dynamics insufficiently explored (e.g., why layer-1 activations increase on first iteration, effect of global softmax scaling, spatial evolution of gating variables).  
- Framing as reinforcement learning is weak—deterministic policy with a single end reward resembles an unrolled recurrent/dynamical system more than an RL problem.  
- Clarity issue: Algorithm 1 omits an explicit definition of h(M_t) and ambiguously handles fitness score/parameter accumulation per image or policy.  
- Equation issues: confusing/incorrect notation (e.g., reuse of index i in Eq. 11, missing image indexing in Eq. 8, undefined/ambiguous symbols d, v_T, x).  
- Regularization unclear: L2 penalty on policy parameters in Eq. 11 is not justified and its incorporation into SNES updates is unspecified.  
- Originality: novel and significant idea to add learnable dynamic internal attention to deep CNNs and to use SNES for very high-dimensional policy search.  
- Significance: empirical gains are meaningful and could inspire adaptive models, but limited by lack of interpretability and clarity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel architecture, strong SOTA results, advances direction toward dynamic/recurrent computation in vision.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient explanation of how/why it works, questionable theoretical framing, reproducibility-hindering clarity issues, limited scientific contribution without deeper analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept but require revisions—improve interpretability/analysis (e.g., hard-case studies), reconsider RL framing (connect to RNN/dynamical systems), and clarify algorithm/equations and regularization.
