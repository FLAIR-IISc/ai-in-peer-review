## Review of “Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections”

This paper introduces dasNet, a convolutional neural network augmented with feedback connections to implement internal selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification via a gating mechanism, trained using reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES). While no single component is groundbreaking, the combination of feedback connections, attention gating, and RL-based training is a well-motivated and effectively executed approach. 

The authors position their work within the broader context of biologically inspired neural networks, highlighting the limitations of purely feedforward CNNs and drawing parallels to recurrent processing in the visual cortex. They correctly identify prior work in attention mechanisms and reinforcement learning for vision, but convincingly argue that their approach offers a novel combination capable of achieving state-of-the-art performance. 

The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are strong, demonstrating that dasNet outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets. The ablation study and visualization of filter activations provide some insight into *how* the attention mechanism is functioning, showing that the network learns to emphasize and suppress filters in a task-dependent manner. The clarity of the paper is high, with a logical flow and clear explanations of the methodology. The writing is generally concise and well-organized.

However, a key weakness is the limited scope of the experiments. While achieving SOTA on CIFAR is a positive result, the authors acknowledge this is a relatively simple dataset. The recommendation to scale the approach to larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet is crucial. It remains unclear whether the benefits of this approach will translate to more realistic scenarios with significantly higher dimensionality and complexity. Furthermore, the computational cost of training with SNES is substantial, and a discussion of scalability and potential optimizations would be valuable. The choice of Maxout networks, while justified, might also limit the generalizability of the findings; exploring other base architectures could be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on CIFAR datasets.
*   Novel and well-motivated combination of existing techniques.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Provides empirical support for the potential of feedback/attention in vision.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experiments (CIFAR only).
*   Scalability concerns regarding SNES training.
*   Reliance on Maxout networks might limit generalizability.



**Overall Recommendation:** Accept, with the strong encouragement to address the scalability concerns and extend the experiments to larger datasets like ImageNet. This work represents a promising direction for future research in attention mechanisms and recurrent processing for computer vision.
