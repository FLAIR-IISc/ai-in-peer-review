This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates selective attention into deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through feedback connections. The authors' main idea is to allow each layer to influence all other layers on successive passes over an image, thereby modulating the activity of the convolutional filters. This is achieved by learning a control policy that is trained using reinforcement learning after the CNN has been trained in the usual way via supervised learning.

The dasNet architecture is an extension of the Maxout network, which is a state-of-the-art model for object recognition. The authors use a variant of Natural Evolution Strategies (NES), called Separable NES (SNES), to evolve the policy that controls the attention of the dasNet. The policy is represented by a function approximator, specifically a neural network, parameterized by θ. The authors use a deterministic policy, which always outputs the same action given an observation.

The experimental evaluation of dasNet focuses on ambiguous classification cases in the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. The results show that dasNet outperforms the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets, achieving a relative improvement of 6%. The authors also provide visualizations of the attentional policy, which demonstrate that the network is able to selectively focus on certain features in the image.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning by introducing a novel architecture that incorporates selective attention into CNNs. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in improving classification performance, especially in cases where the images are ambiguous or difficult to classify.

However, the paper has some limitations. The analysis of the internal dynamics of the network is inadequate, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of why the method works. The paper also lacks a deeper analysis of the learned parameters, specifically the mean and variance of the policy. Additionally, the notation and equations in the paper are not always clear, and there are some typos and ambiguities.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but there are some areas that could be improved. The exposition is clear, but some of the equations and notation could be explained more clearly. The authors could also provide more details about the implementation of the SNES algorithm and the policy network.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the dasNet architecture in improving classification performance. However, the paper could be improved by providing more analysis of the internal dynamics of the network and the learned parameters, as well as clarifying some of the notation and equations.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the learned parameters, specifically the mean and variance of the policy.
* The notation and equations in the paper are not always clear, and there are some typos and ambiguities.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide a deeper analysis of the internal dynamics of the network, including why layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration and the effect of global scaling.
* Analyze the learned parameters, specifically the mean and variance of the policy, and provide more details about the implementation of the SNES algorithm and the policy network.
* Clarify the notation and equations in the paper, and provide more details about the implementation of the dasNet architecture.
* Explore connections to dynamical systems, as the model appears closer to recurrent networks than to reinforcement learning.
* Test the effects of constraining learned theta statistics and analyze hard cases (images misclassified by standard Maxout but corrected by the proposed model).
