This paper proposes a novel deep neural network architecture, Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which incorporates attention-based top-down feedback to improve image classification performance. The authors draw inspiration from the human visual cortex, where feedback connections play a crucial role in object recognition and attentional processing. The dasNet architecture is built on top of a standard Maxout network, with the addition of feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively focus its attention on the most discriminative features in an image.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The use of reinforcement learning to train the feedback connections is a novel approach, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) algorithm used to evolve the policy. The experimental results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the dasNet architecture in improving image classification performance, particularly on difficult classification instances.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and intuitive explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including visualizations of the attentional policy and the classification performance of the network. The use of reinforcement learning to train the feedback connections is a novel approach, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the SNES algorithm used to evolve the policy.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the lack of results on larger datasets, such as ImageNet. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the dasNet architecture on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, it is unclear whether the architecture will scale to larger datasets. Additionally, the authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the dasNet architecture, which could be a concern for large-scale image classification tasks.

Another limitation of the paper is the lack of analysis on the representation quality of the dasNet architecture. While the authors provide visualizations of the attentional policy and the classification performance of the network, they do not provide a thorough analysis of the representation quality of the network. This is an important aspect of deep neural networks, as it can affect the generalization performance of the network.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel deep neural network architecture that incorporates attention-based top-down feedback. The use of reinforcement learning to train the feedback connections is also a novel approach. However, the paper builds on previous work on attentional processing and reinforcement learning, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and deep learning. The dasNet architecture has the potential to improve image classification performance on difficult classification instances, and the use of reinforcement learning to train the feedback connections is a novel approach that could be applied to other deep learning tasks.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the dasNet architecture in improving image classification performance, and the use of reinforcement learning to train the feedback connections is a novel approach. However, there are some limitations to the paper, including the lack of results on larger datasets and the lack of analysis on the representation quality of the dasNet architecture.
