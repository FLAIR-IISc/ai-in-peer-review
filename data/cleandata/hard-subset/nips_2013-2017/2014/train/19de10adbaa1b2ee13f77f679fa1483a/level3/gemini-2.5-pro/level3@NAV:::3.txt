This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward CNN with top-down feedback connections. Drawing inspiration from recurrent processing in biological vision, the model deviates from purely feedforward approaches. The core idea is to use reinforcement learning to train a policy that modulates the activity of convolutional filters over multiple sequential passes. This allows the network to iteratively refine its internal representations and selectively "focus" on the most discriminative features for a given image. The work builds upon state-of-the-art Maxout networks by incorporating a dynamic, attention-like mechanism at the filter level.

The paper’s primary strengths are its originality and strong empirical results. The forward-looking approach of combining bottom-up feature extraction with a learned top-down attentional policy presents a compelling research direction. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet by achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (unaugmented), which is a significant contribution. Furthermore, the inclusion of visualizations and analysis of the model's dynamics over time provides valuable insight into the learned policy's behavior, enhancing the quality and clarity of the work.

The main weakness of the paper is the question of scalability. While results on CIFAR are impressive, the method is notably more complex and computationally intensive than standard feedforward CNNs. The absence of experiments on a large-scale dataset like ILSVRC is a significant omission, leaving the practical viability of the approach for modern, large-scale problems an open question. The paper could also be strengthened by providing an analysis of the attention policy's failure cases. Since the representation quality is highly dependent on this policy, demonstrating or suggesting its limitations would provide a more complete assessment of the method's robustness.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and well-executed idea. It is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are significant for the datasets tested. While the lack of large-scale experiments is a concern, the proposed model is interesting and worth further study. The work is a valuable contribution that is likely to inspire further research into feedback and attention mechanisms in deep networks. I recommend acceptance.
