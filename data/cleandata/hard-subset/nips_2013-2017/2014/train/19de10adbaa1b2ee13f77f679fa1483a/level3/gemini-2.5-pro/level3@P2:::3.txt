### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN). The core idea is to augment a pre-trained Maxout network with a modulatory mechanism that can dynamically alter the activity of its convolutional filters over multiple, sequential processing passes of the same input image. This feedback system is framed as a form of internal, selective attention, allowing the network to refine its initial, "first-shot" classification. The attention policy, which determines how to gate the filter activations at each layer, is learned using reinforcement learning. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over a million parameters), the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization. This work is motivated by the recurrent and top-down processing observed in the primate visual cortex, which is believed to be crucial for complex recognition tasks that require more than a single feedforward sweep. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where dasNet achieves state-of-the-art performance on the unaugmented datasets, outperforming the baseline Maxout network it builds upon.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed architecture is a non-trivial extension of a strong baseline (Maxout networks), and the training procedure, which combines supervised pre-training with a subsequent reinforcement learning phase, is well-defined. The claims of improved performance are supported by empirical results on two standard benchmarks. A particular strength is the inclusion of experiments that go beyond simple accuracy metrics. The authors provide visualizations that attempt to interpret the modulatory effects of the attention policy (Figure 3), and they analyze the network's dynamics over multiple time steps (Figure 2). The experiment showing that the final gate activations alone contain significant class-discriminative information is a clever way to validate that the learned policy is indeed capturing relevant features.

However, the primary weakness concerning the work's quality and broader impact is its evaluation is limited to the CIFAR datasets. While challenging, these datasets are significantly smaller in scale and complexity than, for example, the ILSVRC dataset. The proposed method introduces considerable complexity in both the model architecture and the training process (RL on top of supervised learning, 4 days of training for the policy). It is unclear how this approach would scale to datasets with thousands of classes and much larger images. The lack of results on a large-scale benchmark like ILSVRC is the main concern, making it difficult to assess whether this is a generally applicable technique or one that is primarily effective on smaller-scale problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-argued, linking the computational model to concepts from neuroscience. The description of the dasNet architecture and the RL-based training algorithm is generally easy to follow. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is very helpful. The visualizations, while inherently difficult to interpret for high-level features, do provide some intuition about how the model works by emphasizing or de-emphasizing filter maps to correct an initial misclassification.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. At a time when deep learning research was heavily focused on developing deeper and more efficient feedforward architectures, this paper proposes a compelling alternative that integrates feedback and recurrent processing. The combination of a state-of-the-art CNN with a reinforcement learning-based attention mechanism is novel and thought-provoking. While the idea of top-down influences in vision is not new, implementing it in this specific way within a modern deep network is a significant and creative contribution. This forward-looking approach of combining bottom-up feature extraction with top-down attentional modulation seems like a very promising research direction.

**Significance:** The results are significant, as they establish a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100 without data augmentation. This demonstrates that there are performance gains to be had by moving beyond purely feedforward models. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and could inspire other researchers to explore recurrent and attentional mechanisms in deep vision models. The work successfully shows that a complex control policy for internal network states can be learned, even in a very high-dimensional space, which is an important proof of concept.

The main factor limiting the immediate significance is the aforementioned scalability question. If the method can be shown to work on larger-scale problems, its impact would be substantially greater. Practitioners may be hesitant to adopt such a complex training pipeline without evidence of its effectiveness in more challenging, real-world scenarios.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel and interesting architecture that integrates feedback, attention, and reinforcement learning into a modern CNN. This is a significant departure from the prevailing feedforward models.
2.  **Strong Results:** It achieves state-of-the-art performance on two standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100), empirically validating the approach.
3.  **Good Analysis:** The paper includes thoughtful analysis and visualizations that help build an understanding of the model's internal workings.
4.  **Significance:** The work opens up a promising research direction and provides a strong proof of concept for learning top-down attentional control in deep networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Scalability:** The main concern is the lack of experiments on large-scale datasets like ILSVRC. This makes it difficult to gauge the practical utility and generality of the proposed method.
2.  **Complexity:** The model and training procedure are significantly more complex than standard CNNs, which may be a barrier to adoption.
3.  **Limited Failure Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of the failure cases of the attention policy. For instance, does the recurrent processing ever hurt performance by turning a correct initial guess into an incorrect one?

### Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality, original, and interesting paper that presents a novel approach to incorporating attentional feedback into deep networks. The strong empirical results on CIFAR and the thoughtful analysis make a compelling case for the method's potential. While the lack of large-scale experiments is a notable limitation, the novelty and significance of the core ideas outweigh this concern. The model is certainly worth further study by the community. I recommend acceptance. I would strongly encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns in the final version of the paper, even if just through discussion, and to consider adding an analysis of the policy's failure modes.
