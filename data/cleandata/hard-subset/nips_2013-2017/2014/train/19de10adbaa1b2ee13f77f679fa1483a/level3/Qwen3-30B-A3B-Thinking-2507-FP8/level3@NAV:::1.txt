

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that integrates feedback connections with convolutional networks to implement selective visual attention through filter gating. The authors propose augmenting standard Maxout networks with reinforcement learning-trained attention policies that dynamically modulate filter activations across multiple passes over an image. This approach builds upon established work in neural networks (e.g., Maxout networks [Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013]) and neuroscience (feedback connections in visual cortex [Felleman & Essen, Cerebral Cortex 1991]), creating a well-executed combination of existing techniques rather than introducing highly novel components.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation. The authors provide compelling evidence that dasNet successfully corrects misclassifications through iterative attention refinement, with Figure 3 offering a clear visualization of how filter activations change across processing steps to disambiguate similar classes (e.g., cat vs. dog). The training methodology using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) effectively handles the million-dimensional policy space, though the paper could better explain why SNES was chosen over other RL methods.

Strengths include excellent clarity in presentation, thorough experimental evaluation, and meaningful empirical validation of the core hypothesis that attention mechanisms improve CNN performance. The work provides significant evidence supporting feedback connections as a promising direction for vision models, addressing a meaningful gap in understanding how to incorporate attention into deep networks.

Weaknesses include the limited scope of evaluation (only CIFAR datasets) and the incremental nature of the contribution. While the results are impressive on CIFAR, the authors should scale the approach to larger datasets like ImageNet to demonstrate broader applicability. The paper also could better distinguish how dasNet differs from prior work on recurrent networks for vision [e.g., Wyatte et al., J. Cogn. Neurosci. 2013].

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to understanding attention mechanisms in deep learning. The significance is clear for the vision community, though the limited scale of experiments represents a notable limitation. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that future work should address the scalability concern.
