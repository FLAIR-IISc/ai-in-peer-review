

dasNet introduces a novel architecture that integrates selective attention via feedback connections into deep CNNs, addressing a key biological inspiration absent in standard feedforward models. The core innovation lies in dynamically modulating convolutional filter sensitivities during inference through a reinforcement learning (RL) policy, learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to iteratively refine classifications. This contrasts sharply with stationary CNNs [5,6,7] that fix weights post-training, and prior work on recurrent processing [48,49] that typically targets simplified tasks or reconstruction rather than state-of-the-art classification. The method builds on Maxout networks [7] with dropout, augmenting them with gated attention mechanisms (Equation 5) that allow layer-level filter weighting across sequential passes (Algorithm 1). Crucially, the policy is trained to boost performance on misclassified samples (Equation 10), leveraging a "hindsight" correction mechanism absent in prior feedforward systems.

**Strengths:**  
The paper excels in *originality* and *significance*. It proposes a biologically plausible mechanism—top-down feedback for selective attention—applied directly to SOTA CNNs, moving beyond incremental improvements. The SNES approach elegantly sidesteps the million-dimensional policy optimization challenge [19], making the method computationally feasible where conventional RL would fail. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate a 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks (Table 1), establishing a new SOTA for unaugmented data, which is significant given CIFAR’s difficulty in distinguishing visually similar classes (e.g., dog vs. cat, Fig 3). The *clarity* of the method is enhanced by insightful visualizations: Fig 3 shows how attention shifts across layers (e.g., layer 3 filter changes most influence output), and the dynamics analysis (Fig 2) confirms stability beyond training steps. The *quality* is robust, with thorough ablation (e.g., gate information content via 15-NN, Section 6.2) and careful handling of misclassified samples via λboost. The authors also responsibly address limitations (e.g., "complex suppression/enhancement patterns" in high-level features).

**Weaknesses:**  
The *significance* is tempered by critical gaps. The absence of results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ILSVRC) is a major concern—CIFAR-10/100 are small, and dasNet’s scalability to real-world applications remains unproven. While the paper notes "future work" on spatial attention [7], the current model’s reliance on global gating (not spatially localized) limits its applicability to tasks like object detection. More concerning is the lack of *failure case analysis*: the paper demonstrates success but does not identify scenarios where dasNet fails (e.g., highly occluded objects, texture-dominated classes), which would strengthen claims about robustness. The computational overhead (4 days training on GTX 560 Ti, Section 6) also raises practicality questions compared to feedforward CNNs. While the complexity is justified by performance gains, the paper does not quantify inference-time costs or compare against efficient alternatives like [15].

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong *accept* candidate. The work is technically sound (Quality), clearly presented (Clarity), and represents a meaningful advance in attentional mechanisms for CNNs (Originality). It directly addresses a gap in the literature [30,34] and demonstrates measurable SOTA gains on a standard benchmark. The weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions: (1) Add ILSVRC experiments or a scalability discussion; (2) Include failure case analysis (e.g., misclassified examples where dasNet fails); (3) Clarify computational trade-offs. The current results are compelling, and the method opens promising avenues for biologically inspired deep learning. While not a paradigm shift, it significantly advances the state of the art in attentional CNNs—exactly the type of work NIPS prioritizes. The authors’ focus on *how* the network corrects errors (not just *that* it does) provides valuable theoretical and practical insights for the community.
