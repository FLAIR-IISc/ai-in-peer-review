Summary
This paper augments a standard Maxout CNN with multiplicative, per-feature-map gates that are updated across multiple passes over the same image via top-down “feedback.” The gating policy is a deterministic neural network mapping summary observations (pooled layer activations, penultimate activations, and class probabilities) to actions (gates) at each step; its parameters (≈1M) are optimized by Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The result is a recurrent-style inference procedure that adaptively reconfigures internal feature sensitivities at test time to fix difficult misclassifications. On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented test data), the method improves upon a strong Maxout baseline and sets new single-model results at submission time. The work connects to decades of vision research on feedback and selective attention, to RL-for-attention (e.g., Denil et al.; Larochelle & Hinton’s glimpses), and to recurrent/iterative inference in vision (e.g., O’Reilly; Wyatte et al.).

Quality
Strengths
- Clear problem framing: enabling adaptive reconfiguration of a trained CNN at test time under resource/task limits.
- Strong empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a competitive Maxout baseline; dynamics appear stable beyond the trained horizon T.
- Sensible design choices (positive gates via scaled softmax; emphasis on misclassified examples via λboost).

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Black-box training and limited analysis: Why and when the gating policy succeeds remains largely unexplained. The analysis of image representations and the learned policy parameters (θ) is superficial; reporting just mean/variance is not informative.
- Algorithmic clarity issues:
  - Algorithm 1: hM (or h(·)) is not defined precisely. Please specify the exact statistics, their ordering, and dimensions. Clarify whether observations include per-layer means only or any variances/normalizations.
  - Algorithm 1 lines 14–15: Are F[i] and Θ[i] stored per sampled policy and aggregated over the batch, or overwritten per image? If aggregated, specify the reduction (sum/mean) and at what scope.
  - hM is undefined in the text; please define hM := h(Mt) and its components formally.
- Equation issues and notation:
  - Eq. 8: Li = −λboost d log(vT). This seems to require either vT[d] (probability of the true class d) or a one-hot sum; also an image index j may be needed for clarity.
  - Eq. 10: d is called “the correct classification,” but earlier d is used inconsistently; define d ∈ {1,…,C} unambiguously.
  - Eq. 11: The summation index collides with earlier i; please use a distinct index (e.g., j over images) and specify whether the fitness is the average or sum over the batch.
  - After Eq. 11, symbols x and d are used without local definitions (they appear earlier but should be reintroduced).
- Regularization and optimization:
  - Why L2-regularize θ inside the sampled fitness f(θi) rather than regularizing µ directly? With NES, the update is to distribution parameters (µ, Σ). Please justify the chosen formulation and clarify whether this effectively regularizes the posterior over θ (via µ, Σ) or just penalizes sampled θi.
  - The text mentions natural gradients (for SNES) but is otherwise vague about “final gradient updates.” Please make explicit that gradients are with respect to the search distribution parameters (µ, Σ), not θ itself.
- Interpretability and internal dynamics:
  - The presented visualization is anecdotal. Please provide deeper analyses: why do layer-1 activations typically increase on the first iteration? What is the effect of the global scaling constraint (mean gate ≈ 1) on dynamics and stability? How do gates evolve over iterations within specific local image regions or feature groups? Are there consistent, class-specific gating motifs?
  - Analyze hard cases systematically: collect images misclassified by the base Maxout but corrected by dasNet; report confusion-pair breakdowns, gate trajectories, and affected layers/features.
  - Study constraints on θ statistics: e.g., fixing the mean gate to 1, bounding per-layer gate variance, or tying gates across filters; assess the performance/robustness trade-offs.
- Positioning as RL vs recurrence: The reward is immediate (at step T), the policy is deterministic, and horizons are short. This looks closer to a learned recurrent dynamical system for iterative inference than to standard RL with exploration/credit assignment. A dynamical-systems treatment (fixed points, stability, Jacobian spectra, contraction properties as T grows) would sharpen the conceptual contribution and help explain why the method works.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-motivated, and related work is broad. However, several equations contain typos/ambiguous indices as noted, Algorithm 1 needs precise definitions, and variable reuse (i, d) hinders reproducibility. A complete specification of h(·), action/observation dimensions, and SNES hyperparameters (step-size, damping, sampling variance schedule) should be included.

Originality
- The combination of per-feature-map gating, closed-loop sequential inference, and black-box policy search on top of a state-of-the-art CNN is novel for the NIPS 2014 timeframe. Conceptually, the work extends fixed-parameter feed-forward networks toward adaptive, recurrent-style models without retraining base weights.

Significance
- Demonstrated accuracy gains on standard benchmarks are meaningful and likely to spur follow-up on test-time adaptation and selective attention within CNNs. Despite interpretability shortcomings, this is an important step toward practical recurrent feedback in modern convnets.

Specific suggestions
- Provide ablations: T=0/1/2/5; random gates; frozen gates; per-layer versus per-filter gating; replacing SNES with (stochastic) policy gradients for smaller policies; effect of λboost and λL2.
- Report computational overhead: training time attributable to SNES and inference slow-down factor ≈ T; memory footprint for storing gates/observations.
- Explore dynamical-systems analyses: measure stability beyond trained T, sensitivity to gate perturbations, and fixed-point behavior for repeated iterations on the same image.
- Improve interpretability: cluster gates by class; visualize gate changes aligned to receptive-field locations; quantify how gates shift feature distributions.

Pros
- Strong empirical improvements on CIFAR-10/100 with a principled, architecture-agnostic mechanism.
- Clear motivation grounded in neuroscience and prior machine vision work on feedback and attention.
- Simple, modular implementation on top of Maxout CNNs.

Cons
- Method functions as a black box; limited insight into why it works and when it fails.
- Several notational and algorithmic ambiguities impede reproducibility.
- Missing ablations and limited analysis of the learned policy and dynamics.

Overall evaluation
Interesting and effective recurrent-style approach with clear accuracy gains. I recommend acceptance if the authors address the algorithm/equation clarity issues and substantially deepen the analysis of internal dynamics and policy behavior; otherwise, this is a borderline accept primarily on empirical strength. My confidence is medium.
