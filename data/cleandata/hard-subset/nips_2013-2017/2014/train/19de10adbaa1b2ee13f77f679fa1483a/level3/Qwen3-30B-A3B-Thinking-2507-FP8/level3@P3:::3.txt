

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates attention-based top-down feedback into convolutional neural networks. The authors propose a model that dynamically adjusts filter sensitivities during classification through feedback connections, allowing the network to iteratively focus on relevant features across multiple passes of the same image. This approach draws inspiration from human visual processing, where top-down feedback plays a critical role in resolving ambiguous visual inputs. The core innovation lies in using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the attention policy in a high-dimensional parameter space, circumventing the limitations of conventional reinforcement learning methods for large-scale networks.

The paper builds upon the strong foundation of Maxout networks [7] and extends them with a feedback mechanism. Unlike previous work on recurrent neural networks for vision [48-50] or attention mechanisms [40-41], dasNet integrates feedback directly into the training process of state-of-the-art feedforward CNNs rather than building new architectures from scratch. The approach aligns with recent NIPS work on attention mechanisms [42-43, 46], but distinguishes itself by focusing on internal selective attention within a pre-trained network rather than learning new visual features.

The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on unaugmented data. The authors provide compelling visualizations showing how the network corrects misclassifications by dynamically emphasizing/de-emphasizing specific filters across multiple passes. The ablation studies on dynamics (Figure 2) and information content of the gate values (Section 6) further support the effectiveness of their approach.

The strengths of this paper are significant: (1) It presents a genuinely novel model design that bridges the gap between biological vision and deep learning; (2) The forward-looking approach of combining bottom-up and top-down signals for training is conceptually elegant; (3) The experiments include insightful visualizations that help interpret the model's behavior; (4) The evaluation across time using gated signals to constrain representations is thorough.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. The most significant limitation is the lack of results on larger datasets like ImageNet (ILSVRC), which makes it difficult to assess the scalability of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's a major concern for a paper claiming state-of-the-art results on challenging vision tasks. The method's complexity compared to standard feedforward CNNs is also notable, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for the additional computational cost.

The paper could be strengthened by addressing the representation quality concern more thoroughly. The authors state that "representation quality hinges on the attention policy," yet they don't provide failure cases or suggest how to improve the policy when it fails. Including examples where dasNet still misclassifies images despite multiple passes would greatly enhance the paper's credibility.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel architecture that successfully leverages feedback connections in CNNs. The results on CIFAR datasets are compelling and demonstrate the potential of this approach. The main concern regarding scalability to larger datasets should be addressed in the final version with a brief discussion of future work on ImageNet.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel model design that effectively integrates top-down feedback into CNNs
- State-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 without data augmentation
- Clear visualizations that help interpret the attention mechanism
- Thorough evaluation of the dynamics and information content of the feedback mechanism

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of results on larger datasets (ILSVRC) limits the demonstrated significance
- Method is more complex than standard feedforward CNNs without sufficient justification
- Insufficient analysis of failure cases or limitations of the attention policy

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of deep learning for vision. The authors have successfully demonstrated that incorporating attention-based top-down feedback can significantly improve classification performance on challenging datasets. While the scalability to larger datasets remains an open question, the approach is promising and warrants further study. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines novel model design with strong experimental results that advance the state of the art.
