This paper, "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections," proposes a novel architecture, Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), which incorporates attention-based top-down feedback into traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors draw inspiration from the human visual cortex, where top-down connections play a crucial role in modulating the activity of neurons. By allowing the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on certain features, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the use of reinforcement learning to train the attention policy. The experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive, demonstrating a relative improvement of 6% over the vanilla CNN. The visualizations provided in the paper help to illustrate the effectiveness of the attention mechanism in disambiguating difficult classification cases.

One of the strengths of the paper is its novelty and originality. The authors propose a new approach to incorporating attention into CNNs, which is distinct from previous work in the field. The use of reinforcement learning to train the attention policy is also a unique aspect of the paper. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the use of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to evolve the policy.

The paper is also well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The related work section is thorough, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous work in the field.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is the lack of results on larger datasets, such as ImageNet. While the authors demonstrate impressive results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, it is unclear how their approach will scale to larger datasets. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach will generalize to other tasks, such as object detection or segmentation.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, demonstrating a new approach to incorporating attention into CNNs. The results are impressive, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the lack of results on larger datasets and the unclear generalizability of the approach to other tasks are potential limitations of the paper.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it makes a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and demonstrates a novel approach to incorporating attention into CNNs. However, I would suggest that the authors provide additional results on larger datasets and clarify how their approach will generalize to other tasks.
