Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture that augments a strong feedforward Maxout CNN with internal, attention-based top-down feedback. The core idea is to run an image through the network multiple times; at each iteration, a policy computes a vector of positive, per-feature-map gates that up- or down-weight filters throughout the network, thereby modulating internal representations before the next pass. The policy observes aggregated activations across layers and the current class probabilities and outputs the next set of gates. The policy is learned after standard supervised training of the base CNN using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a scalable black-box optimizer, due to the million-plus parameters needed for high-dimensional observation/action spaces. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art accuracy among single models on unaugmented data, primarily by correcting ambiguous cases through iterative, top-down modulation. The paper connects to a long line of work on recurrent processing and attention in vision (e.g., feedback in cortical circuits, competitive inhibition, and iterative inference), and to prior computational attention via RL and glimpses, e.g., Larochelle & Hinton, Denil et al., and Zeiler & Fergus for visualization. Compared to prior RL-based attention that often targets saccades or foveation, dasNet performs internal, channel-wise attention within a modern CNN and demonstrates improvements on established benchmarks.

Strengths
- Clear, forward-looking formulation of combining bottom-up feedforward recognition with top-down, learned attentional feedback, trained sequentially (supervised pretraining + policy learning).
- Conceptual novelty: using a high-dimensional, per-feature-map gating policy that conditions on internal activations across layers and evolves its parameters via SNES to modulate the network over time.
- Empirical results: state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100 among single models on unaugmented data; improvements concentrate on ambiguous cases, matching the stated motivation.
- Temporal evaluation: accuracy as a function of recurrent steps, including stability beyond the trained horizon; analysis showing that gate vectors themselves carry class-relevant information.
- Interpretability: informative visualizations of changing map activations across time and layers; qualitative examples that illustrate successful disambiguation (cat vs dog).
- Careful engineering of the action parameterization (positive gates centered around 1 via scaled softmax) to maintain stability.

Weaknesses
- Scalability and external significance: no experiments on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ILSVRC), where the approach’s computational overhead, memory footprint, and optimization stability would be more telling; this is the main concern.
- Complexity vs gain: compared to a single-pass CNN, dasNet adds a million-parameter policy, multiple test-time passes (T up to 5), and evolutionary training; compute/latency trade-offs are not quantified.
- Limited attention granularity: the policy operates on feature-map gates averaged over space; it cannot perform spatial attention, which may cap the benefits on complex scenes.
- Baselines and ablations: missing comparisons to simpler alternatives (e.g., learned linear gating, gradient-based policy training, backprop-through-time over T steps, or learned gates restricted to the top layer only). An ablation that freezes some layers’ gates or restricts observation components would clarify where the gains come from.
- Failure modes: since performance hinges on the attention policy, the paper should document representative failure cases (e.g., when gates amplify spurious features, confirmation bias across steps, or over-correction) and provide diagnostic signals.
- Clarity issue: the text mentions both training with data augmentation for the base Maxout and reporting “unaugmented data” SOTA; please clarify whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time augmentation or to single-model vs committees.
- NES choice: the paper argues for SNES due to dimensionality; however, comparisons to simpler policy gradient baselines or modern diagonal natural gradients would strengthen the methodological choice.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent algorithm, sensible policy parameterization, and stable multi-step behavior. Claims are supported by CIFAR experiments, temporal analyses, and visualizations. However, the empirical study would be stronger with large-scale validation, compute/latency reporting, and ablations vs simpler gating strategies and training procedures.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The learning algorithm and observation/action design are clear, and hyperparameters are reported. Please clarify the “unaugmented” claim vs the stated data augmentation in training the base model, and provide more detail on SNES settings and stopping criteria (some of which are in the supplement).

Originality
High. While feedback, recurrence, and RL-based attention have precedents, the specific integration of a modern CNN with learned, per-map top-down gating trained via SNES, and the demonstration of accuracy gains on CIFAR, is novel and interesting.

Significance
Moderate to high. The approach advances the discussion of how to combine bottom-up and top-down signals in practical CNNs and shows measurable gains. Its broader impact depends on scalability; demonstrating benefits on larger datasets would substantially increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ILSVRC subset, STL-10, SVHN) to assess scalability and practical viability; report wall-clock and memory costs per recurrent step.
- Provide ablations: (a) linear vs nonlinear policy, (b) top-layer-only gates, (c) gradient-based policy learning vs SNES, (d) observation components removed, (e) allowing negative gates or rescaling bounds.
- Quantify accuracy/compute trade-offs and consider adaptive stopping when confidence exceeds a threshold.
- Document failure cases and analyze when the policy hurts, including per-class confusions and robustness to occlusion/noise.
- Explore a spatial attention variant to complement channel-wise gating.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled integration of top-down feedback into CNNs with a learned attention policy.
- State-of-the-art results on CIFAR datasets with informative temporal analyses and visualizations.
- Forward-looking contribution bridging bottom-up features and top-down control that is likely to inspire follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- No evidence of scalability to larger, more realistic datasets; computational overhead and complexity not fully justified.
- Missing ablations and baseline comparisons; unclear how much complexity is necessary for the observed gains.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and practical deployment considerations.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is novel and interesting, demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR, and advances the conversation on internal attention and feedback in deep vision models. Addressing scalability and ablations would strengthen the contribution further; the method is worth further study.
