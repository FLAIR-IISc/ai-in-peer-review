This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to enable dynamic filter sensitivity during classification, effectively implementing internal selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus on relevant features, mimicking aspects of human visual processing where multiple passes are often required for disambiguation. This is achieved by learning a control policy, through Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), that modulates the activation of convolutional filters across layers. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, outperforming previous models on unaugmented data.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of work in deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [1, 5, 6, 7, 8], particularly Maxout networks [7], which serve as the underlying model. It acknowledges the biological inspiration behind recurrent processing in the visual cortex [28, 29, 30] and connections to work on attention mechanisms [40, 41, 42, 43, 44, 45] and iterative processing [48, 49, 50]. However, it distinguishes itself by applying reinforcement learning to a network of significant scale, addressing a key limitation of earlier work often constrained by computational resources. The use of SNES [19] is particularly noteworthy as a practical solution for optimizing a high-dimensional policy space (over 1 million parameters). While the individual components—CNNs, reinforcement learning, and attention—are not novel in isolation, their integration and successful application to a challenging image classification task represent a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data.
* **Sound Methodology:** The combination of a well-established CNN architecture (Maxout) with a reinforcement learning-based attention mechanism is thoughtfully designed. The choice of SNES to handle the large parameter space is a pragmatic and effective solution.
* **Clear Motivation & Biological Plausibility:** The work is clearly motivated by observations of human visual processing, providing a compelling rationale for exploring feedback connections and iterative refinement.
* **Good Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and experimental setup. The visualization of filter activations (Figure 3) is particularly helpful in understanding the network's behavior.
* **Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides sufficient detail to allow for replication of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results on CIFAR are encouraging, the paper would benefit from evaluation on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet. Scaling the approach to these datasets would provide a more rigorous test of its generalizability and practical applicability.
* **Analysis of Attentional Policy:** While the authors visualize the filter activations and changes, a more in-depth analysis of the learned attentional policy would be beneficial.  Understanding *what* the network is attending to and *why* could provide further insights into its behavior.
* **Computational Cost:**  The training process (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is computationally expensive. A discussion of the computational requirements and potential strategies for reducing them would be valuable.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper mentions related work in attention mechanisms, but a more direct comparison to other attention-based models would strengthen the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of dasNet.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep convolutional neural networks.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement in performance on challenging image classification tasks.
* The work is grounded in sound theoretical principles and biological inspiration.
* The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experiments are limited to relatively small datasets (CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100).
* The computational cost of training is significant.
* A more detailed analysis of the learned attentional policy would be valuable.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed dasNet architecture is a promising approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs, and the empirical results are compelling.  While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors consider scaling their approach to larger datasets and providing a more in-depth analysis of the learned attentional policy in future work.
