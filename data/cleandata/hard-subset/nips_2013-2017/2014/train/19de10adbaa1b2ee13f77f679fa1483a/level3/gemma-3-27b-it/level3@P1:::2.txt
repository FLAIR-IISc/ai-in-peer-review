This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel CNN architecture incorporating feedback connections to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to mimic the brain’s non-stationary processing by allowing iterative refinement of feature selection through a learned attentional policy. This policy, controlling the activation of convolutional filters, is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the large parameter space. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, particularly on challenging instances where a standard CNN would struggle. The work builds upon previous advancements in CNNs [1, 7, 8] and draws inspiration from neuroscientific findings on the role of feedback connections in visual processing [28, 30, 31]. It also connects to research in reinforcement learning applied to visual attention [40, 41] and recurrent processing for image analysis [48, 49].

The strengths of this paper lie in its innovative approach to introducing adaptability into CNNs. The demonstration of improved performance on CIFAR datasets is a significant achievement, pushing the state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a pragmatic solution, allowing for the training of a complex attentional mechanism. The concept of allowing a network to “think” through an image iteratively, focusing on different features, is a compelling and biologically plausible idea.  The authors appropriately acknowledge prior work in both the computer vision and neuroscience domains, establishing a strong foundation for their contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the performance gains are notable, the mechanism by which dasNet achieves these gains remains largely a black box. The analysis of the learned attentional policy is insufficient; a deeper understanding of *why* certain filters are emphasized or suppressed would be invaluable. The discussion of the internal dynamics of the network is lacking. For example, the observation that layer-1 activations increase on the first iteration, as highlighted in the experiments, requires further explanation.  Furthermore, the connection to reinforcement learning feels tenuous; with immediate rewards and a deterministic policy, the system appears more akin to a recurrent neural network. Exploring connections to dynamical systems theory could provide a more appropriate framework for analysis.

Clarity is generally good, but several equations contain minor errors or could benefit from more thorough explanation. For instance, the summation index in Equation 11 is ambiguous, and Equation 8 may require an image index.  There are also notational inconsistencies (unexplained symbols ‘x’ and ‘d’ after Eq.11). The description of Algorithm 1 is unclear, specifically the undefined function h_M and the ambiguity surrounding whether F[i] and Θ[i] are accumulated per image or overwritten. The justification for theta regularization is also not fully convincing, and the details of the gradient updates are sparse.

The originality of the work is strong. While recurrent connections and attention mechanisms have been explored in other contexts, the combination of SNES-driven policy learning with a CNN architecture to achieve dynamic filter weighting represents a novel approach.  The significance of the results is also considerable, as the demonstrated performance improvements suggest a promising path towards more robust and adaptable image classification systems.  However, the lack of interpretability limits the broader impact; it's difficult to generalize the findings or apply them to other domains without a clearer understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on challenging datasets.
*   Presents a novel and potentially impactful architecture for dynamic filter weighting.
*   Addresses an important limitation of standard CNNs – their lack of adaptability.
*   Successfully applies SNES to a high-dimensional policy learning problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of interpretability and insufficient analysis of the learned attentional policy.
*   Algorithmic and notational clarity issues hinder reproducibility.
*   Weak justification for certain design choices (e.g., theta regularization).
*   The connection to reinforcement learning feels superficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and effective approach to improving CNN performance by introducing a recurrent-style attentional mechanism. The results are impressive, but the paper’s impact would be significantly enhanced by a deeper analysis of the internal dynamics and a clearer explanation of the learned policy.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on clarifying the algorithm, improving the interpretability of the results, and exploring connections to dynamical systems theory. The authors should address the specific concerns outlined regarding equation notation and algorithmic description.
