This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture with recurrent feedback that modulates the gain of feature maps across multiple evaluation passes. Starting from a strong Maxout CNN trained in the standard supervised fashion, the authors add multiplicative “gates” on each feature map and learn a policy that, given a summary of the network’s internal activations and output probabilities, sets these gates over T recurrent steps to refine the final prediction. The policy is learned by direct policy search using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for scalability in a million-parameter regime. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet improves over the base CNN and reports state-of-the-art results on the unaugmented test sets. The paper positions this as a computational instantiation of top-down, modulatory feedback known from visual neuroscience and connects to prior work on RL-driven attention and recurrent processing.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on Maxout networks and dropout, and the idea of recurrence for vision (e.g., Fukushima’s feedback paths, O’Reilly’s recirculation, Wyatte et al. on recurrent robustness). It differs from saccade/glimpse models that move a fovea (e.g., Larochelle & Hinton; Denil et al.) by keeping the input fixed and instead modulating internal features. The work is contemporaneous with increasing interest in attention and recurrence at NIPS and elsewhere; while individual ingredients (feature-wise gating, RL/policy search, feedback) are not novel in isolation, their integration into a competitive CNN in 2014 is a timely contribution that targets state-of-the-art performance rather than toy demos.

Technical summary: The base Maxout CNN is augmented so that each convolutional feature map y^ℓ_j is multiplied by a scalar gate a^ℓ_j prior to pooling. At pass t, an observation vector concatenates average map activations across layers, the pre-softmax activations, and the current class probabilities. A linear policy πθ(o) = softmax(θo) (scaled to maintain mean gate 1) produces the next set of gates. After T passes, the final prediction is used to compute a loss that boosts misclassified cases to focus policy learning on hard examples. Because the policy has a very high dimensional parameter vector, the authors use SNES to evolve θ; fitness includes the boosted loss and L2 regularization. Experiments fix T=5 and show: (1) improved accuracy over the base CNN, reaching SOTA on CIFAR-10/100 without test-time augmentation; (2) stability of dynamics when run beyond the trained number of steps; and (3) evidence that the learned gates carry label-relevant information (nearest-neighbor and logistic regression on gate vectors perform well).

Quality: The method is clearly defined and technically plausible. The use of SNES is reasonable for scale, and the ablations on dynamics and gate informativeness are helpful. However, important baselines and analyses are missing or underdeveloped: (a) training the same feedback controller end-to-end with gradient-based methods (e.g., BPTT through T steps) to compare against black-box SNES; (b) a baseline with additional passes but fixed gates or random gates to isolate the effect of recurrent computation; (c) a comparison to simply making the feedforward CNN larger or using an ensemble for equivalent compute; (d) a breakdown of where the gains occur (per-class confusion matrices, effect on ambiguous classes targeted by the method). Computational cost is only briefly described; more detail on training/inference overhead relative to the base model would help assess practicality. There is also a potential inconsistency: the text states both that the model “outperforms SOTA on unaugmented datasets” and that the base Maxout network was “trained with data augmentation.” Clarifying what “unaugmented” refers to (training vs. test-time augmentation) is important for fair comparison.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized, with a clear algorithm outline and design choices (e.g., scaling of softmax to keep gates near 1). The observation vector design is explained, although its loss of spatial structure could be acknowledged more explicitly. Hyperparameters (population size, T, loss weights) are given; reporting wall-clock cost and parameter counts for the policy would further improve reproducibility. Figures illustrating the changes in activations are informative; a few more concrete examples would aid interpretability.

Originality: Moderate. Feedback in CNNs, attention via gating, and RL/policy search have precedents; the main novelty is their combination at scale on competitive benchmarks with demonstrated accuracy gains. The choice of SNES for million-parameter policy search is unconventional in contemporary deep learning and may interest the community.

Significance: Moderate to high. Empirically, the paper shows that learned top-down modulation can correct base CNN mistakes and improve accuracy on widely used benchmarks, lending support to feedback/attention as a useful ingredient for vision. The approach is likely to inspire follow-ups that replace SNES with differentiable controllers, add spatially selective attention, or apply to larger datasets. Scalability to ImageNet and more complex architectures remains to be demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the augmentation claim and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with prior SOTA (train/test-time augmentation, ensembles).
- Add baselines: fixed gates across T passes; gradient-based training of the controller via BPTT; compute-matched larger feedforward model or ensemble.
- Report training/inference cost (FLOPs, runtime) and accuracy vs. compute trade-offs.
- Analyze where improvements occur (per-class confusion matrices, hard/easy subsets).
- Explore richer observations (including spatial summaries) or spatially localized gates.
- Discuss stability and convergence properties of SNES and sensitivity to T.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed integration of feedback attention with a strong CNN baseline.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100, with analyses of dynamics and gate informativeness.
- Timely reinforcement of the role of feedback/attention in deep vision models.
- Method is conceptually simple and potentially compatible with many CNNs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty in core components; contribution is primarily integrative.
- Missing baselines and incomplete comparisons to gradient-based recurrent training.
- Scalability and compute overhead not fully quantified; evaluation limited to small images.
- Some ambiguity about augmentation in comparisons.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a solid empirical case that learned feedback improves recognition and presents a clear framework; addressing the suggested comparisons and scaling the approach to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would strengthen the impact.
