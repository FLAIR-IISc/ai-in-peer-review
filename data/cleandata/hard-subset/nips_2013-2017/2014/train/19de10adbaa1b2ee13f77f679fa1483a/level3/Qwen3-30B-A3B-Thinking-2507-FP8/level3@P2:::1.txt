

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to implement internal selective attention. dasNet dynamically modulates convolutional filter sensitivities during classification through a reinforcement learning (RL) policy, learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) over a million-dimensional parameter space. This allows the network to iteratively refine its focus on discriminative features across multiple passes over an image, correcting initial misclassifications without retraining the base CNN. The approach builds on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., NIPS 2013) and leverages insights from neurobiology about top-down feedback in visual processing (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Bar et al., 2003). On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, dasNet achieves state-of-the-art results on unaugmented data, demonstrating a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical execution is robust. dasNet’s design effectively combines Maxout networks (a NIPS 2013 contribution) with SNES for scalable policy learning, addressing the challenge of high-dimensional RL in CNNs. Experiments are thorough: they validate the attention mechanism visually (Fig. 3), quantify dynamics across passes (Fig. 2), and confirm that gate values contain meaningful classification information (40.70% 15-NN accuracy). The ablation (T=5 steps) and boosting strategy (λ_misclassified > λ_correct) are well-motivated.  
*Originality*: While individual components (Maxout, SNES, feedback connections) are not entirely new, their *integration* for selective attention in CNNs is novel. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work: unlike RL-based attention in simplified settings (e.g., Schmidhuber 2010), dasNet applies to state-of-the-art CNNs on standard benchmarks. It also contrasts with attention mechanisms for RNNs (Bahdanau et al., NIPS 2014), making a distinct contribution to *feedforward* vision models.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written. Algorithm 1 precisely details the training procedure, and Figures 1–3 effectively illustrate the attention dynamics. The explanation of how feedback alters filter activations (e.g., "change of layer 0" in Fig. 3) is intuitive. Minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., Equation 5 vs. text) are easily fixable.  
*Significance*: dasNet provides compelling empirical evidence that feedback-driven selective attention improves CNN performance on challenging cases (e.g., misclassifying cats as dogs). This advances the field by demonstrating that iterative refinement—inspired by human visual processing—can be practically implemented in deep learning, opening avenues for more biologically plausible models.

**Weaknesses**  
*Scope and Impact*: The work is limited to CIFAR-10/100, which are small-scale benchmarks. While the results are strong, the significance would be substantially amplified by demonstrating scalability to ImageNet or larger datasets (as noted in the key points). The paper lacks ablation studies on T (number of passes) beyond T=5, making it unclear if the gains saturate or degrade with more iterations.  
*Methodology*: The RL policy is trained via SNES, but the paper does not compare against standard RL methods (e.g., policy gradients), which could better justify the choice of SNES for this task. The claim that dasNet "does not disrupt or forget what has been learned" relies solely on test accuracy; a more rigorous analysis of catastrophic forgetting on the base CNN’s training data would strengthen this.  
*Related Work*: The related work section cites key neurobiology papers but could better engage with recent NIPS work on attention (e.g., "Learning to Attend" by Bahdanau et al., 2014), which, while for RNNs, shares conceptual parallels. Clarifying how dasNet’s *internal* attention differs from *external* attention mechanisms (e.g., spatial gating) would enhance context.

**Recommendation**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of attention in deep vision models. The technical soundness, clarity, and empirical results are strong, and the work is clearly positioned within the NIPS community (citing relevant proceedings). While the CIFAR-only scope is a limitation, the approach is promising and the authors rightly recommend scaling to larger datasets. The paper advances the state of the art and provides a compelling framework for future work. **Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the T ablation, RL comparison, and ImageNet scalability discussion). The significance lies in demonstrating that feedback-based attention is not just biologically plausible but *practically effective* for improving CNNs, a direction that merits broader exploration.
