This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with top-down feedback connections. These feedback connections are designed to modulate the activity of convolutional filters over multiple processing passes on the same input image, effectively creating a form of internal, selective attention. The model is trained in two stages. First, a base CNN (a Maxout network) is trained via standard supervised learning. Subsequently, the paper frames the control of the filter modulations (attentional gates) as a reinforcement learning problem. A policy, represented by a linear mapping from network state to gate activations, is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to minimize classification loss, with a focus on correcting initially misclassified examples. This approach is well-grounded in neuroscience literature on the human visual system, which posits a fast, pre-attentive feedforward pass followed by a slower, recurrent attentional phase for refining perception. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet by achieving state-of-the-art results on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Model:** The primary strength of this work is its novel and conceptually ambitious approach. The idea of incorporating a learned, dynamic, internal attention mechanism into a deep network via feedback represents a significant and forward-looking departure from the purely feedforward models that dominate the field. The combination of bottom-up feature extraction with top-down attentional signals is a powerful paradigm that is biologically plausible and holds great promise.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model's excellent performance provides compelling evidence for the utility of the proposed method. Achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10/100 for unaugmented data is a significant achievement and validates that the additional complexity of the feedback mechanism can lead to tangible performance gains.
3.  **Thoughtful Analysis and Interpretation:** The authors provide valuable analysis to support their claims. The visualization of filter modulation in Figure 3 is particularly insightful, offering a concrete example of how the model corrects an initial misclassification by re-weighting internal feature maps. Furthermore, the experiments investigating the model's dynamics over time and the analysis showing that the final gate activations contain significant class information are well-executed and strengthen the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the question of scalability. While impressive on CIFAR, the method's complexity and training time (4 days on a GPU for the RL phase on a relatively small dataset) raise concerns about its practicality for larger-scale problems like ImageNet (ILSVRC). The number of parameters in the policy network grows quadratically with the state/action dimension, and applying this to much deeper and wider modern architectures seems challenging. A demonstration, or at least a thorough discussion, of how this approach could scale would substantially strengthen the paper's impact.
2.  **Robustness of the Attention Policy:** The success of dasNet hinges entirely on the quality of the learned attention policy. The paper demonstrates a successful case but does not explore or discuss potential failure modes. For instance, could the policy learn to over-emphasize spurious features or become unstable over many time steps? An analysis of failure cases or a discussion of the policy's robustness would make the work more complete and provide a clearer picture of its limitations.
3.  **Complexity of the Training Pipeline:** The proposed method adds considerable complexity to both the model architecture and the training pipeline. The two-stage process involving supervised pre-training followed by a high-dimensional black-box policy search is more intricate and likely harder to tune than standard end-to-end training. This complexity could be a barrier to wider adoption.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
-   The choice of SNES is justified by the high dimensionality of the policy parameters. Could the authors comment on whether gradient-based RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) were considered or attempted? While SNES is a powerful black-box optimizer, it might be interesting to know if the problem structure allows for more direct gradient-based optimization.
-   The state representation `o_t` is a concatenation of average map activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities. Was this representation chosen after experimenting with other options? It seems to discard a great deal of spatial information from the feature maps.
-   The reward function uses a simple form of boosting (`λ_boost`). Have the authors experimented with different reward shaping strategies? The current formulation directly optimizes the final classification loss, which is reasonable, but other formulations might encourage different and potentially useful dynamics.

**Evaluation:**
-   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by the experiments on CIFAR. The use of SNES for the high-dimensional policy search is a reasonable and well-justified choice.
-   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors effectively explain the model's architecture, the training process, and the connection to neuroscience. The visualizations are particularly helpful.
-   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The combination of a state-of-the-art CNN with an RL-trained feedback mechanism for internal selective attention is a novel and significant contribution.
-   **Significance:** The paper's significance is high. Empirically, it advances the state of the art on a standard benchmark. Conceptually, it introduces a promising and biologically plausible paradigm for incorporating sequential processing into deep networks. The ideas presented are very likely to be influential and inspire future research into attention and recurrent processing in computer vision.

**Summary:**
This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and exciting research direction. By adding a dynamically controlled feedback loop to a CNN, the authors show that a network can learn to "re-think" its initial perception and correct its own mistakes, leading to state-of-the-art performance. While there are valid concerns about the method's scalability and complexity, the originality of the idea and the strong empirical results on CIFAR make this a valuable contribution that is well worth further study by the community. I recommend acceptance.
