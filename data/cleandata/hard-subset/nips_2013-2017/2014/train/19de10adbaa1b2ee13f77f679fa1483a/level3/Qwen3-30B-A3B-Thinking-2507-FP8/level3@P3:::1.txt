

# Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through feedback connections. The authors propose a model that dynamically modulates filter activations during classification using a reinforcement learning (RL) policy, allowing the network to iteratively refine its predictions through multiple passes over the input image. This work builds on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) and extends them with a gating mechanism controlled by a policy learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The approach is inspired by biological visual systems, where feedback connections play a crucial role in selective attention, as documented in the work of Felleman and Essen (1991) and Lamme (2000).

dasNet's key innovation lies in its combined approach: using RL to learn a gating policy that modulates filter activations across multiple passes through the network, rather than using a fixed attention mechanism. This differs from previous attention mechanisms in vision (e.g., Learning to Attend with Deep Architectures for Image Tracking, Denil et al., 2014) by learning the attention policy rather than hard-coding it, and from other reinforcement learning approaches to vision (e.g., Learning to Generate Artificial Fovea Trajectories, Schmidhuber & Huber, 2003) by applying it directly to improve state-of-the-art CNNs rather than to simplified tasks.

The paper is technically sound, with thorough experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 showing state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data (6% relative improvement over the vanilla CNN). The authors provide a clear explanation of the attention mechanism through a detailed example (Figure 3), demonstrating how the network corrects a misclassification of a cat as a dog by selectively emphasizing certain filters. The ablation studies (e.g., the dynamics analysis for different numbers of passes) further demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a strong related work section that carefully distinguishes the authors' contribution from previous research. The presentation of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the training procedure. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the approach has only been tested on CIFAR datasets and suggesting future work on scaling to larger datasets.

The main limitations of the work are that it has only been tested on CIFAR datasets, not on larger-scale problems like ImageNet. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, it would have been valuable to discuss potential challenges in scaling the approach to larger datasets. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates that the attention mechanism improves performance, it doesn't deeply analyze why certain filters are emphasized or suppressed, which could provide additional insights into how the network is learning.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing empirical evidence that incorporating feedback connections and attention mechanisms can improve CNN performance. The results are particularly compelling given the limited computational resources used (a single GTX 560 Ti GPU for 4 days of training). The work advances the field by demonstrating that attention mechanisms can be effectively learned rather than hard-coded, and that this approach can yield state-of-the-art results on challenging vision benchmarks.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with appropriate experiments and analysis.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized.
- Originality: While no single component is highly novel, the combination of techniques is well-chosen and effectively executed.
- Significance: The results demonstrate a clear improvement on a challenging benchmark, providing empirical support for a promising direction in vision models.

The authors should be encouraged to scale this approach to larger datasets like ImageNet to further validate its potential. The current work is a strong foundation for future research on attention mechanisms in deep learning.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong technical execution, clear empirical results, well-written paper, promising direction for future research.  
Arguments against acceptance: Limited to CIFAR datasets, incremental nature of the combination of existing techniques.

This is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of deep learning for vision. The approach is well-motivated, well-executed, and provides compelling evidence for the value of attention mechanisms in CNNs. I recommend acceptance.
