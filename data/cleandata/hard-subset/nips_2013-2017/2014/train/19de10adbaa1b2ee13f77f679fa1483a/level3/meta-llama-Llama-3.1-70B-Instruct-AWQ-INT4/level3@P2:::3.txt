This paper proposes a novel deep neural network architecture, called Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), which incorporates attention-based top-down feedback to improve image classification performance. The authors draw inspiration from the human visual cortex, where feedback connections play a crucial role in object recognition. The proposed architecture combines a standard Maxout network with a feedback structure that allows the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on certain features extracted from images.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction provides a thorough background on the state-of-the-art in object recognition, highlighting the limitations of traditional feedforward convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors then introduce the dasNet architecture, explaining how it differs from traditional CNNs and how it can be trained using reinforcement learning.

The technical soundness of the paper is strong, with a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The authors provide a detailed description of the training algorithm, including the use of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to evolve the policy. The experimental evaluation is also thorough, with results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets showing state-of-the-art performance.

One of the strengths of the paper is its novelty and originality. The authors propose a forward-looking approach that combines bottom-up and top-down signals for training, which is a departure from traditional feedforward CNNs. The use of gated signals to constrain representations is also an interesting aspect of the architecture. The experiments include visualizations that help interpret the model, providing insight into how the attention policy affects the network's performance.

However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's limitations. The method is more complex than traditional feedforward CNNs, and the lack of results on larger datasets, such as ILSVRC, raises concerns about its scalability. The authors should consider including experiments on larger datasets to demonstrate the practical insights of their approach.

Another concern is the representation quality, which hinges on the attention policy. The authors should suggest or demonstrate failure cases to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's limitations. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the dynamics of the dasNet architecture, including the effects of different values of T on the network's performance.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in image classification, namely, how to improve performance on difficult classification instances. The dasNet architecture provides a novel solution to this problem, and the results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrate its effectiveness. The paper also provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to image classification, which is likely to inspire further research in this area.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and original. While there are some concerns regarding its limitations, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, and the results are promising. The paper is definitely worth further study, and the authors should consider addressing the concerns raised above in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original deep neural network architecture that incorporates attention-based top-down feedback.
