This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture incorporating attention-based top-down feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies). The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically adjust its filter sensitivities post-training, iteratively refining its classification by focusing on salient features. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showcasing the potential of combining bottom-up and top-down processing in deep learning.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of research in CNNs (LeCun, 1989; Ciresan et al., 2012), Maxout networks (Goodfellow, 2013), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018). It addresses a long-standing observation in neuroscience – the prevalence of recurrent connections and top-down modulation in the visual cortex (Felleman & Van Essen, 1991; Gilbert & Sigman, 1999) – and attempts to translate these principles into a more powerful computational model. Recent work in attention mechanisms (e.g., Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) and recurrent processing (e.g., Larochelle & Hinton, 2010) serves as related context, however dasNet distinguishes itself by *learning* the attention policy through RL, rather than relying on predefined mechanisms.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Interesting Model Design:** The combination of CNNs with attention-based feedback, trained using reinforcement learning, is a novel and intriguing approach. It moves beyond purely feedforward architectures and explores a more biologically plausible processing paradigm.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR datasets, even with unaugmented data, is a significant accomplishment and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Forward-Looking Approach:** The paper tackles a critical limitation of standard CNNs – their static nature – and proposes a solution that allows for dynamic adaptation and refinement. This approach is likely to be increasingly important as we move towards more complex and challenging vision tasks.
* **Interpretability:** The provided visualizations (Figure 3) offer valuable insights into how the attention mechanism operates, showing how filter activations change across iterations and revealing the network’s internal focus.
* **Evaluation of Dynamics:** The analysis of the model's behaviour across multiple iterations (Figure 2) is insightful and demonstrates the stability of the learned attention policy.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** The primary weakness is the lack of results on larger, more realistic datasets like ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). While the CIFAR results are encouraging, it remains unclear whether the method will scale effectively to handle the increased complexity and dimensionality of ImageNet. The computational cost of training the attention policy using SNES is likely a significant factor.
* **Limited Failure Case Analysis:** While visualizations are provided for successful cases, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of scenarios where the model fails. Identifying failure cases, and analyzing *why* the attention mechanism doesn’t help, would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach. What kinds of images or classes are particularly challenging for dasNet?
* **Policy Sensitivity:** The authors acknowledge that representation quality hinges on the attention policy. However, the paper doesn’t offer much guidance on how to design or initialize the policy effectively. Exploring different policy architectures or initialization strategies could be beneficial.
* **Complexity:** Compared to standard feedforward CNNs, dasNet is significantly more complex, involving an additional reinforcement learning phase and a larger parameter space. This increased complexity may limit its practical applicability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and well-motivated approach to improving CNN performance.
* The empirical results on CIFAR are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The visualizations provide valuable insights into the model’s internal workings.
* The work addresses a fundamental limitation of standard CNNs and opens up new avenues for research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of results on larger datasets raises concerns about the scalability of the method.
* The limited failure case analysis leaves some unanswered questions about the model’s limitations.
* The increased complexity may hinder its practical adoption.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed dasNet architecture represents a promising step towards more flexible and adaptable deep learning models. While the lack of ImageNet results is a concern, the compelling CIFAR performance and insightful visualizations justify further investigation. I recommend acceptance with a request for the authors to address the scalability and failure case analysis concerns in future work. Experiments on larger datasets are crucial to demonstrate the practical relevance of this approach. Additionally, the authors should investigate the sensitivity of the model to different policy initialization and architecture choices.
