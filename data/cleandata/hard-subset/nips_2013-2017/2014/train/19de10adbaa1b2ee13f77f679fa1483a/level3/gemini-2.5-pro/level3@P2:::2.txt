### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. These connections allow the network to dynamically modulate the activity of its convolutional filters over a sequence of processing steps for a single input image. The core idea is to move beyond the static, one-shot inference of traditional CNNs by introducing an internal, selective attention mechanism.

The model is built upon a pre-trained Maxout network. A control policy, implemented as a linear mapping from network state to filter modulation weights, is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method framed here as a reinforcement learning problem. Over a small number of iterations (T=5), the policy adjusts the filter activations to refine the network's prediction. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves classification accuracy on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data. This work connects to broader themes of recurrence and top-down modulation in both biological vision and neural networks, but is novel in its application to modern deep learning architectures and its use of evolution strategies for policy search in a very high-dimensional action space.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper presents a technically interesting approach, and the empirical results are a significant strength. Achieving a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 without data augmentation is impressive and demonstrates the practical potential of the method.

However, the work's primary weakness is its lack of scientific insight. The model is presented as a black box, and the analysis of *why* it works is insufficient. The visualizations in Figure 3 are uninformative, showing that activations change but not providing a clear, interpretable reason for these changes. A deeper analysis is needed. For example:
*   An analysis of "hard cases"—images misclassified by the baseline Maxout network but corrected by dasNet—would be invaluable. What specific features are being emphasized or suppressed to correct the initial error?
*   The internal dynamics are not well-explored. Why do layer-1 activations seem to increase on the first iteration? What is the effect of the global scaling in the softmax of Eq. 7? How do gating variables evolve over local image regions corresponding to the object versus the background?

Furthermore, the justification for framing the problem as reinforcement learning is weak. With a deterministic policy and an immediate reward signal delivered at the end of a fixed number of steps (T), the model more closely resembles a recurrent neural network unrolled in time. Recasting the work in the language of dynamical systems or recurrent networks might provide a more solid theoretical foundation and open up more powerful analytical tools.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but several key details are unclear, hindering reproducibility and full comprehension.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The function `h(M_t)` used to construct the observation vector `o_t` is not explicitly defined, only described in prose. It is also ambiguous whether the fitness scores `F[i]` and parameters `Θ[i]` are collected per image `j` or are overwritten such that only the final cumulative score for a policy `θ_i` is stored.
*   **Equations:** There appear to be several typos or unclear definitions.
    *   In Eq. 11, the summation `∑_{i=1}^{n}` is confusing, as `i` is also used to index the policy sample `θ_i`. A different index (e.g., `j`) should be used for the image batch.
    *   In Eq. 8, `L_i` is the loss for policy `i`, but the log-probability `log(v_T)` is for a specific image. This equation should likely be indexed by the image as well, e.g., `L_{i,j}`.
    *   The symbols `d` and `v_T` are used in Eq. 10, but the text following Eq. 11 refers to `x` and `d` without defining them in that context.
*   **Regularization:** The L2 regularization on the policy parameters `θ` in Eq. 11 is not justified. It is also unclear how the SNES update step incorporates this term.

**Originality:**

The high-level concept of adding a learnable, dynamic, internal attention mechanism to a deep CNN is original and significant. While recurrent connections are not new, applying them in this manner to modulate filter activations in a state-of-the-art CNN architecture is a novel contribution. The use of SNES to train the high-dimensional control policy is also an unconventional and interesting choice. This work represents an important conceptual step beyond fixed-parameter, purely feed-forward vision models.

**Significance:**

The results are significant, as they demonstrate a clear performance gain over a very strong baseline and establish a new state-of-the-art. If the mechanisms can be better understood, this work could inspire a new class of dynamic and adaptive deep learning models. However, the lack of interpretability and clarity currently limits its impact. Without a better understanding of what the learned policy is doing, it is difficult for other researchers to build upon these ideas or to trust the method's robustness.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and conceptually interesting architecture that adds dynamic, internal attention to deep CNNs.
2.  The method achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), which is a strong empirical contribution.
3.  The work explores an important future direction for deep learning, moving beyond static feed-forward processing toward more flexible, recurrent computation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper provides very little insight into *how* or *why* the method works. The analysis is superficial, treating the model as a black box.
2.  The framing as a reinforcement learning problem is questionable and may not be the most appropriate theoretical lens. The model behaves more like a recurrent network.
3.  Significant clarity issues in the algorithm description and equations hinder reproducibility and a complete understanding of the method.
4.  The lack of deeper analysis and interpretability limits the scientific contribution, making it difficult for others to build on this work.

### Conclusion and Suggestions for Improvement

This paper presents an interesting and effective approach for improving CNN classification performance through a learned recurrent attention mechanism. The strong empirical results are commendable. However, the work is held back by a significant lack of analysis and several clarity issues. I recommend acceptance but strongly urge the authors to address the following points in the final version:

1.  **Improve Analysis and Interpretability:** Please provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned policies. A qualitative study of "hard cases" that are corrected by dasNet would be highly valuable.
2.  **Re-evaluate the Theoretical Framing:** Consider discussing the connection to recurrent neural networks and dynamical systems, as this may be a more fitting framework than reinforcement learning.
3.  **Clarify the Manuscript:** Please revise Algorithm 1 for clarity, explicitly define `h(M_t)`, and correct the notational issues in Equations 8 and 11. Explain the motivation for the L2 regularization on `θ`.

Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution from a promising engineering result to a more complete and impactful piece of scientific work.
