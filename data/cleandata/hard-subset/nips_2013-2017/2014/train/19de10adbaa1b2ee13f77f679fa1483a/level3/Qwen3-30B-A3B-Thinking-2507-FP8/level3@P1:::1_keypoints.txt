- Introduces dasNet: a CNN with feedback connections that iteratively modulate filter sensitivities via an RL-trained policy for internal selective attention.
- Core innovation: feedback gates that modulate convolutional activations.
- Core innovation: a policy learned with Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to control high-dimensional gates.
- Core innovation: a boosting mechanism (λboost) that focuses training on misclassified examples.
- Validated on CIFAR-10/100, achieving state-of-the-art on un-augmented data (≈6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout).
- SNES is well-motivated for the million-dimensional policy space and technically well-executed.
- Experimental design isolates the attention mechanism (comparison to first-pass baseline) and empirically validates boosting.
- Lacks ablation studies (e.g., against static gating) and deeper quantitative analysis of gate–feature correlations beyond qualitative visuals.
- Training stability (Figure 2) is convincing, but using a fixed T=5 steps raises questions about scalability to more complex tasks.
- Theoretical justification for the specific SNES implementation is limited.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written; architecture, RL formulation, and experiments are reproducible.
- Algorithm and figures effectively illustrate the iterative attention process; gate modulation is precisely described.
- Minor clarity issues: need explicit observation-space dimensionality and SNES computational cost relative to standard RL.
- Originality: novel synthesis of feedback, RL, and SNES applied practically to standard benchmarks and post-training adaptation of CNNs.
- Significance: provides empirical evidence that recurrent attention refines classification performance, but impact is currently limited to CIFAR and needs ImageNet-scale validation.
- Recommendation: technically sound and significant—accept, with scalability to larger datasets noted as important future work.
- Pros: SOTA CIFAR results, elegant integration of ideas, clear execution, strong biological motivation.
- Cons: limited ablation analyses, no ImageNet validation, and superficial theoretical grounding of the attention mechanism.
