

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances traditional feedforward CNNs with feedback connections enabling selective internal attention during classification. The authors propose augmenting Maxout networks with a gating mechanism that dynamically modulates filter sensitivities through reinforcement learning, specifically using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize a million-dimensional policy. The key innovation lies in allowing the network to iteratively refine its classification by emphasizing/de-emphasizing specific filters across multiple passes on the same image, rather than relying solely on the initial feedforward pass. This approach addresses a critical limitation of standard CNNs: their static nature during inference.

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (6% relative improvement) and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation, outperforming previous methods. The work connects to important prior research on recurrent processing in visual cortex (Felleman & Essen, 1991) and computational models of selective attention (Bar et al., 2003), while extending reinforcement learning applications in vision (Schmidhuber, 2007; Whitehead, 1991) to improve existing CNN architectures. The method differs from prior work by directly enhancing state-of-the-art CNNs rather than developing new architectures from scratch.

The primary strength is the impressive empirical performance on challenging classification tasks, demonstrating that iterative refinement through attentional gating can substantially improve accuracy. The approach is technically sound, with clear experimental design and appropriate baselines. The authors address the practical challenge of optimizing high-dimensional policies through SNES, which is a reasonable choice given the scale of the problem.

However, several weaknesses significantly impact the paper's scientific contribution. The most critical issue is the lack of interpretability: the method functions as a black box with minimal explanation of why the attention mechanism works. The analysis of internal representations (e.g., Figure 3) shows activation changes but fails to connect these to the classification improvements. The authors don't analyze why specific filters are emphasized or how the gating policy correlates with perceptual features. The paper would benefit greatly from examining hard cases where dasNet corrects misclassifications by standard Maxout networks, analyzing how gating patterns differ between correct and incorrect cases.

Theoretical and algorithmic clarity is compromised by several issues. Algorithm 1 contains undefined terms (h(M_t)), ambiguous variable usage (F[i] and Θ[i] are unclear whether collected per image or overwritten), and the summation index in Equation 11 is unspecified. Equations 8-10 have indexing issues (missing image index j), and symbols x and d after Equation 11 remain undefined. The regularization term (λL2∥θi∥2) lacks justification, and it's unclear how the final gradient updates are computed or whether they adjust posterior hyperparameters.

The authors' claim that this is a reinforcement learning approach is questionable. The immediate reward structure (Equation 8) and deterministic policy (Section 3) make the method more akin to iterative refinement than traditional RL. The paper would be stronger if it explicitly connected to dynamical systems theory, as the dynamics resemble recurrent networks more than RL. The authors should explore this connection to clarify the method's theoretical underpinnings.

The exposition is generally clear, but the technical details require substantial improvement. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of gating dynamics (e.g., why layer-1 activations increase on first iteration, effect of global scaling), and the authors should investigate constraints on learned theta statistics to understand the policy's behavior.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and effective approach to improving CNNs through internal selective attention, with strong empirical results that advance the state-of-the-art. The method represents an important step beyond fixed-parameter feedforward networks. However, the lack of interpretability, theoretical clarity, and inadequate analysis of internal dynamics significantly limit its scientific contribution. The authors should address these issues to transform an interesting empirical finding into a meaningful theoretical advance. The paper is promising but currently suffers from being too much of a "black box" solution. With deeper analysis of the learned attention mechanisms and clearer theoretical justification, this work could make a substantial contribution to the field. As presented, the method's empirical success is impressive but the lack of understanding of why it works diminishes its broader scientific impact.
