This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to model selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically alter its convolutional filter sensitivities during classification, mirroring aspects of biological visual processing. This is achieved by learning a control policy via Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to modulate filter activity across multiple passes over an image. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The work builds upon the established foundation of deep convolutional neural networks [1, 7, 8], acknowledging the success of feedforward architectures while seeking to address their limitations in handling ambiguous or complex visual inputs. The motivation, drawing parallels with human visual processing [16, 17], is compelling – humans often engage in iterative analysis, refining their perception through feedback and attention. This perspective is well-articulated and positions dasNet within a broader context of biologically-inspired computation. Related work is discussed thoroughly, referencing advancements in recurrent processing [48, 49, 50], reinforcement learning applied to vision [40, 41, 44], and the importance of top-down influences in visual perception [28, 29, 37]. The authors correctly point out the challenges of applying these approaches to large-scale datasets and position their work as a step towards bridging that gap.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The core concept of introducing learned feedback connections to modulate filter activity is novel and represents a potentially significant contribution to the field. The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are impressive, demonstrably outperforming previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets. The visualizations provided (Figure 3) are insightful, offering a glimpse into the internal dynamics of the network and illustrating how the attention mechanism selectively emphasizes and suppresses filters. The use of SNES to train the policy is a pragmatic solution to the challenges posed by the large parameter space, acknowledging the limitations of traditional reinforcement learning methods. The inclusion of dynamics experiments (Figure 2) and an analysis of gate information content adds further depth to the evaluation. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the performance on CIFAR datasets is encouraging, a crucial limitation is the absence of results on larger, more challenging datasets such as ImageNet (ILSVRC). Demonstrating scalability to real-world problems is critical for establishing the practical significance of this work. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not offer a strong justification for why ILSVRC results were not included. Without such results, it’s difficult to assess whether the computational cost and complexity of dasNet are justified by a substantial performance gain. While the authors demonstrate the network *can* correct misclassifications, a more thorough analysis of failure cases would be beneficial. Showing examples where dasNet *fails* to improve performance, and analyzing *why* would strengthen the paper considerably. Furthermore, the regularization parameter selection and its impact on performance could be discussed in more detail. The explanation of why the action vector averages to 1 rather than summing to 1 is subtle and could be clarified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture with a biologically inspired motivation.
*   State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
*   Insightful visualizations demonstrating the attention mechanism.
*   Pragmatic solution to training a high-dimensional policy using SNES.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of results on larger datasets (ILSVRC) to demonstrate scalability.
*   Limited analysis of failure cases.
*   The benefits of this system relative to simply training larger feedforward networks remains unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The idea of incorporating learned feedback connections into CNNs is promising, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the lack of results on larger datasets is a significant concern. While the current results are valuable, demonstrating scalability is crucial for establishing the long-term significance of this work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to address the scalability concern by including (or at least discussing in detail plans for) experiments on a larger dataset. A more thorough analysis of failure cases would also strengthen the paper. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
