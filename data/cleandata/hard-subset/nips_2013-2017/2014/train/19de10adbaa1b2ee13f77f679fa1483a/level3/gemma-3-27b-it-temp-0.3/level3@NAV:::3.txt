## Review of “Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections”

This paper introduces dasNet, a novel CNN architecture incorporating attention-based top-down feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies). The core idea – allowing a network to iteratively refine its internal representations through feedback – is a compelling one, drawing inspiration from the human visual system and addressing the limitations of purely feedforward CNNs. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showcasing the potential of this approach to improve classification accuracy, particularly in ambiguous cases.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of previous research in CNNs (LeCun et al., 1989; Ciresan et al., 2012), attention mechanisms (Itti & Koch, 2000), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018). It distinguishes itself by integrating these concepts in a unique way, learning the attentional policy *after* supervised training, and applying it iteratively to refine classifications. The visualizations provided (Figure 3) are particularly helpful in understanding how the feedback mechanism alters filter activations and contributes to improved performance. The analysis of dynamics (Figure 2) and the information content of the gates further strengthen the claims.

However, a significant weakness lies in the lack of evaluation on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). While the results on CIFAR are promising, the scalability of this approach – particularly the computational cost of evolving the attention policy in a high-dimensional parameter space – remains unclear. The method’s increased complexity compared to standard feedforward CNNs necessitates demonstrating its benefits on more challenging benchmarks. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge the difficulty in qualitatively analyzing the effect of the alterations, and should more proactively explore and demonstrate potential failure cases or scenarios where the attention mechanism might be detrimental. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and interesting architecture combining bottom-up and top-down processing.
*   State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR datasets.
*   Clear and insightful visualizations aiding model interpretation.
*   Forward-looking approach with potential for broader applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of evaluation on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.
*   Limited discussion of potential failure cases or limitations.
*   Computational complexity of the reinforcement learning component.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a novel contribution. While the lack of large-scale experiments is a concern, the promising results and insightful analysis warrant further investigation. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and explore potential limitations in future work.
