This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture that augments a trained Maxout CNN with multiplicative gates on feature maps and learns a deterministic feedback policy that modulates these gates over multiple passes on the same image. The policy maps summary statistics of the network’s current activations (averaged feature maps per layer, classification-layer activations, and class probabilities) to channel-wise gains aℓj via a scaled softmax, producing positive, unity-mean rescalings per pass. The policy has ∼10^6 parameters and is optimized by Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the high dimensionality. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, allowing T=5 recurrent passes improves top-1 accuracy over a strong Maxout baseline; the policy particularly corrects mistakes on ambiguous classes. The authors position dasNet as a selective attention mechanism inspired by cortical feedback and related to iterative, recurrent vision models and RL-based attention.

Relation to prior work: The model builds on Maxout networks with dropout and standard data normalization, and it connects to recurrent processing and top-down modulation in vision (e.g., Lamme & Roelfsema; Wyatte et al.), “glimpse”/attention mechanisms trained with RL (e.g., Denil et al., Larochelle & Hinton), and evolution strategies for large-scale policy search (NES/SNES). Relative to feedforward CNNs (Krizhevsky et al.), dasNet introduces internal, learned, deterministic attentional feedback. Unlike most RL attention works that explore saccades or glimpses, dasNet modulates channel gains globally and iteratively within the same input.

Quality:
- Strengths: The core mechanism is simple, well-motivated, and effective: per-channel multiplicative gating under a learned policy can correct misclassifications, yielding SOTA on unaugmented CIFAR at the time. The dynamics appear stable when evaluated beyond the training horizon, and the gate vectors contain class-relevant information. SNES provides a practical optimizer for a million-parameter policy.
- Weaknesses: The method functions largely as a black box. The analysis of learned dynamics (e.g., why and where gates change; sensitivity by layer; effect of the scaled softmax) is limited. The RL framing is thin: the policy is deterministic, reward is immediate at step T, and there is no explicit exploration or credit assignment across time via gradients—this looks more like training a recurrent controller via black-box sequence-level loss than an RL problem. Some algorithmic details are unclear or inconsistent (see clarity). The regularization, boosting weights, and SNES update details are insufficiently justified. Computational overhead at test time (multiple passes) is not quantified.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally readable, but several equations and the training algorithm need tighter notation and definitions.
  - Algorithm 1: define h(M) formally (currently “h(Mt)” vs “h_M” is ambiguous). Clarify whether F[i] and Θ[i] accumulate per policy sample across images or are overwritten; consider using L_{i,j} for image j and policy i, then F[i]=Σ_j L_{i,j}+λ_L2||θ_i||^2.
  - Eq. 8–9: index the image explicitly, e.g., v_{i,j}^{(T)} = M^{(T)}(θ_i, x_j); Li,j = −λ_boost(d_j) log v_{i,j,d_j}^{(T)}.
  - Eq. 11: summation index collides with i used for policy samples; also define x_j and d_j near the equations where they’re used.
  - Explain the scaled softmax for actions (σ scaled by |A|) and justify its stability implications.
  - Clarify whether “unaugmented” in the SOTA claim refers to test-time augmentation only. The text states that M was trained with data augmentation, which appears inconsistent with “unaugmented datasets.”
- Provide pseudo-code or references for SNES updates and specify all hyperparameters (population, learning rates for µ and Σ, initial Σ).

Originality:
- Modulating internal feature-map gains with a learned policy across recurrent passes is a novel, straightforward instantiation of internal selective attention in CNNs. Using SNES for million-dimensional policy search is unusual in vision. The work is distinct from glimpse-based models in that it performs global, channel-wise attention and from common RNN training in that it uses black-box evolution rather than gradient backpropagation through time.

Significance:
- The accuracy gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline are meaningful, especially on ambiguous cases, and the approach opens a promising direction for dynamic, test-time reconfiguration of CNNs via internal feedback. The idea is broadly applicable and likely to inspire follow-up on learned internal attention and recurrent control in deep networks.

Detailed suggestions and questions:
- Dynamical systems perspective: Frame the model as a deterministic RNN with multiplicative gates and analyze fixed points/stability (e.g., why performance peaks at t=T and then remains stable). Relate to recurrent batch normalization–like channel scalings.
- Deeper dynamics analysis: quantify per-layer gate distributions over time, correlations with class probabilities, and spatial effects (even though gates are global, show how activation maps change locally). Explain the consistent increase in early-layer activations on the first iteration and the role of global scaling.
- Hard cases: present a systematic analysis of images corrected by dasNet but misclassified by Maxout—what patterns of gate changes occur per confusion pair?
- Ablations: T sensitivity; per-layer vs all-layer gating; remove softmax scaling; stochastic vs deterministic policy; remove λ_boost; remove/alter λ_L2 on θ; smaller policy capacity. Report test-time cost (×T FLOPs, latency).
- Policy distribution constraints: experiment with clamping or narrowing Σ (or priors on µ) to study whether performance depends on high-variance exploration or large-magnitude gates.
- Regularization: justify the inclusion of λ_L2||θ||^2 inside the fitness in SNES; discuss its effect on the learned µ and Σ. Are updates done on µ, diag(Σ) only? Are posterior hyperparameters adjusted or fixed?
- Baselines: compare to gradient-based training of the same gating policy via backprop through time (treat gates as deterministic functions of observations) if feasible on a reduced policy, or to simpler heuristics (e.g., one extra linear pass conditioned on class probabilities).
- Reproducibility: specify random seeds, exact preprocessing, and release learned θ statistics (e.g., µ, diag(Σ)).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, simple mechanism for internal selective attention with solid empirical gains.
- Demonstrates the value of feedback/recurrent processing on top of strong CNNs.
- Scalable black-box optimization for high-dimensional policies.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited interpretability and theoretical grounding of the dynamics.
- Ambiguities and typos in algorithm and equations; RL framing may be overstated.
- Missing ablations, runtime analysis, and some baselines; possible inconsistency around “unaugmented” claim.

Overall: A compelling and effective recurrent-style approach that advances performance and opens a useful line of inquiry, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by clearer algorithmic specification and a deeper analysis of the learned dynamics and design choices.
