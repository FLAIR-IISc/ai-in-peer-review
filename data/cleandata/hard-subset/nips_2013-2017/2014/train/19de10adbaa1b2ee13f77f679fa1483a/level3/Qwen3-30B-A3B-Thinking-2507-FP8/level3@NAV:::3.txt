

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates attention-based top-down feedback into convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors propose a framework where a pre-trained CNN can iteratively refine its classification by dynamically modulating filter sensitivities through learned attention policies. This approach draws inspiration from biological vision systems, where top-down feedback plays a critical role in object recognition. The method extends Maxout networks with gated signals that allow the network to "rethink" its initial classification through multiple passes over the same input. The attention policy is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), which efficiently handles the high-dimensional parameter space required for controlling CNN filters.

The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showing a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs. The authors provide insightful visualizations that illustrate how the network modifies its internal representations across successive passes, particularly highlighting changes in filter activation patterns that correct misclassifications. The work is conceptually novel in combining reinforcement learning with CNNs for internal selective attention, addressing a gap in current feedforward architectures.

Strengths include the clear biological motivation, thorough experimental evaluation on challenging datasets, and compelling visual evidence of the attention mechanism's operation. The method is well-motivated by previous work on visual cortex architecture (Felleman & Essen, 1991) and recurrent processing (Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000).

Weaknesses include the lack of evaluation on larger-scale datasets (ILSVRC), which limits the demonstration of scalability. The paper does not provide failure cases where the attention policy fails to correct misclassifications, and the computational cost of the method (4 days on a single GPU) raises practical concerns compared to standard feedforward networks.

The work makes a significant contribution to understanding how attention mechanisms could be integrated into deep learning architectures. While the method is more complex than standard CNNs, the results on CIFAR datasets demonstrate its potential. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on larger datasets and analysis of failure cases to fully establish its practical utility. The novelty and technical soundness of the approach warrant acceptance, with the caveat that future work should address scalability concerns.
