This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a model that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to implement a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, rapid feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "internal thoughts" or passes over the same input. In these subsequent passes, a learned policy modulates the activations of the convolutional filters, effectively gating their influence on the final classification. This allows the network to dynamically emphasize or suppress certain features to improve its prediction, particularly for difficult or ambiguous cases.

The approach is structured as a two-stage learning process. First, a strong baseline CNN (a Maxout network) is trained conventionally using supervised learning. Then, its weights are frozen, and a policy for controlling the filter gates is trained using reinforcement learning (RL). The state for the RL agent is derived from the network's internal activations, and the action corresponds to the vector of gating values for the filters. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over 1 million), the authors cleverly opt to use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization algorithm, for direct policy search.

This work connects to a long line of research inspired by the human visual system, which features extensive feedback and recurrent connections alongside the primary feedforward pathway (e.g., Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). While attention and RL have been explored in vision before, this paper's contribution lies in successfully integrating these concepts into a state-of-the-art deep learning architecture and demonstrating a performance improvement on challenging benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance & Novelty:** The paper's primary strength is its successful demonstration that a learned, internal attention mechanism can improve the performance of a strong, pre-trained CNN. While none of the individual components (CNNs, RL, feedback) are entirely new, their combination into a single, effective system is a novel and important contribution. It provides compelling empirical evidence that moving beyond purely feedforward architectures towards models that incorporate iterative refinement and top-down modulation is a promising direction for computer vision.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors build upon a state-of-the-art model (Maxout networks) and show a clear and significant performance gain, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data at the time of publication. The choice of SNES to train the high-dimensional policy is well-justified and pragmatic. The experiments are well-conducted, including an analysis of the model's dynamics over multiple time steps and an interesting experiment confirming that the learned gate activations contain significant class-discriminative information.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The architecture and training algorithm are described with sufficient detail, and Figure 1 effectively illustrates the iterative process. The authors do a good job of explaining the biological motivation and positioning their work within the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability:** The most significant question left unanswered is the scalability of the approach. The RL training via SNES took four days on a GPU for the relatively small CIFAR datasets. Training a policy for a much deeper network on a larger dataset like ImageNet would be computationally challenging with this method, which is known for its sample complexity. A discussion of these limitations and potential paths to scaling (e.g., exploring whether policy gradient methods could be adapted) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost at Inference:** The model requires multiple forward passes (T=5) through the network at test time, which increases inference time by a factor of T. This trade-off between improved accuracy and higher computational cost is an important practical consideration that should be explicitly discussed. For many real-world applications, inference speed is critical.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** While the visualization in Figure 3 is illustrative, the analysis of what the attention mechanism has learned could be deeper. It would be highly valuable to see if there are consistent patterns of filter modulation across multiple examples of the same class or between confusing class pairs (e.g., "cat" vs. "dog"). This could provide more generalizable insights into the strategy learned by the policy.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and well-executed combination of ideas. It successfully demonstrates the value of incorporating feedback and learned attention into deep vision models, backing up its claims with state-of-the-art results. The main weakness is the potential difficulty in scaling the training method to larger problems. Despite this, the paper makes a significant contribution by opening up a promising new avenue for research. The authors should be encouraged to investigate the scalability of their approach on more challenging benchmarks like ImageNet, as this would further solidify the importance of their findings.
