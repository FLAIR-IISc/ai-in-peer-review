## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces dasNet, a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating attention-based top-down feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically adjust its filter sensitivities *after* initial supervised training, iteratively focusing on relevant features for challenging classification instances. This is a compelling approach, drawing inspiration from the recurrent processing observed in the biological visual system. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showcasing a clear improvement over previous models on unaugmented datasets.

The work is novel and well-motivated. The concept of adding a learned attentional mechanism *post-training* is distinct from existing attention mechanisms typically integrated directly into the forward pass. The use of SNES to navigate the large parameter space of the attention policy is a pragmatic choice, given the complexity of the problem. The visualizations provided—showing filter activation changes and class probability shifts—are particularly helpful in understanding the model’s internal workings and provide some intuition for how the feedback connections are influencing the classification process. The dynamics experiments, demonstrating stable performance beyond the training iterations, are also a strong point. 

However, the paper’s primary weakness lies in its limited scalability assessment. While the CIFAR results are promising, the authors acknowledge, and I concur, that the lack of results on the larger and more challenging ImageNet dataset is a significant concern. Establishing performance on ImageNet is crucial to demonstrate the practical applicability and scalability of dasNet. The added complexity of the feedback mechanism raises questions about computational cost, and this needs to be addressed with larger-scale experiments. 

Furthermore, while the authors mention a hypothesis about focusing on difficult cases, a more systematic investigation into failure cases, and why dasNet succeeds where a standard CNN might fail, would strengthen the analysis.  Exploring different attention policies and their impact on representation quality is also an area for future work.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture combining bottom-up and top-down signals.
*   State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR datasets.
*   Clear explanation and insightful visualizations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of scalability demonstrated through experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
*   Limited analysis of failure cases and attention policy impact.



**Overall:** This is a strong and interesting paper. While the lack of ImageNet results prevents a definitive endorsement, the novelty and promising results on CIFAR warrant further investigation. I recommend acceptance with the strong suggestion that future work prioritize scalability testing and a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention mechanisms.
