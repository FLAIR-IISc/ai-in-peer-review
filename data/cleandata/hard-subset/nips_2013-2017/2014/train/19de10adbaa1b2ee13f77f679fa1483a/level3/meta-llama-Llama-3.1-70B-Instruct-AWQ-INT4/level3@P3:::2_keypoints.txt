- Introduces dasNet, a novel CNN architecture using iterative selective attention via feedback connections to focus on discriminative features.  
- Augments a Maxout network with pass-dependent filter weighting to allow multiple attention-driven passes over the same image.  
- Paper is well-written with clear motivation, architectural description, and reinforcement learning training approach for the attentional policy.  
- Provides a thorough review of related work spanning human vision, computer vision, and reinforcement learning.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, including a 6% relative gain over a vanilla CNN on unaugmented data.  
- Shows dasNet can correct misclassifications made by a fully trained feedforward Maxout network.  
- Major concern: the learned attentional policy is a black box with little interpretability or explanation of why it works.  
- Analysis of image representations and learned theta parameters is inadequate and uninformative.  
- Some equations and notation are unclear (e.g., Equation 11 summation index, Equation 8 may need image index j).  
- Symbols x and d after Equation 11 are not defined and require clarification.  
- Questionable justification and necessity of theta regularization; more detail on final gradient updates is needed.  
- Unclear whether posterior hyperparameters are adjusted during training—authors should clarify gradient computation.  
- Originality is solid, but the model may be closer to recurrent/dynamical systems than pure reinforcement learning and this connection is underexplored.  
- Suggest analyzing internal dynamics (e.g., why layer-1 activations increase on first iteration, effects of global scaling, evolution of gating variables).  
- Recommend examining hard cases (images misclassified by Maxout but corrected by dasNet) to gain insight into policy behavior.  
- Propose testing effects of constraining learned theta statistics to better understand regularization impacts.  
- Overall: important advancement beyond fixed-parameter feedforward networks despite interpretability shortcomings; more insight into the attentional policy is needed.
