

The paper introduces dasNet, a novel deep convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections to implement internal selective attention through a reinforcement learning (RL)-trained policy. DasNet dynamically modulates filter sensitivities across multiple passes over an image, allowing the network to iteratively refine its classification decisions after an initial feedforward pass. This approach directly addresses the biological observation that human visual processing involves recurrent feedback (e.g., Felleman & Essen, 1991; Bar et al., 2003), contrasting with standard stationary CNNs. The core innovation lies in the combination of three elements: (1) feedback connections that gate convolutional filter activations, (2) a policy learned via Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to control these gates (avoiding the intractability of conventional RL for million-dimensional spaces), and (3) a boosting mechanism that focuses on misclassified examples. The authors validate dasNet on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving state-of-the-art results on un-augmented data (6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks).

**Quality.** The technical execution is strong. The SNES approach is well-motivated for the high-dimensional policy space, and the experimental design appropriately isolates the attention mechanism’s contribution (e.g., comparing to the baseline Maxout network trained only on the first pass). The use of λboost to prioritize misclassified examples is sensible and empirically validated. However, the paper lacks ablation studies on the necessity of feedback (e.g., comparing to a static gating policy) or deeper analysis of *how* gate values correlate with feature importance (beyond the qualitative activation visualizations in Figure 3). The training stability (Figure 2) is convincing, but the fixed T=5 steps for CIFAR could be questioned for scalability to more complex tasks. Overall, the results are well-supported, though the theoretical justification for the specific SNES implementation remains light.

**Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The architecture (Section 4), RL formulation (Section 3), and experimental setup (Section 6) are clearly articulated, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) and figures (especially Figure 3) effectively illustrate the iterative attention process. The explanation of how gates modulate filter activations (Equation 5) is precise, and the connection to biological visual processing (Section 5) is concise yet relevant. Minor improvements could include clarifying the exact dimensionality of the observation space in Section 3 and explicitly stating the computational cost of SNES relative to standard RL.

**Originality.** While individual components (feedback connections, RL for attention, SNES) have precedents (e.g., Bar et al. on top-down attention; Schmidhuber on RL for saccades; Glasmachers on SNES), the *integration* of these elements into a practical, SOTA-performing framework for standard image classification is novel. The paper explicitly differentiates itself from prior work by focusing on *post-training adaptation* of existing CNNs (not just attention as a training objective), and by demonstrating feasibility on challenging datasets (CIFAR-10/100) rather than toy problems. This is a significant synthesis, as previous RL-based attention methods (e.g., Schmidhuber, 2010) did not achieve comparable performance on standard benchmarks. The related work (Section 5) adequately contextualizes this contribution.

**Significance.** DasNet provides compelling empirical evidence that recurrent, attention-based refinement can improve performance in vision tasks without architectural overhaul. The 6% relative gain on CIFAR-100 (a difficult, high-dimensional dataset) is meaningful and demonstrates clear practical value. This work validates a biologically inspired direction (feedback loops for attention) as a viable path to SOTA performance, moving beyond purely feedforward models. However, the significance is currently limited to CIFAR; the authors rightly note that scaling to ImageNet (Section 7) is critical for broader impact. The paper does not claim to solve all vision challenges, but it establishes a promising methodology for incremental improvement in existing pipelines.

**Recommendation.** DasNet presents a technically sound, well-executed, and significant contribution to the vision community. The combination of feedback, SNES, and iterative attention is novel in its practical implementation and empirical success. The paper’s clarity and quality exceed typical standards. While the CIFAR results are compelling, the authors should explicitly address scalability to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) as a key limitation and future direction. This is not a weakness in the current work but a natural next step. The paper is well-positioned to influence future architectures that incorporate recurrent processing for attention.

**Pro:** SOTA results on CIFAR-10/100; elegant synthesis of feedback, SNES, and attention; clear technical execution; strong biological motivation.  
**Con:** Limited ablation studies; no ImageNet validation; theoretical grounding of the attention mechanism is superficial.  

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, addresses a meaningful problem (improving classification via internal attention), and provides a reproducible framework. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution. Accept.
