- Introduces dasNet: a CNN augmented with learned top-down feedback that modulates convolutional filters over multiple passes for internal selective attention.
- Two-stage training: supervise a base Maxout CNN, then learn a linear attention policy via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to correct misclassifications.
- Grounded in neuroscience: models a fast feedforward pass followed by slower recurrent attentional refinement.
- Empirical result: achieves state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.

Strengths:
- Novel, biologically plausible mechanism combining bottom-up features with top-down learned attention.
- Strong empirical validation showing tangible performance gains from the feedback mechanism.
- Thoughtful analysis and visualizations (e.g., filter modulation, temporal dynamics, class info in gates).

Weaknesses:
- Scalability concerns: high complexity, long RL training time, and quadratic policy-parameter growth may not scale to datasets like ImageNet.
- Policy robustness not analyzed: potential failure modes (over-emphasis on spurious features, instability) are not explored.
- Training pipeline complexity: two-stage supervised + high-dimensional black-box search is intricate and likely hard to tune.

Detailed questions/comments:
- Why choose SNES over gradient-based RL methods (e.g., policy gradients)? Were gradients attempted?
- State representation o_t discards spatial information—was this design choice validated against alternatives?
- Reward shaping is simple (λ_boost); were other reward formulations or shaping strategies tried?

Evaluation:
- Quality: high technical quality with well-supported claims on CIFAR.
- Clarity: clearly written and well-organized with helpful visualizations.
- Originality: highly original combination of CNN with RL-trained feedback attention.
- Significance: high—conceptually promising and likely influential despite practical concerns.

Summary:
- Strong, original paper showing that learned feedback enables a network to "re-think" and correct mistakes; recommend acceptance despite scalability and complexity caveats.
