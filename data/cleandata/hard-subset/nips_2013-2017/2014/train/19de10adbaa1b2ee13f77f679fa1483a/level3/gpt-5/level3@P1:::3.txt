Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal selective attention via top-down feedback. A base Maxout CNN is first trained in the standard supervised way; then a gate vector modulates each feature map’s activation before pooling on subsequent passes over the same image. A policy maps observations (layer-wise average activations, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities) to actions (per-map multiplicative gains), iterating for T steps. The policy is learned by Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to maximize a boosted log-likelihood objective that emphasizes misclassified examples. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, with visualizations illustrating how gates change internal activations and correct ambiguous cases (e.g., cat vs. dog). The approach builds on known ideas of recurrence and top-down modulation in vision neuroscience and prior machine learning work on attention and recurrent processing, but applies them to modern CNNs and demonstrates state-of-the-art results on unaugmented CIFAR benchmarks.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: A clear, implementable mechanism for iterative, internal attention through channel-wise gates controlled by a learned policy. Distinct from prior “glimpse”-style attention or purely feedforward Maxout ensembles.
- Empirical gains: Competitive or state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100 without committees, with an informative analysis of temporal dynamics (performance peaks around the trained number of steps and remains stable thereafter).
- Interpretability: Useful visualizations of how gates modulate feature maps across layers and time, plus an information-content study showing gate vectors alone carry discriminative signal.
- Biological plausibility and engineering practicality: Bridges bottom-up and top-down signals in a way consistent with literature on recurrent visual processing, while remaining compatible with standard CNN pipelines.
- Careful training objective: Boosting misclassified cases aligns the iterative attention with the intended purpose—disambiguating hard examples.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited scalability evidence: Results are restricted to CIFAR-10/100. Absence of ILSVRC/ImageNet-scale experiments makes it hard to assess practicality for large-vocabulary, high-resolution settings, where iterative passes and large policy networks may be more costly.
- Complexity and compute: The method adds T forward passes per example at test time and a million-parameter policy trained via SNES with population sampling. The paper would benefit from explicit reporting of test-time latency and training cost relative to the baseline.
- Choice of optimizer for policy: SNES is motivated by dimensionality, but a comparison to gradient-based alternatives (policy gradients, backprop-through-time on unrolled gates, or simpler linear policies) is missing. The current policy is essentially a linear-softmax mapping; showing that SNES is necessary and superior would strengthen the case.
- Clarity about “unaugmented” claims: The experiments state data augmentation was used to train M, yet the method is said to reach SOTA on “unaugmented data.” Please clarify the precise training and testing protocols used for comparisons.
- Failure modes: While a success case is visualized, the paper lacks systematic or qualitative failure cases (e.g., when iterative attention reinforces a wrong hypothesis, becomes unstable beyond T, or collapses to uniform gating).
- Ablations: It is unclear how much each design choice contributes (e.g., gating all layers vs. top layer only; including class probabilities in the observation; the specific boosting scheme; number of gates per layer).

Quality:
- The approach is technically sound and consistent with the literature cited; the algorithm is described with sufficient detail (observation vector, gating mechanism, objective). However, more ablations and comparisons (including to backprop-through-time training and simpler baselines like “last-layer gating only” or “average of T feedforward passes without feedback”) would better support the causal role of learned attention.
- Experimental analysis beyond accuracy (latency, compute, robustness under distribution shift, per-class improvements and confusion reduction) would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
- The writing is generally clear and well organized. Algorithm 1 is helpful. Please resolve the ambiguity around “unaugmented” vs. augmented training, and detail the policy architecture (exact dimensionalities, normalization of observations, and gating ranges). Reporting variance across runs and confidence intervals would improve clarity.

Originality:
- Combining channel-wise multiplicative feedback gates with a learned policy on modern CNNs and training that policy via SNES is novel relative to prior recurrent/attention work cited. The framing as internal selective attention over feature maps, with explicit top-down control, is an interesting addition to the design space.

Significance:
- If scalable, the approach could influence architectures that adapt representations at test time based on intermediate evidence. Even on CIFAR, the demonstrated gains and interpretability are promising. Evidence on larger datasets is needed to fully assess impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ImageNet (or at least a sizable subset) experiments to assess scalability, memory, and runtime.
- Provide ablations: gating only top conv layer; excluding class probabilities from observations; simpler policies trained by SGD; backprop-through-time training across unrolled T steps; effect of T on accuracy/cost.
- Report test-time compute/latency and accuracy vs. cost trade-offs.
- Include failure cases and per-class/confusion analyses to show where feedback helps/hurts.
- Analyze gate sparsity, layer-wise contributions, and sensitivity to observation normalization.
- Clarify augmentation/committee comparisons and ensure apples-to-apples baselines.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated architecture injecting learned feedback into CNNs.
- Solid gains on CIFAR with insightful visualizations and stability analysis.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired ideas with practical deep learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing large-scale validation and compute/latency analysis.
- Limited ablations and baseline comparisons for the policy learning choice.
- Ambiguity around “unaugmented” claims and limited discussion of failures.

Verdict:
Promising and forward-looking work that extends CNNs with internal selective attention via learned feedback. Despite concerns about scalability and missing ablations, the idea is interesting and the results on CIFAR are competitive. I recommend acceptance if the authors can clarify training protocols, add key ablations, and discuss compute/latency and failure modes; otherwise borderline accept.
