This paper proposes a novel approach to deep neural networks, introducing feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on certain features extracted from images. The authors achieve this by augmenting a standard Maxout network with special connections that modulate the activity of the convolutional filters. These connections implement a control policy learned through reinforcement learning.

The paper relates to previous work on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and their applications in object recognition, segmentation, and scene parsing. However, it differs from previous approaches by introducing a non-stationary CNN that can adapt its behavior post-training through feedback connections. This approach is inspired by the human visual cortex, which is known to have bidirectional connections between different areas.

The authors use a variant of Natural Evolution Strategies (NES) called Separable NES (SNES) to evolve the policy controlling the attention of the network. This approach is chosen due to the large number of parameters required to control the CNN, making standard RL methods impractical.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed approach, called Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), focuses on ambiguous classification cases in the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. The results show that dasNet outperforms the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets, achieving a relative improvement of 6%.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' ideas and understand the proposed approach. The use of figures and tables helps to illustrate the key concepts and results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide empirical support for the idea that feedback and attention are promising directions for vision models. The authors demonstrate that their approach can improve the performance of a state-of-the-art CNN on challenging datasets.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed analysis of the learned policies and how they affect the network's behavior. The authors mention that the policies are learned through reinforcement learning, but they do not provide a clear explanation of how the policies are represented and how they are updated during training.

Another potential weakness is that the paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed approach. The authors mention that training dasNet took around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train and evaluate the network.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and computer vision. The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are impressive. However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved, such as providing a more detailed analysis of the learned policies and discussing the computational cost of the approach.
