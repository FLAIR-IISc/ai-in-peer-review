Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a standard Maxout CNN with multiplicative gates on feature maps that are updated over multiple passes through the network via top-down feedback. A separate policy network observes a compact summary of current activations (average feature-map responses, intermediate classifier activations, and current class probabilities) and outputs gating actions that reweight convolutional feature maps before maxout pooling. The policy is trained post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a misclassification-weighted log-loss after T iterative passes. At test time, a fixed number of passes (T) is executed to refine predictions. On CIFAR-10/100 without test-time augmentation, dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and claims new state-of-the-art performance for unaugmented data. The paper includes visualizations of gate dynamics and a stability study when running beyond the training horizon.

The work is motivated by extensive neuroscience evidence on bidirectional and recurrent connectivity in visual cortex and the functional role of top-down modulation. It connects to prior machine learning work on attention and recurrence in vision (e.g., learned saccades and glimpses, recurrent processing for robust recognition) and to RL-based perceptual control. Methodologically, it builds on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013), dropout regularization, and natural evolution strategies (Wierstra et al.; Schaul et al. SNES) for high-dimensional black-box optimization. Compared to prior recurrent CNNs trained end-to-end, the novelty here is an explicit gating policy that operates on feature-map channels with an RL objective, rather than backpropagation through time.

Quality
- Soundness: The proposed mechanism is straightforward, differentiable, and implemented cleanly: actions are constrained positive via scaled softmax (average 1), which stabilizes activations. The use of a misclassification-boosted loss focuses the policy on hard cases. The choice of SNES is reasonable for million-parameter policies, though it is sample-inefficient.
- Empirics: On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), the method shows a reported ~6% relative improvement over the Maxout baseline, with qualitative visualizations, gate-information analysis (final gates alone carry class-relevant information), and a stability analysis showing performance peaks at step T and remains stable when unrolled further.
- Missing controls: Important ablations are absent: (i) per-layer vs per-map gating; (ii) policy complexity and observation design; (iii) comparison to end-to-end differentiable training via BPTT (the system is differentiable); (iv) comparisons to simpler iterative refinements (e.g., reweighting only logits) or test-time ensembles; (v) compute/latency analysis versus standard multi-crop or model ensembling; and (vi) scaling beyond CIFAR.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The training loop is described clearly with a compact algorithm. The observation vector composition and action constraints are specified. It would help to:
- Provide SNES update details in the main text or a brief summary of update rules and hyperparameters.
- Quantify compute cost: T passes per image, policy network size, training time per epoch, and test-time latency relative to baseline and ensembles.
- More systematically present the visualization methodology (e.g., consistent color scales and per-layer summaries).

Originality
Adding an RL-trained, high-dimensional gating policy that modulates internal CNN feature maps across iterative passes is novel compared to concurrent attention models and standard recurrent CNNs. Using SNES for such a large policy also stands out. The conceptual link to top-down, selective attention is timely and well-motivated.

Significance
If robust, the approach could influence how we design recurrent, attention-enabled CNNs for classification, especially as a mechanism for post-training refinement focused on hard examples. However, the experimental scope is limited to CIFAR-10/100, making it difficult to assess scalability and practical impact. Demonstrating benefits on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet or Tiny-ImageNet) or on higher-resolution tasks would strengthen significance.

Specific strengths
- Clear and biologically inspired formulation of selective, internal attention via multiplicative gating.
- Demonstrated, reproducible improvement over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR without test-time augmentation.
- Insightful visualizations and a stability experiment beyond training horizon.
- Sensible action constraints that keep the dynamics stable.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited scope: Evaluate on a larger dataset (e.g., ImageNet subset or Tiny-ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability and practical relevance. Include compute/latency trade-offs.
- Missing baselines: Compare to (a) deeper/wider Maxout, (b) test-time ensembling/multi-crop/MC-dropout, (c) iterative refinement by reweighting logits only, and (d) standard recurrent CNNs trained by BPTT.
- End-to-end training: Since the policy and gating are differentiable, compare SNES to gradient-based training (e.g., BPTT with unrolled steps; REINFORCE or deterministic policy gradients), and report sample efficiency and final accuracy.
- Ablations: Assess (i) per-layer vs per-map gating; (ii) observation features (add spatial stats or attention over layers); (iii) policy capacity; (iv) T sensitivity and effect of misclassification boosting weights; (v) regularization of actions (e.g., entropy/KL to keep gates near 1).
- Failure analysis: Provide systematic error analysis—when does iterative attention hurt/help? Report confusion matrices, per-class gains, flip-flop cases, and calibration changes.
- Reproducibility: Include random-seed variability and error bars; specify all hyperparameters and policy architecture details.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated integration of top-down attention with strong empirical gains on standard benchmarks.
- Simple mechanism, broadly applicable to existing CNNs.
- Solid qualitative analyses and promising dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Scope limited to CIFAR; no evidence of scalability to more realistic, higher-resolution datasets.
- Missing comparisons to strong, practically relevant baselines and end-to-end recurrent training.
- Computational trade-offs and sample efficiency not quantified.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is interesting and timely, the method is technically sound, and the empirical gains plus analyses justify publication, but broader experiments and ablations would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Reviewer confidence: High on deep learning and attention/recurrent models; moderate on NES variants.
