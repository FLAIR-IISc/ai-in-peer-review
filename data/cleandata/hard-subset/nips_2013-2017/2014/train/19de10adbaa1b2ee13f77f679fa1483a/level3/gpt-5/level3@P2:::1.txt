Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture augmented with internal feedback connections that multiplicatively gate the activations of convolutional feature maps over multiple passes on the same image. A policy network receives observations summarizing internal activations and class probabilities, and outputs a vector of per-map gates; iterating this process implements internal selective attention that can emphasize or de-emphasize filters to disambiguate hard examples. The base model is a Maxout CNN with dropout; the attention policy is learned after standard supervised training via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for scalability to a million-parameter policy. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report state-of-the-art performance on unaugmented data and qualitative analyses of gating dynamics and stability across timesteps.

The approach sits at the intersection of: (i) CNNs with maxout/nonlinear pooling and dropout (Goodfellow et al.; Zeiler & Fergus; Wan et al.; Lin et al.), (ii) recurrent/feedback models and top-down modulation inspired by visual cortex (Felleman & Van Essen; Lamme & Roelfsema; Wyatte et al.), and (iii) attention and RL for vision (Denil et al.; Larochelle & Hinton; Fukushima; O’Reilly). While none of the components alone is novel, the combination—feedback-based internal attention learned via evolutionary policy search on top of a strong CNN—is timely and effective.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that operationalizes top-down, modulatory feedback in a modern CNN.
- Simple, differentiable gating mechanism compatible with existing convnets and able to improve predictions on difficult cases.
- Pragmatic training via SNES to handle very high-dimensional policy parameters without hand-crafted credit assignment; sensible use of boosting to focus on misclassified examples.
- Empirical gains: state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented); dynamics appear stable beyond training horizon; informative qualitative visualizations.
- Paper is clearly written, with sufficient algorithmic detail and reasonable hyperparameter transparency.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope: results only on CIFAR-10/100; no experiments on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or higher-resolution regimes where attention may be more impactful.
- Incomplete baselines/ablations:
  - No comparison to gradient-based training of the feedback/policy via backpropagation through time, or to simpler policies (e.g., linear gates from logits/penultimate layer).
  - No control for increased computation: compare to additional depth, test-time ensembling, or multi-pass averaging without feedback at equal FLOPs/latency and parameter count.
  - Limited analysis of observation design; averaging map activations may discard spatial structure—how sensitive are results to observation features?
- RL choice not fully justified: SNES is sample-efficient in high-dimensions, but the policy and network are differentiable; an end-to-end recurrent CNN trained with BPTT (or REINFORCE on non-differentiable components) could be a competitive or stronger baseline.
- Gains are described as SOTA on unaugmented data; many relevant contemporaneous results use augmentation, so the absolute competitiveness in a standard protocol remains unclear.
- Computational cost and latency: multiple passes (T=5) at test time; no runtime or energy comparison.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a coherent learning objective and a stable gating parameterization (positive gates normalized to mean 1). The fitness shaping and L2 regularization are reasonable.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines (as above) limit causal attribution of gains to feedback vs. extra compute/parameters.
- Qualitative analyses are helpful; the quantitative “gate-only” classification probe is interesting, though more rigorous probing (e.g., mutual information, per-layer ablations) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- Well organized and readable. Algorithmic description is clear; the observation vector construction and policy mapping are explicit.
- Some details deferred (e.g., SNES update rules), which is acceptable if included in the supplement. Please add runtime, parameter counts for policy vs. base net, and per-class improvements.

Originality
- The components (CNNs, attention/gating, feedback, NES) are known, but their integration to produce SOTA on standard benchmarks is novel and relevant to NIPS. The framing of internal, modulatory attention over multiple passes is a useful contribution distinct from spatial glimpse models.

Significance
- Moderate to high. The results provide empirical support that recurrent, top-down modulation can improve CNN classification, and the method is easy to graft onto existing architectures. Broader impact will depend on scalability and comparisons under standard augmented training pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on a larger dataset (e.g., ImageNet) and/or higher-resolution CIFAR variants; report compute/latency trade-offs per T.
- Add ablations: (i) linear vs. nonlinear policies; (ii) policy trained by BPTT vs. SNES; (iii) random/static gates; (iv) equal-compute baselines (deeper net, test-time augmentation, ensembles).
- Report parameter counts and wall-clock costs for base vs. dasNet; analyze sensitivity to T and early stopping based on confidence.
- Explore richer observations (e.g., spatial summaries) and spatially selective actions; clarify robustness with data augmentation.
- Provide per-class confusion improvements and calibration metrics to illustrate where feedback helps most.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually compelling and biologically motivated integration of feedback into CNNs.
- Demonstrated performance gains on standard benchmarks with clear methodology.
- General, modular technique that may spur further work on learned feedback in deep vision models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation scope and missing strong baselines make it hard to isolate the source of improvement.
- Unclear scalability and efficiency on larger datasets; reliance on evolutionary search may be unnecessary.

Overall assessment
- Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper is well executed and delivers interesting, positive results with a clear idea that is likely to influence subsequent work on feedback and attention in CNNs. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and scaling to larger datasets would make this a clear accept.

Reviewer confidence
- High on deep learning and attention; medium on evolutionary optimization specifics.
