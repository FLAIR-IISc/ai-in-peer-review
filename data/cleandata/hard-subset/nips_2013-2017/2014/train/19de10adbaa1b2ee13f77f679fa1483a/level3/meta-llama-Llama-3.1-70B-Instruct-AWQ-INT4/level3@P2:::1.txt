This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that combines traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to implement visual attention. The authors propose a reinforcement learning approach to learn the attention policy on top of a pre-trained CNN. The key idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on certain features extracted from images, thereby improving classification performance.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the limitations of traditional CNNs and the benefits of incorporating feedback connections. The proposed architecture is well-described, and the use of reinforcement learning to learn the attention policy is a natural choice. The experimental evaluation on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, achieving state-of-the-art performance on unaugmented datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and presentation. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the proposed architecture, including the underlying CNN model, the attention mechanism, and the reinforcement learning approach. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, and the results are clearly presented.

The novelty of the paper lies in the combination of traditional CNNs with feedback connections and reinforcement learning. While individual components of the approach may not be highly novel, the combined approach is well-chosen and effectively executed. The authors provide empirical support for the idea that feedback and attention are promising directions for vision models.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a new perspective on how to improve the performance of CNNs. The use of feedback connections and reinforcement learning to learn attention policies has the potential to be applied to a wide range of vision tasks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, but it would be interesting to see how the approach scales to larger problems and datasets, such as ImageNet.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is well-motivated and effectively executed. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, and the paper provides a new perspective on how to improve the performance of CNNs.
