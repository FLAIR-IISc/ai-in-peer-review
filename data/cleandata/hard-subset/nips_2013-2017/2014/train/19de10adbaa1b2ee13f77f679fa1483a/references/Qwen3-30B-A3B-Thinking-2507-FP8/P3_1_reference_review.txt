

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing deep CNNs by incorporating feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively focus attention on different features through multiple passes over the same image. The core idea is that after a standard feedforward classification pass, the network can use reinforcement learning to learn a policy that modulates filter activations on subsequent passes, specifically targeting ambiguous classification cases. The authors use a Maxout network as the base architecture and employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to evolve the attention policy due to the high dimensionality of the parameter space (over 1 million parameters).

The paper makes a connection to human visual processing, noting that humans often require multiple "looks" to distinguish between similar visual stimuli, and argues that CNNs could benefit from a similar mechanism. The authors evaluate dasNet on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, reporting a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on ambiguous cases.

This work builds on several strands of research: the growing interest in attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., [40-45]), the use of recurrent processing for visual tasks (e.g., [48-50]), and the application of reinforcement learning to computer vision. However, it differs from prior work by focusing on *internal* selective attention within a pre-trained network rather than learning new attention mechanisms from scratch or applying attention to external tasks like saccades.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The paper presents a compelling idea that aligns with biological evidence about human visual processing, where multiple feedforward passes with attentional modulation are used for difficult classifications. This is a fresh perspective on how to improve CNNs without fundamentally altering their architecture.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors correctly identify that standard CNNs are static once trained, while humans can "think" about a problem multiple times to improve classification. The paper clearly articulates the two requirements for their approach: a non-stationary CNN and a mechanism to decide how to adapt.

3. **Good Experimental Design**: The evaluation focuses on the most interesting cases (ambiguous classifications) rather than overall accuracy, which aligns well with the paper's hypothesis. The visualization of attention changes across passes (Figure 3) is particularly insightful.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a concrete implementation that works within the constraints of existing CNN architectures, using Maxout networks as the base and adding only the attention gates.

5. **Thoughtful Analysis of Dynamics**: The analysis of dasNet's behavior across different numbers of passes (Figure 2) demonstrates that the network can maintain stable performance even when running for more steps than it was trained on.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological Concerns with Reinforcement Learning Approach**: The authors justify using SNES instead of gradient-based RL methods "due to the large number of parameters," but this seems like a weak argument. The policy is a simple neural network mapping observations to actions, and gradient-based methods should be applicable. The paper lacks a comparison to even simple gradient-based approaches (e.g., policy gradients with a small learning rate), which would have provided valuable insight into whether the RL approach is necessary.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides no theoretical analysis of why this approach should work or what guarantees it might provide. For instance, how many passes are theoretically needed for convergence? What is the relationship between the number of passes and the complexity of the ambiguous cases?

3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims to establish a "new state-of-the-art" for CIFAR-10/100, but the comparison is only to a "vanilla CNN" (presumably a Maxout network without attention). The paper does not compare to recent SOTA models like EfficientNet, ResNet variants, or transformer-based approaches that have achieved much higher accuracy on these datasets (e.g., 94%+ on CIFAR-10 with modern architectures). The 6% relative improvement seems modest when compared to the gap between standard CNNs and SOTA.

4. **Insufficient Experimental Details**: The paper lacks crucial details about hyperparameters (e.g., the exact architecture of the policy network, the specific values used for λcorrect and λmisclassified beyond what's mentioned in the text), and the computational cost of the approach (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti for dasNet training) is substantial compared to the small accuracy gain.

5. **Mischaracterization of Related Work**: The paper claims that "this research is often applied to simplified datasets for demonstration purposes due to computation constraints, and are not aimed at improving the state-of-the-art." However, several recent papers (e.g., [48-50]) have shown significant improvements on standard benchmarks using iterative processing, and some have even been integrated into SOTA systems.

6. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to determine the contribution of different components (e.g., the effect of using SNES vs. other RL methods, the importance of different observation features used to construct ot).

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound in its implementation of dasNet, and the experimental results support the main claims. However, the methodology has significant limitations, particularly the choice of SNES over gradient-based methods. The evaluation is somewhat limited in scope (only CIFAR-10/100, only ambiguous cases), and the paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the approach. The authors are careful to note that dasNet is "not aimed at improving the state-of-the-art" but then claims to establish a new SOTA, which is contradictory.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and good visualizations. However, some sections (particularly the RL section) could be clearer about why the specific approach was chosen. The supplementary material is mentioned but not provided, so it's unclear if it contains important details.

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of using feedback connections for internal selective attention is novel within the context of CNNs. However, the approach is somewhat incremental compared to existing work on attention mechanisms. The paper would have been stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks [51], CBAM [52], or transformer-based approaches [53]).

## Significance Assessment

The significance of the work is somewhat limited. While the idea is interesting, the improvement (6% relative) is modest compared to recent SOTA improvements. The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti for dasNet training) is substantial for a relatively small gain, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach scales to larger datasets or more complex tasks. The paper would have been more significant if it had shown applications to more challenging benchmarks or demonstrated a more substantial improvement.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

### Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper presents a novel approach to internal attention in deep networks that aligns with biological evidence.
- The implementation is well-executed and the results are consistent with the hypothesis.
- The visualization of attention changes across passes is insightful and provides concrete evidence of the mechanism.
- The paper addresses an important limitation of standard CNNs (static behavior after training).

### Arguments Against Acceptance
- The use of SNES instead of gradient-based RL methods is not well-justified and seems like a missed opportunity to use more efficient methods.
- The claimed "state-of-the-art" improvement is modest and not compared to recent SOTA models.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison to other attention mechanisms that have been shown to be effective in similar tasks.
- The computational cost of the approach is high relative to the accuracy gain.
- The theoretical justification for the approach is weak.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. The core idea of internal selective attention is interesting and has potential, but the execution has significant limitations that prevent it from making a substantial contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to gradient-based methods for the attention policy, a comparison to recent SOTA models on CIFAR-10/100, and a more thorough analysis of the computational cost versus accuracy gain.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Compare to Gradient-Based Methods**: Run experiments using policy gradients or other gradient-based RL methods to determine if the SNES approach is truly necessary or if it's just a computational limitation.

2. **Expand Experimental Comparison**: Compare dasNet to recent SOTA models on CIFAR-10/100 (e.g., EfficientNet, ResNet, or transformer-based approaches) to provide context for the claimed improvement.

3. **Provide More Implementation Details**: Include the exact architecture of the policy network, the specific hyperparameters used, and the computational resources required for training dasNet.

4. **Include Ablation Studies**: Show the contribution of different components (e.g., the effect of using different observation features, the importance of multiple passes).

5. **Reframe the Contribution**: The paper should clarify that the improvement is relative to the base Maxout network, not to the current state-of-the-art, to avoid misleading readers.

6. **Better Contextualize Related Work**: Provide a more thorough comparison to existing attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM, transformers) to clarify the novelty of the approach.

## Final Thoughts

This paper tackles an interesting problem with a novel approach that has conceptual merit. However, the execution leaves several important questions unanswered, and the claimed significance is not fully supported by the results. With the suggested improvements, this could be a strong paper, but as it stands, it doesn't make a sufficiently significant contribution to the field to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.
