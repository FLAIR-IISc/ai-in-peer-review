Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, selective attention via multiplicative gates that modulate the activation strength of each feature map across layers over multiple passes on the same image. A policy network maps observations (per-map average activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to actions (per-map gains), and is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) after the base Maxout CNN is trained with supervised learning. The system performs T recurrent passes per image; only the final prediction is scored with a boosted log-likelihood that upweights misclassified instances. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show improvements over the base Maxout network, with qualitative visualizations suggesting the policy learns to up-/down-weight specific filters, and a simple stability analysis of running more steps than trained.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned at the intersection of CNNs, attention/feedback, and RL. It connects to biological evidence for top-down modulation and recurrent processing, and to machine learning work on reinforcement-learning-based attention (e.g., saccades/glimpses), recurrent/iterative inference, and gating/competition in deep nets. The key novelty is applying a learned, internal per-channel attention policy across multiple CNN layers to a modern CNN backbone and demonstrating gains on CIFAR. Compared to prior “glimpse” models and recurrent reconstruction/localization pipelines, the contribution is an in-network channel-wise feedback policy operating on a full-frame classifier.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: multiplicative, per-map gains applied at all layers, constrained to be positive and mean-1 for stability. Easy to integrate with existing CNNs.
- Sequential processing: a practical, lightweight recurrent loop (T small) demonstrating that iterative internal reweighting can correct initial mistakes on ambiguous inputs.
- Empirical improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout+dropout baseline; claimed SOTA for unaugmented test-time evaluation.
- Diagnostics: qualitative visualizations and a simple analysis of dynamics (performance peaking near trained T and stable thereafter).
- Bridging communities: connects CNNs with learned internal attention and evolutionary black-box optimization for high-dimensional policies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient to isolate what drives the gains:
  - No comparison to end-to-end differentiable training via BPTT (the entire loop is deterministic and differentiable). The argument for SNES (“too many parameters”) is unconvincing given that modern CNNs routinely train millions of parameters with backprop; a supervised loss at the final pass could be optimized with standard SGD and would be considerably more sample efficient than NES.
  - Missing ablations on the observation vector: how much do per-map means vs. logits vs. probabilities contribute? Does removing any component hurt?
  - No comparison to simpler post-hoc calibrators that do not alter internal activations (e.g., an MLP on logits/probabilities over T passes, or simply retraining the last layer).
  - No comparison to static learned per-map gains (per-layer channel scaling learned and fixed at test time), or to a single-pass policy (T=1) versus multi-pass.
- Quantitative reporting is incomplete:
  - Table with precise CIFAR-10/100 error rates, standard deviations over multiple runs, and comparisons to contemporary single-model state-of-the-art is not included in the text. The “6% relative improvement” is ambiguous without absolute numbers and variance.
  - “Unaugmented data” claim is unclear: the base Maxout is trained with augmentation; is test-time augmentation used or not? Please specify evaluation protocol and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Choice of SNES:
  - Black-box evolution for ~1M parameters is typically very sample-inefficient; the paper lacks evidence that gradient-based training fails. Without such comparisons, it is hard to argue RL/evolution is necessary here.
  - Risk of overfitting: the SNES objective boosts misclassified training instances, but there is no explicit validation-based early stopping during SNES optimization described. How is overfitting monitored?
- Architectural characterization:
  - The “feedback connections” are external multiplicative gates, not learned synapses within the CNN; the phrasing could be toned down to avoid implying dense, trainable top-down synapses within the CNN.
  - The action is global channel-wise and spatially uniform. This limits the form of “attention” (no spatial selectivity). Some discussion comparing global channel attention versus spatial attention would help.
- Analysis depth:
  - The “gates contain information” claim (KNN/logistic regression on gate vectors) is interesting but not decisive—accuracy around ~40–46% on CIFAR-100 is far from SOTA and the setup is unclear (train/test split? normalization?).
  - No confusion-matrix analysis or per-class breakdown to substantiate that corrections concentrate on known confusions (e.g., cat vs. dog).

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the mechanism and training loop are clearly described, but important experimental details and baselines are missing. The choice of SNES is not justified empirically against more standard, differentiable alternatives. Claims of SOTA need precise, reproducible numbers.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Notation has a few inconsistencies, and some implementation details are omitted: the exact dimensionalities of O and A, value of n (batch size for SNES), SNES optimizer hyperparameters, and how the per-layer gates are concatenated and applied. Figures referenced in text (e.g., Table 1, Fig. 2/3) are essential for evaluation.

Originality
Moderately novel combination: internal channel-wise attention driven by a learned multi-pass policy applied to a modern CNN and trained with SNES. The individual components (gating, feedback, iterative inference, RL) are known, but integrating them in this way and demonstrating gains on CIFAR is a worthwhile idea.

Significance
Potentially impactful if the gains are robust and attributable to the proposed mechanism. As presented, the contribution is promising but limited by missing comparisons to stronger baselines/end-to-end training and incomplete reporting. The idea could inspire subsequent work on internal attention policies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full quantitative tables with absolute errors, standard deviations over multiple seeds, and precise evaluation protocols.
- Add ablations: remove each observation component; T=1 vs T>1; per-layer gating on/off; static learned gains; post-hoc calibrator that only reweights logits.
- Compare SNES to gradient-based end-to-end training via BPTT (same architecture and loss). If SNES still wins, analyze why (optimization landscape, stability).
- Use a validation set to select T and to monitor SNES overfitting; report learning curves.
- Include per-class confusion matrices and examples of corrected vs. introduced errors.
- Clarify architectural details (dimensions of O/A; mapping gates to layers; SNES hyperparameters).

Reasons to accept
- Simple, elegant mechanism for internal, sequential attention within CNNs.
- Empirical improvements on standard benchmarks with insightful qualitative analysis.
- Bridges CNNs and sequential decision-making; could catalyze further research.

Reasons to reject
- Missing critical baselines (especially differentiable end-to-end training) and ablations.
- Incomplete quantitative reporting; SOTA claim not verifiable from text alone.
- Questionable choice of SNES without empirical justification; potential sample inefficiency and overfitting concerns.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially valuable, but the experimental evidence and comparisons are not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested would likely tip this to acceptance.
