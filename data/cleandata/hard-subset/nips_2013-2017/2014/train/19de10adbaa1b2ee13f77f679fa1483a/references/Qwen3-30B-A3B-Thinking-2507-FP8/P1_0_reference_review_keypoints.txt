- Proposes dasNet: adds internal selective attention via feedback connections to CNNs, using RL to modulate filter weights over multiple passes without retraining CNN weights.  
- Builds on Maxout networks and uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn attention policies.  
- Claims biological inspiration from human visual processing and recurrent attention literature, but applies directly to state-of-the-art CNNs on standard benchmarks.  
- Demonstrates a reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 (unaugmented), claiming new state-of-the-art for that setting.  
- Provides qualitative analysis of filter activations showing how attention changes across iterations.  
- Experiments are well-structured and include dynamics analysis showing stability when running beyond trained steps.  
- Algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is detailed and clear, facilitating reproducibility.  
- Novel use of RL (SNES) to handle large attention-policy parameter spaces is a practical contribution.  
- Strong motivation linking the method to human visual processing and difficult classification cases.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis explaining why the approach works or how it maps to biological attention.  
- Training is computationally expensive (≈4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) with no thorough analysis of scaling or comparisons of cost.  
- No statistical significance testing reported for the 6% improvement, raising concerns about robustness.  
- Limited empirical comparison to other attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation, CBAM).  
- Insufficient detail on the reward function design and how weighting misclassified samples affects policy learning.  
- Some technical details are unclear: relation between observation and action spaces, measurement of gate information content, and SNES variant implementation.  
- Overall paper is technically sound, complete, and well-written, though theoretical grounding and statistical validation are weak.  
- Original contribution: novel combination of RL-controlled internal attention with standard CNNs, and practical use of SNES for large policy spaces.  
- Significance: potentially meaningful improvement on CIFAR-10 and generalizable approach to other vision tasks if validated.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant empirical improvement, novel and general approach, strong experimental design and qualitative insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing significance tests, high computational cost, limited comparisons to other attention methods, weak theoretical justification.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address statistical validation, comparisons, and clarified details.  
- Suggested revisions: add statistical significance testing, compare to other attention mechanisms, expand theoretical justification, discuss limitations/failure cases, clarify observation/action spaces and SNES details, analyze computational cost vs. alternatives, and deepen analysis of gate information content.
