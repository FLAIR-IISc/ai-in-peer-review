

# Review for "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that enables deep CNNs to iteratively refine their internal representations through selective attention mechanisms implemented via feedback connections. The key insight is that, similar to how humans may need multiple "passes" to correctly identify visually similar objects, CNNs can benefit from a mechanism to adaptively emphasize or suppress internal features across multiple passes over the same image. The authors propose learning an attention policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to modulate filter activations in a pre-trained Maxout network, with the goal of correcting misclassifications on difficult examples.

**Originality:** This paper presents a highly novel conceptual framework that challenges the standard feedforward paradigm of CNNs. While attention mechanisms have been explored in computer vision (e.g., in transformers or spatial attention modules), dasNet's approach is fundamentally different in that it enables *internal* selective attention through feedback connections within a pre-trained network, rather than adding new attention modules or modifying the architecture during training. The paper effectively connects this approach to biological visual processing, where top-down feedback plays a crucial role in attentional processing. This is a bold conceptual shift that significantly differs from previous work on attention mechanisms in deep learning. The use of SNES to handle the large number of parameters in the attention policy is also a clever, albeit pragmatic, solution to a challenging optimization problem.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. More importantly, it successfully addresses a fundamental limitation of standard CNNs: their inability to adaptively refine internal representations after training. The experiments show that dasNet can correct misclassifications by selectively emphasizing or suppressing specific filters across multiple passes, with the most significant changes occurring in higher convolutional layers closest to the classification output. This represents a meaningful advance in the field, as it demonstrates how to make existing state-of-the-art networks more robust without retraining from scratch.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and training procedure. The experimental design is appropriate for the research question, and the results are convincing. The paper demonstrates the dynamics of the attention mechanism through visualizations of how filter activations change across passes, which is particularly compelling for understanding the model's behavior. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed comparison to other attention mechanisms (e.g., those in transformer architectures) and a deeper analysis of why certain filters are emphasized or suppressed. The choice of SNES over other RL methods is justified, but the paper could better explain why standard RL methods would be infeasible for this problem.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from motivation to method to results. The algorithm description is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the core concept. The connection to biological visual processing is well-articulated, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the approach.

**Strengths:** The paper's bold conceptual framework is its greatest strength. It successfully shifts the paradigm from static feedforward networks to systems capable of internal iterative refinement. The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is significant for a challenging dataset, and the attention mechanism is clearly demonstrated through visualizations of filter activations. The connection to biological vision adds depth to the work.

**Weaknesses:** The paper could provide more thorough comparison to existing attention mechanisms, particularly those that use feedback (e.g., Recurrent Neural Networks for attention). The computational cost of multiple passes (T=5) is not thoroughly discussed in terms of practical deployment considerations. The paper also lacks analysis of how the approach scales to larger networks or more complex datasets beyond CIFAR.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. While the paper has some methodological limitations (particularly in comparison to existing attention mechanisms), its conceptual novelty is substantial and represents a significant shift in how we might think about CNNs. The 6% improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is meaningful for this dataset, and the approach opens up a new direction for research on adaptive neural networks. The paper is not perfect, but it's the kind of bold, novel work that NIPS should prioritize.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a more detailed comparison to existing attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers, spatial attention modules)
2. Discuss the computational trade-offs of using multiple passes in more depth
3. Provide a brief analysis of why certain filters are emphasized/suppressed (beyond the visualizations)
4. Consider testing the approach on a more challenging dataset (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability

This paper presents a compelling new direction in neural network architecture design that challenges the conventional feedforward paradigm. Its conceptual novelty and significant performance gains make it an excellent fit for NIPS, and I believe it will inspire important follow-up work in the field.
