

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which introduces feedback connections to CNNs that allow the network to iteratively adjust filter weights across multiple passes over the same image, with the attention policy learned via reinforcement learning. The authors claim this approach achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/100, establishing a new state-of-the-art.

## Strengths

The paper presents a technically detailed approach with a clear algorithmic formulation. The use of Maxout networks as the base architecture is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the dasNet architecture and the reinforcement learning component. The experiments are conducted on standard benchmarks, and the authors include visualizations of attention dynamics, which are valuable for understanding the model's behavior. The implementation of SNES for policy evolution to handle the large parameter space is a reasonable technical choice.

## Weaknesses

**Quality concerns:** The paper's claims of "state-of-the-art" performance are problematic. The 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs appears modest, especially when compared to recent SOTA methods on CIFAR-10/100 (e.g., recent ResNet variants achieve >95% accuracy on CIFAR-10, while the authors' baseline appears to be significantly lower). Crucially, the paper fails to compare against contemporary SOTA methods, making the "new state-of-the-art" claim unsubstantiated. The paper also lacks essential ablation studies to determine which components of dasNet contribute to the performance gain. For instance, it's unclear whether the improvement stems from the attention mechanism, the specific RL approach, or simply the multiple passes through the network.

**Methodological concerns:** The paper makes an unsubstantiated claim that "this approach is now feasible" for improving state-of-the-art networks, but provides no evidence that dasNet would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet. The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU for dasNet training, plus T times longer inference time) is substantial and not adequately justified for the modest gains achieved. The paper also fails to address whether the attention mechanism could simply be replaced by a larger or deeper standard CNN, which would likely yield better results with less complexity.

**Originality concerns:** While the specific combination of Maxout networks with RL for selective attention is novel, the paper fails to clearly distinguish this from existing work. The related work section cites numerous papers on feedback connections in visual cortex and recurrent processing, but doesn't adequately explain how dasNet differs from previous computational approaches that have used similar ideas (e.g., the Neural Abstraction Pyramid, recurrent processing in object recognition, or attention mechanisms in transformers). The paper also doesn't sufficiently address why RL is necessary for this task when simpler mechanisms (like attention gates) might suffice.

**Significance concerns:** The claimed 6% relative improvement is modest for a paper claiming to establish a new state-of-the-art. The paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that dasNet would be useful in real-world applications, given the substantial computational overhead. The paper also lacks discussion of how this approach might generalize to other vision tasks beyond classification, or whether it could be applied to more challenging datasets like ImageNet.

## Conclusion

While the paper presents an interesting technical approach to incorporating selective attention into CNNs, the claims of novelty and significance are overstated. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the "new state-of-the-art" claim, as the paper fails to compare against contemporary methods and lacks necessary ablation studies. The computational cost of the approach appears to outweigh the modest performance gains.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Comparing against recent SOTA methods on CIFAR-10/100
2. Conducting ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components
3. Providing a more thorough discussion of how dasNet differs from existing approaches
4. Discussing the practical applicability of the method, including computational costs

Given the current evidence, the paper does not demonstrate sufficient methodological soundness or significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The approach appears to be an interesting but incremental extension of existing ideas that lacks the rigorous validation and clear demonstration of significant improvement required for NIPS.
