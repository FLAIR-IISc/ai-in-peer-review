## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES) to selectively modulate filter activations. The core idea is to allow the network to refine its classifications through iterative processing, mimicking aspects of human visual attention. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging instances, compared to a baseline Maxout network.

While the concept of incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs is not novel (see [40, 41, 44, 45] cited in the paper, and a wealth of more recent work), the approach of learning attentional weights *post-training* through reinforcement learning, and specifically applying it at the level of individual filters within layers, is a distinct contribution. The authors correctly identify a limitation of standard CNNs – their static nature after training – and attempt to address it. 

However, despite the potential of the idea, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws that ultimately diminish its impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (limited):** The combination of post-training reinforcement learning to modulate individual filter activations within a deep network is a relatively unexplored area.
* **Potential Impact:**  The idea of adapting CNN behaviour post-training to address specific failure cases is appealing and could have practical benefits.
* **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements on CIFAR-10/100, even if modest, demonstrate the potential of the approach.
* **Clear Motivation:** The authors provide a relatable analogy to human visual processing and a logical motivation for the work.

**Weaknesses (substantial):**

1. **Methodological Inconsistencies & Lack of Detail:** The implementation details are surprisingly sparse.  The description of how the observation vector *o<sub>t</sub>* is constructed (Algorithm 1, line 9) is vague.  "Extracting values from M by *h(·)*" is insufficiently precise. What specific information beyond average activations and class probabilities is included? The dimensionality of *o<sub>t</sub>* is not stated, hindering reproducibility.  The choice of T=5 is justified only by computational tractability, lacking an analytical or experimental basis.
2. **SNES Justification:** While SNES is presented as a necessity due to the large number of parameters, the rationale for choosing SNES *over* other scalable RL methods (e.g., distributed actor-critic algorithms, parameter sharing) is weak. The authors acknowledge other RL methods but dismiss them too quickly without a proper comparative analysis.
3. **Reward Function:** The reward function (Equation 8) is simplistic.  The large weight given to misclassified samples (λ<sub>misclassified</sub> > λ<sub>correct</sub>) could lead to overfitting to the training set and unstable learning. A more nuanced reward function incorporating confidence scores or margin-based loss might be more effective.
4. **Action Space:**  The scaling of the softmax function in Equation 7 to ensure positive activations feels arbitrary. Why is averaging to 1 necessary instead of summing to 1 as is standard? This needs better justification.
5. **Figure Clarity and Information:** Figure 3, intended to illustrate the effect of attention, is cluttered and difficult to interpret. The “change of layer 0” visualization is particularly unclear.  What does a change in activation *mean* in this context?
6. **Related Work Gaps:** The related work section, while mentioning relevant areas, omits key recent developments in attention mechanisms and reinforcement learning for computer vision (e.g., attention-based recurrent networks, transformer-based models).  The citations are dated in several instances.
7. **Formatting and Writing Errors:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, equation numbering).  The writing style is occasionally awkward and could benefit from polishing.  For example, the phrase "In this work π : O → A is a deterministic policy" is redundant and unnecessary.
8. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks crucial ablation studies.  What is the impact of each component of the observation vector *o<sub>t</sub>*? What happens if the action space is reduced?  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of SNES hyperparameters?

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
* Novel combination of techniques.
* Demonstrable performance improvement, albeit modest.
* Addresses a relevant problem (static CNNs).

**Con:**
* Significant methodological shortcomings and lack of detail.
* Weak justification for SNES and reward function design.
* Insufficient experimental analysis (lack of ablation studies).
* Numerous minor flaws in writing and formatting.
* Related work is not comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind dasNet is interesting, the paper's numerous deficiencies prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of methodological rigor, the insufficient experimental analysis, and the minor flaws in presentation raise serious concerns about the reproducibility and robustness of the results.  The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the aforementioned issues and resubmit to a future conference. A more thorough experimental evaluation and a stronger theoretical justification for the design choices are essential.
