

# Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through internal selective attention mechanisms enabled by feedback connections. The authors propose augmenting a pre-trained Maxout network with a gating mechanism that allows the network to iteratively adjust filter weights across multiple passes through the same image. The attentional policy, which determines how to modulate filter activations, is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) rather than conventional reinforcement learning methods due to the large number of parameters involved. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where it achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on ambiguous classification cases.

The paper builds upon the observation that humans use multiple feedforward evaluations to resolve ambiguous visual stimuli, and posits that CNNs could similarly benefit from sequential processing. It distinguishes itself from prior work on recurrent networks (e.g., [48-50]) by applying this perspective directly to state-of-the-art CNNs rather than simplified tasks, and from attention mechanisms (e.g., [40-41, 44-45]) by learning the attentional policy through reinforcement learning rather than hand-engineering.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach with clear justification for using SNES to handle the large parameter space of the attentional policy. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and ablation studies (dynamics analysis, information content of gates). The results demonstrate consistent improvement on difficult classification cases without disrupting previously learned knowledge.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized, with clear notation, consistent terminology, and effective use of figures to illustrate the dasNet mechanism. Algorithm 1 provides a precise description of the training procedure. The related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution relative to both neuroscience and machine learning literature.

3. **Novelty**: The core contribution of using reinforcement learning to learn an internal attentional policy that modifies filter activations across multiple passes through the same image is novel. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work on recurrent networks and attention mechanisms, and the approach is not a simple combination of existing techniques.

4. **Significance**: The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 for un-augmented data, addressing a meaningful problem in deep learning: improving classification performance on ambiguous cases. The findings suggest that even well-trained CNNs can benefit from internal iterative refinement, which could have broader implications for deep learning architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Scalability Analysis**: The paper does not discuss how the approach scales to larger datasets or more complex architectures (e.g., ResNets, Vision Transformers). The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU) may be prohibitive for large-scale applications.

2. **Ad Hoc Boosting Mechanism**: The "boosting" mechanism (Equation 10) that weights misclassified samples higher is somewhat ad hoc. The paper does not provide thorough justification for the specific weighting scheme or explore alternative approaches.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Modern Attention Mechanisms**: The paper does not compare against modern attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers, self-attention) that have become standard in computer vision. This omission makes it difficult to assess how dasNet's approach compares to the current state of the art in attention mechanisms.

4. **Limited Analysis of Beneficial Cases**: While Figure 3 provides a qualitative example, the paper lacks systematic analysis of which image classes or specific features benefit most from the dasNet approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach to enhancing deep CNNs through internal selective attention.
- The experimental results are significant, achieving state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100.
- The methodology is well-motivated and clearly described.
- The approach addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning (improving classification of ambiguous cases).
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The computational cost may limit practical applicability compared to simpler attention mechanisms.
- The comparison to modern attention mechanisms (which have become standard in vision) is lacking.
- The scalability to larger architectures and datasets is not addressed.
- The boosting mechanism lacks thorough justification.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of how feedback connections can enhance deep neural networks, and the results are convincing within the scope of the experiments. While the computational cost and lack of comparison to modern attention mechanisms are notable limitations, the core idea is novel and well-supported by the experimental results. The paper addresses a genuine problem in deep learning and provides a technically sound approach to improve upon the state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field by demonstrating how reinforcement learning can be used to learn internal selective attention mechanisms in deep networks, with clear experimental validation on standard datasets. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
