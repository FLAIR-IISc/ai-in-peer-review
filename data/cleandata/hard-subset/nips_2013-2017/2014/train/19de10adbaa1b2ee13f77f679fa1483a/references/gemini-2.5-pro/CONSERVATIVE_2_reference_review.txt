**Summary:**
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for improving the performance of a pre-trained Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) on difficult classification tasks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) with feedback connections that modulate the activity of convolutional filters over multiple passes for a single input image. This modulation is controlled by a policy, which is trained using reinforcement learning (RL) after the initial supervised training of the CNN is complete. The policy observes the internal state of the network (e.g., average map activations, class probabilities) and outputs an action that weights the feature maps for the next pass. The authors use an evolution strategy, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to learn the policy parameters, arguing that standard RL methods are impractical for the high-dimensional parameter space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this iterative "internal attention" mechanism can correct initial misclassifications and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The paper presents a novel and interesting combination of techniques. The idea of post-hoc training of an attentional policy using RL on top of a fixed, supervisedly-trained deep network is unconventional. The motivation, drawn from the distinction between rapid, pre-attentive human vision and slower, deliberate analysis, is compelling and provides a plausible justification for the multi-pass architecture.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a significant performance improvement over a strong baseline (Maxout networks). A 6% relative improvement on the CIFAR-100 benchmark is not a trivial gain and suggests the proposed mechanism is effective. Achieving a state-of-the-art result, even with caveats, is a notable strength.

3.  **Interesting Problem Formulation:** The work frames the problem as "correcting" the mistakes of a powerful, pre-trained model. This is a practical and relevant direction for research, as it offers a potential path to improving existing models without requiring a full retraining from scratch.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Justification and Baselines:** The paper's primary weakness is its reliance on an unconventional training method (SNES) without sufficient justification or comparison to more standard alternatives.
    *   The claim that standard RL methods like policy gradients are "impractical" for the ~1 million parameter policy is a very strong assertion that is not adequately supported. Policy gradient methods have been used with similarly large parameter spaces. The authors should have provided a more detailed analysis or at least attempted a comparison to demonstrate this impracticability. Using a black-box, derivative-free optimization method like SNES when the underlying system is a neural network feels poorly motivated and less principled than gradient-based alternatives.
    *   The experimental evaluation is critically missing comparisons to more established methods for improving CNN performance. The only baseline is the vanilla Maxout network itself. A much more convincing comparison would be against an ensemble of Maxout networks. Given that the dasNet approach requires multiple forward passes (`T=5`), its computational cost at inference time is significantly higher than the baseline. A fair comparison would be against an ensemble of 2-3 Maxout networks, which might achieve similar performance gains for a comparable computational budget. Another relevant baseline would be simply training a larger Maxout network. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gain comes from the proposed attention mechanism or simply from the increased computation and parameterization.

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope and Analysis:** The experiments are confined to the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, the scalability of the approach to larger-scale problems like ImageNet is not addressed. The SNES training process, which requires evaluating a population of policies on batches of images over multiple passes, seems computationally prohibitive for larger images and datasets. The analysis of the learned mechanism is also quite limited. The visualization in Figure 3 is for a single, likely cherry-picked, example. It provides a hint of what the network is doing but fails to offer generalizable insights into the learned strategy. The "information content" analysis using k-NN is a weak form of validation; it merely confirms that the learned gate values are correlated with the final classification, which is expected by construction. It does not prove that this is an efficient or optimal mechanism.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The description of the state space (observation `ot`) is somewhat vague. It is a concatenation of average map activations, intermediate activations, and the final probability vector. The precise dimensionality and structure are not fully specified. Furthermore, the choice of hyperparameters (T=5, lambda values) appears arbitrary, and no ablation study is presented to show their impact on performance, making the method seem sensitive and difficult to reproduce.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel idea of using a post-hoc, RL-trained policy to control internal attention in a CNN.
*   The reported results on CIFAR-100 represent a new state-of-the-art for that dataset (at the time of writing, for unaugmented data), which is a significant empirical contribution.
*   The conceptual link to cognitive science and human vision provides a strong motivation for the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The choice of learning algorithm (SNES) is unconventional and poorly justified; standard gradient-based RL methods are dismissed without evidence.
*   The experimental validation is weak due to the lack of crucial baselines, particularly ensembles and larger feedforward models, which are the established methods for improving performance.
*   The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets, raising serious questions about the scalability and general applicability of the proposed method.
*   The analysis of what the model has learned is superficial and relies on a single example rather than a systematic investigation.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a creative and thought-provoking idea, its execution and evaluation fall short of the standards expected for NIPS. The reliance on a black-box optimizer without a rigorous comparison to more standard, gradient-based RL methods is a major methodological flaw. More importantly, the lack of comparison against well-established and powerful baselines like model ensembling makes it impossible to judge the true significance of the reported performance gains. The work is an interesting proof-of-concept, but it is not a complete or convincingly validated piece of research in its current form.
