**Summary:**
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (CNN) with a mechanism for internal selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "thinking" steps on the same image. In each step, a policy, learned via Reinforcement Learning (RL), modulates the activations of the CNN's convolutional filters through feedback connections. This allows the network to iteratively re-weight its internal features to improve classification, particularly on difficult or ambiguous examples that the base network misclassifies. The policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) after the base CNN (a Maxout network) has been trained with standard supervised learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data.

**Review and Justification:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and significant contribution. The central idea of learning a policy to modulate internal network features for iterative refinement is both conceptually elegant and well-motivated by analogies to human perception.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is a sensible and well-reasoned combination of existing powerful techniques: state-of-the-art CNNs (Maxout), reinforcement learning, and black-box optimization (SNES).

*   **Strengths:** The justification for using SNES over traditional RL methods is convincing; given the vast, continuous action space (modulating every filter map) and the non-differentiable nature of the fitness function with respect to the policy parameters, a powerful black-box optimizer is an appropriate choice. The experimental setup is solid. The authors build upon a strong baseline (a high-performing Maxout network) and demonstrate a clear, quantifiable improvement. The analysis of the network dynamics (Figure 2) and the information content of the learned gates are good additions that go beyond simply reporting a final accuracy number, providing evidence that the attention mechanism is indeed learning a meaningful strategy.

*   **Weaknesses:** The claims are well-supported by the experiments on CIFAR, but the scalability of the approach remains a significant question. The policy network itself has over 1 million parameters, and training it takes 4 days on top of the initial CNN training. It is unclear how this would scale to much deeper networks and larger datasets like ImageNet, where both the number of filters to control and the state space would increase dramatically.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building the reader's understanding, starting with the components (Maxout, RL) before integrating them into the dasNet framework.

*   **Strengths:** Algorithm 1 provides a clear, concise overview of the training procedure. The description of how the observation vector `o_t` and the action vector `a` are constructed is specific enough to allow for a deep understanding of the mechanism. The illustrations (Figure 1 and 3) are very helpful in visualizing the iterative process and the effect of the learned policy on the feature maps. The paper provides sufficient detail (hyperparameters, model architecture) for an expert to likely reproduce the results.

*   **Weaknesses:** No major weaknesses in clarity. A minor point is that the distinction between the policy `πθ` and its parameter matrix `θ` is slightly conflated in Equation 7, which describes a single-layer linear policy, but this is a minor nitpick and does not detract from understanding.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While ideas of recurrence, feedback, and attention in neural networks are not new, this paper's contribution is a novel and practical instantiation of these concepts.

*   **Strengths:** The key innovation is the specific mechanism: learning a top-down policy to directly modulate the gain of entire feature maps within a modern, deep CNN. This differs significantly from prior work on attention, which often focuses on spatial attention (e.g., learning saccades or cropping regions), or from recurrent models that use more generic, untied weights. The combination of a pre-trained supervised model with a post-hoc RL-trained "attentional controller" is a novel training paradigm. The related work section is thorough and correctly positions this work as a step towards scaling these biologically-inspired ideas to state-of-the-art computer vision systems.

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this work is high. It points toward a promising direction for moving beyond purely feedforward architectures, which currently dominate computer vision.

*   **Strengths:** The paper tackles a very important problem: how to improve performance on the "long tail" of difficult examples that stump standard models. The idea of dedicating more computation to harder inputs is powerful and efficient. The demonstrated state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark proves that this is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical method for improving performance. Other researchers are likely to build on these ideas, exploring different policy architectures, more efficient RL algorithms, and applications beyond classification. This work successfully bridges concepts from cognitive science, reinforcement learning, and deep learning in a compelling way.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary barrier to immediate, widespread adoption is the significant computational expense of training the policy. This might limit its application in resource-constrained environments. However, as a research contribution, it opens up a new avenue for exploration that is of high value to the community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a new and original method for internal attention in deep networks.
2.  **Significance:** Addresses the important problem of handling difficult classification cases and provides a compelling alternative to simply increasing model size.
3.  **Strong Results:** Achieves a state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (CIFAR-100), empirically validating the approach.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex idea easy to understand.
5.  **Inspiring:** The work is likely to inspire follow-up research into hybrid supervised/RL systems, iterative processing, and internal attention mechanisms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The training procedure is very computationally expensive, which may limit its practical utility and scalability.
2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is confined to CIFAR-10/100. While these are good datasets, demonstrating the method on a larger-scale problem like ImageNet would make the contribution even stronger.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the idea, the significance of the problem it addresses, and the strong empirical results—far outweigh the weaknesses related to computational cost and experimental scope. The paper introduces a compelling and well-executed new direction for research in deep learning.
