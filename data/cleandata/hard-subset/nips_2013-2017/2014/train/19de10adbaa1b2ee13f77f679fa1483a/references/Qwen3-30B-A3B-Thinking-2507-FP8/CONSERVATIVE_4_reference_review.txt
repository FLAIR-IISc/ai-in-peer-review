

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which aims to improve CNN performance by adding feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust filter weights through reinforcement learning after initial training. The authors claim their method can "correct" misclassifications by selectively emphasizing/de-emphasizing filters across multiple passes over an image, achieving a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for replication. The connection to human visual processing is conceptually interesting, and the authors make a reasonable attempt to ground their work in neuroscience literature. The implementation of the SNES-based policy learning is described with appropriate technical precision.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The most significant issue is the lack of rigorous experimental validation against strong baselines. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but fails to compare against relevant attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM, or transformer-based approaches) that have been shown to significantly improve CNN performance. The modest 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is not convincingly demonstrated as meaningful given the substantial computational overhead (4 days additional training time on a GTX 560 Ti GPU). The paper also lacks proper ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of their method. Crucially, they don't establish whether the attention mechanism is actually learning meaningful patterns rather than merely overfitting to the validation set.

**Originality:** While the idea of adding feedback connections to CNNs for selective attention has been explored previously (e.g., in recurrent networks and attention mechanisms), the authors don't clearly distinguish their approach from existing work. The use of SNES instead of standard RL methods is not sufficiently justified - the claim that "standard RL methods" are impractical for "close to a million" parameters is unsubstantiated, as many RL methods can handle large-scale problems. The paper doesn't adequately explain why their approach is fundamentally better than simpler alternatives like layer-wise attention mechanisms or iterative refinement approaches that have been demonstrated to work with standard optimization.

**Significance:** The claimed improvement is modest (6% relative) on standard benchmarks, which is not sufficient to justify the additional complexity and computational cost. The paper doesn't address whether this approach scales to larger datasets or more complex tasks, nor does it provide evidence that the attention mechanism provides interpretable insights or has practical applications beyond incremental accuracy gains. The claim that dasNet "establishes a new state-of-the-art" is overstated without proper comparison to recent strong baselines like ResNet variants or attention-augmented CNNs.

**Methodological Concerns:** The reinforcement learning approach is questionable. The paper states they use "reinforcement learning" but the described method bears little resemblance to standard RL frameworks. The "policy" they learn is essentially a set of filter weights rather than a decision-making process, and the reward function is simplistic (weighted loss). The training process described (with T=5 passes) is more like a simple iterative refinement than a true RL approach.

## Critical Assessment

This paper presents an interesting conceptual idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The experimental validation is incomplete, lacking proper comparison against relevant baselines, ablation studies, and analysis of failure cases. The computational cost of the method appears disproportionate to the marginal improvement. The authors' claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is unsubstantiated without comparison to contemporary approaches.

While the connection to human vision is intriguing, the paper doesn't provide sufficient neuroscience evidence to justify the specific architecture they've chosen. The methodological justification for using SNES over standard RL approaches is weak, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that this choice is necessary or beneficial.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents a novel idea but fails to provide rigorous validation that it's actually better than existing methods. The modest improvement on standard benchmarks, combined with the substantial computational overhead and lack of proper baselines, doesn't justify the claims of "state-of-the-art" performance. The methodological approach is questionable, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed attention mechanism provides meaningful benefits over simpler alternatives.

For future work, I would recommend: (1) thorough comparison against strong attention mechanisms, (2) ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component, (3) analysis of the computational cost-benefit tradeoff, and (4) demonstration of the approach on larger, more challenging datasets. Without these, the paper doesn't meet the high standards expected for NIPS.
