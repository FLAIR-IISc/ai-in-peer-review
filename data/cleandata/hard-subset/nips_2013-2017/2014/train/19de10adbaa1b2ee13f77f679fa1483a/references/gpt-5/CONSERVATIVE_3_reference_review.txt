Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with multiplicative gates on each feature map, applied across multiple recurrent passes over the same input. The gates are selected by a learned policy that observes summary statistics from intermediate activations and outputs per-map scaling factors; this is intended to realize internal selective attention via top-down feedback. The policy is trained post hoc using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the high dimensionality of the observation/action spaces and the large number of policy parameters. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over the base Maxout model (a claimed 6% relative improvement and “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data”), qualitative visualizations of attention changes, and some analysis of dynamics and gate informativeness.

Relation to prior work
The idea of recurrent processing and attention for vision is well-established, including RL-driven saccades/attention, and feedback in neural models. The paper situates itself among this literature and explicitly builds on Maxout CNNs and dropout. The specific combination—feature-map gating across recurrent passes trained with a black-box optimizer—is less common, though related to earlier work on adaptive/selective attention and recurrent CNNs.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear mechanism for internal attention: simple multiplicative gating of feature maps driven by an observation of intermediate activations.
- The method is modular: starts from a trained CNN without changing its weights and learns a control policy on top.
- Empirical results suggest nontrivial improvements on CIFAR-10/100 and show corrections on ambiguous cases.
- Some analysis beyond raw accuracy: stability of dynamics across more steps than trained for; gate vectors contain class-relevant information.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited and potentially inconsistent empirical validation:
  - Only CIFAR-10/100 are evaluated. No larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) or tasks beyond small images.
  - The “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” claim is confusing since the Maxout baseline was trained with data augmentation; it is unclear what “unaugmented” refers to.
  - Exact numbers and tables are not fully presented in the text; the magnitude of gains and statistical significance are unclear.
- Missing key baselines and ablations:
  - No comparison to simpler test-time procedures with similar compute cost, e.g., test-time augmentation/ensembling, MC-dropout, multiple stochastic forward passes, or averaging across T independent dropout masks.
  - No ablation to isolate the contribution of recurrence vs. a single-pass learned gate (T=1 vs T>1), nor the effect of the boosting loss vs. standard loss.
  - No comparison to end-to-end differentiable recurrent/gating training (e.g., backprop through time with REINFORCE or policy gradients) or to more standard RL methods; SNES is used without benchmarking its necessity.
- Methodological choices appear ad hoc and under-justified:
  - The policy is effectively a linear softmax mapping from aggregated activations to gates; given ~1M parameters, overfitting is a risk. Regularization and validation strategy are not detailed.
  - The observation is a coarse average over maps; the paper asserts sufficiency but gives little analysis of observability vs. performance.
  - The boosting weights (λcorrect, λmisclassified) are heuristics; sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Computational cost and practicality:
  - Inference is T times more expensive; there is no comparison of accuracy vs. compute budget to baseline methods.
  - Training uses SNES with a population of 50 and takes ~4 days in addition to base CNN training; scalability is unclear.
- Reproducibility:
  - Important details are deferred to the supplement or omitted (exact architectures, sizes of action/observation vectors per layer, full SNES hyperparameters). No code availability is indicated.

Quality
The paper is technically sound at a high level and the mechanism is plausible. However, the empirical support is not rigorous enough: key baselines are missing, the reported improvements are insufficiently quantified, and there is limited analysis of sensitivity/ablation. Claims such as “state-of-the-art” need clearer substantiation and fair comparisons.

Clarity
The main idea and algorithm are explained clearly, and the motivation is well written. However, the experimental description lacks essential details to reproduce results and to assess fairness of comparisons. Several claims rely on figures/tables not provided in the text excerpt and on ambiguous terminology (“unaugmented data”).

Originality
The specific instantiation—per-feature-map gates controlled by an evolved policy over recurrent passes—is a novel combination of known ingredients. Still, the conceptual contribution sits close to existing lines of work on recurrent attention and feedback in CNNs.

Significance
Potentially interesting direction, but current evidence is limited to small datasets with relatively modest improvements and without strong baselines or compute-normalized comparisons. As presented, it is difficult to conclude that the approach provides a robust advance over established methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complete, quantitative results (absolute error rates, confidence intervals) and ensure fair “unaugmented” claims.
- Add strong baselines matched on compute: T-fold test-time augmentation, MC-dropout, ensemble of T snapshots, and a T-pass averaging without gating.
- Include ablations: T=1 vs T>1, no boosting, different observation sets, nonlinear policy networks, SNES vs gradient-based policy learning.
- Report runtime and parameter overheads; consider adaptive T at test time (only for low-confidence cases).
- Evaluate on at least one larger-scale benchmark.
- Clarify training/validation splits, early stopping, and regularization; release code or detailed hyperparameters.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact CIFAR-10/100 numbers for base Maxout and dasNet (with/without augmentation), and how do they compare to contemporaneous baselines under identical training regimes?
- What are the dimensions of the observation and action vectors per layer? Is the policy strictly linear?
- How sensitive are results to T and to λcorrect/λmisclassified? Did you try training with standard cross-entropy without boosting?
- Why SNES over policy gradients or backprop-through-time with REINFORCE? Any empirical comparison?
- What is the test-time compute overhead and memory footprint relative to the base model, and how does accuracy scale with that budget?

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). Interesting idea and clear presentation, but the evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous, key baselines and ablations are missing, and claims of state-of-the-art are not convincingly established. With stronger empirical validation and clearer comparisons, this could be competitive. Confidence: high.
