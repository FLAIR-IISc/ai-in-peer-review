**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to enable iterative, selective attention. The base model is a Maxout network, trained via supervised learning. Subsequently, a control policy is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a reinforcement learning method. This policy modulates the activity of convolutional filter maps over multiple passes (T>1) of the same input image. The goal is for the network to iteratively refine its "internal attention" to correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples. The authors demonstrate this approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, claiming a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data on the latter.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of learning a post-hoc attentional policy to modulate the internal workings of a pre-trained CNN is novel and compelling. It moves beyond the static, one-shot inference paradigm of most deep networks and draws plausible inspiration from the recurrent, attentional phases of human vision. If robust, this method could provide a general framework for improving the performance of existing models on challenging, ambiguous inputs.

2.  **Ambitious Problem:** The paper tackles the important problem of correcting errors made by a strong baseline model, focusing on the "long tail" of difficult classification instances. This is a significant challenge in deploying robust recognition systems.

3.  **Strong Empirical Result:** The reported 6% relative improvement over a strong Maxout network baseline on CIFAR-100 is significant and suggests the method is effective. Claiming a new state-of-the-art result, even within a specific data augmentation category, is a noteworthy contribution.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from critical omissions to minor but distracting inconsistencies. These issues severely undermine the work's quality, clarity, and reproducibility.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**

*   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The paper is fundamentally incomplete. Table 1, Figure 2, and Figure 3 are all referenced extensively but are not included. The entire empirical discussion of results, dynamics, and filter visualizations is therefore unverifiable and impossible to properly assess. This is a critical flaw that, on its own, warrants rejection.
*   **Mathematical Ambiguity and Errors:** Several key equations are unclear or appear to contain errors.
    *   In Eq. (2), the maxout pooling is defined as `max i=0..b`, which includes `b+1` elements. Is this intended, or should it be `i=0..b-1`?
    *   Eq. (3) is exceptionally poorly explained. It is unclear how `i` indexes the rows of matrix `F`. The description "similar to the pooling layer" is insufficient for understanding the operation, making this part of the architecture impossible to reproduce.
    *   In Eq. (8), the loss `Li = −λboostd log(vT)` is confusing. `d` is the correct class, but `vT` is a vector. The expression should presumably be `log(vT_d)`, where `vT_d` is the probability assigned to the correct class `d`.
    *   In Eq. (11), the variable `F` is reused for the vector of fitness values, having previously been used to denote filter tensors. This is poor notation practice.
*   **Vague Descriptions:** The construction of the observation vector `ot` is described as "concatenating" several values, but the precise dimensions and composition are not specified. Similarly, the policy `πθi(o)` in Eq. (7) is a simple linear transformation followed by a scaled softmax, which seems like a very limited model for controlling over a million parameters; this choice is not sufficiently justified.

**2. Methodological and Experimental Rigor:**

*   **Baseline Comparison:** The claim of a new state-of-the-art result rests on the missing Table 1. Without it, it is impossible to see which methods dasNet is being compared against. The primary comparison is to the "vanilla CNN," which is appropriate for an ablation study but insufficient to establish a SOTA result without proper context.
*   **Hyperparameter Selection:** Key hyperparameters like `T=5`, `λcorrect`, `λmisclassified`, and `λL2` are presented without justification or sensitivity analysis. The performance of the model could be highly dependent on these choices. The claim that `T=5` was "experimentally determined" needs to be substantiated with data.
*   **Computational Cost:** The training of the policy takes 4 days on a GPU. While the specific GPU is old, this is a significant computational cost added on top of the initial supervised training. This cost is a major practical drawback of the method and should be discussed more transparently as a limitation.
*   **Analysis:** The "Dynamics" subsection relies on the missing Figure 2. The analysis of gate information content is interesting, but the conclusion is weakened by vagueness. The reported k-NN and logistic regression performances (40.70% and 45.74%) are stated to be "similar to the performance of dasNet," but the actual performance of dasNet on that specific task is not given, making the comparison meaningless.

**3. Formatting and Professionalism:**

*   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Many entries are incomplete, lacking conference or journal names, years, and proper formatting (e.g., "[1] Neural network model for a mechanism of pattern recognition unaffected by shift in position - Neocognitron, K. Fukushima, In: Trans. IECE J62-A(10), , ,"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
*   **Typos and Style:** Minor typographical errors and stylistic inconsistencies are present throughout the text (e.g., the spacing in `a ℓ j` in Eq. (5)).

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel and exciting idea for improving deep networks through learned, iterative attention. The preliminary results, as described, seem promising. However, the manuscript in its current form is far below the standards expected for NIPS. It is fundamentally incomplete due to the missing figures and table. Furthermore, it suffers from a lack of clarity in its mathematical formulation, insufficient experimental detail, and a general lack of polish, particularly in the bibliography.

While the core contribution may be significant, the sheer volume of flaws, both major and minor, makes it impossible to properly evaluate the work. The paper requires a thorough revision to address the clarity of the method, to include the missing results, and to be polished to a professional standard.

**Recommendation: Reject**
