## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing CNN performance by incorporating internal selective attention via feedback connections. After initial supervised training, dasNet leverages reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES) to learn a policy that modulates filter activations on successive passes over an image. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its classifications by focusing on salient features, particularly in challenging cases. The authors demonstrate improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing dasNet corrects misclassifications by emphasizing and de-emphasizing filters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning attention mechanisms *within* a pre-trained CNN using RL is a compelling and novel approach.  It moves beyond traditional attention mechanisms that typically operate on input features or hidden states, and instead allows the network itself to direct its own processing. This is a significant departure from standard CNN architectures.
* **Theoretical Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by observations of human visual processing, drawing parallels between feedforward and recurrent processes in both biological and artificial systems.  The argument for allowing networks to "think" for longer on difficult cases is plausible and grounded in cognitive science.
* **Empirical Results:** The results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate a clear performance improvement over a baseline Maxout network. The analysis of filter activations (Figure 3) provides some qualitative insight into how the attention mechanism is functioning, showing dynamic changes in filter importance.
* **Addressing a Difficult Problem:** Improving performance on ambiguous cases is a crucial challenge in computer vision. This work directly addresses this problem, offering a potentially valuable tool for enhancing robustness.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The inclusion of the Maxout network details is helpful for readers unfamiliar with that architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The RL training process, using SNES, is computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti). This limits the scalability of the approach to larger, more complex networks and datasets.  The authors acknowledge this but don’t offer potential solutions for mitigation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of the SNES hyperparameters (population size, learning rate, regularization). It's unclear how robust the results are to changes in these parameters.
* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.  It's unclear whether the learned attention policies would generalize well to other datasets or architectures.  A more thorough evaluation on a wider range of tasks would be beneficial.
* **Black Box Nature:** While the authors visualize activation changes, the learned attention policies remain largely a “black box”. A better understanding of *why* certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized would increase confidence in the approach.  The authors acknowledge the difficulty of interpreting these high-level changes, but further investigation is needed.
* **SNES Justification:** While the authors state why they chose SNES over other RL methods, a brief comparison of SNES performance to a simpler RL algorithm (even on a smaller scale) would strengthen the justification.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies are needed to determine the contribution of different components of dasNet (e.g., the number of passes T, the specific features used in the observation vector *o<sub>t</sub>*).



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are supported by experimental results. The methodology is clearly described. However, the computational cost and lack of extensive generalization analysis raise some concerns. (Score: 7/10)

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The explanations are clear and concise. (Score: 8/10)

**Originality:** The combination of internal attention, feedback connections, and reinforcement learning is a novel contribution. While the individual components are known, their integration in this way is unique. (Score: 8/10)

**Significance:** The results are promising, suggesting that dasNet could be a valuable tool for improving CNN performance in challenging scenarios.  However, the computational cost and limited generalization analysis temper the overall significance. (Score: 7/10)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to internal attention mechanisms.
* Demonstrated performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* Well-motivated by cognitive science principles.
* Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* High computational cost limits scalability.
* Limited generalization analysis.
* Lack of extensive ablation studies.
* “Black box” nature of the learned attention policies.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of deep learning. The idea of learning internal attention mechanisms is both novel and potentially impactful. However, the computational cost, limited generalization analysis, and lack of deeper interpretability are significant weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses by:

1.  Discussing potential methods for reducing the computational cost of the SNES training.
2.  Conducting experiments on at least one additional dataset to assess generalization.
3.  Performing more detailed ablation studies to understand the impact of different design choices.
4.  Providing a more thorough analysis of the learned attention policies and attempting to interpret *why* certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized.
