**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends standard deep convolutional neural networks (in this case, Maxout networks) with a feedback mechanism. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple forward passes on a single image, where each pass is modulated by an "attentional policy." This policy, implemented as a simple neural network, takes an observation from the network's internal state (e.g., average map activations, class probabilities) and outputs a set of weights that scale the output maps of each convolutional layer. This mechanism allows the network to selectively emphasize or suppress features to refine its classification, particularly for difficult or ambiguous cases. The attentional policy is trained post-hoc, after the base CNN is fully trained with supervised learning, using a reinforcement learning approach—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to handle the high-dimensional action space. The authors claim that this approach improves performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, establishing a new state-of-the-art result.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty:** The primary contribution is the combination of a state-of-the-art feedforward CNN with a reinforcement learning-based feedback mechanism for internal selective attention. This approach to making a trained network adaptive and non-stationary is a compelling research direction, moving beyond the static nature of conventional classifiers.

2.  **Motivation:** The work is well-motivated by parallels to the human visual system, particularly the distinction between a rapid pre-attentive feedforward pass and a slower, recurrent attentional phase. The related work section provides a solid, if brief, overview of the relevant concepts from both neuroscience and computer vision.

3.  **Analysis:** The proposed experiment to validate the information content of the final attention gates (by using them as features for a k-NN and logistic regression classifier) is a thoughtful piece of analysis that helps substantiate the claim that the policy is learning meaningful behavior.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is intriguing, the manuscript is plagued by a significant number of technical inaccuracies, notational ambiguities, and presentational flaws that severely undermine its quality and credibility.

1.  **Technical and Notational Imprecision:**
    *   **Indexing in Equations:** There appear to be consistent off-by-one errors in the indexing of summations and max operations. For example, in Eq. (1), the sum over `c` input maps is indexed `i=0` to `c`, which is `c+1` terms. Similarly, in Eq. (2) and (3), the max operation over a block of size `b` is indexed `i=0` to `b`, implying `b+1` elements. These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading and raise concerns about the implementation's correctness.
    *   **Ambiguous Description of Attention:** The text states the method modulates "the activity of the convolutional filters" (Introduction) and allows the network to "focus the attention of its filters" (Section 4). However, Equation (5) shows that the attentional weight `a_lj` is applied as a scalar multiplier to the *entire j-th output map* after convolution and summation over all input maps. This is a map-level modulation, not a filter-level one. The description is imprecise and potentially misleading.
    *   **Algorithm 1 Ambiguities:** The notation in Algorithm 1 is not rigorous. In line 6, `a0 <= 1` is non-standard notation for initializing a vector with ones. In line 12, the subscript on the loss `Li` and the boost factor `lambda_boost_d` are confusingly similar to the classification label `d`.
    *   **Non-Standard Softmax:** Equation (7) introduces a non-standard softmax activation, which is scaled by `dim(A)`. The justification that this makes the vector elements "average to 1" is plausible, but this is a significant deviation from standard practice that is not analyzed or discussed further. Its effect on the learning dynamics is unknown.

2.  **Missing Critical Information:**
    *   **Missing Results:** The paper's central experimental claims are entirely unsubstantiated in the provided text. Table 1, which allegedly shows the state-of-the-art performance, is referenced but not included. Figure 2, used to discuss the system's dynamics, is missing. Figure 3, which is discussed at length to provide a qualitative analysis of the attention mechanism, is also missing. Without these key results, it is impossible to evaluate the empirical contributions of the paper. This is a critical omission.
    *   **Incomplete Description:** The observation function `h(.)` is described textually but not formally defined, making it difficult to understand the exact composition of the state vector. The SNES update step is relegated to supplementary material, omitting a core part of the algorithm from the main text.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   **References:** The reference list is highly inconsistent and appears to have been assembled without care. For instance, reference [3] ("Back-Propagation Applied...") is missing the year and volume. Reference [6] has a typo ("I Sutskever"). Reference [12] ("Pedestrian Detection...") includes "IEEE," in the author list. Reference [18] is a duplicate of [15]. Reference [21] is missing venue and year. Many references are missing page numbers, volume/issue numbers, or publishers. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Language:** The tone in some sections is not entirely objective. The final sentence of the Related Work section ("...to show that this approach is now feasible and actually increases performance") reads more like a sales pitch than a scientific summary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The central concept of using RL to learn an internal, adaptive attentional policy on top of a trained CNN is novel and significant.
*   The approach is well-grounded in principles from neuroscience and addresses a fundamental limitation of static feedforward networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is critically incomplete due to the omission of all key figures and tables containing the experimental results. The claims are therefore entirely unsupported.
*   The manuscript contains numerous notational and mathematical errors (e.g., indexing in core equations) that demonstrate a lack of rigor and careful preparation.
*   Key algorithmic details are either ambiguous (e.g., the observation function) or missing from the main text.
*   The formatting, particularly of the reference section, is extremely poor and falls far below the expected standard for a NIPS publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the underlying ideas of this paper are promising, the execution of the manuscript is deeply flawed. The combination of missing results, technical inaccuracies, and poor presentation makes it impossible to properly evaluate the work. The sheer number of minor and major issues suggests a rushed and careless submission. The work is not ready for publication in its current form and requires a substantial and meticulous revision to address the identified problems. Only then could its scientific contributions be properly assessed.
