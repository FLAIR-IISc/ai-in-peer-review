### **Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (a Maxout network) with a recurrent, attentional mechanism. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, a control policy can modulate the activations of the network's convolutional filters over several subsequent passes on the same image. This policy is trained using reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies, SNES) to maximize a reward function that encourages correct classification, particularly for difficult, initially misclassified examples. The goal is to mimic the human ability to spend more time "thinking" about challenging visual recognition tasks. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves performance over the baseline Maxout network on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 at the time of publication.

**Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria:**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The proposed methodology is sound. The authors build upon a strong, state-of-the-art baseline (Maxout networks) and introduce a principled mechanism for iterative refinement. The choice of SNES for training the high-dimensional policy is well-justified; standard RL algorithms like policy gradients would likely struggle with the nearly one million parameters of the policy network. The formulation of the state (concatenated activations), action (filter modulation weights), and reward (boosted log-loss) is logical and serves the intended purpose.
*   **Experimental Support:** The claims are well-supported by the experiments. The performance improvement on CIFAR-100 is statistically significant and demonstrates the efficacy of the method. The authors go beyond simply reporting top-line accuracy by including insightful analyses:
    1.  A qualitative visualization of a cat/dog misclassification being corrected, showing how class probabilities evolve and which layers are modulated.
    2.  An analysis of the system's dynamics, showing that a policy trained for a small number of steps (T=2) remains stable for longer rollouts.
    3.  A clever experiment demonstrating that the learned gate activations contain significant class-discriminative information, confirming that the policy is not learning a trivial solution.
*   **Completeness:** This is a complete piece of work, presenting a novel idea, a full implementation, and a thorough evaluation. The authors are reasonably honest about the difficulty of interpreting the learned filter modulations, which is a known challenge in deep learning.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized, but could be improved in a few areas.

*   **Organization:** The structure is logical, moving from the base model (Maxout) to the RL framework and finally to the combined dasNet model and experiments. The introduction and related work sections effectively motivate the problem and situate the contribution within both neuroscience and computer vision contexts.
*   **Readability:** The core concept is explained clearly. However, some implementation details that are crucial for reproducibility are either sparse or relegated to supplementary material. For example, the precise architecture of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters, kernel sizes) is not specified. The exact construction of the observation vector `o_t` from `h(M)` could also be described more concretely. While Algorithm 1 provides a good overview, the SNES update step is a black box in the main text.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original.

*   **Novelty:** The central idea of using an RL-trained policy to control the internal, layer-wise activations of a deep CNN for iterative classification is novel and conceptually significant. While recurrence and attention had been explored before, this work was one of the first to propose learning a high-dimensional *internal* control policy for a modern, state-of-the-art deep network.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work. It correctly positions itself as scaling up ideas from neuroscience and earlier, smaller-scale RL-for-vision models to the then-current deep learning paradigm. The approach is a novel combination of deep supervised learning and black-box policy search (SNES) to solve a problem that is difficult to frame in a purely supervised or standard RL manner.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and has had a lasting impact.

*   **Impact:** This paper helped pioneer the integration of explicit attentional and recurrent mechanisms within deep feedforward networks. The idea of "thinking for longer" or iterative refinement has become a major theme in subsequent research, influencing architectures like recurrent attention models (RAM), Transformers (with their multiple self-attention layers), and dynamic networks that adapt their computation based on input difficulty.
*   **Advancement of the State of the Art:** The paper demonstrably advanced the state of the art on a standard benchmark (CIFAR-100). This empirical success gives strong credibility to the proposed ideas.
*   **Future Work:** The concepts presented are generative and likely to be built upon. The paper itself points to future work on more complex, spatially-focused attention, which has indeed become a major area of research. The main weakness is the computational cost (4 days of training for the policy alone, plus T-times slower inference), which might limit its direct practical application. However, its value as a foundational research contribution is unquestionable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Highly original and conceptually elegant idea of learning an internal attentional policy for a CNN.
*   **Pro:** Strong empirical results that establish a new state-of-the-art on a competitive benchmark.
*   **Pro:** Thorough analysis that goes beyond accuracy metrics to provide insight into the model's behavior.
*   **Pro:** Significant contribution that opens up a new avenue of research combining deep learning, reinforcement learning, and recurrence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The computational cost of both training and inference is very high, potentially limiting its practical applicability.
*   **Con:** Some key implementation details are missing from the main text, which could hinder reproducibility.
*   **Con:** The experiments are limited to small-image datasets (CIFAR). It is unclear how the approach would scale to high-resolution images where the number of parameters in the policy would become even more massive.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses regarding computational cost and some missing details, the paper's strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance are overwhelming. It introduces a powerful and influential new idea, backs it up with strong empirical evidence, and provides thoughtful analysis. This is a clear and enthusiastic recommendation for acceptance.
