

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
The paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections to enable selective attention in deep CNNs. The authors augment a pre-trained Maxout network with a reinforcement learning (RL) policy that modulates filter activations across multiple passes through the same image. The RL policy is evolved using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to improve classification on difficult cases. The approach claims to achieve state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 by allowing the network to "correct itself" through internal iterative processing.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in deep learning by modeling attention mechanisms through feedback connections rather than external mechanisms. The idea of using RL to evolve attention policies for internal feature selection is conceptually interesting and aligns with biological visual processing. The experiments demonstrate a 6% relative improvement over a baseline Maxout network on CIFAR-10/100, which is a notable result for a relatively lightweight extension to existing architectures.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Algorithmic inconsistencies**: Algorithm 1 contains several technical inconsistencies. The initialization "a0 ⇐ 1" is problematic since a0 is a vector but 1 is a scalar. The observation vector is described as "o0" but used as "ot" in the algorithm, creating confusion. The description of the observation vector ("Avg(yj) of each Maxout layer") conflicts with Equation (2), which defines pooling, not average activation.

2. **Equation formatting errors**: Several equations contain poor formatting (e.g., Equation 1: "i=c∑ i=0" should be "i=0∑^c"; Equation 7: "dim(A)σ(θiot)" lacks proper spacing and subscripts). These formatting errors make the equations difficult to parse and undermine the paper's technical rigor.

3. **Experimental justification gaps**: The paper states "The number of steps was experimentally determined and fixed at T = 5" but provides no details on how this value was chosen or why it's optimal. The dynamics section shows performance peaks at T but doesn't explain why this is the optimal number of passes.

4. **Baseline comparison**: The paper claims "a relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN" but fails to specify what the baseline model is. The "vanilla CNN" is not clearly defined in the experiments section, making it impossible to assess the significance of the improvement.

### Writing and Presentation Issues
1. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses both "dasNet" and "Deep Attention Selective Networks" without consistently defining the acronym at first use. The acronym should be defined when first introduced in the abstract or introduction.

2. **Reference issues**: The reference list contains redundant entries (e.g., Zeiler & Fergus is cited twice as #15 and #18) and includes irrelevant historical papers (e.g., Fukushima's Neocognitron from the 1970s). Some references lack publication years and page numbers.

3. **Typographical errors**: "Training took of dasNet took around 4 days" contains a clear grammatical error. The paper also misspells "Matthew Luciw" as "Matthew Luciw" in the References section.

4. **Figure description issues**: The paper refers to "change of layer 0" in Figure 3 without clear explanation or labeling. The figure caption should explicitly define this terminology.

### Conceptual and Significance Concerns
1. **Overstated claims**: The paper states "This establishes a new state-of-the-art result for this challenging dataset, for unaugmented data" but fails to provide context about current SOTA results on CIFAR-10/100 without data augmentation. Without this context, the claim lacks meaningful impact.

2. **RL approach justification**: The authors claim SNES is used "due to the large number of parameters (over 1 million)" but don't compare it to simpler RL approaches that might be more appropriate for this problem size (e.g., policy gradient methods with function approximation).

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach to incorporating attention mechanisms in deep networks, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and lack of clarity in experimental details significantly undermine its scientific rigor. The paper makes strong claims about performance improvements without providing sufficient context to assess their significance, and contains multiple errors that suggest insufficient proofreading.

The paper's technical flaws are too numerous and fundamental to be overlooked, especially given the high bar for NIPS. The authors have a promising idea but have not presented it with the level of detail and precision expected at this venue. I recommend rejection.

## Pro/Con Summary
**Pro**: Novel approach to internal attention mechanisms; interesting biological inspiration; promising experimental results on CIFAR-10/100.

**Con**: Numerous technical errors and inconsistent notation; unclear baseline comparison; poor equation formatting; redundant references; grammatical errors; overstated claims without sufficient context.
