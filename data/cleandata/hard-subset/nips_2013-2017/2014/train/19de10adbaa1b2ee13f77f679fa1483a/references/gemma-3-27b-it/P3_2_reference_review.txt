## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections (dasNet)

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep convolutional neural networks via reinforcement learning. The core idea of learning to selectively emphasize and de-emphasize filters *after* initial supervised training is novel and tackles a recognized limitation of standard CNNs - their inflexibility post-training. The experiments demonstrate a performance improvement on challenging instances in CIFAR-10/100, supporting the central hypothesis. However, the paper suffers from a lack of thoroughness in analyzing the learned attention policies, and a relatively limited experimental evaluation. While I believe the work is a solid contribution, it leans towards a "weak accept" and requires significant revision to reach a "strong accept".

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a standard CNN (specifically Maxout networks) with feedback connections allowing each layer to modulate the activations of others. This modulation is controlled by a policy learned through Reinforcement Learning (RL), using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the action space. The motivation stems from observations in human vision, where complex classification tasks often require multiple passes and attentional focusing. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of attention mechanisms and recurrent processing in CNNs. They appropriately cite relevant work on visual cortex feedback connections (Felleman & Essen, Gilbert, etc.), RL for visual attention (Denil, Schmidhuber), and iterative processing (Behnke, Larochelle). The novelty lies in combining these ideas to learn internal attention *within* a pre-trained CNN, rather than building attention mechanisms directly into the architecture or applying RL to a simplified visual task. The choice of SNES is also well-justified given the action space size.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning internal attention policies to refine CNN predictions is a genuinely new direction.
* **Strong Motivation:** The analogy to human visual processing provides a compelling rationale for the work. The idea of a CNN "thinking" multiple times over an image makes intuitive sense.
* **Solid Experimental Results:** The 6% relative improvement on challenging instances in CIFAR-10/100 is a positive result, demonstrating the potential of the approach.
* **Clear Explanation of SNES:** The paper provides a concise and understandable explanation of SNES, acknowledging its limitations while justifying its use.
* **Demonstration of Dynamics:** The experiments showing stable dynamics even when evaluating beyond the training steps (T) are reassuring.
* **Information Content Analysis:**  The analysis demonstrating information contained within the final gate values provides evidence that the policy is learning something meaningful.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of Attention Policies:** While the paper shows *that* dasNet improves performance, it offers limited insight into *how* it achieves this.  The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) is helpful, but rather superficial. A more systematic analysis of the learned attention policies is needed. For example:
    * Are certain filters consistently emphasized/de-emphasized for specific classes?
    * How does the attention policy change for different types of misclassifications?
    *  What is the impact of increasing the number of passes (T)?
* **Narrow Experimental Evaluation:**  The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 with a specific Maxout architecture. While a good starting point, the generalizability of the approach to other datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and network architectures (e.g., ResNet, VGG) is unknown. 
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters like learning rates, regularization parameters, and the number of SNES samples (p).  
* **Computational Cost:**  The 4-day training time on a GTX 560 Ti is significant. This limits scalability and hinders widespread adoption. A discussion of computational efficiency and potential optimizations would be valuable.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  It would be helpful to see the performance of dasNet with different components disabled (e.g., removing the top-down connections, using a different RL algorithm).
* **Overstated Significance:** The claim of establishing a “new state-of-the-art” is slightly overstated. While the 6% improvement is notable, it’s within the range of incremental improvements often seen in the field.

**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to internal attention in CNNs.
* Positive experimental results on CIFAR-10/100.
* Sound methodology and clear presentation.
* Addresses a relevant problem in deep learning – the inflexibility of trained networks.

**Con:**

* Limited analysis of the learned attention policies.
* Narrow experimental evaluation.
* Significant computational cost.
* Lack of ablation studies.
* Slightly overstated claims of significance.



**5. Recommendations and Questions for the Authors:**

* **Expand the analysis of the attention policies:** Provide a more detailed characterization of the learned filter activations and their relationship to classification performance.
* **Conduct experiments on more challenging datasets and with different network architectures:** This will help demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
* **Investigate hyperparameter sensitivity:** Analyze how the results change with different hyperparameter settings.
* **Explore techniques to reduce computational cost:** Consider techniques like parallelization or model compression.
* **Include ablation studies:** Evaluate the contribution of different components of dasNet.
* **Moderate the claims of significance:** Frame the results as a promising first step rather than a definitive breakthrough.
* **How does the choice of Maxout impact the performance?**  Would dasNet be equally effective with other CNN architectures?




**Overall, I recommend a weak accept with major revisions. The paper presents a promising idea with encouraging results, but it requires significant further work to fully realize its potential and convincingly demonstrate its impact.**
