## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections between layers. These connections are modulated by learned weights, forming an attentional policy trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) *after* standard supervised training. The core idea is to enable the network to refine its classifications by selectively emphasizing or suppressing filters on multiple passes over an image, particularly for difficult cases.  The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and provide visualization of the filter weighting changes.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundations of CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998), Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and Reinforcement Learning (Sutton & Barto, 1998). It acknowledges and situates itself within the broader context of biologically inspired visual processing (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000) that highlights the importance of feedback connections.  The work is related to other attention mechanisms in computer vision (e.g., Denil et al., 2012; Stollenga et al., 2015), but differentiates itself by learning an *internal* attentional policy *post-training* specifically to refine the behavior of existing CNN filters, rather than incorporating attention directly into the feedforward pass or relying on external attention signals.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning a post-training attentional policy to modulate internal CNN filters is a novel approach. This differs from typical attention mechanisms that are learned jointly with the network.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the work by drawing parallels to human visual processing, where deliberation and multiple passes over information are common in challenging perception tasks.
* **Empirical Results:** The performance improvement on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, albeit modest, is demonstrated and establishes a state-of-the-art result.  The visualization of filter weighting changes provides some insight into how the network is adapting its attention.
* **Addressing Practicality:** The use of SNES is a pragmatic choice given the large parameter space of the attentional policy.  The authors explicitly acknowledge the challenges of applying conventional RL methods.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of relevant work in visual attention, recurrent processing, and reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis:** While the visualizations are helpful, a more thorough analysis of *why* specific filters are being emphasized or suppressed would be valuable. The authors acknowledge the difficulty of interpreting high-level filters but could explore techniques like activation maximization or feature visualization.
* **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet requires a significant computational investment (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), raising questions about scalability to larger datasets and more complex architectures.  This should be clearly stated as a limitation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of various hyperparameters (e.g., the number of passes T, the SNES population size, the boosting parameters) on performance is not thoroughly investigated.  Sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
* **SNES Limitations:** While SNES is a practical choice, it’s a black-box optimization technique. The authors should acknowledge the potential for getting stuck in local optima and the difficulty of understanding the learned policy beyond observing its behavior.
* **The Value of the Boost:** While the boosting loss function seems reasonable, its impact isn’t explicitly demonstrated, and its parameters were empirically determined.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are supported by experimental results. The methodology is well-defined, and the authors are upfront about the limitations.  However, the analysis could be more rigorous.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and methodology are clearly explained.  However, the details of the SNES implementation could be elaborated upon (though references are provided).

**Originality:** The core idea of learning a post-training attentional policy is original and represents a valuable contribution.  The combination of CNNs, feedback connections, and SNES is novel.

**Significance:** The results are promising, but the significance of the improvement is somewhat limited by the relatively small datasets and the computational cost.  The potential for scaling this approach to more challenging tasks and larger models remains to be demonstrated.  Nevertheless, the paper opens up a new direction for research in deep learning, exploring the benefits of iterative refinement and internal attention mechanisms.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and interesting approach to improving CNN performance.
* Demonstrable performance gains on benchmark datasets.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Addresses a relevant problem in deep learning: improving robustness to difficult cases.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited analysis of the learned attentional policy.
* Significant computational cost.
* Potential hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Modest performance gains.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** despite the weaknesses mentioned above. The novelty and potential of the idea, combined with the empirical results and clear presentation, outweigh the limitations. The work is a valuable contribution to the field and opens up exciting avenues for future research.  I recommend the authors address the reviewer comments regarding analysis and computational cost in a revised version.
