This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhance CNN performance through internal selective attention modulated by feedback connections and learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES). The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its classification by adaptively weighting filters on successive passes over an image, effectively simulating a more deliberate, human-like visual inspection process. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, particularly on challenging instances.

This work builds upon the established foundation of CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and extends the concept of attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017 – although not directly cited) by making it internal and learnable post-training.  It draws inspiration from human visual processing where top-down feedback plays a crucial role in refining perception (Felleman & Van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000; Bar et al., 2006). Existing work in applying RL to vision (Whitehead, 2014; Denil et al., 2017) provides a basis, however, dasNet distinguishes itself by targeting the internal filter activations of a pre-trained CNN, rather than saccades or low-level feature learning. The use of SNES (Schaul et al., 2012) for policy optimization addresses the high dimensionality of the action space—a practical consideration.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning internal attentional mechanisms *after* supervised training is a worthwhile contribution. It offers a way to refine existing, well-trained models without retraining from scratch.
* **Practicality:** The choice of SNES is well-justified given the large number of parameters involved in controlling the network’s attention.
* **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on difficult instances, are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the approach. The visualization of filter activation changes provides some insight into how the network is focusing its attention.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing and identifying the limitations of standard CNNs.
* **Sound Methodology:**  The use of Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) as the underlying model provides a solid baseline. The experimental setup appears well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the results show overall improvement, a more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of different components (e.g., the number of passes *T*, different regularization parameters). For example, the benefit of *T* > 1 isn’t fully explored beyond showing stability.
* **Lack of Comparison to other Attention Mechanisms:** The paper doesn't compare dasNet against more contemporary attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks (Hu et al., 2018), CBAM (Woo et al., 2018)) that can be integrated directly into CNN architectures. It would be beneficial to understand how dasNet’s post-hoc attention compares to these approaches.
* **Interpretation of Filter Changes:**  The qualitative analysis of filter changes is insightful but limited.  It would be stronger if the authors could connect specific filter activation changes to interpretable visual features. It is stated that visualizing what these filters do is an open problem, but further attempts to correlate changes with high-level semantics would add value.
* **Computational Cost:**  The training time (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is significant. A discussion of the computational resources required for training and inference, as well as potential strategies for reducing the computational burden, would be helpful.
* **Missing Related Work:** The related work section does not mention more recent developments in attention mechanisms that have become prevalent in the computer vision field.  Specifically, the absence of any discussion on Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks or Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) is a notable omission.
* **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** Algorithm 1 is hard to follow. There is a lot of text in each step, and the notation could be improved.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful approach to improving CNN performance through learned internal attention.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* The use of SNES is a practical solution for optimizing the high-dimensional policy space.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The lack of a comprehensive ablation study and comparison to more recent attention mechanisms limits the scope of the evaluation.
* The computational cost is significant, and the paper does not address potential optimization strategies.
* The interpretation of filter changes remains largely qualitative and lacks strong semantic grounding.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and promising approach to enhancing CNNs with internal selective attention.  The novelty of the idea and the encouraging empirical results are significant strengths. However, the lack of comprehensive evaluation and comparison to existing attention mechanisms, along with the computational cost, are substantial weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Reject with Major Revision.**

I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but encouraging the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest conducting a more thorough ablation study, comparing dasNet to state-of-the-art attention mechanisms, providing a more in-depth analysis of filter changes, and discussing potential strategies for reducing the computational cost. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
