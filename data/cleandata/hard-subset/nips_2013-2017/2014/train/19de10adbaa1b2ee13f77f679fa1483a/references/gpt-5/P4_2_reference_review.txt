Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a modification of a trained convolutional Maxout network that introduces internal, selective attention via feedback across multiple passes over the same image. On each pass, per-feature-map multiplicative gates aℓj modulate the activations of convolutional outputs (post-nonlinearity), effectively reweighting features layer-wise. A policy π maps observations o (composed of per-map average activations from all Maxout layers, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to actions a (the gate values) for the next pass. The policy is trained post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a diagonal-covariance NES variant suited to high-dimensional parameter vectors (≈10^6), using a sequence of T passes (T=5 in main experiments) and a terminal loss that upweights misclassified examples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, claiming state-of-the-art performance for single-model, unaugmented test-time evaluation. Analyses include visualization of gate-induced changes and stability of the recurrent dynamics when run for more steps than used in training.

This work sits at the intersection of deep vision models with feedback and reinforcement learning for attention. It is related to prior biologically motivated feedback/attention models and RL-based attention (e.g., saccadic/“glimpse” models), as well as recurrent processing in vision; the novelty here is a global policy that modulates internal CNN feature maps through multiplicative gains across iterative passes, learned via black-box NES rather than gradient-based RL. The use of Maxout + dropout follows contemporary state of the art.

Quality:
- Methodology: The approach is clearly specified at a high level: train a Maxout CNN; freeze it; learn a deterministic linear (softmax) policy mapping observations to per-map gates; iterate for T steps; optimize policy parameters via SNES using a terminal cross-entropy-like loss with boosted weight for misclassified samples. The gating formulation yℓj = aℓj ∑i ϕ(xi ∗ Fℓi,j) is reasonable, and the scaling of the softmax to keep average gate ≈1 helps stabilize activations across passes. Treating each image as a short RL episode is sensible; the use of black-box evolution avoids credit assignment through the CNN.
- Empirical validation: Results on CIFAR-10/100 indicate non-trivial gains over the Maxout baseline, with analysis showing that gates are informative (KNN/logistic regression on final gate vectors yields decent accuracy) and that dynamics remain stable when unrolled beyond T. However, critical ablations are missing: (1) a static learned set of per-map gates (no feedback, no iteration); (2) random or heuristic gates; (3) a deeper/wider baseline CNN matched in computation to T forward passes; (4) a policy trained via standard policy gradients or supervised regression to correct labels; (5) per-layer-only gating ablations. These would better isolate the contribution of feedback, iteration, and NES.
- Soundness and evaluation details: Some notational issues reduce precision. In Eq. (8–10), d is described as the correct class, yet Eq. (10) tests d = ||vT||∞ to select λboost; this should be clarified as “predicted class equals ground truth.” The exact policy architecture appears to be a single linear mapping with softmax and no bias/hidden layers; if true, that limits expressivity and should be stated explicitly. Details of SNES hyperparameters, utility shaping, and stopping criteria are deferred to supplementary material; inclusion of key settings would improve reproducibility. The claim of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” appears inconsistent with earlier mention that the Maxout model is trained with data augmentation (likely meaning no test-time augmentation/ensembling); please clarify.
- Overfitting risk: Because the policy is evolved on training images with boosted weight on misclassified examples, a validation protocol should be described. The reported test set improvements partially address this, but a held-out validation for early stopping or distribution updates would increase confidence.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with a clear algorithm box. Improvements:
- Fix notation in the loss definition and the condition for λboost.
- Specify action and observation dimensionalities, per-layer gate counts, and whether gates include classifier layers.
- Provide the exact policy network (input normalization, bias terms, any hidden layers).
- Expand core CNN architecture details (filter sizes, channels per layer) in the main text for reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of internal multiplicative gating of CNN feature maps, controlled by a learned global feedback policy evaluated iteratively, is a distinct contribution relative to prior feedforward CNNs and earlier RL/attention models that typically focus on spatial attention (glimpses) or external saccades. The use of high-dimensional NES for policy learning is an interesting engineering choice given the scale.

Significance:
If the improvements over strong Maxout baselines are robust, the method demonstrates that learned internal feedback can correct ambiguous cases post hoc, potentially opening a path to more adaptive test-time behavior in CNNs. The approach is applicable to other architectures and tasks. That said, the evaluation is limited to CIFAR-10/100; demonstrating benefits on larger or more structurally ambiguous datasets, or on detection/segmentation, would strengthen impact. The computational overhead (≈5× inference time; several days of policy training) should be discussed in terms of trade-offs.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of internal selective attention via feedback.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains over a strong CNN baseline on standard benchmarks.
- Insightful analyses of dynamics stability and gate informativeness.
- Conceptual bridge between biological feedback and practical deep vision systems.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Missing ablation studies to disentangle the roles of iteration, gating, and NES.
- Limited scope of evaluation (only CIFAR-10/100) and lack of comparisons to compute-matched baselines.
- Some clarity/reproducibility issues (loss notation, policy architecture/hyperparameters, training/validation protocol).
- Computational cost and scalability to larger models/datasets not fully addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations: static learned gates; random/heuristic gates; per-layer gating; deeper/wider baseline; policy learned with gradient-based methods.
- Clarify and correct the loss/boosting notation; detail policy architecture and SNES settings; provide more CNN architectural specifics.
- Report results with/without training-time augmentation, and clarify the “unaugmented” claim (test-time only).
- Include per-class confusion analyses to show which ambiguities are resolved, and report gains versus number of passes to quantify cost/benefit.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel approach with promising results, though stronger ablations and expanded empirical scope would solidify the claims.
