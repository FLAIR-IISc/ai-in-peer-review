### **Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

#### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, fast feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "thinking" passes over the same input image. In these subsequent passes, a control policy modulates the activations of the network's internal feature maps (filters), effectively up-weighting or down-weighting certain features to refine the classification.

The base model is a pre-trained Maxout network. The control policy, which maps the network's internal state (concatenated feature map activations and class probabilities) to a set of modulatory weights, is learned via Reinforcement Learning (RL). Due to the high dimensionality of the policy parameters, the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization. The reward function is designed to focus learning on difficult, misclassified examples. The authors demonstrate that dasNet improves classification accuracy on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 over a strong Maxout baseline, achieving a new state-of-the-art result at the time of writing. The paper supports its claims with quantitative results and insightful qualitative analyses of the network's behavior.

This work connects to several lines of research. It is biologically motivated by the recurrent and top-down connections in the human visual cortex, which are thought to facilitate attentional processing after an initial feedforward sweep. It also relates to prior work using RL for visual attention (e.g., learning saccades), but distinguishes itself by applying the concept of attention *internally* to the feature maps of a modern, high-performing CNN, rather than spatially to the input image.

---

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The central idea of learning a policy to modulate internal features of a CNN over multiple iterations is highly novel. It presents a compelling computational model for the cognitive concept of "rethinking" or "double-checking" an initial guess. The motivation drawn from neuroscience is well-articulated and provides a strong conceptual foundation for the architecture.

2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The authors make sensible design choices. Using a strong, pre-trained network (Maxout) as a base allows them to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism. The choice of SNES to train the high-dimensional policy is a practical solution to a difficult RL problem where standard policy gradient methods might struggle. The formulation of the state, action, and reward (with its boosting-like term for hard examples) is clear and well-justified.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experiments:** The evaluation is a significant strength. Beyond just reporting improved accuracy, the authors provide several analyses that build confidence in their claims:
    *   The qualitative analysis of the cat/dog misclassification (Fig. 3) provides a concrete, albeit difficult to interpret, example of the model in action.
    *   The dynamics study, showing that a model trained for T=2 steps remains stable when run for more steps, is an important check on the robustness of the learned policy.
    *   The experiment demonstrating that the final gate activations alone contain significant class-relevant information is particularly clever and effectively validates that the RL process is learning a meaningful feature-reweighting strategy, not just noise.

4.  **Significant Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear performance improvement (6% relative on CIFAR-100) over a very strong baseline. Achieving a new state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark like CIFAR-100 is a significant accomplishment and underscores the practical value of the proposed method.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The proposed method is computationally very expensive. Training requires a fully trained base CNN followed by a lengthy (4 days) RL phase. More critically, inference requires T=5 forward passes through the network, making it 5x slower than the already-expensive base model. This is a major practical drawback. Furthermore, the scalability of the approach to deeper and wider networks (e.g., for ImageNet) is questionable. The policy parameterization (a single weight matrix) and the state vector size would grow quadratically and linearly, respectively, with the number of feature maps, making the RL problem even harder.

2.  **Limited Sequential Decision Making:** The network is run for a small, fixed number of steps (T=5). While framed as a sequential process, it is not clear if the system is learning a true temporal policy or a complex one-step correction mechanism. The policy takes the state at step `t` to produce an action for `t+1`, but the process terminates before any long-term credit assignment becomes necessary. It would be interesting to see if there is benefit to a much larger T or a dynamic stopping criterion.

3.  **Clarity on Policy Implementation:** The policy network uses a softmax function scaled by the action space dimensionality, causing the action vector to *average* to 1. This is a non-standard choice compared to the typical softmax which *sums* to 1. While the authors state this is to keep activations stable, the rationale is not fully explored. A standard softmax would preserve the total "attentional energy," while this formulation allows the policy to globally amplify or suppress all features. A more detailed justification or ablation study on this design choice would strengthen the paper.

#### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by the experiments provided. The authors are honest about the difficulty in interpreting the learned filter modulations. The work is a complete contribution, not a preliminary idea.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 is very helpful for understanding the training loop. The description of how the state vector `o_t` is constructed is clear. The one minor point of confusion is the non-standard softmax in the policy network, as mentioned above.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While attention and recurrence in neural networks were not new ideas, the specific formulation of learning a policy for *internal* feature modulation within a deep CNN was a novel and impactful contribution. It clearly differentiates itself from related work.
*   **Significance:** The results are important. This paper was one of the early works demonstrating that incorporating mechanisms like attention and recurrence directly into modern deep learning architectures could push the state of the art. The ideas presented have likely influenced subsequent work on attention mechanisms, dynamic computation, and adaptive networks (e.g., self-attention, dynamic routing).

---

#### **Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and well-motivated architecture combining deep learning and RL for internal attention.
*   Achieves a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-100).
*   The experimental analysis is thorough and provides strong evidence supporting the claims, particularly the analysis of the information content in the learned gates.
*   The work is significant and likely to inspire future research into adaptive computation in deep networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method has a very high computational cost for both training and inference, limiting its practical applicability.
*   There are open questions about the scalability of the approach to larger-scale problems like ImageNet.
*   The sequential aspect of the model is limited (T=5), making it feel more like a single-step refinement than a true multi-step reasoning process.

#### **Recommendation**

Despite the weaknesses related to computational cost and scalability, the novelty of the core idea, the strength of the experimental validation, and the significance of the results strongly support acceptance. This paper introduces a new and exciting way to think about computation in deep networks. The weaknesses are more limitations of the initial implementation than fundamental flaws in the concept. I recommend **acceptance**.
