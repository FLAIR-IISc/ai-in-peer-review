This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for iteratively refining CNN classifications via reinforcement learning to modulate filter activations. The core idea – allowing a CNN to "re-think" its initial classification by selectively emphasizing or suppressing filters – is inspired by human visual processing. While the motivation is plausible, and the authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10/100, I find the work ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of rigorous comparison and justification for the chosen methodology.

The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explains the dasNet architecture. The connection to existing work on recurrent processing and attention mechanisms is adequately addressed. However, the motivation feels somewhat hand-wavy. The analogy to an ornithologist needing multiple passes to identify similar bird species, while evocative, doesn’t inherently justify the complexity of adding a reinforcement learning component on top of an already trained CNN. 

My primary concern lies with the methodological choices. The use of Separable NES (SNES) for policy optimization, while acknowledged as a pragmatic necessity due to the high dimensionality of the action space, raises red flags. The authors state SNES is “theoretically less powerful” than standard NES but don’t adequately address *how* this limitation might impact the learned policy. Are we finding a truly optimal attentional strategy, or simply a locally optimal one due to the constraints of SNES? Furthermore, the reinforcement learning framework feels tacked on. The reward function, solely based on correct/incorrect classification, is simplistic and doesn’t explicitly encourage interpretable attentional behavior.

The experimental evaluation is also insufficient. While dasNet outperforms a baseline Maxout network, the comparison is not comprehensive. The paper states it achieves a "new state-of-the-art result," but only mentions performance on *unaugmented* data. Given the prevalence of data augmentation in modern image classification, results on augmented data are crucial. More importantly, there is no comparison to other attention mechanisms, such as those integrated directly within the CNN architecture (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation networks, CBAM). These approaches achieve attention *without* the added complexity of reinforcement learning and a separate policy network. Demonstrating superiority over such methods is essential to justify the proposed approach. The analysis of the learned attentional weights (Figure 3) is qualitative and doesn’t provide strong evidence that the network is learning meaningful, interpretable attention patterns. The claim that the gates contain “significant information” based on a 15-nearest neighbor and logistic regression experiment is weak and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the policy is learning a useful representation.

The dynamics experiments (Figure 2) showing performance saturation after T steps are interesting but don’t address the fundamental question of whether the learned policy is truly beneficial or simply exploiting artifacts in the training data. The observed stability beyond T steps could simply indicate that the network has reached a point of diminishing returns, rather than a truly optimized attentional state.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of reinforcement learning to modulate existing CNN features.
*   Demonstrated performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous comparison to existing attention mechanisms.
*   Justification for SNES is weak; potential impact on policy optimality is not addressed.
*   Simplistic reward function and limited interpretability of learned attention.
*   Insufficient experimental validation; lack of results on augmented data.
*   Qualitative analysis of attention weights does not provide compelling evidence of meaningful learning.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting idea, but falls short of convincingly demonstrating its benefits. The reliance on a potentially suboptimal optimization algorithm (SNES), the lack of comprehensive comparison to existing attention methods, and the limited experimental validation leave me unconvinced of the significance of this work. I recommend **rejection**, with encouragement to address the concerns outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, the authors should explore more powerful optimization methods, compare against state-of-the-art attention mechanisms, and provide a more thorough analysis of the learned attentional policies.
