Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal selective attention via feedback connections. After training a standard Maxout CNN, the authors freeze its weights and learn a separate policy that, over T recurrent passes on the same image, multiplicatively gates each feature map in each layer. The observation to the policy consists of global statistics of intermediate activations (map-wise means), pre-softmax activations, and current class probabilities; the policy outputs per-map positive gains (via a scaled softmax). Because the action/observation spaces are high-dimensional (≈10^3) and the policy has ≈10^6 parameters, they evolve the policy with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted log-loss that emphasizes misclassified training examples. On CIFAR-10/100 the method improves over a strong Maxout baseline, with qualitative evidence that the recurrent feedback can flip some ambiguous predictions (e.g., dog→cat).

This connects to a large body of neuroscience-inspired top-down modulation and prior ML work on attention and feedback: RL for saccades/glimpses, recurrent processing/recirculation, and iterative inference. Compared to prior glimpsing models (e.g., Larochelle & Hinton; Denil et al.), dasNet does not move a fovea but learns internal, feature-wise gating of a modern CNN. Compared to generic RNNs, the recurrence is structured as multiplicative feature-map modulation, learned by a separate policy. The use of black-box NES to scale high-dimensional policy search for attention in large CNNs is also a distinct design choice.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clear, high-level idea—learn internal, top-down, per-feature-map attention to iteratively “think twice” at test time. This is a fresh direction relative to purely feedforward CNNs and contemporaneous attention models that focus on spatial glimpsing.
- Conceptual elegance: The mechanism is simple (feature-wise gains) yet expressive, and the observation design is pragmatic. The multi-pass loop is easy to plug onto existing CNNs.
- Technical feasibility at scale: Using SNES to handle ≈10^6 policy parameters is a thoughtful choice that makes the approach practical; the paper explains the rationale.
- Empirical signal: Improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; dynamics analysis shows performance peaks around the trained T and remains stable beyond, suggesting well-behaved recurrence.
- Interpretability angle: The method surfaces gate activations whose final-state values alone contain class-discriminative information, indicating the policy is meaningfully using its degrees of freedom.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/reproducibility gaps: Key details are deferred to supplementary material (SNES update specifics, policy architecture hyperparameters). Table 1 with exact numbers isn’t included in the text provided; claims such as “new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” conflict with earlier mention of data augmentation in training. Please reconcile and report exact test errors with means/std across seeds, and specify augmentation protocols for fair comparison.
- Limited baselines/ablations:
  - No comparison to alternative training of the same gating mechanism via gradient-based RL (REINFORCE), BPTT with straight-through estimators, or a deterministic MLP trained by backprop to map observations to gates on a validation-set objective.
  - No comparison to recurrent/attention baselines (e.g., a small ConvRNN/ConvLSTM on top of Maxout; spatial transformer networks—if contemporaneous; or a multi-pass inference heuristic that averages logits).
  - Ablations of the observation vector (remove class probs, remove intermediate activations, etc.) and of which layers are gated would strengthen causal claims.
- Efficiency and scalability: Test-time cost scales with T feedforward passes; training requires evolving a million-parameter policy, taking multiple days. A quantitative analysis of compute overhead vs. accuracy, and results on larger images/datasets would help assess practicality.
- Overfitting/validation: The policy is evolved on training data with a boosted loss for misclassified samples; it’s unclear how validation is used for early stopping or selection, and whether the gains hold under multiple random seeds.
- Scope of improvements: The reported relative 6% improvement is promising, but absolute numbers, comparison to contemporary SOTA (e.g., NIN, stochastic pooling, DropConnect), and whether gains persist when ensembling/bagging baselines are used need to be explicit.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a reasonable optimization setup and careful design decisions. However, the empirical section is thinner than ideal on baselines, ablations, and compute analysis. Reproducibility would benefit from more detail and exact numbers.

Clarity
The narrative is clear and well-motivated. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., indices in Eq. 1–5; action normalization rationale). Please include the missing table, hyperparameters for the policy network, SNES settings, and the exact observation dimensionality.

Originality
High. Learning internal, per-feature-map attention via a separate policy trained with NES, applied iteratively to a modern CNN, is a distinctive combination. The framing of top-down, non-stationary behavior post-training is compelling.

Significance
Moderate to high. The idea opens a direction—test-time adaptive internal modulation—that others can build on (e.g., spatially-aware gates, differentiable training, meta-learning). While experiments are on CIFAR, the concept is broadly applicable. Stronger baselines and scaling evidence would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report exact CIFAR-10/100 test errors (mean±std over multiple runs), and clarify augmentation. Compare against contemporary SOTA under identical augmentation.
- Add baselines: (i) same gating but trained by backprop with a held-out validation objective, (ii) ConvRNN/ConvLSTM on top of Maxout, (iii) spatial attention model if feasible, (iv) a simple two-pass heuristic (e.g., increase margin on top-2 classes).
- Ablate observation components and gated layers; analyze the effect of T and of policy capacity.
- Provide compute metrics: train/test time vs. baseline; memory overhead.
- Include validation protocol to mitigate overfitting due to boosted loss on misclassified training samples.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel mechanism for internal selective attention with clear neuroscientific motivation.
- Simple, general plug-in to strong CNNs; conceptually influential.
- Empirical gains and stable dynamics; promising qualitative evidence.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing experimental details and limited baselines/ablations weaken the empirical case.
- Scalability and compute implications not fully quantified.
- Claims about SOTA need precise, fair comparisons.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-leaning positive). The central idea is novel and likely to stimulate follow-up work, and the paper demonstrates tangible gains. I would not hold this back for missing ablations if the authors can tighten experimental reporting and add at least one stronger baseline in the camera-ready.

Confidence
Moderate.
