Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with internal, multiplicative gates on each feature map (across convolutional and maxout layers). At test time, an image is processed for T passes. After each pass, a policy observes summary statistics of network activations (mean activation per map, pre-softmax layer activations, and current class probabilities) and outputs a new action vector that scales each feature map for the next pass. The policy is trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), treating the entire T-step process as a black-box optimization with a boosted cross-entropy loss emphasizing misclassified examples. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet is reported to improve accuracy over a strong Maxout baseline, with a claimed relative 6% improvement and “state-of-the-art” for unaugmented data. The paper relates this idea to top-down attention and recurrence in biological vision and to prior RL/attention models.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Maxout CNNs and dropout and draws motivation from biological feedback pathways and prior work on attention via RL and recurrence in vision. Compared to prior RL attention works that learn saccades or glimpses, dasNet’s novelty lies in internal, global, per-feature-map gating over multiple passes, with the policy driven by network-internal observations. It is also related to classical recurrent CNNs and iterative inference but differs by optimizing a separate non-differentiable policy with SNES rather than backpropagating through time. The related work section is broad, though some contemporaneous deep recurrent/attention models trained with gradient-based methods would be valuable comparators.

Quality
- Method: The core mechanism is simple and technically sound: apply positive, normalized per-map gates (softmax scaled to keep the average gate at 1) and iterate. The observation vector is a plausible sufficient statistic for selecting actions, though it discards spatial information, restricting attention to global channel reweighting.
- Policy learning: Using SNES to optimize a ∼10^6-parameter linear policy is reasonable given the black-box objective, but the choice is not compared to gradient-based alternatives (e.g., backprop through time on differentiable gates, or policy gradients/REINFORCE). Since gating and the network are differentiable, a first-order baseline is an important omission.
- Objective and dynamics: The boosted loss that upweights misclassified examples is sensible for focusing on hard cases. The stability analysis (training for T=1–2 and evaluating for more steps) is a nice touch showing dynamics do not diverge.
- Experiments: The empirical section is the weakest part:
  - Exact absolute error rates, standard deviations, and full Table 1 are not in the text; only a relative improvement is quoted. Statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported.
  - The SOTA claim “for unaugmented data” is confusing because the Maxout baseline is trained with data augmentation; this needs clarification and fair comparison to contemporaneous baselines with/without augmentation and ensembles.
  - Missing ablations: per-layer gating vs. all layers; single-pass gating (T=1) vs. multi-pass; using a nonlinear policy; gating without SNES (learned by backprop); using only top-layer gates; effect of observation components.
  - Missing efficiency metrics: inference cost multiplies by T; wall-clock overhead and throughput are not reported; training time is heavy (4 days for SNES, excluding CNN pretraining).
  - Missing robustness analysis to random seeds, and per-class confusion improvements to substantiate “fixing ambiguous cases.”
  - Baselines such as ensembling, test-time augmentation, deeper/wider CNNs, or multi-crop evaluation are not compared.
- Qualitative analysis: The single cat/dog example is illustrative but anecdotal; more systematic visualization of gate distributions and their correlation with corrections would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized. The method is clearly described with equations and an algorithm box. However:
- Several implementation details are missing: exact action/observation dimensionalities, policy architecture (appears linear), SNES hyperparameters beyond population size, learning schedule, early stopping, and number of SNES generations.
- The inconsistency about “unaugmented” results needs correction. Provide full numeric tables, confidence intervals, and training/inference costs.
- Some typos and equation index issues are present. Figures/tables referenced (e.g., Table 1, Figure 2/3) should be fully included with captions.

Originality
Moderately original. The idea of iteratively reweighting internal features via learned feedback is a novel twist on attention at the time, especially using global channel-wise gates across multiple layers and passes. Using SNES for high-dimensional policy optimization is less common in deep vision. However, the components (feature reweighting, recurrence, RL) are known, and the paper would benefit from stronger differentiation from gradient-trained recurrent attention and feedback models.

Significance
Conceptually interesting: shows that learned internal feedback can correct hard mistakes after an initial feedforward pass. Practical impact is uncertain given the modest reported gains, lack of strong baselines, and added computational cost at inference. With more rigorous evaluation and comparisons, the approach could influence research on learned feedback and iterative inference.

Pros
- Clear, biologically inspired formulation of internal selective attention via feedback.
- Simple, general mechanism (per-map gates) applicable to many CNNs.
- Demonstrates non-trivial improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline.
- Stability analysis across steps and an information-content sanity check for gates.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient: missing absolute numbers, variance, efficiency, and key baselines/ablations.
- SNES choice is not justified against differentiable training alternatives.
- Observations discard spatial information; attention is limited to global channel reweighting.
- Additional test-time compute (T passes) without reporting trade-offs.

Questions for authors
1. Please report absolute test errors with standard deviations over multiple seeds, and include full Table 1. Clarify the “unaugmented” claim.
2. What are the action/observation dimensions per dataset, and the exact policy architecture? Any nonlinearity beyond a linear map + softmax?
3. What is the runtime overhead (training and inference) relative to the baseline per image? How does accuracy vary with T and cost?
4. How does dasNet compare to: (a) backprop-trained recurrent gating; (b) gating only the top convolutional layer; (c) ensembling multiple Maxout nets; (d) test-time augmentation?
5. Can you provide per-class confusion changes to substantiate improvements on ambiguous pairs?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is compelling and potentially impactful, but the current experimental evidence and comparisons are not sufficient for NIPS. Strengthening the empirical section with rigorous baselines, ablations, complete metrics, and efficiency analysis could move this to accept. Constructive suggestions above aim to help the authors get there.
