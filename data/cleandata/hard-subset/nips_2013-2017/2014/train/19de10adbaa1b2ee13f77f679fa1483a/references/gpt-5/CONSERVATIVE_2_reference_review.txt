Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a modification of a trained convolutional Maxout network that introduces per-feature-map multiplicative “gates” applied across multiple recurrent passes over the same input. A deterministic policy maps observations (average activations of maps, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities) to gate values. The policy is learned post hoc by black-box reinforcement learning (Separable Natural Evolution Strategies, SNES), with a loss emphasizing misclassified examples. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report a relative improvement over a vanilla Maxout CNN and claim new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. Qualitative visualizations suggest that the gates modulate higher-layer features to correct initially ambiguous cases.

Relation to prior work
The work is motivated by biological top-down feedback and selective attention, and relates to recurrent processing in vision, RL for attention/saccades, and iterative inference. The base architecture is Maxout with dropout. The distinctive aspects here are: (i) learned internal per-channel attention applied recurrently; (ii) training the attention policy via SNES rather than gradient-based RL or backprop-through-time; and (iii) use of image-level observations derived from internal activations. While recurrent modulations and RL-for-attention have prior art, combining them to control internal CNN gates across passes on competitive vision benchmarks is a useful direction.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple mechanism: per-channel multiplicative gates with an observation-to-action mapping; easy to integrate into existing CNNs.
- Biological motivation is clear and well argued; the approach operationalizes top-down modulation.
- Empirical results indicate non-trivial gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; qualitative examples are informative.
- Stability analysis across T steps suggests reasonable dynamical behavior beyond the training horizon.
- The paper is generally readable and provides algorithmic details at a high level.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evidence is limited and missing critical ablations:
  • No comparison against simpler alternatives such as (a) static per-channel scaling learned by supervised fine-tuning, (b) end-to-end backprop-through-time to learn gates without SNES, (c) policy learned with a standard policy gradient, or (d) iterative test-time ensembling or confidence-based calibration.
  • No comparison to training a recurrent CNN with feedback connections optimized by SGD on the same objective (the direct baseline for a recurrent attention mechanism).
- The observation and action design appears ad hoc and constrained: gates are positive and normalized via a scaled softmax, which couples all channels and may limit expressivity; the observation discards spatial information (only averages). It is not shown that these choices are necessary or optimal.
- Methodological clarity issues:
  • The loss definition uses overloaded notation for d (true label vs argmax), and Eq. (10) is unclear/inconsistent.
  • The claim of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” conflicts with the earlier statement that data augmentation was used to train the Maxout model. Clarify what “unaugmented” refers to (no test-time augmentation?).
  • Hyperparameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2, T, batch sizes n, etc.) look hand-tuned; selection protocol and validation set usage are not described.
  • SNES update details are relegated to supplementary; without them reproducibility is reduced.
- Experimental reporting:
  • Absolute numbers, standard deviations, and statistical significance are not provided; only a claimed “6% relative improvement.” The table referenced is not visible here, and it is unclear whether comparisons are apples-to-apples (same data preprocessing/augmentation, parameter counts, training budgets).
  • No per-class confusion improvements or analysis of whether “easy” cases are harmed by recurrent passes.
  • Only one qualitative example; more evidence is needed to support the attention narrative.
- Computational cost and scalability: The policy has ~1M parameters and is trained via black-box optimization with population size 50 over multiple passes. Training time is reported only for one GPU and excludes base CNN training; the cost-accuracy tradeoff and scaling to larger datasets/models remain unclear.
- Risk of overfitting: The RL phase appears to optimize on the training set with boosted loss on misclassified instances; no clear early stopping/validation protocol is described.

Quality
The idea is technically plausible and the mechanism is implementable, but the empirical validation is not sufficiently thorough to substantiate strong claims. Key baselines and ablations are missing, reporting lacks rigor (numbers, variance, reproducibility details), and some notational inconsistencies remain.

Clarity
Generally well written, but sections 3–4 would benefit from clearer notation (esp. Eqs. 8–10) and a more precise description of dimensions for observations/actions. Explicitly list action dimensionality per layer, batch size n, the number of SNES generations, and stopping criteria. Clarify the “unaugmented” claim.

Originality
Moderate. The combination—internal per-channel attention controlled by a learned policy via SNES on top of a fixed CNN—is a novel twist on recurrent attention, though components are known. The decision to use NES due to dimensionality is interesting but not strongly justified relative to gradient-based alternatives.

Significance
Potentially interesting if convincingly shown to improve SOTA on competitive benchmarks with careful baselines. As presented, improvements are promising but insufficiently validated. Without ablations and broader comparisons, it is hard to assess generality or impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Compare against: (i) static learned gates trained by SGD on the same boosted loss; (ii) end-to-end BPTT training of gates; (iii) policy gradient with a smaller policy; (iv) iterative test-time ensembling/temperature scaling.
- Provide full experimental details: exact accuracies with confidence intervals over multiple runs; training/validation splits; SNES generations and convergence; sensitivity to T and λ parameters.
- Analyze whether recurrent passes degrade easy cases; report accuracy as a function of initial confidence.
- Justify the scaled softmax for gates; consider independent sigmoid/tanh with mean-one normalization to decouple channels. Provide an ablation.
- Include more qualitative examples and per-class confusion analyses.
- Clarify the “unaugmented” claim and ensure fair baseline comparisons.

Pros
- Simple, biologically motivated mechanism for internal selective attention.
- Works on top of strong CNNs; promising gains on CIFAR-10/100.
- Interesting application of NES to high-dimensional policy learning.
- Initial analysis of stability with multiple recurrent passes.

Cons
- Missing key baselines and ablations; limited empirical rigor.
- Ambiguities in loss definition and experimental protocol.
- Computational cost/scalability and reproducibility not convincingly addressed.
- Observation/action design is somewhat ad hoc and unvalidated.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is intriguing and potentially valuable, but the current empirical evidence and methodological clarity are insufficient for a strong endorsement. With thorough ablations, clearer reporting, and stronger baselines, the work could be significantly strengthened.
