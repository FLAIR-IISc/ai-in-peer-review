This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to improving CNN performance by incorporating feedback connections and learning an attentional policy via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies). The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its activations by emphasizing or suppressing filters on subsequent passes through the image, addressing difficult classification instances.

The work builds on a strong foundation of previous research in CNNs (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), maxout networks (Goodfellow, 2013), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018). It also connects to the broader understanding of human visual processing, where feedback connections are crucial for attention and accurate perception (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). The authors appropriately situate their work within this context, referencing relevant studies on visual attention and recurrent processing. The use of SNES for policy optimization is justified by the high dimensionality of the action space, acknowledging the limitations of conventional RL methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections *and* reinforcement learning to learn an attentional policy for internal CNN filters is a genuinely novel contribution. This goes beyond simply adding attention mechanisms to existing architectures, proposing a dynamic, post-training adaptation process.
* **Soundness:** The methodology appears technically sound. The use of Maxout networks provides a strong baseline, and the adaptation of SNES for this task is well-motivated. The experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 provide a reasonable empirical evaluation.
* **Significance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the challenging CIFAR datasets demonstrates the potential impact of this approach. The idea of allowing a network to “think” through difficult cases could be valuable for applications requiring high accuracy and robustness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are explained clearly, and the mathematical notation is understandable. The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) is particularly helpful.
* **Complete Work:** This feels like a fairly complete piece of work. The authors have clearly defined the problem, proposed a solution, and provided empirical evidence to support their claims. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The training process, requiring 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, is computationally expensive. This limits the scalability of the approach to larger datasets and more complex architectures. The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss strategies for reducing this cost.
* **Limited Analysis of the Policy:** While the authors show *that* the policy is effective, they provide limited insight into *how* it works. What specific features are being emphasized or suppressed? Are there patterns in the learned attention weights? More detailed analysis of the learned policy would be beneficial. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is a good start, but more systematic investigation is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The success of the approach may be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., T, λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2). The paper doesn't explore the impact of these parameters extensively, potentially overlooking optimal configurations.
* **Comparison to Existing Attention Mechanisms:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from existing attention mechanisms used in CNNs (e.g., spatial attention, channel attention). A more thorough comparison to these approaches, highlighting the unique benefits of dasNet, would strengthen the contribution.
* **Restricted Scope of RL:** The paper acknowledges using a deterministic policy. Exploring stochastic policies, while potentially more complex, could offer increased robustness and exploration capabilities.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance.
* Achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR datasets.
* Demonstrates the potential for dynamic, post-training adaptation of CNNs.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* High computational cost limits scalability.
* Limited analysis of the learned policy.
* Lack of a thorough comparison to existing attention mechanisms.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity is not adequately addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to improving CNN performance. While there are weaknesses, particularly regarding computational cost and analysis of the learned policy, the strengths outweigh the drawbacks. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning and warrants acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Investigate techniques for reducing the computational cost of training, such as parallelization or more efficient RL algorithms.
* Conduct a more detailed analysis of the learned policy, visualizing and interpreting the attention weights to understand how the network is adapting its behavior.
* Perform a more thorough comparison to existing attention mechanisms, clearly demonstrating the advantages of dasNet.
* Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis to identify optimal configurations and assess the robustness of the approach.
* Explore the potential benefits of using stochastic policies.
* Expand experiments to larger and more complex datasets beyond CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
