**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a pre-trained convolutional neural network (a Maxout network) with a feedback mechanism to improve classification performance. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple passes over a single image. On each pass, a control policy, learned via reinforcement learning, modulates the activations of the network's feature maps. This mechanism is intended to mimic a form of selective attention, where the network can "re-examine" an image by emphasizing or suppressing certain internal features to resolve ambiguity in difficult classification cases. The control policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) after the base CNN has been trained via standard supervised learning. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data on the latter.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty:** The central concept of learning a sequential, internal attention policy on top of a fixed, pre-trained feature extractor is compelling and novel. It provides a principled way to incorporate recurrent processing to refine an initial feedforward guess, which is a departure from typical end-to-end recurrent vision models.

2.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of "difficult" classification examples—those that a standard feedforward pass might misclassify. The proposed two-stage training process (supervised pre-training followed by RL-based fine-tuning of the attention policy) is a logical and practical approach to this problem.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvement on CIFAR-100 is significant. Achieving a 6% relative improvement over a strong baseline (Maxout network) demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method and suggests it is more than a proof-of-concept.

4.  **Analysis:** The experiment analyzing the information content of the final attention gates is a clever and insightful piece of analysis, providing evidence that the learned policy is indeed capturing class-relevant information.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel idea and strong results, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its technical presentation, methodological justification, and formatting, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision:**
    *   **Errors in Equations:** Several key equations appear to contain errors. In Equation (1) and (5), the summation `i=c∑ i=0` runs over `c+1` terms for `c` input maps. Similarly, in Equations (2) and (3), the max operation is taken over `b+1` terms (`i=0..b`) for a block size of `b`. These off-by-one errors in fundamental model definitions are concerning.
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** In Algorithm 1 (line 12) and Equation (11), the loss/fitness formulation is confusing. The index `i` is used for both the policy sample and the summation over images, and the pseudocode implies that the per-image loss `Li` is overwritten in each step of the inner loop, which seems incorrect.

2.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:**
    *   **Choice of RL Algorithm:** The paper dismisses standard RL methods as "impractical" due to the large parameter space and opts for SNES. This claim requires stronger justification. Why not policy gradient methods like REINFORCE, which are commonly used to train neural network policies? A comparative discussion is needed.
    *   **State Representation:** The observation vector `o_t` is formed by, among other things, the *average* activation of each output map. This design choice discards all spatial information from the feature maps, which is a critical source of information in computer vision. The authors' claim that this is "meaningful enough" is unsubstantiated and seems like a major limitation of the approach.
    *   **Attention Mechanism:** The attention mechanism is coarse, applying a single scalar weight `a_lj` to an entire output feature map (Eq. 5). It is not clear why this simple approach was chosen over more expressive, spatially-aware attention mechanisms.
    *   **Reward Sparsity:** The reward is only provided at the final timestep `T`. This creates a very difficult temporal credit assignment problem for the RL algorithm. The paper does not justify why an intermediate reward signal was not used to potentially ease the learning process.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   The paper's description of the modified softmax function (Eq. 7) is brief. The claim that scaling by `dim(A)` "keep[s] the filter activations stable" is not well-explained, especially since a standard softmax also produces positive outputs.
    *   The qualitative analysis of Figure 3 is highly speculative ("Some filters focus more on surroundings whilst others de-emphasise the eyes"). The authors concede that "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect," which highlights a lack of interpretability that is not adequately addressed.
    *   The observation that performance drops after the trained number of steps `T` (Figure 2) is interesting but left completely unexplained.

4.  **Formatting and Professionalism:**
    *   The list of references is in a state of disarray. Numerous entries are incomplete, missing years, venues, page numbers, and are riddled with formatting errors (e.g., dangling commas). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference like NIPS.
    *   The acknowledgement that another person provided the literature review for the Related Work section is highly unorthodox and raises questions about the authors' contributions to the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and promising direction for improving deep networks using learned recurrent attention.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Significant lack of technical precision, including apparent errors in core equations and pseudocode.
*   Poor justification for key methodological choices (RL algorithm, state representation, attention mechanism).
*   The paper's presentation is marred by a profoundly unprofessional and incomplete reference list.
*   The analysis of the model's behavior is superficial and leaves key questions unanswered.

**Decision**

While the core idea presented in this paper is intriguing and the results appear strong, the execution and presentation are deeply flawed. The numerous technical inaccuracies, poorly justified design choices, and unacceptable state of the reference list prevent a confident assessment of the work's validity and reproducibility. The paper feels premature and is not yet ready for publication at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**, with the strong suggestion that the authors perform a thorough revision to address the technical precision and clarity issues before resubmission to another venue.
