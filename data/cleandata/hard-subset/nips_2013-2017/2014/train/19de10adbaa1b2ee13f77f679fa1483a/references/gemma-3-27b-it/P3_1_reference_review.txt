## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections (dasNet)

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and ambitious approach to injecting a form of dynamic attention into deep convolutional networks via reinforcement learning. The core idea of allowing a network to iteratively refine its classifications by modulating filter activations is well-motivated by analogies to human visual processing and addresses a key limitation of static, feedforward CNNs. While the experiments demonstrate a performance improvement, the paper lacks sufficient detail in certain areas and the methodology feels somewhat ad-hoc, hindering a strong recommendation for acceptance. I lean towards **weak reject** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors propose dasNet, a system that augments a standard Maxout CNN with feedback connections.  These connections are weighted by an attentional policy learned through Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The network iteratively processes an image, adjusting filter activations based on the policy, aiming to improve classification accuracy, particularly on difficult examples. 

The introduction effectively positions the work within the context of successful deep learning architectures and highlights the inspiration drawn from human visual processing, referencing key works on visual attention and recurrent processing (Felleman & Essen, Lamme & Roelfsema). The related work section does a good job of summarizing relevant research in areas like recurrent neural networks for vision (e.g., face localization, image reconstruction), RL for attention mechanisms, and biologically plausible feedback connections.  Crucially, the authors correctly position their work as *applying* this perspective to existing state-of-the-art architectures to demonstrably improve performance, a compelling goal.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:**  The idea of learning an internal attentional mechanism *post-training* using RL to modulate filter activations is a novel contribution.  It moves beyond simply adding attention layers during initial training.
* **Demonstrated Improvement:** The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is encouraging and suggests the approach has merit. Correcting misclassifications via iterative refinement is a strong practical benefit.
* **SNES Justification:**  The authors provide a reasonable justification for choosing SNES over more traditional RL algorithms given the large parameter space.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) offers some insight into the network’s internal dynamics and is a valuable addition, showing that the network isn’t simply amplifying all activations.
* **Connection to Human Vision:** The paper convincingly draws parallels to human visual processing and the benefits of recurrent processing, providing a strong conceptual motivation.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Detail on Policy Network Architecture:** The policy network (πθ) is described only as a neural network mapping observations to actions.  The size, specific architecture (number of layers, activation functions), and training details of this network are missing. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and prevents a proper assessment of the policy learning process.
* **Observation Space Design:** The choice of observation features (average activations, intermediate activations, class probabilities) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks strong justification.  Why were these features chosen specifically?  Was any ablation study performed to assess their importance?  The observation space seems limited and could potentially benefit from more comprehensive state representation.
* **Action Space Design & Scaling:** The authors mention scaling the softmax output of the action network to average to 1.  While a reasonable design choice, the rationale behind this decision isn't fully explained.  The impact of this scaling on learning dynamics and filter activations warrants further discussion.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity. How sensitive is the performance to the learning rate of SNES, the population size, or the reward shaping parameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified)? 
* **Computational Cost:** Training takes 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti.  This is a significant computational cost and raises questions about the scalability of the approach to larger datasets and more complex networks.  A discussion of computational requirements is necessary.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the dynamics experiment (Figure 2) is helpful, more comprehensive ablation studies are needed. For instance, how does the performance vary with different numbers of iterative passes (T)?
* **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. The results don't necessarily generalize to more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet).

**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel approach to incorporating dynamic attention into CNNs.
* Achieves a measurable performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* Is well-motivated by analogies to human visual processing.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks sufficient detail in several key areas (policy network architecture, observation space design).
* The methodology feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a thorough hyperparameter analysis.
* Computational cost is high, and scalability is unclear.
* Limited evaluation on datasets beyond CIFAR-10/100.



**5.  Specific Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

* Please provide a detailed description of the policy network architecture (layers, activation functions, size).
* Justify the choice of observation features and consider exploring alternative or more informative representations.
* Discuss the rationale behind the softmax scaling in the action space.
* Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results.
*  Address the computational cost and scalability concerns.
*  Consider evaluating the approach on a larger and more complex dataset (e.g., a subset of ImageNet).
*  Provide more details on the implementation of SNES, including any optimizations used.




**Overall, while dasNet presents a promising direction, the lack of detail and limited scope of the experiments prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.**
