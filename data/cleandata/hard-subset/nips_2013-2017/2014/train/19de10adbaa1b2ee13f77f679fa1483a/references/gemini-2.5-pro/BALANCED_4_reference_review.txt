### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel method for incorporating an iterative, attention-like mechanism into deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Drawing inspiration from the human visual system, which employs both a fast, initial feedforward pass and a slower, more deliberate attentional phase for visual processing, the authors propose a model that can "re-think" its initial predictions for difficult images.

The method begins with a standard, pre-trained feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network). This network is then augmented with modulatory "gates" that can scale the output of each feature map in every layer. An external control policy, implemented as a linear neural network, is trained using Reinforcement Learning (RL) to dynamically adjust these gates over several passes (`T` steps) of the same input image. At each step, the policy observes an internal state representation of the CNN (composed of feature map activations and output probabilities) and outputs an action, which is the set of gate values for the next pass. The policy is optimized using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method chosen for its ability to handle the high-dimensional parameter space of the policy. The objective is to find a policy that iteratively refines the network's activations to correct initial misclassifications. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this approach improves classification accuracy over the strong Maxout network baseline, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 at the time of publication.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**

*   **Strengths:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Given the challenge of training such a dynamic, recurrent system, the proposed solution of decoupling the training of the base CNN (via supervised learning) and the control policy (via RL) is a pragmatic and effective choice. The use of SNES is well-justified for the large, non-differentiable policy optimization problem. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating a clear and significant improvement over a powerful baseline on established benchmarks.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation, while showing positive results, could be more thorough. The work is evaluated only on the CIFAR datasets. While challenging, these are small-image datasets, and it remains unclear how the approach would scale, both in performance and computational cost, to larger-scale problems like ImageNet. The paper also lacks ablation studies that would provide deeper insight into the method's components. For example, the contribution of the "boosting" mechanism (`lambda_boost`) or the sensitivity to the number of passes `T` is not systematically investigated. Finally, the analysis of *what* the policy learns is interesting but primarily qualitative; the cat-vs-dog example is illustrative but does not lead to generalizable principles about the learned attentional strategies.

**Clarity:**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and generally easy to follow. The core idea is communicated effectively, and the connection to concepts from neuroscience is articulated well, providing strong context. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the training procedure.
*   **Weaknesses:** Certain key details required for reproducibility are missing. The specific architecture of the base Maxout network (e.g., number of filters, kernel sizes) is not provided. The composition and dimensionality of the state vector `o_t` used as input to the policy could also be specified more precisely.

**Originality:**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is highly original. The central concept of using an RL-trained policy to control the internal modulatory gates of a deep network for iterative refinement was a novel contribution at the time. It represents a significant departure from the purely feedforward models that dominated computer vision and introduces a compelling new paradigm for integrating attention and sequential processing. This work can be seen as an early and creative exploration of ideas that have since become central to the field, albeit approached from a unique RL perspective.

**Significance:**

*   **Strengths:** The work is significant for several reasons. First, it demonstrates that moving beyond single-pass, feedforward architectures can yield tangible performance gains. Second, it presents a successful and novel application of reinforcement learning to control the internal computation of a neural network, opening a promising avenue for future research. The achievement of state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark validates the approach and ensures that the ideas presented are likely to be influential for researchers working on attention, recurrence, and meta-learning in deep networks.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Interest:** The paper proposes a genuinely new and thought-provoking architecture that combines CNNs, recurrence, and RL in a compelling way.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10, demonstrating its effectiveness over a very strong baseline.
3.  **Significant Research Direction:** The work opens up a new paradigm for "internal attention" and iterative processing that is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments against:**
1.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The evaluation is confined to CIFAR datasets, leaving questions about scalability and generalizability unanswered.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic analysis of its components to understand why the method works.
3.  **High Computational Cost:** The training of the policy is extremely computationally expensive (4 days on a GPU), which may limit its practical utility and adoption.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a fascinating core idea. My recommendation leans towards acceptance. The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the manuscript for its final version and to guide future work in this area:

1.  **Clarify Experimental Details:** To improve reproducibility, please provide the full architectural details of the base Maxout network. Also, please clarify the claim regarding data augmentation in Table 1. The text states the base model was trained *with* augmentation, but the table caption seems to imply a comparison on unaugmented data. A precise statement would resolve this ambiguity.
2.  **Strengthen the Analysis:** The qualitative analysis is a good start, but a more quantitative analysis of the learned policy would be valuable. For example, could you analyze the average attention patterns (the `a` vectors) for specific classes or for images that were initially misclassified but later corrected? This could reveal systematic strategies learned by the policy.
3.  **Discuss Alternatives and Limitations:** A brief discussion on alternative approaches could add valuable context. For instance, could this recurrent process be made end-to-end differentiable and trained with backpropagation through time? Acknowledging this possibility and explaining why the RL approach was chosen (e.g., stability, exploration, non-differentiable state representation) would strengthen the paper. Acknowledging the computational cost as a limitation and suggesting potential avenues for making it more efficient would also be welcome.
4.  **Add Ablation Studies (if possible):** While I understand that running new experiments may not be feasible during the rebuttal period, future versions of this work would benefit greatly from ablations. Investigating the impact of the number of steps `T`, the composition of the state vector `o_t`, and the boosting factor in the reward function would provide crucial insights into the model's behavior.
