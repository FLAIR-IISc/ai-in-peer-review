### **Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard, pre-trained feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable the network to perform multiple, iterative passes over a single input image, dynamically modulating the activity of its convolutional filters on each pass. This modulation is framed as a form of internal, selective attention, allowing the network to "re-think" its initial, fast prediction by emphasizing or suppressing specific features to resolve ambiguity. The attentional mechanism is implemented as a control policy, which maps the network's internal state (activations and outputs) to a set of weights applied to the filter maps. This policy is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—after the base CNN has been trained via conventional supervised learning. The authors demonstrate their approach on a Maxout network, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 by correcting misclassifications on difficult examples.

The work is well-situated in the context of both neuroscience, drawing inspiration from the recurrent and top-down connections in the visual cortex, and prior machine learning research on attention and recurrent processing. However, it distinguishes itself by applying these concepts directly to a state-of-the-art deep learning model to push classification performance, rather than demonstrating them on simpler, proof-of-concept tasks.

### **Strengths (Reasons to Accept)**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual contribution. It challenges the dominant paradigm of purely feedforward inference in deep recognition models. The idea of a network that can iteratively refine its own internal representations through a learned, top-down attentional policy is a significant and exciting departure from conventional architectures. This is not an incremental improvement but a proposal for a fundamentally different mode of computation for deep networks, moving them from static function approximators to dynamic, deliberative systems.

2.  **Creative Synthesis of Fields:** The paper does an excellent job of combining ideas from supervised deep learning, reinforcement learning, and computational neuroscience. Using RL to learn a control policy for the internal mechanics of a CNN is a highly original approach. This cross-pollination is precisely the kind of creative work that can open up new and fruitful research directions.

3.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The analogy to human perception—a fast, initial "glimpse" followed by slower, more deliberate attentional processing—is compelling and provides a strong conceptual foundation for the work. The goal of specifically targeting "difficult" or ambiguous classification cases that fool a standard feedforward pass is well-posed and addresses a key limitation of existing models.

4.  **Demonstrated Efficacy:** Despite the methodological challenges, the approach works. The authors show that dasNet can take a high-performing, pre-trained network and improve its accuracy, achieving a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100. The qualitative example in Figure 3, showing the network correcting its initial "dog" classification to "cat" over several steps, provides a powerful illustration of the proposed mechanism in action.

### **Weaknesses (Points for Improvement)**

1.  **Choice of RL Algorithm:** The use of SNES, a black-box evolution strategy, is a pragmatic choice to handle the million-parameter policy space, but it lacks the elegance and efficiency of gradient-based methods. While understandable, this choice makes the learning process less direct and potentially harder to scale. It would be interesting to see if this framework could be adapted to work with modern policy gradient methods, perhaps by using a more structured or lower-dimensional action space.

2.  **Simplicity of the State/Action Representation:** The state representation is a simple concatenation of averaged activations, and the action directly modulates every filter map. This is a brute-force approach. Future work could explore more sophisticated representations of the network's state or more structured actions (e.g., modulating entire layers, or learning to spatially focus attention within feature maps). However, the fact that the system works even with these simple representations is, in itself, an encouraging result.

3.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are convincing for CIFAR-10/100 but leave open the question of how the approach scales to more complex, higher-resolution datasets like ImageNet. The computational overhead of multiple forward passes (T=5) could become prohibitive for very deep networks. While the current results are sufficient for a proof-of-concept, demonstrating the viability on a larger-scale problem would have made the paper's claims incontrovertible.

4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis of *how* the network modifies its activations is intriguing but remains speculative, as the authors rightly admit ("It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect..."). This is a general problem in deep learning, but it feels particularly acute here where the central claim is about a dynamic attentional process.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that I would argue strongly for accepting. It presents a bold, highly novel, and conceptually rich idea that has the potential to influence the field for years to come. The authors are proposing a new way to think about computation in deep networks, and they provide a working, state-of-the-art implementation to back it up.

The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the implementation details and the scope of the experiments, rather than the core concept. These are not fatal flaws but rather limitations that can be—and likely will be—addressed by future work that builds directly on this paper's foundation. In my view, it is far more valuable for the community to see a paper that introduces a powerful, if not perfectly polished, new idea than one that achieves a minor performance gain through meticulous tuning of an existing architecture. This work prioritizes a significant conceptual leap over methodological perfection, and for that, it should be highly commended. It is thought-provoking, original, and precisely the kind of work NIPS should showcase.
