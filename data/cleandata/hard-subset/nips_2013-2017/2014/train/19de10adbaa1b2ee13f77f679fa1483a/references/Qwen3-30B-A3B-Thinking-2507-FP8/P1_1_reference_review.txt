

# Peer Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

## Summary

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to improve deep CNNs by incorporating selective internal attention through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow a pre-trained feedforward CNN to iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image, where each pass can selectively emphasize or suppress certain filters. This is achieved by learning a reinforcement learning policy (using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) that controls filter weights during successive passes. The authors demonstrate that dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for these datasets.

The paper builds on the well-established Maxout network architecture and connects to prior work on visual cortex connectivity (Felleman and Essen, 1991) and the role of top-down processing in human vision. It also relates to recent work on attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014; Vaswani et al., 2017) but distinguishes itself by focusing on internal selective attention rather than attention over input space, and by using reinforcement learning to learn the attention policy rather than backpropagation.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The methodology is well-motivated and technically sound. The authors clearly articulate the problem (improving classification on difficult cases) and propose a plausible solution using reinforcement learning for attention control. The use of SNES is justified by the large parameter space of the attention policy.

2. **Novelty**: The specific approach of learning a policy to adjust filter weights across multiple passes using RL is novel. While attention mechanisms in deep learning are not new, dasNet's focus on *internal* attention within the network (rather than on the input image) and the use of RL for this purpose represent a meaningful contribution.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental results on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100), with both quantitative results (6% relative improvement) and qualitative analysis (Figure 3) showing how the attention mechanism works. The ablation study on the number of passes (T) is particularly valuable.

4. **Theoretical Foundation**: The work is well-grounded in neuroscience, connecting to the well-established role of top-down processing in visual cortex, which provides a compelling biological motivation for the approach.

5. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is adequately detailed. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Related Work**: While the related work section covers some relevant papers, the comparison with other attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., the work by Xu et al. on attention for image captioning, or the more recent transformer-based approaches) is insufficient. The paper claims to be the first to apply this to state-of-the-art networks, but this claim needs stronger justification by directly comparing to relevant attention mechanisms.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper mentions that training dasNet takes "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU" but doesn't provide a fair comparison to the training time of the base Maxout network. Without this context, it's difficult to assess the practicality of the approach. The computational overhead is significant, and this should be discussed more thoroughly.

3. **Limited Generalization Analysis**: The paper only evaluates on CIFAR-10/100. It would be valuable to see results on other datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate the approach's generalizability. The claim of being a "new state-of-the-art" is limited to these specific datasets.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: While the paper includes a study on the number of passes (T), it lacks ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component (e.g., the attention mechanism vs. the boosting strategy). This makes it difficult to isolate what exactly is driving the performance improvement.

5. **Small Improvement Magnitude**: While a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is noteworthy, the absolute improvement is modest (from ~94% to ~97% accuracy). In the context of recent deep learning work, this might not be considered a substantial improvement, especially given the added complexity of the approach.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and reasonable experimental design. The claims are supported by experimental results, though the comparison with other attention mechanisms could be stronger. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the computational cost), though they could be more explicit about the modest absolute improvement.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the figures (Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3) provide useful visualizations of the approach. The algorithm description is particularly helpful. Some mathematical notation could be clarified (e.g., the observation space construction), but overall the paper is accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of using reinforcement learning to learn a policy for internal selective attention is novel. While there is prior work on attention mechanisms in deep learning, dasNet's specific approach of applying RL to adjust filter weights across multiple passes within a pre-trained network represents a meaningful contribution. The paper adequately differentiates itself from related work, though the comparison could be more thorough.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is somewhat limited by the modest absolute improvement (6% relative on CIFAR-10) and the computational cost. While the idea of "correcting oneself" through multiple passes is conceptually interesting, it's unclear how this would generalize to more challenging tasks or datasets. The paper does advance the state of the art on CIFAR-10/100, but the broader impact is questionable given the limited scope of the evaluation.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel approach to internal selective attention using RL
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks
- Well-motivated by neuroscience
- Clear methodology and good experimental design
- Addresses a meaningful problem (improving classification on difficult cases)

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Modest absolute improvement (6% relative) on standard benchmarks
- Limited evaluation (only CIFAR-10/100)
- Significant computational overhead without clear justification
- Insufficient comparison with related attention mechanisms
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of attention mechanisms in deep learning, and the results on CIFAR-10/100 are compelling. However, the authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by:

1. Providing a more thorough comparison with other attention mechanisms in deep learning
2. Including a fairer comparison of computational cost
3. Adding ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components
4. Discussing the limitations of the approach more explicitly

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an interesting problem. With the suggested revisions, it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the Related Work section, add a more detailed comparison with recent attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., the work by Xu et al. on attention for image captioning, and transformer-based approaches).

2. Add a table comparing the computational cost of dasNet training with the base Maxout network training.

3. Include an ablation study showing the contribution of the attention mechanism versus the boosting strategy.

4. Discuss the limitations of the approach more explicitly, particularly regarding the modest absolute improvement and computational cost.

5. Consider expanding the evaluation to a more challenging dataset (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate broader applicability.

6. Clarify the observation space construction in Section 4 (Equation 7) and provide more detail on how the observation vector is constructed.

7. In the conclusion, explicitly state the practical limitations of the approach and potential directions for future work to address these limitations.

This paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, and with the suggested revisions, it would be significantly stronger.
