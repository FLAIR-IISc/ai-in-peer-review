Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), an augmentation of a trained Maxout CNN with iterative, internal selective attention. At test time, the network reprocesses an image for T passes. After each pass, a policy π maps an observation vector (average feature-map activations across layers, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to an action vector a that multiplicatively gates each feature map for the next pass. The policy is trained after the base CNN is supervised-trained, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize the final-step cross-entropy (with boosted weight for misclassified examples). Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show improvements over the baseline Maxout model and claim a new state-of-the-art for “unaugmented” data.

Relation to prior work
The paper is grounded in extensive neuro/psychophysics literature on recurrent and top-down processing. On the ML side, it relates to recurrent/iterative inference for vision, RL-based attention (glimpses/foveation), and feedback in neural networks. Compared to previous “glimpse” models and other recurrent perceptual systems, the innovation here is internal, feature-wise gating across multiple layers, controlled by a learned policy, and executed over multiple passes on the same image. While there is a large relevant body of work on recurrent processing and dynamic feature modulation, this particular combination—feature-wise multiplicative gating across the entire stack trained by black-box evolution—was not standard at the time and is interesting.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A simple yet general mechanism to implement top-down modulation using per-channel gates that are image-dependent and iteratively updated.
- Orthogonal to architecture: Can be layered on top of a strong feed-forward CNN without changing its weights; complements discriminative training.
- Empirical gains: Shows measurable improvement on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline. The “difficult sample” emphasis is a reasonable target for feedback.
- Stability of dynamics: Preliminary analysis suggests that running more steps than trained does not destabilize predictions.
- Clarity of intuition: The cat-vs-dog example illustrates how gates can shift evidence between confusable classes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological choice (RL vs differentiable training): The entire system is differentiable through time; training the controller by SNES on the final cross-entropy is essentially black-box supervised optimization, not RL in the sequential decision-theoretic sense (no stochastic environment, no partial observability beyond design, no return/discounting). This choice is computationally heavy and under-justified. An ablation with gradient-based training of the controller (e.g., backprop through unrolled T steps) is a crucial baseline.
- Limited controller expressivity: π is effectively a linear map plus softmax to produce positive, mean-1 gates. Given the dimensionality, linearity may be a bottleneck; conversely, it calls into question the need for SNES if a linear controller can be trained via SGD.
- Ambiguous claim about “unaugmented” state-of-the-art: The base CNN is trained “with data augmentation,” yet the text claims SOTA “for unaugmented data.” Please clarify whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time only. Provide exact numbers, with standard deviations and clear settings, and compare to single-model Maxout results under the same train/test protocol.
- Missing baselines:
  - Static per-channel reweighting learned post hoc (no recurrence).
  - A learned dynamic gating controller trained end-to-end with backprop (deterministic or with small MLP).
  - A deeper/wider feedforward CNN with comparable parameter count and compute budget.
  - Multi-pass with no gating (sanity check).
- Incomplete experimental details reduce reproducibility: Numbers of maps per layer, block sizes, learning rates, batch sizes for SNES and SGD, initialization of SNES (means/variances), n (images per generation), T sensitivity, and exact data augmentation protocol are not fully specified.
- Notational issues in the loss: Eq. (10) uses d = ||vT||∞ in a boolean comparison; it likely intends argmax(vT) equals the true class. Eq. (8) has Li = −λboost d log(vT), which is ambiguous unless d is one-hot and the expression denotes the dot-product with log vT. Please correct for clarity.
- Biological claim vs implementation: The “feedback connections” are realized as global per-channel gains that are broadcast and updated between passes via a global controller; there are no learned synaptic top-down connections per se. The paper’s framing sometimes suggests more biologically faithful recurrence than implemented.
- Compute/latency overhead: T passes increase inference time linearly. The paper should report test-time cost and accuracy/compute trade-offs.
- Analysis is mostly anecdotal: Beyond a single example and a small stability study on a 2-layer model, there is little quantitative analysis of which confusions improve, how gates distribute across layers/classes, or which features are consistently modulated.

Questions for the authors
1. Why not train the controller with backprop through time? Did you attempt it? If not, what prevented it?
2. What are the exact CIFAR-10/100 error rates (with/without dasNet), the absolute gains, and variance across runs?
3. Clarify “unaugmented” claim and ensure comparisons are apples-to-apples.
4. Provide full architectural details and SNES hyperparameters and batch sizes (n, p), plus runtime at test time.
5. Does constraining gates to be positive with mean 1 limit the controller? Did you try allowing gates < 0 or changing mean?

Suggestions
- Include ablations suggested above, especially gradient-trained controllers and static gating.
- Report confusion matrices and per-class improvements; analyze gate statistics by layer/class.
- Explore modestly deeper controller (e.g., one hidden layer or recurrent controller) trained with SGD to compare against SNES.
- Provide wall-clock inference overhead and accuracy vs T curves on the final model.
- Clean up notation (loss, λboost condition) and move crucial training details from supplementary to main text.

Significance and originality
The idea of iterative, internal, feature-wise attention to refine predictions is compelling and, at the time, relatively novel as a practical augmentation to strong CNNs. If validated with stronger baselines and clearer reporting, it could influence subsequent work on dynamic feature modulation. As presented, the empirical evidence is promising but not conclusive.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general mechanism; promising empirical gains on standard benchmarks.
- Bridges biological inspiration (top-down modulation) with practical CNNs.
- Potentially influential idea of iterative, internal, feature gating.

Cons:
- Key methodological choice (SNES) is under-justified; missing differentiable baselines.
- Reporting clarity and reproducibility issues; ambiguous SOTA claim.
- Limited analysis and ablations; heavy compute for modest gains; unclear trade-offs.

Recommendation
Borderline reject without the ablations and clearer reporting; borderline accept if authors provide rigorous comparisons (especially gradient-trained controllers), clarify results, and strengthen analysis.

Reviewer confidence
High in methodology/analysis; moderate in exact empirical claims given missing details.
