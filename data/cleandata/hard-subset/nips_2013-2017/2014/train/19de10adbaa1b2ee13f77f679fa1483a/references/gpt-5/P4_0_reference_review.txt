Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for adding learned, recurrent, top-down modulation to a trained convolutional neural network (CNN), specifically a Maxout CNN with dropout. At test time, the same image is processed over T passes. After each pass t, a policy observes summary statistics of the network’s internal activations (per-map average activations across convolutional layers, classification-layer pre-activations, and class probabilities) and outputs a vector of per-feature-map multiplicative gates a that modulate the next pass’s activations. The policy is a linear mapping with a scaled softmax nonlinearity, ensuring positive gates with mean approximately 1 to maintain stability. The policy parameters (≈1M) are optimized with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a diagonal-covariance NES variant, using a loss that upweights misclassified examples and depends on the final pass’s log-probability for the correct class. The base CNN is trained in the usual supervised fashion; its weights are frozen during policy learning.

Related work connects to: (1) biological evidence for recurrent and top-down modulation in visual cortex; (2) reinforcement learning (RL) for attention and feedback in vision; and (3) recurrent processing in computer vision tasks (e.g., reconstruction, tracking, face localization). The proposed approach distinguishes itself by applying top-down attentional modulation within a state-of-the-art CNN and optimizing it with SNES at scale, reporting improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 over a strong Maxout baseline. The paper also analyzes dynamics across different numbers of passes and reports that gate vectors alone carry discriminative information.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and plausibility: per-map multiplicative gates provide a clear mechanism for top-down modulation without changing base CNN weights.
- Scalable optimization: SNES is a reasonable choice for million-parameter policies; the training recipe, including stability via scaled softmax, appears to work in practice.
- Empirical gains: the method reportedly improves over a strong Maxout baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art for “unaugmented data” on CIFAR-10/100 (details in Table 1, not included here).
- Diagnostics: analysis of multi-pass dynamics (performance peaks around trained T and remains stable beyond), and demonstration that gate vectors contain class-relevant information.
- Clear connection to prior neuroscience and RL-attention literature, situating the contribution within existing themes of recurrent, modulatory vision.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete experimental reporting: precise numbers, standard deviations, and full baseline details (Table 1) are missing here; it is unclear what “unaugmented” entails given that training used data augmentation.
- Limited ablations: no comparisons to simpler alternatives (e.g., running multiple passes with identity gates, fine-tuning the base CNN on hard examples, or using supervised learning for the policy via backprop).
- Policy capacity and design: the policy is a single linear layer over high-dimensional observations; justification versus a smaller or more structured policy is limited, and no analysis of observation components’ importance is provided.
- Reproducibility concerns: several training choices (e.g., T=5 “experimentally determined”), SNES hyperparameters, and selection of images per SNES generation are not fully specified; the effect of different seeds is not reported.
- Computational cost and trade-off: training took four days on a single GPU excluding base CNN training, but the test-time latency and throughput overhead of T passes are not quantified against accuracy gains.
- Evaluation scope: improvements are presented on CIFAR-10/100; broader datasets or robustness studies (e.g., to corruptions, occlusions) would strengthen the case that recurrent attention provides general benefits.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound at a high level: the gating mechanism is well-defined, the policy is trained with an appropriate black-box optimizer, and stability is considered (positive gates with mean ≈1).
- The loss focuses on final-step performance with boosted weights for misclassifications; this directly targets hard cases but may bias the policy without demonstrating no regression on easy cases. A breakdown by instance difficulty would help.
- Empirical evidence indicates benefits, but the absence of detailed results, statistical variation, and key baselines limits the strength of the claims in this excerpt.

Clarity
- The method and equations are generally clear. The training algorithm is outlined, and the observation/action definitions are explicit.
- Some points need clarification: exact meaning of “unaugmented” performance; architecture sizes and dimensionalities of observation/action vectors; validation protocol for choosing T; test-time compute cost; details of SNES updates relegated to supplementary.
- Minor presentation issues (e.g., a mid-sentence footnote) could be polished.

Originality
- Combining per-map top-down gating learned post hoc via black-box RL with a modern CNN backbone is a novel combination. The particular design—linear policy over internal summaries, multi-pass modulation, SNES for million-parameter control—is distinctive relative to prior RL-attention and recurrent vision works cited.
- The mechanism focuses on feature-selective rather than spatial attention; while the general idea of feedback is not new, the concrete, scalable instantiation in a state-of-the-art CNN is original enough for a conference contribution.

Significance
- If the reported improvements over a strong Maxout baseline and new SOTA for “unaugmented data” hold under fair comparison, the work is practically significant.
- The approach suggests a general recipe to retrofit trained CNNs with learned feedback, potentially impactful beyond CIFAR.
- The lack of broader benchmarks and comprehensive ablations tempers the assessed impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report full quantitative results with means/std across runs, compute overhead per image for varying T, and clear definition of “unaugmented.”
- Add baselines: (a) T > 1 with identity gates; (b) fine-tuning the CNN on hard examples; (c) supervised policy training via backprop; (d) smaller/structured policies; (e) gating limited to certain layers.
- Provide per-class confusion changes and analyses showing that easy cases are not degraded.
- Include ablations on observation components and policy architecture; consider sparsity or grouping in gates to reduce parameter count.
- Evaluate on an additional dataset or robustness benchmark.

Pros
- Clear, biologically inspired mechanism for feedback in CNNs.
- Scalable optimization via SNES; practically feasible training.
- Reported improvements over a strong baseline; analysis of dynamics and gate informativeness.

Cons
- Incomplete reporting of results and experimental details; unclear fairness of “unaugmented” claim.
- Missing key ablations and baselines.
- Limited discussion of computational trade-offs and reproducibility.

Recommendation
Leaning toward weak accept, contingent on complete and fair experimental reporting and inclusion of key ablations. The core idea is sound and potentially impactful, but stronger empirical support is needed to fully substantiate the significance claims.
