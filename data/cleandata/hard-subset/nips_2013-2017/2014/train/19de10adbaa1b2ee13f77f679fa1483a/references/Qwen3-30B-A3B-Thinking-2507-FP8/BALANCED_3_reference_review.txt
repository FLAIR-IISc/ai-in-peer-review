

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to incorporating selective attention into deep CNNs through feedback connections. The authors propose augmenting a pre-trained Maxout network with a reinforcement learning (RL) policy that modulates filter activations across multiple passes over the same image. The key innovation is using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to efficiently learn the attention policy, which addresses the high dimensionality of the policy space. The paper demonstrates that dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10, with qualitative evidence showing how attention mechanisms correct misclassifications.

The work relates to a growing body of NIPS research on attention mechanisms (e.g., Bahdanau et al. 2014, Vaswani et al. 2017) and recurrent processing in neural networks (e.g., Sutskever et al. 2014, Schmidhuber 2015). It also builds on computational neuroscience work about top-down feedback in visual processing (Felleman & Essen 1991, Lamme 2000). However, dasNet differs from most prior work by focusing on learning selective attention *after* the CNN has been trained, rather than incorporating attention into the initial training process.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured method that addresses a genuine limitation of standard CNNs (inability to revise initial classifications). The use of SNES to handle the large policy space is theoretically justified and practical for the problem size.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The CIFAR-10/100 experiments provide compelling evidence that dasNet can correct misclassifications by selectively emphasizing/de-emphasizing filters. The qualitative analysis of filter activations (Figure 3) offers valuable insights into the attention mechanism's operation.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written with a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The algorithm description and mathematical formulations are precise and accessible.

4. **Novelty:** While related to attention mechanisms and recurrent networks, dasNet's specific approach of learning attention policies post-training using SNES represents a distinct contribution to the literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison:** The paper compares dasNet only to a vanilla Maxout network, not to more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets, or transformer-based approaches) that have since become standard on CIFAR-10/100.

2. **Computational Cost:** The 4-day training time for dasNet (on a GTX 560 Ti) is substantial compared to the original CNN training. The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether this cost is justified by the marginal improvement.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the feedback mechanism versus simply running multiple passes through the network.

4. **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10/100. The paper doesn't demonstrate whether the approach generalizes to more challenging datasets like ImageNet or to other tasks beyond classification.

5. **Weak Theoretical Justification:** While the RL framework is well-described, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of why this specific approach should work better than other attention mechanisms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to selective attention in deep networks
- Demonstrates significant improvement on a standard benchmark
- Well-executed experiments with insightful qualitative analysis
- Addresses a meaningful limitation of current CNN architectures

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods
- Substantial computational cost without clear justification
- Minimal theoretical analysis of the proposed mechanism
- Limited evidence of generalizability beyond CIFAR-10/100

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an interesting and well-executed approach to incorporating selective attention into CNNs. The core idea of using RL to learn feedback connections for post-hoc correction of misclassifications is novel and well-motivated. However, the marginal improvement (6% relative) on CIFAR-10 compared to a vanilla Maxout network is not sufficiently compelling for NIPS, especially given the lack of comparison to more recent architectures that have achieved much higher performance on these datasets. The computational cost also seems high for the modest gain.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Compare dasNet to more recent state-of-the-art architectures (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) on CIFAR-10/100, not just the vanilla Maxout network.

2. Provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of the feedback mechanism versus simply running multiple passes with fixed weights.

3. Conduct experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. Include a more thorough theoretical analysis of why this approach works better than other attention mechanisms.

5. Discuss the computational cost in more detail, including how this would scale to larger networks and datasets.

While the paper has a compelling core idea, the current version does not sufficiently demonstrate the significance of the contribution relative to the state-of-the-art. With the suggested improvements, particularly comparing to more recent architectures and demonstrating broader applicability, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the marginal gains on CIFAR-10 with a substantial computational cost make the paper less compelling for NIPS.
