- Proposal: Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet) augment a CNN with recurrent multiplicative gates controlled by a policy to rescale feature maps across multiple passes.  
- Policy design: Observations are per-map averages, classification pre-activations, and softmax outputs; actions are positive, mean-one gates applied to feature maps.  
- Training pipeline: Train Maxout CNN supervised first, then train the policy with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) using a terminal cross-entropy loss with boosted weight for misclassified samples.  
- Empirical result: Small but consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over the Maxout baseline, with qualitative gating dynamics and stability beyond the trained number of steps.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to top-down modulation and attention literature; novelty is global channel-wise, multi-step gating rather than spatial glimpse attention.  
- Strength — simplicity/generalizability: Feature-map gating is easy to implement on top of strong CNNs and uses a lightweight observation vector.  
- Strength — design choices: Positive, mean-one scaled-softmax gates and compact observations help stabilize dynamics and keep controller inputs small.  
- Strength — targeted improvements: Demonstrated focus on correcting ambiguous/hard examples and stable recurrent behavior.  
- Weakness — reproducibility: Missing crucial details (layer sizes, kernel/stride specs, exact observation/action dims, SNES hyperparameters, policy training/validation protocol) and an inconsistency about data augmentation.  
- Weakness — limited baselines/ablations: No comparison to gradient-based BPTT training of the controller, no static or per-class gating baselines, no ablation of observation components or boosting, and no comparison to ensembling/TTA or hard-example fine-tuning.  
- Weakness — questionable RL framing: The terminal supervised reward makes the black-box evolutionary approach unnecessary; BPTT would be a natural, likely more sample-efficient alternative.  
- Weakness — potential overfitting: Large (~10^6) policy trained on the same training set as the CNN with no held-out validation, early stopping, or hyperparameter selection described.  
- Weakness — effect size/reporting: Improvements are modest; paper lacks confidence intervals, training-vs-test curves, and analysis of which classes benefit.  
- Weakness — unclear claims: The gate-only classification result (40–46%) is under-specified and may be implausible; needs clarification and sanity checks.  
- Weakness — scalability/computation: Policy training time (4 days) and use of diagonal-covariance NES raise concerns about scalability and optimization efficiency.  
- Quality assessment: Method is broadly sound conceptually but experimental validation, baselines, and reporting are weak.  
- Clarity: Well-written overall but reproducibility harmed by missing details and inconsistencies.  
- Originality: Moderately original — applying a learned multi-step, global channel-wise gating controller trained with NES is a novel combination.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful as a general test-time adaptation mechanism, but empirical evidence is currently limited.  
- Suggested improvements: Supply full architecture/training details, resolve augmentation inconsistency, add BPTT and simple baselines, perform ablations on observations/boost/T, use held-out validation and report variance/class-wise effects, and clarify the gate-only experiment.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (6) — interesting idea and promising results but needs stronger empirical validation and clearer reporting.
