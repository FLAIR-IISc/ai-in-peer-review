Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture that performs multiple passes over the same image while modulating intermediate feature maps through multiplicative gates. Gates are produced by a learned policy that observes summary statistics of activations and class scores and outputs per-map weights for the next pass. The policy is trained post hoc, after the base Maxout CNN is supervised-trained, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the observation/action spaces. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 reportedly show improved accuracy over a baseline Maxout CNN, with qualitative analysis of gate dynamics and a small stability study when evaluating more time steps than trained.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to biologically inspired feedback and top-down modulation in vision (e.g., Felleman & Van Essen; Lamme & Roelfsema) and computational attention/recurrency in vision (e.g., Denil et al. on learned saccades; Fukushima; O’Reilly’s recirculation; Behnke’s iterative reconstruction). Compared to prior “glimpse”/foveation RL (Denil et al.; Larochelle & Hinton), the novelty here is internal, feature-wise attention rather than spatial attention, and the use of black-box NES to learn a high-dimensional policy that gates all convolutional maps. The use of Maxout+dropout as the base model is standard for the era.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level idea is reasonable, but several methodological details are ambiguous or inconsistent:
  - Loss/boosting: Eq. (8) uses −λboost d log(vT), but d is the label while vT is a probability vector. It should be −λboost log vT[d]. In Eq. (10) λboost depends on “d = ||vT||∞,” which conflates predicted class with ground-truth—likely a typo. The specification of the training objective for SNES is unclear.
  - Policy mapping: πθ(o) = dim(A)σ(θo) implies a linear mapping followed by softmax scaled to have mean 1. This ensures positivity but not per-layer normalization. Without per-layer normalization or layer-wise temperature control, gates can redistribute mass unfairly across layers of different sizes; this design choice is not justified or ablated. Feature scaling for o is not described; mixing averages of feature maps, pre-softmax logits ȳ, and post-softmax probabilities likely yields disparate scales.
  - Notation and equations: Several equations appear malformed (e.g., Eq. (1) indexation; Eq. (2) “b max i=0” unclear; Eq. (3) combines a linear map and max without defining shapes; definitions of x⃗, F dimensions, and strides/paddings are incomplete).
  - RL framing: Despite the RL motivation, the training reduces to black-box optimization of terminal loss at step T; no intermediate rewards or explicit temporal credit assignment are used. Baselines using standard policy gradients or differentiable surrogate objectives are absent.
- Experimental evidence: The paper claims a 6% relative improvement vs. the baseline CNN and “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data,” but Table 1 is not included here, and critical details are missing:
  - Exact error rates for CIFAR-10/100, with/without data augmentation, are not enumerated; statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported.
  - The paper states both that data augmentation is used to train M and that results are “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data,” which is contradictory and needs clarification.
  - Stronger baselines are missing: e.g., ensembling multiple test-time passes with a0=1, extra epochs of supervised fine-tuning, deeper/wider Maxout networks, test-time augmentation, or multi-sample dropout averaging. A random or fixed (non-RL) gating baseline would be informative.
  - Ablations: No ablation of observation components, per-layer gating vs. per-map gating, linear vs. nonlinear policy, per-layer normalization, number of passes T, or sensitivity to λ parameters.
  - The “gate information” experiment (k-NN/logistic regression on final gate vectors) is intriguing but under-specified (which dataset? split? features normalized?), and it is unclear how it compares to using intermediate CNN features directly.
- Reproducibility: Insufficient details to reproduce results (see below).

Clarity
The manuscript is readable but contains numerous notation inconsistencies, ambiguous equations, and incomplete descriptions (e.g., architectural hyperparameters, SNES hyperparameters/learning rates, batch sizes n and population p roles in compute, stopping criteria). References are inconsistently formatted and duplicated (e.g., Zeiler & Fergus appears twice; many entries miss years/pages). Algorithm 1 has indexing/notation issues (a0 initialization and effect at t=0 vs. t=1; ot vs. o0).

Originality
Moderately novel combination: internal multiplicative attention applied across all convolutional maps, driven by an evolved high-dimensional policy. While feedback and attention in CNNs are not new conceptually, the specific instantiation with Maxout and SNES for continuous, per-map gating over multiple passes is a nontrivial engineering contribution for the time.

Significance
Potentially useful if robust and reproducible, but the empirical gains appear modest and are not convincingly isolated from simpler alternatives. The lack of thorough comparisons, ablations, and statistical reporting limits the impact. Training time and million-parameter policy evolution may hinder adoption.

Pros
- Clear, biologically inspired motivation for feedback and selective internal attention.
- Simple mechanism (multiplicative gates) that is easy to retrofit to existing CNNs.
- Demonstrates that a learned recurrent policy can correct some initial misclassifications.
- Stability analysis for T steps (albeit small model) is a nice touch.

Cons
- Ambiguous/incorrect equations and notational inconsistencies (loss, boosting, indexing).
- Insufficient experimental detail and missing baselines; claims of SOTA not substantiated here.
- Contradiction regarding use of data augmentation vs. “unaugmented” SOTA.
- No ablations on observation composition, policy class, gate normalization, or per-layer effects.
- Black-box evolution of ~1M parameters with diagonal covariance and small population is hard to trust without more evidence; sample efficiency and variance are unreported.
- Reproducibility: architecture and SNES hyperparameters largely unspecified.

Questions for authors
1. Provide full CIFAR-10/100 results (error rates, with/without augmentation, std across ≥3 seeds), plus the exact Maxout baseline used.
2. Clarify the loss/boosting equations and fix notation; is the optimized objective the negative log-likelihood at step T?
3. Detail the observation vector (dimensions per layer, normalization), action mapping (per-layer normalization?), and all architectural hyperparameters (filters, strides, k, b).
4. Report SNES hyperparameters (learning rates, noise scale, initialization, elite fraction, number of generations, batch sizes), and validation-based early stopping.
5. Add baselines: random gates, per-layer scalar gates, supervised fine-tuning with extra passes, test-time augmentation/ensembles, and a deeper Maxout network.
6. Ablate T, policy nonlinearity, and the contribution of each observation component.
7. Provide per-class confusion improvements and examples beyond a single cat/dog image.

Minor comments
- Fix numerous typos, index bounds, and reference formatting. Avoid duplicate references and provide complete bibliographic details.
- In Eq. (5), clarify whether gates are applied before/after nonlinearity and before/after batch/dropout; discuss effect on activation scales.
- Consider per-layer softmax-normalized gates to avoid cross-layer competition due to different map counts.

Reproducibility checklist
- Release code or at least full hyperparameter tables and configuration files.
- Specify data augmentation pipeline exactly.
- Report compute budget, epochs/generations, and wall-clock per experiment.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many technical ambiguities, missing experimental details/baselines, and clarity issues to support its claims. A revised, more rigorous submission with corrected equations, comprehensive ablations, and stronger empirical evidence could be competitive. Confidence: high.
