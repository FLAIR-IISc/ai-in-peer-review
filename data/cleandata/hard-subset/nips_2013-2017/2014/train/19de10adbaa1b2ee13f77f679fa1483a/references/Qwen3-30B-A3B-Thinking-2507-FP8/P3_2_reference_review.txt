

# NIPS Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary

This paper introduces "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), a method that enhances deep CNNs by enabling them to iteratively refine their internal feature representations through feedback connections. The core idea is to train a reinforcement learning (RL) policy that modulates filter weights across multiple passes of the same image, allowing the network to "correct" initial misclassifications by selectively emphasizing or suppressing certain features. The authors use a Maxout network as the base architecture and train the attention policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the policy space.

The approach builds on the biological observation that humans often require multiple "passes" to distinguish between similar visual stimuli (e.g., two similar bird species), suggesting that iterative refinement could improve machine vision systems. The paper demonstrates modest improvements (6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs) on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, with visualizations showing how the network's internal representations change across passes.

This work relates to several lines of research: 1) Recurrent processing in neural networks for improved recognition (e.g., work by Lee et al. on iterative refinement), 2) Attention mechanisms in CNNs (e.g., CBAM, SE blocks), and 3) Reinforcement learning for visual attention (e.g., work by Mnih et al. on learning saccades).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of RL**: The paper presents a creative application of reinforcement learning to control internal network dynamics in a post-training phase, which is different from most RL approaches in vision that focus on learning the entire network from scratch.

2. **Biologically Motivated**: The motivation from human vision (multiple passes for difficult classifications) is well-justified and provides a compelling rationale for the approach.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The use of SNES instead of standard RL methods to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a practical and well-justified choice.

4. **Clear Experimental Results**: The paper provides concrete examples (e.g., the cat vs. dog classification) showing how the attention mechanism operates, which helps readers understand the proposed approach.

5. **Adequate Baselines**: The paper compares against a vanilla Maxout network, which is a reasonable baseline for the proposed method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining why this approach should work better than standard CNNs. The authors claim the approach "corrects itself" but don't provide a formal argument for why the RL policy would learn better attention mechanisms than a standard CNN.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work**: The paper fails to properly contextualize its approach with respect to recent attention mechanisms. For example:
   - It doesn't discuss how dasNet differs from or improves upon the "Convolutional Block Attention Module" (CBAM) or "Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks" (SE blocks), which are now standard attention mechanisms in CNNs.
   - It overlooks the work on "iterative refinement" in neural networks (e.g., "Deeply Supervised Nets" by Lee et al., "Iterative Refinement Networks" by Huang et al.), which also use multiple passes to improve classification.

3. **Modest Performance Gains**: The claimed 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs is modest, especially when considering the computational overhead (4 days of training on a GTX 560 Ti GPU). For context, a standard ResNet-50 on CIFAR-10 achieves ~94% accuracy, so the absolute gain is likely small. The paper doesn't compare against modern state-of-the-art architectures (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets) that would provide a more meaningful benchmark.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't analyze the inference-time cost of dasNet. Since it requires multiple passes through the network, it would be significantly slower than a standard CNN during inference, which is a major practical limitation.

5. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The experiments are restricted to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which are relatively small datasets. The approach would need to be tested on larger, more challenging datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate broader applicability.

6. **Unclear Contribution to the Field**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate what new theoretical or practical insights it provides beyond a slightly better performance on a specific dataset. The approach seems to be a relatively straightforward application of RL to control filter weights, without significant conceptual advances.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound in its methodology, but the experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks sufficient comparison with state-of-the-art techniques. The claims about the "new state-of-the-art" are misleading since they're only compared to a vanilla Maxout network, not modern architectures. The paper doesn't adequately address the weaknesses of their approach (e.g., the computational cost during inference), which is a concern for a high-impact venue like NIPS.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear descriptions of the dasNet architecture and the SNES algorithm. However, some key details are missing:
- The exact dimensions of the observation and action spaces aren't clearly specified.
- The construction of the observation vector (Section 4) is somewhat vague, and the paper doesn't explain why these particular features were chosen.
- The paper refers to "Figure 3" but doesn't provide sufficient description to understand the visualization in the text.

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of using feedback connections to modulate internal feature representations is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing work. The authors claim that their approach "harnesses the power of sequential processing," but this is similar to other recurrent neural network approaches for image recognition. The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review that clearly positions dasNet relative to existing attention mechanisms and iterative refinement approaches.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is limited by the modest performance gains and the lack of demonstration on larger, more challenging datasets. The computational overhead of multiple passes through the network during inference makes the approach less practical for real-world applications where speed is critical. While the idea of "internal selective attention" is interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach provides a meaningful advance over simpler attention mechanisms that can be integrated into standard CNNs without the need for iterative processing.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting but incremental approach to attention in deep networks. While the idea has merit, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate the significance of the contribution. The performance gains are modest, the comparison with state-of-the-art is insufficient, and the computational cost during inference is not addressed. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these limitations, including:

1. Comparison against modern CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) on CIFAR-10/100 and potentially ImageNet.
2. Analysis of the computational cost during inference.
3. A more thorough literature review that clearly differentiates dasNet from existing attention mechanisms.
4. A clearer explanation of why this approach is better than simpler attention mechanisms that don't require multiple passes.

Without these improvements, the paper does not provide a sufficiently strong contribution to justify publication at NIPS, especially given the high bar for acceptance at this venue.

## Pro/Con Summary

**Pros:**
- Biologically motivated approach to iterative visual processing
- Creative application of reinforcement learning to control internal network dynamics
- Clear visualization of how attention mechanisms affect internal feature representations
- Practical implementation using SNES to handle high-dimensional policy space

**Cons:**
- Modest performance gains (6% relative improvement) compared to standard CNNs
- Lacks comparison with modern state-of-the-art architectures
- No analysis of inference-time computational cost
- Fails to properly contextualize the work with respect to recent attention mechanisms
- Limited to small-scale CIFAR datasets without demonstration on larger benchmarks
- Doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why the approach should work better

The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of making a significant contribution to the field that would warrant publication at NIPS.
