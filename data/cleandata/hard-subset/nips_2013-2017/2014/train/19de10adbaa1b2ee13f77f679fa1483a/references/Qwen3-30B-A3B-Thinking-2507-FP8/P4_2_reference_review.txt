

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard CNNs by incorporating reinforcement learning to learn a policy that modulates filter activations through multiple passes over the same image. The authors build upon Maxout networks [7] and use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to efficiently optimize a control policy with over 1 million parameters. This approach enables the network to iteratively focus attention on different features, correcting misclassifications made by the initial feedforward pass. The work relates to neuroscience findings about top-down feedback connections in visual cortex [28-30] and previous computer vision approaches that use recurrent processing [48-50], but distinguishes itself by applying this framework to state-of-the-art CNNs rather than simplified models.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-defined methodology. The authors justify their use of SNES over conventional RL methods due to the large parameter space (over 1 million parameters), which is a reasonable consideration. The experimental design is appropriate, with clear ablation studies on CIFAR-10/100 showing a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs. The authors include important validation by showing that gate values contain significant classification information (40.70% and 45.74% accuracy with nearest neighbor and logistic regression). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) and focus on CIFAR datasets rather than larger benchmarks.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) and mathematical formulations are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. Figure 3 provides an excellent visual demonstration of how dasNet corrects misclassifications through attention modulation. The writing is accessible to researchers in the field, though the RL background section could be condensed for a specialized audience.

**Originality:** The core idea of using RL to learn a policy that modulates filter activations across multiple passes is novel. While recurrent networks and feedback connections have been explored in vision [48-50], the authors clearly differentiate their approach by applying it to state-of-the-art CNNs rather than simplified models. The connection to neuroscience [28-30] is appropriately referenced, and the paper adequately distinguishes itself from related work on attention mechanisms [40-45, 49] and recurrent processing.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem: the inability of standard CNNs to correct their own misclassifications. The results represent a new state-of-the-art for CIFAR-10 with unaugmented data, demonstrating that this approach can improve performance without retraining the base network. The technique could potentially be applied to other vision tasks, though the paper doesn't explore this extensively. The approach advances the state of the art on a widely used benchmark.

**Strengths:**
- Novel integration of RL with CNNs for internal attention
- Practical implementation using SNES to handle large parameter spaces
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks
- Good analysis of how attention gates change across passes
- Clear explanation of the method and its dynamics
- Appropriate comparison to related work

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to CIFAR datasets; generalizability to larger benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) is untested
- Computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) may limit practical applicability
- Theoretical justification for the approach is limited, relying primarily on empirical results
- Doesn't explore integration with contemporary attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers)
- The neuroscience connection, while interesting, isn't deeply integrated into the technical approach

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through internal selective attention.
2. The results demonstrate a significant improvement over state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100.
3. The methodology is well-motivated and carefully implemented.
4. The paper addresses a meaningful limitation in standard CNNs.
5. The work is directly applicable to improving existing state-of-the-art CNNs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1. The results are limited to CIFAR datasets without evidence of scalability to larger benchmarks.
2. The computational overhead may limit practical adoption.
3. The theoretical contribution is somewhat limited compared to the empirical results.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating how reinforcement learning can be used to enhance existing CNNs through internal selective attention. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results represent a meaningful improvement on standard benchmarks. While the scope is limited to CIFAR datasets, the approach has potential for broader application and represents an important step toward more flexible, adaptive neural network architectures. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a foundation for future work on internal attention mechanisms.
