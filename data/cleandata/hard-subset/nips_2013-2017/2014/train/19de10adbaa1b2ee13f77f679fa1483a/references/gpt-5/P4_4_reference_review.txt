Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a modification of convolutional neural networks that introduces internal selective attention via feedback connections. After standard supervised training of a Maxout CNN, the model is augmented with multiplicative gates on every feature map across layers. At test time, a fixed image is processed for T recurrent “passes.” On each pass, a policy maps observations (average activations per feature map, intermediate classifier activations, and the current class probability vector) to an action vector that scales feature-map outputs before maxout pooling. The policy is trained post hoc to improve hard classifications using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimizer maintaining only a diagonal covariance, chosen for scalability to a million+ policy parameters.

Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 report a relative improvement over a strong Maxout baseline, focusing especially on ambiguous cases. The authors show qualitative examples of how feedback reweights filters and a small analysis of dynamics across different T. They argue that policy-learned gating captures useful class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on deep CNNs (Krizhevsky et al., NIPS 2012; Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013) and adds recurrent, top-down modulation inspired by visual neuroscience (e.g., Lamme & Roelfsema; Bar et al.) and prior ML work on RL-driven attention/saccades (e.g., Schmidhuber & Huber; Denil et al.), recurrent processing for vision, and Maxout/dropout for strong baselines. Unlike earlier RL attention that selects spatial glimpses or saccades, dasNet modulates internal feature maps globally and repeatedly. The use of NES/SNES as a scalable evolutionary policy search approach in high dimensions is also grounded in prior work by the authors.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a simple, general mechanism for internal attention via multiplicative gating across layers, decoupled from the base CNN training.
- Practicality: uses an existing trained CNN and learns a policy on top; no changes to base supervised training are needed.
- Scalability considerations: SNES enables optimization with ~10^6 parameters where conventional RL may struggle.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100; analysis suggests stable dynamics beyond the training horizon.
- Interpretability angle: qualitative inspection of gate changes and evidence that gate vectors contain class-relevant information.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines and ablations: no comparison to gradient-based end-to-end training of gating via backpropagation through time, or to simpler gating strategies (e.g., per-layer scalar gates, per-class gating, or a small learned linear readout on top of intermediate features).
- Incomplete reporting: absolute test accuracies and standard deviations are not provided in the text; “unaugmented data” is mentioned despite earlier stating data augmentation was used; compute overhead at inference (T passes) is not quantified against competing methods.
- Policy architecture: the policy appears to be a single linear layer plus softmax; it is unclear whether greater representational power is needed and whether the huge parameter count is justified.
- Observation design: using only averaged feature map activations discards spatial information; it is unclear how sensitive performance is to this choice.
- Optimization and generalization: evolving a million-parameter deterministic policy with diagonal covariance may overfit; there is limited analysis of sample efficiency, convergence stability, or validation-based early stopping.
- Scope: experiments are limited to CIFAR-10/100; no results on larger-scale or higher-resolution datasets, nor on modern architectures.
- Fairness vs. SOTA: the SOTA claim is difficult to verify without precise numbers, confidence intervals, and comparison to contemporary strong baselines/ensembles.

Quality
- The method is technically well-defined: gating mechanism, observation/action spaces, and the SNES training loop are described. The loss includes a boosting term to emphasize hard examples.
- However, important details are deferred or unclear (exact gate dimensionalities per layer, SNES hyperparameters and update settings, how overfitting is controlled, exact baseline numbers, data augmentation protocol). The lack of stronger baselines (e.g., BPTT-trained recurrent modulation) limits conclusions about necessity of evolutionary RL in this setting.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with a clear algorithm box. Some inconsistencies (augmentation vs. “unaugmented” claim), missing absolute performance numbers, and limited architectural specifics hinder reproducibility. Suggest clarifying: gate dimensionalities by layer, observation vector composition and normalization, policy architecture depth, SNES initialization and learning rates, and precise evaluation protocol.

Originality
- The combination of global per-map feedback gating learned via policy search, applied repeatedly over the same image to refine predictions, is a distinctive contribution relative to prior RL-based attention (mostly spatial/temporal glimpses) and standard feedforward CNNs. The use of SNES for such high-dimensional policies is also noteworthy.

Significance
- The idea of internal selective attention via feedback is of broad interest and could inspire further work on recurrent modulation in deep nets. The reported gains on CIFAR are encouraging but modest, and the absence of stronger baselines and broader datasets limits impact. Computational overhead from multiple passes and policy complexity may further constrain adoption.

Questions for the authors
- Provide absolute accuracy/error rates with standard deviations and exact baselines; clarify the “unaugmented data” statement vs. augmentation used to train M.
- Compare against backprop-based training of the gating policy (e.g., BPTT), and against simpler baselines (per-layer scalar gates, shallow policy, or small MLP).
- Report inference-time cost (xT) and wall-clock overhead vs. accuracy gain.
- Analyze sensitivity to T, observation design, and policy capacity; provide ablations.
- Describe SNES hyperparameters, early stopping, and validation monitoring.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting, biologically motivated approach to internal attention with clear mechanism.
- Demonstrates measurable improvements on standard datasets.
- Potentially general framework applicable to many CNNs.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient baselines/ablations to isolate where gains come from and to justify evolutionary RL over gradient-based training.
- Limited scope of experiments and incomplete reporting hinder assessment and reproducibility.
- Modest gains relative to increased complexity and compute.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and relevant, but stronger empirical validation, clearer reporting, and comparisons to simpler and gradient-based alternatives are needed to substantiate significance and technical soundness.
