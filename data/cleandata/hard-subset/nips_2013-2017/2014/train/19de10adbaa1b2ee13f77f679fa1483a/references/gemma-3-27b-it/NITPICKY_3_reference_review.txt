## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a framework for endowing CNNs with a form of internal attention mechanism realized via recurrent feedback connections and learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES). The motivation, stemming from observations of human visual processing, is to allow CNNs to refine their classifications through iterative processing of an image, selectively emphasizing and suppressing filters to correct initial misclassifications.

The core idea is sound and builds upon well-established concepts in both deep learning (Maxout networks, CNNs) and reinforcement learning. The authors correctly identify the challenge of applying standard RL techniques to the high-dimensional action space required to control a large CNN and opt for SNES, a pragmatic choice. The experimental evaluation on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 is a necessary step to demonstrate the efficacy of the approach.

However, despite the novelty and potential, I have significant concerns regarding the presentation, methodological rigor, and ultimately, the strength of the claims made. My assessment is largely based on a large number of minor (but collectively substantial) issues that detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of recurrent connections *within* a CNN, controlled by a learned policy via RL, is a novel approach to attention mechanisms.
*   **Motivation:** The analogy to human visual processing is compelling and provides a clear rationale for the work.
*   **Pragmatic Approach:** The choice of SNES for the policy learning is reasonable given the dimensionality of the problem.
*   **Demonstrated Improvement:** The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is a positive result.

**Weaknesses (Numerous and Significant):**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison:** While the paper claims state-of-the-art performance, the comparison is only against a “vanilla CNN” trained on the same data. No comparison to established attention mechanisms (e.g., SENet, CBAM, self-attention) is provided. It's unclear whether the improvement stems from the recurrent feedback or simply from a better-tuned network.
*   **Insufficient Detail in SNES Implementation:** The implementation details of SNES are relegated to the supplementary material. Critical parameters such as population size, learning rate, and stopping criteria are mentioned in the main text, but lack justification. The statement "Training took around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU" is insufficient; computational cost and scalability are vital considerations.
*   **Unclear Observation Space:** The construction of the observation vector 'o' is vaguely described.  "Averaging map activations" is imprecise. What type of averaging (mean, max, etc.) is used? How are the activations normalized? The impact of this observation design on the policy learning is unexplored.
*   **Action Space Scaling & Interpretation:** The scaling of the softmax function in the action selection (to average to 1 instead of summing to 1) is unusual and requires more justification.  The intuition behind this scaling is not explained.
*   **Loss Function Detail:** The loss function (Equation 8) uses `λboost` which introduces a potentially large variance. The sensitivity to `λcorrect` and `λmisclassified` isn’t explored or discussed.
*   **Overstated Claims regarding Visualization:** The analysis of the filter changes (Figure 3) is subjective and lacks quantitative support. Statements like “complex suppression and enhancement patterns are found” are vague. The discussion regarding what the high-level filters "do" is speculative.
*   **Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors throughout the paper: inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts, redundant use of layer index 'ℓ', imprecise language, and a generally cluttered writing style. For instance, equation (6) states vt = Mt(θ, x), yet the notation used in equation (9) is different.
*   **Related Work Gaps:** The related work section is extensive, but fails to mention more recent advancements in recurrent neural networks and attention mechanisms relevant to the task.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The core idea is interesting and the experimental results suggest some potential.

**Con:** The paper lacks rigor in its experimental setup, methodological details, and analysis. The presentation is marred by numerous minor flaws that collectively detract from the credibility of the work. The absence of comparisons to existing attention mechanisms is a significant omission. The claims made about the visualization results are unsupported.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but its execution and presentation fall short of the standards expected for NIPS. While the improvement over the baseline is encouraging, it’s insufficient to overcome the numerous methodological and presentational shortcomings. The lack of comparisons to existing attention mechanisms significantly weakens the claim of novelty and significance.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. I suggest the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, provide a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and significantly improve the clarity and precision of the writing before resubmitting. A more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art attention mechanisms is crucial.
