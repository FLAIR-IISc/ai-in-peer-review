- Proposes DasNet: a CNN with learned internal feedback that multiplicatively gates feature maps over multiple passes to implement selective attention.  
- Policy maps compact observations (per-map averages, penultimate activations, class probs) to per-channel gating actions.  
- Policy is trained post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) treating multi-pass classification as a sequential decision problem.  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show improved accuracy over a strong Maxout baseline, especially on ambiguous examples.  
- Dynamics analysis shows stability when evaluating for more steps than trained and that gate vectors carry discriminative information.  
- Paper is motivated by neuroscience (top-down modulatory feedback, two-stage vision) and relates to recurrent/attention and gating literature.  
- The formulation—global, channel-wise, recurrent gating trained via black-box policy search—appears novel relative to prior NIPS work.  
- Conceptually anticipates squeeze-and-excitation style channel attention but differs by being recurrent, per-image, and SNES-trained.  
- Mechanism is clearly defined: positive-scaled softmax gating, compact observations, and unrolled recurrent loop.  
- Learning objective focuses on misclassified examples with boosting-style weighting and includes L2 regularization.  
- SNES is justified by the policy’s very high dimensionality (~10^6 parameters), at the cost of sample efficiency.  
- Empirical support is solid but limited: improvements and informative stability/gate analyses are provided.  
- Major missing baselines/ablations: no gradient-based BPTT training of the gating policy or simpler supervised alternatives.  
- Unclear how gains decompose across factors: multiple passes, inclusion of class probs, boosting loss, or positivity constraint.  
- Compute/latency costs (T× inference) and wall-clock impact are not reported or quantified.  
- Claim of “unaugmented” SOTA is ambiguous because the Maxout base used augmentation; absolute numbers and variances are missing.  
- Paper is generally well written and concrete, but reproducibility needs full architectural details and error bars over multiple runs.  
- Originality is strong: novel per-image, per-pass top-down modulation and creative use of SNES for attention policy learning.  
- Significance is promising but limited by CIFAR-scale scope, extra inference cost, and lack of scaling/gradient-based training evidence.  
- Strengths: clear formulation, empirical gains over strong baseline, dynamic analysis, and neuroscience-practical bridge.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on SNES (scalability/efficiency concerns), insufficient ablations, ambiguous augmentation claims, missing compute analysis, limited datasets.  
- Suggestions: add BPTT baseline, full mean±std tables, clarify augmentation, report inference overhead, run targeted ablations, and show per-class/confusion improvements.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — novel and well-motivated with clear gains, but needs stronger baselines and clearer experimental reporting.
