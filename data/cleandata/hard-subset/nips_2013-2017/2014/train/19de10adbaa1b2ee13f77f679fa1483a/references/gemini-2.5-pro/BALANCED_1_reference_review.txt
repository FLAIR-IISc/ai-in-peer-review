### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks with a mechanism for internal selective attention. The core idea is to enable a pre-trained CNN to perform multiple inferential passes on a single input image, iteratively refining its internal representations and final prediction. This is achieved by introducing modulatory gates (`a_j^l`) that scale the output of filter maps in each layer. A policy network, trained via reinforcement learning (RL), determines the values of these gates at each pass. The state for the policy is derived from activations within the CNN, and the action corresponds to the gate values for the next pass. The policy itself is a large linear map optimized using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to overcome the challenge of a high-dimensional action space. The authors draw an analogy to the human visual system, which combines a fast feedforward sweep with slower, recurrent, attentional processing. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, where it is shown to improve the performance of a strong baseline Maxout network, particularly on ambiguous examples, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 for unaugmented data at the time of writing.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel conceptual framework. The idea of using RL to learn an explicit policy for top-down modulation of internal features within a CNN is highly original. It moves beyond purely feedforward architectures and proposes a plausible, learnable mechanism for iterative refinement and attentional processing. This addresses a significant question in the field: how can models "think" for longer on difficult problems? The proposed approach could inspire a new class of models that dynamically allocate computational resources based on input difficulty.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The authors make a sensible, pragmatic choice by using an evolution strategy (SNES) to train the control policy. Given the high dimensionality of the action space (one gate per filter map) and the non-differentiable nature of the evolutionary search, standard policy gradient methods would be challenging to apply. Using a black-box optimizer like SNES is a practical way to handle the large parameter space of the policy. The two-stage training process—first supervised training of the CNN, then RL training of the policy—is also a logical decomposition of the problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper demonstrates a clear and meaningful improvement over a strong baseline (Maxout networks), achieving a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10. The qualitative analysis (Figure 3), while speculative, provides a valuable glimpse into the model's behavior, showing how it modulates feature maps to correct an initial misclassification. Furthermore, the experiments on the network's dynamics (Figure 2) and the information content of the final gate values provide convincing evidence that the learned policy is implementing a non-trivial and effective control strategy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several key details of the implementation are underspecified, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   The composition of the observation vector `o_t` is described conceptually but lacks precise detail. The exact dimensions and the method of concatenation for the average map activations, intermediate classification layer activations, and class probabilities are not given.
    *   The policy `π_θ(o)` is a simple linear map followed by a scaled softmax. A discussion on why this simple form was chosen over a more expressive nonlinear policy (e.g., a small MLP) would be beneficial.
    *   The `UPDATESNES` function is deferred to supplementary material, leaving the main text with an incomplete picture of the learning algorithm.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The experiments are confined to the relatively small-scale CIFAR datasets. A major question is whether this approach can scale to larger-resolution images and datasets like ImageNet. In such a scenario, the number of feature maps, and thus the dimensionality of the action space and the policy parameters, would increase dramatically, potentially making the SNES-based training computationally infeasible. The reported training time of 4 days for the policy on CIFAR-10 with a modest GPU is already substantial and highlights this potential limitation.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope and Ablation:**
    *   The number of refinement steps `T` is fixed to 5. The paper would be strengthened by a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `T`. While the dynamics experiment is informative, it was conducted on models trained with smaller `T` values.
    *   The paper lacks an ablation study on the components of the observation vector `o_t`. It is unclear how crucial each component (map activations, intermediate layer, output probabilities) is for the policy's success.
    *   The performance improvement is reported without statistical significance testing (e.g., results over multiple runs with different random seeds). Given the stochastic nature of the training, this would make the claims more robust.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Enhance Clarity:** In a future version, I would strongly recommend the authors provide a precise mathematical definition of the state vector `o_t`, including the dimensionality of each component. This would significantly improve the paper's clarity and aid reproducibility.

2.  **Address Scalability:** The authors should discuss the computational complexity and potential scalability issues of their approach. It would be valuable to speculate on or propose methods to make the policy training more efficient for larger models, perhaps by exploring more sample-efficient RL algorithms or techniques for parameterizing the policy more compactly (e.g., sharing policy weights across layers).

3.  **Broaden Experimental Analysis:** To strengthen the empirical claims, I suggest:
    *   Running the main T=5 experiment with multiple random seeds and reporting the mean and standard deviation of the accuracy.
    *   Conducting an ablation study to understand the relative importance of the different information sources that constitute the state `o_t`. For instance, how well does a policy perform if it only observes the final class probabilities?
    *   Comparing the learned policy to simpler, handcrafted modulation strategies. For example, a heuristic that boosts the activations of filters most correlated with the initial top prediction could serve as a useful baseline to demonstrate the value of the learned RL policy.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a novel and exciting idea for integrating attentional, recurrent processing into deep neural networks. The proposed dasNet architecture, which uses a learned RL policy to modulate internal filter activities, is a significant conceptual contribution. The method is supported by solid empirical results on CIFAR, demonstrating a clear improvement over a strong baseline. Despite some weaknesses regarding clarity, scalability concerns, and the scope of the experimental evaluation, the strengths of the paper—particularly its originality and potential impact—are substantial. The work opens up promising new avenues for research into more dynamic and computationally flexible neural architectures. I recommend acceptance.
