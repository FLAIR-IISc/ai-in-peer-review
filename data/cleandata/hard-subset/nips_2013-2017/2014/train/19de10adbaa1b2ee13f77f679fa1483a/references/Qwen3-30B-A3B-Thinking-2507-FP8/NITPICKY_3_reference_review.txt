

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which enhances standard CNNs by introducing feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust filter weights across multiple passes through the same image. The authors claim their method improves classification performance on ambiguous cases by learning a reinforcement learning policy (evolved using SNES) to selectively emphasize or suppress features.

## Quality

The paper has several significant technical issues that undermine its quality. First, the paper claims SNES is used "due to the large number of parameters (over 1 million)" but fails to justify why standard RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) would be infeasible. The authors should provide a clear comparison of computational requirements between SNES and standard RL methods for this problem size.

The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) contains critical inconsistencies. Line 6 states "a0 ⇐ 1 {Initialise gates a with identity activation}" but then claims "the action has no effect" in the first pass. If a0 is initialized to 1, the first pass should be identical to the standard CNN, not "without effect." This contradiction needs clarification.

The paper states "the number of steps was experimentally determined and fixed at T = 5" but provides no ablation study or justification for this choice. Without demonstrating T=5 is optimal (or even reasonable), the claim that it's "small enough to be computationally tractable while still allowing for enough interaction" is unsubstantiated.

The paper claims a "relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN" but fails to provide absolute accuracy numbers, making it impossible to assess the significance of this improvement. Without baseline accuracy on CIFAR-10/100, this claim is meaningless.

The reward function (Equations 8-10) is poorly justified. The paper states "misclassified samples are weighted higher than correctly classified samples" but doesn't explain why this particular weighting scheme is optimal for the attention mechanism.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from multiple formatting inconsistencies and notational errors:

1. Equation (5): "yℓj = a ℓ j i=c∑ i=0 ϕ(xi ∗ F ℓi,j)" contains a clear typo - the "a ℓ j" should be "a^ℓ_j" or "a_{ℓ,j}" for proper mathematical notation.

2. Equation (2): "yℓj,x,y = b max i=0 yℓ−1j·b+i,x,y" is confusingly written. It should be "yℓj,x,y = max_{i=0}^{b-1} yℓ−1,j·b+i,x,y" to indicate the maximum over the correct range of indices.

3. Equation (7): "πθi(o) = dim(A)σ(θiot) = at" is mathematically confusing. The "dim(A)" should be incorporated into the softmax function definition.

4. The paper inconsistently uses layer indexing (sometimes ℓ, sometimes j), which creates confusion in the description of the architecture.

5. The reference list contains multiple incomplete citations (e.g., "Neural network model for a mechanism of pattern recognition unaffected by shift in position - Neocognitron, K. Fukushima, In: Trans. IECE J62-A(10), , ,") that fail to follow standard formatting conventions.

## Originality

The paper's claim of novelty is questionable. The authors cite work by Bar et al. [37] on top-down projections in visual cortex but fail to connect their method to this biological basis. The related work section mentions "recurrent connections" and "feedback" but doesn't clearly differentiate dasNet from existing approaches like the Neural Abstraction Pyramid [48,49] or other recurrent CNN architectures.

The paper states "this research is often applied to simplified datasets for demonstration purposes due to computation constraints, and are not aimed at improving the state-of-the-art" but provides no evidence that their approach is fundamentally different from previous work in terms of computational feasibility or performance gains.

## Significance

The claim of "new state-of-the-art result" is unsubstantiated without proper comparison to current methods. The paper doesn't provide absolute accuracy numbers or compare against other state-of-the-art models on CIFAR-10/100. The 6% relative improvement claim is meaningless without context.

The paper also fails to demonstrate that dasNet avoids catastrophic forgetting when correcting misclassifications. The claim that "dasNet must aim at fixing these erroneous predictions without disrupting, or forgetting, what has been learned" is not supported by any experimental evidence.

## Conclusion

While the idea of using feedback connections for selective attention in CNNs is interesting, the paper suffers from multiple technical flaws, inconsistent notation, unsubstantiated claims, and insufficient experimental validation. The quality issues significantly undermine the paper's scientific contribution, despite the potentially interesting concept.

The paper would benefit from: (1) clarifying the algorithm and notation inconsistencies; (2) providing ablation studies for key hyperparameters like T; (3) reporting absolute accuracy numbers; (4) providing proper comparisons to state-of-the-art methods; and (5) justifying the choice of SNES over standard RL methods.

Given these substantial issues, I recommend rejection. The paper does not present a complete, well-justified piece of work that makes a demonstrable contribution to the field.
