Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a feedforward Maxout CNN with recurrent “attention” via multiplicative gates applied to convolutional feature maps across multiple passes over the same input. A policy maps observations (average feature-map activations per layer, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities) to an action vector of per-map scaling coefficients. The policy is trained post hoc (the CNN is fixed) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted cross-entropy loss at the final time step T. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over a Maxout baseline, claiming new state-of-the-art for “unaugmented data.” Qualitative visualizations and a small dynamics study suggest the recurrent controller can up-/down-weight features to correct ambiguous cases.

Relation to prior work
The idea of top-down modulation/recurrent refinement is well-motivated by vision neuroscience and earlier ML work on recurrent/attentional processing, saccades, and feedback (e.g., Fukushima, O’Reilly, Larochelle & Hinton foveal glimpses, and other RL/attention papers). The model can be seen as a simple multiplicative gating controller stacked on a pre-trained CNN, reminiscent of recurrent or stacked refinement approaches. Using NES/SNES for black-box optimization is established in neuroevolution. Compared to previous work, the main novelty is the specific design of per-layer channel-wise gates computed from global layer summaries, trained via SNES at scale.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and generally applicable: channel-wise gates that modulate internal activations across passes.
- Clear description of the gating mechanism and observation structure; positivity constraint via scaled softmax is sensible for stability.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; qualitative examples are plausible and the small dynamics experiment shows stable behavior beyond trained T.
- Pragmatic use of SNES to handle a very large policy parameterization.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Empirical validation and baselines:
- The “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” claim is unclear or contradictory. The Maxout baseline was trained with data augmentation; do the reported comparisons use augmented or unaugmented data? Please clarify and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- The magnitude of improvement (“6% relative”) is modest and may be within run-to-run variance. There are no error bars, multiple seeds, or statistical tests. The table is not included in the submission text; exact numbers vs. comparable published baselines are needed.
- Missing ablations: (a) benefit of multiple passes vs. a single-pass gating controller; (b) effect of the observation components (map means vs. logits vs. probabilities); (c) action scope (which layers benefit); (d) sensitivity to T, λcorrect/λmisclassified, and SNES settings; (e) comparison to simply increasing capacity of the feedforward CNN to match dasNet’s extra parameters/compute; (f) ensembling or test-time averaging (e.g., dropout sampling) that also leverages multiple “looks” at the data.

2) Methodological framing:
- Despite the RL language, the setup is a deterministic, differentiable mapping whose objective is the final cross-entropy at time T. Given that the base CNN is fixed, gradients could be backpropagated through the unrolled gating steps to train the policy by standard supervised learning (or policy gradients with reparameterization), avoiding black-box evolution. The choice of SNES seems driven by convenience rather than necessity. This weakens the RL motivation.
- The hand-crafted boosting of misclassified samples (λmisclassified >> λcorrect) is ad hoc; sensitivity analysis and validation-based tuning are missing. The impact on calibration is not discussed.

3) Compute and practicality:
- Test-time cost scales roughly linearly with T; latency is increased 5× for T=5. There is no analysis of accuracy-vs-compute trade-offs compared to standard alternatives (e.g., slightly larger CNNs, test-time augmentation).
- Training with SNES (50 candidates per generation, 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is heavy and likely sensitive to hyperparameters. Reproducibility may be challenging.

4) Reproducibility and clarity:
- Important architectural details are missing: exact number of maps per layer, dimension of the action and observation vectors, whether the policy is linear (the equation suggests a single affine layer + softmax) or has hidden layers, and normalization of observations. Without these, reproduction is difficult.
- The dynamics study uses a “small 2-layer” net; the main model is 3 conv + 1 FC maxout. It is unclear whether the stability observations transfer.
- The gate-information analysis (using gates alone for kNN/logistic regression) is intriguing but underexplained (train/test protocol? dataset split?).

5) Positioning vs. established methods:
- The gating effectively implements per-channel attention akin to learned feature recalibration. A straightforward feedforward controller trained end-to-end (single pass) could be a strong baseline and would be more in line with established supervised training practices.
- The paper lacks comparison to other recurrent/refinement methods evaluated on CIFAR, which would better contextualize the gains.

Questions for the authors
- Provide the exact test errors (with standard deviations over multiple runs) for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and clarify the augmentation status for each result and baseline.
- What is the full policy architecture and the dimensions of O and A? How many additional parameters does dasNet add?
- Did you try backprop through time to train the policy (with the CNN fixed)? If so, how did it compare?
- How sensitive are results to T and to λboost hyperparameters? What is the compute/latency overhead at test time?
- Does gating at earlier layers matter? What happens if you only gate the top conv layer?
- How do results compare to an equally expensive test-time augmentation or an ensemble of Maxout models?

Pros
- Interesting, biologically inspired idea; simple mechanism; potentially broadly applicable.
- Some empirical improvements on standard datasets; qualitative analyses are thoughtful.

Cons
- Limited, ambiguous empirical evidence and missing strong baselines/ablations; unclear significance of gains.
- RL framing overstated; black-box evolution seems unnecessary for the deterministic objective.
- Compute overhead and reproducibility concerns; missing key implementation details.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is promising and the paper is clearly written, the empirical validation is insufficient by NIPS standards, the methodological framing is not compelling relative to simpler, established training approaches, and key comparisons/ablations are missing. Strengthening the evaluation (clear, reproducible numbers with error bars; thorough ablations; fair, modern baselines; and a more principled training approach) could make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
