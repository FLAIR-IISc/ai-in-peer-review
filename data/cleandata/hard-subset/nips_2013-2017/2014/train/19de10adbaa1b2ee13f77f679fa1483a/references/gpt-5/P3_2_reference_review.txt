Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal selective attention via recurrent feedback connections. The mechanism is simple: on each pass over the same image, multiplicative gates aℓj rescale the activation of each feature map, and the next action vector a is computed by a policy πθ that observes a summary of internal activations (per-map averages, classification layer pre-activations, and softmax outputs). After standard supervised training of a Maxout CNN, the policy is trained to improve classification on difficult cases by repeatedly processing an input for T steps, updating gates at each step. Because the policy parameterization is large (≈10^6 weights), the authors optimize θ with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), using a terminal cross-entropy-like loss with boosted weighting for misclassified samples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show small but consistent gains over the base Maxout model, with qualitative analyses of gating dynamics and a small stability study varying the number of recurrent steps.

Relation to prior work
The work is situated within biologically inspired top-down modulation and recurrent processing, and within ML literature on attention and RL for perception (e.g., learned saccades, feedback for denoising, iterative reconstruction). The specific contribution is a generic, model-agnostic way to modulate feature maps across layers using a learned controller. This differs from earlier “glimpse”-based spatial attention and from local competitive mechanisms; it is closer to top-down channel-wise modulation. The use of NES for policy optimization is pragmatic given dimensionality.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: feature-map gating is easy to implement on top of strong CNN baselines, and the observation/action formulation is straightforward.
- Demonstrated improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with a focus on correcting ambiguous errors. The dynamics experiment suggests stability beyond the trained horizon.
- The design choices (positive, mean-one gates via scaled softmax) are sensible and help prevent runaway amplification.
- The proposed observation vector is lightweight and avoids needing to expose full feature maps to the controller.
- The paper connects to biologically plausible top-down modulation and offers an interpretable mechanism (even if qualitative analysis remains challenging).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: Crucial details are missing to reproduce results: full architecture specs (number of maps per layer, kernel sizes, strides), dimensioning of observation/action vectors per layer, T’s selection procedure, SNES hyperparameters (learning rates, number of generations), and the exact training/validation protocol for policy optimization. The text also seems inconsistent regarding data augmentation (stating both that augmentation was used to train M and claiming “unaugmented data” SOTA).
- Baselines and ablations are limited:
  - No comparison to simpler alternatives that do not require RL/evolution, e.g., supervised training of the gating policy by backprop through time on the same terminal loss (the whole network is differentiable), or even a static per-class/per-layer rescaling at test time.
  - No ablation of observation components (per-map averages vs classification pre-activations vs probabilities), or of the boosting term. A baseline where gates are fixed to 1 for T>0 would help separate purely recurrent computation from learned modulation.
  - No comparison to iterative ensembling (averaging predictions over T identical passes), test-time augmentation, or to discriminatively retraining the final layers on hard examples.
- RL framing vs. supervised optimization: The “reward” is the terminal negative log-probability of the true label; there is no bandit/exploration aspect or partial observability in the usual sense. This is effectively black-box supervised optimization of a recurrent controller. Gradient-based BPTT through the recurrent gating would be natural and probably more sample-efficient.
- Overfitting risk and evaluation protocol: The SNES-trained policy has ~10^6 parameters and is trained on the same training set used for the CNN. There is no mention of a held-out validation set to choose T, early stopping, or to tune λboost, λL2. Without this, improvements may reflect overfitting to the train distribution. Report training vs. test curves and validate hyperparameters on a separate set.
- Effect size and scope: Improvements appear modest; the paper should quantify absolute error reductions and confidence intervals, and clarify whether the gains are concentrated on specific confusing pairs (e.g., cat/dog) or broadly distributed.
- Interpretation experiments: The claim that final gate values alone achieve ~40–46% accuracy (with kNN/logistic regression) is intriguing but unclear: on which dataset? If CIFAR-100, these numbers seem implausibly high relative to the base model; more detail and sanity checks are needed.
- Computation: Training took 4 days for policy learning alone; scalability to larger datasets/models is uncertain. NES with diagonal covariance may be suboptimal given strong parameter correlations across layers.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the experimental section lacks ablations, strong baselines, and rigorous validation. The RL formulation is not essential to the method and may be suboptimal.

Clarity
Generally well-written and motivated, but reproducibility suffers due to missing details, and there are inconsistencies (augmentation vs. “unaugmented” claim). Equations and algorithm description are clear.

Originality
Moderately original. Channel-wise top-down modulation in CNNs is familiar, but applying a learned multi-step, global gating policy driven by internal activations and trained with NES is a novel combination for classification.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a general mechanism to adapt feedforward CNNs at test time. Current empirical gains are modest but on standard benchmarks; stronger baselines/ablations would better establish significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full architecture and training details; resolve the augmentation inconsistency; report validation protocol.
- Add baselines: BPTT-trained policy, static per-layer scaling, iterative ensembling, hard-example fine-tuning, TTA.
- Ablate observation components, boosting weights, and T; report class-wise confusion improvements.
- Analyze overfitting with a held-out validation set; report variance across runs.
- Clarify and expand the gate-only classification experiment; include qualitative gate distributions across classes.

Pros
- Simple, generic idea; biologically motivated.
- Demonstrated gains over a strong baseline.
- Clear mechanism and stable dynamics.

Cons
- Missing baselines/ablations and validation protocol.
- Questionable RL framing; likely suboptimal optimization choice.
- Reproducibility gaps and some inconsistencies.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The idea is interesting and the results promising, but the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation and clearer reporting.
