## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections (dasNet)

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to enhancing CNN performance through internal selective attention mechanisms implemented via learned feedback connections and reinforcement learning. The idea of allowing a network to "think" for multiple passes, focusing on different features, is inspired by human visual processing and tackles a crucial limitation of standard CNNs – their inflexibility after training. While the results are promising, showing a performance boost on challenging CIFAR datasets, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed analysis of *how* the attention mechanism works and a slightly limited scope of evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the points raised below.

**Summary:**

The authors introduce dasNet, a novel architecture that extends Maxout CNNs with learned feedback connections.  These connections enable selective attention by weighting the activations of convolutional filters across layers during multiple forward passes. This attention policy is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) after the CNN is pre-trained with supervised learning. The core hypothesis is that by allowing the network to iteratively refine its focus, it can improve classification accuracy, especially on difficult instances. The paper demonstrates performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showcasing the network's ability to correct misclassifications by emphasizing and de-emphasizing specific filters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of learned feedback connections and reinforcement learning for internal attention in CNNs is a novel contribution.  While recurrent connections have been explored (as discussed in the Related Work), the use of RL to *learn* these connections for selective attention is a significant step.
* **Motivation:** The biological inspiration and the rationale behind allowing CNNs to re-evaluate inputs – mirroring human visual processing – is strong and well-articulated.  The authors convincingly argue that initial predictions aren't always sufficient and that iterative refinement can be beneficial.
* **Empirical Results:** The performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are encouraging, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the unaugmented datasets. The visualization of filter changes (Figure 3) provides a qualitative glimpse into the attention mechanism's operation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The implementation details are clearly explained, and the choice of SNES for policy optimization is justified given the large parameter space.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant literature on recurrent processing, attention mechanisms, and reinforcement learning in the context of computer vision.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of Attention Mechanism:** While the authors show *that* the network changes filter weights, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *what* the network is learning to attend to.  What specific features are being emphasized/de-emphasized, and why? Is there a pattern to the changes that relates to the type of misclassification? More in-depth analysis beyond Figure 3 is needed to understand the learned policy.
* **SNES Complexity:** While justified, the reliance on SNES makes the approach potentially less accessible to researchers without expertise in evolutionary strategies. A discussion of the computational cost and potential alternatives (e.g., policy gradient methods with careful parameterization) would be valuable.
* **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR datasets. While these are good benchmarks, the generalizability to more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) remains unclear. Demonstrating performance on a more challenging dataset would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameters such as `T` (number of passes), `λcorrect`, `λmisclassified`, and `λL2` on performance isn't thoroughly explored. A sensitivity analysis would help establish the robustness of the approach.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** Ablation studies to assess the contribution of specific components (e.g., the choice of Maxout networks, the specific reinforcement learning algorithm) are missing.  For example, how would dasNet perform with a different base architecture like ResNet?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to enhancing CNN performance through learned attention mechanisms.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate a clear performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis of the learned attention mechanism hinders understanding of *how* the system works.
* The limited scope of evaluation (CIFAR datasets only) raises concerns about generalizability.
* The reliance on SNES might limit accessibility for some researchers.

**Recommendations:**

* **Major Revision:** I recommend acceptance with a major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically:
    * **Expand the analysis of the attention mechanism.** Provide more detailed visualizations and quantitative metrics to understand *what* features the network is attending to and *why*.  Consider using techniques like activation maximization to interpret the learned filters.
    * **Discuss generalizability.** Acknowledge the limitations of the CIFAR experiments and outline future work to explore performance on more complex datasets.
    * **Include a sensitivity analysis.** Investigate the impact of key hyperparameters on performance.
    * **Consider ablation studies.** Evaluate the contribution of specific components of the dasNet architecture.
    * **Provide a more accessible discussion of SNES.** Briefly compare it to alternative RL methods and highlight the trade-offs.



**Overall, this is a valuable contribution that pushes the boundaries of CNN architecture and learning. Addressing the suggested revisions will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.**
