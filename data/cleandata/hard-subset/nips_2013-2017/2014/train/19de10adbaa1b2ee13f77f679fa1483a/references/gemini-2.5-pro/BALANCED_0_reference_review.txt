### Summary

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates a recurrent, top-down attention mechanism into a pre-trained feedforward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The core idea is inspired by the two-stage process of human vision: a fast, initial feedforward pass followed by a slower, more deliberate attentional phase for difficult stimuli. The authors augment a standard Maxout network by introducing modulatory gates (`a_j^l`) that can scale the output of each feature map in each convolutional layer. After an initial forward pass, a policy network observes the internal state of the CNN (average map activations, intermediate layer outputs, and class probabilities) and decides how to adjust these modulatory gates for a subsequent forward pass. This process is repeated for a fixed number of steps (`T`). The policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method suitable for the high-dimensional parameter space of the policy network. The objective is to correct initial misclassifications on challenging examples. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this iterative refinement process improves classification accuracy over the baseline Maxout network, achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data at the time.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation of an *internal* selective attention mechanism. While attention models in computer vision often focus on spatial regions of the input image (e.g., saccades), this work proposes to modulate the feature detectors themselves. The idea of learning a policy to dynamically re-weight the importance of internal filters over multiple "thoughts" or passes is a creative and powerful concept. The connection to the neuroscience of recurrent processing in the visual cortex provides strong motivation.

2.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles an important and difficult problem: improving model performance on ambiguous or challenging inputs that a single feedforward pass might misclassify. Framing this as a sequential decision-making problem and using Reinforcement Learning (RL) to learn a control policy is an elegant approach. The choice of SNES to handle the large, continuous action space is a practical and well-justified engineering decision, avoiding the difficulties of applying traditional RL algorithms like policy gradients to such a large parameter space.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a clear performance improvement on both CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 over a strong baseline (Maxout networks). Achieving a new state-of-the-art result, even on a specific benchmark setup, is a significant accomplishment and validates the potential of the proposed approach.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide a qualitative analysis (Figure 3) that attempts to visualize the effect of the learned policy. Although interpreting high-level feature maps is notoriously difficult, the visualization effectively shows that the policy is making non-trivial, complex adjustments to correct an initial misclassification. The additional experiment confirming that the final gate values contain significant class-discriminative information is a clever piece of analysis that strengthens the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful strategy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The proposed method is computationally intensive. Training requires a fully pre-trained network followed by a lengthy evolution-based RL phase (reported as 4 days). More importantly, inference time is increased by a factor of `T` (in this case, 5), which may be prohibitive for many real-world applications. The experiments are also confined to the relatively small CIFAR datasets. It is unclear how the approach would scale to larger models and higher-resolution images (e.g., ImageNet), where the state and action spaces for the policy would become immense.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the overall idea is well-explained, some key details are sparse. The exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network is not fully specified, which hinders reproducibility. The choice of the number of steps `T=5` is described as "experimentally determined" without providing an analysis of how this hyperparameter affects performance. Similarly, the RL formulation is more akin to black-box optimization of a fixed-horizon rollout than a general RL problem with intermediate rewards, and this distinction could be made clearer.

3.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study. For example:
    *   How does performance vary as `T` increases or decreases? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
    *   What is the impact of modulating only specific layers (e.g., only the final convolutional layer vs. all layers)?
    *   How sensitive is the model to the choice of RL algorithm? Could a simpler optimization scheme work just as well?

4.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section provides an excellent overview of the neuroscience background. However, it could be strengthened by a more direct comparison to contemporary attention mechanisms in machine learning, such as spatial attention models (e.g., [41] in the paper) or other forms of recurrent processing in deep networks. A discussion of how dasNet's *internal feature attention* differs from or complements *spatial attention* would help situate the work more broadly within the field.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Computational Analysis:** I would encourage the authors to include a more explicit discussion of the computational trade-offs. A table comparing training time, inference time (ms/image), and number of parameters for the baseline Maxout net vs. dasNet would be very informative for practitioners.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Providing a plot of performance vs. the number of steps `T` would significantly strengthen the experimental section and provide valuable insight into the dynamics of the recurrent process.
*   **Expand on RL Framing:** Clarifying why the problem is framed as RL versus direct black-box optimization of the policy parameters against the final loss could be helpful. While SNES is a black-box optimizer, the sequential nature (state -> action -> new state) is a core part of the model, and a slightly more detailed discussion of this framing would be welcome.
*   **Future Work on Scalability:** While potentially out of scope for the current paper, a brief discussion on potential paths to scaling this method to larger datasets like ImageNet (e.g., modulating only a subset of layers, using a more sample-efficient RL algorithm) would enhance the paper's impact.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for improving CNN performance through a learned internal attention mechanism. The strengths in originality and empirical results outweigh the weaknesses related to computational cost and experimental scope. It is a solid and interesting contribution to the field.
