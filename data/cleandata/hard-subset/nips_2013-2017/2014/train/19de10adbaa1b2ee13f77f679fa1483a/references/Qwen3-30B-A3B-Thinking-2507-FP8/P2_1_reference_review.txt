

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to incorporating selective attention mechanisms in deep convolutional neural networks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (specifically a Maxout network) with a reinforcement learning (RL) component that learns to modulate the activation of filters across multiple passes through the same image. The authors argue that humans often need multiple "passes" to correctly classify similar objects (e.g., distinguishing between two similar bird species), and they hypothesize that CNNs could benefit from a similar iterative process. 

dasNet achieves this by adding gating mechanisms that weight the output maps of each layer differently on successive passes. The RL policy (learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies due to the large number of parameters) determines how to adjust these weights to improve classification on ambiguous cases. The authors evaluate their approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, reporting a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on attention mechanisms (e.g., the SE block [51] and CBAM [52] which incorporate attention in a feedforward manner), as well as research on recurrent processing in vision [38, 39, 48-50]. It differs from these by using RL to learn attention policies rather than incorporating attention mechanisms directly into the network architecture.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

### Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-justified methodology. The authors provide a clear explanation of how dasNet modifies standard Maxout networks, and the RL component is appropriately chosen (SNES) to handle the large parameter space. The experimental design is solid, with careful attention to evaluating performance on ambiguous cases where the method is most relevant.

**Strengths:**
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the attention dynamics, showing how specific filters are emphasized or suppressed across passes
- The authors provide evidence that the learned gates contain meaningful information for classification (via the 15-nearest neighbor and logistic regression experiments)
- The ablation study of different numbers of passes (T) demonstrates the expected behavior (performance peaks at T and remains stable beyond that)

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM, or other RL-based attention approaches) beyond the stated state-of-the-art comparison
- The computational cost is high (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU), which is not thoroughly discussed in relation to practical deployment
- The theoretical justification for why this approach works is limited - the paper demonstrates that it works, but doesn't deeply explain why it's effective
- The paper doesn't explore how dasNet might handle more complex attention patterns beyond simple filter weighting (e.g., spatial attention)

The paper is a complete piece of work with well-supported claims, but it would be strengthened by addressing these weaknesses.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written, logically organized, and clearly explains the technical concepts. The motivation is compelling, the methodology is well-described, and the results are presented effectively with helpful visualizations (Figure 3 is particularly informative).

**Strengths:**
- The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented and well-commented
- The figures (especially Figure 3) effectively illustrate the attention dynamics
- The writing is accessible to the NIPS audience, with appropriate technical depth

**Areas for Improvement:**
- The explanation of SNES could be more detailed for readers unfamiliar with this optimization method
- Some mathematical notations (e.g., the observation vector construction) could be better explained
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations and potential failure cases

Overall, the paper is clear and well-organized, but could be made even more accessible with minor improvements to the explanations of certain technical elements.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel approach to incorporating attention mechanisms in deep networks. While attention mechanisms have been widely studied in deep learning (e.g., [51, 52]), dasNet's use of reinforcement learning to learn attention policies through feedback connections represents a distinct contribution.

**Strengths:**
- The authors effectively distinguish their approach from previous attention mechanisms by emphasizing the "internal selective attention" through feedback connections rather than external attention mechanisms
- The combination of RL with feedback connections to enable multiple passes over the same image is novel
- The focus on correcting misclassifications on ambiguous cases is well-motivated and distinct from many other attention-based approaches

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could better articulate how this approach differs from previous work on recurrent networks for image classification [48-50]
- The related work section mentions attention mechanisms but doesn't thoroughly compare with them, making it harder to assess the true novelty
- The paper doesn't explicitly state how dasNet differs from other "iterative" approaches to image classification

The paper's novelty is clear, but the distinction from related work could be more explicitly articulated.

### Significance

The paper claims to establish a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 with a 6% relative improvement, which is significant for this benchmark dataset. The approach addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning: improving classification on ambiguous cases where the network initially makes mistakes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper demonstrates that dasNet can successfully correct misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing a cat from a dog)
- The insights into how the network "thinks" about ambiguous cases provide valuable theoretical understanding
- The approach could potentially be applied to other domains beyond image classification

**Weaknesses:**
- The improvement is limited to CIFAR-10/100, which are relatively small datasets; it's unclear how the approach would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet
- The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) might limit practical adoption
- The paper doesn't discuss how dasNet could be integrated into existing pipelines or whether it would be compatible with other state-of-the-art techniques

While the results are impressive for the benchmark datasets used, the significance would be greater if the authors could better demonstrate the broader applicability of their approach.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Conceptually interesting approach to incorporating attention mechanisms through feedback connections
- Well-designed experiments that clearly demonstrate the method's effectiveness on ambiguous cases
- Clear visualizations of the attention dynamics that provide valuable insights
- Good theoretical grounding in visual attention mechanisms and reinforcement learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with other attention mechanisms (beyond stating it's the new state-of-the-art)
- High computational cost without thorough discussion of practical implications
- Insufficient theoretical justification for why the approach works
- Limited discussion of how the approach might scale to larger datasets or tasks

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work presents a novel and well-executed approach to incorporating selective attention in deep CNNs through feedback connections learned with reinforcement learning. The results are compelling, with a clear demonstration of the method's effectiveness on challenging ambiguous cases. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison with related work and theoretical justification, these do not undermine the overall quality of the contribution.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating how reinforcement learning can be used to implement a form of "internal reflection" in deep networks, allowing them to correct their initial misclassifications. This is a significant step toward creating more human-like visual recognition systems that can "think" about ambiguous cases.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents results that advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. While the computational cost is high, the approach is theoretically interesting and could inspire future work to make it more efficient.

I believe this paper would be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community and would make a valuable contribution to the conference proceedings.
