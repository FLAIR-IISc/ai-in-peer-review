This paper introduces dasNet, a CNN augmented with feedback connections and an attentional policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, SNES) to selectively emphasize or de-emphasize filters during iterative processing of images. The motivation, drawing a parallel to human visual processing, is to improve classification performance on difficult instances by allowing the network to “think” about the input. While the concept is intriguing, the paper presents a significant number of concerns regarding methodological rigor and justification.

The core idea isn't entirely novel. Recurrent connections and attention mechanisms have been explored in computer vision (as the authors acknowledge in their related work), and the use of RL to refine network behavior is also not new. However, the authors’ approach of applying RL to modulate *internal* CNN filters – rather than, for instance, attentional gating of input features – is where they attempt to differentiate their work. The claim of achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100, while tempting, is insufficient without a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods.  The authors only mention “outperforming a previous state-of-the-art CNN” without identifying *which* CNN, or detailing the extent of the improvement across multiple metrics.

My primary concern revolves around the complexity and lack of justification for the chosen RL approach. Employing SNES, while acknowledged as a pragmatic choice given the parameter count, feels like a workaround rather than a principled solution. The use of a relatively small population size (50) and a limited number of training steps (4 days on a GTX 560Ti) raises doubts about the effectiveness of the learning process. The reward function itself is simplistic – boosting performance on misclassified samples – and lacks nuance. A more carefully designed reward, perhaps incorporating information about the entropy of the filter activations or the consistency of the classification over iterations, could potentially lead to more robust and interpretable results. 

Furthermore, the provided visualizations (Figure 3) are intriguing but lack quantitative analysis. Describing filter changes as "complex suppression and enhancement patterns" is insufficient. A quantitative assessment of how specific filters contribute to the correction of misclassifications is needed. The information content estimation via k-NN and logistic regression is a weak attempt to demonstrate policy effectiveness, and could be more rigorous. A proper ablation study, systematically removing components of the attentional policy and observing the impact on performance, is critical. 

The paper also lacks a thorough analysis of the dynamics of the iterative process. While Figure 2 shows performance curves for different values of T, it doesn’t explain *why* performance degrades after T steps. Is this due to overfitting to the current image, or a fundamental limitation of the policy? A deeper investigation of the state space and the learned policy is necessary to understand the behavior of dasNet.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of leveraging recurrent connections and RL to refine CNN behavior is potentially promising.
*   The authors demonstrate a performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100, albeit without sufficient benchmarking.
*   The paper provides some visualization of the filter changes, offering a glimpse into the internal workings of dasNet.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Justification for using SNES is weak; alternative RL algorithms should be considered.
*   Reward function is simplistic and lacks nuance.
*   Quantitative analysis of filter changes is missing.
*   Insufficient analysis of the dynamics of the iterative process.
*   Limited discussion of potential failure cases and limitations of the approach.
*   The experiments are not easily reproducible given the limited details provided.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:** The paper presents an interesting idea but lacks the methodological rigor and comprehensive evaluation necessary for NIPS acceptance. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are unsubstantiated, and the RL approach feels ad-hoc. 

**For Acceptance (with major revisions):** If the authors significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more detailed analysis of the learned policy, and address the concerns outlined above, the paper could potentially be reconsidered. Specifically, they need to provide a comparative analysis against stronger baselines and a more robust justification for their methodological choices.

**Overall:** I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. The work has potential, but requires substantial revision to meet the standards of NIPS.
