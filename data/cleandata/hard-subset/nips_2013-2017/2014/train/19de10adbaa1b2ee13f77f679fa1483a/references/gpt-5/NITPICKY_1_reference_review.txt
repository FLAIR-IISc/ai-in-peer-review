Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained Maxout CNN with recurrent, multiplicative, per-feature-map gates that are updated over multiple passes on the same image. A learned policy maps observations (averaged feature-map activations across layers, classification-layer pre-activations, and class probabilities) to actions (layer-wise channel gates). The policy is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the large parameter count (~1M). On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet reportedly improves over the baseline Maxout CNN, with the claim of state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10. The method is motivated by biological top-down modulation and selective attention.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Maxout+dropout and adds recurrent top-down modulation reminiscent of biologically inspired feedback. It is related to recurrent/attention models that modulate intermediate representations, RL-based saccading/glimpses, and feedback models in visual processing. The specific novelty claimed is the internal, global (channel-wise) selective attention within a strong modern CNN plus an RL-trained policy via SNES.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: per-layer channel reweighting over multiple passes to correct initial mistakes.
- Uses a strong baseline (Maxout) and shows improvements on standard benchmarks.
- Practical training procedure: deterministic policy, stable gating via scaled softmax, and SNES to handle high dimensionality.
- Biological plausibility and alignment with evidence for feedback/attention.
- Some analysis of dynamics (performance peaks around the trained number of steps), and an attempt to probe whether gate activations are informative.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality (technical correctness, experimental support)
- Inconsistent/unclear loss definition. Eq. (8–10): Li = −λboost d log(vT). d is referred to as the correct class, vT is a probability vector. Eq. (10) uses d = ||vT||∞ to decide λboost, which confounds the class index with the max probability. The definition appears incorrect/misprinted and should be clarified precisely (e.g., −λboost log vT[d]). This is nontrivial (affects training).
- Policy architecture is underspecified. πθ is described as a “neural network,” but Eq. (7) suggests a single linear layer plus softmax. No architecture, initialization, or regularization details are given for the policy network beyond L2; details are essential given ~1M parameters.
- Reproducibility gaps: the Maxout architecture lacks specifics (number of maps per layer, kernel sizes, strides, block sizes, parameter counts per layer). The SNES hyperparameters (learning rates, sampling variance schedules, fitness shaping, population size justification) are not described beyond population=50. “Details in supplementary material” for the SNES update is insufficient for NeurIPS standards.
- No validation protocol described for policy training (early stopping/selection). Stopping is said to occur when “no improvement for several epochs” but whether this is measured on a held-out validation set is not stated. Overfitting risk is high given multiple passes and powerful policy.
- The “unaugmented” SOTA claim is contradictory: the Maxout backbone is trained “with data augmentation,” yet Table 1 is said to report unaugmented performance. Please clarify whether test-time augmentation or training-time augmentation is used; claims must match the evaluation protocol of prior work.
- Lack of ablations: 
  - T (number of passes) is fixed to 5 for main results; only a small dynamics study is shown on a “small 2-layer” model with T∈{1,2}. A full ablation on T for the main model is necessary.
  - Effect of observation components (feature-map averages vs. classifier pre-activations vs. probabilities).
  - Gating location (post-conv, pre-/post-activation, effect relative to maxout pooling).
  - Static per-channel scaling learned end-to-end vs. dynamic, recurrent gating; and BPTT-trained recurrent gating vs. SNES.
- Baseline fairness: compare to (i) a larger Maxout model with similar parameter increase, (ii) adding one more layer, (iii) simple test-time ensembling over multiple passes without attention (to separate recurrence from learned gating), (iv) end-to-end SGD on the recurrent/gated model with BPTT or REINFORCE instead of SNES.
- The “gates contain information” analysis is potentially flawed: using final gate values to train kNN/logistic regression requires careful dataset splits. It is unclear if this evaluation is performed only on held-out test gates with a classifier trained on training gates; details are missing and leakage would inflate numbers. Also, achieving ~40–46% on CIFAR-100 from gates alone is surprisingly high—please substantiate carefully.

Clarity (writing/organization)
- Numerous notational issues:
  - Eq. (5): clarify whether gating occurs before or after nonlinearity/maxout; biases omitted.
  - Eq. (7): use of o vs. ot inconsistent; the softmax scaling by dim(A) should be defined once clearly; strictly speaking this is not a softmax anymore but a scaled softmax.
  - Eq. (3): maxout classification layer notation is unclear (indexing of Fℓ and the role of b).
- Algorithm 1: signals/variables not consistently defined (e.g., d in line 12), and the footnote on deterministic policy is injected mid-algorithm.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), duplicated (Zeiler & Fergus listed twice), or nonstandard; this needs thorough cleanup.
- Figures described (Figure 1–3) are critical for understanding, but the text includes only partial descriptions; ensure captions and figure references are self-contained.

Originality
- The idea of recurrent feedback and internal attention is not new; the novelty here is specifically the per-channel, layer-wise multiplicative gating driven by an RL-evolved policy atop a modern CNN. This is a reasonable, incremental contribution, but the reliance on SNES rather than end-to-end gradient-based training is not well justified beyond “too many parameters.” At scale, policy-gradient or BPTT through gating seems plausible and would be more standard and possibly stronger.

Significance
- Improvements on CIFAR-10/100 could be impactful if clearly quantified and reproducible. However, the paper omits exact numbers in the text provided (means/standard deviations, multiple trials), lacks strong ablations, and does not quantify test-time compute overhead (T=5 implies ~5× latency). Without rigorous comparisons and clarity on protocols, the significance is hard to assess.

Minor issues and formatting
- Typos/grammar throughout (e.g., “reduced the dimensionality,” “took of dasNet,” inconsistent use of notation and capitalization).
- Missing clarity on training batch size n, number of generations/epochs, and wall-clock for baseline vs. dasNet.
- Ensure test-time dropout is disabled consistently across all T steps.

Questions for the authors
- Provide exact CIFAR-10/100 numbers (mean±std over multiple seeds), with and without augmentation, and clear comparison baselines.
- Clarify the loss in Eqs. (8–10) and the λboost condition; fix notation.
- Detail the policy architecture, SNES hyperparameters, and validation/early-stopping protocol.
- Report compute overhead (train/inference) relative to baseline.
- Provide ablations (T, observation components, gating location, static vs. dynamic, SNES vs. BPTT/REINFORCE).
- Ensure the “gates contain information” experiment uses a proper train/val/test split.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current manuscript has too many methodological and clarity issues (including a likely error in the loss definition), insufficient ablations, incomplete baselines, and significant referencing/formatting problems. With substantial revisions and stronger experimental rigor, this could become a solid contribution.
