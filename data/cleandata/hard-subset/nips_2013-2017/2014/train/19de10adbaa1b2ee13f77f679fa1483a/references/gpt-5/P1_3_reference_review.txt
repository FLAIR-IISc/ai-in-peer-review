Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a way to equip a trained convolutional network with internal, top-down selective attention via feedback connections. Concretely, the authors augment a Maxout CNN so that each feature map has a multiplicative gate aℓj that can be modulated across repeated passes over the same image. At each time step t, an “observation” vector is constructed by concatenating summary statistics of activations (mean feature map activations per layer), intermediate classification layer activations, and the current class probabilities. A learned policy π maps this observation to the next set of gates at+1. The policy is a large linear mapping plus softmax that outputs positive gate values that average to 1. Because the observation/action spaces are high-dimensional, the policy parameters (≈10^6) are trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), using a boosted negative log-likelihood loss on the final pass. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and reportedly achieves state-of-the-art for unaugmented data, while qualitative analyses suggest interpretable emphasis/de-emphasis of higher-layer maps on difficult examples.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-motivated by biological literature on recurrent and top-down feedback in visual cortex. It connects to RL-based visual attention (e.g., saccadic/gaze models), recurrent processing for vision, and earlier feedback-based neural architectures (e.g., Fukushima, O’Reilly). On the deep learning side, it builds on Maxout + dropout and contrasts with purely feedforward CNNs. Compared to prior RL attention works that operate on spatial glimpses, this paper modulates internal feature maps. Compared to recurrent CNNs trained end-to-end, the novelty is the explicit gating policy learned by black-box evolution acting on layer statistics.

Strengths
- Clear, biologically inspired idea: modulate internal feature maps across multiple passes under a learned control policy.
- Simple mechanism with minimal architectural changes to a strong baseline (Maxout) and compatible with standard CNNs.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 and qualitative evidence that multi-pass feedback corrects confusions (e.g., cat vs. dog).
- Stability analysis: performance peaks near the trained number of steps T and remains stable beyond, suggesting reasonable dynamics.
- Interesting diagnostic showing that final gate values alone carry class-relevant information.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines. Missing comparisons to simpler or more standard alternatives:
  - End-to-end gradient-based training of a recurrent/gated CNN via unrolled backprop (BPTT) with the same observation/gating interface.
  - A one-shot conditional gating model (policy predicts gates in a single pass) or static per-map scaling learned with SGD.
  - Curriculum/hard-example fine-tuning on the baseline Maxout without attention but with the same boosted loss.
  - Random or heuristic policies and smaller policies (e.g., per-layer or low-rank θ).
- Methodological clarity:
  - The policy appears to be a single linear layer with softmax; the paper should justify capacity vs. overfitting and report dim(O) and dim(A) explicitly.
  - The exact placement of gates relative to Maxout pooling is described but could be made crisper; a schematic per layer would help.
  - “State-of-the-art for unaugmented data” seems inconsistent with the stated use of data augmentation to train M; please clarify which numbers use augmentation and ensure fair comparisons.
- Optimization and “RL” framing:
  - The objective is a supervised, final-step cross-entropy optimized by SNES; there is no explicit sequential reward or credit assignment across steps. The “RL” framing is somewhat stretched. A discussion comparing SNES to policy gradient or differentiable surrogates (e.g., straight-through or reparameterization) would be valuable.
  - No validation protocol or early stopping criterion is described for evolving θ; risk of overfitting is not quantified.
- Experimental depth:
  - Missing ablations: which observation components matter, per-layer importance of gates, effect of T on test performance, and the trade-off in compute (T× cost) vs. accuracy.
  - No per-class or confusion-matrix analysis to support the “ambiguous pairs” narrative.
  - Runtime, memory, and wall-clock training/inference overheads are not reported beyond “4 days on a GTX 560 Ti.”

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, and the mechanism is implementable. However, the empirical evaluation is thinner than ideal for a NIPS paper establishing a new control mechanism: crucial baselines and ablations are missing, and several details (policy dimensions, hyperparameters for SNES, validation) are underspecified. Claims of state-of-the-art need precise, apples-to-apples comparisons.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Specify dim(O), dim(A), the per-layer channel counts, kernel sizes, and block sizes; include a table of architecture details to enable reproduction.
- Clarify the “unaugmented” claim and provide the exact error rates and standard deviations over multiple runs.
- Briefly outline SNES update hyperparameters (learning rates, fitness shaping) or include them in supplementary material.
- Minor issues: duplicated references, footnote formatting, and a few notational inconsistencies.

Originality
The combination of internal per-map gating controlled by a learned policy trained after supervised pretraining, with iterative multi-pass processing, is novel relative to prior CNNs and RL attention works at the time. Using SNES to train a million-parameter control policy for a large CNN is also a distinctive choice.

Significance
Moderate. The idea is conceptually interesting and plausibly general; showing gains on standard datasets is positive. The practical impact depends on (i) whether similar or larger gains can be achieved with simpler gradient-based recurrent/gating mechanisms, (ii) compute overhead at test time, and (iii) scalability to larger datasets. With a stronger empirical section, this could influence follow-on work in top-down modulation.

Questions for the authors
- What are dim(O) and dim(A)? How many gates per layer and total parameters θ?
- How are validation and early stopping handled for SNES to mitigate overfitting?
- What is the absolute improvement on CIFAR-10/100, with/without data augmentation? Please list competing methods and data regimes clearly.
- How does a single-pass conditional gating model (T=1 with learned π) compare?
- Can the policy be trained end-to-end by backprop through the unrolled T steps? If tried, how does it perform?
- What is the test-time cost multiplier and latency impact for T=5?

Pros
- Elegant, biologically motivated mechanism; simple to integrate.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains and qualitative interpretability.
- Stable iterative dynamics; diagnostic evidence that gates encode class information.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear fairness of SoTA claim.
- Limited methodological details impede reproducibility.
- SNES choice not convincingly justified over differentiable alternatives.
- Additional compute at test time not quantified.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The idea is interesting and timely, and the initial results are promising, but the paper would benefit from stronger comparative experiments and clearer reporting.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
