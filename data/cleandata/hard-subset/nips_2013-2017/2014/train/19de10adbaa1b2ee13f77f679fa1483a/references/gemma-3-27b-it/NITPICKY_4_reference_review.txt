This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES) to modulate filter activations. The core idea is to enable the network to refine its classifications through iterative processing, akin to a human expert’s deliberate consideration of ambiguous cases. The approach builds upon Maxout networks and aims to improve performance on difficult instances without manual filter inspection.

While the motivation is compelling – mimicking human visual processing and addressing limitations of static CNNs – the paper suffers from a concerning number of shortcomings, ranging from minor inconsistencies to issues that undermine confidence in the reported results. 

First, the writing is often imprecise. For instance, the statement that "the network never changes its weights or filters during evaluation" is trivially true of *any* trained network. The introduction repeatedly highlights the analogy to human ornithologists, but this analogy feels strained and doesn’t sufficiently justify the complexity of the proposed method. 

The technical details also raise concerns. Equation (1) is poorly explained; it lacks clarity regarding the meaning of the summation index `i` and its relation to the input maps. While the equation itself is standard convolution, its presentation within the paper is confusing. The explanation of the Maxout pooling layer (Equation 2) is similarly awkward. Referring to ‘reducing every b consecutive maps’ without clarifying the index `i` within the max operation is imprecise.

More critically, the methodology feels under-specified. Algorithm 1 lacks crucial details. What does `NEXTBATCH(n)` actually *do*? Is the batch randomly sampled with replacement? The regularization term in Equation (11) uses `λL2`, yet its value is only mentioned in the experimental section, without justification for its choice. The details of the SNES implementation are relegated to the supplementary material—critical algorithmic details should be in the main text. Furthermore, the authors mention using data augmentation, but do not specify *which* data augmentation techniques were employed. This omission affects reproducibility.

The experimental setup also invites scrutiny. The population size of 50 for SNES seems rather small, potentially leading to premature convergence. The choice of T=5 steps is asserted as a pragmatic compromise, but lacks a more thorough justification. The claim of a “6% relative improvement” (Table 1) is vague; relative to *what* baseline? This needs to be explicitly stated. The discussion of Figure 3 is descriptive but lacks quantitative analysis. Simply stating that “complex suppression and enhancement patterns are found” is insufficient. Where is the statistical significance testing?

The section on related work is decent but contains several minor errors. The citation for Fukushima’s Neocognitron [1] is missing the year. There's also a citation to a thesis [48] that would be better replaced with a peer-reviewed publication covering similar work.

Finally, the formatting is inconsistent. There are spaces missing before punctuation marks in several places.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** The methodology lacks sufficient detail and justification, hindering reproducibility and assessment of validity.
*   **Imprecise Writing:** The paper suffers from ambiguous language and unclear explanations.
*   **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments lack thorough statistical analysis and provide insufficient justification for key choices.
*   **Minor Flaws:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the overall impression of quality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novel Idea:** The concept of learning attentional mechanisms via RL within a CNN is interesting and potentially valuable.
*   **Positive Results:** The reported performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is encouraging, albeit lacking robust statistical support.



**Overall:** While the idea behind dasNet is intriguing, the numerous flaws – both technical and presentational – outweigh its strengths. The lack of rigor, imprecise writing, and weak experimental evaluation raise serious concerns about the validity and reproducibility of the results. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the quality standards expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. A revised version addressing the concerns outlined above, with more rigorous methodology, clearer writing, and a more thorough experimental evaluation, might be reconsidered.
