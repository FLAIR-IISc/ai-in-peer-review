### Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a model that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (a Maxout network in this case) with a recurrent attention mechanism. The core idea is that after an initial, fast feedforward pass, a control policy can modulate the activations of convolutional filters over several subsequent passes on the same image. This allows the network to iteratively refine its prediction, supposedly correcting initial misclassifications on difficult or ambiguous examples. The control policy, which outputs attention weights for the feature maps, is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—after the base CNN has been trained via standard supervised learning. The authors claim state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 for unaugmented data by demonstrating that this iterative "thinking" process can correct the network's initial guesses.

This work relates to a growing body of literature on attention mechanisms and recurrent processing in deep learning, drawing inspiration from the feedback pathways in the human visual cortex. Unlike much prior work which applies attention spatially (e.g., saccades) or focuses on smaller-scale problems, this paper attempts to learn an internal, feature-based attention policy for a state-of-the-art classification model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty:** The core concept of training a separate RL policy to modulate the internal feature maps of a pre-trained, high-performing CNN is novel and compelling. It proposes a clear mechanism for "iterative refinement" or "thinking," moving beyond the single, static feedforward pass common to most vision models.
2.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles an important and challenging problem: how to improve classification on ambiguous instances where a standard CNN might fail. The separation of initial training (supervised) from policy training (RL) is a pragmatic approach.
3.  **Potential Significance:** If the method proves robust and scalable, it could offer a new paradigm for designing more dynamic and accurate deep learning models. The idea of a fast "first guess" followed by slower, deliberate refinement has significant potential.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper, despite its interesting premise, is marred by a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, notational ambiguities, and presentational flaws that undermine its contributions.

1.  **Technical and Notational Imprecision:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of meticulousness that is concerning.
    *   **Equation Errors:** There appear to be several off-by-one errors in the summations and max operations. For example, in Equation (1), the sum `∑_{i=0}^{i=c}` implies `c+1` input maps, which is non-standard. Similarly, in Equations (2) and (3), `max_{i=0}^{b}` implies `b+1` elements for a block size of `b`. These should likely be `i=0` to `c-1` and `b-1`, respectively.
    *   **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation is frequently imprecise. In Equation (6), `vt = Mt(θ, x)` is introduced without clearly defining how `Mt` relates to the base network `M` or how the policy parameters `θ` are incorporated at step `t`. In Algorithm 1, `Li = -λboostd log(vT)` is poorly written; it should specify the log-probability of the correct class `d`, i.e., `log(v_T[d])`. Furthermore, the observation function `h(Mt)` (Algorithm 1, line 9) is confusing, as `Mt` seems to represent the network, not its state.
    *   **Unconventional Methodological Choices:** The policy's activation function in Equation (7), `dim(A)σ(θiot)`, is a highly non-standard scaled softmax. The justification that this makes the action vector "average to 1" is unusual and requires much stronger theoretical or empirical support. It is unclear why this was chosen over more conventional activations (e.g., sigmoid or tanh per gate).

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:**
    *   The paper claims that standard RL methods are "impractical" due to the large parameter space, but this is a strong claim made without sufficient evidence. Modern policy gradient methods are regularly used in domains with millions of parameters. The authors need to provide a more compelling reason for choosing SNES over, for example, PPO or DDPG, beyond simply stating the parameter count.
    *   The composition of the observation vector `o_t` seems ad-hoc. Concatenating average map activations provides a very coarse signal. While the authors acknowledge this is "partial state information," there is no analysis of why this specific construction is sufficient or how sensitive the performance is to its composition.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation and Presentation:**
    *   The most critical flaw is that **Table 1, Figure 2, and Figure 3 are all referenced but are not present in the provided paper.** The central performance claims (including the new state-of-the-art result) are therefore entirely unsubstantiated. The detailed description of Figure 3, which is meant to provide qualitative insight into the model's behavior, is rendered meaningless without the figure itself. This is a disqualifying omission for a rigorous scientific review.
    *   The hardware used for experiments (GTX 560 Ti) is severely outdated. While this does not invalidate the results, it makes the reported training time of "around 4 days" very difficult to interpret or compare against modern benchmarks.
    *   The information content analysis is a good idea but is presented vaguely. Reporting that performance was "similar to the performance of dasNet" is not sufficiently precise for a top-tier publication.

4.  **Formatting and Professionalism:** The reference list is formatted inconsistently, with missing conference/journal names, inconsistent author listings, and other errors. This reflects a general lack of care in the manuscript's preparation. The claim that the method omits "manual inspection [18]" misrepresents the purpose of visualization tools like those in Zeiler & Fergus, which are for understanding, not tuning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and interesting framework for combining deep networks with RL for iterative classification.
*   The core idea of learning a policy to control internal attention is promising and could inspire future work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental results, including the main state-of-the-art claim, are completely unverifiable due to missing tables and figures.
*   The manuscript is rife with technical errors, notational ambiguities, and sloppy presentation, suggesting a lack of rigor.
*   Key methodological choices (e.g., the scaled softmax activation, the use of SNES) are poorly justified.
*   The overall quality of the manuscript does not meet the high standards expected at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the high-level idea presented in this paper is intriguing, the execution is severely lacking. The complete absence of the figures and tables containing the primary results makes a proper evaluation impossible. Beyond this critical flaw, the paper is plagued by a multitude of minor—but collectively significant—errors, from incorrect mathematical formulations to poor notational choices and unprofessional formatting. The work is not yet in a state suitable for publication at a premier venue like NIPS. The authors should be encouraged to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these numerous issues and resubmit their work in the future.
