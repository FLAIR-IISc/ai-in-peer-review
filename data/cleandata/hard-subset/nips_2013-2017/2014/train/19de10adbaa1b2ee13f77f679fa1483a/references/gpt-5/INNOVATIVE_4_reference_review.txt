Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, learned, selective attention via feedback connections. Concretely, each convolutional feature map has a multiplicative gate whose value can be modulated across multiple passes over the same input image. A policy network maps observations (average map activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to the next action (gate vector). The policy is learned post hoc via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing the final-step cross-entropy (with a boosting-like weighting for misclassified samples). On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves over a strong Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art results for the specified setting. Qualitative visualizations suggest the policy amplifies/suppresses specific filters to resolve confusions (e.g., cat vs dog). A small dynamics study shows performance peaks around the trained number of iterations and remains stable for more steps.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to: (1) recurrent/feedback processing and attention in biological vision; (2) selective attention and saccade RL for vision; (3) deep CNNs with Maxout and dropout; and (4) ES/NES for high-dimensional black-box optimization. Unlike spatial “glimpse” models, dasNet modulates internal feature channels globally across iterations, and unlike standard recurrent CNNs trained end-to-end, the controller is learned with SNES after supervised training of the feedforward net.

Strengths
- Novelty: A clear, bold idea—internal, iterative, learned attention over CNN feature maps to correct initial feedforward guesses. The use of an RL/ES controller to drive top-down modulation is conceptually fresh relative to prior spatial attention or plain recurrency.
- Significance: Demonstrates measurable gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with qualitative evidence that the feedback disambiguates hard cases. Advances the conversation on feedback in deep vision systems beyond purely feedforward pipelines.
- Simplicity of mechanism: Feature-wise multiplicative gates with a straightforward observation vector; easy to retrofit onto existing CNNs; deterministic policy at test time.
- Empirical stability: Dynamics remain stable beyond the trained number of steps.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological comparisons missing: Since the gating and observation pipeline are differentiable, a natural baseline is to train the controller end-to-end via backpropagation through time (BPTT) rather than SNES; comparisons to policy gradient or supervised gating (e.g., a small MLP conditioned on logits/activations) are absent.
- Evaluation scope: Results are limited to CIFAR-10/100; no comparisons to concurrent recurrent or attention-based models on the same benchmarks; the improvement magnitude and absolute numbers (Table 1) are not shown in the text, making it hard to assess effect size.
- Clarity/inconsistencies: The claim of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” is confusing given that the Maxout base model is trained with data augmentation. Please clarify whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time augmentation only. References are partly incomplete/duplicated, and some hyperparameters are under-specified (policy network architecture beyond linear θ, SNES update details, validation/early stopping).
- Credit assignment and sample efficiency: Evolving ~1M controller parameters with population size 50 is computationally heavy and likely data-inefficient; no ablations on observation design, action dimensionality, or which layers benefit most from gating.
- Limited analysis: The study uses average map activations as observations, which may be too coarse; more diagnostics (e.g., ablations per layer, T sensitivity on test accuracy, policy generalization to unseen corruptions) would strengthen the case.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the mechanism is well-defined and the training objective is clear. However, the choice of SNES over differentiable training is insufficiently justified empirically; key baselines and ablations are lacking. Experimental reporting should include absolute accuracies, standard deviations over runs, and a validation protocol. Current qualitative analyses are interesting but limited.

Clarity
Readable and mostly well-organized; Algorithm 1 helps. Some notation is terse (Mt(θ, x)) and a few statements are ambiguous (augmentation claim). Several references are incomplete or duplicated. More detail on the policy network, SNES hyperparameters, and train/val/test splits is needed for reproducibility.

Originality
High. The idea of learned internal selective attention via feature-wise gates driven by a policy network is distinct from prior spatial attention and standard recurrency at the time. The use of SNES to train a large controller is unconventional.

Significance
Moderate to high. If gains are robust and the approach generalizes, this is a compelling direction for integrating attention and feedback into CNNs. The potential to retrofit existing models with iterative internal attention is attractive.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new, interesting mechanism for internal attention and feedback in deep CNNs.
- Demonstrates improvements on standard benchmarks with qualitative insights.
- Opens a line of work at the intersection of attention, recurrence, and RL/ES.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing critical baselines (end-to-end BPTT, simpler supervised controllers).
- Limited experimental breadth and incomplete reporting of absolute numbers.
- Computational overhead and practicality not thoroughly analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines training the controller via backpropagation (deterministic MLP with BPTT) and simple linear gating from logits; include RAM/glimpse-style comparisons where applicable.
- Provide absolute accuracies with variance, train/val/test protocol, and clarity on augmentation. Include ablations: which layers are gated, observation components, T, action normalization.
- Report inference-time overhead (factor T) and training cost vs benefit.
- Explore richer observations (e.g., low-rank summaries or attention over spatial statistics) and nonlinear policies; quantify information content more rigorously than KNN/logreg on gates.
- Clean up references and provide enough detail to reproduce.

Score: 7 (accept, clear contribution with notable novelty despite methodological gaps)
