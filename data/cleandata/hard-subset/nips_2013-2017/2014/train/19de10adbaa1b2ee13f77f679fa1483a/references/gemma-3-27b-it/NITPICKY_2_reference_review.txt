## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning to selectively attend to features within the network itself. The core idea is to allow the network to refine its classifications by iteratively adjusting filter activations based on an attentional policy learned through SNES. The motivation stems from observations of human visual processing, where complex classification tasks often require multiple passes and focused attention.

While the core idea of internally-directed attention within a CNN is intriguing, and the experimental results demonstrate a modest improvement over a baseline Maxout network, the paper is riddled with numerous minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigor that collectively weaken its contribution and raise significant concerns. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (Limited):** The concept of learning an attention policy *within* a CNN, rather than as a pre- or post-processing step, is relatively novel.
* **Empirical Results:**  The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100, albeit modest, provides some evidence supporting the efficacy of the proposed approach.
* **Motivation:** The connection to human visual processing is a reasonable motivation for exploring iterative refinement and internal attention mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Inconsistencies and Lack of Detail:**  Equation (1) defines the convolutional layer output but lacks a clear explanation of the activation function 'ϕ'. It's simply stated as "element-wise nonlinear," but specific details (ReLU, sigmoid, etc.) are crucial. Similarly, the discussion of the convolutional operator '*' lacks specifics regarding padding, stride, and bias terms.  Equation (2) introduces 'b' as the block size in the maxout pooling layer but doesn’t explain *how* ‘b’ is chosen or its impact on the network architecture. This lack of precision permeates the paper.
* **RL Implementation Details:** The justification for choosing SNES over conventional RL methods relies heavily on the sheer number of parameters.  However, the authors fail to adequately discuss potential alternative approaches like parameter sharing or hierarchical RL to reduce dimensionality. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is high-level and glosses over crucial implementation details. For example, the observation vector 'o' constructed by function 'h()' is vaguely described – what specific statistics are computed for "average activation”? How are these values concatenated and normalized?  The regularization term in Equation (11) uses λL2, but its value is only provided in the experiments section, making it difficult to assess its impact.
* **Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.  The specific architecture of the Maxout network (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) is not clearly specified beyond "three convolutional maxout layers.” Data augmentation details are mentioned but lack precision.  A 4-day training time on a GTX 560 Ti for the policy learning phase is substantial, yet there is no discussion about the computational cost.
* **Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks crucial ablation studies.  For example, how does the performance of dasNet vary with different numbers of iterative passes (T)? What is the impact of varying the SNES population size?  Are there specific layers where the attention mechanism is more effective?
* **Analysis of Attention:** While the authors provide a visual example of filter activations changing (Figure 3), the analysis is superficial.  The claim that “complex suppression and enhancement patterns are found” is not quantified or rigorously analyzed.  The “information content” of the gates (estimated using 15-NN and logistic regression) is presented as supporting evidence, but the choice of these specific methods is not justified, and their robustness is not assessed.  
* **Related Work:** While the related work section is extensive, it lacks focus. The citations are largely broad surveys rather than specific papers directly relevant to the *internal attention* mechanism being proposed.
* **Formatting and Writing:** Numerous minor formatting issues exist (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, inconsistent equation numbering). The writing style is occasionally verbose and repetitive. For example, the explanation of SNES is needlessly lengthy.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel idea of internal attention.
* Demonstrable improvement in classification accuracy.

**Con:**

* Numerous technical inconsistencies and lack of detail.
* Insufficient experimental detail and lack of ablation studies.
* Superficial analysis of the attention mechanism.
* Poorly formatted and written.
* Lack of a compelling justification for the chosen RL method (SNES).



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the core idea, the numerous flaws outlined above significantly detract from the paper’s quality. The lack of technical rigor, insufficient experimental detail, and superficial analysis raise serious concerns about the reliability and reproducibility of the results. The modest improvement in performance does not outweigh these deficiencies.  While the concept is interesting, the paper feels underdeveloped and premature for NIPS publication. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject.** The authors need to address the numerous technical inconsistencies, provide a more detailed experimental setup, conduct thorough ablation studies, and offer a more rigorous analysis of the learned attention mechanism before resubmitting. A more concise and clearly written manuscript would also be beneficial.
