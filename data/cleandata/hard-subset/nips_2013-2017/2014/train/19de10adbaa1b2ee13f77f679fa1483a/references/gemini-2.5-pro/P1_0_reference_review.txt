### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "thinking" steps on the same input image. In each step, a control policy modulates the activations of the network's feature maps, effectively up-weighting or down-weighting the influence of certain filters to refine the classification.

The method is implemented in two stages. First, a strong baseline model (a Maxout network) is trained via standard supervised learning. After this, its weights are frozen, and a control policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL) to control the modulatory gates (`a` in Equation 5). Due to the high dimensionality of the policy parameters, the authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization. The state for the RL agent is constructed from averaged filter activations and outputs from the higher layers of the network. The reward function is designed to specifically focus on correcting misclassified examples from the initial feedforward pass.

This work is motivated by the observation that human experts often take time to classify difficult visual stimuli, suggesting an iterative refinement process. It relates to a body of work on recurrent processing and attention in both neuroscience and computer vision. However, it distinguishes itself by applying these concepts to modulate the *internal* feature maps of a modern, state-of-the-art deep network, rather than modeling external attention (e.g., saccades) or using older network architectures. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that dasNet can correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 for unaugmented data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-defined, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors don't just present a final performance number; they include insightful analyses to support their claims. The study of the system's dynamics (Figure 2) shows that the learned policy is stable even when run for more steps than it was trained for. The experiment testing the information content of the final gate values is a clever way to verify that the policy is learning a meaningful representation rather than just overfitting. The choice of SNES for the high-dimensional RL problem is well-justified.

A potential weakness is the ad-hoc nature of the state representation for the RL policy. The observation vector is formed by concatenating the *average* activation of every output map, along with other activations. Averaging throws away all spatial information, which seems like a significant loss of information. While the results show this is sufficient, a discussion of why this representation was chosen and whether alternatives were considered (e.g., using spatially-pooled features) would strengthen the paper. Another significant weakness, not adequately discussed, is the immense computational cost at inference time, which requires T+1 forward passes through the network. This makes the approach impractical for many real-world applications.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is compelling, the method is described clearly (with the help of Algorithm 1), and the connection to related work is well-articulated. The figures, particularly the schematic in Figure 1 and the qualitative example in Figure 3, are helpful for understanding the process and results. The writing is clear and easy to follow. A minor point of confusion is in Table 1, where the performance of the "vanilla CNN" baseline is given. It is not explicitly stated whether this is a result from the original Maxout paper or the authors' own re-implementation, which is a crucial detail for assessing the relative improvement.

**Originality:** The core idea of learning feedback connections to modulate internal feature maps of a deep CNN is highly original. While attention and recurrence are not new concepts, their application here is novel. Most attention-based work focuses on spatial attention (i.e., where to look), whereas this paper introduces a form of *feature-based* internal attention (i.e., what features to trust). The combination of a pre-trained deep network with a policy learned via evolutionary RL to fine-tune its internal behavior on a per-instance basis is a novel and powerful paradigm. The work is well-differentiated from prior art, which either used RL for saccade-like mechanisms or applied recurrence in older, simpler models.

**Significance:** This paper has high potential for impact. It proposes a principled way to move beyond static, feedforward classifiers towards more dynamic models that can "re-evaluate" their own internal representations. This is a fundamentally important direction for machine learning. The results, showing a state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark, demonstrate that this is not just a conceptual contribution but a practical one. The framework could inspire a wealth of follow-up research exploring different policy architectures, state representations, and applications of internal attention. While the absolute performance gain is modest, the novelty of the approach and the new capabilities it demonstrates are highly significant.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core concept of learning an RL policy to control an internal, feature-based attention mechanism in a modern CNN is novel and opens up a promising new research direction.
2.  **Strong Experimental Support:** The claims are backed by solid experiments on CIFAR-10/100, including a new state-of-the-art result (albeit qualified) and thoughtful ablation/analysis studies that go beyond simple accuracy metrics.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex idea easy to understand.
4.  **Inspiring Idea:** The work successfully bridges concepts from neuroscience (recurrent processing) with modern deep learning and reinforcement learning in a practical and effective way, which is likely to inspire future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires multiple forward passes at inference time, making it 5-6x slower than the baseline CNN. This is a major practical limitation that is not sufficiently addressed or discussed in the paper.
2.  **Modest Absolute Improvement:** The absolute error reduction on CIFAR-10 is less than 1% compared to the baseline. One could argue that this gain does not justify the significant increase in computational complexity.
3.  **Ad-hoc State Representation:** The choice to average feature maps to form the state for the policy is not well-justified and may be suboptimal.

### Final Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The originality of the idea and its potential significance for the field are very high. The authors have proposed a novel and exciting way to make deep networks more dynamic and have provided a strong proof-of-concept with compelling experimental results. While the practical issue of computational cost is a valid concern, it does not diminish the scientific contribution. This work is an excellent fit for NIPS and is likely to be influential. I recommend **acceptance**.
