Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, learned feedback that modulates feature-map activations over multiple passes on the same image. Starting from a trained Maxout CNN, the authors introduce per-feature-map multiplicative gates aℓ that scale activations before maxout pooling. A policy network maps observations (per-map average activations, penultimate activations, and class probabilities) to these gates at each pass. Because the observation/action spaces are high dimensional (hundreds to thousands), the policy parameters (~1M) are learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), rather than standard RL. The loss boosts misclassified samples to focus learning on hard cases. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over the base Maxout network and claim state-of-the-art for unaugmented data, with qualitative analyses of gating dynamics and a small study indicating that learned gates carry class-discriminative information.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The gating mechanism and recurrent inference are conceptually simple and reasonable, and the use of SNES is a pragmatic choice for large parameter vectors. The observation design (layerwise mean activations, logits/probabilities) is plausible for guiding control. The training loop is specified at a useful level.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10/100 suggest improvements over the baseline Maxout net and possibly SOTA for unaugmented data; however, critical quantitative details are missing from the excerpt (exact error rates, confidence intervals, comparisons to strong single-model baselines and test-time augmentation/ensembles). Ablations are limited: there is some analysis of varying T and stability, and a proxy analysis showing gates carry information, but there is no comparison to end-to-end backprop through the unrolled recurrent model, no study of policy architecture size, no ablation of observation features, no comparison to simpler feedback (e.g., per-layer scalars), or to gradient-based policy search. Computational overhead and sample complexity of SNES are not analyzed.
- Methodological clarity: The loss definition (Eq. 8–10) is ambiguous: d is called the “correct classification,” but then compared to ∥vT∥∞. It seems λboost switches based on whether the final prediction is correct; please clarify the indicator and ensure the loss is well-defined. The policy network architecture (depth/width, nonlinearities), action dimensionality per layer, and full Maxout architecture/hyperparameters are underspecified for reproducibility. Regularization and stability of the gates are discussed (softmax scaled by dim(A)), but additional constraints (e.g., clipping ranges) are not discussed.
- Scope: The approach is evaluated on 32×32 CIFAR images; claims about “selective attention” are supported by one qualitative figure and dynamics analysis, but a stronger case would include more systematic error analyses and per-class confusion improvements.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and motivates feedback and attention with solid neuroscience citations. The high-level algorithm is clear and the intuition is easy to follow. However, several notational issues and missing details reduce reproducibility: the exact policy network, layer sizes, action dimensions per layer, and the precise boosting loss need clarification. A concrete table with baselines, error rates, augmentation settings, and standard deviations is needed. The qualitative figure is helpful; more examples and per-layer gating statistics would improve understanding.

Originality
The contribution is a novel combination of: (i) per-feature-map multiplicative feedback gates across layers; (ii) sequential re-evaluation of an image with policy-driven gate updates; and (iii) large-scale black-box policy optimization (SNES) for controlling a modern CNN. While recurrent processing, attention, and RL for vision have precedent (and are well-cited here), applying a learned, global, internal gating policy on top of a state-of-the-art Maxout CNN and demonstrating gains on CIFAR is a meaningful and timely extension for the era. Using SNES instead of gradient-based RL/backprop through time is an interesting pragmatic choice, though it raises questions about efficiency vs. alternatives.

Significance
If the reported CIFAR-10/100 gains over a strong Maxout baseline are accurate and fairly compared, this is a useful step showing that learned feedback at inference can correct hard cases in standard image classification. The idea is likely to inspire follow-ups on spatially selective gating, differentiable policy learning, and broader recurrent control within CNNs. That said, the current empirical evidence is not fully convincing due to incomplete quantitative reporting and lack of key ablations. The approach increases inference cost by a factor of T and introduces a large policy; practical impact would benefit from analysis of accuracy–compute trade-offs.

Pros
- Clear, biologically motivated idea of internal selective attention via feedback gates.
- Simple mechanism (multiplicative per-map scaling) that is easy to integrate into existing CNNs.
- Demonstrates improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong baseline; potentially SOTA for unaugmented data.
- Sensible observation design and stability analysis across T.
- Qualitative visualizations and a proxy showing gates encode class-relevant information.

Cons
- Missing exact quantitative results, comparisons, and variance; unclear strength of SOTA claim.
- No comparison to end-to-end differentiable training of the recurrent/gated model or to simpler controllers.
- Ambiguity in the loss/boosting definition; several architectural details omitted, hindering reproducibility.
- Computational cost and scalability (training via SNES, test-time multiple passes) not analyzed.
- Gating is global per feature map (no spatial selectivity); the paper acknowledges this as future work.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a complete results table with exact error rates, augmentation settings, and standard deviations; include strong single-model baselines (e.g., Maxout with/without dropout, stochastic pooling, DropConnect).
- Compare SNES to backprop through unrolled recurrent gates (policy network trained with gradient descent) and to simpler feedback (per-layer scalars, linear policies).
- Clarify Eq. 8–10 and fully specify the policy network architecture and action dimensionalities. Report compute overhead (train/test) vs. gains.
- Ablate observation components; assess sensitivity to T at train/test and to λ parameters; provide confusion matrices and analysis of which classes benefit.
- Discuss robustness: does dasNet ever degrade easy examples? Report accuracy stratified by baseline confidence.
- Release code or detailed specs to enable reproduction.

Overall assessment
Promising and relevant idea with initial positive results, but the current presentation lacks crucial quantitative details and ablations to firmly establish significance and isolate what drives the gains. With stronger empirical evidence and clarified methodology, this would be a solid contribution.

Preliminary rating: Weak accept if the missing results table and key comparisons demonstrate clear gains; otherwise borderline. Confidence: medium.
