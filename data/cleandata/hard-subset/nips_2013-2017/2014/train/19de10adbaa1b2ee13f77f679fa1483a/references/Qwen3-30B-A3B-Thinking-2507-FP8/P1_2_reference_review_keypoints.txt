- Summary: Proposes dasNet—adding feedback-controlled internal selective attention to CNNs via RL (SNES) to iteratively modulate filters across multiple passes, improving classification on hard cases.

Strengths:
- Novel Concept: Introduces a fresh, human-vision-inspired internal selective attention mechanism using feedback connections.
- Strong Experimental Results: Shows ~6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 with clear presentation and ablations.
- Technical Soundness: Uses SNES to feasibly apply RL to large networks and provides thorough implementation details.
- Clear Motivation: Well-motivated link to human visual processing and the idea of "thinking" through multiple passes.
- Comprehensive Related Work: Thorough coverage of neuroscience and machine learning literature relevant to attention and feedback.

Weaknesses:
- Limited Scope: Experiments confined to CIFAR-10/100, unclear scalability to ImageNet or detection tasks.
- Computational Cost: Multiple passes (T=5) significantly increase inference time without a detailed trade-off analysis.
- Lack of Theoretical Justification: No deep theoretical explanation for why feedback helps more than making networks larger/deeper.
- Limited Analysis of Attention Mechanism: Insufficient analysis of why specific filters are up- or down-weighted beyond qualitative examples.
- Missing Comparisons: No comparison to other attention modules (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM) to establish relative benefits.

Quality Assessment:
- Technically sound with well-designed experiments supporting claims, but limited theoretical and dataset scope.

Clarity Assessment:
- Well-written and organized with replicable method descriptions; some RL observation-space details could be clearer.

Originality Assessment:
- Novel combination of feedback-based internal attention and RL control; distinguishes itself from spatial attention work.

Significance Assessment:
- Promising proof-of-concept with notable CIFAR gains, but practical impact limited by compute cost and unclear scalability.

Arguments For Acceptance:
- Innovative internal attention approach inspired by vision science.
- Meaningful empirical gains on standard benchmarks.
- Solid engineering and handling of RL challenges.
- Strong motivation and related work coverage.
- Clear writing and organization.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- Narrow experimental evaluation limited to small datasets.
- No head-to-head with established attention mechanisms.
- Increased inference cost (~5x) undermines practicality.
- Lack of theoretical insight into mechanism's efficacy.
- Insufficient analysis of attention behavior.

Overall Recommendation:
- Weak accept: interesting and well-executed but limited scope, comparisons, and practical applicability.

Suggestions for Improvement:
- Evaluate scalability on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
- Compare performance to common attention modules (SE, CBAM).
- Analyze why specific filters are modulated and their effect on decisions.
- Provide detailed computational trade-off (inference time, memory) analysis.
- Offer theoretical insights or hypotheses explaining the advantage over larger/deeper nets.

Final Comments:
- Well-written and compelling proof-of-concept that needs broader experiments and deeper analysis to be a stronger NeurIPS contribution.
