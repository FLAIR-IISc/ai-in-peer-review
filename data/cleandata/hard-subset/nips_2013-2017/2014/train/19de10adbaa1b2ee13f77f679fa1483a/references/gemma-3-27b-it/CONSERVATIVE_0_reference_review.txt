This paper proposes dasNet, a deep convolutional network augmented with feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) to selectively attend to features and improve classification accuracy, particularly on difficult examples. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its predictions by modulating filter activations. While the motivation – mimicking human visual processing involving multiple passes and attentional mechanisms – is reasonable, and the experimental results show a modest improvement on CIFAR-10/100, the paper falls short of convincing me of its significance as a contribution to NIPS.

The approach relies heavily on established building blocks – Maxout networks, backpropagation, and convolutional layers – and the novelty resides in the RL-driven feedback mechanism. However, the RL component feels somewhat bolted on rather than deeply integrated into the network's architecture. The choice of SNES, while justified by the high dimensionality of the action space, feels like a pragmatic workaround rather than a principled design choice. More importantly, the paper lacks a convincing comparison to simpler, well-established techniques for improving robustness or handling difficult examples. Have the authors explored techniques like adversarial training, data augmentation with more challenging examples, or ensemble methods? A clear demonstration that dasNet outperforms these more standard approaches is crucial, and currently missing.

The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While these are standard benchmarks, the gains shown (6% relative improvement) are relatively modest and could be attributed to various factors beyond the proposed attention mechanism. The analysis of the filter activations (Figure 3) is suggestive but lacks quantitative rigor. What specific filters are being emphasized/de-emphasized, and how does this correlate with the image content and the initial misclassification?  The claim that the gate values learned contain significant information for classification (based on nearest neighbor/logistic regression) feels somewhat disconnected from the primary contribution, and the specific results aren’t particularly compelling. 

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive but lacks critical engagement with the literature on attention mechanisms in CNNs. Several recent works explore attention in a more integrated fashion, often utilizing spatial or channel-wise attention mechanisms directly within the convolutional layers. The authors should clearly position their work within this broader context and highlight the unique advantages of their feedback-based approach.

**Strengths:**

*   The motivation of incorporating iterative refinement and attention into CNNs is intuitive and aligns with observations of human visual processing.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a small but measurable improvement in classification accuracy on CIFAR-10/100.
*   The paper is generally well-written and the approach is described clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The RL component feels like an add-on rather than an integral part of the architecture.
*   The choice of SNES feels like a pragmatic compromise, lacking theoretical justification.
*   Lack of comparison to simpler, well-established techniques for improving robustness.
*   Limited experimental evaluation on only two datasets.
*   Insufficient quantitative analysis of the learned attention mechanism.
*   Weak positioning within the broader literature on attention mechanisms in CNNs.
*   Modest performance gains compared to state-of-the-art methods.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel approach to incorporating attention into CNNs.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a positive, albeit small, improvement in classification accuracy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical contribution is incremental and does not represent a significant advance in the field.
*   The lack of comparison to established baselines weakens the claims of superiority.
*   The limited scope of the experiments and analysis limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   The reliance on SNES feels like a workaround rather than a principled design choice.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and presents a reasonable idea, it lacks the originality, significance, and rigorous evaluation necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The gains are modest, the comparison to baselines is lacking, and the contribution feels incremental. More extensive experimentation, a more compelling comparison to existing techniques, and a deeper analysis of the learned attention mechanism are needed to justify acceptance. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a significant contribution to the field.
