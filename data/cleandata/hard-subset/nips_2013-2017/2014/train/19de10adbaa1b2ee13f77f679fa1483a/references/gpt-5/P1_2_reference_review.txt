Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method to add internal, learned, selective attention to a trained convolutional network. Concretely, a standard Maxout CNN is augmented with multiplicative, per-feature-map gates a^l_j applied before maxout/pooling. Given a single image, the network is evaluated over T passes. After each pass t, an observation vector is constructed by concatenating per-map average activations from each Maxout layer, intermediate activations of the classification layer, and the class probability vector. A deterministic policy πθ maps this observation to the next set of gates a_{t+1}, thereby modulating feature-map gains on the next pass. The policy parameters θ (on the order of 10^6) are optimized with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), using a boosted negative log-likelihood loss that upweights misclassified examples. The idea is to improve difficult, ambiguous classifications by iteratively emphasizing/suppressing internal features via feedback.

Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, including qualitative demonstrations that the model can flip initial wrong predictions (e.g., dog→cat) and an analysis suggesting stability beyond the training horizon T and that the final gates contain class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to a long literature on recurrent and top-down processing in vision neuroscience and to machine learning work on attention and reinforcement learning for visual tasks (e.g., saccades, feedback-based recognition, iterative inference). Unlike “glimpse”-based attention methods, dasNet applies global, per-channel modulation akin to feature-wise top-down gain control. It also differs from prior recurrent CNNs that perform iterative reconstruction or deblurring by focusing on modulating a powerful discriminative CNN post hoc. The use of black-box evolutionary strategies to train a large continuous policy is a practical choice given the dimensionality, although it sidesteps differentiable alternatives.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mechanism is simple and well-specified (equations, Algorithm 1). The gating is constrained to be positive and average to 1 to stabilize activations. The training objective and SNES setup are described, including a boosting scheme to focus on hard cases.
- Empirical support: The paper claims a relative 6% improvement over the vanilla Maxout CNN and “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data,” but the exact numbers in Table 1 are not provided in the text. The absence of detailed quantitative results, error bars, and comparisons to strong baselines makes it hard to assess the strength of the claims. The dynamics study (performance peaking at the training T and stable beyond) and the gate-information analysis are interesting but limited.
- Missing baselines/ablations: There is no comparison to (i) simple test-time ensembling/augmentation, (ii) increasing base CNN capacity, (iii) end-to-end differentiable recurrent training (BPTT) of the same gating mechanism, (iv) policies trained with standard policy gradients/actor-critic, or (v) image-independent per-map scalars (to control for the mere addition of parameters). Ablations of the observation vector components and action space (which layers are gated) are missing. The computational overhead (T× inference cost) and training cost vs. accuracy tradeoffs are not quantified.
- Overfitting/validation: The boosting of misclassified training samples could overfit; the paper does not describe validation procedures, early stopping, or generalization diagnostics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The mechanism is easy to follow, with clear notation and an explicit training loop. However:
- Please include the full Table 1 with exact test accuracies/errors, standard deviations, and settings (augmentation, cropping, committee averaging).
- Clarify the apparent inconsistency: the method section states data augmentation is used to train the base CNN, yet the result is described as “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.” Specify precisely what is (not) augmented at train/test time and what SOTA claim refers to.
- Specify the dimensionalities: number of gated maps per layer, whether fully connected maxout units are gated, observation vector dimension, and policy architecture (currently a linear map with softmax).
- Provide training details for SNES (batch size n per generation, learning rates, stopping criterion) and validation protocol.

Originality
The combination of (i) per-feature-map gain modulation over multiple passes, (ii) learning a global feedback policy conditioned on internal activations, and (iii) training that policy via SNES on top of a strong CNN baseline is, to my knowledge, novel at the time. Prior works on attention and feedback have focused on saccades, spatial attention, or iterative reconstruction; here the contribution is a simple, scalable mechanism to endow a feedforward CNN with internal, class-conditional recurrence. The evolutionary optimization choice is pragmatic but not conceptually novel.

Significance
If the quantitative gains over strong baselines hold, the approach is impactful: it is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and offers a general way to exploit test-time recurrence to correct initial mistakes. The analysis of dynamics and gates is suggestive of meaningful feedback. That said, the missing details and limited comparisons make it difficult to judge how much better this is than more conventional alternatives (e.g., deeper/wider CNNs, test-time augmentation, or differentiable recurrent gating). The computational cost and sample efficiency of SNES with ~10^6 parameters also raise questions about practicality.

Strengths
- Clear, simple mechanism for internal attention; integrates with existing CNNs.
- Demonstrates improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline.
- Interesting qualitative analysis and study of stability across time steps.
- Motivated by and connected to neuroscience literature on feedback.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient quantitative details; key table not shown in the text and no error bars.
- Limited baselines and ablations; no comparison to differentiable recurrent training.
- Compute and sample efficiency not analyzed; training is expensive and inference is T× cost.
- Potential overfitting due to boosted loss; validation protocol unclear.
- Policy is a large linear map; parameter efficiency and generalization are questionable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report full results with exact numbers, confidence intervals, and per-class confusion matrices.
- Add baselines: (a) deeper/wider Maxout CNN with matched parameter count; (b) test-time augmentation/ensembles; (c) end-to-end BPTT of the same recurrent gating; (d) image-independent per-map scalars; (e) RL with policy gradients.
- Ablate observation components and action scope (which layers are gated) and analyze sensitivity to T.
- Report compute time and throughput at train/test; discuss practical tradeoffs.
- Clarify augmentation claims and provide a validation protocol to mitigate overfitting.
- Consider reducing policy dimensionality (e.g., low-rank/shared gates, grouped channels) or adding nonlinearity for better parameter efficiency.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel way to add top-down, iterative inference to CNNs.
- Simple, general mechanism with promising empirical gains.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired feedback with modern deep nets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is incomplete relative to NeurIPS standards; missing key baselines and details.
- Practicality concerns (compute, sample efficiency) not addressed.
- Unclear how much of the gain is attributable to recurrence vs. added parameters/training tricks.

Recommendation: Weak accept if the authors can provide complete quantitative results and stronger baselines/ablations; otherwise borderline. Confidence: medium.
