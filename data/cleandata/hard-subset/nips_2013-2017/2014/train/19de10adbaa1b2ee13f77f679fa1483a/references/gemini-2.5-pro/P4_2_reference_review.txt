### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple, iterative passes over a single input image, refining its classification over time. This is achieved by introducing modulatory "gates" that can weight the output feature maps of each convolutional layer.

The central contribution is the method for learning to control these gates. Instead of being fixed, the gate values are determined at each pass by a policy network. This policy takes the internal state of the CNN (including average map activations and class probabilities) as input and outputs a set of weights for the gates in the next pass. This sequential decision-making process is framed as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem, where the policy is trained to maximize a reward signal based on the final classification accuracy. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space, the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the policy weights after the underlying CNN has been pre-trained via standard supervised learning.

This work draws inspiration from the human visual system, which is understood to involve not just a fast feedforward sweep but also slower, recurrent, and top-down attentional processes for analyzing complex scenes. While previous work has explored recurrent connections or RL for attention in simpler settings, this paper applies the concept directly to a state-of-the-art CNN architecture (Maxout networks) on challenging benchmark datasets (CIFAR-10/100), aiming to improve performance on difficult or ambiguous classification instances.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed mechanism for modulating filter activations is straightforward and well-integrated into a standard CNN framework. The formulation of the problem in an RL context is appropriate for the sequential nature of the task. The experiments are conducted on established benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100) using a strong baseline model (Maxout network), which allows for a fair evaluation. The authors provide supporting analyses beyond final accuracy scores, including a qualitative visualization of an example where dasNet corrects an initial misclassification, an investigation of the system's dynamics beyond its training horizon, and a test confirming that the learned gate values contain significant class-relevant information. Claims are generally well-supported by the presented results.

However, there are some areas where the technical evaluation could be strengthened. The choice of SNES as the RL algorithm is justified by the large parameter space, but it is a black-box optimization method. A discussion or comparison with gradient-based policy optimization methods (e.g., policy gradients), perhaps on a reduced-scale problem, would provide valuable context on whether SNES is a uniquely suitable choice or simply a practical one. Furthermore, the observation vector for the policy is a highly compressed representation of the network's state, based on averaging activations. The impact of this information bottleneck on the policy's performance is not deeply explored.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for the work is well-articulated, and the proposed dasNet architecture is described effectively through text, equations (e.g., Eq. 5), and a formal algorithm (Algorithm 1). The distinction between the initial supervised training of the base CNN and the subsequent RL training of the attention policy is made clear. The figures, particularly the schematic in Figure 1 and the dynamic analysis in Figure 2, are helpful in understanding the model's operation and behavior. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3, while acknowledged by the authors as difficult to interpret fully, provides a valuable glimpse into what the model is doing internally.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is highly original. While the constituent components (CNNs, feedback connections, RL) are not new in isolation, their synthesis into a system where an RL agent learns to modulate the internal workings of a deep CNN for classification is novel. It moves beyond external attention mechanisms (e.g., learning saccades) to a form of *internal* attention, where the network learns to re-weight its own learned features on the fly. This differs significantly from standard feedforward architectures and represents a new conceptual approach to applying iterative processing in deep learning for vision. The work is adequately situated within the context of related research in both computational neuroscience and machine learning.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of static, feedforward networks and proposes a plausible, biologically-inspired mechanism for improvement. The results are significant, demonstrating a tangible performance increase over a strong baseline and achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (for unaugmented data at the time of publication), as shown in Table 1. This suggests that iterative refinement can indeed resolve ambiguities that a single feedforward pass cannot. The ideas presented could inspire further research into dynamic, adaptive neural network architectures that can allocate computational resources more effectively.

A potential weakness regarding significance lies in the practical aspects of the method. The RL training phase adds considerable computational overhead (4 days on a GPU for CIFAR-10). Furthermore, inference time is multiplied by the number of passes (`T`), making the model significantly slower than the baseline CNN. While the performance gain is measurable (a relative 6% error reduction on CIFAR-10), the trade-off between this improvement and the substantial increase in computational cost might limit its immediate practical adoption. The scalability of the SNES training approach to much larger models and datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is also an open question.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **High Novelty:** The core idea of using an RL-trained policy to control internal, multiplicative gates in a deep CNN for iterative classification is a novel and compelling research direction.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a clear performance improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline on competitive benchmark datasets.
3.  **Conceptual Importance:** The paper provides a concrete implementation of "thinking" or iterative refinement in deep networks, addressing a key difference between current models and human cognition.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written, the method is clearly described, and the experiments are methodologically sound.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  **Computational Cost:** The approach introduces significant computational overhead in both training and inference, which may limit its practical utility and scalability.
2.  **Limited Ablation of RL Method:** The paper uses SNES without comparison to alternative policy learning algorithms, leaving its necessity and relative performance unevaluated.
3.  **Modest Absolute Gains:** The absolute error reduction, while establishing a new state-of-the-art, is relatively small compared to the added model complexity and computational cost.

Overall, the paper's novel concept and strong empirical validation outweigh its potential limitations regarding computational efficiency. It presents a significant and thought-provoking contribution to the field.
