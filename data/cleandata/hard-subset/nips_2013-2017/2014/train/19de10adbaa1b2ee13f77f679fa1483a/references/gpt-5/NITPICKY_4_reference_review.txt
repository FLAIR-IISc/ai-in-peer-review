Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with multiplicative, per-feature-map gates that are updated over multiple passes on the same input. A lightweight “policy network” takes as observation summary features from the CNN at each pass (per-map average activations, classification-layer activations, and class probabilities) and outputs the next set of gates via a softmax scaled to keep mean activation near 1. The policy is trained after the base CNN is supervised-trained, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize a terminal loss that upweights misclassified examples. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over a vanilla Maxout CNN and claim state-of-the-art for “unaugmented” data. Qualitative visualizations show the gates modulating filters to correct ambiguous cases.

Strengths
- Clear, biologically inspired motivation for recurrent, top-down modulation within deep nets; nice connection to neuroscience literature on feedback and attention.
- Simple, implementation-friendly mechanism (per-channel gates) that is compatible with standard CNNs.
- Deterministic, multi-pass inference with positive gates and mean-preserving normalization is a reasonable stability heuristic.
- Some empirical improvement on CIFAR-10/100; qualitative example illustrates the intended effect.
- Analysis of stability over varying numbers of passes suggests dynamics do not diverge.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity: the approach is framed as RL, but the learning is a black-box evolutionary optimizer with a terminal loss; no temporal credit assignment or per-step rewards. This is better described as black-box policy search than RL. No comparison to gradient-based training of the same policy.
- Baselines: missing comparisons to simpler and fair alternatives (e.g., single-pass learned gates trained end-to-end by backprop, standard fine-tuning of the base network, deeper/wider CNNs, test-time augmentation/ensembling, or simple per-layer reweighting learned supervised). Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains to multi-pass attention vs added parameters/compute.
- Reproducibility details are insufficient: the base architecture (filters per layer, kernel sizes/strides, Maxout block sizes), the exact observation/action dimensions, policy network details (is it purely linear?), SNES hyperparameters (step size, σ initialization, population adaptation), learning-rate schedules, and data augmentation specifics are missing.
- Claims vs setup: the text first states training with data augmentation, yet later claims SOTA for “unaugmented data.” This is inconsistent and needs clarification. Table 1 is referenced but not provided; no numeric results, error bars, or multiple seeds.
- Objective and notation issues: loss (8–10) is confusing. λboost definition compares d to ||vT||∞; the notation mixes the correct label d with a max operation on vT. Equation formatting/semantics need tightening. Algorithm 1 indexes/images are inconsistent (xi vs xj; ai vs a).
- Overfitting/selection: the SNES stopping criterion seems based on training fitness; no validation protocol is described. Statistical significance and variability across runs are not reported.
- Efficiency: multi-pass inference multiplies test-time cost by T (here 5×). No wall-clock or FLOPs comparisons, nor accuracy-vs-cost analysis.
- Ablations: limited. T is “experimentally determined and fixed.” The “Dynamics” experiment uses a 2-layer net and lacks numbers. No ablations on observation components, gate placement (which layers), or action constraints.
- Analysis: only a single qualitative example; no systematic per-class confusion improvements, or visualization of how gates evolve across steps on average.
- References and formatting are sloppy (many missing years/venues; duplicates; inconsistent styles). Several equation typos and indexing issues.

Quality
Technically plausible but under-evaluated. The training/inference pipeline is coherent, but the choice of SNES over backpropagation is not justified experimentally. Many crucial engineering details are omitted; several equations/algorithm lines are ambiguous or inconsistent. The empirical section lacks rigorous baselines, clear numeric reporting, and statistical analysis.

Clarity
The high-level idea is conveyed, but clarity suffers from notation mistakes, missing tables/figures, incomplete algorithmic details pushed to “supplementary,” and inconsistent statements (augmentation vs “unaugmented”). The policy architecture and dimensionalities should be explicitly specified. Proofreading is needed, and references must be completed.

Originality
The combination of per-channel gating applied recurrently with top-down control is interesting and reasonably novel relative to cited work. However, multiplicative gating and feedback/recurrent modulation are not entirely new, and the contribution is primarily an instantiation on top of Maxout with a black-box optimizer. Without stronger baselines (e.g., supervised differentiable gating or simple recurrent fine-tuning), the incremental novelty is hard to assess.

Significance
Potentially useful idea, but the current empirical evidence is too limited. CIFAR improvements are claimed but not substantiated numerically in the text; compute cost is higher; generalization to larger datasets or architectures is not demonstrated. As written, impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complete, numeric results (tables) with means/std over multiple seeds; include baseline Maxout, stronger CNN baselines, test-time augmentation, and end-to-end differentiable gating (single- and multi-pass).
- Justify SNES: compare against backprop-through-time training of the policy and gates; report sample efficiency and convergence.
- Detail all architectural and training hyperparameters; define observation/action sizes; describe policy network exactly.
- Fix notation and algorithm inconsistencies (eqs. 5–11; Algorithm 1 indexing). Clarify λboost and the role of d; ensure figures/tables are present.
- Use a validation set for model selection; report test results only once.
- Quantify compute overhead and provide accuracy-vs-cost curves as T varies; include ablations on which layers are gated and which observation components matter.
- Provide systematic analyses: per-class confusion changes, average gate trajectories, and robustness tests.
- Clean up and complete references.

Minor issues
- Numerous incomplete references (missing years/venues), duplicates (Zeiler & Fergus listed twice), inconsistent citation styles.
- Typos and formatting: mismatched indices; use of 0-based sums; inconsistent notation for vectors/scalars; missing figure/table captions.

Recommendation
Reject (current form). Interesting direction, but the paper has too many methodological and presentation shortcomings, missing baselines, and inconsistent claims to warrant acceptance at this time.

Confidence: High.
