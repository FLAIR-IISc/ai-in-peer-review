## Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances standard CNNs with internal feedback connections. These connections, controlled by a policy learned through reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES), allow the network to selectively emphasize or de-emphasize filters across multiple passes over an image. The core idea is to mimic human visual attention by allowing the CNN to iteratively refine its understanding of an image, correcting initial misclassifications by focusing on different features. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging instances, outperforming a state-of-the-art Maxout CNN.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a rich history of work in CNNs, reinforcement learning, and attention mechanisms.  The use of Maxout networks (Goodfellow, 2016) provides a strong baseline, and the authors correctly position this work within the context of recurrent processing in vision (e.g., Fukushima, 1980; Larochelle & Hinton, 2010; Behnke, 2011) and the influence of top-down processing in the visual cortex (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Gilbert & Sigman, 1999). The application of RL to attention mechanisms is also well-connected to existing work (e.g., Schmidhuber & Huber, 2013; Denil, 2017). However, this paper uniquely combines these ideas by learning a complex, filter-level attention policy *within* a deep CNN architecture using SNES, allowing for fine-grained, iterative refinement of feature representations.  The authors do a good job of situating their work within the broader landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning an internal attention mechanism via RL *within* a CNN to refine predictions across multiple passes is a genuinely novel contribution.  Most attention mechanisms are either pre-defined or learned at a higher, feature-map level, not at the granular level of individual filters.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is significant, especially given the competitive baseline. The demonstration of correcting misclassifications through filter emphasis/de-emphasis provides compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the attention mechanism.
* **Detailed Analysis:**  The analysis of filter activation changes (Figure 3) offers valuable insight into *how* dasNet achieves its improvements. This level of interpretability is crucial for understanding the behavior of deep networks.
* **Scalability Consideration:** The authors address the challenge of learning a policy with a large parameter space by employing SNES, a practical choice for this application.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet takes a significant amount of time (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), which is a substantial barrier to entry for researchers without access to similar resources.  A discussion of computational efficiency and potential strategies for reducing training time would be beneficial.
* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** While performance is shown on CIFAR-10/100, the authors do not explore generalization to other datasets or network architectures. It's unclear how well this approach would transfer to more complex tasks or different CNN backbones.
* **Policy Interpretability:** While the filter activation analysis provides some insight, the learned attention policy itself remains a black box.  Further investigation into the policy's learned representations and its relationship to visual features would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the SNES parameters (population size, learning rate, regularization strength) or the number of iterations (T).  A more robust hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the results.
* **SNES Justification:** The paper mentions SNES as a solution to the large parameter space but doesn't provide a comparative analysis against other RL algorithms adapted for such scale, or why SNES was preferred beyond efficiency.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to incorporating attention into deep CNNs.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear performance improvement.
* The detailed analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the network.
* The work addresses a challenging problem (improving robustness in image classification) in a creative and effective manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of training is high.
* The generalization ability of the approach is not thoroughly evaluated.
* The lack of interpretability of the learned policy limits our understanding of its workings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a promising new approach to internal attention mechanisms in deep learning. The novelty of the method, coupled with the strong empirical results and insightful analysis, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. While the computational cost and limited generalization analysis are concerns, they do not overshadow the overall quality of the work.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with the suggestion that the authors:

1.  Discuss the computational cost in more detail and explore potential strategies for reducing it.
2.  Provide some preliminary results on a different dataset or network architecture to demonstrate generalization potential.
3.  Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter analysis, particularly for the SNES algorithm.
4.  Provide a brief justification for choosing SNES over alternative RL algorithms given the large parameter space.
