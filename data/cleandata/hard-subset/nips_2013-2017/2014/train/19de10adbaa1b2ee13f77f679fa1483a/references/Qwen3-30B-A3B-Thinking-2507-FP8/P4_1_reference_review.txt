

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a framework that enhances deep CNNs by incorporating internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors propose allowing each layer to influence all other layers on successive passes over an image, with the attention policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) after standard supervised training. dasNet modulates filter activity through weights (a^l_j) applied to output maps in each layer, enabling the network to iteratively focus on different features to correct misclassifications. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, demonstrating a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for un-augmented data.

The paper builds upon Maxout networks [7] and connects to the neuroscience literature on top-down feedback connections in visual cortex [28-30]. Unlike previous approaches that use feedback for tasks like image reconstruction [48] or face localization [49], dasNet applies this concept directly to improve classification performance on standard benchmarks.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-motivated and technically sound. The authors provide clear mathematical formulations of the dasNet architecture (Equations 1-5), the reinforcement learning setup (Equations 7-11), and the training algorithm (Algorithm 1). The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is well-justified given the large number of parameters (over 1 million) required to control the network. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful attention to hyperparameters (T=5, λcorrect=0.005, λmisclassified=1) and thorough ablation studies on the number of passes (T) and information content of gate values.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical content is presented logically, with clear explanations of the key innovations. The figures (particularly Figure 3) effectively illustrate how dasNet modifies filter activations across passes to correct misclassifications. The algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Novelty:** The paper makes a clear contribution by introducing a novel mechanism for internal selective attention in deep CNNs through feedback connections. While attention mechanisms have been explored in other contexts [40-46], dasNet's focus on internal, recurrent processing within a pre-trained network for the specific purpose of correcting classification errors is distinctive. The related work section thoroughly contextualizes this contribution against previous approaches, highlighting the key differences.

**Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating a 6% relative improvement over state-of-the-art CNNs on CIFAR-10 (a challenging dataset where many classes share common features). The authors convincingly show that dasNet can correct misclassifications (e.g., cat vs. dog) through iterative refinement, with the most significant changes occurring in higher layers. The approach is practical, as it builds upon existing CNN architectures without requiring major architectural changes.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Cost:** The training process is computationally intensive, requiring approximately 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU to evolve the attention policy (excluding the time to train the base Maxout network). This high cost may limit practical adoption, particularly for larger networks or datasets.

**Limited Evaluation Scope:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which are relatively small datasets. It's unclear how dasNet would perform on larger, more complex benchmarks like ImageNet. The paper would be strengthened by demonstrating scalability to larger datasets or more challenging vision tasks.

**Lack of Comparison to Modern Attention Mechanisms:** The paper does not compare dasNet to more recent attention mechanisms, such as those in transformer architectures or other attention modules that have become prevalent in vision tasks. This omission makes it difficult to assess how dasNet compares to the current state-of-the-art in attention mechanisms.

**Interpretability:** While the paper shows how filter activations change across passes, it does not provide a detailed analysis of whether these changes correspond to meaningful visual features. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that "visualizing what these high-level actually do is an open problem in deep learning," but a more thorough interpretation of the attention patterns would strengthen the contribution.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to internal selective attention through feedback connections
- Demonstrated state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10
- Well-motivated and technically sound methodology
- Clear, well-organized presentation

**Con Against Acceptance:**
- Limited to small-scale datasets (CIFAR-10/100)
- High computational cost for training
- No comparison to modern attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers)
- Limited analysis of attention patterns' interpretability

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound and well-executed paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of deep learning. The dasNet framework presents a novel and effective approach to incorporating internal selective attention in CNNs, demonstrating significant improvements on standard benchmarks. While the computational cost and limited evaluation scope are notable weaknesses, the paper's strong technical contribution, clear presentation, and meaningful results outweigh these concerns. The approach is sufficiently different from previous work to be considered novel, and the results are significant enough to advance the state of the art in image classification.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written and organized, original in its approach to attention mechanisms, and significant in its demonstrated performance improvements. The authors have carefully addressed the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, providing a complete and honest evaluation.

I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS.
