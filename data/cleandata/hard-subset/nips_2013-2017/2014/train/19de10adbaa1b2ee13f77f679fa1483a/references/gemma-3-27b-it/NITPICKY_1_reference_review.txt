## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a framework for endowing CNNs with selective attention through learned feedback connections. The core idea is to allow each layer to modulate the activity of other layers via a reinforcement learning-trained policy, enabling iterative refinement of feature representations for challenging classification instances. The work builds upon existing Maxout networks and utilizes Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for policy optimization due to the high dimensionality of the action space. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, highlighting instances where the network corrects misclassifications by emphasizing and de-emphasizing filters.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning internal attentional mechanisms *post*-training via RL, directly modulating filter activations, is a novel approach. This differs from attention mechanisms typically seen in CNNs which are often pre-defined or learned during supervised training.
* **Potential for Improvement:** The idea of iteratively refining feature representations aligns with human visual processing and offers a plausible path towards more robust and adaptable CNNs. The demonstrated correction of misclassifications is encouraging.
* **Practicality (SNES):** The authors acknowledge the challenges of applying standard RL methods to this problem and reasonably justify their choice of SNES given the large parameter space.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation – addressing difficult classification cases that challenge standard feedforward CNNs.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Methodological Rigor & Detail:** Numerous minor, yet concerning, methodological details lack sufficient clarity and justification.
    * **Equation 1:** The summation index `i` is used for both the input map index and the summation over maps. This is confusing and potentially incorrect.
    * **Equation 2:** The index `j` is used for both the output map and the pixel position. Again, confusing and should be clarified.
    * **Observation Space (Section 3 & 4):** The construction of the observation vector `o` in Algorithm 1 feels arbitrary. Averaging map activations and taking intermediate activations *seems* reasonable, but lacks clear justification. What information is *specifically* encoded in these values that guides the policy?  Why these particular values and not others (e.g., gradients)?
    * **Action Space & Softmax Scaling:** The scaling of the softmax function in Equation 7 to ensure average activation of 1 is unusual and not adequately explained. This seems like a potentially impactful choice that requires further justification. Why not a standard softmax? What are the implications of this scaling?
    * **SNES Parameters:** The population size for SNES (50) is stated, but there is no discussion of sensitivity to this parameter or any justification for its selection.
    * **Training Duration & GPU:**  The 4-day training time on a GTX 560 Ti is reported without any information on batch size, learning rate for the initial Maxout training, or the specific SNES parameters used (beyond population size). This hinders reproducibility.
* **Experiments & Analysis:**
    * **Limited Ablation Studies:** The experiments lack sufficient ablation studies. For instance, what is the performance gain of using *T=5* steps versus *T=1* or *T=2*? While Figure 2 shows dynamics for different T, the training *process* for varying T is not discussed.
    * **Qualitative Analysis is Shallow:** The visual analysis of filter activations in Figure 3 is descriptive but lacks quantitative rigor. How much does the activation change correlate with the improved classification?
    * **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison is solely to the vanilla CNN. Comparing to other attention mechanisms or recent state-of-the-art methods would provide a more compelling benchmark.
    * **Information Content Analysis:** The "information content" analysis using k-NN and logistic regression feels tacked on and isn’t deeply explored. The claim that gate values contain “significant information” is weak without further analysis (e.g., feature importance scores).
* **Clarity & Writing:**
    * **Repetitive Language:** The phrasing is sometimes repetitive (e.g., the repeated emphasis on "difficult classification instances").
    * **Equation Formatting:** Some equations could benefit from better formatting for readability.
    * **Algorithmic Description:** Algorithm 1 is dense and could be broken down into smaller steps for better clarity.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel approach to internal attention.
* Demonstrated performance improvement on CIFAR datasets.
* Reasonable justification for using SNES.

**Con:**

* Numerous methodological details lacking sufficient clarity and justification.
* Limited experimental analysis and ablation studies.
* Shallow qualitative analysis.
* Writing quality could be improved.
* Comparison to limited baselines.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of dasNet is intriguing and the results are modestly positive, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws and a lack of methodological rigor. The absence of detailed justification for key design choices, limited experimental analysis, and shallow qualitative evaluation raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach.  The numerous issues related to equation formatting, unclear indexing, and presentation of experimental parameters are particularly distracting.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  

The paper demonstrates a potentially interesting direction, but requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses before it could be considered for publication in NIPS. I would encourage the authors to focus on providing more detailed explanations of their methods, conducting more thorough experiments, and strengthening the justification for their design choices.  A clearer and more rigorous presentation of the work is crucial for establishing its scientific validity.
