The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning to selectively attend to different filters across multiple passes over an image. The core idea is to allow the network to refine its classifications by modulating filter activations, mimicking the iterative process humans employ in challenging visual tasks. The attentional policy is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the action space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate performance improvements on difficult instances compared to a baseline Maxout network.

This work builds upon a rich history of research exploring attention mechanisms in neural networks (e.g., [40, 41, 44, 45]) and recurrent processing for improved perception ([48, 49, 50]). It’s also related to efforts to incorporate biologically inspired feedback connections into CNNs, drawing parallels to the hierarchical and recurrent architecture of the visual cortex ([28, 29, 30]). The use of reinforcement learning to learn attention policies is a notable connection to previous work aiming to make networks more adaptable and robust ([23, 24]). The specific approach of using SNES for policy optimization is justified by the large parameter space, differentiating it from standard RL methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections, reinforcement learning for attention control, and the application to deep CNNs constitutes a novel contribution. While attention mechanisms are common, learning to selectively modulate *internal* filters via RL is a less explored avenue.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-defined and appears technically sound. The use of Maxout networks is clearly explained, and the integration of the attentional policy is logically presented. The justification for using SNES is reasonable.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that dasNet can improve classification performance, particularly on challenging instances. The ablation study regarding dynamics (Figure 2) is helpful. The visualization of filter activations (Figure 3) offers some qualitative insight into the learned attention mechanism.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the different components of dasNet are explained in a logical order. Mathematical notation is used appropriately and defined clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Ablation Studies:** While the dynamics study is informative, a more comprehensive ablation analysis would strengthen the paper. For example, analyzing the impact of different network architectures (beyond Maxout) or varying the number of iterations (T) would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Interpretation:**  The visualization of filter activations provides limited insight into *why* dasNet achieves improvements.  A more thorough analysis of the learned attentional policy and its relation to image features would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty in interpreting high-level filters, further investigation is needed.
* **Computational Cost:** The training process takes 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti, which is significant. The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational limitations and potential strategies for reducing the training time.
* **Comparison to existing Attention Mechanisms:** While the paper discusses related work, it could benefit from a more direct comparison to other attention mechanisms, such as those used in transformers or spatial attention modules. How does this internal selective attention differ and what advantages does it offer?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to incorporating attention into deep CNNs.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement on challenging image classification tasks.
* The use of reinforcement learning to learn the attentional policy is a creative and potentially impactful idea.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The ablation studies are limited, and a more thorough analysis of the learned attentional policy is needed.
* The computational cost of training is relatively high.
* The connection to other existing attention mechanisms could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and promising results. While the ablation studies and interpretability could be improved, the current work represents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and warrants acceptance. The authors have successfully demonstrated that learning selective internal attention through feedback connections can enhance the performance of CNNs.
