### Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable a pre-trained network to perform multiple, iterative passes over a single image, dynamically modulating the activations of its internal feature maps at each step. This modulation is controlled by a policy network trained via reinforcement learning (RL)—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to maximize classification accuracy, with a focus on correcting initial misclassifications on difficult examples.

The work is conceptually grounded in the neuroscience of the human visual system, which features extensive recurrent and top-down connections thought to enable an initial, rapid "pre-attentive" pass followed by slower, deliberative "attentional" processing. While prior work has explored recurrence and RL for visual tasks like learning saccades or image reconstruction, this paper is novel in its direct application of an explicit, learned attentional control policy to the *internal mechanisms* of a state-of-the-art classification CNN (a Maxout network) to improve its performance on challenging benchmarks like CIFAR-100. It reframes the inference process from a single feedforward shot to a sequence of internal "thoughts."

**Strengths**
The primary strength of this work is its outstanding novelty and conceptual boldness. In a field often dominated by incremental architectural tweaks, this paper proposes a fundamentally different way to think about inference in deep networks. The idea of a network actively "reconsidering" an input by modulating its own internal feature extractors is a significant and exciting departure from the static, feedforward paradigm.

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The combination of a pre-trained deep network with a separately trained RL policy for internal, dynamic feature modulation is a genuinely new and powerful concept. It introduces a mechanism for post-training adaptation and iterative refinement, which has the potential to open up a rich new area of research.

2.  **Strong Motivation:** The approach is well-motivated by compelling theories from visual neuroscience. This grounding provides a strong conceptual anchor for the architectural choices and distinguishes the work from being a mere collection of engineering tricks.

3.  **Demonstrated Efficacy:** The authors don't just present a novel idea; they successfully implement it on top of a strong baseline (Maxout networks) and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the challenging CIFAR-100 dataset. The qualitative example of correcting a cat/dog misclassification is particularly compelling, providing concrete evidence that the attentional mechanism is performing its intended function.

4.  **Ambitious Technical Approach:** Tackling the optimization of a policy with over a million parameters using RL is non-trivial. The choice of SNES, a black-box optimizer, is a pragmatic and clever solution to navigate the challenges of a high-dimensional action space and potentially difficult reward landscape, for which standard policy gradient methods might struggle.

**Weaknesses**
The paper's weaknesses are largely consequences of its ambition and novelty. While they do not detract from the core contribution, they are worth noting as areas for future exploration.

1.  **Methodological Complexity and Cost:** The two-stage training procedure (supervised pre-training followed by computationally expensive RL) presents a significant barrier. The 4-day training time for the policy on a single GPU highlights the cost, which would likely be prohibitive for larger-scale datasets like ImageNet.

2.  **Limited Ablation and Design Justification:** Some design choices feel somewhat ad-hoc. The construction of the observation vector for the policy, by averaging map activations, is a reasonable heuristic but is not compared against alternatives. A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity to this representation would strengthen the work. Similarly, the choice of SNES is justified by the parameter count but isn't benchmarked against other possible RL algorithms.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The validation is confined to CIFAR-10/100. While impressive results are shown here, it remains an open question how the approach scales to higher-resolution images where the number of feature maps, and thus the dimensionality of the action space, would be vastly larger.

**Recommendation**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a significant, novel, and thought-provoking concept: enabling deep networks to perform iterative, internal attentional processing. This is the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that NIPS should champion.
*   The conceptual contribution is more important than methodological perfection. The weaknesses—computational cost, limited ablation, and dataset scale—are understandable limitations for a paper breaking new ground and represent exciting avenues for follow-up work rather than fatal flaws.
*   The work successfully demonstrates the viability of its core idea by improving upon a strong baseline and achieving state-of-the-art results, proving that the concept is not just speculative but has practical merit.
*   It is likely to be highly influential and to inspire a new line of research into dynamic, adaptive inference processes in neural networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The methodology could be seen as complex and not fully validated against alternatives.
*   The computational expense and restriction to smaller datasets might limit its immediate practical impact.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance are far more compelling. This work prioritizes a groundbreaking idea over safe, incremental progress. It is precisely the kind of research that pushes the boundaries of the field. The authors have successfully demonstrated a proof-of-concept for a new and exciting paradigm, and the community will benefit greatly from its publication.
