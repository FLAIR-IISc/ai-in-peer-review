

# NIPS Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

## Summary

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances standard feedforward CNNs by incorporating selective internal attention through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow a pre-trained CNN to iteratively adjust its filter weights across multiple passes over the same image, enabling the network to "think" about ambiguous cases and correct its initial classification. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture and learn the attention policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization technique suitable for high-dimensional policy spaces. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, where it achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on difficult classification cases.

The work connects to several important lines of research: the biological inspiration from human visual cortex with its top-down feedback connections [28-31], the growing interest in recurrent processing in deep learning [48-50], and the application of reinforcement learning to visual attention [40-41]. The paper positions itself as a bridge between these areas, applying RL to improve state-of-the-art CNNs rather than using simplified datasets for demonstration purposes.

## Quality

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical soundness:** The methodology is well-motivated and technically sound. The authors clearly explain how they extend standard Maxout networks to incorporate attention weights and how they use SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

2. **Experimental rigor:** The experiments are well-designed to test the core hypothesis. The focus on "difficult" classification cases (where two classes are often confused) is particularly relevant to the proposed mechanism. The ablation studies (dynamics analysis, information content of gate values) provide strong evidence that the attention mechanism is actually doing something meaningful.

3. **Appropriate baselines:** The comparison against vanilla CNNs and the mention of how it outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods (though the exact comparison is somewhat vague) provides a reasonable baseline for evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for the RL approach:** While the authors justify using SNES due to the high dimensionality of the policy space, they don't provide a theoretical argument for why RL is the right framework for this problem. It's unclear why a supervised approach (e.g., learning the attention weights directly from the desired output) wouldn't work better. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of alternative approaches and why they were deemed unsuitable.

2. **Limited ablation of key design choices:** The paper uses T=5 steps for all experiments, but doesn't explore how performance varies with different numbers of passes. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) shows that performance peaks at T steps and then decreases, but it's unclear whether this is due to overfitting to the training distribution or a fundamental limitation of the approach.

3. **Weak theoretical analysis:** The paper lacks any theoretical analysis of why this approach should work or how the attention mechanism relates to biological visual processing. The connection to human vision is mentioned but not rigorously developed.

4. **Evaluation on standard benchmarks:** While CIFAR-10/100 are standard benchmarks, the paper doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets) that would provide a more meaningful benchmark for the proposed approach.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-structured:** The paper is well-organized, with clear sections explaining the background, methodology, experiments, and related work.

2. **Effective use of figures:** The figures (particularly Figure 3 showing the attention dynamics) are very effective in illustrating the core mechanism of dasNet.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Ambiguous terminology:** The paper uses "feedback connections" and "top-down connections" interchangeably, which is confusing given that the feedback connections in the proposed method are not top-down in the traditional neural network sense (they're not connecting higher layers to lower layers in a hierarchical manner, but rather modulating the weights of filters within the same layer).

2. **Insufficient detail on implementation:** While the algorithm is described, there's insufficient detail on how the observation space h(M) is constructed (e.g., what specific features are concatenated, how they're normalized). This makes it difficult to assess the scalability of the approach.

3. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for the attention weights (a^ℓ_j vs. a^ℓ_j in equations 5 and 7), which can be confusing for readers.

## Originality

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel combination of ideas:** The paper successfully combines several established techniques (Maxout networks, reinforcement learning, feedback connections) in a novel way to address the problem of selective attention in deep networks.

2. **Focus on internal attention:** The approach differs from most previous work on attention mechanisms (which typically focus on spatial attention or external attention) by modulating the internal filters of the network itself.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty relative to existing work:** The core idea of using iterative processing to improve classification has been explored before. For example, the "Neural Abstraction Pyramid" [48-50] uses iterative processing for image reconstruction and face localization. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works.

2. **Lack of clear distinction from other attention mechanisms:** The paper doesn't clearly explain how dasNet differs from other attention mechanisms like the "Additive Attention" [41] or "Channel Attention" [51] mechanisms that have been proposed in the literature.

3. **Not significantly different from previous NIPS work:** The paper's approach is similar to "Deep Recurrent Attention Networks" [42-43] presented at NIPS 2014, which also used reinforcement learning to control attention in CNNs. The paper should explicitly acknowledge and contrast with this prior work.

## Significance

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a meaningful problem:** The paper tackles an important problem in deep learning - how to improve classification of ambiguous cases without requiring retraining of the entire network.

2. **Potential for broader applications:** The approach could be extended to other domains where iterative refinement of predictions is beneficial (e.g., medical imaging, video analysis).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited performance gains:** A 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is modest compared to the progress made in recent years (e.g., ResNet achieved ~90% on CIFAR-10, with recent models reaching 95%+). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this small gain is significant enough to warrant the added complexity.

2. **Computational cost:** The paper mentions that training dasNet took "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU" (which is an older GPU), but doesn't provide any information about inference time. Given that dasNet requires multiple passes over the same image, inference time could be significantly higher than standard CNNs, which would limit its practical utility.

3. **No discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss important limitations, such as how the approach scales to larger networks (e.g., ResNet), how it performs on tasks beyond classification (e.g., segmentation), or whether it generalizes to other datasets.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to incorporating selective attention into deep CNNs through feedback connections, with some promising experimental results on CIFAR-10/100. However, the novelty is limited, the theoretical justification is weak, and the performance gains are modest compared to recent state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The method is technically sound and well-executed.
- The qualitative analysis of attention dynamics provides valuable insights into how the network processes ambiguous cases.
- The approach is a novel application of reinforcement learning to improve existing CNN architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The performance gains are modest and not compared to the most recent state-of-the-art models.
- The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on attention mechanisms and iterative processing.
- The theoretical justification for using reinforcement learning is weak.
- The computational cost of the approach (multiple passes over the image) is not adequately addressed.

## Recommendations

1. **Expand the comparison to more recent state-of-the-art models** (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets) to better establish the significance of the performance gains.

2. **Provide a more thorough theoretical justification** for using reinforcement learning over alternative approaches (e.g., supervised learning of attention weights).

3. **Clarify the relationship to prior work** on attention mechanisms and iterative processing, particularly the NIPS 2014 paper on Deep Recurrent Attention Networks.

4. **Include a discussion of computational cost** (both training and inference time) and how it impacts the practical utility of the approach.

5. **Provide more detail on the observation space** used for the RL policy, including how features are selected and normalized.

6. **Conduct ablation studies** on the number of passes (T) and show how performance varies, rather than fixing T=5.

7. **Address the limitations** of the approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding scalability to larger networks and other tasks.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea with some promising results, the novelty is limited, the theoretical justification is weak, and the performance gains are modest compared to the current state of the art. The paper would need to make significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, it needs to better establish its novelty relative to prior work, provide a stronger theoretical foundation, and demonstrate more substantial performance improvements on relevant benchmarks.

The paper has potential but currently falls short of the quality and significance expected for NIPS publication.
