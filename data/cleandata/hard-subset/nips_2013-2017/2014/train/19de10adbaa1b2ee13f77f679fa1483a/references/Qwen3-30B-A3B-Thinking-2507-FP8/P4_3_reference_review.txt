

**Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

**Summary:**  
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing deep CNNs by incorporating feedback connections that enable iterative selective attention through multiple passes over an image. The authors propose learning a reinforcement learning (RL) policy to modulate filter activations across layers, inspired by human visual processing where experts re-evaluate images to distinguish similar objects. dasNet builds on Maxout networks and uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a policy that adaptively emphasizes or suppresses features during sequential passes. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, demonstrating a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs, particularly on ambiguous classification cases. The work relates to prior research on human visual cortex connectivity (e.g., Felleman and Essen [28]) and RL-based attention mechanisms (e.g., Bar et al. [37]), while distinguishing itself by applying RL to learn internal attention policies rather than external saccades or simple attention modules.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The paper is technically sound, with clear descriptions of the dasNet architecture, RL formulation, and training procedure. The use of SNES is well-justified for handling the high-dimensional policy space (over 1 million parameters), and the experimental setup on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100) is appropriate. Results demonstrate a statistically significant improvement (6% relative) over state-of-the-art CNNs, with ablation studies (e.g., dynamics analysis in Figure 2) supporting the approach’s efficacy. The authors acknowledge limitations, including computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) and the lack of theoretical justification for the observation vector.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from motivation to method to experiments. Algorithm 1 provides a precise description of the training procedure, and the experimental section details hyperparameters (e.g., λcorrect = 0.005, λmisclassified = 1) and implementation specifics (e.g., dropout rates, SNES population size). The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 effectively illustrates how dasNet corrects misclassifications by modulating filter activations.

*Originality:* The core contribution—learning internal selective attention via RL with feedback connections—is novel. While attention mechanisms (e.g., SENet [51]) and recurrent processing (e.g., [48, 49]) exist, dasNet uniquely combines RL to learn *how* to modulate filters across multiple passes, rather than using fixed attention weights or external feedback. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work (e.g., Bar et al. [37] on top-down expectations) by focusing on *internal* network adaptation post-training, not just initial processing.

*Significance:* The results address a meaningful challenge (ambiguous classification) with a practical solution that improves state-of-the-art performance. The approach is generalizable to other CNN architectures and tasks, and the demonstration that RL can enhance existing models (without retraining) is a valuable contribution to the field. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with clear metrics (6% relative improvement) on widely used benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Theoretical Foundation:* The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for key design choices (e.g., the observation vector construction in Section 4). While the human vision motivation is compelling, the authors do not rigorously analyze *why* the proposed RL policy structure is optimal or how it relates to information-theoretic principles of attention.

*Computational Cost:* The 4-day training time for dasNet (on a GTX 560 Ti) is substantial, though the paper does not compare this to alternative methods (e.g., ensembling or larger CNNs). This limits practical applicability, especially for resource-constrained settings.

*Attention Mechanism Analysis:* The qualitative analysis (Figure 3) is insightful but superficial. The paper acknowledges "visualizing what these high-level [filters] actually do is an open problem," yet provides no deeper investigation into how the learned attention patterns correlate with human interpretability or feature importance.

*Related Work Context:* The paper could better differentiate dasNet from recent attention mechanisms (e.g., CBAM [52] or non-local networks [53]), which also modulate feature importance. A brief comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel RL-based internal attention mechanism, significant performance gains on standard benchmarks, clear methodology, and strong empirical validation.  
*Con:* Limited theoretical grounding, high computational cost, and superficial analysis of the attention mechanism.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper presents a technically sound, novel approach that advances the state of the art in deep learning for image classification. While the theoretical foundation is modest and computational costs are non-trivial, the empirical results are compelling and the contribution is clearly distinct from prior work. The paper meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, with a well-executed evaluation that demonstrates meaningful progress. I recommend acceptance.
