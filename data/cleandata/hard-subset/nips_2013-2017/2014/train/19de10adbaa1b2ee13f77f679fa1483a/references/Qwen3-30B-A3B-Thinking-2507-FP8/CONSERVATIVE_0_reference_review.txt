

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which introduces feedback connections to deep CNNs to enable iterative selective attention through reinforcement learning. The authors claim that by allowing the network to make multiple passes over an image, with filter weights modulated by a learned policy, they can correct misclassifications and achieve state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100.

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly articulates the motivation for iterative processing, drawing connections to human visual processing.
- The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 show a modest improvement (6% relative) over the base Maxout network.
- The visualization of attention changes across layers provides some intuitive insight into how the model operates.

**Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous comparison. The claimed "state-of-the-art" result is relative to a specific base network (Maxout with dropout), not to contemporary architectures. The CIFAR-10/100 results are marginal (6% relative improvement), yet the paper fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods that have achieved significantly better performance on these benchmarks. For instance, the paper doesn't compare against ResNets, DenseNets, or other modern architectures that have pushed performance much higher on CIFAR-10/100. The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti for dasNet training) is substantial for a marginal improvement, which undermines the practical value of the approach.

**Methodological Soundness:** The reinforcement learning component is particularly concerning. The authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize a policy with "close to a million weights" due to the large action space. This approach is questionable for several reasons: (1) SNES is designed for black-box optimization of continuous functions, not for learning control policies in neural networks; (2) The paper provides no justification for why SNES is better than simpler RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) for this specific task; (3) The policy's action space is defined as "close to a thousand dimensions," but the paper fails to explain how this dimensionality is manageable or why it's necessary. This represents a methodological leap without sufficient justification.

**Originality:** While the paper cites related work on feedback connections in the visual cortex and some computer vision applications, the specific approach of using RL to learn attention modulation in a pre-trained network is not substantially novel. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish itself from prior work on recurrent networks or attention mechanisms. The "internal selective attention" concept is similar to approaches like "recurrent neural networks for image classification" (e.g., [50]), but the paper fails to demonstrate how dasNet is fundamentally different or superior.

**Significance:** The claimed improvement is marginal and comes with substantial computational overhead. Given that standard practice in the field has moved toward deeper networks, better data augmentation, and more efficient architectures (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets), a 6% improvement with a 4-day training cost for dasNet seems unlikely to be compelling for the community. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this approach is preferable to simply training a larger, more complex network—which is the standard practice for improving performance on image classification tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the explanation of the reinforcement learning component is confusing. Algorithm 1 contains unclear notation (e.g., "ot ⇐ h(Mt)"), and the description of the observation space lacks sufficient detail. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the observation vector is constructed and why it's sufficient for the policy to learn effective attention modulation.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper explores an interesting idea related to iterative processing in neural networks, the methodological approach is questionable, the claimed improvements are marginal and not compared against contemporary baselines, and the computational cost is high for the gains achieved. The paper fails to demonstrate that this approach provides a meaningful advancement over established practices in deep learning. For a NIPS paper, this level of marginal improvement with an unproven method does not meet the bar for significance and rigor required by the conference.
