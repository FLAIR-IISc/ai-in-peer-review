

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to incorporating internal selective attention into deep CNNs through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow a pre-trained CNN to iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image, with a reinforcement learning policy (learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) dynamically adjusting the importance of different filters on each pass. This enables the network to "think" about the image multiple times, correcting initial misclassifications by emphasizing or de-emphasizing certain features.

The paper makes a compelling case for the biological plausibility of this approach, drawing parallels to human visual processing where experts take multiple passes to distinguish between similar objects. The dasNet architecture is well-motivated and the RL approach to learning the attention policy is a creative solution to the challenge of handling the large number of parameters required for such a policy.

The main strength of this paper is its bold conceptual novelty. The authors propose a fundamentally different way of thinking about how deep networks process information, moving beyond the standard feedforward paradigm. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it opens up new directions for research on attention mechanisms in deep learning. The paper successfully demonstrates that dasNet can achieve a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this dataset. The visualizations in Figure 3 are particularly compelling, showing how the network's attention shifts across layers to correct a misclassification from "dog" to "cat."

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection to related work in both neuroscience and computer vision. The authors have made a clear effort to situate their work within the broader context of visual attention mechanisms, citing relevant literature on both biological vision and machine learning.

While the paper has some methodological limitations (the RL approach is not the most conventional, and the paper could provide more detailed ablation studies), these are overshadowed by the paper's novel contribution. The authors acknowledge that the current implementation is computationally intensive (taking 4 days to train on a GTX 560 Ti), but this is a practical concern rather than a fundamental flaw.

The significance of this work lies in its conceptual innovation. By allowing networks to iteratively refine their internal representations through feedback connections, dasNet challenges the standard feedforward assumption that underlies most deep learning architectures. This is a paradigm shift that could inspire many future developments in attention mechanisms and neural network design.

The paper does not claim to be a complete solution (it's limited to CIFAR-10/100 and requires multiple passes for inference), but it presents a compelling new direction that could lead to significant advances in the field. The authors' claim that this approach establishes a new state-of-the-art for CIFAR-10/100 is supported by their experiments, though it would be strengthened by comparison to the most recent SOTA methods.

As a reviewer who highly values novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The novel conceptual approach outweighs any methodological imperfections, and the idea of allowing CNNs to iteratively refine their internal representations through feedback connections is both bold and promising. This work will likely inspire significant future research on attention mechanisms and internal processing in deep neural networks.

Recommendation: Accept
