

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to improve classification performance of deep CNNs by incorporating selective attention through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow a pre-trained CNN to iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image, with a reinforcement learning policy determining how to adjust filter weights on each pass. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture and train a policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to select filter weights that improve classification on difficult examples. The method achieves a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 compared to vanilla CNNs, establishing a new state-of-the-art for these datasets. The work connects to previous research on recurrent processing in visual cortex (Felleman & Essen, 1991) and recent attention mechanisms in deep learning, while distinguishing itself by focusing on improving state-of-the-art networks rather than using simplified datasets.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough experimental validation. The methodology is clearly explained, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The use of SNES to handle the large policy space is well-justified, and the authors acknowledge the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti). The experiments focus on ambiguous classification cases where the method shows clear benefits, and the authors appropriately address limitations (e.g., computational cost). However, the paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of why the approach works, and the connection to biological vision is somewhat superficial. The authors do acknowledge the limitations of their approach and note that their method is designed for difficult cases rather than general improvement.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear descriptions of the methodology. Algorithm 1 is particularly helpful for understanding the training process. However, there are some confusing elements: the notation for attention weights (a^l_j) is not consistently explained, and the relationship between the observation space (h(M_t)) and action space could be clarified. The description of how observations are constructed (Section 4) would benefit from more detail about why these specific elements were chosen. The figures (particularly Figure 3) are helpful for visualizing the attention mechanism, though the captions could be more descriptive.

## Originality
The core idea of using reinforcement learning to dynamically adjust filter weights across multiple passes through a CNN is novel. While there has been previous work on recurrent processing in CNNs (Ciresan et al., 2011; Xu et al., 2015), dasNet specifically focuses on using RL to learn how to selectively attend to different features on successive passes. The authors make a clear distinction between their approach and previous work, noting that most prior work has focused on simplified datasets or specific tasks rather than improving state-of-the-art networks. The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is also a novel application of this technique. However, the paper could better differentiate itself from recent work on attention mechanisms in transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) and other attention-based architectures.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in deep learning: how to improve classification performance on difficult examples without retraining the entire network. The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is significant for these benchmark datasets and represents a clear advancement over the state-of-the-art. The method is generalizable to other CNN architectures, which increases its potential impact. The approach also has interesting connections to human visual processing, which could inspire future work. However, the computational cost (4 days for dasNet training on top of the base CNN) may limit its practical applicability for large-scale applications. The paper would be stronger if it discussed potential scaling to larger networks or datasets.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to incorporating selective attention through feedback connections
- Thorough experimental validation on standard benchmarks
- Clear explanation of methodology and algorithm
- Demonstrated improvement over state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100
- Good connection to biological visual processing

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of why the approach works
- Some unclear notation and descriptions (e.g., observation space construction)
- Computational cost is significant (4 days on GTX 560 Ti)
- Limited comparison to other attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers)
- Superficial connection to related work on attention mechanisms

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field of deep learning for computer vision. The method is novel, the experiments are thorough, and the results are significant. While there are some weaknesses in the presentation and theoretical grounding, these do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work.

## Suggested Improvements
1. Clarify the observation space construction and relationship between observations and actions
2. Provide a more detailed comparison with attention mechanisms in transformers
3. Discuss potential scaling to larger networks or datasets
4. Include a more thorough theoretical analysis of the attention mechanism
5. Consider adding a comparison with recent work on attention mechanisms in CNNs

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating how reinforcement learning can be used to enhance existing CNN architectures through internal selective attention, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The work is well-suited for NIPS and would be of significant interest to the community.
