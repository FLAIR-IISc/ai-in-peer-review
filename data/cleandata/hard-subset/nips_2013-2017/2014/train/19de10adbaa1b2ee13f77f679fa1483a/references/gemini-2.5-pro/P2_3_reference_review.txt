**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is to improve classification performance, particularly on difficult or ambiguous examples, by allowing the network to perform multiple "passes" over an input image. After an initial feedforward pass, a control policy modulates the activations of convolutional filter maps in subsequent passes, effectively emphasizing or suppressing certain features. This control policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL)—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—*after* the underlying CNN (a Maxout network) has been fully trained via supervised learning. The authors frame this as mimicking a human's ability to take a "second look" or think more deeply about a difficult problem. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this approach improves upon the baseline Maxout network, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data.

**Review**

This paper presents a novel and interesting combination of deep learning, reinforcement learning, and neuroscience-inspired concepts. The work is well-motivated, and the results are promising.

**Quality: (Good)**

The paper is technically sound. The proposed mechanism for modulating filter activity via learned attention weights `a` (Equation 5) is a straightforward and reasonable extension to a standard CNN. The two-stage training process—supervised learning for the base network followed by RL for the attention policy—is a logical way to tackle the problem, allowing the policy to fine-tune the behavior of an already powerful feature extractor.

*   **Strengths:** The experimental analysis is a significant strength. The authors don't just report a final accuracy number; they provide a qualitative analysis of a corrected misclassification (Figure 3), which offers valuable insight into what the network is doing. The additional experiments on the dynamics (showing stability for `T >` trained `T`) and the information content of the learned gates further strengthen the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful, non-trivial strategy. The choice of a strong baseline (Maxout network) makes the reported performance gains more credible.

*   **Weaknesses:** The justification for using SNES over other RL algorithms could be more thorough. The paper states that standard RL methods are "impractical" for the ~1 million parameters in the policy network, but modern policy gradient methods are often applied to networks of this size or larger. A more detailed comparison or justification would strengthen this choice. Additionally, the experimental evaluation is confined to the relatively small CIFAR datasets. While achieving SOTA here is valuable, the computational cost (4 days for policy training on one GPU) raises questions about the method's scalability to larger datasets like ImageNet, where both the base model and the policy would be significantly larger. The reward function (Equation 8) includes a boosting-like mechanism (`λboost`). It is unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the learned attentional policy versus this targeted focus on difficult examples. An ablation study that trains the policy without this boosting mechanism would help disentangle these effects.

**Clarity: (Good)**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The core idea is explained clearly in the introduction, and the methods section, particularly Algorithm 1, provides a good overview of the training procedure. Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the multi-pass process.

*   **Suggestions for Improvement:** The description of the state/observation space `o` could be more precise. It's described as a concatenation of average map activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities, but the exact dimensions and structure are not specified. Similarly, the RL formulation is a bit high-level; providing more details about the SNES update in the main text, rather than deferring entirely to supplementary material, would make the paper more self-contained.

**Originality: (Excellent)**

The central contribution of this paper is highly original. While attention mechanisms and recurrent connections are not new to neural networks, the specific formulation here is novel. The concept of an external RL agent learning to control the *internal* feature processing of a pre-trained, feedforward network is a fresh perspective. Most prior work on attention in vision focuses on spatial attention (i.e., where to look in an image, like learning saccades), whereas this work proposes a form of *feature-based* attention that modulates the contribution of learned filters. The idea of post-hoc correction of a trained model via iterative "thoughts" is a compelling and powerful concept that differentiates it from standard end-to-end training of recurrent models.

**Significance: (Very Good)**

The work is significant for several reasons. First, it demonstrates a practical method for improving the performance of a state-of-the-art model, backed by strong empirical results on a standard benchmark. The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-100 is a meaningful advance. Second, it opens up a promising new research direction. The idea of decoupling feature extraction from the control policy that uses those features is powerful and could be extended in many ways (e.g., more complex policies, spatially-varying attention, application to other domains). The approach provides a potential framework for addressing catastrophic forgetting, as it modifies the network's *behavior* on difficult examples without altering the base weights learned on the entire dataset. Researchers working on attention, reinforcement learning, and deep learning architectures are likely to find these ideas inspiring and build upon them.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Originality:** The core idea of using RL to learn an internal attention policy for a pre-trained CNN is novel and conceptually elegant.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-100), validating the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide good qualitative and quantitative analysis beyond simple accuracy metrics, lending credibility to their claims.
*   **High Potential for Impact:** The work introduces a new paradigm for improving deep networks that is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scalability demonstrated:** Experiments are limited to CIFAR, and the high computational cost may hinder application to larger-scale problems like ImageNet.
*   **Insufficient Justification for RL Method:** The choice of SNES over more standard policy-based RL methods is not fully justified.
*   **Confounding Factors in Experiments:** The contribution of the boosting scheme in the reward function is not isolated from the contribution of the attention policy itself.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance**. The weaknesses, while valid points for future work, are outweighed by the high degree of originality, the conceptual elegance of the approach, and the strong empirical results. This paper introduces a genuinely new and promising idea to the field and supports it with a solid, if not exhaustive, set of experiments. It is a stimulating piece of work that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
