- Proposes dasNet: a pre-trained Maxout CNN augmented with multiplicative per-feature-map gates updated over multiple recurrent passes by a learned policy (trained with SNES) to improve classification.
- Reports nontrivial CIFAR-10/100 improvements over the Maxout baseline and shows qualitative examples where later passes correct initial mistakes.
- Connects to biological feedback/attention and recurrent ML models but differs by learning a black-box control policy for internal feature reweighting rather than spatial glimpse attention.
- Conceptually simple and generic: internal multiplicative attention that can be bolted onto existing CNNs without retraining feedforward weights.
- Empirically plausible dynamics: emphasis/de-emphasis of filters on ambiguous examples and gate values containing class-relevant information.
- Sensible design choices: summary-statistic observations, positive mean-centered actions via scaled softmax, and emphasis on misclassified examples during policy training.
- Choice of SNES over gradient-based training is insufficiently justified given the policy and gates are differentiable; no gradient-based or policy-gradient baselines provided.
- Missing capacity-matched baselines (wider/deeper feedforward, single-pass learned reweighting, gating only top layers) so gains may be due to extra parameters.
- Computational cost and inference/training overhead (wall-clock, FLOPs, memory) are not reported despite T-fold increased passes.
- Methodological details are underspecified (exact Maxout architecture, observation dimensions/normalization, policy and SNES hyperparameters, training schedule), harming reproducibility.
- Loss definition and notation are confusing (symbols reused); criteria for “misclassified” need clarification.
- Action parameterization via scaled softmax enforces competition among gates and may preclude independent gating—no ablation with independent sigmoids/tanh.
- Evaluation is narrow: only CIFAR-10/100, no statistical significance, robustness tests, confusion matrices, or analysis of correct→incorrect vs incorrect→correct flips.
- Gate informativeness analysis conflates correlation and causation; stronger tests (randomizing/freezing gates or predicting gates from class probs) are missing.
- Stability analysis is limited to a toy 2-layer model; need stability/performance vs T on the main architecture and behavior past T.
- Literature positioning could be strengthened by comparing to iterative refinement and other contemporary recurrent/feedback architectures.
- Pros: clear, broadly applicable internal attention mechanism; empirical gains on strong baselines; bridges neuroscience-inspired feedback with deep CNNs.
- Cons: missing critical baselines/ablations, incomplete experimental characterization, notational errors, and insufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Suggested improvements: add capacity-matched and gradient-based baselines, ablations for gating locations and policy architectures, report compute costs and significance, clarify loss/notation and SNES details, release code, analyze flips/confusion matrices and robustness, and test alternative action parameterizations.
- Review score: 6.5 (weak accept if strengthened); reviewer confidence: high.
