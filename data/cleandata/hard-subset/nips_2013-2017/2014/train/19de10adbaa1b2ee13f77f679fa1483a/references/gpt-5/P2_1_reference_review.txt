Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, learned feedback connections that modulate per-feature-map activations across multiple passes on the same image. The gating policy maps summary observations of the network’s internal activations and current class probabilities to multiplicative gates applied to each feature map in each layer. The policy is trained post hoc (after standard supervised training of the base Maxout CNN) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the high dimensionality of the observation/action spaces and resulting policy parameter count (~10^6). On CIFAR-10/100, the approach yields improvements over the underlying Maxout baseline, with the paper claiming state-of-the-art performance (for unaugmented data) at the time.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of attention, recurrence, and RL for perception. It differs from “glimpse”-based attention (e.g., Mnih et al.) by focusing on internal, feature-level attention rather than spatial attention. It relates to recurrent and top-down feedback in vision (O’Reilly; Wyatte et al.) but implements feedback via learned multiplicative channel-wise gates driven by a policy. Using black-box evolution strategies to learn a high-dimensional policy to control a deep net is unusual and distinguishes the work from gradient-based recurrent models. The connections to prior work are generally acknowledged, though some modern gradient-based alternatives are not explored.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing idea: iterative, internal attention to correct initial feedforward mistakes.
- Simple architectural intervention (per-feature-map multiplicative gates) compatible with standard CNNs.
- End-to-end sequential decision-making formulation with a well-defined policy and observation space.
- Demonstrated improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with qualitative analyses of dynamics and gate informativeness.
- Empirical evidence that multiple passes improve predictions on ambiguous cases, aligning with cognitive motivation.

Weaknesses
- Justification for NES over gradient-based training is weak; the system is differentiable and could be trained by BPTT through the recurrent unrolled computation (policy network included), at least in principle. A comparison would be valuable.
- Limited baselines and ablations: no comparison to simpler post-hoc calibrators (e.g., a learned residual classifier on internal features), to channel-wise gating trained by supervised gradients, or to ensembling multiple stochastic passes of the base model.
- Inference/runtime cost increases by a factor of T; computational overhead and throughput/latency trade-offs are not quantified.
- Observations discard spatial information (map averages), so the “attention” is strictly internal/channel-wise; this limitation should be emphasized, and potential benefits from spatial attention are not explored.
- Reporting lacks statistical rigor: no confidence intervals, multiple runs, or significance tests; some notation issues (e.g., d and λboost definition) create ambiguity.
- Scope limited to CIFAR; no results on larger-scale benchmarks to establish robustness.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The method is plausible and internally coherent. The training loop is clearly specified, and stability considerations (softmax scaling to keep average gate near 1) are sensible. However, the choice of black-box optimization for a million-parameter policy raises concerns about sample efficiency and scalability. The loss formulation with boosted weights for misclassified examples is reasonable but somewhat ad hoc, and the notation around d in the loss is confusing (it appears to conflate indicator of correctness with argmax).
- Empirical support: Gains are shown, including dynamics analysis and a gate-information sanity check. That said, the evaluation would be stronger with broader baselines, variance estimates, and per-class confusion improvements to substantiate the claim of fixing ambiguous cases.
- Completeness: The paper presents a complete pipeline but leaves out comparisons to plausible alternatives, making it hard to attribute gains specifically to the recurrent attention mechanism versus other forms of post-hoc adjustment.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable, with an algorithm box and clear descriptions of observations/actions.
- Some notational imprecision (e.g., Eq. 10, definition of d; toggling between layer indices and vectorization) should be fixed.
- The claim “unaugmented data” seems at odds with earlier mention of data augmentation; this needs clarification.
- Details of SNES settings and policy architecture are deferred; include enough in the main text to reproduce.

Originality
- The combination of internal channel-wise attention via feedback, learned as a sequential policy, is novel relative to contemporaneous attention and recurrent vision models.
- The use of NES to learn a high-dimensional policy for deep nets is unusual (though debatably necessary).
- The work is a substantive step toward learned, top-down control in CNNs.

Significance
- On standard benchmarks, the reported improvements over strong baselines suggest practical value; at the time, the results appear competitive.
- The idea of learned channel-wise attention across time is influential conceptually and likely to inspire follow-on work (e.g., learned gating/SE/FiLM-like mechanisms).
- Concerns: scalability to larger datasets and models, and the lack of gradient-based baselines, limit immediate impact.

Questions for the authors
- Why not train the policy by backpropagation through time? Did you attempt this? If not, what failure modes led you to prefer SNES?
- What is the runtime overhead at inference (per image), and how does accuracy vary with T versus compute?
- How sensitive are results to the observation design (e.g., adding spatial moments, using intermediate layer statistics)? Any ablations?
- Can you report mean/std over multiple runs and clarify augmentation usage vs “unaugmented” claim?
- How does a simpler linear policy or gating only the last convolutional layer compare?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated architectural idea with demonstrated empirical gains.
- Bridges cognitive inspiration (feedback/attention) with modern CNNs.
- Clear method and some insightful analyses of the learned dynamics.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (gradient-based recurrent training, simpler post-hoc models).
- Computational cost and scalability concerns not addressed.
- Reporting/notation issues and limited statistical rigor.

Overall rating: 7/10 (accept)
Confidence: 3/5
