### **Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

#### **Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with a mechanism for internal, selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple, iterative passes over the same input image. After an initial feedforward pass, a policy network observes the internal state of the CNN (e.g., feature map activations) and generates a set of modulatory weights. These weights are then used to amplify or suppress the outputs of specific convolutional filters in subsequent passes. This process aims to mimic human-like iterative reasoning, where an initial "glance" is refined by focused attention to disambiguate difficult cases. The policy for modulating the filters is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—on top of a pre-trained, state-of-the-art Maxout network. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves classification accuracy on CIFAR-100, achieving a new state-of-the-art result for that dataset at the time of writing.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of adding a learnable, top-down modulatory mechanism to a pre-trained deep network is highly original and conceptually appealing. It moves beyond the purely feedforward paradigm that dominates deep learning and provides a plausible computational model for recurrent, attentional processing observed in the visual cortex. The distinction between a fast, pre-attentive feedforward pass and a slower, iterative refinement phase is well-motivated by neuroscience.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The technical approach is sound. Using a pre-trained network and learning the attention policy as a separate, subsequent phase is a practical way to make the problem tractable. The choice of SNES to optimize the policy is well-justified; given the high dimensionality of the action space (modulating every filter map) and the non-differentiable nature of the evolutionary search reward, traditional policy gradient methods would likely be less stable or sample-efficient. The reward function, which boosts the loss for misclassified examples, is a clever way to focus the learning process on the "difficult" cases where attention is most needed.

3.  **Significant Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement over a strong baseline (a state-of-the-art Maxout network). Achieving a 6% relative improvement on a challenging benchmark like CIFAR-100 is significant and provides compelling evidence that the proposed mechanism is not just conceptually interesting but practically effective. The qualitative analysis of the cat/dog example, while anecdotal, provides valuable insight into the model's behavior, showing how the network dynamically re-weights features to correct an initial misclassification.

4.  **Strong Connection to Related Fields:** The authors do an excellent job of connecting their work to cognitive science and neuroscience, particularly regarding the dual-stage (pre-attentive vs. attentive) theories of vision and the role of feedback connections. This cross-disciplinary grounding enriches the paper and strengthens its intellectual contribution.

#### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, certain methodological details could be clarified to aid reproducibility.
    *   The composition of the observation vector `o_t` is critical but described somewhat vaguely. Precisely how the average activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities are concatenated and normalized would be important for anyone trying to implement the model.
    *   The policy network `πθ` is described as a neural network in one place (Eq. 7) but appears to be a single linear layer with a scaled softmax. This should be made explicit. If it is just a linear policy, this is an important detail.
    *   Key hyperparameters for the SNES optimization (e.g., learning rate, initial variance) are not mentioned in the main text, which are crucial for this kind of black-box optimization.

2.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary practical drawback of dasNet is its computational expense, both at training and inference time. Training the policy takes four days on a GPU, and inference requires `T` (here, T=5) full forward passes through the network. This represents a 5x increase in inference time compared to the baseline. This trade-off is not sufficiently discussed. A crucial comparison would be against a baseline feedforward network with a similar computational budget (e.g., a wider or deeper network that takes roughly 5x longer for a single pass). It is possible that simply increasing the capacity of the feedforward model could yield similar gains.

3.  **Limited Ablation and Analysis:**
    *   The choice of `T=5` is stated as being experimentally determined, but no results are shown for other values of `T`. The dynamics analysis for `T=1` and `T=2` is interesting but would be more compelling if it included the `T=5` model used for the main results.
    *   The authors justify using SNES by stating that standard RL methods are "impractical" for the high-dimensional parameter space. While this is a reasonable hypothesis, the paper would be stronger if it included an empirical comparison against a standard policy gradient method (e.g., REINFORCE), even if it performed poorly. This would provide direct evidence for their methodological choice.
    *   The "boosting" mechanism in the reward function is a key component. An analysis of its effect is missing. For example, does focusing on misclassified images cause the performance on initially correct images to degrade? An analysis of the accuracy change on different subsets of the data (e.g., easy vs. hard examples) would be insightful.

4.  **Relation to Other Attention Mechanisms:** The related work section is strong on neuroscience but could be better situated within the machine learning literature on attention. While the paper's approach of modulating *internal filters* is unique, it would be beneficial to compare and contrast it with other contemporaneous attention mechanisms, such as spatial attention models (e.g., Spatial Transformer Networks) or recurrent attention models (e.g., DRAW), which focus attention on different parts of the input image rather than internal feature maps.

#### **Final Recommendation**

This is a strong, innovative paper with a compelling core idea, solid execution, and significant results. It successfully bridges concepts from neuroscience with modern deep learning practice to create a novel and effective architecture. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to a need for deeper analysis, clarification of certain details, and a more thorough discussion of computational trade-offs, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The work clearly advances the state of the art and opens up a promising new direction for research into more dynamic and deliberative neural network models. It is a clear contribution to the field and well-suited for NIPS.
