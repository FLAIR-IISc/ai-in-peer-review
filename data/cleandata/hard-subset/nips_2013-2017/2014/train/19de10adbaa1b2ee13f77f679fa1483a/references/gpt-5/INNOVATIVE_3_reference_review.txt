Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which add learned, internal selective attention to a trained CNN via recurrent feedback. Concretely, each forward pass over an image is followed by a controller that observes summary statistics from multiple layers (per-map average activations, classification pre-activations, and class probabilities) and outputs multiplicative gates for each feature map in each layer. The same image is then re-processed with these gates, iterating for T passes. The controller (a neural policy with ~1M parameters) is trained post hoc using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted negative log-likelihood that emphasizes misclassified cases. The base network is a Maxout CNN trained in the standard way. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over the base CNN and claim state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data, as well as empirical stability beyond the trained number of recurrent steps and some evidence that the learned gate vectors carry class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work: The paper is positioned at the intersection of deep CNNs, recurrent/feedback processing, and RL for attention. It cites biological evidence for top-down feedback, prior RL-based attention/saccade work, and Maxout/dropout. The key novelty versus earlier RL-for-attention papers is applying feedback modulation inside a strong, modern CNN and showing measurable gains on CIFAR-10/100, rather than toy datasets. The mechanism differs from glimpse-based attention: it modulates channel-wise feature gains across all layers rather than selecting spatial glimpses.

Strengths
- Bold idea: internal, closed-loop attention that modulates feature-map gains across multiple passes, learned with RL after supervised training. This is a distinct and promising direction.
- Practical integration with strong CNNs: keeps base weights fixed and augments with a policy; simple to bolt onto existing models.
- Empirical improvements on CIFAR-10/100, with claims of state-of-the-art for unaugmented data, and analysis of multi-step dynamics (peak near trained T, stable beyond).
- Sensible design choices for stability (softmax gating normalized to average 1, positivity constraint) and scalability (SNES with diagonal covariance).
- Initial interpretability attempts (visualizing gating changes; showing gate vectors alone carry discriminative signal).

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: only CIFAR-10/100; no evidence on larger-scale datasets or higher-resolution images.
- Incomplete baselines/ablations: missing comparisons to simpler alternatives such as:
  - Single-pass learned per-channel scaling trained by backprop (static gating).
  - Multi-pass inference without feedback (averaging T passes, MC dropout, or test-time augmentation) to control for computational budget.
  - Recurrent backprop-through-time training of the gates (since the gating is differentiable) or REINFORCE/policy-gradient baselines.
  - Gating only top layers vs all layers; effect of T; effect of observation components.
- Computational cost: T forward passes per image; the accuracy vs compute trade-off is not quantified or compared against equally costly ensembling.
- Policy learning details thin: no sensitivity to SNES hyperparameters, convergence behavior, or sample efficiency; unclear how robust SNES is in such high dimensions versus gradient-based methods.
- Some clarity gaps: exact numbers in Table 1 are not provided here; “unaugmented data” claim seems at odds with training augmentation; architecture sizes/hyperparameters and the policy network architecture are under-specified.

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level; the recurrence and gating are well-defined, and the training objective is clear. However, the experimental validation is not exhaustive and lacks key baselines/ablations to isolate where the gains originate (sequential feedback vs extra compute vs static gating). The stability analysis with varying T is a useful touch.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear algorithm description. Some inconsistencies/ambiguities remain (augmentation claims; missing table values; limited policy architecture details). Figures (not shown here) purportedly aid understanding.

Originality
- High. The idea of internal, channel-wise, recurrent attention controlled by an RL-trained policy on top of a modern CNN is novel and distinct from standard feedforward, glimpse-based, or purely recurrent CNN approaches. Using SNES to handle a million-parameter policy is also an unconventional but interesting choice.

Significance
- Moderate to high. If the reported improvements indeed reach SOTA on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), that’s notable. More importantly, the conceptual contribution—internal feedback to modulate internal representations over multiple passes—is compelling and likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., differentiable controllers, spatially selective actions, conditional computation).

Questions for the authors
- Can you report exact error rates and standard deviations for CIFAR-10/100, and clarify the “unaugmented data” claim given training-time augmentation?
- How does dasNet compare to: (a) static learned per-channel scales; (b) T-pass averaging without feedback; (c) REINFORCE/BPTT for the same gating architecture?
- What is the per-image inference overhead versus the baseline and versus an ensemble with a similar compute budget?
- How large is the policy network; what is θ’s structure beyond being a matrix? Any regularization besides L2? Any sparsity?
- Does gating only the top layer recover most of the gains?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the ablations/baselines above to disentangle the sources of improvement.
- Report compute vs accuracy trade-offs and compare to ensembles/MC dropout.
- Explore gradient-based training of the controller (BPTT) as a comparison; the gating is differentiable.
- Provide per-class confusion improvements and qualitative analyses on multiple examples.
- Consider spatially selective actions as future work (as you note), or groupwise/channel-attention with structured sparsity.

Recommendation: Accept
Despite some experimental shortcomings, the paper introduces a clear, novel, and bold idea—internal selective attention via feedback gating learned with RL—that advances the conversation on recurrent processing in deep vision systems and shows tangible gains on standard benchmarks. The conceptual contribution outweighs the missing ablations, and the work is likely to catalyze further research.
