This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple passes over a single input image, where on each pass, a learned policy can modulate the activations of entire filter maps. This allows the network to iteratively refine its internal representations and "re-think" its initial, feedforward classification, with the goal of correcting errors on difficult or ambiguous examples.

The proposed method operates in two stages. First, a strong baseline CNN (a Maxout network) is trained conventionally using supervised learning. Then, its weights are frozen. In the second stage, a control policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL) to output modulation weights (actions) for the CNN's feature maps. The state for the RL agent is derived from the activations within the CNN. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy parameters, the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) instead of traditional gradient-based RL methods. The reward function is designed to specifically focus on misclassified examples, effectively boosting performance on the model's weaknesses. This work connects well to a line of research inspired by the recurrent and top-down processing in the human visual cortex and previous, smaller-scale attempts to use RL for visual attention. The main contribution is demonstrating that such a recurrent, attention-based approach can be scaled to state-of-the-art CNNs on challenging benchmarks like CIFAR-10/100 and yield significant performance gains.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The central idea of adding an RL-trained feedback loop to modulate internal filter activations is novel and compelling. It provides a principled way to incorporate sequential "reasoning" into otherwise static feedforward networks. The motivation drawn from neuroscience and the limitations of purely feedforward visual processing is strong and provides a good conceptual grounding for the work.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement over a very strong baseline (Maxout networks). Achieving a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data at the time of writing, is a significant result. This shows the practical value of the proposed approach.

3.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The two-stage training paradigm is sensible. Training the policy on a frozen, pre-trained network isolates the learning problem and makes it more tractable. The choice of SNES as the optimization algorithm is well-justified, given the non-differentiable nature of the evolutionary search and the extremely high-dimensional parameter space of the policy.

4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide more than just a final accuracy number. The qualitative analysis of a corrected cat/dog misclassification (Figure 3) provides some insight into what the network is doing. The stability analysis (Figure 2), showing that the network doesn't diverge when run for more steps than it was trained for, is a good sanity check. The experiment demonstrating the information content of the final gate values is a clever way to validate that the policy is learning a meaningful, task-relevant strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Coarse State and Action Representation:** The state representation for the policy is formed by concatenating average activations of entire feature maps, which discards all spatial information. Similarly, the action is a single scalar multiplier for an entire feature map. While this proves effective, it is a rather coarse mechanism for attention. A more sophisticated approach might involve spatially-varying modulation, which could allow the network to focus on specific object parts.

2.  **Computational Expense:** The proposed method is computationally very demanding. First, a large CNN must be trained. Then, the SNES-based policy training requires evaluating a population of `p` policies on `n` images for `T` forward passes in each generation. The paper reports a training time of 4 days, which could be a significant barrier for adoption and further experimentation. The inference time is also increased by a factor of `T` (here, T=5).

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the overall algorithm is described well, some details are sparse. For instance, the exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters, kernel sizes) is not specified, which would make exact reproduction difficult. Details of the SNES update are also relegated to supplementary material. The policy network itself is a simple linear map, but this could be stated more explicitly early on.

4.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While these are good benchmark datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a higher-resolution and more complex dataset like ImageNet would make the contribution significantly stronger and show its scalability.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and conceptually elegant idea for incorporating recurrent, attentive processing into deep CNNs.
    *   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-10 without augmentation).
    *   The methodology is sound, and the choice of RL/ES for the problem is well-justified.
    *   The paper includes good analysis that provides evidence for the mechanism's effectiveness beyond a single accuracy score.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The computational cost is very high, potentially limiting its practical applicability.
    *   The state/action representation is simplistic and could be a major limitation.
    *   Reproducibility is hampered by a lack of some architectural details in the main paper.
    *   The experimental validation is limited to small-image datasets.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and significant idea. The combination of state-of-the-art deep learning with reinforcement learning to create an internal attention mechanism is original and likely to inspire future work. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method with strong results and thoughtful analysis. While there are weaknesses related to computational cost and the simplicity of the attention mechanism, these feel more like limitations and avenues for future research rather than fatal flaws. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the approach and the impressive empirical gains, outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
