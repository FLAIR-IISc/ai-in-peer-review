

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces dasNet, a novel approach to deep learning that incorporates reinforcement learning to modulate the attention of filters within a pre-trained Maxout network through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its classification of difficult examples by selectively emphasizing or suppressing specific filters across multiple passes. The authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize the attention policy, claiming a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/100.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between biological visual processing and machine learning. The concept of using feedback connections for internal selective attention is well-motivated by neuroscience literature. The related work section is thorough and well-cited, particularly regarding the role of feedback connections in biological vision.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Incorrect SNES Citation**: The paper claims to use "Separable NES (SNES; [19])" but references [19] as "High dimensions and heavy tails for natural evolution strategies," which is not about SNES. SNES was introduced in Wierstra et al. (2014), not referenced here. This fundamental error undermines the methodological credibility.

2. **Equation Errors**: Multiple typographical errors in equations:
   - Equation (1): "i=c∑ i=0" should be "∑_{i=0}^{c-1}"
   - Equation (3): "max i=0..b" should be "max_{i=0..b}"
   - Equation (5): "a ℓ j i=c∑ i=0" should be "a^ℓ_j ∑_{i=0}^{c-1}"

3. **Notation Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for weights (e.g., "aℓj" vs "a^ℓ_j"), and the description of the action vector (Equation 5) doesn't match its implementation in Algorithm 1. The action vector should be dimension c' (number of output maps), not c' per layer.

4. **Misunderstanding of Feedback Connections**: The paper claims to implement "feedback connections" but describes a gating mechanism that weights filter outputs rather than true feedback connections that would modulate lower layers. This misrepresentation of the mechanism is significant.

### Methodological Concerns
1. **Unjustified RL Approach**: The paper states SNES is used "due to the large number of parameters (over 1 million) required to control CNNs," but this is incorrect. Standard RL methods like policy gradients with function approximation can handle high-dimensional policies. The authors don't justify why RL is necessary when simple fine-tuning would be more straightforward.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparisons**: The paper claims "a relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN" but doesn't compare to other attention mechanisms (e.g., SENet, CBAM) or iterative refinement methods (e.g., iterative refinement networks). This omission makes it impossible to assess whether the approach is truly novel or just marginally better than existing techniques.

3. **Weak Evidence for Gate Information**: The claim that "the gates contain significant information" is supported by 15-nearest neighbor achieving 40.70% accuracy and logistic regression 45.74%, which is remarkably low for a meaningful feature representation. This undermines the paper's core claim.

4. **Incomplete Experimental Details**: The paper states "Training took of dasNet took around 4 days" (typo: "took of"), and claims "Figure 3 shows the classification..." but Figure 3 is not included in the provided text. The reader cannot verify the claimed results.

### Significance and Impact
1. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "a new state-of-the-art result" for CIFAR-10/100, but doesn't provide absolute accuracy numbers. A 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 (where state-of-the-art is ~95%) is modest compared to other recent work.

2. **Lack of Practical Impact**: The approach requires multiple passes through the network, which increases inference time by a factor of T (5 in their experiments). The paper doesn't address whether the marginal accuracy gain justifies the computational cost.

3. **No Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper shows one example of correct classification but doesn't analyze cases where dasNet fails, which would be valuable for understanding the method's limitations.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical concept, it suffers from multiple technical errors, methodological flaws, and significant inconsistencies that undermine its scientific contribution. The incorrect citation of SNES, fundamental misunderstandings of the mechanism, and lack of proper baselines make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The minor improvements claimed are not compelling enough to justify the complexity of the approach, especially given the methodological weaknesses.

The paper would benefit from major revisions: correcting all technical errors, properly comparing to relevant baselines (especially other attention mechanisms), clarifying the feedback mechanism, and providing complete experimental details. As it stands, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.
