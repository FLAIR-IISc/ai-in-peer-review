- Proposes dasNet: recurrent Maxout CNN with learned per-feature-map multiplicative gates applied over multiple passes to implement internal selective attention.  
- Policy observes summary statistics (avg feature maps, penultimate activations, class probs) and is trained post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES).  
- Base CNN is fixed; only the attention policy is learned after initial feedforward training.  
- Reports ~6% relative error reduction on CIFAR-10/100 versus a strong Maxout baseline and shows qualitative disambiguation examples.  
- Situates work within biological top-down modulation and prior RL/attention literature; novelty is internal, feature-wise recurrent attention on strong CNNs.  
- Gating mechanism is simple, differentiable, and stabilized by softmax-scaled positive gates with unit average gain.  
- Training objective uses boosted cross-entropy on the final pass only; SNES chosen for high-dimensional policy optimization.  
- Fixing the base CNN isolates attention effects but may prevent jointly optimal solutions.  
- Experiments lack exact absolute numbers, standard deviations, and multiple runs to assess variance and robustness.  
- Missing key baselines/ablations: small corrective classifier on penultimate features, fine-tuning the base network, and BPTT-based gradient training of the policy.  
- Additional missing ablations: per-layer gating, logits-only gating, effect of different observation components, and varying T values.  
- Need comparisons to test-time averaging, ensembling, and other compute-accuracy trade-offs to contextualize extra passes.  
- Reproducibility concerns: missing architecture widths, dims of observation/action, SNES hyperparameters, and termination criteria.  
- Paper is readable with a clear algorithm sketch but has minor equation typos and unclear claims about "state-of-the-art" and augmentation.  
- Originality: novel combination of per-channel recurrent multiplicative gating trained post hoc by NES on a strong CNN, distinct from saccade/glimpse attention work.  
- Significance: promising direction if gains are robust, but reliance on SNES raises sample-efficiency and scalability questions.  
- Pros: biologically motivated, modular per-channel gates, demonstrated improvements, stable recurrent dynamics, and informative gate vectors.  
- Cons: incomplete experimental reporting, questionable choice of SNES over BPTT, unclear compute/latency overhead, and narrow CIFAR-only evaluation.  
- Suggestions: report exact errors with stddevs, add ablations (T values, gating scopes, observation components), compare SNES vs BPTT, quantify test-time overhead, and release architecture/SNES details.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — interesting and promising but needs stronger empirical evidence and clearer comparisons to justify choices.
