This paper introduces dasNet, a fascinating approach to injecting dynamic, attention-like mechanisms into deep convolutional networks via reinforcement learning. The core idea – allowing a trained CNN to iteratively refine its processing through feedback connections guided by a learned policy – is genuinely novel and quite bold. It directly addresses a limitation of standard CNNs, their static nature, and draws inspiration from the more flexible processing observed in human vision. The use of SNES to handle the large policy parameter space is a pragmatic and clever choice, given the impracticality of many conventional RL methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the core concepts. The authors relate their work to relevant literature on visual attention, recurrent processing, and reinforcement learning, demonstrating a good understanding of the field. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 are promising, showing improved performance on challenging instances, and the visualization of filter activations provides some qualitative insight into the network’s attention mechanism. 

However, the work feels somewhat preliminary. While the performance improvement is encouraging, the gains aren’t massive and the analysis is limited. The choice of Maxout networks, while justified, might limit the generalizability of the findings. A more comprehensive evaluation on larger datasets (ImageNet, for example) would significantly strengthen the paper. The analysis of the learned attention policies is also superficial; while visualizations of activation changes are provided, a deeper understanding of *what* the network is attending to and *why* is lacking. The dynamics section is brief, and further exploration of how the number of iterations (T) affects performance is warranted. The connection to biological plausibility is asserted but not rigorously demonstrated. The related work section is thorough but could benefit from highlighting the challenges of applying RL to deep vision more explicitly.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of learning attentional feedback connections within a CNN is a significant departure from standard approaches.
*   **Boldness:** The authors tackle a challenging problem – adapting a pre-trained CNN post-hoc – with a relatively unexplored method (RL).
*   **Pragmatic Solution:** Using SNES is a sensible choice for optimizing a high-dimensional policy.
*   **Promising Results:** Demonstrates performance improvement on difficult classification instances.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Generalization:** Evaluation primarily on CIFAR-10/100 and Maxout networks.
*   **Superficial Analysis:** Lack of in-depth understanding of the learned attention policies.
*   **Modest Performance Gains:** The improvement, while statistically significant, is not dramatic.
*   **Preliminary Dynamics Analysis:** Limited exploration of the impact of the number of iterations (T).
*   **Biological Plausibility:** Asserted but not rigorously investigated.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and opens up a new direction in CNN research.
*   The use of RL to dynamically adapt CNN behavior is a significant departure from conventional approaches.
*   The paper demonstrates a proof-of-concept that this approach can improve performance on challenging instances.
*   The authors address a practical limitation of standard CNNs (their static nature).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The performance gains are modest, and the results might not generalize to other datasets or network architectures.
*   The analysis of the learned attention policies is limited and lacks depth.
*   The paper feels somewhat preliminary and could benefit from more extensive experimentation and analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards acceptance. While the paper has its weaknesses, the novelty and boldness of the idea are compelling. The authors have presented a convincing proof-of-concept that deserves further investigation. It feels like the beginning of something potentially significant. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work, but I believe that the paper, as it stands, represents a valuable contribution to the field. Specifically, expanding the experiments to ImageNet and performing a more thorough analysis of the learned attention policies would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focused on strengthening the analysis and discussing the limitations of the current approach.
