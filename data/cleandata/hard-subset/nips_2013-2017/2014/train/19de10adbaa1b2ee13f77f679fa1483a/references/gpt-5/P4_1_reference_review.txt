Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a feedback-augmented CNN that performs multiple passes over the same input and selectively reweights convolutional feature maps via multiplicative gates. A policy maps observations (layer-wise average activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to an action vector that scales each feature map’s activation before maxout pooling. The policy is deterministic, parameterized as a linear map followed by a scaled softmax, and is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a weighted negative log-likelihood focused on misclassified examples. At test time, the model runs for T steps (typically 5), updating gates based on observations to refine the prediction. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 starting from a trained Maxout CNN report improved accuracy (claiming a 6% relative error reduction over the baseline, for “unaugmented data”).

Relative to prior work, this approach falls within the broader class of recurrent/feedback models for vision, and attention mechanisms trained by RL. It differs from “glimpse” models and saccadic attention by modulating internal feature gains rather than selecting spatial glimpses; and from prior recurrent CNNs by learning a top-down control policy over internal activations. Using NES/SNES for high-dimensional policy search is consistent with prior black-box policy gradient literature, though most deep attention work typically uses differentiable end-to-end training. The paper situates itself within biologically motivated feedback literature and modern CNNs (Maxout, dropout), and claims to improve state-of-the-art single-model unaugmented performance on CIFAR-10/100.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple and generally applicable: gating per feature map is easy to implement on top of existing CNNs and yields a form of learned internal attention.
- Leverages sequential processing to improve difficult cases, aligning with evidence for feedback in biological vision.
- The observation design is practical (summary statistics across maps), enabling tractable policies with many outputs.
- Empirical improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, plus a stability analysis showing performance peaks near the trained T and remains stable for more steps.
- Some analysis of learned gates (e.g., gates alone contain class-relevant information).

Weaknesses:
- Baselines are incomplete. It is not shown whether similar gains can be achieved by:
  - Simply increasing depth/width or adding parameters to the baseline.
  - Learning the same gating policy end-to-end via (truncated) backpropagation through time, which is feasible since the objective is differentiable.
  - One-shot (T=1) learned gains conditioned on the initial forward pass (“adaptive rescaling”) vs iterative control.
- The choice of SNES over differentiable training is motivated by parameter count but not empirically justified; diagonal covariance SNES in million-parameter regimes can be sample-inefficient, and comparisons to standard policy gradient or supervised training are absent.
- Experimental reporting is sparse: concrete error rates, confidence intervals, and comparisons against contemporaneous single-model results (with/without augmentation) are not clearly presented here. It is unclear whether improvements hold against current best single models or committees.
- Design choices (observation components, per-map vs per-layer gating, T selection) are heuristic; ablations are minimal.
- Computational cost and latency: T passes at inference multiplies cost; the paper does not quantify the overhead or the trade-off in accuracy-per-FLOP.
- Risk of overfitting to misclassified cases via loss boosting is not analyzed (e.g., calibration, robustness).

Quality:
- The method is technically plausible and internally consistent. The gating mechanism, observation-action loop, and fitness computation are clearly defined.
- Claims of improved accuracy are plausible but under-supported without detailed numbers, error bars, and stronger baselines. The lack of end-to-end differentiable comparisons is a notable gap given the differentiability of the pipeline.
- No theoretical analysis of convergence or stability beyond empirical curves; however, this is acceptable for an empirical paper if experiments are thorough.

Clarity:
- The high-level idea and algorithm are reasonably clear, with equations and a pseudo-code overview. Some notation is terse and there are minor typographical issues.
- Important details are missing or under-specified: exact architecture sizes, dimensions of observations/actions, per-layer gate counts, training schedules, and a complete experimental table with absolute errors and standard deviations.
- The related work section is broad but could better differentiate this method from prior recurrent/gating work and attention trained via supervised learning.

Originality:
- The specific combination of per-map gain control learned as a sequential policy via SNES on top of a pre-trained Maxout CNN is novel. Prior work on attention/feedback exists, but this instantiation and its application to strong CNN baselines is a distinct contribution.
- The policy is simple (linear softmax), which is a modest design choice, but the overall closed-loop control over internal CNN features across multiple passes is a meaningful twist.

Significance:
- If the reported gains over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100 single-model, unaugmented settings hold against thorough baselines, the contribution is incremental but useful, showing that top-down internal attention can correct errors of feedforward CNNs.
- Practical impact depends on accuracy vs compute trade-offs and whether similar (or better) benefits can be obtained via simpler end-to-end training.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide full quantitative results with absolute error rates, standard deviations, and comparisons to contemporary single-model baselines (with/without augmentation).
- Add ablations: T values, observation subsets, per-layer vs per-map gating, one-step vs multi-step, policy capacity (linear vs MLP), and loss-boosting effect.
- Compare SNES to differentiable training (BPTT) and to simpler conditional rescaling learned with supervised loss.
- Report compute and latency overhead at inference, and accuracy vs T trade-off.
- Provide per-class confusion improvements and robustness analyses.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, general idea with simple mechanism for internal attention.
- Demonstrated empirical gains on standard benchmarks starting from a strong baseline.
- Biological plausibility and alignment with feedback literature.
- Stability of dynamics across steps.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete baselines and missing comparisons to end-to-end differentiable training or parameter-matched models.
- Sparse experimental reporting; unclear absolute performance and significance.
- Additional compute cost at inference not analyzed.
- Heuristic design choices without ablations.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation and baselines are insufficient for NIPS. Strengthening the experimental section as suggested could move this to accept.
