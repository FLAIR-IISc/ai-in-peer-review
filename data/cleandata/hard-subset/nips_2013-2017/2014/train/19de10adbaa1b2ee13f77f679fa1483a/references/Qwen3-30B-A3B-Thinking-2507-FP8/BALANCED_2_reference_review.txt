

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard CNNs by adding feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its internal filter weights through reinforcement learning. The key insight is that, like humans who may need multiple "passes" to correctly identify similar objects, CNNs can benefit from a process that allows them to modify their attention to different features across multiple passes over the same image. The authors propose learning an attention policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle the high-dimensional action space required to control CNNs.

The work relates to previous NIPS research on recurrent networks (e.g., "Recurrent Processing During Object Recognition" by O'Reilly), attention mechanisms (e.g., "Learning to Generate Artificial Fovea Trajectories" by Schmidhuber), and biological visual processing (e.g., Felleman and Essen's cortical hierarchy). While the paper acknowledges this body of work, it would benefit from a more detailed comparison to recent attention mechanisms in CNNs like "Non-local Neural Networks" (2018) or "Transformer" approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Concept**: The idea of allowing a CNN to "think" multiple times about an image by adjusting its internal attention is compelling and well-motivated by biological evidence of top-down processing in visual cortex.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional action space (over 1 million parameters) is a practical solution to a real problem, and the authors clearly justify why standard RL methods would be infeasible.

3. **Clear Experiments**: The experiments on CIFAR-10/100 demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on ambiguous classification cases. The visualization in Figure 3 provides valuable insight into how the attention mechanism alters feature activations across successive passes.

4. **Technical Soundness**: The framework is well-constructed and the training algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Modest Performance Gain**: The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is modest for a NIPS paper. Given that the authors note Maxout networks have only been "outperformed (by a small margin) by averaging committees of several convolutional neural networks," this small improvement doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper states that "Training took of dasNet took around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, excluding the original time used to train M." This high computational cost is not adequately justified against the small performance gain, especially since the approach requires multiple passes through the network at test time.

3. **Limited Comparison**: The paper doesn't compare against other attention mechanisms or recurrent approaches that have been shown to improve CNN performance on similar tasks (e.g., "Recurrent Models of Visual Attention" by Mnih et al.). The relationship to recent work on attention in CNNs is underdeveloped.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper presents average performance numbers without providing statistical significance testing or error bars, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the reported improvement.

5. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical argument for why this approach should work better than simply training a deeper network or using ensemble methods.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting idea that bridges biological visual processing with deep learning, and the implementation is technically sound. However, the performance gain is modest and the computational cost is relatively high, which limits the practical significance of the work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. A more thorough comparison to recent attention mechanisms and recurrent approaches in the literature
2. Statistical significance testing for the reported results
3. A clearer justification of the computational cost versus performance gain
4. A more detailed analysis of the conditions under which this approach provides the most benefit

While the concept is intriguing, the contribution doesn't sufficiently advance the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way to meet NIPS's high standards for significance. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison to recent methods and a clearer demonstration of the practical advantages of the approach.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper presents an interesting concept with a technically sound implementation, but the performance gain is modest and the computational cost is high without sufficient justification. The contribution doesn't advance the state-of-the-art significantly enough for NIPS. The authors should consider comparing their approach to more recent attention mechanisms, providing statistical significance testing, and exploring whether the approach can be made more computationally efficient before resubmitting.
