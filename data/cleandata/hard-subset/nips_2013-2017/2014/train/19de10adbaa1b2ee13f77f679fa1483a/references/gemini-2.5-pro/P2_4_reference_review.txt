**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard, pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to enable selective internal attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, a control policy can modulate the activations of the CNN's filters over several subsequent passes on the same image. This iterative refinement process is inspired by human visual cognition, where a quick initial perception is followed by more deliberate thought to resolve ambiguity. The control policy is trained using reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies, SNES) to maximize a reward signal based on classification accuracy, with a focus on correcting initially misclassified examples. The authors apply this method to a state-of-the-art Maxout network on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating improved performance and establishing a new state-of-the-art result at the time of publication.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-defined, and the experimental setup is thorough. The authors build upon a strong baseline (Maxout networks) and conduct experiments on standard, challenging benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100).

*   **Strengths:** The claims of improved performance are well-supported by the experimental results presented in Table 1, showing a clear, if modest, improvement over the vanilla CNN. The authors go beyond simply reporting top-line accuracy by including additional analyses to support their claims. The investigation into the system's dynamics (Figure 2), which shows stability even when run for more steps than it was trained for, is a valuable addition. Furthermore, the experiment demonstrating that the learned gate values contain significant class-relevant information (via k-NN and logistic regression classifiers) provides compelling evidence that the RL agent is learning a meaningful policy rather than a trivial one.
*   **Weaknesses:** The choice of SNES as the RL algorithm is justified by the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space. However, as a black-box, derivative-free optimization method, it offers less insight into the learning process than gradient-based RL methods (e.g., policy gradients). While the authors state that standard RL is "impractical," a more detailed discussion or empirical comparison to a simplified gradient-based approach would have strengthened this claim. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is interesting but, as the authors admit, "hard to qualitatively analyze." It demonstrates that the network is indeed changing its activations but provides limited insight into the *semantic* reason for these changes.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the overall process is well-illustrated in Figure 1 and described in Algorithm 1.

*   **Strengths:** The structure is logical, moving from the baseline model (Maxout) to the RL framework, and then to the proposed dasNet architecture. The core mechanism—modulating filter activations via learned gates `a`—is explained clearly in Equation 5.
*   **Weaknesses:** Some details could be specified more clearly. The exact composition and dimensionality of the observation vector `o_t` are described as a concatenation of three components, but its final structure is not fully detailed. Equation 7 defines the policy `π_θi(o) = dim(A)σ(θ_i o)` with a non-standard scaling of the softmax function; while the rationale (making the vector average to 1) is given, this is an unusual choice that could benefit from further justification. A reader unfamiliar with NES might find the description of the training process brief, though it is reasonable to defer details to supplementary material.

**Originality**

The paper presents a highly original contribution. While its components—CNNs, recurrent processing, and reinforcement learning—are not new, their synthesis is novel and creative.

*   **Strengths:** The core idea of retrofitting a pre-trained feedforward network with a separate, RL-trained attentional controller is a unique approach. Most contemporary work on attention either integrates it directly into the architecture to be trained end-to-end (e.g., spatial attention transformers) or uses recurrence in a fully integrated model. This paper’s approach of decoupling the initial supervised training from the subsequent RL-based refinement is a novel architectural and training paradigm. It effectively frames the problem of correcting "hard" examples as a sequential decision-making problem solved by a controller that manipulates the internal state of a powerful, pre-trained perception model.
*   **Comparison to prior work:** The authors do a good job of situating their work relative to neuroscience literature and prior computer vision research using RL. They correctly point out that previous RL-based attention models often operated on simpler problems, and a key contribution here is scaling this class of ideas to a state-of-the-art deep learning model to push performance boundaries.

**Significance**

The results and ideas presented in this paper are significant and likely to inspire follow-up work.

*   **Strengths:** The paper demonstrates that performance on challenging classification tasks can be improved by adding a deliberative, recurrent process on top of a fast, feedforward one. This provides a concrete, successful implementation of the "thinking for longer" concept. Achieving a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark validates the approach's practical utility. The general framework is appealing and could be extended in many ways: using more sophisticated policies, defining different action spaces (e.g., spatial modulation), or applying it to other domains beyond classification, such as segmentation or detection.
*   **Weaknesses:** The primary limitation on significance is the method's computational cost. The inference time is multiplied by the number of steps `T` (here, `T=5`), making it significantly slower than the baseline CNN. The RL training itself is also very expensive (4 days on a GPU). This trade-off between accuracy and computational cost may limit its practical deployment in resource-constrained environments. Furthermore, the experiments are limited to the relatively small CIFAR datasets. It is an open question how well the approach would scale to larger-resolution images and models (e.g., ImageNet), where the number of filters, and thus the dimensionality of the action and observation spaces, would be substantially larger.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and conceptually elegant architecture that combines deep CNNs with RL for internal selective attention.
2.  Achieves a state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (CIFAR-100), demonstrating empirical strength.
3.  The core idea is well-motivated by cognitive science and opens up a promising direction for future research on recurrent and deliberative processing in neural networks.
4.  The paper is well-written and includes strong supporting analyses that validate the learning process.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The computational cost of both training and inference is very high, potentially limiting practical impact.
2.  The scalability of the method to larger-scale problems like ImageNet is not explored and remains a major uncertainty.
3.  The choice of a black-box optimization algorithm (SNES) for training the policy makes the learning dynamics less transparent.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the concerns about computational cost and scalability, the novelty of the idea, the strength of the empirical results, and the significance of the research direction make this a strong paper. It introduces a new and effective way to think about incorporating deliberative, top-down control into deep vision models. The work is a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS. I would recommend acceptance.
