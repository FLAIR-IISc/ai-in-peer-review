**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward deep convolutional neural network with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable the network to perform multiple passes, or "thoughts," over a single input image, iteratively refining its internal representations to improve classification accuracy on difficult examples. After an initial supervised training phase of a baseline Maxout network, a control policy is learned using reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to modulate the activation of convolutional filter maps via multiplicative gates. This policy takes the network's internal state (activations and class probabilities) as input and decides how to re-weight different features for the subsequent pass. The authors demonstrate that this "internal selective attention" mechanism can correct initial misclassifications on ambiguous images from the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a new state-of-the-art result.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and the boldness of its core concept. It challenges the dominant paradigm of deep learning models as static, single-pass function approximators. By introducing a dynamic, input-dependent processing loop controlled by a learned policy, the authors open up an exciting new direction for research.

1.  **A Significant Conceptual Leap:** The idea of a network that can "re-think" its initial conclusion by selectively attending to its own internal features is a powerful and fresh perspective. The analogy to an expert ornithologist taking extra time to classify a difficult bird is compelling and provides strong motivation. This moves beyond simple attention mechanisms that re-weight input regions and instead learns to control the computational flow *within* the network's hierarchy. This is a genuinely new idea in the context of state-of-the-art deep networks.

2.  **Ambitious Synthesis of Techniques:** The paper successfully integrates three distinct machine learning paradigms: supervised learning for feature extraction (the base CNN), reinforcement learning for sequential decision-making (the control policy), and black-box optimization for training the policy (SNES). While the combination is complex, it is well-motivated by the problem structure. Using RL to learn an internal control policy for a million-parameter network is a high-risk, high-reward approach that pays off here.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The conceptual novelty is backed by solid empirical evidence. The dasNet architecture not only improves upon a very strong baseline (Maxout networks) but also achieves a new state-of-the-art result. The visualization in Figure 3, where the network corrects its initial "dog" classification to "cat" over several steps, provides a clear and intuitive demonstration of the proposed mechanism in action.

4.  **Opens Many Avenues for Future Work:** This work is likely to be highly influential. It raises numerous interesting questions about learned iterative computation, the nature of internal state representation for control, and the trade-offs between computation time and accuracy. It provides a foundation that many others in the community will be eager to build upon.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is brilliant, the execution has some aspects that could be refined in future work. However, in my view, these are minor points that do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

1.  **Computational Expense and Choice of RL Algorithm:** The policy is trained using SNES, a black-box optimization method. The authors justify this by the large parameter space, but it results in a very slow training process (4 days for the policy alone). This raises questions about scalability to larger models and datasets like ImageNet. It would be interesting to see a discussion or comparison with modern policy gradient methods, which might offer greater sample efficiency, though they would come with their own challenges.

2.  **Ad-hoc State Representation:** The observation vector `o_t` for the policy is constructed by concatenating averaged map activations, intermediate layer outputs, and class probabilities. While this proves sufficient, the choice of features feels somewhat heuristic. A more principled investigation into what constitutes an optimal representation of the network's "cognitive state" could further strengthen the approach.

3.  **Limited Interpretability of the Policy:** The paper rightly notes that "it is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect of the alterations." While the analysis showing that the final gate values contain class-relevant information is a good sanity check, the work stops short of providing a deeper understanding of the *strategies* the learned policy employs. Are there common patterns for resolving specific class confusions (e.g., cat vs. dog)? This is a very difficult problem but is a clear next step for understanding this type of model.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Arguments for acceptance:
*   **Highly Novel and Original:** The paper introduces a genuinely new and thought-provoking paradigm for deep learning models, moving from static feedforward processing to dynamic, iterative refinement.
*   **Significant Contribution:** It opens a new research direction in learning internal control policies for neural networks, which could have a major impact on the field.
*   **Strong Results:** The novel idea is validated with state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   **High Potential for Impact:** This is exactly the kind of ambitious, forward-looking work that NIPS should champion. It is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The training methodology (SNES) is computationally expensive and potentially not the most efficient.
*   The construction of the state/observation for the RL agent is somewhat ad-hoc.
*   The analysis of the learned policies is preliminary.

**Conclusion:**

This paper is an excellent submission. Its primary contribution lies in its bold and creative reconceptualization of how a deep network can perform classification. While the implementation has room for refinement—as is often the case with truly novel ideas—the conceptual leap is significant and well-supported by strong empirical results. The weaknesses are minor compared to the sheer novelty and potential impact of the work. This paper prioritizes a groundbreaking idea over methodological perfection, and in doing so, it pushes the boundaries of the field in a meaningful way. I strongly recommend acceptance.
