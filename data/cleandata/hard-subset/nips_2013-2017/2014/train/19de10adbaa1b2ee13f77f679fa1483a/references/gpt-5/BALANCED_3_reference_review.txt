Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with learned feedback that modulates the output maps of convolutional and maxout layers across multiple passes over the same input. A policy maps observations—comprising averaged feature map activations across layers, intermediate classification-layer activations, and the current class probability vector—to an action that scales each feature map (gating). The policy is trained post hoc (after supervised training of the base Maxout network) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the large dimensionality of the policy parameter vector. The intuition is to emulate a second “attentional” phase of processing (supported by cognitive neuroscience literature) that can correct initial feedforward mistakes by selectively enhancing/suppressing features. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show modest gains over a strong Maxout baseline; dynamics appear stable for steps beyond those used in training.

This work connects to prior efforts on recurrent processing and attention in vision: it differs from earlier RL-based selective attention and foveation approaches that act on sensor location, instead modulating internal CNN features. Compared to recirculation or feedback models (e.g., O’Reilly; Fukushima) and work on recurrence for recognition robustness, the contribution here is a practical instantiation on state-of-the-art Maxout CNNs with an explicit learned policy to control internal gates. It also relates to contemporary CNN regularization/architecture advances (dropout, maxout, NIN, stochastic pooling) and to multi-column ensembles, positioning dasNet as a complementary inference-time mechanism rather than a training-time regularizer.

Strengths
- Interesting idea: uses sequential inference with internal, learned attention to improve classification, without changing the base CNN weights.
- Practical mechanism: simple multiplicative gating per feature map keeps the approach differentiable and easy to integrate.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; analysis suggests the policy meaningfully reweights deep features for ambiguous cases.
- Clear motivation from vision neuroscience and related computational work; sensible observation design (aggregated activations + logits/probabilities).
- Stability experiment indicates the recurrent dynamics do not explode or collapse when unrolled further than trained.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Limited ablation and diagnostics. It is unclear which components matter most: per-layer gating vs. all layers, role of each observation component, number of steps T, scaling of the softmax-normalized action, effect of λboost, and the size/structure of the policy.
- RL framing vs. supervised/BPTT. The whole pipeline is differentiable; why not train end-to-end with backprop through time (or even REINFORCE with a differentiable surrogate) rather than a black-box optimizer? SNES sample inefficiency and scalability to larger models/datasets are concerns.
- Potential overfitting: the policy is evolved on training images with boosted weights on misclassified cases; no description of validation/early stopping/regularization beyond L2, and no cross-dataset evidence. The magnitude of test improvements is not fully contextualized (absolute vs. relative).
- Clarity/notation issues: Eq. (10) is confusing (d used both as correct class and as ||vT||∞); Algorithm 1 indexing is unclear (t = 0..T yet vT used for loss; a0=1 but is aT or aT+1 applied at final pass?). Some details of SNES updates are deferred to supplement; core hyperparameters of the policy mapping (dimensions, exact nonlinearity—only a softmax?) are under-specified.
- Computation/runtime: dasNet multiplies inference time by T and adds policy computation. No runtime or FLOPs comparison is provided, and no adaptive stopping criterion is explored.
- Claims vs. setup: The text states training used data augmentation, yet later claims “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.” Please clarify whether this refers to test-time augmentation or to using a single model vs. committees.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but sections 4 and 6 should be tightened. Explicitly define the observation dimension, action dimension, and mapping architecture (the policy appears to be a linear transform plus softmax; if so, please state it clearly).
- Figures/tables are referenced for key results, but numerical outcomes are not reported in the text; this limits assessment in isolation.
- The role of “boosting” weights (λcorrect ≪ λmisclassified) could be better motivated and analyzed (e.g., tradeoffs in precision/recall; effect on non-ambiguous cases).

Originality
- Modulating internal feature maps with learned feedback across time for classification is a fresh twist relative to most concurrent CNN work, which is predominantly feedforward. Using SNES for learning such a high-dimensional policy is unusual in this domain. However, the policy itself is simple (essentially a linear map with softmax), and the reinforcement learning setup is minimal (deterministic policy, only terminal reward).

Significance
- On small benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100), the gains appear incremental. The idea could be impactful if shown to scale to larger datasets or if the analysis demonstrates that the feedback mechanism tackles specific failure modes that standard architectural scaling or ensembling cannot. As presented, broader practical impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- Why not train the policy with backpropagation through time (or a hybrid: supervised loss at each step), given the differentiable gates? What prevented this?
- What is the exact architecture of π (linear vs. MLP)? What are dim(O) and dim(A)? Do you tie gates across layers or groups?
- How do you prevent overfitting during SNES training (validation split, early stopping, noise injection)?
- Please clarify the “unaugmented data” claim and provide absolute error rates for all methods in Table 1 in the main text.
- What is the runtime overhead at inference for T=5 relative to the baseline?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add thorough ablations: per-layer gating; policy capacity (linear vs. 1–2 hidden layers); T sensitivity; observation components; λboost; and per-class impact.
- Compare against stronger baselines: (i) fine-tuned/deeper Maxout; (ii) test-time augmentation; (iii) MC-dropout test-time averaging; (iv) small ensembles with equivalent compute.
- Report training/validation curves and provide a validation-based stopping rule. Include confidence intervals over multiple runs.
- Explore end-to-end differentiable training (BPTT) and/or policy gradient with a baseline; compare performance and efficiency to SNES.
- Provide inference-time compute and latency; explore adaptive halting (stop when confidence stabilizes).
- Improve clarity in notation (fix Eq. 10, algorithm indices) and reconcile augmentation claims.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper introduces a compelling idea—learned internal selective attention via feedback—and shows encouraging, albeit modest, gains on CIFAR. However, the experimental evaluation and clarity are not yet up to NeurIPS standards: key comparisons and ablations are missing, the optimization choice is not convincingly justified, and some claims lack precise support. With stronger empirical validation, clearer exposition, and broader comparisons, this work could make a solid contribution.
