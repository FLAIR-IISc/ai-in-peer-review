### **Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

#### **Summary**
This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard, pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to enable iterative, internal selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, a control policy can modulate the activations of the CNN's convolutional filters over subsequent passes on the same image. This policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL) to re-weight internal feature maps, with the goal of correcting initial misclassifications on difficult examples. Specifically, the authors apply this to a state-of-the-art Maxout network. Due to the high-dimensional state-action space, they opt for an evolution strategy (Separable NES) to learn the policy parameters. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves classification performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 for unaugmented data.

#### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by experimental results on standard, challenging benchmarks.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The experimental setup is robust. The authors start with a strong baseline (a Maxout network) and demonstrate a clear, statistically significant improvement (a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-100 error is not trivial).
    *   The authors provide good qualitative and quantitative analysis beyond just the final accuracy numbers. The visualization of filter modulation for a specific cat/dog misclassification (Figure 3) provides valuable intuition about the mechanism. The analysis of the dynamics for different numbers of steps (T) and the information content of the learned gates further strengthen the paper's claims.
    *   The choice of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) is well-justified. The authors correctly identify that traditional RL methods would struggle with the million-plus parameters of the policy, and SNES provides a practical, if computationally expensive, solution.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The computational cost is a significant drawback. Training the dasNet policy took 4 days on a GPU, *in addition* to the time required to train the base Maxout network. This makes the method difficult to apply and scale, and raises questions about its practical utility compared to, for example, training a larger feedforward model or an ensemble for a similar computational budget.
    *   The reward function uses a simple form of boosting (`λboost`) to focus on difficult samples. While effective, it feels somewhat heuristic. A more principled exploration of the reward structure could be beneficial.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized, making a complex idea relatively easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The motivation, drawing an analogy to human experts taking a "second look" at a difficult problem, is compelling and clearly articulated.
    *   Algorithm 1, combined with the description in Section 4, provides a clear overview of the training process. The flow of information—from network state to observation vector `o`, to policy `π`, to action `a`—is logical.
    *   The figures, particularly the schematic in Figure 1 and the visualization in Figure 3, are effective aids to understanding.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Some implementation details could be clearer. For instance, the exact composition and dimensionality of the observation vector `o` are described but not quantified, which would help in appreciating the scale of the policy learning problem. The policy `π` is described as a softmax over a linear transformation, which is simple, but its exact implementation details are brief.
    *   While understandable for space reasons, relegating the details of the SNES update to supplementary material makes it harder to assess the RL component fully from the main text.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original and presents a novel combination of ideas.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The core concept of an *internal* attention mechanism that modulates feature maps over multiple passes, trained via RL, is a significant departure from standard feedforward or even most recurrent/attentional models. While attention in vision often focuses on spatial regions of the input image (e.g., [41]), this work proposes a form of *feature-based* attention deep within the network's hidden layers.
    *   The framing of this iterative refinement as a series of internal "thoughts" is a powerful and fresh perspective.
    *   The application of evolution strategies to train such a high-dimensional control policy for a deep network is also novel and tackles a key technical challenge in a creative way. The work successfully bridges concepts from neuroscience, deep learning, and reinforcement learning.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The individual components (Maxout nets, RL, NES) are not new. The originality lies entirely in their synthesis and application, but this synthesis is non-trivial and leads to a genuinely new type of model.

**Significance:**
The paper has the potential to be highly significant and influential.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   It presents a compelling proof-of-concept for a new class of deep learning architectures that can perform sequential reasoning or iterative refinement on a static input. This moves beyond the purely reactive nature of feedforward networks.
    *   The strong results on CIFAR-100 demonstrate that this is not just a conceptual novelty but a practical approach that can advance the state of the art.
    *   The work could inspire numerous follow-up studies exploring different policy architectures, more efficient (e.g., gradient-based) training methods for such policies, and applications to other domains where iterative refinement is crucial (e.g., medical diagnosis, planning).

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The primary question limiting its immediate impact is scalability. It is unclear if the SNES-based training approach would be feasible for much larger models and datasets like ImageNet. If the computational cost proves prohibitive for larger-scale problems, its significance might be confined to a more niche research direction.

#### **Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **High Originality:** The central idea of training an RL agent to control the internal feature computations of a CNN over multiple "glances" is novel and conceptually exciting.
2.  **Strong Results:** The paper demonstrates a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-100), proving the efficacy of the proposed method.
3.  **High Potential for Impact:** The work opens up a new avenue of research into more deliberative and dynamic neural architectures, moving beyond the standard single-pass feedforward paradigm.
4.  **Good Analysis:** The authors provide solid analysis to support their claims, including visualizations and experiments on the system's dynamics.

#### **Arguments for Rejection**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The training time is very high, which may limit the practicality and further exploration of this method by the broader community.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** It is an open question whether the approach can scale to larger-scale problems like ImageNet, where the policy's parameter space would be even larger.

#### **Recommendation**
Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its striking originality and strong empirical results, far outweigh its weaknesses. The work introduces a fascinating and potentially powerful new direction for deep learning research. While the computational cost is a valid concern, it does not diminish the novelty and significance of the contribution. This paper is a clear candidate for acceptance and is likely to generate considerable interest and follow-up work at NIPS.
