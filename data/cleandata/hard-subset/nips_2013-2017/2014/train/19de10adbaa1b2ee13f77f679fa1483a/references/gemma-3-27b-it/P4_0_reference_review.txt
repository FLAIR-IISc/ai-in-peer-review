This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning to selectively attend to features during iterative processing of an image. The core idea is to allow the network to refine its classifications by modulating filter activations on successive passes, addressing potentially ambiguous cases where initial feedforward processing is insufficient. The policy governing these activations is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the action space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate improved performance on difficult instances compared to a baseline Maxout network.

The paper builds on a rich history of work in CNNs (LeCun, 1989; Ciresan et al., 2012; Krizhevsky et al., 2012), attention mechanisms (Itti, 2007; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018).  It draws inspiration from the hierarchical processing and feedback connections observed in the human visual cortex (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Douglas, 1997; Gilbert & Sigman, 1999; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). The use of evolution strategies for policy learning (Wierstra, 2006; Schaul et al., 2015) is a pragmatic approach to handle the large parameter space.  Related work in recurrent processing for image manipulation and reconstruction (Behnke, 2011; Larochelle & Hinton, 2010) provides context, but this work specifically targets improving classification accuracy on challenging examples.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of iterative refinement via feedback connections within a deep CNN, controlled by a learned attentional policy using reinforcement learning, is a novel approach. The use of SNES to manage the large action space is a reasonable and practical choice.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture and training algorithm are clearly described. The use of Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) provides a strong baseline. The experimental setup with CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 is standard and appropriate.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper demonstrates performance improvements on challenging classification instances, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The visualization of filter activations and changes during iterative processing provides some insight into the network's behavior.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The motivation for allowing CNNs to "think" more like humans (Welinder et al., 2010) in difficult classification scenarios is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While the overall idea is clear, some sections (e.g., the description of SNES) could benefit from more detailed explanation. The notation is dense and requires careful attention. The connection between the specific observed changes in filter activations (Fig 3) and the overall improvement in classification isn’t made convincingly.
*   **Significance:** The improvement in performance (6% relative) while notable, isn't a dramatic leap forward.  The authors acknowledge this by stating it establishes a “new state-of-the-art,” but the practical impact of this improvement needs further discussion. A comparison with more recent, state-of-the-art architectures beyond the baseline Maxout network would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a thorough ablation study to analyze the contribution of different components of the dasNet architecture. For example, what is the impact of the number of iterations (T) or the specific features used in the observation vector?
*   **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet requires significant computational resources (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU). This is not explicitly discussed as a limitation, but it could be a barrier to adoption.
*   **Limited Generalization Analysis:** The experiments focus on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. It’s unclear how well the approach would generalize to more complex datasets or different image domains.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through iterative refinement and learned attention.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a statistically significant improvement in classification accuracy on challenging instances.
*   The work is well-motivated and draws inspiration from both neuroscience and machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The performance improvement, while positive, is not substantial enough to represent a major breakthrough.
*   The lack of ablation studies and generalization analysis limits the understanding of the approach's strengths and weaknesses.
*   The computational cost of training is a potential barrier to adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel and well-executed idea. While the performance improvement is not dramatic, the approach is promising and represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified above could be addressed through further experimentation and analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on improving clarity, conducting ablation studies, and providing a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential impact of the work.
