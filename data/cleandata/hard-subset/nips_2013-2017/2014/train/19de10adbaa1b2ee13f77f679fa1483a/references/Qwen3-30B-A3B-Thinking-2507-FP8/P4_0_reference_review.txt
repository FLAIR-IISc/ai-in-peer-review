

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment standard feedforward CNNs with selective attention mechanisms through feedback connections. dasNet allows each layer to influence all other layers on successive passes over an image, using reinforcement learning to determine how to emphasize or suppress features. The core innovation lies in learning an attention policy via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to correct misclassifications by iteratively modifying filter weights during inference, rather than solely relying on the initial feedforward pass. The approach builds upon Maxout networks [7] and extends previous work on feedback connections in visual cortex [28-30] and reinforcement learning for attention [40-45]. While prior work has explored recurrent processing in vision [48-50] and reinforcement learning for attention [40-45], dasNet distinguishes itself by applying this framework directly to state-of-the-art CNNs to improve performance on challenging classification tasks, rather than using simplified datasets or focusing solely on demonstration purposes.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with appropriate methodology. The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space (over 1 million weights) is well-justified and addresses a key computational challenge. The experimental design on CIFAR-10/100 is well-structured, with appropriate baselines (vanilla Maxout networks) and ablation studies examining dynamics (Figure 2) and information content of gates (Section 6). The 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs represents a meaningful advancement on these challenging benchmarks. The paper carefully addresses both strengths (improved performance on difficult cases) and limitations (computational cost of training dasNet, limited qualitative analysis of filter changes). However, the SNES implementation details are somewhat sparse, and the paper would benefit from more precise descriptions of hyperparameters and computational resources required for reproduction.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, though some equations (e.g., Equation 5) could be more thoroughly explained. Algorithm 1 provides a clear step-by-step description of the dasNet training process. The visualizations in Figure 3 effectively demonstrate the attention mechanism at work, showing how filter weights change across passes to correct misclassifications. The paper adequately explains the relationship between the reinforcement learning framework and the CNN architecture. The writing is accessible to a general machine learning audience with appropriate background.

## Originality

The paper makes a novel contribution by combining feedback connections with reinforcement learning to create an internal selective attention mechanism within deep CNNs. While there is related work on recurrent processing in CNNs [48-50] and reinforcement learning for attention [40-45], dasNet specifically addresses the problem of correcting misclassifications through iterative refinement of filter weights during inference, rather than solely improving training or using simplified datasets. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly in Section 5, where it discusses how previous approaches often focus on demonstration rather than state-of-the-art performance improvements. The approach of learning attention policies after standard CNN training, rather than during training, represents a novel perspective on how to incorporate attention mechanisms.

## Significance

The significance of this work is demonstrated by the 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. The paper successfully shows that this approach can correct misclassifications without disrupting previously learned knowledge, which addresses a key challenge in incremental learning. The computational feasibility of the approach (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) makes it practical for real-world applications. The method has potential for broader application to other vision tasks where difficult classification cases arise. However, the paper could strengthen its significance claim by discussing potential applications beyond classification, such as in object detection or segmentation.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to internal selective attention in deep CNNs
- Demonstrated significant performance improvement (6% relative) on challenging benchmarks
- Well-designed experiments with appropriate ablation studies
- Clear explanation of the reinforcement learning component
- Practical implementation with reasonable computational requirements

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient differentiation from related work on recurrent processing in CNNs (e.g., Schmidhuber's Neural Abstraction Pyramid)
- Missing implementation details for SNES that would aid reproducibility
- Limited discussion of why iterative refinement is better than simply training a larger network
- Superficial qualitative analysis of filter changes (acknowledged as an open problem)

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning (improving difficult classification cases)
- Demonstrates significant performance gains on standard benchmarks
- Provides a technically sound and novel approach to internal attention
- Well-motivated by human vision research
- Clear methodology with appropriate experimental validation

**Con:**
- Limited differentiation from existing recurrent processing approaches
- Some implementation details missing for reproducibility
- Limited discussion of broader applicability beyond classification
- The improvement, while significant, may not be transformative for the field

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to incorporating selective attention mechanisms into deep CNNs through feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning. The methodology is well-justified, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over state-of-the-art CNNs on challenging benchmarks. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on attention mechanisms in deep learning, with significant practical implications. While there are some limitations in differentiating from related work and providing complete implementation details, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has potential for broader application. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
