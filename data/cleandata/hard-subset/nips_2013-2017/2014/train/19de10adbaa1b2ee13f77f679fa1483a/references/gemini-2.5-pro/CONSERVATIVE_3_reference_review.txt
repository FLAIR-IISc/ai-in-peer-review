**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for improving the performance of a pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) on difficult classification tasks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (a Maxout network in this case) with feedback connections that modulate the activity of its convolutional filters. This modulation is controlled by a policy, which is trained using reinforcement learning (RL) after the base CNN has been trained via supervised learning. For a given input image, the system performs multiple forward passes. After each pass, an observation of the network's internal state is fed to the policy, which then outputs an action that scales the filter activations for the next pass. The authors frame this as a form of selective attention, allowing the network to iteratively "re-think" its initial classification. The policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this approach can improve classification accuracy over the baseline Maxout network, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Motivation:** The paper addresses an important and interesting problem: how to improve deep network performance on ambiguous or difficult examples that are not correctly handled by a single feedforward pass. The analogy to human experts taking more time to analyze a difficult case is a compelling motivation.

2.  **Solid Foundation:** The work builds upon a strong and established baseline, the Maxout network, which was a state-of-the-art architecture at the time. Attempting to improve upon such a model is a worthwhile endeavor.

3.  **Reported Performance:** The method demonstrates a clear performance improvement on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, reporting a 6% relative improvement and a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data on CIFAR-10. This is a significant result.

**Weaknesses**

While the high-level idea is intriguing, I have significant concerns regarding the methodological choices and the experimental validation, which prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

1.  **Quality and Methodological Soundness:** My primary concern lies with the choice of SNES for training the policy. The authors justify this by citing the large number of parameters in the policy network (over 1 million), claiming standard RL methods are impractical. This justification is insufficient.
    *   Policy gradient methods (e.g., REINFORCE, Actor-Critic variants) are the standard approach for training parameterized policies in high-dimensional spaces and have been successfully applied to problems with large parameter counts. The paper dismisses these methods without providing any empirical evidence of their failure or impracticality in this context.
    *   Using SNES, a derivative-free, black-box optimization algorithm, seems highly inefficient. The entire system (base CNN and policy) is treated as an opaque function. Given that the underlying CNN is fully differentiable, it seems likely that a more sample-efficient learning algorithm could be devised by leveraging gradient information through the network, rather than relying on a brute-force evolutionary search. This choice feels more like an engineering convenience than a principled methodological decision, casting doubt on the soundness of the approach.

2.  **Originality:** The idea of iterative refinement or feedback in neural networks is not new. The contribution here is the specific formulation of combining a pre-trained CNN with an external RL controller. However, the implementation feels like a straightforward combination of existing components (Maxout nets, RL, SNES) without a deep, novel insight into the learning dynamics. The "feedback" is not a true recurrent connection integrated into the network's architecture, but rather an external loop, which feels less integrated than prior work on recurrent processing.

3.  **Significance and Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully support the claims of advancing the state of the art.
    *   **Insufficient Baselines:** The only baseline presented is the original Maxout network. A crucial comparison is missing: ensembling. The iterative process of dasNet increases computational cost at test time by a factor of T. A fair comparison would be against an ensemble of T different Maxout networks, which would have a similar computational budget. It is well-known that ensembling provides significant boosts in performance, and it is entirely possible that a simple ensemble could match or exceed the performance of dasNet. Without this baseline, the significance of the reported improvement is unclear.
    *   **Limited Scope of Datasets:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While these are standard benchmarks, they are relatively small in scale. For a method that claims to improve a state-of-the-art model, evaluation on a more challenging, large-scale dataset like ImageNet is necessary to demonstrate the generalizability and scalability of the approach.
    *   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The design of the observation vector for the policy is ad-hoc, constructed by concatenating various internal activations. The authors state that these "should still be meaningful enough," which is not a rigorous justification. There are no ablation studies to determine which components of this observation vector are necessary or what the impact of its design is on final performance. Similarly, key hyperparameters like the number of steps `T` are simply stated as "experimentally determined" without further analysis of this choice's sensitivity.

4.  **Clarity:** The analysis of what the network learns is superficial. Figure 3 shows changes in filter activations, but the authors admit that "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect of the alterations." While this honesty is appreciated, it underscores a lack of understanding of the learned mechanism. A stronger contribution would provide deeper insight into the attention strategy learned by the policy.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting idea for improving CNNs through iterative refinement controlled by an RL agent. However, the work is undermined by a questionable choice of optimization algorithm (SNES) over more standard and principled methods, and the experimental evaluation lacks the necessary baselines (e.g., ensembling) and scale (e.g., ImageNet) to convincingly demonstrate a significant advance. The contribution feels more like a proof-of-concept than a robust, well-validated method. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time.
