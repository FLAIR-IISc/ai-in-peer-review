Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward maxout CNN with recurrent, learned “feedback” that modulates per-channel activations across layers over multiple passes on the same image. At each time step t, the controller observes summary statistics of the network state (mean feature map activations per layer, pre-softmax activations, and current class probabilities) and outputs an action vector that multiplicatively gates the output maps in each layer for the next pass. The controller is a linear mapping with a scaled softmax nonlinearity to keep gate means near 1, making the CNN behavior non-stationary across passes. Because the action/observation spaces are high-dimensional, the controller is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a loss that boosts misclassified examples and uses only the final pass’ log-loss. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong maxout CNN baseline; qualitative visualizations suggest that the policy enhances and suppresses particular feature maps to disambiguate confusing classes. Some analysis of stability across different numbers of passes T is provided, and the authors argue that terminal gate values carry class-relevant information.

This work is situated at the intersection of deep CNNs and attention/feedback in vision. It relates to prior NIPS work on recurrent processing and top-down modulation in recognition, reinforcement learning for selective attention and saccades, and black-box evolutionary strategies (NES). Compared to RL-based glimpse models and spatial attention, dasNet performs internal, channel-wise gain modulation. The use of SNES to train a high-dimensional controller is also grounded in previous evolutionary optimization work.

Strengths:
- Interesting and biologically motivated idea: internal, selective attention via top-down, per-channel gain modulation applied recurrently.
- Simple, general mechanism that can be retrofitted to existing CNNs without architectural redesign.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong maxout baseline, apparently correcting some ambiguous cases.
- Some analysis of dynamics (performance versus number of passes) and diagnostic visualizations of gating patterns.
- Pragmatic use of SNES to handle large controller parameterizations when standard RL is impractical.

Weaknesses:
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient to isolate where the gains come from. Missing comparisons include:
  - Parameter-count–matched feedforward baselines (e.g., a slightly larger maxout net).
  - Multi-pass test-time ensembling without feedback (e.g., repeated forward passes with dropout or stochastic pooling and averaging).
  - Fixed per-channel scalings learned by backprop (no recurrent policy).
  - A small differentiable controller trained end-to-end by backprop through time (BPTT), which seems feasible given the differentiability of the gates.
  - Gating only higher layers versus all layers.
- RL/optimization formulation: the policy is deterministic, trained by black-box optimization on the terminal loss, with no per-step rewards or credit assignment. This looks more like direct black-box minimization of a differentiable objective than reinforcement learning; the choice of SNES over gradient-based BPTT is not well justified empirically.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues:
  - The paper states the base model used data augmentation, then later claims “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data,” which is contradictory. Exact data augmentation protocols and what “unaugmented” refers to should be clarified.
  - Table 1 (key results) is referenced but not fully included here; precise numbers, standard deviations, and test-time costs are missing.
  - Important architectural and training details are underspecified (exact layer widths, number of maps, observation dimensionality, batch size n for SNES, early stopping/validation protocol).
  - Some notation typos and indexing inconsistencies (e.g., summation indices, action update).
- Statistical rigor: no error bars, multiple runs, or significance testing; stability across seeds is unclear.
- Compute cost: test-time inference is T times more expensive; the trade-off between accuracy and latency is not quantified.
- The softmax-normalized gates constrain the sum (and positivity), coupling channels and potentially limiting expressiveness; the design choice is not compared to alternatives.

Quality:
Technically sound at a high level, but the learning setup underutilizes available gradients and lacks strong baselines/ablations to substantiate the specific claim that recurrent feedback per se drives the gains. The experimental section would benefit from more rigorous methodology and clearer reporting.

Clarity:
Generally well written and motivated, with helpful figures. However, several key experimental details and comparisons are missing or ambiguous, and some equations/notation could be cleaned up. Reproducibility would be challenging from the current description.

Originality:
Moderately novel combination: channel-wise recurrent gain modulation inside CNNs plus an ES-trained controller. Related ideas exist in feedback/attention and RL-based attention, but the particular instantiation and scale are distinctive.

Significance:
If the claimed improvements over strong baselines hold under careful controls, the idea could influence work on feedback and internal attention in CNNs. As presented, the impact is limited by missing ablations and ambiguity about the sources of improvement and practical trade-offs.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide full, apples-to-apples comparisons with:
  - A parameter-matched larger maxout CNN.
  - Multi-pass averaging without feedback (e.g., repeated stochastic forward passes).
  - Fixed per-channel gates learned by standard backprop on the training set.
  - A small differentiable controller (e.g., 1–2 hidden layers) trained with BPTT through T steps. This is a crucial baseline, given the differentiability of the gating.
  - Ablations gating only the last conv layer vs. all layers; random controller; linear vs. nonlinear controller.
- Report exact CIFAR-10/100 accuracies/errors with standard deviations across multiple runs and clarify augmentation protocols. Resolve the “unaugmented” claim inconsistency.
- Quantify compute/latency overhead at test time and provide accuracy vs. T trade-offs.
- Use a held-out validation set for controller training and describe early stopping; report sensitivity to SNES hyperparameters and seeds.
- Analyze where improvements occur (per-class confusion matrices, especially for known ambiguous pairs) and how often predictions flip from wrong-to-right across passes.
- Revisit gate parameterization: consider independent sigmoids/tanh with learned biases (allowing uncoupled attenuation/amplification), and compare to the current scaled softmax.
- Release code or provide sufficient architectural and training details (filter sizes, number of maps, observation dimensionality, SNES batch size) for reproduction.

Recommendation:
Weak reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation does not meet the NeurIPS bar for rigor and clarity. Strengthening the baselines, ablations, and reporting could make a compelling case.
