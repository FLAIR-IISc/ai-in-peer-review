Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension to convolutional Maxout networks that introduces internal selective attention via feedback connections. After a standard feedforward pass, the network performs multiple additional passes over the same image. At each pass, a learned policy outputs per-feature-map multiplicative gates (actions) to reweight filter activations throughout the CNN, thereby emphasizing or de-emphasizing features to correct initial mistakes. The policy observes summary statistics of the current network state (averaged feature map activations, penultimate activations, and class probabilities) and is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), an evolution strategies variant suitable for high-dimensional policies. The base CNN is fixed; only the policy is learned post hoc. On CIFAR-10/100, the method reportedly improves over a strong Maxout baseline (about 6% relative error reduction), with qualitative examples showing disambiguation (e.g., cat vs. dog). The paper situates the work within biological vision (top-down recurrent modulation) and prior RL/attention literature.

Relation to prior work
- CNNs with Maxout and dropout are standard [Goodfellow et al.]. The novelty is not the base CNN but the recurrent attention.
- Prior attention works in vision learned saccades or foveation and used RL, often on simpler data. Here, attention is internal, feature-wise, and applied to state-of-the-art CNNs.
- The recurrent/top-down idea is consistent with neurophysiology (e.g., Lamme & Roelfsema) and with iterative processing approaches (e.g., denoising, reconstruction).
- The use of NES/SNES for large-scale policy optimization has precedents, though less common in deep vision; most differentiable recurrent attention models use gradient-based training.

Quality
- Method: The gating mechanism is simple, differentiable, and well-integrated with Maxout CNNs. Using softmax-scaled gates ensures positivity and average unit gain, which stabilizes dynamics.
- Training: The policy is evolved with SNES using a boosted cross-entropy objective on the final pass only. The design choice to fix the base CNN isolates the effect of attention but may limit optimality.
- Experiments: CIFAR-10/100 results show improvements over a strong baseline; however, the paper lacks exact absolute numbers, standard deviations, or multiple runs to assess variance. There is a qualitative example and a small analysis of dynamics (stability beyond T) and gate informativeness.
- Missing ablations/baselines: 
  - Compare to training a small corrective classifier on the penultimate features (stacked generalization), or to fine-tuning the base network further on hard examples.
  - Compare to gradient-based training of the policy via backpropagation through time (BPTT). Since all components are differentiable, a supervised recurrent training objective seems feasible and likely more sample-efficient than SNES.
  - Simpler feedback: adjusting only logits or only top-layer feature maps; per-layer ablations; effect of observation components.
  - Compare to additional test-time averaging (e.g., multi-crop) or ensembling, to contextualize compute-accuracy trade-offs.
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing (exact architecture widths, number of maps per layer, precise dim(O)/dim(A), SNES hyperparameters like learning rates, n, p, termination criterion). Reported training time is 4 days on a single GPU for the policy (excluding base CNN), but compute overhead at test time (factor T) should be quantified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with a clear algorithm sketch. Some equations omit biases and have minor typos. The statement about “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” is unclear given that training uses data augmentation; please clarify whether this refers to no test-time augmentation or no model committees.
- Provide a concise architectural table and a quantitative results table with absolute error rates and comparisons to contemporary baselines.

Originality
- The combination of internal, per-feature-map, multiplicative, top-down gating trained post hoc by black-box NES on a strong CNN is novel relative to prior RL attention works that focused on saccades/glimpses or lower-capacity models.
- The architectural idea is related to biologically inspired top-down modulation but instantiated in a scalable, practical CNN. While channel-wise reweighting is a familiar motif, its recurrent, policy-driven use across multiple passes is a distinctive contribution for the time.

Significance
- Improving strong CNNs by adding learned feedback is an important direction. If the reported CIFAR gains are robust, the contribution is practically relevant and could inspire further work on recurrent internal attention.
- The reliance on SNES raises questions about scalability and sample efficiency; demonstrating that gradient-based training is inferior (or not straightforward) would strengthen significance.
- Broader impact would be clearer with more thorough evaluation (full tables, ablations, compute-vs-accuracy analysis, per-class confusion improvements).

Pros
- Clear, biologically motivated idea; simple and modular mechanism (per-channel gates).
- Demonstrated improvements over a strong baseline on CIFAR-10/100; qualitative evidence of correcting initial mistakes.
- Stability analysis suggests the recurrent dynamics are well behaved.
- Policy uses meaningful observations from intermediate activations; gate vectors carry class-relevant information.

Cons
- Incomplete experimental reporting: missing exact numbers, variances, and comprehensive ablations/baselines.
- Choice of SNES over straightforward BPTT is insufficiently justified given full differentiability; may be computationally wasteful.
- Limited analysis of compute overhead and latency introduced by multiple passes.
- Scope of evaluation is narrow (only CIFAR), and comparisons to contemporary strong single-model baselines are unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report exact test errors on CIFAR-10/100 with standard deviations over multiple seeds; include comparisons to Maxout, Network-in-Network, and single-model baselines under identical data augmentation and test-time protocols.
- Add ablations: T ∈ {1,2,3,5}, gating only top/each layer, removing components of the observation vector, and a “logit-only” gate baseline.
- Compare SNES-based training to gradient-based BPTT for the policy (same architecture) in terms of accuracy and training time.
- Quantify test-time overhead and show accuracy-vs-compute trade-offs relative to ensembling or test-time augmentation.
- Clarify architecture details, SNES settings, selection of n and p, and early stopping to guard against overfitting; consider releasing code or sufficient pseudo-code for reproduction.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is interesting and plausibly impactful, with promising results, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence and clearer comparisons to justify the design choices and quantify the benefits.
