Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension of convolutional networks that implements internal, selective attention via feedback. A standard Maxout CNN is augmented with per-feature-map gates aℓj that multiplicatively modulate the activations on successive passes over the same image. A policy π maps observations (concatenated averages of feature-map activations across layers, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities) to the next action vector (gates) using a scaled softmax to keep the average gate near 1. The policy is learned post hoc (after supervised training of the base CNN) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for computational tractability in the high-dimensional policy parameter space. Training emphasizes misclassified samples via a boosted cross-entropy-like loss. On CIFAR-10/100, the method yields improvements over the base Maxout model and claims a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data (single model). The authors also show that the learned dynamics are stable beyond the training horizon and that the final gate values contain information predictive of the class.

Relation to prior work
The paper is motivated by biological evidence for top-down, recurrent processing in visual cortex and prior computational work on attention, recurrent CNNs, and RL-based selective perception. It builds on Maxout networks and dropout and relates to earlier RL-based attention/saccade models and feedback in neural networks. The key novelty is an internal attention mechanism that directly modulates intermediate CNN feature maps across multiple passes, with a learned policy trained via black-box optimization at scale.

Quality
- Soundness: The architectural modification (per-map gates applied before maxout pooling) is straightforward and plausibly stable due to positive, normalized gates. The training procedure with SNES is standard for black-box policy search, and the boosted loss focuses on hard examples. The stability experiment (training with small T but evaluating for more steps) is a useful sanity check.
- Empirical support: The paper reports improvements on CIFAR-10/100 and a qualitative example where “dog” is corrected to “cat.” However, the experimental reporting lacks important details: exact error rates, standard deviations over multiple seeds, fairness of comparisons (augmented vs unaugmented), and the magnitude of gains. The “6% relative” improvement is not contextualized (absolute numbers matter). The claim of “unaugmented data” conflicts with the statement that the base Maxout is trained with data augmentation (please clarify whether test-time augmentation or committee averaging is used).
- Ablations and controls are limited. There is no comparison to simpler baselines such as: (a) fine-tuning the base network with more supervised training, (b) adding a linear or MLP “calibration head” on top of the penultimate layer, (c) end-to-end differentiable training of gates via backprop through time, or (d) policy gradient (REINFORCE) with fewer parameters. The observation vector uses global averages, which discards spatial information; a study of observation design would be valuable.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation specifics are missing (dimensions of O and A; per-layer map counts; gate initializations; exact loss Li indexing; stopping criterion; validation protocol). Algorithm 1 and Equations (8–10) are ambiguous: Li = −λboost d log(vT) likely means −λboost log vT[d]; the condition using ∥vT∥∞ to set λboost appears to test top-1 correctness but is not clearly stated.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with helpful biological context. Some notation is overloaded (M vs Mt, θ used for both policy and base net scope), and several equations and the pseudo-code would benefit from precise indexing and dimensional checks. The text occasionally conflates “augmentation” settings; please clearly separate training-time augmentation, test-time augmentation, and ensemble/committee effects. Figures are referenced to support interpretability; more quantitative summaries would help.

Originality
Moderate to good. While RL-based attention and recurrent processing are not new, the specific formulation—layer-wise gating of feature maps learned via large-scale SNES on top of a strong CNN—is novel. The paper positions itself against prior selective attention/saccade models and feedback architectures, and scales the idea to CIFAR.

Significance
Conceptually, the work advances the idea that internal, learned attention over feature maps can correct initial feedforward mistakes. Practically, the reported improvements on CIFAR-10/100 (single model, unaugmented test-time) are potentially meaningful, but the significance is limited by the lack of rigorous baselines and ablations and by unclear reporting. The approach is computationally expensive (multi-pass inference, SNES training with large policy), raising questions about scalability to larger datasets/models.

Strengths
- Clear, biologically inspired motivation for adding top-down, sequential processing to CNNs.
- Simple architectural hook (per-map gates) that is easy to integrate with existing CNNs.
- Training strategy (post hoc policy learning) decouples representation learning and attention policy.
- Stability of dynamics beyond training horizon is encouraging.
- Qualitative visualizations and a test showing gates carry class information are informative.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient experimental detail and absent ablations; unclear fairness of comparisons; lack of run-to-run variability.
- No comparison to gradient-based recurrent training or simpler calibration/fine-tuning baselines.
- Observation design discards spatial information; action space is very high-dimensional relative to available training signal.
- Computational overhead and scalability not quantified.
- Some notation/algorithmic ambiguities (loss definition, λboost criterion, dimensions in Eq. 7).

Questions for authors
1. Please report exact CIFAR-10/100 error rates (mean±std over multiple seeds) for base Maxout and dasNet, and specify whether test-time augmentation or ensembling is used.
2. What are the dimensions of O and A? Are gates learned per-layer independently or jointly? Are actions constrained (e.g., min/max)?
3. What is the inference-time overhead (multiplicative factor vs base CNN) for T=5?
4. Did you try end-to-end differentiable training of gates via backprop through time, or REINFORCE? How do they compare to SNES?
5. Can you provide ablations: remove parts of the observation; restrict gating to top layer only; vary T; remove boosting; compare to simply fine-tuning the base network?
6. In the gate-only classification experiment, was the evaluation on held-out test data? The reported ~45% accuracy on CIFAR-100 seems hard to reconcile with “similar to dasNet performance.”

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify equations and pseudo-code; define the loss precisely and the λboost criterion.
- Add rigorous baselines and ablations as noted above.
- Provide full tables with absolute error rates, variance, and training/inference cost.
- Explore spatial attention or channel-group gates to reduce action dimensionality.
- Discuss scalability to larger models/datasets.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept) contingent on improved experimental reporting and stronger baselines. The idea is interesting and timely, and the method is simple and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evidence is not fully convincing.

Confidence
Medium.
