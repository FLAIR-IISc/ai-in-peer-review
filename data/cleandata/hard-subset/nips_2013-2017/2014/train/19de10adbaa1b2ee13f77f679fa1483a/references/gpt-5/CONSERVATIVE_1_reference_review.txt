Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with internal “selective attention” via multiplicative gates on feature maps that are updated across multiple passes over the same image. A policy network maps observations (averaged feature map activations across layers, intermediate classification activations, and class probabilities) to gate values for the next pass. The policy is trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the high-dimensional continuous observation/action spaces (on the order of ~1M policy parameters). On CIFAR-10/100, with T=5 passes, dasNet reportedly improves over a strong Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art performance for “unaugmented data.” Qualitative analyses show some examples where predictions flip from incorrect to correct after several passes, and simple stability checks suggest performance peaks at the trained T and remains stable for more steps.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive idea: iteratively reweight feature maps to refine ambiguous predictions, inspired by feedback/attention in human vision.
- Practical mechanism: simple multiplicative gates with a softmax constraint (mean ≈ 1) to stabilize activations; observations designed from easily available internal statistics.
- Empirical gains over a strong Maxout model on CIFAR-10/100; some analysis of dynamics and an attempt to probe whether gates carry label information.
- The paper is generally readable and includes an algorithmic description of the training loop.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient baselines and ablations. There is no comparison to:
  - An end-to-end differentiable recurrent gating model trained with backpropagation through time (same gating parameterization, no RL/ES).
  - Simpler supervised alternatives (e.g., a small MLP that predicts a single-pass per-map scaling conditioned on features; or learning static per-map scalars per layer via backprop).
  - Compute-matched baselines (e.g., a larger CNN or test-time ensembling/augmentation that consumes similar inference budget as T=5 passes).
- Justification for black-box evolutionary RL is weak. The environment is fully differentiable and deterministic; modern supervised optimization comfortably handles >1M parameters. NES/SNES is used largely due to dimensionality, but the paper does not demonstrate that gradient-based training performs poorly or is impractical here.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility issues:
  - The “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” claim is ambiguous given that the base CNN is trained with data augmentation. Please clarify what “unaugmented” refers to (test-time only?).
  - Missing or incomplete details for SNES (learning rates, schedule, stopping criteria, normalization, population selection); action/observation dimensionalities; exact Maxout architecture/hyperparameters sufficient to reproduce results.
  - No reporting of variability across runs, confidence intervals, or statistical tests; single-seed results are insufficient for small absolute gains.
  - It is unclear whether a validation set is used for SNES model selection; using the test set risks bias.
- Objective/optimization design is ad-hoc. The boosted loss that upweights misclassified training samples can overfit “hard” train cases; overfitting checks are not presented.
- Scope is limited to CIFAR-10/100 (32x32). Claims of generality and biological plausibility are not supported by results at larger scales or different modalities.
- Overhead not contextualized. T=5 increases inference cost roughly 5×; comparison to compute-matched baselines is necessary.

Quality
Technically plausible and mostly sound at a high level, but the evaluation is not rigorous enough. The absence of key baselines and ablations makes it difficult to attribute gains to the proposed feedback mechanism vs. extra compute or other design choices. Reproducibility is hindered by missing details.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. However, important training details are relegated to supplementary material or omitted. The “unaugmented” claim needs clarification. A more precise description of observation/action sizes and SNES settings would help.

Originality
Combining internal feature gating with iterative passes and learning the gating policy is a reasonable, moderately novel twist relative to prior recurrent/attention and RL-for-vision work. Using SNES for high-dimensional policy optimization is less common, though not clearly necessary here.

Significance
Potentially interesting direction, but current evidence is limited. Gains on CIFAR are modest and not contextualized against strong, compute-matched baselines or gradient-trained recurrent alternatives. Without stronger empirical validation, it is hard to recommend this as a robust method practitioners will adopt.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines:
  - Backprop-through-time with the same gating architecture (no ES/RL).
  - A single-pass supervised gating network conditioned on internal features.
  - Compute-matched larger CNN and/or test-time ensembling/augmentation.
- Provide ablations:
  - Gates per layer vs per feature map vs per channel group.
  - Effect of T and policy capacity; remove the “boosting” weight.
  - SNES vs standard ES vs policy gradients (if feasible).
- Report multiple runs with mean/std; use a validation set; avoid tuning on the test set.
- Clarify and fully specify SNES and network hyperparameters; release code if possible.
- Quantify overhead and accuracy vs compute trade-offs; show per-class confusion reductions and number of flips from wrong to right.
- Evaluate on at least one higher-resolution dataset to support generality.

Recommendation
Reject (weak). The idea is appealing, but the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous, especially the lack of gradient-based recurrent baselines and compute-matched comparisons. With stronger experiments and clearer methodology, this work could become compelling.
