## Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing the performance of deep convolutional neural networks by incorporating internal selective attention via feedback connections.  The core idea is to allow a trained CNN to iteratively refine its classifications by dynamically weighting the activations of its filters through a policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES). The network effectively “re-thinks” its initial classification, focusing on more discriminative features. The authors demonstrate improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on difficult classification instances, surpassing the performance of a baseline Maxout CNN.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of attention mechanisms in deep learning and builds on existing work in recurrent processing for image analysis. The authors acknowledge the biological inspiration of feedback connections in the visual cortex (Felleman & Essen, Gilbert & Sigman) and relate their work to previous attempts to incorporate recurrence and RL into CNNs (e.g., Schmidhuber, Denil, Wyatte). They differentiate their approach by applying RL to modulate *internal* filter activations within a standard CNN architecture, and by employing SNES to handle the high dimensionality of the control policy.  The use of Maxout networks (Goodfellow) as a base architecture is also well-motivated, referencing its competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning an attentional policy to modulate internal filter activations *after* supervised training is a fresh and interesting approach.  This differs from most attention mechanisms which are typically integrated into the network architecture and learned jointly with the classification task.
* **Addressing a Real Problem:** The motivation of tackling difficult, ambiguous instances where CNNs struggle is well-defined and relevant.  The paper directly addresses the limitations of feedforward networks in complex visual reasoning.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate a clear performance improvement over the baseline Maxout CNN, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.  The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provides intuitive insight into how the attention mechanism is working.
* **Scalability (SNES):** The authors appropriately chose SNES to address the high dimensionality of the policy space, making the approach more tractable than traditional RL methods.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the methodology clearly. The equations are well-defined and the algorithm is presented in a structured manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet requires significant computational resources (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU), making it less accessible for researchers with limited resources. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it’s a practical consideration.
* **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the visualization of activation changes is helpful, the analysis of *why* certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized is relatively limited. Deeper investigation into the learned policy and its correlation with image features would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't extensively explore the sensitivity of the results to the various hyperparameters, such as the number of iterations (T), the learning rates for SNES, and the regularization parameter (λL2). A more comprehensive hyperparameter study would increase confidence in the robustness of the findings.
* **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While these datasets are standard benchmarks, it’s unclear how well dasNet would generalize to more complex, real-world datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
* **Information Content Analysis:** Although the authors perform an experiment to verify the information content of the learned gates, it seems somewhat ad-hoc. Using the final gates directly for classification with KNN/Logistic Regression doesn't fully reveal *what* information the gates are encoding.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving CNN performance through internal selective attention.
* The empirical results demonstrate a statistically significant improvement on challenging datasets.
* The use of SNES addresses a key practical challenge (high dimensionality) in applying RL to deep networks.
* The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high computational cost and lack of extensive hyperparameter tuning could limit the wider adoption of the method.
* The analysis of the learned policy could be more in-depth to provide a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
* Limited generalization to more complex datasets.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of deep learning. The idea of learning to selectively attend to internal features is innovative and shows promising results. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding computational cost and depth of analysis, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I would encourage the authors to:

* Discuss the computational cost more explicitly and consider strategies for reducing it.
* Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter study.
* Explore more sophisticated methods for analyzing the learned policy and understanding the information encoded in the attention weights.
* Briefly discuss the potential challenges of applying dasNet to larger, more complex datasets.
