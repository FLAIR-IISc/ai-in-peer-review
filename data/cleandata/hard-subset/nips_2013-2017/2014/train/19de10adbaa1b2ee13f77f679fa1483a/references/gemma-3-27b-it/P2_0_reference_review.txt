## Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections between layers. These connections are controlled by a policy learned through reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES) after supervised training. The goal is to enable the network to refine its classifications through iterative processing, focusing on relevant features and correcting initial misclassifications, mimicking aspects of human visual attention. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging instances.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to improving CNN performance by incorporating an attention mechanism learned *post-hoc* through reinforcement learning. While the experimental results demonstrate improvement, the paper has some weaknesses in terms of clarity and justification of design choices.  I lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity concerns and providing more detailed justification for their approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of learning an attentional policy *after* supervised training to refine classifications is a novel approach. It differs from traditional attention mechanisms that are typically learned end-to-end. This decoupling allows leveraging already powerful, pre-trained networks.
*   **Demonstrated Improvement:** The reported performance gains on CIFAR-10/100, particularly on difficult instances, provide evidence that the approach is effective.  The visualization of filter activation changes is insightful.
*   **Addressing a Relevant Problem:**  The paper tackles the limitations of standard CNNs in handling ambiguous cases, a crucial aspect of real-world image recognition. The connection to human visual processing is well-motivated.
*   **Practicality:** Using SNES to handle the large parameter space of the attentional policy is a reasonable choice, given the computational challenges of standard RL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Detail:** The paper suffers from a lack of detail in several areas. The description of the observation space `h(Mt)` is vague.  Specifically, what kind of averaging is done for the activations, and why are those particular features chosen? More explanation of how the action `a` modulates the filters (equation 5) would be helpful. The choice of T=5 is stated as "experimentally determined," but the process of determining this is not described.
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** The use of Maxout networks, while mentioned as a state-of-the-art baseline, isn’t strongly justified.  Why Maxout specifically?  Would the approach work equally well with ReLU or other activation functions? Similarly, the specific values for the boosting parameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2) aren't motivated.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** The experiments lack thorough ablation studies.  How does performance change with different SNES population sizes or learning rates? How sensitive is the approach to the initial supervised training?  Exploring these parameters would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Comparison to Existing Attention Mechanisms:**  The related work section mentions existing attention mechanisms but doesn’t clearly position dasNet *relative* to them. A more detailed comparison of dasNet to, for example, spatial transformer networks or channel attention, would be beneficial. How does this post-hoc, feedback-based approach compare to learning attention end-to-end?
*   **Scalability:** While CIFAR-10/100 are good benchmarks, the authors don’t discuss the potential scalability of this approach to larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet. The computational cost of the iterative refinement process could be a significant bottleneck.

**Quality:**

The paper is generally technically sound. The SNES implementation seems appropriate for the problem size. However, the lack of detailed justification and ablation studies weaken the overall confidence in the results.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is a significant concern. Several aspects of the method are not explained sufficiently, making it difficult to fully understand and reproduce the work.

**Originality:**

The combination of post-hoc reinforcement learning with feedback connections for attention is a novel contribution.

**Significance:**

The potential to improve CNN performance on difficult cases through iterative refinement is significant. However, the practical impact and scalability need further investigation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to attention with potential for improvement.
*   Demonstrated performance gains on CIFAR-10/100.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in image classification.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of clarity and detail in method description.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices.
*   Limited ablation studies and scalability analysis.




**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept**. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the clarity concerns and provide more detailed justification for their choices. Specifically, they should:

1.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the observation space and action modulation.
2.  Justify the choice of Maxout networks and the boosting parameters.
3.  Include additional ablation studies to assess the sensitivity of the approach.
4.  Provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing attention mechanisms.
5.  Discuss the potential scalability challenges.
