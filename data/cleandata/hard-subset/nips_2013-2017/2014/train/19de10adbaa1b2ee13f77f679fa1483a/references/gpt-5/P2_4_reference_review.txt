Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN with internal selective attention implemented via feedback connections. A standard Maxout CNN is augmented with per-feature-map gating variables aℓj that modulate activations before pooling. Given an input image, the network is run for T passes; after each pass, an observation vector is constructed from average feature-map activations, classification-layer activations, and the current class probabilities. A policy π maps observations to the next set of gates that scale feature maps on the following pass. The policy parameters (≈10^6) are learned after supervised training of the base CNN via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimizer that estimates a diagonal-covariance natural gradient update. The objective boosts misclassified examples to focus the policy on hard cases. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline and claim state-of-the-art on unaugmented data. Qualitative visualizations and a small dynamical analysis (varying T) suggest the recurrent process is stable and that gate activations contain class-relevant information.

This work connects to a rich literature on attention and feedback in biological vision and in machine learning: saccadic/glimpse models and RL for attention, recurrent processing for denoising/reconstruction, and prior CNN-based SOTA (Maxout). The main novelty is internal, feature-map-level attention learned by RL and applied iteratively within a modern CNN.

Quality
- Soundness: The mechanism (Eq. 5) is simple and well-motivated; constraining gates via a scaled softmax to be positive and average to 1 is sensible for stability. The training loop (Algorithm 1) is coherent, with loss applied only after T steps and with misclassification boosting. Using SNES avoids brittle credit assignment and heavy engineering required for high-dimensional policy gradients.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent improvements over the base Maxout net, with qualitative evidence that the policy corrects initial mistakes on ambiguous images. The “dynamics” experiment shows performance peaking near the trained T and staying stable for longer rollouts. An analysis using only final gate values to classify suggests those gates encode class-relevant information.
- Missing controls/ablations: Important baselines are absent: (1) a learned linear policy trained by backprop through unrolled passes (deterministic policy gradient/REINFORCE) to assess whether NES is necessary; (2) randomized or fixed-gate policies to quantify the value of learned attention; (3) per-layer/partial observation ablations to see which signals matter; (4) effect of T and the boosting weights on performance; (5) computation/latency overhead and energy per image vs. the baseline; (6) whether fine-tuning the Maxout CNN alone could recover similar gains. Statistical significance of improvements and variance across runs are not reported.
- Reproducibility: Several details are underspecified (exact observation/action dimensionalities, policy network architecture—appears linear, treatment of dropout during recurrent passes, early stopping and validation protocol for the policy, data augmentation consistency). The claim of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” appears inconsistent with earlier mention that the Maxout net was trained “with data augmentation.”

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with a clear motivation and a concise algorithm. The biological and ML related work are well-situated.
- However, there are ambiguities: the policy is defined as πθ(o) = dim(A)·softmax(θo), suggesting a single linear layer; this should be stated explicitly. The training/validation/test protocol for evolving π needs clarification. The presentation of results lacks full tables and precise error rates; the “6% relative improvement” is hard to interpret without absolute numbers. The “gates-as-features” experiment reports 40–46% accuracy, but the comparison point (“similar to dasNet”) is unclear for CIFAR-100 where SOTA is much higher; specify dataset/split/model.

Originality
- The combination of internal feature-map attention, applied recurrently within a CNN, and learned via black-box evolution at large scale, is novel relative to prior attention models focused on spatial saccades or external glimpses and prior feedback models aimed at reconstruction/denoising. Modulating learned feature maps to correct mistakes “in hindsight” is an interesting and distinct contribution.
- That said, the individual components (feature-wise gating, iterative inference, RL/evolutionary search) are known; the contribution is a well-executed integration atop a strong CNN.

Significance
- Practical impact: Demonstrating improvements on CIFAR-10/100 from an internal attention loop on top of an already strong baseline is meaningful, suggesting headroom in iterative inference beyond a single feedforward pass.
- Scope/limits: Gains appear modest (the paper cites ~6% relative over the baseline), and the compute overhead and sample efficiency of SNES for million-parameter policies may limit adoption. The approach is currently evaluated on 32×32 benchmarks; it is unclear how it scales to higher-resolution tasks or to detection/segmentation where spatial attention could be critical. Without stronger ablations and comparisons, it is hard to attribute gains specifically to the proposed feedback mechanism versus additional capacity/regularization.

Pros
- Clear, biologically motivated idea: internal attention via feature-map gating with feedback across passes.
- Simple mechanism that can be attached to existing CNNs; stable dynamics.
- Consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; qualitative case studies.
- Makes a credible case that iterative processing can correct ambiguous feedforward mistakes.

Cons
- Missing key baselines/ablations and statistical analysis; unclear attribution of gains.
- Ambiguities around data augmentation, validation, and policy architecture.
- Compute cost and sample efficiency of SNES not quantified; feasibility on larger problems uncertain.
- Limited comparison to other recurrent/feedback or attention-within-CNN approaches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: random/fixed gates, simple supervised/REINFORCE policy, and fine-tuning of the CNN alone.
- Report absolute error rates with mean±std over multiple runs; include confusion matrices and per-class effects.
- Clarify augmentation/validation protocols, policy architecture, observation/action sizes, and runtime overhead.
- Explore T sensitivity and show how many images actually change decision after feedback.
- Consider a constrained policy (e.g., per-layer or per-class gates) to reduce dimensionality and improve sample efficiency.

Overall evaluation
- Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a novel, well-motivated mechanism for internal attention with recurrent feedback that yields measurable gains on standard benchmarks. The idea is likely to stimulate follow-up work. However, the empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines and clearer reporting to firmly establish the source and robustness of the improvements.

Confidence: Medium.
