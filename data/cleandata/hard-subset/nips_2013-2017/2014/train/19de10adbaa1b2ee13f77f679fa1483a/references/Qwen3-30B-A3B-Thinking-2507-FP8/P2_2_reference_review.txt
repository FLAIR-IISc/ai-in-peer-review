

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary

This paper introduces "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing deep CNNs by incorporating internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors propose that CNNs can improve their classification performance on difficult cases by iteratively adjusting the activation weights of filters across multiple passes through the same image, rather than relying solely on a single feedforward pass. The core innovation is using a reinforcement learning policy (learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to control how each layer's filters are weighted during subsequent passes. The method is applied to Maxout networks, with the attention mechanism modifying equation (1) by introducing layer-specific weights (a^ℓ_j) that can be adjusted over multiple passes.

The paper is well-motivated by biological visual processing, where humans often require multiple "looks" to distinguish between similar visual stimuli. The authors demonstrate that dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly for ambiguous classification cases. The work builds on previous research in recurrent networks, attention mechanisms, and reinforcement learning, but offers a novel implementation using SNES to handle the large policy parameter space.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach**: The paper's core idea of using feedback connections for internal selective attention is both biologically plausible and technically interesting. The authors effectively connect their approach to the two-stage visual processing theory in humans (fast feedforward processing followed by attentional processing), which strengthens the motivation for their method.

2. **Significant Experimental Results**: The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 (with new state-of-the-art results for unaugmented data) is substantial for this challenging dataset. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 provides compelling evidence that the attention mechanism is indeed focusing on relevant features to correct misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing cats from dogs).

3. **Clear Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a well-defined technical approach that is relatively straightforward to implement on top of existing CNN architectures. The use of SNES to handle the large policy parameter space (over 1 million parameters) is a practical solution to a non-trivial problem.

4. **Strong Experimental Design**: The evaluation focuses on the most interesting cases for their approach (ambiguous classification cases), which is a thoughtful design choice. The ablation study on the number of passes (T) in Figure 2 provides useful insights into the dynamics of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Existing Work**: While the paper references related work, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish dasNet from other attention mechanisms in CNNs (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation blocks, non-local networks, or attention mechanisms in transformer models). These approaches also use feedback connections or internal attention but in different ways. The authors should clarify how their approach differs from these existing methods.

2. **Lack of Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper mentions that training dasNet takes "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU," but doesn't discuss the computational cost of inference (which requires multiple passes through the network). For real-world applications, the 5x inference time penalty (T=5) could be prohibitive, and this trade-off isn't adequately addressed.

3. **Limited Comparison with Recent SOTA**: The paper claims a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 but doesn't compare against more recent approaches (e.g., EfficientNet, Vision Transformers, or other attention-based architectures). This makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the improvement.

4. **Incomplete Methodological Details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about the SNES implementation (e.g., number of generations, population size, stopping criteria) and hyperparameters. Without these details, it's difficult to fully evaluate the method or reproduce the results.

5. **Limited Analysis of When the Method Fails**: The paper focuses on cases where dasNet improves performance, but doesn't provide examples where it fails or where it might not be beneficial. Understanding the limitations of the approach would strengthen the paper.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with a clear methodology and well-supported experimental results. The claims about the effectiveness of dasNet are supported by the experimental data on CIFAR-10/100. However, the paper could be strengthened by more thorough analysis of the computational cost and comparison to recent SOTA methods. The authors are reasonably honest about the limitations of their approach (not applicable to all classification tasks), but could provide more comprehensive evaluation of these limitations.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The main idea is clearly presented, and the technical details are explained with appropriate notation. The figures (particularly Figure 3) are effective in illustrating how the attention mechanism works. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed explanation of the SNES implementation and hyperparameters in the main text (rather than relegating them to supplementary material).

## Originality

The specific combination of Maxout networks with a reinforcement learning policy for internal selective attention is novel. While the broader concept of attention mechanisms in CNNs is not entirely new, dasNet's approach using feedback connections to iteratively adjust filter weights in a post-training phase is distinctive. However, the paper could better highlight how this differs from existing attention mechanisms to strengthen the originality claim.

## Significance

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning: improving classification performance on difficult cases without retraining the entire network. The 6% improvement on CIFAR-10 is significant for this dataset, and the approach could be valuable for practitioners looking to enhance existing CNNs. However, the computational cost of the iterative process (5x inference time) limits its practical applicability for real-time systems. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how this trade-off compares to other approaches that might achieve similar improvements with less computational overhead.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach with strong experimental results that advance the state of the art on CIFAR-10. The main issues that need to be addressed are:

1. Better differentiation from existing attention mechanisms in CNNs
2. More thorough analysis of computational costs (both training and inference)
3. Comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100
4. Additional details about the SNES implementation and hyperparameters

With these revisions, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning with a biologically inspired approach
- Demonstrates a significant improvement (6% relative) over vanilla CNNs on a challenging dataset
- Well-motivated by human visual processing
- Presents a clear, implementable framework that can be applied to existing CNN architectures

## Arguments for Rejection

- Doesn't sufficiently differentiate from existing attention mechanisms
- Limited analysis of computational costs
- Doesn't compare to recent state-of-the-art methods
- Some methodological details are missing

## Final Recommendation

Accept (with minor revisions) - The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field with a novel approach to internal attention in CNNs. The experimental results are strong, and the method is well-motivated. With the suggested revisions, this would be a high-quality NIPS paper.
