This paper presents dasNet, a fascinating approach to injecting a form of internal deliberation into deep convolutional networks via reinforcement learning. The core idea – allowing a CNN to revisit and re-evaluate its initial classification through feedback connections modulating filter activations – is genuinely novel and, frankly, quite bold. It directly addresses a limitation of standard CNNs: their static nature post-training, contrasting with the more nuanced, iterative processing observed in human vision. The authors rightly point to the need for models capable of handling ambiguous cases and the potential benefits of internal self-correction.

The technical implementation, utilizing SNES to navigate the high-dimensional policy space, is a pragmatic choice given the complexity of controlling a large CNN. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100, while not groundbreaking in absolute terms, *do* demonstrate a performance improvement, particularly in challenging cases as illustrated by the cat/dog example. The visualization of filter activation changes provides a compelling, albeit preliminary, glimpse into the network’s “thought process”.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The description of the observation space (h(M)) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors acknowledge it's partial, a stronger justification for the chosen features would be beneficial. Furthermore, the details of the SNES implementation are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess its effectiveness. The dynamics experiments are insightful but limited; more extensive analysis of the iterative process would strengthen the argument for the benefits of recurrent processing. The connection to the broader literature on visual attention and recurrent neural networks, while present, could be more deeply explored – especially regarding the biological plausibility arguments. The claim of establishing a "new state-of-the-art" result feels a little overstated; a more nuanced phrasing would be appropriate.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of learning attentional feedback connections within a CNN is genuinely new and addresses a significant limitation of existing architectures.
*   **Boldness:** The approach is ambitious and tackles a complex problem with a relatively unexplored methodology.
*   **Pragmatism:** The use of SNES is a reasonable choice given the dimensionality of the problem.
*   **Positive Results:** The experimental results demonstrate a tangible performance improvement, particularly in ambiguous cases.
*   **Illustrative Example:** The cat/dog example effectively showcases the potential benefits of the iterative refinement process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Observation Space:** The construction of the observation space feels somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Implementation Details:** Critical details of SNES are in supplementary material.
*   **Limited Dynamics Analysis:** The study of the iterative process could be more comprehensive.
*   **Biological Plausibility:** The connection to biological vision is acknowledged, but could be strengthened.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of a “new state-of-the-art” result is a bit strong.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a truly novel concept with the potential to significantly impact the field of deep learning.
*   The authors have demonstrated the feasibility of their approach with encouraging experimental results.
*   The work is well-motivated and clearly presented.
*   The paper opens up exciting new avenues for research in internal attention mechanisms and recurrent processing in CNNs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical details are not fully fleshed out (observation space, SNES implementation).
*   The experimental evaluation, while positive, is limited in scope.
*   The paper's claims could be more carefully worded.




**Overall:**

Despite its minor flaws, this is a compelling and innovative paper. The core idea is strong, the implementation is reasonable, and the results are promising. I believe the novelty and potential significance of this work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding the observation space and provide more details about the SNES implementation in the final version. This is the kind of bold, forward-thinking research that NIPS should be championing.
