

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that enables deep CNNs to iteratively refine their classification through internal selective attention mechanisms. The key insight is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can use reinforcement learning to modulate filter activations across multiple passes over the same image, correcting initial misclassifications. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture and learn an attention policy via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to control filter weighting. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, showing a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs, particularly on ambiguous classification cases.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with a clear methodology that builds on established concepts (Maxout networks, reinforcement learning). The authors make a reasonable choice of SNES for handling the large parameter space of the attention policy. The experimental design is appropriate for the task, with evaluation focused on ambiguous classification cases where the approach should shine.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in its evaluation. The claimed 6% relative improvement is modest, and the comparison to other state-of-the-art methods is limited to "vanilla CNN" without comprehensive comparison to recent attention mechanisms (e.g., Transformers) or iterative processing approaches. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the results, which is essential for claiming improvements. Additionally, the paper doesn't adequately address computational costs - the iterative nature of dasNet significantly increases inference time, which is not discussed in relation to the modest performance gains.

The paper is a complete piece of work, but it's not fully reproducible without additional architectural details (e.g., number of filters, kernel sizes in the Maxout network) that should have been included. The authors acknowledge the need for multiple passes but don't quantify the trade-off between performance gain and inference time.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly states the motivation, and the methodology is explained step-by-step with sufficient detail. Algorithm 1 provides a clear description of the training process. The related work section is strong, covering both neuroscience and computer vision perspectives on attention mechanisms.

The main clarity issue is the lack of sufficient architectural details to reproduce the experiments. While the authors reference their Maxout network implementation, the specific architecture details needed to reproduce their results are missing from the main text. The paper also doesn't provide enough detail about the SNES implementation beyond the general approach.

## Originality

The core idea of using reinforcement learning to learn how to modulate filter activations across multiple passes is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on attention mechanisms by focusing on *internal* selective attention rather than spatial attention, and by applying it as a post-training adaptation to a feedforward network.

The related work section is thorough, covering key neuroscience findings on visual attention and previous computer vision work on attention mechanisms. However, it could better address recent attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., Transformers, which have become dominant in many vision tasks) and more recent work on iterative processing in CNNs.

## Significance

The significance of the paper is limited by the modest performance gains (6% relative improvement) and the lack of evaluation on larger, more challenging datasets like ImageNet. While the approach demonstrates that iterative processing can improve classification performance, it's unclear how much this contribution advances the state of the art compared to simpler approaches like using larger networks or ensemble methods.

The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this approach is preferable to alternative methods for achieving similar performance gains. The authors mention that the approach "corrects itself" on difficult cases, but don't provide a thorough analysis of *which* cases are improved and by how much, making it hard to assess the practical value.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to internal selective attention in deep networks
- Well-written paper with clear motivation and methodology
- Strong related work section covering both neuroscience and computer vision
- Insightful analysis of how attention affects filter activations (Figure 3)
- Demonstrated improvement on standard benchmarks

**Weaknesses:**
- Modest performance improvement that doesn't strongly justify the added complexity
- Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods
- Lack of statistical significance testing
- Missing architectural details for reproducibility
- No evaluation on larger datasets or more complex tasks
- Insufficient discussion of computational trade-offs

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and novel approach to internal selective attention in deep networks, with a technically sound methodology and clear experimental results. However, the significance of the contribution is limited by the modest performance gains and lack of comprehensive evaluation against recent state-of-the-art methods.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A more comprehensive comparison to recent attention mechanisms (e.g., Transformers)
2. Evaluation on ImageNet or other larger datasets
3. Statistical significance testing for the results
4. More detailed analysis of the computational cost versus performance trade-off
5. Additional architectural details for reproducibility

While the paper makes a novel contribution to the understanding of attention mechanisms in deep learning, the current results don't sufficiently demonstrate a substantial advancement of the state of the art. I would recommend borderline acceptance with major revisions addressing these limitations. The paper has potential to be a strong contribution if the authors can provide more comprehensive evaluation and better contextualize their results within recent literature.

Given the modest but clear improvement and the novel approach, I would lean toward acceptance with major revisions rather than rejection, but the current version doesn't meet the high bar for NIPS acceptance due to the limited significance of the results.
