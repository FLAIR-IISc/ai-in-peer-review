This paper presents dasNet, a fascinating approach to introducing a form of internal state and adaptive computation into deep convolutional networks via reinforcement learning. The core idea – allowing a network to ‘re-think’ its classifications through iterative refinement using learned attention weights – is genuinely novel and addresses a compelling question about the rigidity of standard CNNs. The analogy to human visual processing, where initial impressions are often refined with further scrutiny, is well-motivated. While the experimental validation is currently limited to CIFAR-10/100, the reported performance improvements and the qualitative analysis of filter activation changes are promising.

The technical implementation, leveraging Maxout networks and SNES for policy learning, feels pragmatic given the scale of the problem. Using SNES instead of a standard RL algorithm to handle the high-dimensional action space is a reasonable choice, and the authors acknowledge this trade-off. The inclusion of dynamics analysis and the check for information content in the learned gate values adds further value, demonstrating that the learned policy isn’t merely introducing noise. The writing is generally clear, though sections relating to the RL methodology (SNES) could benefit from more accessible explanations for readers unfamiliar with the technique.

However, there are several areas that warrant consideration. The choice of T=5 steps feels somewhat arbitrary. While the dynamics analysis provides some justification, a more thorough investigation of the optimal number of iterations, potentially adaptive to the input image, could be beneficial. The boosting mechanism, while simple, feels somewhat ad-hoc. Exploring more sophisticated reward functions tailored to the specific types of classification errors could further improve performance.

A significant limitation is the lack of analysis on *what* the network is re-evaluating. The qualitative visualization of filter changes is interesting, but more systematic attempts to interpret the learned attention patterns would strengthen the conclusions. Are specific filters consistently re-activated for certain types of errors? Is there a hierarchical pattern to the filter activations across layers?

Furthermore, the computational cost of the iterative refinement process is substantial. While the authors mention 4 days of training on a GTX 560 Ti, a discussion of the inference time overhead would be crucial for assessing the practical applicability of dasNet. Is the performance gain worth the extra computational burden?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of learned internal attention via feedback connections is highly original and presents a significant departure from standard CNN architectures.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could lead to more robust and interpretable deep learning models capable of handling ambiguous or challenging inputs.
*   **Promising Results:** The performance improvements on CIFAR-10/100, albeit modest, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
*   **Solid Technical Foundation:** The combination of Maxout networks and SNES provides a reasonable technical framework for implementing and evaluating the idea.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Validation:** The experiments are currently restricted to relatively small datasets (CIFAR-10/100).
*   **Lack of Interpretability:** The learned attention patterns are not fully understood, hindering our ability to assess the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Computational Cost:** The iterative refinement process introduces significant computational overhead.
*   **Ad-hoc elements:** The reward function and the fixed number of steps (T) feel somewhat arbitrary.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I believe the potential of this work is significant enough to warrant acceptance. The novelty of the idea, combined with the promising preliminary results, outweighs the current shortcomings. The paper introduces a bold new direction for research in deep learning, and even if the current implementation is not perfect, it opens up exciting avenues for future exploration. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that future work will address the limitations outlined above – particularly regarding interpretability, computational cost and generalization to larger and more complex datasets. I would encourage the authors to include a discussion on inference time in the camera-ready version.
