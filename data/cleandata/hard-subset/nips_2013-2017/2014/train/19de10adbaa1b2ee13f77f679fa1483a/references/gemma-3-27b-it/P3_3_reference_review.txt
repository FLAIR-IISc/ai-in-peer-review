## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections (dasNet)

**Summary:**

This paper presents Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that introduces feedback connections within a deep convolutional neural network to enable selective attention post-training. The core idea is to allow each layer to influence others iteratively, modulating filter activations based on a control policy learned through reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES). The authors argue that this mimics human visual processing, where multiple passes over an image allow for refined understanding, particularly in ambiguous cases. They demonstrate improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on difficult instances, outperforming a baseline Maxout CNN.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper effectively contextualizes its contribution within a rich body of work. It appropriately cites relevant CNN architectures (AlexNet, Maxout networks), reinforcement learning techniques, and biological inspiration from the primate visual cortex (Felleman & Essen, Gilbert, Lamme). The authors correctly highlight the connection to existing work on attention mechanisms in vision (saccades, recurrent processing) but emphasize that their approach focuses on *internal* attention within the network itself, rather than external mechanisms or simplified datasets.  They differentiate their work by applying RL to a large-scale, state-of-the-art CNN, pushing the boundaries of feasibility, and demonstrating tangible performance gains.  The citation of related work on iterative refinement (e.g., reconstructing images, face localization) is also helpful.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning a feedback policy to modulate internal filter activations is genuinely novel. While recurrent connections and attention are not new, applying RL to learn a *selective* internal attention mechanism within a deep CNN is a significant contribution.
* **Biological Plausibility:** The motivation based on human visual processing—multiple passes for difficult discrimination—is compelling and provides a strong rationale for the approach. The analogy to top-down influences in the visual cortex is well-articulated.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The demonstrated improvement on CIFAR-10/100, especially on challenging instances, provides concrete evidence of the efficacy of dasNet. The ablation studies (dynamics analysis, gate information content) offer further insights into how the learned policy operates.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology, combining Maxout networks with SNES, appears technically sound. The explanation of SNES is clear and concise, justifying its choice over traditional RL methods for this high-dimensional control problem. The implementation details, such as the choice of loss function (boosting misclassified examples), demonstrate careful consideration.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and structured, making it relatively easy to follow the complex interplay of components.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While SNES addresses the dimensionality challenge, its computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is substantial. This limits the practicality of applying dasNet to larger datasets or more complex architectures.  The paper doesn't explicitly discuss the potential for leveraging parallelization or more efficient RL algorithms.
* **Policy Interpretability:** While the paper visualizes filter activation changes, it remains difficult to interpret *why* the learned policy makes the decisions it does. Understanding the specific features being emphasized or suppressed would be valuable for gaining deeper insights into the network’s reasoning.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successful disambiguation examples. A more thorough analysis of failure cases—instances where dasNet *doesn’t* improve performance—would strengthen the evaluation. Understanding the limitations of the approach is crucial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of hyperparameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2, T) could potentially impact performance significantly.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the effect of these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Maxout Network Specificity:** The reliance on Maxout networks might limit the generalizability of dasNet.  While the authors justify their choice, it would be valuable to investigate whether the approach can be effectively applied to other common CNN architectures (e.g., ResNets, DenseNets).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to introducing internal selective attention in deep CNNs.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements on standard benchmarks, particularly in challenging scenarios.
* The work is well-motivated by biological principles and is clearly situated within the relevant literature.
* The authors address a significant challenge (learning high-dimensional control policies) with a reasonable solution (SNES).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost of dasNet is a significant limitation, hindering its scalability.
* The interpretability of the learned attention policy remains limited.
* The reliance on Maxout networks might restrict the generalizability of the approach.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and deserves **acceptance with minor revisions.** The novelty of the approach, coupled with the strong empirical results and clear presentation, outweighs the concerns regarding scalability and interpretability. 

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Discuss scalability limitations and potential solutions:**  Address the computational cost more explicitly and explore options for reducing it (e.g., parallelization, more efficient RL algorithms).
* **Provide more in-depth analysis of failure cases:** Investigate instances where dasNet fails to improve performance and identify potential causes.
* **Consider a brief investigation of other CNN architectures:** Demonstrate the potential for applying dasNet to networks beyond Maxout.
* **Include a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis:** Evaluate the impact of key hyperparameters on performance.
