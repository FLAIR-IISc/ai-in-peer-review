

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment standard feedforward CNNs with feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its internal attention through reinforcement learning (RL). The key innovation is using a reinforcement learning policy (evolved via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to modulate the strength of feature maps across multiple passes through the same image, with the goal of correcting misclassifications. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on difficult classification cases.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning - improving classification performance on ambiguous cases without retraining the entire network. The core idea of using feedback connections for selective attention draws reasonable inspiration from human visual processing, as noted in the related work section. The methodology is technically sound, with a clear implementation of the dasNet architecture and a well-justified choice of SNES for policy evolution given the high-dimensional action space. The experimental results are well-presented, with Figure 3 providing a compelling visualization of how the attention mechanism works on a specific misclassified image. The paper also appropriately acknowledges computational costs (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) and limitations of the approach.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited Novelty:** While the paper presents a novel implementation, the core concept of using feedback connections for attention has been explored in prior work. The paper cites "Recurrent processing during object recognition" and "Neural Abstraction Pyramid" but doesn't sufficiently differentiate dasNet from these approaches. For instance, the Neural Abstraction Pyramid (Behnke, 2012) also uses iterative processing with feedback connections for object recognition, though it uses a different learning mechanism. The paper should provide a more detailed comparison to these existing methods.

**2. Superficial Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a theoretical explanation for why this approach works. The mechanism by which feedback connections improve performance is not well understood, and the paper doesn't provide any analysis of when or why the attention mechanism helps (e.g., does it primarily help with specific types of misclassifications?). The paper claims the approach "corrects itself" but doesn't explain the underlying mechanism.

**3. Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are restricted to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which are relatively small datasets. The paper doesn't test on larger, more challenging datasets like ImageNet, where the computational cost of multiple passes would be more prohibitive. The paper also doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond the vanilla CNN baseline. The 6% improvement seems modest compared to recent advances in CNN architectures.

**4. Ambiguous Implementation Details:** While the paper describes the dasNet architecture, some critical implementation details are missing. For example, the observation vector h(Mt) is described as "concatenating the average activation of every output map... and the class probability vector," but the paper doesn't specify how these are normalized or weighted. The exact relationship between the observation space and the action space also needs clarification.

**5. Questionable Practicality:** The paper doesn't adequately address the practical limitations of the approach. Running the network for 5 passes (T=5) increases inference time by approximately 5x, which could be prohibitive for many applications. The paper claims the approach "corrects itself" but doesn't discuss how this would work in real-time applications where speed is critical.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand the Related Work Section:** Provide a more detailed comparison with existing approaches that use feedback connections for attention (e.g., Neural Abstraction Pyramid, Recurrent CNNs). Clarify how dasNet differs and improves upon these methods.

2. **Add Theoretical Analysis:** Include some theoretical analysis of why the feedback mechanism works, perhaps by examining the changes in feature activations during the iterative process. This would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution.

3. **Expand Experimental Validation:** Test the approach on a larger dataset like ImageNet and compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods. Include ablation studies to determine which components of the approach are most important.

4. **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide more specific information about the observation vector, normalization procedures, and the exact relationship between observation and action spaces.

5. **Address Practical Limitations:** Discuss the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost more thoroughly. Consider how the approach might be adapted for real-time applications.

## Final Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound approach that builds on established concepts in deep learning and reinforcement learning. While the 6% improvement on CIFAR-10 is notable, the paper's contribution is limited by its lack of theoretical grounding, limited experimental validation, and insufficient differentiation from prior work. The approach is interesting but not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

**Quality:** 7/10 - The methodology is sound, but the experimental validation is limited and the theoretical justification is weak.

**Clarity:** 8/10 - The paper is well-written but lacks some critical implementation details.

**Originality:** 5/10 - The core idea has been explored before, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work.

**Significance:** 6/10 - The approach addresses a meaningful problem, but the limited scope and modest performance gains reduce its significance.

**Overall Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the contribution is incremental and lacks the novelty and impact expected at NeurIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the theoretical foundation, expand the experimental validation, and more clearly differentiate their approach from prior work to be competitive with recent NeurIPS publications. The paper would be better suited for a venue focused on applied deep learning rather than theoretical or methodological advances.
