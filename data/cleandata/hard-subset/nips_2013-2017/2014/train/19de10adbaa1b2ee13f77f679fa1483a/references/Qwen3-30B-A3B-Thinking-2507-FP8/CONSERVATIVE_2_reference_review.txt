

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which extends standard CNNs by adding feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust filter weights across multiple passes through the same image. The authors claim their method improves classification accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 by 6% relative to vanilla CNNs through a reinforcement learning policy that "corrects" initial misclassifications.

The core idea of using multiple passes to refine classification is conceptually appealing, drawing inspiration from human visual processing where "several feedforward evaluations" are performed for difficult classification tasks. However, the implementation and claims require significant scrutiny.

**Strengths:** The paper acknowledges the computational challenge of learning a policy with "close to a million weights" and justifies using SNES (a variant of Natural Evolution Strategies) rather than standard RL methods. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 are presented with some visualization of filter activations during the iterative process, which is helpful for understanding the method's behavior.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Marginal Performance Gains:** The claimed 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs is modest, especially when compared to recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper fails to compare against more recent architectures like ResNets, EfficientNets, or Vision Transformers that have significantly outperformed traditional CNNs on CIFAR datasets. Without these comparisons, the claimed "state-of-the-art" result is questionable.

2. **Lack of Methodological Justification:** The paper states SNES was chosen "due to the large number of parameters" but provides no evidence that SNES is better suited for this problem than other RL approaches. The authors do not demonstrate why standard RL methods (e.g., PPO, DDPG) would be unsuitable for this task. The justification is insufficient for such a critical design choice.

3. **Computational Cost Not Addressed:** The paper claims "small enough to be computationally tractable" with T=5 steps, but fails to quantify the computational overhead. Training dasNet took "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU" - a very modest GPU by current standards. The paper does not address how this would scale to modern architectures or larger datasets, nor does it compare the inference speed to standard CNNs (which would be significantly slower with multiple passes).

4. **Lack of Proper Baselines:** The paper only compares to "vanilla CNN" and "previous state-of-the-art CNN," but doesn't include comparisons to other attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM) or iterative processing approaches (e.g., recurrent CNNs) that have been shown to improve performance on similar tasks.

5. **Weak Validation of the Core Mechanism:** The authors claim the method "corrects" misclassifications, but the experiments don't sufficiently demonstrate this. For instance, Figure 3 shows one example where the network corrects a misclassification, but doesn't establish whether this happens consistently across the dataset. The paper also fails to show how often the method actually improves classification (vs. the baseline) on difficult cases.

6. **Questionable Novelty:** The concept of using feedback connections for attention in CNNs has been explored in prior work (e.g., the "Neural Abstraction Pyramid" by Behnke, 2013, which uses iterative processing for object recognition). The paper cites this work but doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.

7. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the proposed components (e.g., the feedback connections, the RL policy, or the multiple passes). Without this, it's impossible to determine which elements contribute to the performance gain.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting conceptual idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The marginal performance gain, lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods, insufficient justification for the chosen RL approach, and failure to properly validate the core mechanism all undermine the paper's contribution. The method appears to be adding significant complexity without demonstrable advantage over simpler alternatives.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Comparing against recent SOTA methods (ResNet, EfficientNet, etc.)
2. Providing a detailed analysis of computational costs
3. Including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component
4. Demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple examples, not just one

Given NIPS's high standards for methodological rigor and the lack of convincing evidence for the claimed improvements, I cannot recommend acceptance. The paper would be better suited for a venue focused on more incremental methodological advances rather than a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Summary of Key Concerns:**
- ✘ Marginal improvement (6%) without comparison to recent SOTA
- ✘ Unjustified choice of SNES over standard RL methods
- ✘ No computational cost analysis for inference
- ✘ Weak validation of the core mechanism
- ✘ Insufficient comparison to related work on iterative processing
- ✘ Lack of ablation studies to isolate contributions

The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by evidence, and the methodological approach lacks the rigor expected for NIPS. While the concept is interesting, the current work does not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate its value.
