### **Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that for difficult classification tasks, a single feedforward pass may be insufficient. Inspired by iterative processing in human vision, dasNet processes an input image over multiple passes (T steps). After each pass, a policy network observes the internal state of the CNN (e.g., feature map activations) and outputs an action, which consists of a set of modulatory weights that enhance or suppress the activations of specific feature maps in the next pass.

This control policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL)—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), which is chosen to handle the high-dimensional parameter space of the policy. The training is performed on top of an already trained, high-performing baseline model (a Maxout network). The goal of the RL agent is to learn how to dynamically reconfigure the network's focus to correct initial misclassifications. The authors demonstrate their approach on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showing that dasNet can improve upon the strong baseline and achieve a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10.

This work connects to several lines of research, including recurrent networks for vision, attention mechanisms, and the application of RL to perception. While previous works have used RL to learn spatial attention (e.g., saccades), this paper's novelty lies in learning to modulate the network's *internal* feature representations, which is a compelling and underexplored idea.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The central contribution—using an RL-trained policy to dynamically modulate the internal feature maps of a deep CNN over successive passes—is highly original. It presents a new paradigm for network design that moves beyond static, purely feedforward architectures. The synthesis of ideas from state-of-the-art CNNs, reinforcement learning, and computational neuroscience is creative and thought-provoking.

2.  **Significant Potential Impact:** The concept of iterative refinement for difficult instances has significant potential. If this mechanism can be made more efficient, it could provide a general method for improving the robustness and accuracy of deep networks without simply increasing their size or depth. The paper opens up a new and exciting research direction into dynamic, adaptive computation in neural networks, which could influence how researchers approach challenging recognition problems.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors validate their approach on challenging benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100) using a strong baseline (Maxout network). The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art at the time, is a non-trivial gain and provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed method. The qualitative analysis of a corrected cat/dog misclassification, while preliminary, provides a helpful illustration of the mechanism at work.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem statement is well-posed, and the proposed dasNet architecture is described effectively with both text and diagrams. The connection to biological vision provides a strong and intuitive motivation for the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Expense:** The most significant drawback of the proposed method is its computational cost. The training process involves a lengthy RL phase *after* fully training a large CNN. More importantly, inference requires T forward passes through the network, making it T times slower than the baseline model. This high cost could be a major barrier to practical application. The paper would benefit from a more direct discussion of this limitation.

2.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a thorough investigation of its key design choices. A number of questions remain unanswered:
    *   **State Representation:** How sensitive is the performance to the composition of the state vector `o_t`? Would a simpler state (e.g., only the final class probabilities `v_t`) suffice, or is the high-dimensional internal state essential?
    *   **Action Space:** Is it necessary to modulate all feature maps in all layers? An ablation studying the effect of modulating only the final convolutional layers, for instance, would be very informative and might lead to a more efficient model.
    *   **RL Algorithm:** While SNES is justified by the large parameter space, a comparison or discussion regarding other approaches would be valuable. For example, could a factored policy be trained with standard policy gradient methods? Or could the system be trained end-to-end as a recurrent network using backpropagation through time (BPTT)? A justification for choosing RL over BPTT would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are conducted on the relatively low-resolution CIFAR datasets. The dimensionality of the state and action spaces scales with the number of feature maps in the network. For higher-resolution tasks like ImageNet, state-of-the-art models have many more feature maps, which would make the RL problem substantially harder. The paper does not address how this approach might scale to such tasks.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

-   **Discuss Limitations and Future Work:** I strongly encourage the authors to add a section discussing the limitations, particularly the computational cost and scalability. This could be framed constructively by outlining avenues for future research, such as learning an adaptive number of steps `T` per image, or designing more efficient policy networks.

-   **Strengthen Experimental Analysis:** For a future version of this work, ablation studies like those mentioned in the "Weaknesses" section would significantly strengthen the claims. Understanding the contribution of each component is key to establishing a complete piece of work. An experiment showing that the learned policy outperforms a random modulation policy would also be a simple but powerful baseline.

-   **Deeper Qualitative Analysis:** The visualization in Figure 3 is a good start. To provide deeper insight, it would be interesting to analyze if there are consistent patterns in the policy's behavior. For example, when confusing cats and dogs, are specific "ear-related" or "snout-related" filters consistently modulated across different images? This could help move the analysis from "what changed" to "why it changed."

-   **Clarify Minor Details:** The description of the modified softmax function in Equation 7 is slightly unconventional. A more explicit formulation or justification for why this was chosen over, for example, a scaled sigmoid or tanh activation for the gates would improve clarity for reproducibility.

### **Decision**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely novel and exciting core idea. It successfully demonstrates that learning a dynamic internal attention policy can improve the performance of a state-of-the-art CNN on challenging classification problems. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and empirically sound. While there are valid concerns about computational cost and scalability, the originality of the contribution and its potential to inspire future research are substantial. The weaknesses are primarily in the scope of the experimental analysis rather than fatal flaws in the methodology. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original and conceptually interesting contribution.
*   Opens a new and promising research direction in dynamic, adaptive deep learning.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark over a strong baseline.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Significant computational cost at both training and inference time.
*   Lack of ablation studies to dissect the contribution of key design choices.
*   Unclear scalability to larger-scale problems like ImageNet.
