Summary
The paper proposes dasNet, a Maxout CNN augmented with per-feature-map, multiplicative “gates” that are set adaptively over multiple passes through the same image. A policy network observes summary statistics of internal activations and outputs a new set of gates for the next pass, effectively implementing internal, selective attention. The base CNN is trained supervised; the policy is trained post hoc via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for scalability given ~10^6 policy parameters. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over the vanilla Maxout network, claim robustness on ambiguous cases, and show qualitative examples and some dynamical stability (performance peaks near the trained number of steps T and degrades slowly afterward).

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned among biological evidence for top-down feedback and computational attention/recurrence/RL approaches. The idea of recurrent inference with modulatory top-down signals aligns with longstanding hypotheses in vision and with prior ML work on selective attention and recurrent processing. However, the experimental comparisons are primarily to a vanilla Maxout baseline rather than to other recurrent/attentionized deep models. The paper cites relevant background but many references are incomplete and several closer ML baselines are missing.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clean formulation of internal, feature-wise attention via multiplicative gates controlled by a learned policy. Multi-pass inference is intuitive and aligns with cognitive evidence.
- Modularity: The approach is an add-on to an already trained CNN, leaving base weights unchanged; potentially widely applicable.
- Scalability choice: SNES is a reasonable black-box optimizer for very high-dimensional policies; the diagonal covariance variant is computationally pragmatic.
- Empirical signal: Improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over the base Maxout network, qualitative examples illustrating corrective behavior, and preliminary analysis of dynamical stability (running beyond trained T).
- Observations design: Using average map activations, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities is a sensible, low-cost summary.

Weaknesses
Technical/methodological
- Baselines: Lacks comparisons to other recurrent/attention mechanisms (e.g., unrolled fine-tuned recurrence without RL, simple learned per-channel gates trained end-to-end by backprop, iterative re-evaluation with temperature scaling, ensembles or test-time augmentation). It is unclear whether the gains stem from recurrence per se, gating, or RL vs supervised training of the controller.
- Objective/notation: The loss in Eq. (8)–(10) is ambiguous. Li = −λboost d log(vT): d is later called the correct class, but Eq. (10) tests d = ||vT||∞ (a scalar), which is inconsistent; likely they mean argmax(vT) = d. It should be −λboost log vT[d]. As written, the definition is ill-posed.
- Policy/action mapping: Eq. (7) sets at = dim(A)·softmax(θ o_t), while Algorithm 1 uses a_{t+1} = π(o_t). This off-by-one mismatch recurs elsewhere. Also, scaling softmax by |A| guarantees mean 1 only globally, not per layer; no discussion of per-layer normalization or stability when action magnitudes drift across layers.
- Observation function h(·): Underspecified. Are activations normalized (per-layer/per-map/over batch)? Without scale normalization, gate decisions may be sensitive to global magnitude variations. No ablation on which observation components matter.
- Gating placement: Eq. (5) applies gates after convolution and nonlinearity but before maxout pooling; no rationale vs gating pre-nonlinearity or at other stages; no ablation.
- SNES details: Population size, learning rates, fitness shaping, and stopping criteria are not fully specified; update details relegated to supplementary (not provided). No discussion of sample efficiency vs alternative policy-gradient or actor-critic methods with function approximation.
- Overfitting and variance: The policy is trained on mini-batches drawn from the training set; no clear validation-based early stopping, no multiple seeds, no reported variance/std. Overfitting risk is unaddressed.
- Computational cost: Inference cost scales with T; there’s no analysis of accuracy vs latency trade-off or wall-clock inference overhead. Training time for the controller is given, but not inference throughput.
- Scope of improvement: Claims of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” conflict with earlier statement that data augmentation was used during training. Table 1 (not included) is essential to evaluate the claim; lack of results detail (means, stds, exact test error) hinders assessment.
- CIFAR-100 gate-information analysis: The reported 40.7%/45.74% accuracy from gates alone is claimed to be “similar to dasNet,” which would imply rather low performance vs known CIFAR-100 baselines for Maxout; this conflicts with the “state-of-the-art” narrative elsewhere and needs clarification.

Clarity/presentation
- Notation inconsistencies and typos:
  - Eq. (1) uses i=c∑ i=0 without clear bounds; biases omitted throughout.
  - Eq. (2) lacks braces/limits; indices j·b+i may exceed map count.
  - Eq. (3): ȳℓ_j = max_i F^ℓ_{j·b+i} x⃗ mixes linear projections and max; dimensions unclear; bias terms missing.
  - Eq. (4): v = softmax(F^{ℓ+1} ȳ^ℓ); dimensions unspecified.
  - Eq. (6): vt = Mt(θ,x) but Algorithm 1 calls Mt(θ_i, x_i) with t loop; unclear dependence.
  - Algorithm 1 lines 8–10 mismatch notation (vt, Mt, h(Mt)).
- Many references are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and duplicated (Zeiler & Fergus listed twice, for example). This is below NIPS standards.
- Figures/Tables: Table 1 is referenced but not present; Figure 3 is described at length but not shown; readers cannot verify claims.
- Formatting nits: Nonstandard punctuation, footnote on policy determinism awkwardly interleaved in Algorithm 1, and stray “∗Shared first author.”

Significance/originality
- The idea of multi-pass, top-down gating is interesting and relevant. However, the experimental scope is limited to CIFAR-10/100 with modest improvements over a single baseline, and without comparisons to other recurrent/attentionized alternatives. The reliance on black-box evolution for a massive controller is novel pragmatically but raises questions about efficiency and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Provide complete Table 1 with exact test errors, std across seeds, and baselines including recurrent/fine-tuned multi-pass without RL, and a supervised controller trained with backprop.
- Clarify and correct Eqs. (7)–(11): exact loss, condition for λboost, and action indexing. Include biases and dimensions for all layers.
- Detail h(·) normalization, per-layer action normalization, and ablations on observation components and gating placement.
- Report inference cost vs T, and accuracy-latency trade-offs.
- Address overfitting controls, validation, and variance across runs.
- Justify SNES vs policy gradient/actor-critic; include hyperparameters and update details.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing internal attention mechanism compatible with existing CNNs.
- Empirical improvements on standard benchmarks; qualitative evidence of corrective dynamics.
- Pragmatic high-dimensional controller optimization.

Cons
- Methodological ambiguities, missing details, and notation errors.
- Weak baselines; absent comparisons to alternative recurrent/attention training.
- Incomplete/contradictory reporting and references; missing figures/tables.
- No analysis of computational trade-offs or robustness/variance.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is promising, the accumulation of technical ambiguities, missing baselines, incomplete results, and presentation issues makes it difficult to assess the true contribution and reproducibility. With clearer exposition, stronger experimental comparisons, and thorough reporting, this work could be much stronger.

Confidence
High (familiar with deep CNNs, attention/recurrence, and NES).
