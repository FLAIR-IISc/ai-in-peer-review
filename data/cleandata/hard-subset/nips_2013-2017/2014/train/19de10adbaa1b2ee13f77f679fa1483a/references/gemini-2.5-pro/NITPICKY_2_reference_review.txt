This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for augmenting pre-trained Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with an attention mechanism learned via reinforcement learning (RL). The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple feedforward passes on a single image. Between passes, a learned policy modulates the activations of convolutional feature maps using multiplicative gating connections. This iterative process is motivated by human visual processing, where an initial fast perception is followed by slower, more deliberate analysis for difficult recognition tasks. The policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize a classification loss, with a focus on correcting initially misclassified examples. The authors apply this method to a Maxout network on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, claiming a new state-of-the-art result on unaugmented data.

### Strengths

The paper presents a novel and interesting synthesis of deep learning and reinforcement learning. The central idea of learning a policy to control an internal, iterative "thought" process to refine an initial classification is compelling and well-motivated by cognitive science. Augmenting a pre-existing, trained network without modifying its base weights is a promising direction for model adaptation and correction, potentially offering a more efficient alternative to complete retraining. The problem formulation is original, and the paper connects ideas from neuroscience, RL, and deep learning in a creative way.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is beset by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor formatting issues to significant technical and methodological concerns. The cumulative effect of these issues severely undermines the quality and credibility of the work.

**1. Technical and Notational Imprecision:** The manuscript contains numerous errors and ambiguities that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
*   **Incorrect Indexing:** In Section 2, the summations and max operations for the Maxout network appear to have incorrect bounds. For instance, Equation (2) defines a maxout over `b` maps, but the index is given as `max_{i=0}^b`, which corresponds to `b+1` elements. This should likely be `max_{i=0}^{b-1}`. Similar indexing inconsistencies appear in Equations (1) and (3).
*   **Grave Notational Error:** Equation (10) defines the network's prediction `d` as `d = ||v_T||_inf`. The L-infinity norm gives the maximum value in the probability vector, not the index of that value. The correct notation should be `d = argmax(v_T)`. This is a fundamental error in a core equation of the proposed method.
*   **Ambiguity:** In Equation (8), the notation `log(v_T)` is ambiguous for a vector `v_T`. It should specify the log probability of the correct class, i.e., `log(v_{T,d})`.
*   **Sloppy Formatting:** The "Shared first author" footnote (∗) is attached to the "Introduction" heading rather than to the authors' names. The bibliography is in a state of extreme disrepair; nearly every entry is incomplete, missing conference or journal names, page numbers, and years (e.g., references [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [7], etc.). This is unacceptable for a scientific submission and makes it impossible for a reviewer to properly situate the work.

**2. Methodological Concerns:** Several design choices in the dasNet architecture are not sufficiently justified.
*   **State Representation:** The observation `o_t` for the RL agent is formed by concatenating the *average* activation of every output map, among other features. Averaging discards all spatial information within the feature maps, which seems like a critical loss of information for a vision task. The authors do not justify why this heavily compressed state representation is sufficient.
*   **Attention Mechanism:** The modulatory weight `a_lj` in Equation (5) is applied to the entire output map *after* the convolution and summation over input channels. This is a coarse form of attention that simply scales an entire feature map. More granular approaches, such as modulating individual filter applications before summation, might be more effective but are not explored or discussed.
*   **Policy Network:** The policy `pi_theta` is a simple linear network followed by a non-standard scaled softmax activation (Equation 7). Given the complexity of controlling the activations of a deep network, the choice of a linear policy is limiting and not motivated.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are presented with insufficient rigor.
*   **"State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The paper claims a "new state-of-the-art result" based on a 6% relative improvement. This corresponds to a very small absolute improvement, and the claim is qualified by "for unaugmented data," which is not the standard setting for SOTA claims on these datasets. The crucial Table 1, which contains these results, is referenced but not included in the provided text.
*   **Anecdotal Analysis:** The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is based on a single, cherry-picked example. The authors' own admission that "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect of the alterations" highlights the weakness of this analysis.
*   **Information Content Analysis:** The claim that a k-NN classifier using only the final gate values achieves performance "similar to the performance of dasNet" is extraordinary and requires a more thorough investigation. This result is counterintuitive, as it suggests the policy may have learned to simply encode the final classification decision in its gate values, rather than guiding an iterative refinement process as motivated by the paper. This could be a sign that the learned mechanism is not functioning as intended.
*   **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies. For example, what is the sensitivity to the number of steps `T`? How does the choice of state representation affect performance? How does the simple linear policy compare to a more complex one?

### Summary

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and conceptually interesting approach for improving CNNs via an RL-trained internal attention mechanism.
*   The motivation, drawing parallels to human cognition, is compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is marred by numerous significant notational errors, unclear mathematical formulations, and an exceptionally poorly formatted bibliography, indicating a lack of diligence.
*   Key methodological choices regarding the state representation, attention mechanism, and policy architecture are simplistic and not well-justified.
*   Experimental validation is weak. The central performance claims are modest and not presented in the standard context, and the qualitative and supporting analyses are either anecdotal or raise more questions than they answer.

In its current form, the paper falls well short of the standards for publication at NIPS. While the core idea has merit, the execution, presentation, and experimental validation are deeply flawed. A recommendation for rejection is therefore warranted. The authors should be encouraged to perform a thorough revision to correct the technical errors, justify their methodological choices, and conduct a more rigorous experimental evaluation before considering resubmission.
