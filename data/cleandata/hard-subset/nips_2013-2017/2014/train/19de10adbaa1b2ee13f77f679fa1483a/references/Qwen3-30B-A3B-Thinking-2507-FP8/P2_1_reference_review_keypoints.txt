- Introduces dasNet: augmenting a Maxout CNN with RL-learned gating to modulate filter activations over multiple passes.  
- Uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a policy controlling per-layer filter weights across iterations.  
- Motivated by human iterative perception for ambiguous cases; aims to correct initial misclassifications via internal feedback.  
- Reports a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100.  
- Thorough analysis of attention dynamics showing specific filters are emphasized or suppressed across passes.  
- Evidence that learned gates encode meaningful information (15-NN and logistic regression experiments).  
- Ablation study on number of passes (T) shows performance peaks at T and remains stable beyond.  
- Lacks comprehensive comparison with other attention modules (e.g., SE, CBAM) and RL-based attention methods.  
- High computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) with limited discussion of practical deployment implications.  
- Limited theoretical explanation for why the approach is effective beyond empirical demonstration.  
- Does not explore more complex attention patterns like spatial attention—focuses on simple filter weighting.  
- Well-written and organized; Algorithm 1 and Figure 3 effectively illustrate the method and dynamics.  
- SNES and some mathematical notations (e.g., observation vector) need clearer explanation for unfamiliar readers.  
- Would benefit from an explicit discussion of limitations and potential failure cases.  
- Novel contribution: combines internal feedback with RL to enable iterative attention-like processing in deep nets.  
- Distinction from recurrent image-classification approaches and other attention work could be articulated more clearly.  
- Significant benchmark improvement on CIFAR datasets, demonstrating correction of ambiguous misclassifications.  
- Unclear scalability to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and integration with existing state-of-the-art pipelines.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, conceptually interesting, and experimentally convincing despite noted weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: accept—novel, well-executed work that advances attention mechanisms via feedback and RL.
