

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that allows deep CNNs to iteratively refine their classification through feedback connections and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to enable networks to "think" about an image multiple times, with each pass allowing the network to selectively emphasize or suppress specific features through learned attention mechanisms. This is achieved by augmenting a standard Maxout network with a reinforcement learning policy that modulates filter weights across successive passes through the network, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle the large parameter space.

The paper makes a compelling conceptual contribution by connecting neural network design to human visual processing, where experts often require multiple evaluations to distinguish similar objects. The authors convincingly argue that this sequential processing approach could improve classification performance on ambiguous cases, which aligns with human cognitive processing patterns. The paper distinguishes itself from related work on attention mechanisms (e.g., SENet, CBAM) by focusing on internal selective attention through feedback connections rather than channel or spatial attention, and from recurrent networks by using reinforcement learning to learn the attention policy rather than fixed recurrent connections.

The most significant strength of this paper is its bold conceptual novelty. While attention mechanisms and recurrent networks have been explored in computer vision, dasNet's specific approach of learning a feedback policy through reinforcement learning to modulate internal features during multiple passes represents a genuinely new direction. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach can achieve a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 (6% relative improvement) without disrupting previously learned knowledge.

The paper's methodology is sound, with appropriate use of SNES to handle the large parameter space required for controlling attention in a deep network. The experimental design is well-considered, with careful attention to evaluating the network's behavior on ambiguous classification cases. The visualizations of attention changes across layers (Figure 3) provide compelling evidence of the mechanism's operation, showing how lower layers focus on different features while higher layers make more complex adjustments.

The main weakness is the lack of comparison with attention mechanisms that have recently become standard in CNNs (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM). While the authors acknowledge related work, a direct comparison with these approaches would strengthen the significance claim. Additionally, the computational cost of training dasNet (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) might limit its practical applicability, though this is a common trade-off for performance gains.

I am particularly impressed by the paper's conceptual framework, which bridges neuroscience (human visual processing) with deep learning in a meaningful way. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but actually improves performance on standard benchmarks. The paper's claim that networks can "correct themselves" through multiple passes is well-supported by the experimental results.

The paper also makes a strong case for the biological plausibility of the approach, citing extensive work on cortical connectivity and visual processing. This interdisciplinary perspective adds significant depth to the contribution.

While the paper could benefit from more detailed comparison with recent attention mechanisms and a more thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs, these are minor issues relative to the paper's conceptual novelty. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find the core contribution to be highly significant and worthy of acceptance.

The paper's main contribution is not a small incremental improvement but a new direction in how we think about deep network processing - one that allows networks to engage in internal "thought" processes similar to humans. This represents a significant step toward more human-like visual processing in artificial systems.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a bold, novel approach that advances the field in a meaningful way, demonstrates clear empirical benefits, and opens up new avenues for research in deep learning. The conceptual contribution alone makes this paper worthy of publication at NIPS, even if some methodological details could be improved. The authors have successfully shown that networks can "think" about an image multiple times to improve classification accuracy, which is a fundamentally new perspective on deep network design.
