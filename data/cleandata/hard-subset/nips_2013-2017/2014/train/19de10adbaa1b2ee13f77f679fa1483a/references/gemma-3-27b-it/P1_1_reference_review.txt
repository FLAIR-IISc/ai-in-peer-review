## Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances deep convolutional neural networks with internal selective attention mechanisms driven by reinforcement learning. The core idea is to allow each layer of a Maxout CNN to influence all others through learned feedback connections, effectively modulating filter activations on successive passes over an image.  A Separable Natural Evolution Strategy (SNES) is used to learn a policy for modulating these connections, aiming to correct misclassifications and improve performance on difficult instances. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging cases where initial predictions are incorrect.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the established success of deep CNNs (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and Maxout networks (Goodfellow, 2013). It draws inspiration from biological visual systems, acknowledging the importance of feedback connections and iterative processing (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). The use of reinforcement learning to control network behavior aligns with a growing body of research exploring attention mechanisms (Schmidhuber, 2015) and learning to attend (Denil, 2017; O'Reilly, 2006).  However, the authors differentiate themselves by applying this RL approach directly to the internal activations of a state-of-the-art CNN, rather than focusing on simpler datasets or external attention mechanisms.  They also appropriately highlight the challenges of applying traditional RL methods to large-scale CNNs, justifying their choice of SNES.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning internal attentional policies to refine CNN activations *post-training* is a significant contribution.  This moves beyond fixed feature extractors and introduces a dynamic element to deep learning.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 establishes a new state-of-the-art result, demonstrating the effectiveness of dasNet.  The inclusion of an example showing the network correcting a misclassification (cat vs. dog) provides compelling qualitative evidence.
* **Justification for SNES:** The authors clearly articulate why SNES was chosen over traditional RL methods due to the high dimensionality of the policy space.  This is a crucial point, addressing a potential criticism.
* **Insightful Analysis:**  The analysis of filter activation changes (Figure 3) reveals that the network is not simply amplifying all activations, but rather learning complex suppression and enhancement patterns. This hints at a deeper understanding of the learned attention mechanism.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of Maxout networks and SNES is sufficient for readers unfamiliar with those concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The training time of 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU is substantial. While the authors acknowledge this, the practical limitations of this approach need more explicit discussion. It raises questions about scalability to larger datasets and more complex architectures.
* **Limited Dynamics Exploration:** While the authors explore the dynamics of the network with varying T values, the analysis is somewhat limited. A more thorough investigation of the learned policy's convergence behavior and sensitivity to different hyperparameters would be beneficial.
* **Action Space Design:** The choice of using a softmax function to scale the action vector to average to 1 is somewhat arbitrary.  A more detailed justification for this specific design choice, and an exploration of alternative scaling methods, would strengthen the paper.
* **Observation Space:** The choice of observation features (average activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities) is somewhat ad-hoc. While reasonable, it is unclear if these are the *optimal* features for the policy to learn from.  Exploring different observation spaces could potentially lead to further improvements.
* **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.  Demonstrating the effectiveness of dasNet on more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The authors adequately address potential criticisms and provide a convincing justification for their design choices.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of deep learning by introducing a dynamic, adaptive element to CNNs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high computational cost raises concerns about scalability.
* The analysis of the learned policy dynamics is somewhat limited.
* The generalizability of the results to more complex datasets remains unproven.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to improving CNN performance.  The authors have addressed a challenging problem and demonstrated a clear advantage over existing methods. While there are some weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with the following minor revisions:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability limitations.
2.  Expand the analysis of the learned policy dynamics, including a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.
3.  Consider including a brief discussion of alternative action space scaling methods.
4. Briefly discuss the potential benefits and challenges of applying dasNet to more complex datasets like ImageNet.



**Confidence:** 8/10
