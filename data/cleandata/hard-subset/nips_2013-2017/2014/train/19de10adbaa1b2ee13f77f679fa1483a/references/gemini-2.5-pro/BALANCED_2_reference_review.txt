This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can perform a series of subsequent "thoughts" or passes over the same input image. In each subsequent pass, a learned policy modulates the activations of the convolutional filters, effectively enhancing or suppressing certain features to improve classification, particularly for difficult or ambiguous examples.

The proposed model builds upon a pre-trained Maxout network, a state-of-the-art CNN architecture. The modulatory policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL), where the state is derived from the network's internal activations and the action corresponds to a vector of weights applied to the convolutional feature maps. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over 1 million), the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization, framing the problem as black-box optimization rather than using traditional RL algorithms. The reward function is based on the classification loss at the final pass, with a boosting mechanism to prioritize correcting misclassified examples. This work is motivated by the human visual system, which is known to employ recurrent and top-down connections for deliberative processing beyond an initial fast, feedforward sweep. The authors position their work as a practical implementation of this concept on a state-of-the-art vision model, in contrast to prior related work in RL for attention which often used simpler models or tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—a mechanism for a pre-trained CNN to iteratively refine its own internal representations using an RL-trained feedback policy—is highly novel and significant. It presents a principled approach to move beyond the static, single-pass paradigm of most deep learning models. This concept of enabling a network to "think longer" on difficult inputs has the potential to be very impactful, opening up new research directions in areas like model introspection, robustness, and computational efficiency (by allocating more compute to harder problems).

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of dasNet by achieving a new state-of-the-art result on the challenging CIFAR-100 dataset (at the time of publication). The reported 6% relative improvement over a strong Maxout network baseline is a compelling validation of the proposed method. This is not just a proof-of-concept on a toy problem but a tangible improvement on a standard benchmark.

3.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the problem is clear and technically sound. Using a pre-trained network as a base and learning the modulatory policy on top is a sensible strategy. The choice of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy parameter space is well-justified and pragmatic. The design of the state representation (concatenating various network activations) and the action space (modulatory gates on feature maps) is intuitive and well-explained.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is described in sufficient detail with the help of Algorithm 1 and Figure 1, and the experiments are presented logically. The authors also make a good effort to analyze the model's behavior through the qualitative example (Fig. 3) and the dynamics/information content analyses.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary weakness of the proposed method is its significant computational expense. Training requires many generations of SNES, where each candidate policy is evaluated on multiple images, and each image evaluation involves T+1 forward passes through the network. The authors report that training the policy took 4 days, which is substantial. A crucial missing comparison is how dasNet compares to simply training a larger feedforward model with a comparable computational budget. It is possible that the performance gains could be matched by a wider or deeper network that requires the same amount of training FLOPs. Adding such a baseline would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Missing Experimental Details:** For reproducibility, several key details are missing.
    *   The performance of the authors' specific baseline Maxout network *before* applying dasNet (i.e., at T=0) is not explicitly reported in Table 1 for CIFAR-100. Stating this would make the exact improvement clearer.
    *   The construction of the observation vector `o_t` is described conceptually but lacks specifics. What are the precise dimensions? Are the components (average activations, intermediate activations, class probabilities) normalized or pre-processed before concatenation?
    *   The SNES hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, variance schedule, number of generations) are not fully specified, with some details deferred to supplementary material which may not be available to all reviewers.

3.  **Depth of Analysis:** While the attempts to analyze the learned policy are commendable, they could be more rigorous.
    *   The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is illustrative but, as the authors note, difficult to interpret conclusively. A more quantitative analysis would be beneficial. For example, does the policy learn consistent strategies for specific class confusions (e.g., consistently suppressing fur-like textures when distinguishing cats from dogs)?
    *   The choice of T=5 seems arbitrary. How sensitive is the performance to this hyperparameter? The dynamics analysis in Figure 2 is for models trained with T=1 and T=2. It would be more relevant to show this analysis for the T=5 model used for the main results to see if performance continues to degrade past T=5.

4.  **Relation to Recurrent Models:** The related work section is good but could be strengthened by discussing and distinguishing the proposed approach from other forms of recurrent neural networks used in vision, such as those that use recurrence over time for video or those that use iterative refinement for tasks like image generation or reconstruction. This would help to better situate the specific contribution of dasNet within the broader landscape of recurrent computation.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pro:**
*   Highly novel and conceptually interesting idea of adding RL-based iterative refinement to a CNN.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-100).
*   The overall technical approach is sound, and the use of SNES is well-motivated.
*   The paper is clearly written and presented.

**Con:**
*   Very high computational cost for both training and inference.
*   Lacks a key baseline comparison against a larger feedforward network with a similar training budget.
*   Several experimental details required for full reproducibility are missing.
*   The analysis of the learned policy could be more in-depth and quantitative.

### Decision

This paper presents a novel, exciting, and well-executed idea that pushes the boundaries of standard deep learning architectures. The strong empirical results validate the approach and suggest that this is a promising direction for future research. While there are weaknesses concerning computational cost, missing baselines, and some lack of experimental detail, the core contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. The suggested improvements, particularly the inclusion of a compute-matched baseline, would substantially strengthen the paper for its final version.
