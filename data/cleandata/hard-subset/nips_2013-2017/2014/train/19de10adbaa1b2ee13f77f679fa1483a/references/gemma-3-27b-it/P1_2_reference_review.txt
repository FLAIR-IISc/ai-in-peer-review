## Peer Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep convolutional neural networks. The idea of learning to selectively modulate filter activations *after* initial supervised training using reinforcement learning is novel and shows promising results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100. However, the complexity of the system and the limited scope of the experiments warrant a careful evaluation. I believe the paper is a solid contribution, but with some caveats, and recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which aim to mimic the iterative, attentive processing observed in human vision. Unlike standard CNNs, dasNet allows for feedback connections between layers, enabling filter activations to be modulated on successive passes through the network. This modulation is controlled by a policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES), guiding the network to focus on the most discriminative features for challenging instances. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of deep learning, referencing key advancements in CNNs (LeCun, Krizhevsky et al.), maxout networks (Goodfellow et al.), and attention mechanisms. They also relate their work to neuroscientific findings on visual processing (Felleman & van Essen, Lamme & Roelfsema), emphasizing the biological plausibility of iterative feedback. The paper appropriately acknowledges prior work on applying RL to vision (Whitehead, Denil et al.), differentiating their approach by applying it to already state-of-the-art CNNs rather than simplified datasets.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning a post-training attentional policy to modulate internal filter activations is a unique contribution. This differs from existing attention mechanisms that typically operate on input features or layer outputs.
* **Performance:**  The paper demonstrates a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art result. This provides strong empirical evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Biological Plausibility:** The iterative refinement process and the incorporation of feedback connections are inspired by neuroscientific understanding of visual processing, adding a layer of interpretability.
* **Clear Motivation:** The authors provide a compelling argument for the need for iterative processing, drawing parallels to human visual perception and highlighting the limitations of purely feedforward CNNs.
* **Detailed Implementation:** The paper provides a relatively comprehensive description of the dasNet architecture, the RL training process (SNES), and the experimental setup.
* **Analysis of Dynamics:** The investigation into the dynamics of the attention mechanism with varying T values provides valuable insights into the behavior of the system.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The training process (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is computationally expensive, which could limit the scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex networks.  While SNES is used to mitigate the parameter space issue, it remains a significant constraint.
* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** The experiments are primarily focused on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. It is unclear how well dasNet would generalize to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., ImageNet).
* **Policy Interpretability:** While the paper visualizes filter activation changes, understanding *why* the learned policy selects certain actions remains challenging. The analysis of the gates is a good start, but more investigation into the learned policy is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper states that the number of steps (T) was experimentally determined, but doesn’t offer much insight into the sensitivity of performance to this parameter. A more thorough hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the results.
* **SNES Justification:** While SNES is a reasonable choice given the parameter space, the authors could more thoroughly justify why it was preferred over other RL algorithms. A brief discussion of why standard policy gradient methods are impractical would be helpful.



**4. Evaluation Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the experimental results are well-supported. The use of SNES is justified, and the method demonstrates a clear improvement in performance. However, the computational cost and limited generalization analysis raise concerns.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and results. However, some sections (e.g., the detailed explanation of SNES) could be simplified for a broader audience.
* **Originality:** The approach is highly original, representing a novel application of reinforcement learning to internal CNN activations. 
* **Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating a state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark. The potential for improving the robustness and interpretability of CNNs makes this work relevant to a broad range of applications.

**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to improving CNN performance.
* Achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100.
* The biological inspiration adds an interesting perspective.
* Thoroughly described methodology and experimental setup.

**Against Acceptance:**

* High computational cost.
* Limited generalization analysis.
* Difficulty in interpreting the learned policy.
* Lack of a comprehensive hyperparameter analysis.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions.** Specifically, I suggest the following:

* **Expand the discussion of computational cost and scalability.** Consider exploring potential optimizations or alternative RL algorithms.
* **Provide a brief analysis of the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., T).**
* **Include a more in-depth discussion of the learned policy.**  Attempt to extract meaningful insights into the types of features the network is focusing on.
* **Address the potential for generalization to other datasets in the discussion section.**



This is a promising piece of work that has the potential to significantly impact the field of deep learning. Addressing the minor revisions outlined above will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
