

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes DasNet, a framework that enhances deep CNNs by introducing feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively focus attention on discriminative features through reinforcement learning. The core idea is to learn a policy that modulates filter weights across multiple passes over the same image, with the goal of correcting misclassifications in difficult cases. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture and evolve the attention policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES).

**Strengths:**
The paper presents an interesting idea that connects neuroscience-inspired recurrent processing with reinforcement learning for improving CNN performance. The concept of "internal selective attention" through feedback connections is novel in the context of existing CNN architectures. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 showing improved performance (6% relative improvement) over vanilla CNNs is promising, and the qualitative analysis of filter activation changes provides some insight into the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies in Architecture Description:** The paper conflates Maxout layers with max pooling throughout. Section 2 describes a "Maxout pooling layer" that reduces maps by taking the maximum across maps (Equation 2), which is actually standard max pooling, not Maxout. Maxout layers compute max over multiple linear functions (not across channels), and the classification layer description (Equation 3) appears to describe max pooling rather than Maxout. This fundamental confusion undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

2. **Notational and Methodological Errors:** 
   - Equation (2) has a critical indexing error: "b max i=0" should be "max_{i=0}^{b-1}".
   - Equation (5) introduces "aℓj" without defining its dimensionality or how it's applied.
   - The λboost definition (Equation 10) is confusing: "d = ∥vT ∥∞" should be a binary indicator (correct/incorrect classification), not the norm of the probability vector.
   - Algorithm 1's loop "for t = 0 → T do" suggests T+1 passes, but the paper describes T passes.

3. **Lack of Proper Baseline Comparison:** The claim of "new state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated. The paper doesn't compare with other recent state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100 (e.g., ResNets, DenseNets), making it impossible to assess whether the 6% improvement is meaningful in the current landscape.

4. **Insufficient Justification for SNES:** The paper states SNES was chosen "due to the large number of parameters (over 1 million)" but provides no evidence that other RL methods (e.g., PPO, SAC) would be infeasible. The reference [19] cited for SNES is actually about "High dimensions and heavy tails for natural evolution strategies," not SNES specifically.

5. **Missing Critical Analysis:** The paper claims "dasNet must aim at fixing these erroneous predictions without disrupting, or forgetting, what has been learned" but provides no evidence or analysis of catastrophic forgetting. The "dynamics" section (Figure 2) shows performance peaks at T steps but doesn't analyze how this affects the original model's performance on correctly classified examples.

6. **Formatting and Reference Issues:** Several references lack critical information (year, page numbers), and the acknowledgment contains a grammatical error ("included in the Related Work section" should be "which was included"). The paper inconsistently capitalizes "Maxout."

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of using RL to modulate internal attention in CNNs is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from multiple technical inaccuracies, methodological weaknesses, and insufficient comparative analysis. The fundamental confusion between Maxout and max pooling undermines the technical foundation of the approach, and the lack of proper baselines makes it impossible to assess the significance of the claimed improvements. The paper also fails to adequately address the computational cost of the approach (the 4-day training time for dasNet is not contextualized against standard CNN training times).

The paper's claims of "new state-of-the-art" performance on CIFAR-10/100 are unsubstantiated without comparison to current SOTA methods. The quality of the technical contribution is diminished by the numerous notational errors, inconsistent terminology, and unclear methodological justification.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper presents an interesting concept but fails to deliver a technically sound, well-executed, and properly contextualized contribution. The numerous technical errors and lack of proper baselines prevent the reviewers from evaluating the true merit of the approach. The paper would need substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
