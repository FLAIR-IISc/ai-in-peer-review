

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper proposes "dasNet," a method that introduces selective attention to CNNs through feedback connections controlled by a policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies). The authors claim that their approach allows the network to iteratively focus attention on different features across multiple passes through the same image, correcting misclassifications made in the initial feedforward pass. They report a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.

**Summary of Main Ideas**
The paper builds on Maxout networks and adds attention gates that can be adjusted on successive passes through the network. These gates are controlled by a policy learned via SNES, which the authors claim is necessary due to the high dimensionality of the action space. The network makes an initial classification, then iteratively refines its attention before making a final decision. The authors argue this mimics human visual processing where multiple feedforward evaluations may be needed for difficult classification tasks.

**Strengths**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work in neuroscience and computer vision. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 are presented with some qualitative examples showing how the network's attention changes across iterations. The implementation details of dasNet are reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

1. **Lack of rigorous baseline comparisons:** The paper claims a "new state-of-the-art result" but fails to compare against recent state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100. The Maxout network used as the base model is already outdated (paper cites Goodfellow 2013, while current SOTA uses more modern architectures like ResNets). Without comparing against more recent baselines, the claimed improvement lacks significance. For instance, the paper doesn't compare against ResNet-50 or DenseNet, which would have achieved much better performance on these datasets.

2. **Unjustified methodological choice:** The authors claim SNES is necessary due to the "large number of parameters (over 1 million)" but provide no evidence that other RL methods would be infeasible. Standard RL methods like PPO or DDPG have been successfully applied to high-dimensional action spaces in other domains. The paper fails to justify why SNES was chosen over more established RL approaches, making the methodological choice seem arbitrary.

3. **Limited experimental validation:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR-10/100 with only T=5 iterations. The paper doesn't explore how dasNet would perform on other datasets or with different numbers of iterations. The "dynamics" experiment only tests T=1 and T=2, which doesn't provide evidence that the approach generalizes. The paper also doesn't investigate the computational cost of the iterative process, which could be prohibitive for real-world applications.

4. **Lack of ablation studies:** There's no ablation study to determine which components contribute to the performance improvement. For example, is the improvement due to the attention mechanism itself, the RL policy learning, or the specific SNES implementation? Without this, it's impossible to determine what aspects of the approach are actually valuable.

5. **Questionable theoretical justification:** The paper cites neuroscience literature to support the use of feedback connections but fails to clearly articulate how dasNet's mechanism differs from existing attention mechanisms in neural networks (e.g., transformers, or attention modules in CNNs). The connection to human visual processing is speculative rather than evidence-based.

6. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims dasNet "corrects itself by emphasising and de-emphasising certain filters" but provides no evidence that these changes are actually improving the network's performance on the classification task. The qualitative examples show attention changes but don't demonstrate that these changes directly cause the improvement.

**Recommendation**

I recommend rejection. While the idea of using iterative refinement in CNNs is not entirely novel, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The methodological choices lack justification, the experimental validation is limited and incomplete, and the comparison to established baselines is inadequate. The claimed 6% improvement on CIFAR-10 is modest at best, and without proper context against current state-of-the-art methods, it doesn't represent a meaningful advancement. The paper presents an interesting concept but falls short of the rigorous validation required to demonstrate a significant contribution to the field.

The authors should consider:
1. Comparing against more recent state-of-the-art models on CIFAR-10/100
2. Providing a thorough ablation study to identify which components contribute to the improvement
3. Justifying their choice of SNES over other RL methods
4. Exploring the computational cost and scalability of their approach
5. Providing more evidence that the attention changes directly improve classification performance rather than being correlated with it

Without these improvements, the paper does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
