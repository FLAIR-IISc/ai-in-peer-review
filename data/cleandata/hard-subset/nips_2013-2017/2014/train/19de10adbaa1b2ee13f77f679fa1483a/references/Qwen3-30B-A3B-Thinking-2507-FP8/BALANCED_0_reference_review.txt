

**Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard CNNs with feedback connections that enable internal selective attention through reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method where a pre-trained Maxout network can iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image, with filter weights adjusted by a policy learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The goal is to correct misclassifications by emphasizing/de-emphasizing specific filters, with experiments demonstrating a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/100.

**Strengths:**
1. **Conceptual Contribution**: The paper makes a compelling connection between biological visual processing (with top-down feedback connections) and neural network design. The motivation for "internal selective attention" aligns well with neuroscientific evidence about visual cortex organization and attention mechanisms.

2. **Technical Execution**: The implementation of dasNet is well-executed, with appropriate use of SNES to handle the large parameter space of the attention policy. The ablation study on the number of iterations (T) demonstrates stable behavior beyond the training horizon.

3. **Empirical Results**: The CIFAR-10/100 results are convincing for the specific task of correcting misclassifications. The visualizations in Figure 3 provide valuable insights into how attention changes across network layers during iterative refinement, particularly showing how higher layers (closest to classification) are most affected.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper evaluates dasNet only on Maxout networks (which are now outdated for CIFAR-10/100), not on current state-of-the-art architectures like ResNets or EfficientNets. The 6% improvement over vanilla Maxout networks is modest compared to recent advances on these datasets (e.g., ResNet achieves ~94% on CIFAR-10). Without comparison to modern architectures, it's unclear whether dasNet provides meaningful gains for current practice.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Analysis**: While the method is empirically validated, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why this approach works or under what conditions it might fail. The connection between the RL policy and the biological visual system is conceptual but not rigorously analyzed.

3. **Comparison to Related Work**: The related work section doesn't sufficiently distinguish dasNet from prior recurrent vision approaches (e.g., [48-50] on iterative processing). The claim that dasNet is the first to apply this to "state-of-the-art" CNNs is misleading, as the evaluation uses Maxout networks, which are no longer state-of-the-art.

4. **Computational Cost**: The method requires 4 days of training on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, which is significantly more expensive than standard CNN training. The paper doesn't adequately justify this cost with the modest performance gains, especially for applications with tight computational constraints.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Extend experiments to more recent architectures (e.g., ResNet-50) on CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. Include theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of dasNet's training process and the conditions under which it provides benefits.

3. Improve the related work section to clearly differentiate dasNet from prior recurrent vision methods and clarify its relationship to "attention" mechanisms in transformers.

4. Provide a more detailed discussion of computational trade-offs, including inference time costs and scalability to larger networks.

**Conclusion:**
The paper presents an interesting conceptual approach to incorporating selective attention in deep networks through feedback connections. While the empirical results on CIFAR-10/100 are promising for the specific experimental setup, the limited scope of evaluation (using outdated architectures) and lack of theoretical analysis prevent the paper from making a strong enough contribution for NIPS. The work would benefit significantly from extending experiments to current state-of-the-art architectures and providing deeper theoretical insights.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- *Pro*: Conceptually interesting connection to neuroscience, well-executed experiments on a specific task, clear visualizations of attention mechanisms.
- *Con*: Limited evaluation scope (outdated architecture), insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art, lack of theoretical analysis, high computational cost.

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend rejection. However, with the suggested revisions (extending evaluation to modern architectures, adding theoretical analysis, and improving related work comparison), this could become a strong contribution to NIPS. The core idea of using RL to learn internal attention has potential, but the current work doesn't sufficiently demonstrate its significance for contemporary deep learning practice.
