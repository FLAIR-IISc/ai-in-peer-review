**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard, pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) with a dynamic, internal attention mechanism. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "thoughts" or passes on the same input image. During these subsequent passes, a control policy, learned via reinforcement learning, modulates the activations of the network's convolutional filters through feedback connections. This allows the network to selectively enhance or suppress features to correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples. The control policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to manage the high-dimensional action space of modulating thousands of filters. The authors demonstrate their method on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing improved performance over a strong Maxout network baseline, which they claim establishes a new state-of-the-art result.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and the boldness of its core concept. It fundamentally challenges the static, one-shot inference paradigm of conventional deep networks. The idea of a network that can "re-think" an input by internally modulating its own features is a significant conceptual leap. This is not an incremental improvement but rather the introduction of a new, biologically-inspired computational model for deep learning. The connection to the two-stage model of human vision (a fast, pre-attentive feedforward pass followed by a slower, attentional phase) provides a powerful motivation. This work has the potential to open up a new and exciting research direction focused on dynamic, iterative, and adaptive inference.

*   **Bold Technical Approach:** The authors tackle a very difficult problem: learning a control policy over a vast parameter space (modulating every filter map). Instead of being deterred by the unsuitability of standard RL algorithms, they creatively employ a black-box optimization method (SNES), demonstrating a pragmatic and effective solution to a challenging technical hurdle. This willingness to combine techniques from different subfields (deep learning, RL, evolutionary strategies) is commendable.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the training procedure, and Figure 1 effectively illustrates the iterative process. The motivation and connection to related work in neuroscience are well-articulated.

*   **Promising Initial Results:** Despite the limited experimental scope, the results are compelling. The ability of dasNet to correct the mistakes of an already powerful, fully-trained network on ambiguous cases (e.g., the cat vs. dog example) provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the approach. The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-100 over a strong baseline is a non-trivial gain and validates that the complex mechanism introduced is not just an interesting idea but one with practical benefits.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are confined to the relatively small CIFAR-10/100 datasets. While the computational cost is noted as a limiting factor, the true power and scalability of this approach remain an open question. Demonstrating its effectiveness on a more complex, large-scale dataset like ImageNet would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

*   **Simplicity of the Control Mechanism:** The state representation (concatenated average activations) is a coarse summary of the network's internal state, and the policy is a simple linear transformation. While this serves as an effective proof-of-concept, it is likely a bottleneck. A more sophisticated policy and state representation (perhaps using recurrent networks or graph-based methods to model the network's state) could unlock even greater performance.

*   **Analysis is Primarily Qualitative:** The visualization of filter changes in Figure 3 is intriguing but lacks quantitative depth. The authors rightly note that visualizing high-level features is an open problem, but the analysis of *why* certain filters are emphasized remains speculative. The experiment to show the information content of the final gate values is a good step, but further analysis to connect specific modulations to specific image properties would make the work more complete.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a quintessential NIPS paper. It presents a bold, highly original idea that pushes the boundaries of how we think about inference in deep networks. While the experimental validation is not exhaustive and the methodology could be extended in future work, these are acceptable limitations for a paper that introduces a fundamentally new direction. The strengths—particularly the novelty and significance of the core concept—far outweigh the weaknesses. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation on larger datasets because the paper successfully demonstrates the viability of a new and exciting paradigm. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on adaptive computation, recurrent processing, and the intersection of deep learning and reinforcement learning for internal network control.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** Proposes a new paradigm of dynamic, multi-pass inference with internal attention.
2.  **Significant Concept:** Addresses a key limitation of static feedforward models and draws compelling parallels to human cognition.
3.  **Creative Methodology:** Successfully combines state-of-the-art CNNs with RL and evolutionary strategies to solve a difficult control problem.
4.  **Strong Proof-of-Concept:** Demonstrates clear performance improvements on challenging benchmarks, validating the core idea.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scale:** Experiments are only on CIFAR, not on larger-scale problems like ImageNet.
2.  **Preliminary Analysis:** The analysis of what the learned policy is actually doing is more qualitative than quantitative.
3.  **Simple Policy/State:** The implementation of the policy and state representation is a first pass and may not be optimal.
