## Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing deep convolutional neural networks with internal selective attention mechanisms.  The core idea is to allow feedback connections between layers, modulating filter activations post-training via a reinforcement learning (RL) policy. This allows the network to iteratively refine its classifications, particularly for challenging instances, without altering the initial supervised learning weights. The authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to train the attentional policy, due to the high dimensionality of the action space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate performance improvements, especially on difficult examples, outperforming a baseline CNN.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in deep learning, specifically convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and attention mechanisms. It correctly situates itself within the context of advancements in CNN architectures (Maxout networks, dropout), supervised learning with backpropagation, and the use of RL for improving network performance. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work in recurrent processing for image understanding and RL-based attention mechanisms, highlighting that their approach differs by applying this to state-of-the-art CNNs and demonstrating performance gains.  The connection to human visual processing, specifically the need for multiple passes over an image for complex classification (e.g., bird species identification) is a compelling motivation.  The paper also acknowledges biologically plausible recurrent connections in the visual cortex as a relevant inspiration.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of applying RL to modulate filter activations *within* a pre-trained CNN through feedback connections is a novel contribution. It moves beyond simply learning attention weights for layers or features and proposes a dynamic adjustment of the network's internal representation during inference.
* **Practicality:** The use of SNES addresses a key challenge: the high dimensionality of the policy space.  While more sophisticated RL algorithms might be theoretically preferable, SNES provides a viable solution for training complex attentional policies.
* **Empirical Results:** The performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on ambiguous cases, are promising. The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provides some intuition into how the network is adapting its attention. The gate information content analysis further supports the effectiveness of the learned policy.
* **Clear Motivation:** The connection to human visual perception and the limitations of purely feedforward networks are clearly articulated, providing a compelling rationale for the proposed approach.
* **Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, explaining complex concepts in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of the Policy:**  While the authors demonstrate that the policy *works*, there is limited analysis of *how* it works.  What general patterns emerge in the learned attention strategies?  Are certain filters consistently emphasized or suppressed for particular classes?  A more thorough analysis of the policy's behavior would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** The 4-day training time (excluding initial CNN training) for dasNet is significant.  This raises concerns about scalability to larger datasets and more complex networks.  The paper doesn’t thoroughly address this issue.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters like T (number of passes), λboost, and λL2 seems empirically determined. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Generalization:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. Demonstrating the effectiveness of dasNet on more complex, real-world datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is crucial for establishing its broader applicability.
* **Black Box Nature:** Although the visualization of activation changes is helpful, it remains difficult to fully understand the reasoning behind the network’s attentional shifts. This black-box nature limits interpretability and hinders the ability to diagnose potential issues.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and provides empirical evidence to support its claims. The use of SNES is a reasonable compromise given the problem's complexity. The experimental setup is well-defined.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The concepts are explained clearly, and the organization is logical.
* **Originality (8/10):** The application of RL to modulate internal filter activations is a novel idea, building upon existing work in a creative way.
* **Significance (7/10):** The results are promising, but the significance is somewhat limited by the datasets used and the computational cost.  Demonstrating scalability and generalization to more complex problems would significantly enhance the impact of this work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to enhancing CNNs with internal attention.
* Empirically demonstrates performance improvements on challenging datasets.
* Addresses a practical challenge (high-dimensional policy space) with SNES.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost is significant and may limit scalability.
* Limited analysis of the learned policy.
* Results are currently limited to relatively small datasets.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly addressed.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This paper presents a novel and promising approach to improving deep CNNs. While there are some weaknesses regarding computational cost, policy analysis, and generalization, the strengths outweigh the concerns. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the following revisions:

1. **Expand the policy analysis:** Provide a more in-depth investigation of the learned attentional strategies. Identify common patterns and provide insights into how the network is adapting its filters.
2. **Discuss Scalability:** Address the computational cost more directly. Explore potential optimizations or approximations to reduce the training time.
3. **Sensitivity Analysis:** Include a brief sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters.
4. **Future Work:** Explicitly outline plans for evaluating dasNet on more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet).
