Summary
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension of CNNs that implements internal, selective attention through multiplicative gates applied to feature maps across layers. At test time, an image is processed for T iterations. After each feedforward pass, a policy observes summary statistics of intermediate activations and the current class probabilities, and outputs a vector of per-feature-map gains that modulate the next pass. The base CNN (a Maxout network with dropout) is trained in the standard supervised way; the attention policy is then trained with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted cross-entropy loss that emphasizes misclassified examples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a vanilla Maxout CNN, with qualitative analyses of gating dynamics and some stability experiments over T.

The work sits at the intersection of deep vision and RL/black-box optimization. It relates to prior efforts on visual attention and feedback in neuroscience and computer vision, to RL-driven attention/glimpses, and to recurrent processing for recognition. Within the deep learning literature, it is close to ideas of top-down modulation and feature-wise gating (e.g., competitive inhibition/“Compete to Compute”) but distinguishes itself by learning an input-conditional, multi-pass gating policy that acts across all layers.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear formulation of internal, input-conditional, top-down modulation in CNNs via recurrent gating, trained post hoc on a strong baseline.
- Practicality: Integrates with a state-of-the-art (for its time) Maxout CNN and yields measurable gains on CIFAR-10/100. The method is architecturally simple to apply (multiplicative gates).
- Interesting training choice: Uses SNES to handle a very high-dimensional policy without backpropagating through time, which is pragmatic given the million-scale parameters.
- Qualitative insight: Visualizations and a stability study suggest nontrivial, structured modulation and robustness to running more steps than trained.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Limited baselines and ablations. The paper compares only to a vanilla Maxout model; it lacks:
  - A recurrent baseline trained end-to-end with backpropagation through unrolled steps (all operations are differentiable).
  - Simpler controllers (e.g., linear policy, per-layer or per-block gating, or gating driven only by logits).
  - Non-RL alternatives (e.g., FiLM-style feature-wise affine conditioning learned by SGD, test-time ensembling or MC-dropout, confidence-based logit calibration).
- The choice of SNES is motivated by dimensionality, but sample efficiency, convergence behavior, and variance across runs are not reported. Using a diagonal covariance may restrict exploration in a highly correlated space.
- The “boosting” objective focuses on hard examples but may bias evaluation; it is unclear how this affects overall accuracy or calibration. No statistical significance or error bars are provided.
- Some reported analyses are hard to interpret: the “gates-only” KNN/logistic classification results lack clear context (dataset/split) and their relation to dasNet’s accuracy is unclear.

Clarity
- Notation lapses (e.g., Eq. (8) d log(vT) is ambiguous; indexing between at and at+1). The action softmax scaled by dim(A) is unusual and would benefit from justification and bounds analysis.
- Claims of “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” conflict with earlier mention of training with data augmentation; please clarify whether this refers to test-time augmentation only.
- Missing quantitative details in the excerpt (full Table 1, figures) prevent independent assessment of the magnitude of gains and fairness of comparisons.

Originality
- While top-down feedback and attention are well-studied conceptually, learning an input-conditional, multilayer gating policy across multiple passes on strong CNNs is a novel and interesting instantiation. However, the policy-as-black-box optimization angle limits insight into what is learned and how it compares to differentiable recurrent alternatives.

Significance
- Demonstrates that recurrent, selective modulation can improve performance on standard benchmarks, which is valuable. However, improvements appear modest and the compute/training overhead is substantial (multi-pass inference; multi-day SNES training). Without stronger baselines, it is hard to assess the practical advantage over simpler or end-to-end recurrent approaches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations:
  - Train the same unrolled architecture end-to-end with BPTT (policy as a small MLP; no SNES) and compare accuracy, stability, and training cost.
  - Compare different observation sets (only logits vs. added feature stats), action granularities (per-layer vs. per-map), and T.
  - Evaluate simpler controllers (linear policy, low-rank/grouped gating) for parameter and sample efficiency.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Test-time ensembling, MC-dropout, temperature scaling/confidence-based reranking; a shallow corrective head on top of logits; FiLM-like conditioning trained with SGD.
- Provide thorough experimental reporting: multiple seeds, confidence intervals, wall-clock and FLOP overhead at inference, and calibration metrics.
- Analyze learned policies: distributions of gate values by class, temporal trajectories, and correlation with confusions (e.g., dog vs. cat).
- Clarify the “unaugmented” SOTA claim, dataset protocols, and ensure fairness with contemporary baselines.
- Explore stochastic policies with REINFORCE or entropy regularization, or hybrid gradient-based policy optimization to improve efficiency and interpretability.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, compelling idea of internal, sequential attention via top-down modulation.
- Works on competitive CNNs/datasets and yields consistent gains.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired feedback with modern deep nets in a concrete way.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing key baselines and ablations; unclear whether SNES-based policy is necessary or optimal.
- Modest gains relative to added complexity and compute.
- Clarity/experimental reporting gaps hinder rigorous assessment.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is interesting and relevant to NeurIPS, and the results are promising. However, the empirical evaluation lacks critical comparisons and ablations needed to convincingly establish the method’s advantage and to justify the design choices (especially SNES vs. differentiable recurrent training). Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
