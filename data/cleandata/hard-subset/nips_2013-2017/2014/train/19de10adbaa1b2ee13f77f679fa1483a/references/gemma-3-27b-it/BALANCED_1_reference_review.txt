This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to enhancing CNN performance through iterative refinement guided by reinforcement learning. The core idea is to allow feedback connections within a pre-trained Maxout network, modulating filter activations based on a policy learned via SNES. The motivation stems from the observation that human visual processing often involves multiple passes over an image, especially in ambiguous cases. The paper presents experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrating improved performance on challenging instances.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established success of CNNs and drawing inspiration from human visual cognition. The problem addressed—improving CNN robustness to difficult cases—is relevant and important. The proposed solution, combining a deep network with reinforcement learning to dynamically adjust filter activations, is a creative and interesting approach. The use of SNES for policy optimization is a pragmatic choice given the large parameter space. The experimental results are promising, showing a performance gain over a strong baseline. The visualization of filter activation changes provides some insight into the network’s behavior, albeit limited.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the writing quality is uneven. While the introduction and conclusion are relatively clear, sections detailing the Maxout network and reinforcement learning are dense and assume considerable prior knowledge. The equations are presented without sufficient context or explanation of their purpose, hindering understanding. More intuitive explanations, even alongside the formalisms, would be beneficial. Secondly, the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvement, lacks thoroughness. The comparison is limited to a single baseline (the pre-trained Maxout network).  Comparing dasNet to other state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100 would strengthen the claims.  Furthermore, the improvements reported (6%) are modest, and the statistical significance of the results isn't clearly established. Reporting standard deviations or p-values is crucial. The ablation studies could be expanded: what's the impact of the number of passes (T)? How sensitive is performance to the choice of reward function parameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified)?  Thirdly, the analysis of *why* dasNet works is superficial. The visualization in Figure 3 offers a glimpse into filter activation changes, but a more systematic analysis of the learned attention policy is needed. What types of filters are consistently emphasized or suppressed? Do the changes align with human intuition about the relevant features?  Finally, the connection to related work is somewhat cursory.  While the paper mentions relevant areas like recurrent connections and attention mechanisms, it doesn’t deeply engage with the existing literature.  For example, the paper references recurrent processing for image reconstruction but doesn't discuss how dasNet differs conceptually. More recent works on attention mechanisms within CNNs, such as SENet [1] or CBAM [2], should be discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to improving CNN robustness through iterative refinement and reinforcement learning.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating performance gains on challenging datasets.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in computer vision.
*   Pragmatic choice of SNES for policy optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Uneven writing quality and lack of clarity in certain sections.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of statistical significance analysis.
*   Superficial analysis of the learned attention policy.
*   Incomplete engagement with related work.
*   Modest performance improvements.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**. While the idea is interesting and the initial results are encouraging, the paper needs substantial revision to address the weaknesses outlined above. The lack of clarity, limited experimental evaluation, and superficial analysis make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity:** Rewrite sections 2 and 3 to be more accessible and provide more intuitive explanations of the equations.
*   **Experiments:** Conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing dasNet to multiple state-of-the-art methods. Report statistical significance. Perform ablation studies to analyze the impact of key hyperparameters.
*   **Analysis:** Conduct a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention policy. Identify patterns in filter activation changes and relate them to the characteristics of the input images.
*   **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include a more thorough discussion of relevant literature, particularly recent advances in attention mechanisms and recurrent neural networks.
*   **Visualization:** Provide more informative visualizations of the learned attention policy.
