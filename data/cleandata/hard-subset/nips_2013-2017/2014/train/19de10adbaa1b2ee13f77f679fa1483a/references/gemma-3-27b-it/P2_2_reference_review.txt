## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that enhances deep convolutional neural networks with internal selective attention via learned feedback connections. The core idea is to allow each layer to influence all others on successive passes, modulating filter activity based on a policy learned through reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES). This aims to mimic human visual processing where multiple "looks" at an image refine understanding, especially for ambiguous cases. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on difficult classification instances, compared to a standard Maxout CNN.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning *internal* attention mechanisms via feedback connections and reinforcement learning is a compelling and relatively unexplored area. Most attention mechanisms focus on external sources or spatial/channel weighting *within* a layer, rather than dynamic inter-layer modulation.
* **Soundness:** The approach is logically sound.  The motivation – that CNNs could benefit from iterative refinement like human vision – is plausible. The use of SNES, while complex, is justified due to the high dimensionality of the policy space. The experimental setup is clearly described.
* **Empirical Results:**  The reported performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100, especially on difficult cases, provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of dasNet. The visualization of filter activity changes provides qualitative insight into the attention mechanism.
* **Connection to Cognitive Science:** The work draws a clear and well-articulated connection to cognitive models of human visual perception, grounding the technical contribution in broader scientific principles.
* **Completeness:** The paper includes sufficient detail for reproducibility, referencing appropriate algorithms (Maxout, SNES) and providing experimental parameters.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet is significantly more expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) than training the baseline Maxout network. This is a substantial barrier to wider adoption and exploration. The paper doesn't fully address the scalability of this approach to larger datasets and more complex architectures.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of T (number of passes), λ values (boosting parameters), and SNES parameters (population size) likely significantly impacts performance. The paper lacks a thorough ablation study demonstrating sensitivity to these parameters, and provides limited justification for the chosen values.
* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** While improved performance is demonstrated on CIFAR-10/100, it is not clear how well this approach generalizes to other datasets or architectures.  The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more rigorous discussion of potential limitations is warranted.
* **Interpretability:**  While filter activation visualization is provided, it remains challenging to fully interpret *why* dasNet focuses attention in specific ways. Deeper analysis of the learned policy and its relationship to the input data would be beneficial.
* **SNES Complexity:** While justified, the reliance on SNES makes the method less accessible to researchers without expertise in evolutionary computation. A discussion of alternative RL methods and their limitations in this context would be helpful.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and claims are generally well-supported by experiments.  The justification for SNES is reasonable, and the results are promising. However, the limited ablation study and lack of discussion about computational scalability slightly detract from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  However, the details of SNES might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with evolutionary algorithms. More explanation of the internal workings of the attention policy would also improve clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The approach of learning internal attention through feedback and RL is highly original and represents a significant departure from existing attention mechanisms. The integration of cognitive science principles also adds to the novelty.
* **Significance (7/10):** The potential to improve the robustness and accuracy of CNNs, particularly in challenging scenarios, is significant. However, the computational cost and limited generalization analysis temper the overall impact. The work opens up a promising research direction but requires further investigation to realize its full potential.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel and potentially impactful approach to improving CNNs.
* Strong empirical results on challenging datasets.
* Sound theoretical grounding and connection to cognitive science.
* Well-written and generally complete.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* High computational cost.
* Limited ablation study and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Lack of clear generalization analysis.
* Complexity of SNES may limit accessibility.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to improving deep learning models. While the computational cost and limited generalization analysis are concerns, the originality and potential impact of the work outweigh these weaknesses.  I would suggest the authors:

1.  Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters (T, λ values, SNES parameters).
2.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and potential strategies for scaling the approach to larger datasets and more complex architectures.
3.  Investigate the transferability of the learned attention policies to different datasets or architectures.
4.  Expand the interpretability analysis of the learned attention policies, potentially using techniques like attention rollouts or feature visualization.
