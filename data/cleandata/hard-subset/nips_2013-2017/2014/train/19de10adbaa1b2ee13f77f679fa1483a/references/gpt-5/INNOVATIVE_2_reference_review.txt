Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a deep CNN augmented with learned feedback connections that modulate the gain of feature maps across multiple passes over the same image. Starting from a strong, trained Maxout CNN, the method introduces an internal, selective attention mechanism: on each pass t, per-map gates aℓj scale activations before maxout pooling; an observation vector comprising averaged map activations, penultimate-layer activations, and class probabilities is fed to a policy network that outputs the next set of gates. The policy is trained post hoc via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted log-loss that emphasizes misclassified examples. At test time, the network iteratively refines its prediction across T passes. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improved accuracy over the base CNN, with claims of state-of-the-art results for non-committee models.

This work sits at the intersection of recurrent/feedback vision and learned attention. It is well motivated by neuroscience (top-down, modulatory feedback) and builds on: Maxout+dropout CNNs, policy search via NES/SNES, and prior RL-based attention/saccades (e.g., Larochelle & Hinton; Denil et al.) and feedback in neural models (O’Reilly; Fukushima). The key novelty is learning a high-dimensional, internal attention policy that directly gates intermediate feature maps of a modern CNN, trained with black-box policy search, and applied at scale on CIFAR.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A bold, concrete instantiation of top-down, internal attention for CNNs via learned feedback that modulates filter map gains, rather than external spatial glimpses or post-processing.
- Practical feasibility: Demonstrates that policy search (SNES with diagonal covariance) can handle ~10^6 parameters to control a large CNN without BPTT, and that the dynamics are stable for multiple passes.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with qualitative analyses and some evidence that the learned gates carry discriminative information.
- Clean separation of concerns: Train the feedforward CNN fully supervised, then learn the attentional controller to fix residual errors; the boosting-style loss focuses the controller on hard cases.
- Potentially impactful direction: Internal attention via feedback is a compelling route beyond purely feedforward pipelines and may inspire follow-ups with richer observations/actions and end-to-end training.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Baselines and ablations are limited. It would strengthen the case to compare against:
  - A recurrent/gated variant trained end-to-end with backprop-through-time (even if smaller), or a supervised gating controller (no RL).
  - Simpler heuristics (e.g., temperature scaling, class-conditional gating) and smaller policy networks.
  - REINFORCE or deterministic policy gradient on a reduced-size policy to show SNES is necessary.
- Compute/latency tradeoff is underexplored. T=5 implies ~5× inference cost; please quantify wall-clock overhead and energy/throughput impacts vs. accuracy gain.
- Observation design is coarse. Averaging activations discards spatial information; the authors acknowledge this but do not empirically compare alternative observations (e.g., spatial moments, low-rank summaries).
- Metric/reporting ambiguities. The text claims “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” but earlier mentions training with data augmentation. Please clarify exactly what “unaugmented” refers to (no test-time augmentation? no committees?) and provide a fair, up-to-date benchmark table with single-model baselines.
- Statistical rigor. Provide means/standard deviations over multiple runs or SNES seeds; current results appear single-run. Include per-class confusion improvements and a breakdown on “hard” vs “easy” subsets to support the intended use case.
- Limited analysis of failure modes. When does feedback hurt? Are there classes or textures where gates systematically mislead?
- Reproducibility details. Important hyperparameters of SNES (learning rates, population size rationale, stopping), policy architecture (only a single linear-softmax layer?), and exact size of A and O should be specified.

Quality
Technically sound and internally consistent. The control loop and training objective are clear, and the SNES choice is justified by dimensionality. However, stronger baselines, statistical robustness, and compute analysis are needed for a fully compelling empirical case.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Algorithm 1 is helpful. Some notation is overloaded (indices in equations 1–5) and a few typographical issues exist. Clarify the “unaugmented” claim, specify policy dimensions, and describe the policy architecture precisely.

Originality
High. While attention and recurrence have prior art, learning a high-dimensional internal attention policy that modulates feature map gains across a modern CNN via evolutionary policy search is a novel and bold combination that differs from spatial glimpse models and traditional feedback architectures.

Significance
Moderate to high. The idea opens a promising direction—feedback-driven internal attention for discriminative refinement—and shows measurable gains on standard benchmarks. Even if the exact SNES-based training may not become the default, the concept is likely to influence subsequent work on learned gating/attention and recurrent CNNs.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh, biologically inspired mechanism (internal, learned feedback) for modern CNNs.
- Demonstrates feasibility at scale with nontrivial accuracy gains.
- Clear potential for extensions (spatially selective actions, end-to-end differentiable controllers).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited ablations and baselines; unclear how much of the gain stems from feedback vs. other factors.
- Computational overhead and latency costs not carefully quantified.
- Some reporting ambiguities and lack of variance analysis.

Questions for the authors
1) Precisely define “unaugmented” in your SOTA claim and list the compared baselines.
2) What is the exact policy architecture and dimensionalities of O and A? Any nonlinearity beyond a linear-softmax?
3) Can you report compute overhead (FLOPs, latency) for T=1..5 and accuracy vs. cost curves?
4) Did you try training the controller with gradients (e.g., straight-through or BPTT) on a smaller model?
5) Provide variance over multiple SNES seeds and sensitivity to λmisclassified/λcorrect.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Novel and ambitious idea with promising empirical results; despite some experimental gaps, the conceptual contribution and direction are significant and likely to catalyze further research.

Confidence
Medium-high.
