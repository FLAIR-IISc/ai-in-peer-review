This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES) to selectively modulate filter activations. The core idea is to enable the network to refine its classifications through iterative processing, focusing on relevant features for difficult cases. The approach builds upon Maxout networks and aims to mimic aspects of human visual attention by allowing the network to “think” over multiple passes.

The paper is well-motivated, referencing human visual processing and limitations of purely feedforward CNNs. It clearly outlines the problem of ambiguous classifications and proposes a solution that leverages both supervised learning (for initial training) and reinforcement learning (for attentional policy refinement). The experimental evaluation on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrates improved performance on challenging instances, showcasing the benefits of the iterative attention mechanism.  Related work is comprehensive, covering areas of visual attention, recurrent neural networks, and reinforcement learning. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections within a CNN architecture learned through reinforcement learning is a novel approach. While recurrent connections exist in other models, learning their weights specifically for selective attention post-training is a unique contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound. The use of SNES is justified given the high dimensionality of the policy space. The description of the algorithm and the experimental setup are clear and reasonably detailed. The ablation study showing the effect of T provides insight into the model’s behavior.
*   **Significance:**  The demonstrated improvement in performance on challenging CIFAR instances, and establishing a new state-of-the-art result, indicates potential practical significance.  The concept of learning internal attention mechanisms could be applicable to other image classification tasks or even beyond.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the sections on Maxout networks, reinforcement learning, and dasNet are logically structured.  The figures and tables are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The training process is computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti). This limits the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex architectures.  While acknowledged, the practical implications for widespread adoption are significant.
*   **Interpretability:** While the paper visualizes changes in filter activations, the *meaning* of these changes remains largely opaque.  Understanding *why* the network focuses on certain features would enhance the contribution.  The discussion of gate values' information content is a start, but more detailed analysis is needed.
*   **SNES limitations:** While SNES is a pragmatic choice for this problem, it's acknowledged as less powerful than standard NES. A discussion of how this might affect the optimality of the learned policy would be valuable. Could the choice of SNES be a limiting factor?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to hyperparameters like the reward function’s lambda values. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would bolster confidence in the results.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through learned internal attention.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement on challenging image classification tasks.
*   The work addresses a relevant and important problem in deep learning – improving robustness and interpretability.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost is a significant barrier to practical application.
*   The interpretability of the learned attention mechanisms is limited.
*   The choice of SNES might introduce limitations.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity is not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and promising approach to improving CNN performance. The technical soundness is good, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the computational cost and interpretability issues are concerns, the potential benefits of the learned attention mechanism outweigh these drawbacks. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the interpretability concerns and providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity in the rebuttal phase.
