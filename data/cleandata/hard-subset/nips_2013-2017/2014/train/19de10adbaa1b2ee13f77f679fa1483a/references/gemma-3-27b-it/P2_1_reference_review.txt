## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections between layers. These connections modulate filter activations based on a policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES). The goal is to enable the network to iteratively refine its classifications, focusing on relevant features and correcting initial misclassifications, mimicking a more deliberate human-like visual processing approach. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on challenging examples, compared to a baseline Maxout network.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of learned feedback connections *within* a CNN, controlled by a reinforcement learning policy, is a novel approach.  While recurrent connections and attention mechanisms are prevalent, learning *which* filters to emphasize/de-emphasize post-training using RL is a unique contribution.  This moves beyond fixed attention weights or gating mechanisms.
* **Significance:** The idea of mimicking the iterative, reflective process of human vision is compelling.  Improving performance on difficult instances, as demonstrated, is practically relevant.  The method potentially addresses a limitation of standard CNNs, which are essentially “one-shot” classifiers.
* **Soundness:** The experimental setup is reasonably well-defined, using standard datasets (CIFAR-10/100) and a strong baseline (Maxout network).  The reported improvement of 6% on CIFAR-10 is a meaningful result.  The analysis of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provides some insight into the network’s behavior, though more in-depth analysis would be beneficial.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The motivations and core concepts are explained clearly. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the process (Figure 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  Training dasNet is significantly more expensive than training the baseline Maxout network (4 days vs. the time to train M, which isn't specified, but is implied to be shorter). This is a practical limitation, especially for larger and more complex networks.  The use of SNES, while justified for the parameter space, adds to the complexity and runtime.
* **Limited Analysis:**  While the visualization of filter activation changes is a start, the analysis of *why* dasNet corrects certain misclassifications remains superficial.  What specific features are being emphasized, and how do these relate to the correct classification? A more rigorous analysis of the learned attention policy would strengthen the paper. Showing ablation studies on the effectiveness of each layer's attention mechanisms could be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide much detail about how hyperparameters (e.g., the number of passes *T*, the learning rates for SNES, the lambda values) were chosen.  This raises concerns about reproducibility and the robustness of the results.
* **Related Work:** While the related work section is extensive, it lacks a focused discussion on existing work in *dynamic* neural networks or networks with learned modulatory connections.  Specifically, it doesn’t deeply engage with work exploring recurrent implementations or approaches which modify network behavior after initial training.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising and novel approach to improving CNN performance by incorporating learned feedback connections. The results are encouraging, and the idea of mimicking the iterative nature of human vision is intellectually stimulating. However, the computational cost, limited analysis, and potential hyperparameter sensitivity are drawbacks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The approach is novel and addresses a limitation of standard CNNs.
* The experimental results demonstrate a meaningful performance improvement.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.
* The work has the potential to inspire further research in dynamic neural networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost may limit the applicability of the method.
* The analysis of the learned attention policy is superficial.
* The lack of a detailed hyperparameter analysis raises concerns about reproducibility.



**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (7/10)**. 

I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. The novelty and potential significance of the work justify acceptance, especially if the authors can address the weaknesses in a future revision by providing a more detailed analysis of the learned attention policy and discussing the hyperparameter selection process. A discussion of computational scalability in the full version is important.
