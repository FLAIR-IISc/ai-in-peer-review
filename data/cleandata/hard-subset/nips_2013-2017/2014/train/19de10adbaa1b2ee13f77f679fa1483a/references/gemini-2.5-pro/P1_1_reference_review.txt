### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that adds an internal selective attention mechanism to a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (CNN). The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) with feedback connections that can modulate the activity of convolutional filter maps over multiple passes on the same input image. This process is framed as a sequential decision-making problem and is controlled by a policy learned via reinforcement learning (RL).

After an initial, standard feedforward pass, the network's internal state (average map activations, classification layer activations, and output probabilities) is used as an observation for the RL policy. The policy then outputs an action, which is a set of weights that enhance or suppress entire feature maps in the CNN for the subsequent pass. This iterative process is repeated for a fixed number of steps (`T`), allowing the network to "re-think" its initial prediction and focus on more discriminative features. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy, the authors eschew traditional RL algorithms in favor of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the policy's parameters. The authors demonstrate their approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing that dasNet can correct initial misclassifications and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 (for unaugmented data).

The work is motivated by the human visual system, where a fast, feedforward pass is followed by slower, attentional processing involving recurrent and top-down connections. It connects to previous work on RL for visual attention but distinguishes itself by applying the concept to modulate the internal workings of a state-of-the-art deep network to improve classification performance, rather than for tasks like foveation or on simpler models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of learning a policy to modulate the internal feature maps of a pre-trained, high-performing CNN is novel and compelling. It presents a principled way to incorporate feedback and iterative refinement into modern deep learning architectures, moving beyond purely feedforward models. This approach could inspire new research into more dynamic and adaptive neural networks.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement over a strong baseline (Maxout network), achieving a 6% relative improvement and a new state-of-the-art result on the challenging CIFAR-100 dataset (without data augmentation). This shows the practical utility of the proposed method.

3.  **Clever Technical Approach:** The use of SNES to train the RL policy is a smart solution to a difficult problem. The action and state spaces are far too large for standard policy gradient or value-function methods, and the authors correctly identify that a black-box evolutionary approach is a viable and effective alternative for this high-dimensional parameter search.

4.  **Good Analysis and Intuition:** The paper goes beyond just reporting accuracy. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3, showing the correction of a "cat" vs. "dog" misclassification and visualizing the changes in map activations, provides valuable intuition about what the network is learning. The additional experiments on the stability of the dynamics and the information content of the learned gates further strengthen the claim that the attentional mechanism is learning a meaningful strategy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Cost:** The primary drawback is the significant computational expense. Training requires 4 days on a GPU *after* the base model is already trained. More importantly, inference time is increased by a factor of `T` (here, `T=5`). This makes the model impractical for many real-world applications and this trade-off should be discussed more prominently.

2.  **Simplicity of State and Action Representation:** The observation vector `o_t` is formed by concatenating the average activation of every output map, along with higher-level features. Averaging over spatial dimensions discards a large amount of information. A justification for this choice over more complex representations (e.g., spatially-preserved downsampled maps) is missing. Similarly, the action `a_t` modulates entire feature maps uniformly. A more fine-grained, spatially-aware attention mechanism might be more powerful.

3.  **Ad-hoc Reward Function:** The reward function uses a "boosting" mechanism (Eq. 10) with manually set constants `λ_correct` and `λ_misclassified`. While this is a reasonable heuristic to focus on difficult examples, the method's sensitivity to these hyperparameters is not explored. A more formal discussion of the reward design would improve the paper's rigor.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, some details are sparse. The exact architecture of the Maxout network used as a base is not fully specified. The details of the SNES update are relegated to supplementary material, making the core algorithm less self-contained. The state-of-the-art claim on CIFAR-100 in Table 1 is compared against a limited set of methods and is specific to unaugmented data; providing context against augmented results (e.g., from [51], [52]) would give a more complete picture of its performance.

### Arguments for acceptance:

*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated architecture that integrates feedback and iterative refinement into deep CNNs.
*   Addresses a significant challenge (high-dimensional policy learning) with a suitable and effective method (SNES).
*   Demonstrates a clear state-of-the-art performance improvement on a difficult benchmark (CIFAR-100).
*   Provides solid qualitative and quantitative analysis that offers insight into the model's behavior.
*   The overall idea is significant and likely to inspire follow-up work on dynamic and attention-based networks.

### Arguments against acceptance:

*   The practical utility is limited by a massive increase in computational cost for both training and inference, which is not sufficiently emphasized as a limitation.
*   The design choices for the state representation (spatial averaging) and action space (whole-map modulation) are simplistic and not well justified.
*   The reward function is somewhat ad-hoc, and its sensitivity is not analyzed.
*   The paper could be strengthened with a more thorough comparison to contemporary SOTA methods and more detailed experimental specifics to improve reproducibility.

### Overall recommendation

This is a strong paper presenting a novel, interesting, and effective method. The idea of using RL to learn an internal attention policy for a pre-trained CNN is significant and well-executed. The empirical results are convincing, and the analysis provides valuable insights. While there are weaknesses, particularly concerning computational cost and some design choices, the strengths in originality and significance are substantial. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field and is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
