This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to improving CNN performance by introducing feedback connections and learning an attentional policy via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies). The core idea is compelling: allow the network to revisit and refine its classification based on internal signals, mimicking aspects of human visual processing where initial assessments are often followed by more deliberate scrutiny. The authors motivate this by pointing out that even experts sometimes require more than a single pass to classify ambiguous stimuli.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The related work section is comprehensive, covering both the foundations of CNNs and relevant work on attention mechanisms, reinforcement learning, and recurrent processing in vision. The use of Maxout networks provides a well-defined baseline for the proposed method. The experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate performance improvements, particularly on challenging instances. The inclusion of a dynamics analysis (varying the number of iterative steps) and an information content analysis of the learned attention gates strengthens the claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections within a CNN and reinforcement learning for learning an attention policy is a novel contribution. While attention mechanisms are increasingly common, this approach differs from typical spatial or channel attention by operating *within* the network’s layers, allowing for internal refinement.
* **Soundness:** The method is technically sound. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the dasNet architecture and the reinforcement learning process. The choice of SNES is justified given the large parameter space.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate a statistically significant performance improvement on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, outperforming a state-of-the-art CNN. The visualization of filter activations and the dynamics analysis provide valuable insights into the behavior of the network.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are concise and clear.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors address potential limitations and provide a comprehensive related work section. The inclusion of ablation studies (through the dynamics analysis) is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The training process is computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti). This limits the scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex architectures. The authors acknowledge this but don't explore potential avenues for reducing computational burden.
* **SNES limitations:** While SNES is justified for the large parameter space, it is acknowledged as less powerful than standard NES. The potential performance gains from a more sophisticated RL algorithm (perhaps with approximations to manage the parameter space) are not explored.
* **Interpretability:** While the visualizations of filter activations are informative, it remains challenging to fully understand *what* the network is attending to and *why* it leads to improved performance. More detailed analysis of the learned attention policies is needed. For example, what types of filters are consistently emphasized or suppressed for specific misclassifications?
* **Generalization to other datasets:** The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. It is unclear whether the results would generalize to more complex datasets like ImageNet.
* **Action space dimensionality:** The action space dimensionality (controlling each filter) seems quite high. The authors mention averaging to 1, but this is only to stabilize activations; perhaps a more structured or sparse action space could be explored to reduce complexity and improve learning efficiency.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance by incorporating feedback and reinforcement learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate a statistically significant performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and thoroughly researched.
* The contribution is significant and has the potential to advance the field of deep learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of training is high, limiting scalability.
* The interpretability of the learned attention policies remains limited.
* The generalization to more complex datasets is uncertain.
* The use of SNES, while justified, might limit performance compared to more sophisticated RL methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The idea of incorporating feedback and learning an attentional policy within a CNN is novel and promising. The experimental results are encouraging, and the paper is well-written and thorough. While the computational cost and interpretability issues are concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational cost of training.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the learned attention policies, perhaps by identifying common patterns or correlations between filter activations and misclassifications.
3.  Acknowledge the limitations of SNES and discuss potential future work exploring alternative RL algorithms.
4.  Briefly discuss the potential challenges and opportunities for applying dasNet to larger and more complex datasets.
5.   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters (learning rate, population size, etc.) used in the SNES training.
