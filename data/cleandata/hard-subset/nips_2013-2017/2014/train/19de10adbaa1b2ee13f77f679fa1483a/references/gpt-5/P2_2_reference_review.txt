Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which add learned feedback connections to a feedforward CNN (a Maxout network). At test time, an image is processed for T passes. After each pass, a policy network observes summary statistics of internal activations (per-feature-map averages, classification pre-activations, and class probabilities) and outputs multiplicative gates that reweight each feature map in every layer for the next pass. The policy is learned after standard supervised training of the base CNN, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to optimize a weighted log-loss on the final pass, with higher weight on misclassified examples. On CIFAR-10/100 the method improves over a strong Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art results for “unaugmented data”. Qualitative analysis shows nontrivial gate patterns and stable dynamics beyond the training horizon.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Maxout+dropout CNNs. It connects to biologically inspired top-down modulation and iterative visual processing. Compared to prior RL attention work (e.g., learned saccades/glimpses), the novelty here is internal, channel-wise attentional modulation across passes rather than spatial attention. It also relates to recurrent processing and feedback for recognition, but differs by using a separate policy to control multiplicative gains on feature maps. The choice of NES/SNES instead of gradient-based RL or end-to-end backprop-through-time is unusual in deep vision contexts and is a distinctive aspect.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mechanism (per-channel multiplicative gates and repeated passes) is straightforward and differentiable. The observation and action parametrizations are well specified, and the softmax normalization to keep gates positive and mean ~1 is sensible for stability. The SNES optimization is correctly described and appropriate for very high-dimensional parameter vectors, though it is a heuristic choice with limited covariance modeling.
- Empirical support: The paper reports consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, including a 6% relative improvement and a SOTA claim for unaugmented data. However, the experimental section omits some critical details in the main text: exact error rates, confidence intervals/variance across runs, and the full contents of Table 1. There are limited ablations (e.g., T sweeps shown only for a small 2-layer model; no per-layer gating ablations, no analysis of observation design). The dynamics stability experiment is informative but performed on a smaller network, not the main model.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to (i) simply fine-tuning the base CNN on hard examples, (ii) a recurrent version trained end-to-end via backprop through time with shared gates, (iii) gradient-based training of the policy network (the whole system is deterministic and differentiable; REINFORCE or direct BPTT should be feasible), and (iv) cheaper variants (gating only higher layers).
- Cost/benefit: Inference requires T passes (T=5), roughly 5× runtime, which is not quantitatively discussed. A speed/accuracy analysis would help.
- Claims and honesty: The text says the base CNN was trained “with data augmentation” but later claims “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data.” Please clarify whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time augmentation only, or whether training augmentation was used. Otherwise the SOTA claim is ambiguous.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear, with a helpful algorithm box and precise notation for the gating mechanism. A few places need clarification:
- Exact architecture and hyperparameters (filter sizes, strides, learning rates, SNES settings µ/Σ init, batch sizes, termination criteria).
- The full results table and numerical metrics.
- What “unaugmented” means in the SOTA claim.
- The dimensionality of observation and action vectors for the main model.
- The impact on class-wise confusions and easy vs hard examples.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. While feedback and attention are longstanding ideas, the particular combination—internal, per-channel gating controlled by a learned policy across multiple passes, applied on top of a strong modern CNN and optimized with SNES—is novel for the time. Unlike earlier RL attention work focused on glimpses/saccades, this paper targets internal feature selection.

Significance
Moderate. The technique is general and shows tangible gains on CIFAR-10/100, potentially inspiring further work on learned modulation/feedback. However, the scope is limited to small datasets, and the ablation and baseline coverage is insufficient to isolate what drives the improvement or to assess the necessity of SNES over gradient-based training. The extra inference cost may limit practical impact without a clear accuracy/runtime trade-off.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated architecture for internal selective attention via feedback.
- Consistent empirical improvements on standard benchmarks using a strong baseline.
- Simple, stable gating mechanism with reasonable normalization.
- Interesting qualitative analyses (gate information content; stability beyond training horizon).

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines and ablations; unclear whether simpler or gradient-based methods would achieve comparable gains.
- Limited reporting of exact results and variance; SOTA claim unclear given augmentation comment.
- Computational overhead at inference not analyzed.
- Observation design and layer-wise contributions not systematically evaluated.
- SNES with diagonal covariance may be suboptimal; no justification versus BPTT despite full differentiability.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you report exact error rates and std over multiple seeds for CIFAR-10/100, and include full Table 1 in the main text?
2. Please clarify the augmentation setup for training and test, and the meaning of “unaugmented data.”
3. Did you attempt end-to-end gradient-based training of the policy (e.g., BPTT through T passes) or REINFORCE? What impeded it?
4. What is the runtime overhead (train/test) vs. baseline? Any latency-aware analysis?
5. Ablations: gating only top layer(s); removing classification pre-activations from observations; different T; policy size/regularization.
6. Class-wise confusion changes and effects on easy vs hard subsets?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros: Conceptually novel and timely; solid improvements on standard datasets; simple mechanism; potentially broadly applicable.
Cons: Incomplete experimental analysis; unclear necessity of SNES; limited scope and missing baselines; overhead not quantified; some reporting ambiguities.

Recommendation
Weak accept, conditional on clarifying results and providing stronger baselines/ablations. The idea is interesting and plausibly impactful, but the paper would benefit from tighter empirical evidence and clearer reporting to meet NeurIPS standards on quality and significance.
