- Paper introduces dasNet: a CNN with learned feedback connections enabling internal selective attention across multiple passes, trained via SNES reinforcement learning.  
- Motivation: iterative refinement mimics human vision to improve classification on ambiguous inputs.  
- Empirical claim: dasNet improves performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, especially on difficult cases, versus a standard Maxout CNN.  
- Novelty: learning internal, inter-layer attention via RL is a new and compelling direction distinct from existing spatial/channel attention methods.  
- Soundness: approach is logically justified and experimentally described; SNES choice is reasonable given policy dimensionality.  
- Empirical results: quantitative improvements and qualitative filter-activity visualizations support effectiveness.  
- Cognitive link: work is well-grounded in cognitive science models of human visual perception.  
- Completeness: paper provides sufficient experimental and algorithmic detail for reproducibility.  
- Computational cost: training is expensive (four days on a GTX 560 Ti), raising scalability and adoption concerns.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: lacks thorough ablation on T, λ, and SNES parameters and limited justification for chosen values.  
- Limited generalization analysis: unclear how dasNet scales to other datasets or architectures beyond CIFAR benchmarks.  
- Interpretability: filter visualizations are provided but do not fully explain why attention decisions are made; deeper policy analysis is needed.  
- SNES complexity: reliance on evolutionary strategies may limit accessibility for researchers without that expertise.  
- Quality score (8/10): technically sound with promising results, though limited ablation and scalability discussion reduce score.  
- Clarity score (7/10): generally well-written, but SNES details and attention policy mechanics could be clearer.  
- Originality score (9/10): highly original approach integrating feedback attention and cognitive principles.  
- Significance score (7/10): significant potential but tempered by computational cost and limited generalization evidence.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, impactful idea; strong CIFAR results; solid theoretical grounding; paper is complete and well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, limited ablation and generalization analysis, and complexity of SNES approach.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, requesting more ablation, scalability discussion, transferability tests, and deeper interpretability analysis.
