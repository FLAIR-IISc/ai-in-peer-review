## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections (dasNet)

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into deep convolutional neural networks via reinforcement learning. The idea of allowing a network to iteratively refine its classifications by modulating filter activations is compelling and aligns well with observations of human visual processing. While the experimental results demonstrate a tangible improvement, the paper feels somewhat limited in its exploration of the learned attention policies and could benefit from more in-depth analysis and ablation studies. I lean towards **Accept**, but with requests for minor revisions focusing on clarity and experimental analysis.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The authors introduce dasNet, a deep convolutional neural network augmented with feedback connections. These connections allow each layer to influence all others, modulating filter activity on successive passes over an image. A reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES) policy learns to control these connections, essentially implementing an internal attention mechanism. This allows the network to adapt its behavior post-training to improve classification, particularly in ambiguous cases. 

The paper appropriately situates itself within the context of deep learning for computer vision, referencing key works on CNNs, maxout networks, and reinforcement learning. The discussion of biological inspiration, highlighting the iterative nature of human visual perception and the role of feedback connections in the visual cortex, is well-placed and motivates the approach effectively.  The authors correctly point to related work on RL-based attention and recurrent processing, and distinguish dasNet as tackling the problem directly within the context of state-of-the-art CNN architectures.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of post-training adaptation via reinforcement learning with a fully connected attentional mechanism *within* a deep CNN is novel.  Most attention mechanisms are applied as pre-processing steps or within specific layers. DasNet’s full inter-layer connectivity and learned modulation offers a unique approach.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The methodology is well-defined.  The use of Maxout networks provides a stable baseline, and the choice of SNES, while perhaps not the most conventional RL method, is justified given the large parameter space.  The algorithm is clearly presented.
* **Empirical Results:** The demonstrated improvement on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, specifically on difficult instances, is a significant achievement. The 6% relative improvement is non-trivial.
* **Interpretability (to a degree):**  The visualizations of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provide *some* insight into how the network is adjusting its attention. This is a crucial aspect for demonstrating the functionality of the approach.
* **Addressing a Real Problem:**  The focus on ambiguous cases highlights a limitation of standard CNNs and demonstrates the practical utility of dasNet.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of the Learned Policy:** While the authors show *that* the policy improves performance, they don’t delve deeply into *how* the policy operates. What general strategies are emerging?  Are certain filters consistently emphasized or suppressed for specific classes? A more detailed analysis of the learned attention weights would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **SNES Justification & Scalability:** While the authors justify SNES, the limitations of the method are not fully addressed. Is the computational cost significantly higher than using a standard RL algorithm with careful engineering? A discussion of the scalability of SNES to larger, more complex networks would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters such as the learning rate for SNES, the number of iterations (T), and the regularization parameters.
* **Ablation Studies:**  It would be useful to see ablation studies examining the impact of different components of dasNet. For example, what happens if only certain layers are allowed to participate in the feedback loop? Does the performance degrade significantly?
* **Limited Dynamic Analysis:** The dynamics experiment (Figure 2) is a good start, but could be expanded.  Analyzing the convergence rate of the policy and examining the stability of the learned attention patterns over longer time horizons would be beneficial.
* **Clarity of the "Boost" Reward:** The explanation of the boosting reward function (Equation 8) is somewhat unclear. The intuition behind using different lambda values for correct vs. misclassified samples could be articulated more precisely.




**4. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel approach to internal attention.
* Demonstrable performance improvement on challenging datasets.
* Technically sound methodology.
* Clear presentation of the algorithm.

**Con:**

* Limited analysis of the learned attention policies.
* Lack of detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Absence of ablation studies.
* Scalability concerns with SNES.



**5. Recommendations:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Expand the analysis of the learned policy:** Provide a more detailed characterization of the attention weights. Are there recurring patterns? Can you identify filters that are consistently important for specific classes?
2. **Conduct ablation studies:** Evaluate the impact of different components of dasNet (e.g., feedback connections in specific layers).
3. **Discuss hyperparameter sensitivity:** Analyze how performance changes with different hyperparameter settings.
4. **Clarify the boosting reward function:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the lambda values.
5. **Briefly address scalability concerns:** Discuss the computational limitations of SNES and potential avenues for improvement.




In conclusion, dasNet presents a promising approach to enhancing the capabilities of deep convolutional neural networks. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper demonstrates a compelling combination of technical innovation, empirical results, and biological inspiration. Addressing the suggested revisions will further strengthen the contribution and make it more impactful.
