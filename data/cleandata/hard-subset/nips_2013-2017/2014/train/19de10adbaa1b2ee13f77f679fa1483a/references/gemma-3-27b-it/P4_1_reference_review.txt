The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES) to selectively attend to features during multiple passes over an image. The motivation stems from the observation that humans often require multiple looks to resolve ambiguous classifications, suggesting that iterative processing and internal feedback can improve performance. DasNet learns to modulate the activity of convolutional filters to enhance or suppress features, aiming to correct misclassifications from an initially trained CNN. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate improved performance on difficult instances compared to a baseline Maxout network.

This work builds upon a considerable body of research in attention mechanisms and recurrent neural networks for computer vision.  The idea of feedback connections mirroring the human visual cortex is not new (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Gilbert & Sigman, 1999).  Recent work in visual attention, particularly utilizing reinforcement learning (e.g., Schmidhuber et al., 2014; Denil et al., 2017), provides relevant context.  The use of SNES for optimizing the attention policy is a pragmatic choice given the large parameter space, contrasting with traditional RL methods. The paper appropriately cites related work, though a broader review of recent attention mechanisms in CNNs (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation networks, CBAM) might be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The combination of iterative processing with learned feedback connections, optimized via reinforcement learning (SNES), is a novel approach to improving CNN performance. While recurrent connections have been explored, learning *which* connections to activate adaptively after initial training is a valuable contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears sound, leveraging established techniques like Maxout networks, SNES, and standard supervised learning. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results demonstrate a clear performance improvement.
* **Significance:** Improving classification accuracy, especially on difficult instances, is a significant advancement. The demonstrated ability to correct misclassifications through internal feedback suggests a potential pathway towards more robust and interpretable CNNs. The visualization of filter changes (Figure 3) offers some insight into the network’s reasoning.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful for understanding the training procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The 4-day training time (excluding the initial Maxout network training) is substantial and may limit the scalability of this approach to larger datasets or more complex architectures. This should be explicitly acknowledged as a limitation.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate performance gains, more thorough ablation studies are needed to dissect the contribution of each component (e.g., the number of passes *T*, the architecture of the attention policy network). A study varying *T* beyond the tested values could reveal diminishing returns.
* **Interpretability:** While Figure 3 provides some visualization of filter changes, the overall interpretability of the learned attention policy remains limited. It's unclear *why* the network focuses on specific features in certain situations. Further investigation into the learned policy could provide valuable insights.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison is to a Maxout network, but it would be beneficial to compare against more modern and widely-used architectures (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet) pre-trained on ImageNet. This would provide a better benchmark of the true impact of dasNet.  The claim of “state-of-the-art” on a specific dataset should be carefully qualified, as it's unclear how this compares to recent results on the same data with more recent architectures.
* **SNES Justification:** While the paper explains *why* SNES was chosen, a brief discussion of the limitations compared to full NES could strengthen the argument.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through learned internal attention.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance improvement on challenging classification instances.
* The work has the potential to advance our understanding of how to build more robust and interpretable CNNs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost is a significant limitation.
* The lack of comprehensive ablation studies and comparison to modern architectures weakens the claim of state-of-the-art performance.
* The interpretability of the learned attention policy remains limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a compelling and novel approach to improving CNN performance. The core idea of learning internal selective attention through reinforcement learning is promising, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the computational cost and lack of extensive ablation studies are concerns, the potential benefits of this approach outweigh the drawbacks.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should address the concerns regarding computational cost, provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art architectures, and consider adding further ablation studies to understand the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. A more detailed discussion of the learned attention policies would also strengthen the paper.
