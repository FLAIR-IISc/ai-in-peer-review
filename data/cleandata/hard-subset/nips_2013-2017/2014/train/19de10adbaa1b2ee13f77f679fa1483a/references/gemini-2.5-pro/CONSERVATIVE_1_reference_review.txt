### Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a method for improving the performance of a pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) on difficult classification tasks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) with a control policy that can modulate the activations of feature maps over several processing passes for a single image. After an initial feedforward pass, the internal state of the network is used as an observation for a policy. This policy then outputs a set of weights that enhance or suppress entire feature maps in each layer for the next pass. This iterative process is framed as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem, where the policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to minimize classification error, with a focus on previously misclassified examples. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves classification accuracy over a baseline Maxout network on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper proposes an interesting and intuitive idea: mimicking the human ability to "re-think" or apply focused attention to challenging visual stimuli. The formulation of this as a post-hoc control problem on a pre-trained network is a novel combination of supervised and reinforcement learning.

2.  **Improvement on a Strong Baseline:** The method is applied to a Maxout network, which was a state-of-the-art architecture at the time. Demonstrating a performance improvement on top of such a strong model is a non-trivial achievement.

3.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The introduction provides a compelling motivation for moving beyond purely feedforward architectures, drawing parallels to recurrent and attentional processing in the human visual cortex to handle ambiguity.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is appealing, the paper suffers from significant methodological and experimental shortcomings that undermine the strength of its conclusions.

1.  **Questionable Choice of Learning Algorithm:** The decision to use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization algorithm, to train the control policy is highly unconventional and insufficiently justified. The paper states this is due to the large number of parameters, but standard policy gradient methods (e.g., REINFORCE, A2C, TRPO) are the established approach for such problems and are routinely used to train policies with millions of parameters. The paper fails to compare against or even discuss these standard RL techniques. This makes the approach appear ad-hoc and disconnected from mainstream reinforcement learning research, raising concerns about its efficiency and scalability compared to more principled methods.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is not rigorous enough to support the paper's claims.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The only comparison provided is against the original Maxout network. A key question is how dasNet's performance and computational cost compare to more established techniques for improving accuracy, such as model ensembling. A 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 might be achievable with a simple two-model ensemble, which would likely be far less complex to train and faster at inference than the proposed iterative scheme (which requires T=5 forward passes). The lack of this crucial baseline makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the results.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are confined to the small-scale CIFAR datasets. It is unclear if this approach would be feasible or effective on higher-resolution images like those in ImageNet, where the number of feature maps and the dimensionality of the policy's action space would be substantially larger. The paper does not address the scalability of its method.
    *   **Superficial Analysis:** The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is based on a single, potentially cherry-picked, example. While illustrative, it does not provide systematic evidence of how the attention mechanism operates. The experiment testing the information content of the final gate values is interesting but only confirms that the gates are correlated with the final output, not that the learned policy is implementing a particularly sophisticated or generalizable strategy.

3.  **Coarse "Attention" Mechanism:** The proposed mechanism modulates entire feature maps with a single scalar weight. This is a very coarse form of attention that lacks any spatial specificity. It is unclear why this global modulation is preferable to more localized attention mechanisms. Furthermore, the state representation provided to the policy, which relies on the average activation of each map, discards all spatial information, which seems suboptimal for a visual control task. The design choices for the state and action spaces feel simplistic and are not experimentally justified against alternatives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and creative synthesis of deep learning and reinforcement learning to address iterative inference.
*   The results show a clear, albeit modest, improvement over a strong baseline model on standard benchmarks.
*   The high-level motivation is compelling and could stimulate further research into recurrent and attentional processing in deep networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The use of SNES instead of standard, well-established policy gradient methods is a major methodological weakness that is not adequately justified.
*   The experimental comparison is incomplete, failing to include crucial baselines like model ensembling that would properly contextualize the performance gains versus the significant increase in computational complexity.
*   The evaluation is limited to small-scale datasets, and the scalability of the approach is unaddressed.
*   The analysis of the learned mechanism is superficial and relies on anecdotal evidence rather than rigorous, systematic investigation.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces an intriguing proof-of-concept for improving CNN performance through an RL-trained attentional control policy. However, the work in its current form does not meet the standards of rigor expected for NIPS. The unconventional choice of learning algorithm, the lack of comparison to critical baselines, and the limited scope of the experiments make it difficult to evaluate the true contribution and practicality of the proposed method. While the idea is promising, the paper requires a more methodologically sound foundation and a far more thorough empirical evaluation before its claims can be considered well-supported. I would encourage the authors to re-evaluate their choice of RL algorithm and to compare their approach against simpler, established methods for improving classification accuracy.
