Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method to add internal, selective attention to trained convolutional networks via recurrent feedback over multiple passes on the same input. The base model is a Maxout CNN trained with supervised learning on CIFAR-10/100. After initial training, the network is augmented with multiplicative gates aℓj that scale each feature map before maxout pooling. On each pass t, the model constructs an observation vector from summary statistics of intermediate activations and the current class probabilities. A deterministic policy πθ maps this observation to the next set of gates, and the image is reprocessed. The policy parameters (≈1M) are optimized using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), with a loss that upweights misclassified samples, to improve the final classification after T passes. The authors motivate this design by analogy to top-down attention and feedback in biological vision and cite relevant neuroscience and prior RL-based attention work. They position their contribution as bringing recurrent, attention-like mechanisms to state-of-the-art CNNs and demonstrating accuracy gains on standard benchmarks.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The gating mechanism is straightforward and differentiable. The policy takes summary observations (average feature map activations, classification-layer pre-activations, and softmax outputs) and outputs positive gates via a scaled softmax, stabilizing the mean around 1. The training loop is well specified, including SNES population size, step count T, and a misclassification-boosted loss. However:
  - The choice of SNES over gradient-based policy learning is justified primarily by parameter count; backpropagation through time with appropriate regularization could be feasible and might be more sample-efficient. No comparison is provided.
  - Some equations/notation are unclear or contain apparent errors: in Eq. (10), d simultaneously denotes the correct class and is compared to ||vT||∞; the intent (e.g., using indicator of correct prediction) should be clarified. The exact construction and dimensionality of o are only partially specified.
  - No ablation is reported on the observation components, gating per layer, or the effect of different constraints on a (e.g., bounding gate magnitudes).
  - Computational cost and stability: There is limited reporting on test-time overhead (T passes) and memory/runtime impact of the added policy. The “Dynamics” study suggests stability beyond T, which is positive, but broader analysis of convergence and sensitivity to T is limited.

Clarity:
The paper’s high-level idea and algorithmic flow are clear. The description of the base Maxout architecture and the gating mechanism is understandable. The training algorithm is outlined. Areas for improved clarity include: fixing notation inconsistencies (Eq. 10), detailing the observation vector construction and normalization, reporting exact dataset preprocessing and augmentation (the text both mentions augmentation and later claims unaugmented SOTA), and providing the full results table referenced in the text. A precise statement of the policy network architecture (if any nonlinearity is used beyond a linear softmax layer) would help reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of:
- internal, multiplicative gating of feature maps across multiple passes,
- a learned global (non-spatial) attention policy that conditions on network activations,
- and the use of black-box evolution strategies to train the policy on top of a fixed CNN,
is a novel configuration relative to prior feedforward CNNs and prior RL attention works focused on saccades/glimpses or toy datasets. The paper is well-situated in biological feedback literature and prior RL/recurrency in vision. While recurrent and attention-like mechanisms have been explored, applying them as post-hoc corrective feedback to a strong CNN and showing measurable gains on CIFAR is a meaningful step.

Significance:
The method yields improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, and the qualitative analysis suggests that gates modulate deeper features in useful ways. If the claimed result indeed establishes a new state of the art (for the specified evaluation setting), the contribution is practically relevant. However, the empirical scope is limited to CIFAR; comparisons to other recurrent/attention baselines and to simpler alternatives (e.g., fine-tuning with additional capacity, learned per-channel scaling without recurrence, or gating trained end-to-end via backprop) are missing. Without those, it is difficult to isolate where the gains come from (recurrence vs. gating vs. misclassification-focused training). Reporting compute/latency trade-offs would also strengthen the case for significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, simple mechanism to add feedback-driven selective attention to CNNs.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains on standard benchmarks with a strong baseline.
- Biological and algorithmic motivation is coherent; method remains end-to-end differentiable, suggesting broader applicability.
- Initial analysis of dynamics and gate information content supports that the policy learns meaningful control signals.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited experimental scope and missing baselines (no comparison to gradient-based recurrent policies, attention/gating alternatives, or non-recurrent channel-scaling).
- Clarity issues in equations and some inconsistencies around augmentation and SOTA claims; incomplete reporting of results table and exact hyperparameters.
- Justification for SNES is mostly pragmatic; lack of ablation on learning method and policy architecture.
- Computational overhead and inference-time cost not quantified; scalability to larger datasets unresolved.

Questions for the authors:
- Please clarify Eq. (10), define d precisely, and correct any typographical errors affecting the loss.
- What is the exact composition and dimensionality of the observation vector? Are observations normalized per-layer or per-batch?
- What is the architecture of πθ (purely linear into softmax or with hidden layers)? Are gate magnitudes explicitly bounded?
- Provide quantitative test-time overhead for T=2,5 (FLOPs, latency), and accuracy-vs-T trade-offs.
- Compare against: (a) a single-pass per-channel scaling trained end-to-end; (b) a recurrent, gradient-trained policy (BPTT); (c) random or identity gates as controls.
- Clarify the “unaugmented” claim versus the stated use of data augmentation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations on observation components, per-layer gating, and policy complexity.
- Provide comparisons to gradient-based training of the policy and to simpler non-recurrent gating baselines.
- Report confusion-matrix changes and per-class improvements to substantiate the “fixing ambiguous pairs” claim.
- Quantify compute/memory costs and discuss deployment implications.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The paper introduces a conceptually clear and practically effective feedback mechanism that improves strong CNN baselines on CIFAR. The novelty lies in learned internal attention via recurrent gating. The main drawbacks are limited empirical scope, missing baselines, and some clarity issues. With additional experiments and clarifications, the contribution would be solid.
