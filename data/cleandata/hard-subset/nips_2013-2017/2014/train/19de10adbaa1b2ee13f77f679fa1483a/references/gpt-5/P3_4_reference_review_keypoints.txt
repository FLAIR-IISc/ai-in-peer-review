- Paper proposes dasNet: iterative test-time per-channel multiplicative gating controlled by a policy that reprocesses images T times to refine Maxout CNN predictions.
- Policy maps observations (feature-map averages, intermediate activations, class probs) to gates and is trained after CNN training using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES).
- Empirically shows improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a Maxout baseline and claims a new “unaugmented” state-of-the-art.
- Grounded in neuro/psychophysics and relates to recurrent/attention and feedback models; novelty is feature-wise gating across layers controlled by a learned policy.
- Strength: simple, general mechanism for top-down modulation that augments a trained feed-forward CNN without changing its weights.
- Strength: method is orthogonal to architecture and complements discriminative training.
- Strength: measurable empirical gains on CIFAR benchmarks and focus on improving “difficult” samples.
- Strength: preliminary evidence that running more steps than trained does not destabilize predictions.
- Strength: intuitive illustration (cat-vs-dog) clarifies how gates shift evidence among classes.
- Major concern: training the differentiable controller with SNES is under-justified—backprop-through-time is a crucial missing baseline.
- Major concern: controller is effectively linear with softmax, which may bottleneck expressivity and calls into question the need for SNES.
- Ambiguity: the “unaugmented” SOTA claim is unclear since base CNN used data augmentation; exact protocol and numbers need clarification.
- Missing baselines: static per-channel reweighting, gradient-trained dynamic controller, comparable larger feedforward CNN, and multi-pass without gating.
- Reproducibility issues: important architectural and optimization details (maps per layer, block sizes, learning rates, SNES params, generation size n, T sensitivity, augmentation) are incomplete.
- Notational errors/ambiguities in loss equations (e.g., misuse of infinity norm, unclear one-hot/boosted loss) need correction.
- Biological framing overstates realism: feedback is global per-channel gains via a controller, not learned synaptic top-down connectivity.
- Practical concern: T passes increase inference time linearly; paper lacks wall-clock cost and accuracy/compute trade-off reporting.
- Analysis is limited and anecdotal: little quantitative study of confusion reductions, per-layer/per-class gate statistics, or which features are modulated.
- Key questions for authors: why not backprop through time, exact CIFAR error rates and variances, clarification on “unaugmented,” full SNES/architecture/runtime details, and gate constraint effects.
- Suggestions: include ablations (gradient-trained controllers, static gating), report confusion matrices and gate analyses, try modestly deeper controllers trained with SGD, and show accuracy vs T and runtime.
- Significance: iterative internal feature-wise attention is a compelling and relatively novel idea that could influence dynamic feature modulation if validated.
- Pros: simple, general mechanism with promising gains and biological inspiration; potentially influential concept.
- Cons: SNES choice under-justified, missing differentiable baselines, unclear reporting/reproducibility, limited analysis, and potentially high compute for modest gains.
- Recommendation: borderline reject without ablations and clearer reporting; borderline accept if authors provide rigorous comparisons, clarify results, and strengthen analysis.
