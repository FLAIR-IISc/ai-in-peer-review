Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained Maxout CNN with multiplicative, per-feature-map gates that are updated over multiple passes on the same image. A policy network observes summary statistics of layer activations and class probabilities and outputs the gate values for the next pass. The policy is trained post hoc using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted cross-entropy loss measured only after T passes. The goal is to correct difficult, ambiguous classifications by emphasizing/de-emphasizing internal feature maps. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 reportedly improve over a baseline Maxout CNN, with qualitative visualizations and a small analysis of the temporal dynamics.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to a long line of work on recurrent processing and top-down modulation in biological vision, and to RL approaches for visual attention and feedback. On the ML/CV side, related lines include recurrent CNNs/unrolled inference, iterative refinement methods, and prior attempts to use RL for attention. The specific contribution here is an internal, global attention mechanism trained via black-box policy search, applied on top of a state-of-the-art CNN of the time (Maxout). The related work section cites many relevant biological and ML references, though comparisons to purely gradient-based recurrent/gated architectures are not empirically addressed.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing idea: learn to modulate internal CNN features over multiple inference steps to fix ambiguous cases.
- Simple interface to a standard CNN (multiplicative gates) with a policy trained post hoc; no changes to supervised training of the base network.
- Empirical results suggest measurable gains on CIFAR-10/100 over the authors’ baseline, with qualitative evidence that gating changes are nontrivial.
- Some analysis of dynamics (stability beyond the trained T) and an informative “gates carry class information” probe.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation and baselines are insufficient to support strong claims:
  - Lack of comparison to simpler, established alternatives: (i) training the same gates and recurrency end-to-end by backpropagation (single pass or unrolled T passes with shared parameters), (ii) adding per-channel scalars learned supervised without RL, (iii) iterative inference without policy (e.g., averaging T outputs), (iv) standard test-time augmentation/ensembles matched for compute, and (v) conventional recurrent CNNs trained by BPTT.
  - The policy is effectively a linear-softmax map on global summary features, trained with SNES using only the final-step loss. It is unclear SNES is necessary or advantageous versus gradient-based training; no ablation vs REINFORCE/policy gradients or even random search. Using diagonal covariance further limits search power for a million-parameter policy.
  - Reported “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data” is confusing given the base Maxout model is trained with data augmentation. Precise numbers, settings, and comparisons to contemporary strong baselines are missing from the text provided (Table 1 is referenced but not shown).
- Methodological choices require justification:
  - Observations discard spatial information (averages over feature maps), so the mechanism is not “selective attention” in the usual spatial sense; it is global, per-channel modulation. Why should this suffice, and what is the trade-off versus spatial attention?
  - Only final-step loss is optimized; no intermediate rewards. Credit assignment across steps is weak and depends on black-box optimization.
  - The softmax-normalized, positive gates enforce average 1. Why not allow unconstrained gates? Any evidence that positivity or normalization is crucial?
- Reproducibility and rigor:
  - Many training details are missing (learning schedules, exact architecture, SNES hyperparameters, stopping criteria, selection of T, validation protocol). No code.
  - No statistical significance or confidence intervals. No held-out validation set is discussed; risk of overfitting the policy to the training set.
  - Compute/latency overhead at test time scales with T; the trade-off vs. alternative approaches is not quantified.
- Generality is unclear: Results are limited to CIFAR-10/100. No evidence of scalability to larger images/datasets.

Quality
Technically plausible but under-evaluated. The design choices (global per-channel gates, linear policy, SNES with diagonal covariance, final-step-only objective) are not sufficiently justified, and ablations against simpler baselines are missing. The claimed improvements may be real, but the evidence does not isolate the source of gains or demonstrate necessity of RL/SNES.

Clarity
Writing is generally clear and the high-level algorithm is understandable. Important experimental details and the full quantitative table are missing in the provided text. The connection between “unaugmented” SOTA and the described training with augmentation needs clarification.

Originality
Moderate. The combination—global feature-map gating over multiple passes trained with black-box policy search—has novelty. However, per-channel modulation, recurrent inference, and RL for attention are established ideas; the novelty lies in their specific integration.

Significance
Currently limited by the evaluation scope and lack of strong baselines. Without convincing comparisons to simpler, more established training schemes, it is hard to endorse the RL/SNES machinery as necessary or impactful.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide the exact CIFAR-10/100 numbers (error rates) for both baseline and dasNet, including variance across runs, and clarify whether training used augmentation while the test protocol is “unaugmented.”
2. How does dasNet compare to: (a) per-channel gates trained end-to-end by backprop in a single pass, (b) unrolled T-step recurrent gating trained by BPTT, (c) test-time augmentation or self-ensembling matched in compute?
3. Why SNES over gradient-based training of the policy (e.g., deterministic policy gradients, REINFORCE with baselines)? Any ablation?
4. What is the added inference time and parameter overhead? How does accuracy trade off with T?
5. Do gains concentrate on specific confusion pairs? Provide a confusion matrix analysis.
6. Is there a validation set for selecting T and λ parameters? Any signs of overfitting?

Pros
- Interesting, biologically inspired idea; simple interface to CNNs.
- Some empirical improvement on standard benchmarks.
- Analysis suggests the gates capture class-relevant information.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear necessity of RL/SNES.
- Limited datasets; unclear generalization and compute trade-offs.
- Important experimental details and exact results are under-specified.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the current empirical evidence and comparative analysis are not strong enough to justify acceptance. A revised version with thorough baselines (especially gradient-based recurrent/gated alternatives), clearer and complete results, and broader evaluation would be much more compelling.

Confidence
High.
