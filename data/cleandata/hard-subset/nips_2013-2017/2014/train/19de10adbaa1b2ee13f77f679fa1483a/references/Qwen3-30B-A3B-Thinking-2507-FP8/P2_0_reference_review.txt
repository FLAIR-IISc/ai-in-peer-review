

# Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach to improving CNN performance by enabling internal selective attention through feedback connections. The core idea is to allow a pre-trained CNN to iteratively refine its classification of ambiguous images by modulating filter activations through a reinforcement learning policy. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture and employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a policy that controls filter weights across multiple passes through the image. The method shows a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, establishing a new state-of-the-art for these datasets without data augmentation. The work is well-motivated by neuroscience (feedback connections in visual cortex) and addresses a real limitation of current CNNs: their inability to "think" about ambiguous cases.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The approach is well-defined with a clear algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the choice of SNES for reinforcement learning is well-justified given the large parameter space (over 1 million weights). The experimental design is solid, with appropriate ablation studies (Figure 2) and qualitative analysis of filter activations (Figure 3). The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses, acknowledging that "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect of the alterations" and that the "dynamics stay stable when evaluated for as many as 10 steps" even though the network was trained with only T=5 steps.

The paper is technically complete rather than a position paper, providing sufficient detail for replication. The authors are careful to note limitations, such as the difficulty in interpreting the complex suppression and enhancement patterns shown in Figure 3. The results are well-supported by the experiments, with the 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 being a meaningful advancement.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution within both neuroscience and computer vision literature. The technical sections (Maxout Networks, Reinforcement Learning, dasNet) are logically structured and provide sufficient detail for experts to understand the methodology.

One minor weakness is that the explanation of the "observation" vector (Section 4) could be clearer. While the authors describe it as "the average activation of every output map... of each Maxout layer," "the intermediate activations ȳj of the classification layer," and "the class probability vector," it's not entirely clear how these components are concatenated or weighted in the observation space. A more detailed explanation would improve clarity.

The paper provides enough detail for reproduction, though some implementation specifics (e.g., exact SNES hyperparameters, training details for the Maxout network) might be helpful in the supplementary material.

## Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution by applying reinforcement learning to control internal attention in CNNs through feedback connections. This is distinct from previous work in several ways:

1. Unlike most attention mechanisms that focus on spatial attention (e.g., [40, 41]), dasNet implements internal selective attention by modulating filter weights rather than spatial regions.

2. The authors explicitly connect their work to neuroscience (Section 5), noting that "top-down connections are more numerous than bottom-up connections" in the visual cortex, which provides a compelling biological motivation.

3. Crucially, the paper applies this approach directly to state-of-the-art CNNs (Maxout networks) rather than simplified architectures, as is common in related work (e.g., [48, 49, 50]).

The paper adequately references related work, including recent NIPS papers on attention mechanisms and reinforcement learning. The authors clearly differentiate their contribution from prior work by emphasizing that "this research is often applied to simplified datasets for demonstration purposes due to computation constraints, and are not aimed at improving the state-of-the-art. In contrast, we apply this perspective directly to the known state-of-the-art neural networks to show that this approach is now feasible and actually increases performance."

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Current CNNs are limited in their ability to handle ambiguous cases, which is a critical limitation for real-world applications. The dasNet approach directly addresses this by allowing the network to "think" about ambiguous cases through multiple internal passes, much like humans do when classifying difficult cases (e.g., distinguishing similar bird species).

The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is meaningful, especially since it's achieved on unaugmented data. The paper demonstrates a clear advancement of the state-of-the-art, which is a key criterion for NIPS acceptance. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 provides valuable insights into how the attention mechanism operates, showing that the system "successfully disambiguates a cat from a dog" by focusing on specific features.

The approach also has potential for broader applications beyond classification, as noted in the conclusion: "Future research will also consider more complex actions that spatially focus on (or alter) parts of observed images."

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated by neuroscience (feedback connections in visual cortex)
- Clear demonstration of a 6% relative improvement over state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100
- Novel application of reinforcement learning to control internal filter weights
- Good qualitative analysis of how attention mechanisms work (Figure 3)
- Well-justified use of SNES for the large parameter space
- Clear distinction from previous work in the related work section

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited analysis of computational cost (how much longer does inference take?)
- No comparison with other recurrent or attention-based approaches (e.g., transformers, memory networks)
- Unclear why reinforcement learning is necessary rather than end-to-end training of the feedback connections
- Limited analysis of what specific types of misclassifications are corrected
- No discussion of scaling to larger datasets or more complex tasks

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses a real limitation of current CNNs with a novel approach that is well-motivated by neuroscience and demonstrates clear performance improvements on standard benchmarks. The technical quality is high, and the work is well-executed and well-documented.

The main weaknesses are the limited analysis of computational cost and lack of comparison with other attention mechanisms. However, these do not significantly detract from the paper's overall contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of deep learning, addressing a fundamental limitation of current CNNs with a novel approach that is both theoretically well-motivated and empirically validated. The results represent a meaningful advancement of the state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks, and the approach has clear potential for broader applications.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear distinction from previous work. While there are some minor weaknesses in the analysis of computational cost and comparison with other approaches, these do not undermine the paper's main contribution. The work is highly relevant to NIPS and represents a valuable addition to the literature on deep learning and attention mechanisms.
