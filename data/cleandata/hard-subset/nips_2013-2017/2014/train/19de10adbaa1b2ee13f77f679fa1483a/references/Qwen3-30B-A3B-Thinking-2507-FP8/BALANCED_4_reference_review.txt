

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which enhances deep CNNs by incorporating selective attention through feedback connections. The authors propose a reinforcement learning approach where a pre-trained CNN is augmented with attentional gates that can be adjusted across multiple passes over the same image. The policy for adjusting these gates is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), which is well-suited for the high-dimensional parameter space. The method is motivated by human visual processing, where multiple feedforward passes may be needed to distinguish between similar objects (e.g., bird species), and aims to mimic this capability in CNNs.

The paper builds on previous work in Maxout networks [7], reinforcement learning for attention [40-45], and the neuroscience of visual processing [28-39]. It distinguishes itself from prior attention mechanisms (which typically focus on external spatial attention) by introducing internal selective attention through feedback connections that modulate filter activations within the network itself.

**Strengths:**
1. The core idea of using reinforcement learning to control internal attention mechanisms is novel and well-motivated by human vision.
2. The implementation is technically sound, with appropriate use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space.
3. The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 show a clear 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs, with qualitative analysis demonstrating how the attention mechanism corrects misclassifications.
4. The paper provides clear visualizations (Figure 3) showing how attention changes across layers during successive passes, which helps understand the mechanism.
5. The authors properly acknowledge limitations, including computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) and the difficulty of analyzing high-level filter changes.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The significance of the 6% improvement is somewhat limited, as it's modest compared to recent advances in CNN architectures (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets). The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with recent methods that also use iterative refinement or attention mechanisms.
2. The paper lacks a thorough theoretical justification for why this approach works, beyond the biological motivation. The connection to neuroscience is interesting but not deeply leveraged to inform the design.
3. The computational cost of the approach (4 days for dasNet training) may limit its practical adoption, especially compared to simpler post-processing techniques.
4. The paper doesn't adequately address whether the attention mechanism is learning meaningful representations or merely memorizing specific misclassifications.
5. The evaluation focuses primarily on CIFAR datasets; it would be valuable to see results on more challenging datasets like ImageNet or with more diverse image types.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments, but the theoretical grounding could be stronger. The claims about the attention mechanism improving classification are supported by experimental results, though the magnitude of improvement is modest.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear algorithm descriptions. The visualization of attention changes (Figure 3) is particularly helpful. However, some details about the observation/action space dimensions could be clarified.

**Originality:** The approach of using reinforcement learning to control internal attention through feedback connections in a pre-trained CNN is novel. While there is related work on attention mechanisms and recurrent processing, dasNet's specific implementation with SNES for policy learning represents a significant departure from previous approaches.

**Significance:** The method could be significant for improving CNN performance on difficult classification cases, but the modest improvement on CIFAR datasets and lack of comparison with recent architectures limits its perceived significance. The approach would be more compelling if it demonstrated consistent improvements across multiple datasets or architectures.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection, but with constructive feedback for potential revision.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach to internal attention in CNNs
- Demonstrated improvement on challenging datasets
- Strong biological motivation
- Clear experimental evaluation with qualitative analysis

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Modest performance improvement compared to recent advances
- Limited theoretical justification
- High computational cost without clear justification
- Inadequate comparison with recent methods that also use iterative refinement or attention

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Conduct a more comprehensive comparison with recent attention-based methods and iterative refinement approaches (e.g., recent NIPS papers on residual learning or reconstruction-based attention).
2. Provide a stronger theoretical justification for the approach, perhaps by analyzing the information content of the attention mechanism.
3. Evaluate the method on a larger dataset like ImageNet to demonstrate broader applicability.
4. Investigate whether the attention mechanism generalizes to different architectures beyond Maxout networks.
5. Address the computational cost more thoroughly, perhaps by exploring ways to reduce the training time or make the approach more efficient.

The paper presents an interesting idea with potential for future work, but the current version doesn't sufficiently demonstrate its significance compared to recent advances in the field. With more comprehensive comparisons and a stronger theoretical foundation, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field.
