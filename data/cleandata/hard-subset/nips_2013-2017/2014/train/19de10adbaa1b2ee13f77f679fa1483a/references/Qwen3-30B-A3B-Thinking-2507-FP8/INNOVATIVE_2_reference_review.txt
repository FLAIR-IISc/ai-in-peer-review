

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that enables deep CNNs to adapt their behavior post-training through internal selective attention mechanisms. The core idea is that CNNs can improve classification by iteratively focusing attention on different features through multiple passes over the same image, rather than relying solely on a single feedforward pass. The authors propose a reinforcement learning framework where a policy is learned to modulate filter activations across successive passes, with the goal of correcting misclassifications on difficult examples.

The paper makes a strong conceptual contribution by connecting computer vision to human visual processing. The motivation that experts take more time to classify difficult cases (e.g., similar bird species) and thus require multiple "evaluations" of the image is compelling. This leads to the novel formulation of a non-stationary CNN that can adapt its behavior post-training, which is a significant departure from standard feedforward architectures.

The technical approach is innovative in its use of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn the attention policy due to the large parameter space (over 1 million parameters). The dasNet architecture modifies standard Maxout networks by allowing filter weights to change across passes, with the attention policy learned through reinforcement learning. The paper demonstrates a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 compared to vanilla CNNs, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this dataset without data augmentation. The qualitative examples showing how the network corrects misclassifications (e.g., cat vs. dog) are particularly convincing, with visualizations showing how filter activations change across passes to improve classification.

The paper's strengths lie in its strong conceptual novelty and clear motivation. The connection to human vision research (Felleman and Essen's work on visual cortex connections) provides a solid theoretical foundation. The authors successfully demonstrate that dasNet can fix misclassifications without disrupting previously learned knowledge, which is a significant challenge in continual learning. The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space is a practical and well-justified choice.

While the paper is strong conceptually, it has some methodological limitations. The paper doesn't fully rule out that the improvement could come from simply making multiple passes rather than from the attention mechanism itself. A control experiment comparing dasNet to a version that makes multiple passes with fixed attention would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU) is substantial, though the authors note this is after the initial CNN training. The paper also doesn't fully address how dasNet compares to the cost of training larger networks to achieve similar performance.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution within both neuroscience and computer vision literature, differentiating dasNet from previous approaches that often used simplified datasets.

In terms of the four criteria:

**Originality**: The paper is highly original. While there has been work on recurrent networks and attention mechanisms, dasNet's specific approach of enabling internal selective attention through feedback connections on already-trained networks is novel. The connection to human visual processing provides a fresh perspective that hasn't been explored in this way in deep learning.

**Significance**: The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is significant for this challenging dataset. More importantly, the conceptual contribution of enabling networks to "think" about an image multiple times to improve classification has broader implications for the field. This could inspire new architectures for tasks requiring careful consideration of ambiguous cases.

**Quality**: The methodology is sound, though the paper could have provided more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism. The experimental results are well-presented and convincing for the specific tasks evaluated.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured arguments and effective visualizations of the attention mechanism in action.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be highly compelling. The core concept of "internal selective attention" through feedback connections represents a bold new direction that could significantly influence how we think about deep network processing. While the methodological limitations are worth noting, they don't undermine the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in a novel way, with results that demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The connection to human vision research adds depth to the contribution, making it more than just a technical improvement but a conceptual advance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper's novelty and conceptual contribution outweigh its methodological limitations. The authors have presented a compelling new direction for deep network architectures that could inspire significant future work in the field. The demonstration of the network "correcting itself" on difficult classification instances is particularly impressive and aligns with the reviewer's emphasis on bold approaches.
