This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, fast feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "internal" passes over the same input. In these subsequent passes, a control policy, learned via reinforcement learning (RL), modulates the activations of filter maps throughout the network to refine the classification. This process is motivated by the human visual system, which combines a rapid pre-attentive phase with a slower, more deliberate attentional phase. The authors apply this method to a state-of-the-art Maxout network, training the control policy with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle the high-dimensional action space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that dasNet can correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its novel and conceptually elegant approach to improving deep learning models. The idea of decoupling the initial feature extraction (learned via supervised learning) from a subsequent, adaptive attentional process (learned via RL) is compelling. It addresses a fundamental limitation of standard CNNs: their static nature at inference time. This "thinking for longer" paradigm has the potential to be a significant contribution, opening up new research directions in recurrent and adaptive neural architectures.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its approach by achieving a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (for unaugmented data). A relative improvement of 6% over a strong baseline (Maxout network) is a non-trivial achievement and provides convincing evidence for the method's utility.

3.  **Clever Problem Formulation:** Framing the modulation of internal filter activations as a sequential decision-making problem is an excellent fit for RL. The choice of SNES, a black-box optimization method, is a pragmatic and well-justified solution to the challenge of training a policy with over a million parameters, where traditional policy gradient methods might struggle.

4.  **Biological Motivation:** The work is well-grounded in neuroscience literature on the dual-pathway nature of human vision. This provides a strong conceptual foundation and makes the architectural choices intuitive.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Cost and Baselines:** The most significant weakness is the substantial computational overhead introduced at inference time. A single prediction requires *T*+1 forward passes through the network. The paper does not compare its method against alternative approaches that use a similar computational budget. For instance:
    *   How does dasNet with *T*=5 compare to an ensemble of 5-6 different Maxout networks?
    *   How does it compare to a single, larger feedforward network designed to have a roughly equivalent FLOP count or inference time?
    Without these crucial baselines, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gain comes from the proposed attentional mechanism itself or simply from the increased computation. The claim of advancing the state of the art is weakened if a simpler, computationally equivalent method performs just as well.

2.  **RL Formulation Details:** The RL formulation, while functional, raises several questions:
    *   **State Representation (Observation `o_t`):** The observation is constructed by concatenating average activations of output maps, intermediate activations, and the final class probability vector. This is a massive compression of the network's full state. What is the justification for this specific choice? An ablation study showing the importance of each component of the observation vector would be valuable. It feels somewhat ad-hoc.
    *   **Reward Sparsity:** The reward is only provided at the final timestep *T*. This is a very sparse signal, which can make learning difficult. Have the authors considered providing intermediate rewards at each step to guide the learning process more effectively?
    *   **Policy Network:** The policy is a simple linear transformation followed by a scaled softmax. Given the complexity of the control problem, it is surprising that such a simple policy is sufficient. Was a more complex, non-linear policy (e.g., a small MLP) explored?

3.  **Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper provides some initial analysis (Figure 3, dynamics plot, gate information content), which is commendable. However, this could be deepened.
    *   The visualization in Figure 3 is intriguing but, as the authors admit, hard to interpret. Are there consistent strategies the policy learns? For example, does it consistently suppress certain feature maps when trying to distinguish between "cat" and "dog"? Analyzing the learned policy weights (`θ`) or the average action vectors for specific class pairs could yield more insight.
    *   The experiment measuring the information content of the final gate values is clever. However, the performance of k-NN (40.7%) and logistic regression (45.74%) is not "similar to the performance of dasNet" (which should be ~88% on CIFAR-10). It confirms the gates contain *some* information, but it doesn't seem to be the full picture. This point should be rephrased more carefully.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the paper is generally well-written, some details are sparse. The exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters, kernel sizes) is not fully specified. The details of the SNES update are deferred to supplementary material, which is acceptable, but key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size *n*) should be in the main text.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality, innovative paper that introduces a genuinely new and promising idea. The combination of pre-trained deep networks with a post-hoc, RL-trained attentional mechanism is a significant conceptual contribution. The strong empirical results on CIFAR-10 validate the approach's potential. However, the paper's claims would be substantially strengthened by addressing the major weakness regarding computational cost and the lack of iso-computational baselines. Further exploration and justification of the RL formulation choices and a deeper analysis of the learned policies would also improve the work. Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the core idea are high, making this a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend this paper for acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the baseline comparison in their final version.
