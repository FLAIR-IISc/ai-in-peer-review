This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for improving CNN performance through iterative refinement of feature activations using a reinforcement learning (RL) policy. The core idea is to allow feedback connections between layers, modulating filter responses on successive passes over an image, guided by a policy learned via SNES. The motivation stems from observations of human visual processing, which often involves multiple passes to resolve ambiguity. The paper demonstrates performance gains on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, particularly on difficult instances.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the dasNet architecture and training process. It’s commendable that the authors address a biologically-inspired concept (selective attention) and translate it into a concrete neural network architecture. The use of SNES is justified due to the large parameter space, and the experimental results on CIFAR datasets are promising, showing an improvement over the baseline Maxout network. The analysis of the filter activations and dynamics provides valuable insights into how the network learns to refine its classifications. The related work section is comprehensive, acknowledging both classical and recent research in attention mechanisms, recurrent processing, and reinforcement learning.

However, several aspects require further attention. While the results are promising, the magnitude of the improvement (6% relative) feels somewhat modest given the complexity added by the RL component and the computational cost of training. The paper doesn’t sufficiently compare against more recent and powerful attention mechanisms commonly used today (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation, CBAM).  A comparison to these methods would better contextualize the contribution of dasNet.  Furthermore, the experiments primarily focus on CIFAR datasets.  Extending the evaluation to more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is crucial to demonstrate the scalability and generalizability of the approach. The analysis of the learned policies is relatively limited. Understanding *what* the policy learns to focus on—beyond simply showing activation changes—would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, visualizing the filters that are being selectively emphasized or suppressed in relation to the input image would be beneficial. The choice of T=5 seems somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough investigation of the optimal number of iterations, potentially adapting it based on image difficulty, would be valuable. The ablation study could be more detailed, specifically analyzing the impact of each component of the observation vector used by the RL policy (average activations, intermediate activations, class probabilities).  Finally, while the connection to human visual processing is a good motivating factor, the paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of this analogy. Human attention is far more complex than simply weighting filter activations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating attention into CNNs through RL and feedback connections.
*   Clear and well-written explanation of the method.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a strong baseline on CIFAR datasets.
*   Comprehensive related work section.
*   Interesting analysis of filter activation dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest improvement in performance.
*   Lack of comparison to modern attention mechanisms.
*   Limited evaluation on relatively simple datasets.
*   Limited analysis of the learned policies.
*   Arbitrary choice of the number of iterations (T).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and well-executed idea. However, the limited scope of the experiments and the modest performance gains raise concerns about its overall impact. While the contribution is novel, it needs further validation on more challenging datasets and in comparison to existing state-of-the-art attention mechanisms. 

**Recommendation:** Reject.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include experiments on ImageNet or other large-scale datasets.
*   **Compare to state-of-the-art attention mechanisms:** Benchmark dasNet against Squeeze-and-Excitation, CBAM, and other commonly used attention modules.
*   **Analyze the learned policies in more detail:** Visualize the filters being selectively emphasized/suppressed, and investigate the relationship between the learned actions and image features.
*   **Investigate the optimal number of iterations (T):**  Explore adaptive iteration strategies based on image difficulty.
*   **Perform a more detailed ablation study:** Analyze the impact of each component of the observation vector.
*   **Discuss the limitations of the biological analogy:**  Acknowledge the complexity of human attention and how dasNet provides a simplified model.
