- Introduces dasNet: adds learned feedback connections to CNNs to iteratively modulate feature activations across multiple passes.  
- Core innovation: learns a selective attention policy via reinforcement learning (SNES) to enhance/suppress filters and correct misclassifications.  
- Biological motivation: models top-down feedback observed in visual cortex and a "first pass, then refine" recognition process.  
- Distinguishes from prior recurrent approaches by learning a post-training attention policy on a pre-trained network.  
- Uses SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space pragmatically when standard RL is infeasible.  
- Experimental focus: targets improving classification on ambiguous/difficult examples and analyzes activation changes across layers.  
- Empirical result: achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks on CIFAR-10, claiming a new state-of-the-art for that setup.  
- Strength: clear conceptual motivation and intuitive iterative refinement approach grounded in biology.  
- Strength: careful experiments and insightful activation analyses that illuminate the attention mechanism.  
- Strength: pragmatic implementation choice (SNES) justified by scalability concerns.  
- Weakness: high computational cost (training ~4 days on GTX 560 Ti) with unclear scalability to larger models/datasets.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to modern attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, Transformers, iterative refinement methods).  
- Weakness: lack of strong theoretical justification for the RL/SNES choice; feels somewhat ad-hoc.  
- Weakness: modest performance gains relative to computational complexity and unclear transfer to harder benchmarks or real-world tasks.  
- Weakness: interpretability remains limited despite qualitative activation analyses.  
- Recommendation: weak accept — novel and well-motivated but practical limitations (efficiency, limited comparisons, modest gains) reduce impact; authors should address scalability and broader comparisons.
