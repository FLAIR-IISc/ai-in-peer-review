Summary
This paper augments a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with learned feedback that modulates internal feature maps across multiple passes on the same image. Concretely, each feature map in each layer is multiplied by a learnable gate aℓj that changes from pass to pass. A policy π maps observations (average activations per map, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to the action vector a, thereby implementing selective internal attention. The policy is learned after the CNN is trained, via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the very high dimensionality of the observation/action spaces. At test time, an image is run for T passes; the first pass is the vanilla CNN, subsequent passes use the policy’s gates; the final loss emphasizes misclassified samples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent improvements over the base Maxout CNN, with qualitative analyses suggesting nontrivial emphasis/de-emphasis patterns across layers and stable dynamics beyond the training horizon.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on maxout CNNs with dropout. It connects to biological feedback and top-down modulation and to RL-based attention/glimpses and recurrent processing in vision. Unlike saccade/glimpse models that operate on spatial crops, this paper proposes internal, per-feature-map gating across all layers, learned post hoc via black-box policy search. Compared with prior iterative/recurrent vision models and top-down feedback, this paper’s novelty lies in (i) dense, learned feedback connections that gate the entire representational hierarchy and (ii) training the attention policy with SNES at scale.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clear, distinct proposal to endow a standard CNN with internal selective attention via learned feedback, without changing the base supervised training. The approach is conceptually simple and widely applicable.
- Empirical signal: On CIFAR-10/100, the multi-pass feedback improves accuracy over a strong maxout baseline; dynamics are stable for more steps than trained; gate vectors alone carry class information.
- Biological plausibility and engineering practicality: The mechanism mirrors top-down modulation, yet is implementable with standard components. It offers a pathway to improve a frozen model at test time.
- Clarity of the core idea: The gating mechanism, observation design, and SNES-based training loop are well explained and easy to reproduce in spirit.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope: Results are limited to CIFAR-10/100 at 32×32; no evaluation on larger-scale or higher-resolution datasets, and only one base architecture (maxout). It is unclear how gains scale with modern CNNs.
- Baselines/ablations: Missing comparisons to (1) simple post-hoc calibration or logit reweighting; (2) end-to-end differentiable recurrent gating trained via BPTT; (3) per-layer (not per-map) gates; (4) supervised training of the policy (e.g., mapping observations to gates with cross-entropy on final prediction); (5) ensembling multiple passes without feedback. Ablations on observation components and λboost are also absent.
- Efficiency and practicality: Training the policy with SNES (p=50, ~1M parameters) is computationally heavy and potentially sample-inefficient; wall-clock/time-to-improvement and memory costs are not quantified. The choice of SNES vs. scalable policy gradients is justified mainly by dimensionality, but empirical comparison is missing.
- Design choices: Actions are global per-map scalars (no spatial selectivity), which may limit handling of clutter/occlusion. The reward is final-step cross-entropy with boosted weights; intermediate rewards or curriculum are unexplored.
- Analysis depth: Qualitative visualization is compelling but anecdotal; more systematic analyses of when/why corrections occur (e.g., confusion pairs) would strengthen the case.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The training algorithm and architecture are coherent, and the multi-pass mechanism is well specified. Claims are supported on CIFAR with sensible diagnostics (stability, gate informativeness). However, the experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines and ablations that would isolate the contribution of feedback vs. generic test-time ensembling or post-hoc calibration. Reproducibility would benefit from fuller training details (exact architecture/filter sizes, learning schedules) and open-sourcing.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The description of observations/actions and the policy training loop is easy to follow. Minor issues: some notation is cramped; several hyperparameters and architectural details are not fully specified; table/figure references should be more explicit and self-contained.

Originality
High. The idea of evolving a high-dimensional policy to modulate internal CNN feature maps across recurrent passes is distinct from prior spatial-attention/saccade models and from typical recurrent CNNs. The internal gating of all layers as a test-time corrective mechanism is a novel and promising direction.

Significance
Moderate to high. The concept could influence future work on feedback in deep vision models and on test-time adaptation without retraining base weights. The current empirical gains are modest and on small datasets, but the direction is valuable and could unlock improvements in harder settings.

Questions for the authors
- How does dasNet compare to a differentiable recurrent variant trained end-to-end (BPTT) with the same observation/action design?
- What is the wall-clock overhead at inference (T passes) and training (SNES) versus the base model?
- Do per-layer gates (fewer parameters) yield similar gains?
- How sensitive are results to T, λboost, and the observation vector components?
- Can you provide confusion-matrix analyses showing which class pairs benefit most?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel approach to learned internal attention via feedback.
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable augmentation of standard CNNs.
- Demonstrated improvements and stable dynamics; encouraging diagnostics.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scope and missing strong baselines/ablations.
- Heavy reliance on SNES without comparisons to gradient-based methods.
- Evaluated only on CIFAR-scale tasks; practicality at scale unproven.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite experimental limitations, the paper introduces a fresh and potentially influential idea—internal selective attention via learned feedback—that advances the conversation on recurrence and attention in deep vision. I am willing to overlook the incomplete evaluation given the novelty and the plausible path for broader impact.

Confidence
Medium.
