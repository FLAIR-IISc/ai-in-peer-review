### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a pre-trained Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with a feedback mechanism to improve classification performance through iterative refinement. The core idea is inspired by the human visual system, which combines a rapid, feedforward pass with slower, top-down attentional processing. The proposed system first performs a standard feedforward pass using a trained Maxout network. Subsequently, over a fixed number of additional passes (T), a control policy modulates the activations of the network's internal convolutional filters. This policy, implemented as a neural network, takes a state representation of the CNN's activations from one pass and outputs a set of weights that enhance or suppress entire feature maps in the next pass.

Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space, the authors eschew traditional reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms in favor of a black-box optimization method, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to learn the policy weights. The objective for the RL agent is to minimize a weighted classification loss, with a higher penalty for misclassified examples, effectively focusing the learning process on correcting the base model's mistakes. This approach is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where it is shown to improve upon the performance of a strong baseline Maxout network, achieving a new state-of-the-art result at the time of publication.

The work relates to previous research on recurrent and attentional models in vision, much of which draws inspiration from neuroscience. However, it distinguishes itself by applying these concepts directly to a modern, state-of-the-art deep learning architecture and demonstrating performance gains on challenging benchmark datasets, thereby bridging the gap between biologically-inspired concepts and high-performance computer vision.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-defined, and the motivation for using SNES to train the high-dimensional policy is compelling and well-justified. The claims are supported by experimental results on standard benchmarks. The authors perform several ablations and analyses that strengthen their contribution. For instance, the experiment showing that the final gate values contain significant class-discriminative information provides good evidence that the policy is learning a meaningful function rather than a trivial one. The analysis of the system's dynamics when run for more steps than it was trained for suggests the learned policy is stable.

A minor weakness is the design of the state representation, which is constructed by averaging map activations, thereby losing all spatial information. The authors acknowledge this provides only "partial state information," but do not provide a strong justification for this specific choice over potentially richer, albeit higher-dimensional, alternatives. Furthermore, the number of refinement steps, T, is fixed and determined experimentally. The paper does not explore a dynamic mechanism for determining the number of steps required for a given image, which would be a valuable extension.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and generally easy to follow. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the background sections on Maxout networks and Reinforcement Learning provide the necessary context. Algorithm 1 provides a clear, step-by-step description of the training process, and Figure 1 offers a helpful visual aid.

One area for improvement is the detail surrounding the action space. Equation 5 suggests a single scalar `a_lj` modulates an entire output map. A more explicit description of how the single action vector `a` is mapped to the individual `a_lj` weights for each layer and map would enhance reproducibility. The qualitative analysis based on Figure 3 is speculative, which the authors rightly admit, as interpreting high-level feature map activations remains a challenging open problem.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel combination of several existing techniques to create a new and effective system. While CNNs, RL, and feedback connections are not new concepts in isolation, their synthesis in this paper is original. Specifically, the idea of training a separate control policy via black-box optimization to dynamically modulate the internal feature maps of a pre-trained, state-of-the-art CNN is a novel approach to iterative processing. This contrasts with other recurrent models that typically learn all weights (feedforward and recurrent) end-to-end. The framing of the problem as "correcting" a powerful, pre-trained model is also a pragmatic and interesting perspective.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates a clear, albeit incremental, improvement over a strong baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100. This is a significant result that validates the potential of iterative refinement. The work opens a promising research avenue for creating more adaptive and dynamic neural networks that can allocate variable computational resources based on input difficulty. Other researchers are likely to build on these ideas, perhaps by exploring more sophisticated policies, alternative state representations, or dynamic stopping criteria.

The main limitation affecting the work's significance is the substantial computational cost. The inference time is increased by a factor of T (in this case, 5), and the RL-based training process is computationally expensive, taking several days on top of the initial CNN training. This trade-off between accuracy and computational cost is a critical consideration for practical applications, and it is not extensively discussed in the paper. The performance gain, while statistically relevant, may not be sufficient to justify the increased complexity and latency in many real-world scenarios.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that integrates a feedback mechanism, trained via reinforcement learning, into a state-of-the-art CNN.
*   **Performance:** It achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a challenging benchmark (CIFAR-100), demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed approach.
*   **Soundness:** The methodology is technically sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough, including analyses that support the central claims.
*   **Impact:** The work introduces a promising direction for research into adaptive computation and iterative refinement in deep networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Computational Cost:** The method introduces significant computational overhead for both training and inference, a practical drawback that is not fully addressed.
*   **Incremental Gain:** The performance improvement over the strong baseline is relatively modest in light of the considerable increase in model complexity.
*   **Ad-hoc Choices:** Some design choices, such as the formulation of the state vector and the fixed number of processing steps (T), lack strong theoretical justification and are not explored in depth.
