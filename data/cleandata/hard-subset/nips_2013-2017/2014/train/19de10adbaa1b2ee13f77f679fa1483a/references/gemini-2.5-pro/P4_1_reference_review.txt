**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that a single feedforward pass may be insufficient for classifying difficult or ambiguous images, similar to how humans may require more than a brief glance to recognize certain objects. The proposed model first trains a state-of-the-art Maxout CNN in a standard supervised fashion. After this initial training, the CNN's weights are frozen, and a control policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL). This policy operates over multiple "passes" of the same input image. On each pass, the policy observes the internal state of the network (e.g., average activations of feature maps) and outputs a set of attention weights. These weights modulate the activations of the convolutional filters, effectively allowing the network to dynamically emphasize or suppress certain features. This iterative refinement process aims to correct initial misclassifications. Due to the high-dimensional action space (controlling over a million filter activations), the policy is trained using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where it improves upon the baseline Maxout network and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100.

The work relates to prior research in several areas. It builds directly upon state-of-the-art CNN architectures like Maxout networks. The concept of recurrent processing and feedback is well-motivated by models of the human visual cortex, which feature extensive top-down connections. While previous work has used RL to learn visual attention, such as controlling saccades or foveal glimpses, this paper distinguishes itself by learning to modulate the internal feature extractors of a deep network directly, and by applying this idea to challenging, standard image classification benchmarks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel approach to integrating iterative processing and attention into deep networks. The specific mechanism—using an RL-trained policy to modulate the gain of internal convolutional filters post-training—is a creative and compelling idea. This provides a new perspective on how to move beyond purely feedforward architectures, which have dominated the field.

2.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of static, feedforward models: their inability to perform iterative refinement or "second-look" reasoning. The results are significant, as they demonstrate a clear performance improvement over a very strong baseline on a difficult dataset (CIFAR-100), establishing a new state-of-the-art result at the time. This work has the potential to inspire further research into hybrid models that combine the feature extraction power of deep learning with the sequential decision-making capabilities of reinforcement learning.

3.  **Technical Approach:** The use of SNES to train the policy is a sensible and well-justified choice, given the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space, which would make conventional gradient-based RL methods challenging to apply. The two-stage training procedure (supervised pre-training followed by RL-based policy learning) is a pragmatic approach that leverages the strengths of both paradigms.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core concepts are communicated effectively. The motivation drawn from human vision is clear and provides strong context for the model. The description of the dasNet architecture and the training algorithm is generally easy to follow. The inclusion of a qualitative analysis (Figure 3) is particularly helpful for providing intuition about how the attention mechanism works in practice.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Evaluation and Scalability:** The experimental evaluation is limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While these are standard benchmarks, the relatively small image size (32x32) may not fully expose the challenges the model would face on larger, higher-resolution images like those in ImageNet. A key concern is the computational cost at inference time. The model requires `T` forward passes (here, `T=5`) through the network, representing a roughly 5x increase in computation compared to the baseline. The paper does not report inference times or discuss this trade-off, which is crucial for practical applications. It is unclear if the performance gains justify the additional computational budget.

2.  **Reproducibility and Missing Details:** Certain key details required for full reproducibility are underspecified.
    *   The architecture of the policy network `π_θ` is not described beyond Equation 7, which suggests a simple linear transformation. The dimensionality of the observation vector `o_t` and the action vector `a_t` would be useful for understanding the scale of the RL problem.
    *   The state representation `o_t` is formed by averaging map activations, which is a significant compression of the network's rich internal state. While the authors acknowledge this provides only "partial state information," there is no discussion of why this specific representation was chosen or an exploration of alternatives.
    *   The number of passes `T=5` is stated to be "experimentally determined," but no ablation study or analysis is provided to support this choice. How sensitive is performance to `T`?

3.  **Technical Justification:** The reward function incorporates a simple form of boosting (`λ_boost`) to focus on difficult examples. While intuitive, this is a heuristic choice. A more principled justification or comparison with other techniques for handling hard examples would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the dynamics analysis in Figure 2 shows that performance peaks at the trained number of steps `T` and then decreases slightly. While it remains stable, this suggests the policy might not be learning a general-purpose refinement strategy but is instead overfitting to a fixed number of steps.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated architecture that combines deep learning with reinforcement learning in a new way.
*   Addresses an important conceptual limitation of standard feedforward CNNs.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical results on a challenging benchmark, validating the approach.
*   The work is likely to be influential and inspire new research directions in recurrent and attentive models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The computational overhead at inference time is a significant drawback that is not addressed or quantified.
*   The experimental evaluation is confined to small-scale datasets, leaving questions about the method's scalability.
*   The paper lacks some implementation details and ablation studies that would be necessary for full reproducibility and a deeper understanding of the model's properties.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting an original and significant contribution. The core idea of using RL to learn an internal attention policy is compelling and supported by strong empirical results. The primary weaknesses relate to the lack of analysis on computational cost and scalability, as well as some missing experimental details. Despite these points, the novelty and potential impact of the work are substantial.
