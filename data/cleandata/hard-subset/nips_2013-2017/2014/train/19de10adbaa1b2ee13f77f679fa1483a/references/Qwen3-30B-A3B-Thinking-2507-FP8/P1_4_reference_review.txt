

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which enhances standard CNNs by adding internal selective attention through feedback connections. The key innovation is using reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to learn a policy that modulates filter weights across multiple passes through the same image. The network first processes an image through a standard feedforward Maxout network, then iteratively refines its classification by selectively enhancing or suppressing features through feedback connections. The authors demonstrate a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10/100, claiming a new state-of-the-art result for these datasets.

The work relates to prior research on recurrent processing in visual cortex (Felleman & Essen, 1991), human visual attention (Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000), and reinforcement learning for attention (Schmidhuber, 1991; Bar et al., 2006). However, dasNet differs from previous work by applying this perspective directly to state-of-the-art CNNs rather than simplified tasks, and by learning the attention policy through reinforcement learning rather than manual design.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound in its approach to combining reinforcement learning with CNNs. The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional action space (over 1 million parameters) is reasonable given the limitations of conventional RL methods. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful attention to evaluating the method on "difficult" classification cases where the base CNN makes errors.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in its evaluation. The improvement (6% relative) appears modest compared to recent CNN advances. The authors do not compare against more recent attention mechanisms like SE blocks (Hu et al., 2018) or CBAM (Woo et al., 2018), which have become standard in modern CNNs. The paper also lacks comparison to more recent architectures like ResNet or EfficientNet, which would provide a stronger baseline for "state-of-the-art" claims.

The paper's explanation of the attention mechanism is insufficient. While they show qualitative results (Figure 3), they fail to provide meaningful insight into *why* certain filters are emphasized or suppressed. The claim that "complex suppression and enhancement patterns" are learned is not substantiated with specific analysis.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written overall. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is logically presented. However, several critical details are unclear:

1. The exact mechanism of how filter weights are modulated (equation 5) is ambiguous - it's not clear whether the weights a^ℓ_j multiply the filters or the activations.

2. The observation vector used by the policy (equation 9) is described vaguely, making it difficult to understand what information the policy actually uses to make decisions.

3. The training procedure in Algorithm 1 is confusing - it's unclear why they use a batch of images rather than processing each image multiple times.

4. The paper doesn't adequately explain how the attention mechanism relates to the standard CNN architecture, making it hard to understand the novelty.

## Originality
The core idea of using reinforcement learning to learn attention within a CNN is novel. The specific application to modifying internal filter weights across multiple passes through the same image is distinct from previous work on attention mechanisms. However, the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate itself from related work. For instance, the paper cites "recurrent processing" approaches (Wyatte et al., 2013; Behnke, 2014) but doesn't clearly explain how dasNet differs from these.

The paper also doesn't adequately address whether the attention mechanism is learning anything fundamentally different from what could be achieved with standard CNN architectures. The modest improvement on CIFAR-10/100 suggests the gains may be limited to this specific dataset.

## Significance
The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains and lack of comparison to more recent architectures. A 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is notable but not transformative, especially when compared to the substantial gains achieved by modern CNN architectures. The paper also doesn't provide a clear path for scaling this approach to larger datasets like ImageNet, which would be necessary to demonstrate broader significance.

The computational cost is another significant limitation - the paper notes that training dasNet takes "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU," which is relatively old hardware. This suggests the approach may be too computationally expensive for practical applications.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents an interesting concept that bridges reinforcement learning and CNNs, but the execution has several significant shortcomings. The modest performance gains, lack of comparison to modern architectures and attention mechanisms, and insufficient explanation of the attention mechanism's inner workings weaken the contribution. The paper is technically sound but doesn't make a strong enough case for why this approach represents a meaningful advance over existing methods.

## Recommendation
**Weak Reject**

The paper has merit as a novel exploration of internal attention in CNNs, but the evaluation is insufficient to support the "state-of-the-art" claim, and the contribution doesn't advance the field substantially enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the evaluation gaps and provide more substantial comparisons to recent work before the paper would be suitable for publication.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Compare against more recent attention mechanisms (SE blocks, CBAM) and modern CNN architectures (ResNet, EfficientNet)
2. Provide more detailed analysis of *why* certain filters are emphasized/suppressed
3. Clarify the exact mechanism of filter weight modulation (equation 5)
4. Address the computational cost and scalability of the approach
5. Include a comparison to the performance of the base CNN on the same "difficult" cases before and after dasNet
6. Discuss how this approach might be integrated into other architectures beyond Maxout networks

The paper presents an interesting concept that could be valuable with more thorough evaluation and clearer explanation of the underlying mechanisms.
