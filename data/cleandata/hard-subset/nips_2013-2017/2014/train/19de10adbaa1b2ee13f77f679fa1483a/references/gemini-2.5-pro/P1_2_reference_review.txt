### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (CNN) to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that a single feedforward pass may not be sufficient for difficult classification tasks, similar to how a human expert might take extra time to analyze a complex scene.

The proposed method operates in two stages. First, a standard feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) is trained via supervised learning. After this, its weights are frozen. In the second stage, a control policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL) to modulate the activations of the network's filter maps over several iterative passes on the same input image. For each pass, the policy observes the internal state of the network (e.g., average map activations, class probabilities) and outputs a set of multiplicative weights (`a_j^l` in Eq. 5) that enhance or suppress entire feature maps before the pooling stage. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy, the authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization. The reward function is designed to heavily penalize misclassifications, thereby focusing the learning process on correcting the base network's mistakes.

Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this iterative attention mechanism can correct initial misclassifications on ambiguous images (e.g., distinguishing a cat from a dog). The method achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (for unaugmented data) at the time of writing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The paper presents a novel and compelling combination of deep learning and reinforcement learning. The motivation, drawn from the human visual system's use of both fast feedforward processing and slower, attentional recurrent processing, is intuitive and powerful. Moving beyond purely feedforward architectures to incorporate iterative refinement is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a clear and significant improvement over a very strong baseline (Maxout networks). Achieving a 6% relative improvement and establishing a new state-of-the-art on a competitive benchmark like CIFAR-10 is a major strength.
3.  **Thoughtful Experimental Analysis:** The authors provide more than just a final accuracy table. The qualitative analysis (Figure 3) showing the correction of a misclassified example and the visualization of activation changes is insightful. The "Dynamics" experiment, which shows that a policy trained for `T` steps remains stable for more than `T` steps, is an excellent analysis of the system's robustness. Furthermore, the experiment demonstrating that the learned attention gates contain significant class-relevant information is a clever validation of the learning process.
4.  **Pragmatic Technical Approach:** The use of SNES to optimize a very high-dimensional policy is a smart and practical solution. This choice makes the ambitious goal of controlling over a million parameters tractable, where standard policy gradient methods might struggle. The two-stage training process (supervised pre-training followed by RL fine-tuning) is also a sensible way to ground the system in a strong initial state.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary weakness is the substantial computational overhead. The policy training requires 4 days on a GPU, *in addition* to the time required to train the base Maxout network. The inference process is also `T` times slower than a standard feedforward pass. The paper is upfront about the training time but does not deeply discuss the implications for scalability to larger datasets like ImageNet, where both the network size (and thus the policy's action space) and image resolution would increase dramatically.
2.  **Missing Architectural and Hyperparameter Details:** For reproducibility, the paper could be more specific. The exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network (e.g., number of filters per layer, kernel sizes, strides) is not detailed. Similarly, while some hyperparameters for the RL training are given (`λ` values, population size), others related to SNES (e.g., learning rate, momentum) are omitted or relegated to supplementary material.
3.  **Limited Form of Attention:** The attention mechanism is applied at the level of entire feature maps (channel-wise attention). While effective, this is a coarse form of attention. The conclusion mentions future work on spatial attention, which acknowledges this limitation. It is unclear if modulating an entire map is always the optimal strategy, as a single map might contain both relevant and irrelevant features in different spatial locations.
4.  **Clarity on Observation Space:** The observation vector `o_t` is constructed by concatenating average activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities. The rationale for this specific construction is sound, but its dimensionality is not stated, and there is no analysis of the sensitivity or contribution of each component to the final policy.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by experimental results on standard benchmarks. The analysis is thorough, investigating not just the final performance but also the dynamics and internal workings of the learned policy. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core idea is explained effectively in Section 4, and Algorithm 1 provides a clear overview of the training procedure. Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the iterative process. The paper provides enough information to understand the method, though as noted above, specific architectural details for full reproduction are lacking.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While RL has been used for visual attention before (e.g., for saccades), applying it to modulate the *internal filter activations* of a state-of-the-art deep network in an end-to-end fashion was a novel approach at the time. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work by scaling these ideas to modern CNNs and focusing on post-hoc correction of a powerful, pre-trained model.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. It demonstrates that performance on challenging recognition tasks can be improved by adding a learned, iterative "thinking" process on top of standard feedforward models. This work likely influenced subsequent research into attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation networks) and the combination of RL and deep vision models. The idea of learning a policy to control or adapt a base network's computation is a powerful one with broad potential impact.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and well-motivated framework for internal attention in CNNs.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark, demonstrating clear empirical success.
*   Contains a strong and insightful experimental section that goes beyond simple accuracy reporting.
*   Addresses an important problem—improving classification on difficult/ambiguous cases—in a principled way.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method is computationally very expensive, raising concerns about its practical utility and scalability.
*   Key details for full reproducibility (e.g., network architecture, SNES hyperparameters) are missing from the main text.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, strong results, and insightful analysis—far outweigh its weaknesses. The work presents a significant step forward in building more dynamic and powerful vision models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
