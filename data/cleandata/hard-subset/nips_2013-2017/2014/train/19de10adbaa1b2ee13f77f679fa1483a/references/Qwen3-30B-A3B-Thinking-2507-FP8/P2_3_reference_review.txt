

# Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that enhances deep CNNs through internal selective attention mechanisms enabled by reinforcement learning. The key insight is that humans often require multiple "passes" to correctly identify similar visual objects, suggesting that CNNs could benefit from similar iterative processing. dasNet augments a standard Maxout CNN with feedback connections that allow the network to modulate filter weights across multiple passes over the same image. The attention policy, learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the large parameter space, selectively enhances or suppresses features to correct misclassifications. The authors demonstrate a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 over vanilla CNNs, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this challenging dataset.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-justified methodology. The authors provide a clear explanation of how dasNet differs from standard CNNs and previous work on recurrent networks. The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space (over 1 million weights) is well-justified and appropriate for this context, as standard RL methods would be computationally intractable. The experimental setup is rigorous, with proper baseline comparisons and ablation studies (e.g., the dynamics analysis with varying T values). The qualitative analysis showing how filter activations change across passes provides valuable insight into the attention mechanism's operation.

The paper acknowledges limitations, including the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU) and the fact that dasNet is applied post-training to an existing network rather than being integrated during training. The authors correctly note that dasNet doesn't disrupt the original learning, which is crucial for maintaining performance on correctly classified examples.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The technical details of dasNet's architecture and training procedure are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, including key hyperparameters (λcorrect = 0.005, λmisclassified = 1, λL2 = 0.005, T = 5). The figures (particularly Figure 3 showing the attention mechanism in action) effectively illustrate the paper's key contribution. The biological motivation for feedback connections is well-explained and thoughtfully integrated with the technical approach.

## Originality
The core idea of using reinforcement learning to learn an attention policy that modulates filter weights across multiple passes is novel. While there has been work on recurrent networks and attention mechanisms, dasNet specifically focuses on internal selective attention within a pre-trained network, rather than adding recurrent connections to the network architecture itself. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in Section 5, particularly by noting that previous approaches using RL for vision often apply to simplified datasets rather than state-of-the-art networks. The biological motivation (drawing from visual cortex architecture) is well-researched and appropriately cited.

The paper is significantly different from recent NIPS work, which has focused on attention mechanisms that are either integrated during training (e.g., transformers) or applied to specific tasks (e.g., image captioning), rather than improving existing state-of-the-art networks through iterative processing.

## Significance
The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is substantial for a challenging dataset where state-of-the-art results are hard to beat. The paper demonstrates that a simple post-training modification can improve performance without requiring retraining the entire network. This has practical significance for applications where retraining is costly. The approach also provides a biologically plausible mechanism for how humans might use multiple passes to resolve ambiguous visual stimuli, bridging neuroscience and deep learning.

However, the paper could strengthen its significance claim by more thoroughly addressing why this approach is superior to simply using a larger or deeper network, which is the standard approach for improving CNN performance. The computational cost of dasNet (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is significant compared to the original CNN training time, and the paper doesn't clearly establish that this cost is justified by the performance gains.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
1. Novel approach to internal selective attention through feedback connections
2. Well-justified use of SNES for policy learning in large parameter spaces
3. Strong experimental results on challenging CIFAR datasets
4. Good qualitative analysis of how attention affects filter activations
5. Clear biological motivation and connection to visual neuroscience

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of computational cost vs. performance gain tradeoff
2. Inadequate comparison with other attention mechanisms (e.g., spatial attention, self-attention)
3. Weak theoretical justification beyond biological analogy
4. No exploration of whether dasNet could be integrated during training rather than post-training

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that iterative processing through a learned attention mechanism can significantly improve CNN performance on challenging classification tasks. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and produces strong results. While the computational cost is notable, the 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is substantial enough to justify the approach for many applications.

The paper addresses a genuine limitation of standard CNNs (inability to "think" about ambiguous cases) with a novel solution that leverages reinforcement learning to learn an attention policy. The work is technically rigorous and well-structured, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel, well-executed approach that advances the state of the art on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding computational cost and comparison to other attention mechanisms), these do not significantly detract from the paper's main contribution. The work is both theoretically interesting and practically significant, and it addresses a problem that has been largely overlooked in the deep learning community.
