**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for improving the performance of pre-trained Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) on difficult classification tasks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (specifically, a Maxout network) with feedback connections that allow the network to perform multiple evaluative passes on a single input image. On each pass, a control policy modulates the activations of the network's convolutional filters. This policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL)—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—after the base CNN has been trained with supervised learning. The goal is for the network to learn an "attentional" policy that can iteratively emphasize or suppress internal features to correct initial, potentially erroneous, classifications. The authors demonstrate this approach on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing a performance improvement over the baseline Maxout network and claiming a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-100 without data augmentation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Interesting Conceptual Framework:** The high-level motivation of allowing a network to perform iterative "thinking" to refine its predictions is compelling and draws plausible parallels to human expert cognition and the role of recurrent processing in the visual cortex. Moving beyond purely feedforward processing is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Strong Baseline Model:** The authors build their method on top of a powerful and well-established baseline, the Maxout network. This is good practice, as it ensures that the proposed additions are being tested against a competitive model.

3.  **Positive Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a clear, albeit modest, performance improvement on the CIFAR-100 benchmark over the baseline network (41.05% vs. 38.53% error). This suggests that the iterative modulation approach has merit.

**Weaknesses**

My primary concerns with this paper revolve around its methodological soundness and the thoroughness of the experimental validation. The proposed approach, while novel in its specific implementation, is not sufficiently justified against simpler, more established alternatives.

1.  **Methodological Choices Lack Rigorous Justification:**
    *   **Choice of Reinforcement Learning Algorithm:** The paper justifies the use of SNES, a black-box evolution strategy, by citing the large number of parameters in the policy network. However, this dismisses conventional, gradient-based RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) without a thorough discussion. While challenging, such methods are more standard in the RL literature and often offer better sample efficiency and structure. The reliance on a derivative-free optimization method for such a core component feels more like a workaround for complexity than a principled choice.
    *   **State and Action Representation:** The formulation of the RL problem seems overly simplistic. The state (observation) is constructed by concatenating *average* activations of feature maps, which discards all spatial information—a critical element in image processing. The action globally modulates entire feature maps, which is a very coarse form of attention. It is not clear why these coarse representations were chosen over more informative, spatially-aware alternatives, and the potential performance trade-offs are not discussed.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Baselines:** The central claim is that dasNet is an effective way to improve a trained CNN. To substantiate this, the method must be compared against other standard techniques for achieving the same goal. The current evaluation is limited to a single baseline (the vanilla Maxout network).
    *   **Computational Budget Comparison:** The dasNet approach incurs a significant computational cost at test time, requiring *T* forward passes. A fair comparison would involve baselines with a similar computational budget. For example, how does dasNet compare to simply training a single, larger feedforward network that takes a similar amount of time to evaluate?
    *   **Ensemble Methods:** A classic and powerful method for improving performance is to average the predictions of an ensemble of models. How does dasNet's performance compare to an ensemble of *T* independently trained Maxout networks? This is a crucial and missing baseline that provides a much simpler way to leverage increased computation at test time.
    *   **Comparison to other Recurrent/Attentional Models:** The idea of recurrent processing in vision is not new. The paper would be much stronger if it compared its approach to other established recurrent architectures or attention mechanisms applied to CNNs.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are confined to the relatively small-scale CIFAR-10/100 datasets. It is unclear if this complex, multi-pass approach would be computationally feasible or effective on larger-scale problems like ImageNet. The high cost of training (4 days on a GPU for the policy alone, on top of the initial CNN training) and evaluation raises serious questions about its scalability.

4.  **Superficial Analysis:** While the authors provide some visualizations and analysis of the network's dynamics, the insights are largely qualitative and speculative (e.g., "some filters focus more on surroundings whilst others de-emphasise the eyes"). The analysis confirming that the final gate values contain class-relevant information is interesting, but it does not explain the *mechanism* by which the policy improves classification. A more rigorous analysis is needed to understand what kind of strategy the RL agent has actually learned.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting idea for incorporating iterative refinement into deep networks. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The methodological decisions are not well-justified, and the experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to support the claims of significance. The lack of comparison to fundamental baselines like ensembles or larger models with equivalent computational budgets is a critical omission. While the performance gain is noted, it is not clear that the substantial added complexity of this approach is warranted compared to simpler, more established methods. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time. The authors should focus on strengthening the methodological justification and providing a much more thorough and convincing experimental comparison against relevant baselines.
