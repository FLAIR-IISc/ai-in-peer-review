- Proposes dasNet: add feedback connections and an RL-trained policy (using SNES) to modulate activations of a pre-trained CNN iteratively during inference to improve classification.  
- Uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies to handle the high-dimensional policy/action space.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, especially on ambiguous/difficult examples, with visualizations of activation changes.  
- Situates work within CNN and attention literature, linking to recurrent visual processing and biological inspiration for multiple passes.  
- Novel contribution: RL-based modulation of internal filter activations post-training rather than just learning static attention weights.  
- Practical solution: SNES is a viable approach given the complexity of the policy space.  
- Clear motivation connecting human visual multi-pass processing to limitations of feedforward networks.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- Limited analysis of the learned policy: lacks insight into consistent attention patterns or per-class strategies.  
- High computational cost: additional dasNet training takes ~4 days, raising scalability concerns.  
- Hyperparameter choices (T, λboost, λL2) appear empirical; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Limited generalization: experiments confined to CIFAR-10/100; no evaluation on larger datasets like ImageNet.  
- Interpretability issues: visualizations help but the attentional shifts remain largely a black box.  
- Quality score: 8/10; Clarity: 9/10; Originality: 8/10; Significance: 7/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel internal attention approach, empirical gains, practical handling of high-dimensional policy, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: significant computational cost, limited policy analysis, restricted datasets, missing hyperparameter sensitivity study.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking for deeper policy analysis, discussion of scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and plans for larger-scale evaluation.
