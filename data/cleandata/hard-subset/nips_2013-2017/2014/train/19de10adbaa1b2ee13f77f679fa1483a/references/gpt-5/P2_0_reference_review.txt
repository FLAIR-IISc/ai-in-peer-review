Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, learned feedback connections that implement selective attention over multiple passes on the same image. A policy maps compact observations of the network’s internal state (per-map average activations at each layer, penultimate activations, and current class probabilities) to an action that multiplicatively gates each feature map across layers. The policy is trained post hoc via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), treating the multi-pass classification as a sequential decision problem. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, with the largest gains on ambiguous cases. The authors analyze dynamics (stability when evaluating for more steps than trained) and show that the learned gates carry discriminative information.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of deep CNNs, attention, and recurrent processing. There is substantial neuroscience motivation (top-down modulatory feedback, two-stage vision). Technically, it relates to: recurrent/feedback models in vision and attention (e.g., O’Reilly’s recirculation, Larochelle & Hinton’s glimpses, Denil et al. on learned saccades), and competitive/gating mechanisms (e.g., Maxout, Dropout, Compete-to-Compute). The particular formulation—global, channel-wise gating over multiple passes learned via black-box policy search—appears novel relative to prior NIPS work. Conceptually, it also anticipates later “squeeze-and-excitation” style channel attention, but here the attention is recurrent, per-image, and trained via SNES.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mechanism is clearly defined: actions modulate feature-map gains (kept positive via a scaled softmax), observations are compact summaries, and the recurrent loop is unrolled for T steps. The learning objective emphasizes misclassified examples via a large λmisclassified, and fitness includes L2 regularization. The choice of SNES is justified by the very high-dimensional policy (≈10^6 parameters), though it trades off sample efficiency.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10/100 show improvements over the underlying Maxout network, and the stability experiment (train with T∈{1,2}, evaluate up to 10 steps) is informative. The analysis that gate vectors alone have predictive power supports the claim that the feedback policy learns meaningful structure.
- Missing or weak comparisons/ablations: The most significant gap is the lack of a gradient-based baseline for training the gating policy end-to-end via backprop through time (the system is deterministic and differentiable), or simpler supervised alternatives (e.g., a linear map from observations to gates trained with cross-entropy). It is also unclear how much of the gain comes from: (i) multiple passes per se, (ii) access to the current class probabilities in the observation, (iii) the boosting-style loss, or (iv) the softmax positivity constraint. Compute overhead (T× inference) and wall-clock impact are not quantified. Finally, the paper claims new state-of-the-art for “unaugmented data,” yet the Maxout base model was trained with data augmentation—this needs clarification and precise numbers.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithmic description is concrete (Algorithm 1). Some notational slips exist (e.g., Mt depending on θ via actions, not directly), but they do not obscure the method. Reproducibility would benefit from:
- Complete architectural details (filter counts, kernel sizes, strides, exact number of feature maps per layer, observation dimensionality).
- Exact baseline and dasNet error rates with standard deviations over multiple runs, full tables, and clarity on “unaugmented” claims.
- Ablations: remove components from observations (e.g., exclude v_t), vary T, compare different action parameterizations (softmax vs unconstrained/ReLU), and assess the effect of the boosting weights.

Originality
The idea of learned, top-down, per-image, per-pass modulation of internal CNN features to correct initial mistakes is original and well motivated. Using SNES to train a high-dimensional policy for attention within a modern CNN is an uncommon and creative choice. Related work is broadly covered and the position relative to prior CNN+attention/recurrent approaches is clear.

Significance
The work addresses an important question: can iterative, attentional processing improve strong feedforward CNNs on challenging cases? The proposed mechanism is simple, compatible with existing CNNs, and shows measurable gains on standard benchmarks. If made more scalable and trained with gradient-based methods, the approach could influence practice. As presented, the gains are promising but limited to CIFAR-scale and come with additional inference cost; broader impact would be stronger with larger-scale experiments or stronger baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling formulation of internal selective attention via learned feedback gating.
- Demonstrated improvements over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100.
- Insightful dynamics analysis and evidence that the learned gates encode discriminative information.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired feedback with practical deep architectures.

Weaknesses and questions
- No gradient-based or simpler supervised baselines for learning the policy; reliance on SNES is likely inefficient and may not scale.
- Insufficient ablations to pinpoint where the gains come from (observation components, boosting loss, T, action constraints).
- Ambiguity around “unaugmented” SOTA vs training with augmentation; missing absolute numbers and variance.
- Lack of compute/latency analysis; T passes increase test-time cost.
- Limited dataset scope; no tests on larger images or more modern CNNs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add BPTT-based training of the policy as a baseline; report performance and training cost.
- Provide full quantitative tables (mean±std over seeds) and clarify augmentation claims.
- Report inference-time overhead and accuracy–compute trade-offs versus alternatives (ensembles, test-time augmentation).
- Perform ablations on observation content, action parameterization, and boosting weights.
- Include per-class confusion improvements to support “ambiguous case” claims.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated mechanism for internal attention with clear empirical gains and thoughtful analysis. With stronger baselines and clearer experimental reporting, it would be an easy accept; as is, it remains a valuable contribution likely to stimulate follow-up work on recurrent attention in CNNs.
