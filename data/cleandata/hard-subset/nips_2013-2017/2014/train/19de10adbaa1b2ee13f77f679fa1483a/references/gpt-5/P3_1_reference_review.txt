Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, multiplicative “gates” applied to feature maps that are updated over multiple recurrent passes on the same image. A policy maps observations of the network’s internal activations (average map activations, classification pre-activations, and class probabilities) to actions (per-feature-map scaling factors). The policy is learned post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the large parameter dimension (~10^6) of the policy, and is applied to a pre-trained Maxout CNN. On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over the base Maxout model (claiming SOTA for unaugmented data), and provide qualitative analyses suggesting the policy emphasizes and de-emphasizes useful filters on difficult examples. They also analyze stability with respect to the number of recurrent steps T and show that final-step gate values contain class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work
The approach connects to a long line of work on feedback and attention in biological vision and to recurrent/attention mechanisms in machine learning. It differs from “glimpse” models and saccadic attention (e.g., Denil et al.) by focusing on internal selective attention (feature-map reweighting) rather than spatial attention. Compared to prior recurrent CNNs and recirculation/error-driven feedback (e.g., O’Reilly), the key novelty is to treat the feedback as a learned control policy over filter activations, trained with black-box optimization on top of an already trained CNN. The use of Maxout is incidental; the idea is compatible with standard CNNs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and simplicity: a clear, generic mechanism for internal selective attention via multiplicative gating of feature maps, iterated over T passes. Easy to “bolt on” to an existing CNN without retraining its feedforward weights.
- Empirical signal: nontrivial improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline, with a focus on ambiguous cases. The dynamic is qualitatively plausible: initial prediction corrected by subsequent passes.
- Sensible design choices: observations are drawn from accessible summary statistics; actions are constrained positive and mean-centered (softmax scaled) to stabilize activations; misclassified examples are emphasized during policy training.
- Some analysis beyond headline numbers: dynamics vs T; gate informativeness as features.

Weaknesses and questions
Technical choices and baselines
- The choice of SNES over gradient-based training is insufficiently justified. The gating operations and softmax policy are differentiable; one could train the policy (and even feedback couplings) by backpropagation through time on the supervised loss, or via policy gradients with variance reduction. Without such baselines, it is unclear whether evolutionary strategies are necessary or competitive in sample efficiency.
- Capacity-matched baselines are missing. The policy adds a large number of parameters; improvements might stem from increased capacity rather than recurrence or attention. Please compare to: (a) a deeper/wider feedforward Maxout with comparable parameter count and training budget; (b) a single-pass model with a learned linear reweighting of feature maps (no recurrence); (c) gating only at the top layer(s).
- Computational cost is not analyzed. T passes increase inference time by roughly T; how does this compare to common test-time augmentations or ensembles that also improve accuracy? Report wall-clock, FLOPs, and memory overhead during inference and training.

Methodological clarity and reproducibility
- Important details are underspecified: exact Maxout architecture (filter sizes, strides, number of maps per layer, block sizes), observation vector dimensionality and normalization, policy architecture (the text implies a single linear layer + softmax), SNES hyperparameters (learning rates, damping, σ initialization), stopping criteria, and full training schedules. As written, the work is not readily reproducible.
- The loss definition is confusing. Eq. (8–10) mixes symbols (d appears to be both the label and ||v_T||_∞). Please clarify the exact loss used and the criterion for “misclassified.”
- The action parameterization via a softmax scaled by dim(A) enforces competition among gates. While this stabilizes mean activation, it also couples channels and may preclude beneficial independent up/down-weighting. An ablation with independent sigmoids or tanh offsets around 1 (plus regularization) would be informative.

Evaluation depth
- The empirical section is narrow for a NeurIPS paper. Only CIFAR-10/100 are considered; no statistical significance, confidence intervals, or robustness tests (e.g., under corruptions/noise or distribution shift). Show confusion matrices before/after, the fraction of flips from incorrect→correct vs correct→incorrect, and whether gains concentrate in specific confusable pairs (as motivated).
- The “gate informativeness” result is interesting but conflates correlation and causation: gates may reflect the current prediction rather than drive improvement. A stronger test is to randomize gates at test time or to freeze them to first-pass values and measure degradation; or to predict gates from class probabilities alone to assess whether internal activations add information.
- Stability beyond T: the dynamics appear stable up to 10 steps on a toy 2-layer model, but it is important to show this on the main architecture and to quantify how performance decays past T.

Positioning and related work
- The paper cites relevant biological and early ML attention work, but could better situate itself among contemporary recurrent CNNs and feedback mechanisms (e.g., CRF-as-RNN style iterative refinement, test-time optimization loops, or discriminative recurrent inference). A discussion of how dasNet relates to iterative refinement methods would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, broadly applicable idea of internal attention via learned feedback.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains on standard benchmarks using a strong baseline.
- Bridges neuroscience-inspired feedback with modern deep CNNs; likely to inspire follow-on work.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing critical baselines and ablations; unclear whether recurrence and SNES are essential.
- Incomplete experimental characterization and reporting; limited datasets and no cost analysis.
- Some notational errors and insufficient detail hinder reproducibility.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add capacity-matched and gradient-based baselines; include ablations on where to gate and policy architecture (linear vs MLP).
- Report compute/memory costs, training curves, and significance.
- Clarify and correct the loss, provide full architectural and SNES details, and release code if possible.
- Analyze flips and confusion matrices; test robustness; show results with different T at train/test.
- Consider alternative action parameterizations to relax the softmax coupling.
