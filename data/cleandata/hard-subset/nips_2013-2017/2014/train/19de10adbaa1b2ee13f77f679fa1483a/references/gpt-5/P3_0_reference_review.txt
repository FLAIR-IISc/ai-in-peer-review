Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent augmentation to a trained Maxout CNN that implements internal, feature-wise selective attention via feedback connections. At test time, the same image is processed for T passes. After each pass, the policy observes a compact summary of network activity (global average activation per feature map, intermediate classifier activations, and current class probabilities) and outputs a set of multiplicative gates a that reweight each convolutional feature map on the next pass. The base CNN weights are frozen; only the feedback policy is learned, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high-dimensional action space (≈10^3) and large number of policy parameters (≈10^6). On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves upon a strong Maxout baseline, correcting ambiguous cases over multiple passes and reportedly reaching state-of-the-art among single models for unaugmented data. The paper also analyzes the dynamics beyond the trained number of steps and shows that the learned gates contain class-relevant information.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates dasNet in the context of biological vision (top-down modulatory feedback), iterative processing in computer vision, and reinforcement learning approaches to attention and recurrent inference. It builds directly on Maxout networks with dropout. Compared to prior RL-based attention mechanisms focused on saccades/glimpses, dasNet performs channel-wise, global feature reweighting across layers. It differs from past recurrent CNNs by introducing an explicit, learned control policy that modulates filters across passes without changing base weights.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and simplicity: feature-wise, global multiplicative gating across multiple layers provides an implementable mechanism for top-down modulation.
- Clear integration with a strong baseline (Maxout) and a training protocol that separates supervised feature learning from post hoc control learning.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 and qualitative evidence that multi-pass feedback can fix ambiguous cases.
- Stability analysis showing performance peaks near the trained number of steps and then degrades only mildly.
- The observation design (compact global statistics) is computationally efficient.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality and technical soundness
- Choice of optimizer: The entire pipeline is differentiable if the policy is a neural network mapping o_t to a_t and the base CNN is frozen. Why prefer SNES over backpropagation through time with standard gradients (or deterministic policy gradients)? The justification (“too many parameters”) is unconvincing without comparative evidence; black-box evolution at this scale raises sample-efficiency and overfitting concerns.
- Loss/notation clarity: Eq. 10 uses d = ||v_T||_∞ and also defines d as the correct class. Please clarify the indicator of correctness, the definition of d, and the exact boosted loss. Algorithm 1 has a potential off-by-one in t = 0..T with a_{t+1}; clarify how many passes are executed before scoring v_T.
- Action constraints: Using softmax scaled by dim(A) enforces positive gates whose average is 1, making all modulation relative. This choice prevents uniformly amplifying or dampening all filters and may limit expressiveness. Please justify this design empirically or consider alternative parameterizations (e.g., exp with learned bias or tanh with affine transform).
- Observation sufficiency: The policy only sees global averages per feature map and classifier activations, losing spatial information. The approach therefore effects feature-wise but not spatial attention; the “selective attention” terminology may be misleading. Can you quantify whether spatial information would help, or at least show ablations where actions target specific layers?
- Experimental evidence is thin in the current draft: Table 1 is referenced but not included here; exact error rates, confidence intervals/standard deviations, number of runs/seeds, and statistical significance are not reported. Without these, it is hard to assess robustness of the claimed 6% relative improvement.
- Baseline and ablation gaps:
  - Compare to simple, single-pass channel-wise reweighting learned via supervised training (e.g., a linear policy on o_0 applied once).
  - Compare to end-to-end gradient-based training of the policy (with base CNN frozen) and to fine-tuning the base CNN on hard examples.
  - Compare to gating only the last convolutional block, or only the classifier inputs, to quantify where feedback matters most.
  - Compare to standard test-time ensembling or multi-crop/augmentation to contextualize gains vs computational overhead.
- Generalization/regularization: Focusing the loss on misclassified examples via large λ_misclassified may risk overfitting the policy to training ambiguities. Provide learning curves on a validation set, details on early stopping, and report test-time gains across multiple seeds.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well organized, but several notation issues (above). Provide the exact dimensionality of A and O, the architecture of the policy network (the text implies a linear map plus softmax; confirm), and all SNES hyperparameters (population, learning rates, number of generations).
- State clearly whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time only; the text indicates that training uses data augmentation.

Originality
- The idea of learned, internal, channel-wise feedback that iteratively reconfigures a CNN at test time is interesting and distinct from contemporaneous spatial attention or glimpse models. The combination with black-box evolutionary optimization at this scale is unusual, though arguably not the most principled choice.

Significance
- If the quantitative gains over a strong single-model baseline are consistent and statistically significant, this contributes to evidence that iterative inference with top-down modulation can improve recognition even on small images like CIFAR. The mechanism may inspire subsequent work on learned feature-wise modulation and recurrent refinement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complete quantitative results with error bars across several seeds and clear baselines/ablations listed above.
- Justify SNES with a head-to-head comparison against gradient-based training of the policy (same architecture, same compute).
- Analyze where corrections occur: per-class confusion improvements, calibration shifts per step, and cost-performance trade-offs vs number of passes T.
- Explore alternative action parameterizations (unconstrained positive gates, learned per-layer scale) and layer-specific policies.
- Clarify the boosted loss and training dynamics; include a validation protocol to prevent overfitting to misclassified training examples.

Pros
- Clear, novel mechanism for internal feedback.
- Demonstrated improvements over a strong baseline.
- Conceptually aligned with biological evidence; simple to implement.

Cons
- Insufficient ablations and missing comparisons to simpler/gradient-based alternatives.
- Justification for SNES is weak; concerns about sample efficiency and scalability.
- Notation ambiguities and limited reporting of experimental details.

Recommendation: borderline reject without stronger empirical evidence and clearer justification for the optimization choice; potentially accept if the camera-ready addresses the ablations and clarifies results. Confidence: medium.
