This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture augmented with feedback connections and a reinforcement learning (RL) component to selectively modulate filter activations. The core idea is to allow the network to refine its classifications through iterative processing, focusing attention on relevant features. The policy controlling these attentional weights is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the large parameter space. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate improved performance on difficult classification instances compared to a baseline Maxout network.

The paper builds upon the established success of CNNs (references [1-13]) and addresses a key limitation: their static nature post-training. The motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing ([16, 17]), is compelling – expert classification often requires multiple passes to elicit relevant information. The proposed approach connects to related work in recurrent neural networks ([48-50]), attention mechanisms ([40-47]), and biologically inspired models of visual processing ([28-39]). It differentiates itself by focusing on *internal* selective attention within a CNN, learned via RL, rather than external mechanisms or simplified datasets. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections within a CNN and RL-based learning of attentional weights is a novel approach. It moves beyond purely feedforward architectures and explores dynamic internal adaptation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears well-defined. Using SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a practical choice. The experimental setup, using established datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) and a competitive baseline (Maxout network), is appropriate.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are explained clearly, and the equations are well-defined. The description of the SNES algorithm, while concise, is sufficient with references to the original work.
* **Significance:** Demonstrating improved performance on challenging classification instances is a positive result. The potential for improving robustness and interpretability of CNNs through attention mechanisms is significant. The visualization of filter activations (Figure 3) offers some insight into the network’s decision-making process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the overall performance improvement is shown, the paper lacks detailed ablation studies.  It’s unclear how much each component (feedback connections, SNES, specific reward function) contributes to the overall performance gain. For example, investigating the performance with a simpler RL algorithm (even if less scalable) could provide insights.
* **SNES Justification:**  The rationale for choosing SNES over other RL methods (e.g., policy gradients with function approximation) is adequate, but a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial. Is the scalability gain worth a potential loss in exploration efficiency?
* **Reward Function Design:** The reward function (Equation 8) seems relatively simple. While effective, its sensitivity to different parameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified) could be explored. A more sophisticated reward signal could potentially lead to more refined attention mechanisms.
* **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet takes "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU", excluding the initial Maxout network training. This is a significant computational cost, which might limit the accessibility of the method. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss strategies for reducing this cost.
* **Interpretability:** Although the visualization of filter activations is helpful, a deeper analysis of *what* the network is learning to attend to would be valuable. Is it focusing on specific object parts, texture features, or contextual cues?
* **Lack of comparison to attention mechanisms:** The paper would benefit from a comparison to more standard attention mechanisms used in CNNs (e.g. spatial attention, channel attention) to demonstrate the added benefit of the feedback loop and RL-based learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through internal selective attention.
* The experimental results demonstrate a statistically significant improvement on challenging classification tasks.
* The work addresses an important limitation of standard CNNs – their static nature – and opens up new avenues for research in dynamic neural networks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed ablation studies and analysis of the learned attention mechanisms limits the understanding of the method’s inner workings.
* The high computational cost may hinder its widespread adoption.
* Comparison to other attention mechanisms is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising new approach to improving CNN performance. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty and potential significance of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses raised in the review, particularly through more detailed ablation studies and a discussion of the computational cost. A comparison to established attention mechanisms would also be a valuable addition.
