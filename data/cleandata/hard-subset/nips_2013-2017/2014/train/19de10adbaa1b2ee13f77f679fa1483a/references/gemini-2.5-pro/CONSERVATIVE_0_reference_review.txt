This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a method for augmenting pre-trained convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with a feedback mechanism to improve classification performance. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple passes over a single input image. On each pass, a policy network modulates the activations of the convolutional filters in the base CNN. This policy, which takes the internal state of the CNN as input, is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to minimize the classification loss on difficult examples. The authors demonstrate this approach on a Maxout network, a strong baseline model, and report improved performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, claiming a new state-of-the-art result.

**Strengths:**
1.  The central concept of using an explicit, learned policy to modulate internal filter activations over multiple "glimpses" is novel. It moves beyond a single feedforward pass and attempts to model a more deliberative, iterative refinement process, which is conceptually interesting and has plausible biological motivation.
2.  The approach is applied to a strong baseline model (Maxout network). Demonstrating an improvement on top of a state-of-the-art architecture is more compelling than improving upon a weaker model.
3.  The empirical results show a clear, albeit modest, improvement in classification error on both CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, suggesting the approach has some merit.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Complexity and Justification:** The proposed system is a complex amalgamation of a base CNN, a policy network, and a black-box evolutionary optimization algorithm (SNES). The choice of SNES over more standard policy gradient methods, which are prevalent in deep reinforcement learning, is not sufficiently justified. The authors state that this is due to the large parameter space, but this claim is not substantiated with a comparison. Evolutionary strategies are often sample-inefficient and can be difficult to tune. The paper would be stronger if it either provided a compelling theoretical or empirical reason for this choice over established RL techniques or showed that those methods fail here.

2.  **Limited and Potentially Misleading Empirical Evaluation:**
    *   **Scalability:** The experiments are confined to the relatively small-scale CIFAR datasets. It is unclear if this computationally intensive training process (4 days on a GPU for the RL phase alone) would be feasible for larger models and datasets like ImageNet, where the number of filters, and thus the policy's action space, would be vastly larger.
    *   **Computational Cost vs. Performance Gain:** The method achieves a ~6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 (from 9.38% to 8.81% error). While positive, this gain comes at a tremendous cost. Inference time is multiplied by the number of passes (T=5), making it five times slower than the baseline. More importantly, the complexity of the training scheme is vastly increased. The paper fails to compare against much simpler and more established methods for improving performance, such as ensembling several Maxout networks or simply training a single, larger Maxout network. It is plausible that such methods could achieve similar or greater performance gains with less complexity. Without these crucial baselines, the significance of the results is difficult to assess.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis and Ablation:** The paper provides little insight into *why* the learned policy works. The visualizations of filter modulations are presented, but the authors themselves admit, "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect of the alterations." This lack of deep analysis is a significant weakness. Key questions remain unanswered:
    *   How sensitive is the method to the composition of the state vector `o_t`? An ablation study removing parts of the state (e.g., average activations, class probabilities) would be necessary to understand what information the policy relies on.
    *   How does performance vary with the number of passes, T? The choice of T=5 seems arbitrary and is not explored.
    *   How do the hyperparameters of the reward function (`λ_correct`, `λ_misclassified`) affect learning?

4.  **Novelty of the Combination:** While the high-level idea is new, it combines existing components in a straightforward, if complex, manner. The core contribution is the specific engineering of this system. Given the lack of deep analysis and the limited experimental validation against simpler alternatives, the contribution feels more like a proof-of-concept than a robust, well-understood technique ready for wider adoption.

**Summary:**

The paper presents an interesting, albeit highly complex, method for iterative classification refinement. However, its contribution is undermined by several factors. The methodological choice of SNES is not well-defended against more standard alternatives. The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and fails to compare against simpler, more established methods for improving accuracy, such as ensembling. The performance gains are modest in absolute terms and come at a very high computational cost for both training and inference. Finally, the analysis of the learned behavior is superficial, leaving the reader with little understanding of the mechanism behind the improvement. While the idea is promising, the paper in its current form does not provide the rigorous validation and deep insight expected for a NIPS publication. The work is more of an exploration of an idea than a conclusive demonstration of a practical and significant advance.
