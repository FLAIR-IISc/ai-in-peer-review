

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary

This paper introduces "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), which enhances standard feedforward CNNs by adding feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its internal filter weights through reinforcement learning. The core idea is to enable a trained CNN to "think" about difficult classification cases by making multiple passes over the same image, where each pass can emphasize or suppress different filters to improve classification. The authors use Maxout networks as the base architecture, train them conventionally, and then learn a policy using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to control the filter weights across multiple passes. The approach is inspired by human visual processing, which involves multiple feedforward evaluations for difficult classification tasks.

The paper relates to previous work in several ways: it draws from the neuroscience literature on top-down processing (e.g., Felleman and Essen, 1991), builds on prior work in attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., visual attention models), and applies reinforcement learning techniques to a new domain (internal selective attention in CNNs). It differs from prior work by focusing on *internal* selective attention rather than spatial attention, and by using a novel RL approach (SNES) to handle the large number of parameters required for this task.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Concept**: The paper proposes a genuinely novel way to incorporate selective attention into deep CNNs through feedback connections, which is well-motivated by human visual processing. The idea of "thinking" about an image through multiple passes is conceptually compelling.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10 is significant for a challenging benchmark. The results are clearly presented, and the ablation studies (e.g., Figure 3 showing attention dynamics) provide valuable insights.

3. **Technical Soundness**: The authors carefully address the computational challenges of applying RL to large CNNs by using SNES instead of conventional RL methods, which is a practical and well-justified choice. The implementation details are thorough.

4. **Clear Motivation**: The connection to human visual processing (e.g., ornithologist example) provides a strong conceptual foundation for the approach, making it more than just a technical improvement.

5. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The paper thoroughly covers relevant work in both neuroscience (top-down connections in visual cortex) and machine learning (RL for attention, recurrent networks), demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The experiments are restricted to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. It's unclear how the approach would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet or more complex tasks like object detection. The paper lacks experiments on larger-scale benchmarks.

2. **Computational Cost**: The approach requires multiple passes through the network (T=5), which significantly increases inference time. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of the computational trade-offs compared to other attention mechanisms or more complex models.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the results are promising, the paper doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for why the approach works better than simply using a larger or deeper network. Why do these feedback connections improve performance specifically on ambiguous cases?

4. **Limited Analysis of Attention Mechanism**: The paper shows that attention changes occur, but doesn't thoroughly analyze *why* certain filters are emphasized or suppressed. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 is suggestive but not conclusive.

5. **Comparison to Attention Mechanisms**: The paper doesn't compare to other attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM) that have been shown to improve CNN performance. It's unclear whether dasNet offers advantages over these simpler approaches.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound, with well-designed experiments that support the main claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the computational cost and restricted experimental scope. However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis to fully explain why the approach works, and the experimental validation is limited to a specific dataset. The work is a complete piece of research, not a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithms and methods are described in sufficient detail for replication. Some aspects of the RL setup (particularly the observation space construction) could be clarified, but this is a minor issue. The figures (particularly Figure 3) are excellent and effectively illustrate the attention dynamics.

## Originality Assessment

The paper presents a novel combination of ideas: using reinforcement learning to control internal filter weights through feedback connections for selective attention. While the idea of attention in CNNs is not new, the internal selective attention mechanism through feedback connections is a fresh approach. The paper adequately references prior work on attention mechanisms and top-down processing, and it clearly differentiates its contribution from existing work.

## Significance Assessment

The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is significant for a standard benchmark, but the practical significance is limited by the computational cost of multiple passes. The approach doesn't seem to provide a clear path for integration into existing CNN frameworks or for scaling to larger models. The paper demonstrates a proof-of-concept that could inspire future work, but it doesn't yet provide a compelling case for widespread adoption.

## Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to internal selective attention in CNNs inspired by human vision
- Strong experimental results on standard benchmarks (6% relative improvement)
- Well-executed implementation with appropriate handling of RL challenges
- Clear motivation and thorough related work
- Good writing quality and organization

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope (only CIFAR datasets)
- No comparison to existing attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks)
- Significant computational cost (5x inference time)
- Lack of theoretical justification for the approach
- Limited analysis of *why* the attention mechanism works

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to internal selective attention in CNNs with promising results on CIFAR datasets. However, the limited scope, lack of comparison to other attention mechanisms, and significant computational cost prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance. The work is interesting and could lead to valuable future research, but it doesn't yet demonstrate sufficient significance or generalizability to warrant acceptance as a top-tier NeurIPS contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.
2. Compare dasNet to existing attention mechanisms (e.g., SE blocks, CBAM) to show relative advantages.
3. Provide a more detailed analysis of why certain filters are emphasized or suppressed.
4. Discuss the computational trade-offs more thoroughly, including inference time and memory requirements.
5. Add a theoretical analysis to explain why this approach works better than simply using a deeper network.

## Final Comments

This is a well-written paper with a compelling motivation and promising results on CIFAR datasets. While the approach is novel and technically sound, the limited scope and lack of comparison to existing attention mechanisms prevent it from being a strong NeurIPS contribution. With additional experiments and analysis, this work could be significantly strengthened for future publication.
