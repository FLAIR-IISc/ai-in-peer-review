### **Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"**

#### **Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple passes over an input image, iteratively refining its internal representations and final prediction. This is achieved by introducing modulatory gates (`a_lj`) that can enhance or suppress the output of entire filter maps in each layer. The control of these gates is framed as a sequential decision-making problem, where a policy network, trained via reinforcement learning (RL), determines the gate values for each pass. The policy takes the network's internal state (activations) as input. Due to the high dimensionality of the action space (one gate per filter map), the authors eschew traditional RL algorithms in favor of a black-box optimization method, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The method is applied to a pre-trained, state-of-the-art Maxout network on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, demonstrating improved performance, particularly on difficult, initially misclassified examples, and achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—using a learned RL policy to control the internal computational flow of a deep network for iterative refinement—is highly original and significant. It moves beyond the static, one-shot feedforward paradigm prevalent in deep learning at the time. The analogy to human visual processing, where an initial fast percept is followed by slower, deliberate attention, provides strong motivation and opens a promising new research direction. This work can be seen as a precursor to more complex recurrent and attention-based models.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors don't just present a conceptual idea; they implement it on top of a strong, state-of-the-art baseline (Maxout networks) and demonstrate a clear performance improvement on competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100). The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is a meaningful advance and substantiates their claims effectively. This rigorous evaluation elevates the paper from a mere proof-of-concept to a substantial contribution.

3.  **Clever Methodological Choices:** The formulation of the problem is elegant. The choice to use SNES for policy optimization is a key pragmatic decision that makes the approach tractable. Training a policy with over a million parameters using traditional value-based or policy gradient methods would be extremely challenging due to variance and exploration issues. Using a black-box evolution strategy sidesteps these difficulties, demonstrating sound engineering and problem-solving. Furthermore, the reward function's boosting mechanism, which up-weights misclassified examples, is a simple yet effective way to focus the learning on the "hard cases" the model is designed to solve.

4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes valuable analysis beyond raw accuracy scores. The visualization of filter map modulation (Figure 3) provides some insight into the learned policy, even if interpretation remains difficult (a fact the authors honestly acknowledge). The dynamics experiment (Figure 2), showing that the system remains stable beyond its training horizon `T`, is a good sanity check. Most impressively, the experiment demonstrating that the final gate values themselves contain significant class-discriminative information provides strong evidence that the policy is learning a meaningful, task-relevant control strategy.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Coarseness of the Attention Mechanism:** The "selective attention" mechanism operates at the level of entire feature maps. A single scalar `a_lj` is applied uniformly to all spatial locations in the j-th output map of layer `l`. This is a relatively coarse form of modulation. It cannot, for instance, spatially focus on a specific region of an image (e.g., the ear of a cat vs. the ear of a dog). While effective, this limitation could be discussed more explicitly as a clear avenue for future work (e.g., spatially-varying modulation).

2.  **Information Bottleneck in State Representation:** The observation vector `o_t` for the policy is constructed by concatenating averaged map activations, intermediate classifier activations, and output probabilities. Averaging the activations of each feature map discards all spatial information, which could be critical for making nuanced attentional decisions. It is plausible that a more structured state representation (e.g., using convolutions on the state itself or preserving some spatial resolution) could lead to a more powerful policy.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The proposed method introduces a significant computational overhead at inference time. Instead of one forward pass, the network requires `T` (here, T=5) passes, making it roughly 5x slower. While the accuracy gain is notable, this trade-off is important for practical applications and is not discussed in the paper. The training process is also extremely expensive, requiring days of GPU time *after* the base CNN has already been trained.

4.  **Limited Exploration of Alternatives to SNES:** While the authors provide a reasonable justification for using SNES (high-dimensional action space), the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of why other potential RL approaches, such as policy gradient methods (e.g., REINFORCE), were considered impractical. It is not immediately obvious that they would fail, though they would certainly present challenges. This would help to further situate their methodological choice within the broader RL literature.

#### **Overall Evaluation and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and thought-provoking idea, supports it with strong empirical results, and provides insightful analysis. The fusion of deep learning with reinforcement learning to control internal network dynamics is a powerful concept. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and significantly advances the state of the art.

The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that point towards exciting future research directions rather than fundamental flaws in the current work. The originality of the core idea and the quality of the execution strongly support its acceptance. This paper is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up work on dynamic computation, conditional computation, and attention mechanisms in neural networks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original and significant contribution in combining RL with deep network internals.
*   Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark.
*   The methodology is well-motivated, and the choice of SNES is a clever solution to a difficult optimization problem.
*   Includes thoughtful experiments and analysis that validate the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The attention mechanism is non-spatial and relatively simple.
*   Significant computational overhead at inference time is not discussed.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
