

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image. The authors propose using reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to learn a policy that modulates filter weights across these passes, focusing on difficult classification cases. The approach is motivated by biological visual processing, where humans often require multiple "passes" to distinguish between similar objects. The authors evaluate dasNet on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, reporting a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs for unaugmented data.

The work relates to several strands of research: (1) biological visual processing with top-down connections (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000), (2) attention mechanisms in deep learning (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014), and (3) recurrent processing in neural networks (e.g., Wyatte et al., 2014). The authors position their work as distinct from prior attention mechanisms by focusing on internal selective attention through feedback connections rather than external attention mechanisms.

## Strengths
1. **Conceptual Novelty**: The idea of using feedback connections to allow CNNs to "think" about an image through multiple passes is conceptually interesting and well-motivated by human visual processing. This bridges a gap between biological vision and artificial neural networks.

2. **Technical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear technical description of how dasNet extends Maxout networks with attentional gates and how the reinforcement learning policy is trained. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-specified.

3. **Promising Results**: The method achieves a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 compared to vanilla CNNs, with compelling visualizations showing how the network corrects misclassifications across passes. The ablation study on dynamics (Fig. 2) demonstrates that the policy learns stable behavior.

4. **Clear Motivation**: The paper effectively connects the approach to biological visual processing, making a strong case for why iterative processing might be beneficial.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for why the feedback connections improve performance. There's no analysis of how the learned policies work or why they are effective. The paper doesn't address whether the improvements are due to the feedback mechanism itself or simply the increased capacity from additional passes.

2. **Modest Improvement and Scaling Concerns**: The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is modest, and it's unclear whether this would generalize to larger datasets like ImageNet. The computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU for dasNet training) is substantial, especially compared to the standard CNN training time.

3. **Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work**: While the authors cite relevant work, they don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from prior attention mechanisms in deep learning. For example, the paper doesn't adequately address how dasNet differs from attention mechanisms in neural machine translation (Bahdanau et al., 2014) or visual attention models (Xu et al., 2015).

4. **Lack of Broader Analysis**: The paper doesn't explore potential limitations, such as whether the approach works for other tasks beyond image classification, or how it compares to other attention mechanisms that have been successfully applied to vision tasks.

5. **Overstated Significance**: The paper claims this establishes a "new state-of-the-art result," but the improvement is relatively small on a dataset that is no longer considered challenging for modern CNNs.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting idea with promising initial results, it falls short on several key criteria for NIPS:

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound but lacks sufficient theoretical justification and deeper analysis of why the approach works. The improvement is modest, and the paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and generally clear, but some technical sections (particularly the RL explanation) could be more accessible.

**Originality**: The core idea of using feedback for selective attention is novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from related work in attention mechanisms.

**Significance**: The improvement on CIFAR-10 is modest, and it's unclear whether this approach would generalize to more challenging datasets or tasks. The computational cost is high relative to the benefit.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Conduct more thorough analysis**: Provide deeper analysis of the learned policies, including what features the attention mechanism focuses on and why these features are beneficial for classification.

2. **Expand experiments**: Test the approach on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and for other tasks (e.g., object detection) to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. **Better contextualize with related work**: More clearly differentiate dasNet from existing attention mechanisms, particularly those in computer vision. A comparative analysis would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Address computational costs**: Discuss the trade-offs between the modest performance gain and the significant computational overhead.

5. **Theoretical justification**: Provide more theoretical grounding for why the feedback connections improve performance, potentially through information-theoretic analysis or ablation studies.

While the core idea is promising, the current paper lacks sufficient depth and broader validation to justify acceptance at NIPS. With the suggested improvements, this could become a stronger contribution to the field.
