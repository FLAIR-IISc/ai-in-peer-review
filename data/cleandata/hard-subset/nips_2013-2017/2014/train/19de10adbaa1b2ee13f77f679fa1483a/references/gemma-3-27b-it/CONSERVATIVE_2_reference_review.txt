This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning to selectively modulate filter activations. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its classifications by focusing on different features, mirroring a hypothesized process in human visual perception. While the motivation is plausible – humans *do* sometimes re-examine stimuli – and the reported performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is encouraging, I find the approach and its presentation lacking in several key aspects.

The primary concern lies in the novelty and justification of the method. The paper positions itself as advancing beyond standard CNNs through iterative attention. However, the proposed mechanism – learned filter weighting via RL – feels more like a complex, end-to-end tuning process than a fundamental architectural innovation. Recurrent connections and attention mechanisms are well-established in the literature (see Related Work, though even that section doesn’t fully grapple with relevant areas like attention in LSTMs or visual transformers). The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate *why* this specific formulation of recurrent attention, implemented with SNES, is superior to, or even meaningfully different from, existing approaches. Simply stating that it “works” isn’t sufficient. 

The methodological rigor is also questionable. The use of SNES, while acknowledged as a practical necessity given the parameter count, feels like a workaround rather than a principled choice. The justification for SNES over more conventional RL methods is weak. Furthermore, the experimental setup feels somewhat contrived, focusing on "difficult" classification instances. While identifying such instances is reasonable, the paper doesn't adequately demonstrate that dasNet generalizes well beyond them. The limited dynamics experiments (Figure 2) are suggestive, but far from conclusive. The claim of stability "even when evaluated for as many as 10 steps" is weak given the network was *trained* with only 5 steps.

The ablation studies are insufficient. The “information content” test using k-NN and logistic regression on the final gate values is a rather crude attempt to demonstrate that the learned attention is meaningful. A more thorough analysis would involve systematically ablating different layers or connections to assess their individual contributions. It’s also unclear whether the performance gain is solely attributable to the learned attention mechanism, or if it's partly due to the increased computational cost of multiple passes. 

The clarity is adequate but suffers from a dense writing style and a lack of conceptual grounding. The presentation of Algorithm 1 is difficult to parse without careful reading. The connection between the “boosting” loss function and the overall goal of improving classification accuracy isn’t entirely clear. The explanation of how the filter weighting influences the output is also somewhat convoluted.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of allowing a CNN to iteratively refine its decisions is intuitively appealing.
*   The reported performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is a positive result, suggesting potential benefits of the approach.
*   The paper is well-structured and provides sufficient detail to understand the implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – the approach feels like a complex tuning process rather than a fundamental innovation.
*   Methodological concerns – the use of SNES and the limited ablation studies weaken the conclusions.
*   Lack of generalizability – the focus on “difficult” instances and the limited dynamics experiments raise questions about the robustness of the approach.
*   Insufficient comparison to existing attention mechanisms and recurrent networks.
*   Clarity – the paper could benefit from a more concise and conceptually grounded presentation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The performance improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is a positive indicator.
*   The paper explores an interesting direction in CNN design.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of novelty and methodological rigor are significant concerns.
*   The limited ablation studies and generalizability raise doubts about the robustness of the approach.
*   The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that dasNet offers a substantial advantage over existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents an intriguing idea and shows promising results, the lack of novelty, methodological weaknesses, and limited generalizability outweigh the strengths. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a mature contribution that significantly advances the field. Substantial revisions addressing the concerns outlined above – including a more rigorous experimental evaluation, a clearer justification for the choice of SNES, and a more thorough comparison to existing methods – would be necessary for reconsideration. The claim of “state-of-the-art” performance feels overstated given the limitations of the current study.
