

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces "Deep Attention Selective Networks" (dasNet), a novel approach that enables deep CNNs to iteratively refine their classifications through feedback connections that modulate filter activations. The core idea is inspired by human visual processing, where experts may need multiple passes to distinguish between similar objects (e.g., two similar bird species). dasNet allows a pre-trained CNN to adjust its internal filter weights across multiple passes through the same image, with the attention policy learned via reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies due to the large parameter space).

The authors demonstrate that dasNet achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this dataset. The method is applied to a challenging vision task rather than toy problems, and the paper provides a compelling neuroscience-inspired motivation for the approach.

**Strengths:**
- **Novelty:** The core concept of learning feedback connections to enable internal selective attention through reinforcement learning is genuinely novel in the deep learning literature. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from previous work on recurrent networks and attention mechanisms by focusing on post-training adaptation rather than architectural changes.
- **Bold Approach:** The paper takes a principled step toward mimicking human visual processing by incorporating multiple passes with feedback, which is a significant conceptual leap beyond standard feedforward networks.
- **Practical Implementation:** The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space (over 1 million weights) is a pragmatic and well-justified choice, acknowledging computational constraints while still pursuing a meaningful approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Validation Limitations:** The paper compares primarily to older Maxout networks rather than the most recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet, or recent Vision Transformers). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.
- **Mechanism Explanation:** While the paper shows that dasNet can correct misclassifications, it lacks sufficient analysis of *how* the attention mechanism works—why certain filters are emphasized/de-emphasized, and when the approach fails.
- **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational overhead of multiple passes, which could be a significant limitation for real-world applications.
- **RL Justification:** The choice of SNES is presented as a pragmatic solution to parameter count, but the paper doesn't explore why other RL approaches wouldn't be suitable, weakening the methodological justification.

**Quality:** The methodology is technically sound, though the validation is limited to CIFAR-10/100 without thorough ablation studies or analysis of failure cases. The paper acknowledges the use of SNES as a pragmatic choice due to parameter count, but doesn't sufficiently explore why conventional RL methods wouldn't work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear algorithm description. However, some details about the observation vector used for RL could be expanded to better explain how the policy learns to adjust filter weights.

**Originality:** This is the paper's strongest aspect. The concept of using RL to learn feedback connections for internal selective attention is genuinely new and well-motivated by neuroscience. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work on recurrent networks and attention mechanisms.

**Significance:** While the 6% improvement is notable, the real significance lies in the conceptual innovation. This paper opens a new direction for deep learning research—post-training adaptation through internal selective attention—that could be applied to other vision tasks and potentially lead to more human-like visual processing systems.

**Overall Assessment:**
dasNet presents a bold, novel approach that significantly advances our understanding of how deep networks might more closely mimic human visual processing. While the validation could be more comprehensive, the core idea is sufficiently innovative to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper's main contribution is the conceptual framework rather than a specific technical breakthrough, which aligns with the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's novelty and potential to open a new research direction outweigh the current limitations in validation. Future work could address the computational cost and provide more detailed analysis of the attention mechanism, but the current work is sufficiently compelling for publication at NIPS. The paper successfully bridges neuroscience and deep learning in a way that could inspire significant future research.
