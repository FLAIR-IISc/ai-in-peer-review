Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), an extension to feedforward CNNs (specifically Maxout CNNs) that adds internal, learned, top-down feedback to implement selective attention. The mechanism introduces per-feature-map gates aℓj that multiplicatively modulate activations across layers. At inference, the same image is processed for T passes. After each pass, the network forms an observation vector from layer-wise average activations, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities. A policy π maps this observation to the next set of gates, thereby adjusting internal emphasis and potentially correcting ambiguous classifications. The policy is trained post hoc (after supervised training of the base CNN) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for scalability to million-parameter policies. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline and qualitative evidence that the policy emphasizes/de-emphasizes maps to disambiguate classes (e.g., cat vs. dog).

This connects to a substantial literature on feedback and attention in biological vision, and to computational work on recurrent processing, reinforcement learning-driven visual attention/saccades, and top-down modulation. Relative to prior CNN work (Maxout, NIN, dropout), the novelty here is internal, per-channel attentional gating driven by a learned, sequential policy. Compared to earlier RL-based attention models that move a fovea or choose glimpses, dasNet performs global, feature-wise modulation with multiple passes over the entire image, aiming to improve state-of-the-art classification rather than toy settings.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting idea: conditional, internal feature-map modulation via a learned policy, providing a principled form of top-down attention within a modern CNN.
- Practical mechanism: simple multiplicative gates preserve differentiability and allow stable control; observation design is lightweight (global statistics) and inexpensive.
- Sequential refinement: the multi-pass scheme matches the motivation from human vision and yields measurable improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline.
- Empirical evidence that gates are informative (using gate vectors for downstream classification).
- The paper is generally well written, with an algorithmic description and reasonable biological/machine learning context.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation: results are restricted to CIFAR-10/100 with modest absolute gains; claims of state-of-the-art for “unaugmented data” are unclear given that the base model uses data augmentation.
- Missing baselines and ablations:
  - No comparison to simpler alternatives that keep the same capacity and compute, e.g., a single-pass gating network trained end-to-end by backprop (unrolling T steps), or per-layer learned scale parameters fine-tuned on hard examples.
  - No ablation on which layers are gated, the dimensionality/architecture of the policy, or the observation components.
  - No comparison to other recurrent/feedback CNNs or attention mechanisms available at the time.
- Justification for SNES is mainly pragmatic; however, the gates and policy are fully differentiable. It is important to compare SNES to backpropagation through time (BPTT) or standard policy gradients to demonstrate a real learning advantage beyond implementation convenience.
- Computational cost and practicality: inference time grows linearly with T; training adds a multi-day SNES phase. The paper does not quantify overhead or analyze accuracy vs. compute trade-offs.
- Some clarity issues: notation occasionally overloaded (e.g., y, ȳ, v), and the boosting loss definition could be explained more cleanly. The “unaugmented” claim conflicts with earlier mention of augmentation.
- Risk of overfitting: million-parameter policy trained by black-box optimization; unclear validation protocol/early stopping criterion (on which split is SNES tuned?).

Quality
The method is technically sound as a mechanism for feedback control and is implemented on a competitive CNN. However, the experimental section is not as thorough as expected for NeurIPS standards. Missing baselines and compute reporting make it difficult to assess how much of the gain is due to sequential attention versus additional parameters and multiple evaluations.

Clarity
Generally clear, with a good high-level motivation and algorithm box. Some notational rough edges and a few inconsistencies (augmentation vs. “unaugmented”). More detail on SNES hyperparameters and stopping criteria would help.

Originality
Moderately original. The specific combination of per-channel feedback gates over multiple passes with an evolved policy is novel. The general idea of recurrent/attentional modulation and RL-driven control has prior art; the paper’s contribution is to bring it into a strong CNN and show improvement.

Significance
Incremental but potentially useful. If demonstrated convincingly with broader benchmarks, ablations, and compute trade-offs, the approach could influence conditional computation/attention in CNNs. As is, the scope and analysis limit the impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, implementable mechanism for top-down attention that improves a strong baseline on standard datasets.
- Conceptually appealing link between recurrent attention and modern CNNs.
- Empirical indication that the internal gates capture discriminative information.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient comparative evaluation and ablations; unclear necessity of SNES vs. gradient-based training.
- Limited analysis of compute cost and scalability; modest gains on small datasets.
- Some clarity/inconsistency issues in reporting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines:
  - Single-pass gating predicted from the first pass (no recurrence).
  - Unrolled T-step gating trained end-to-end with BPTT (supervised loss at final step).
  - Gating only top conv layer vs. all layers; show which contributes.
  - Policy gradient (REINFORCE/deterministic PG) vs. SNES.
- Report compute: train/inference time, memory, and accuracy vs. T; evaluate adaptive stopping.
- Provide confusion matrices and an error taxonomy; demonstrate improvements on specific confusable pairs.
- Clarify data augmentation usage and SOTA claims; include comparisons to contemporary recurrent/attention baselines.
- Analyze policy capacity: number of parameters, effect of diagonal vs. full covariance in SNES, observation ablations.
- Consider spatial attention or group-wise/channel-wise normalization alternatives; discuss stability and normalization choices for gates.

Overall recommendation: Reject (borderline)
The idea is interesting and plausibly impactful, but the empirical evaluation, comparisons, and analysis fall short of NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the experimental section and clarifying methodological choices would make this a much stronger submission.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
