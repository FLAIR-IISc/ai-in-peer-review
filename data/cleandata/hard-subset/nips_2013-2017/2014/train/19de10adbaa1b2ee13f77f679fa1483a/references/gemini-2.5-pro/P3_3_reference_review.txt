This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel method for improving the performance of pre-trained Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) on difficult classification tasks. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward CNN (in this case, a Maxout network) with a mechanism for internal selective attention, implemented through feedback connections. After an initial, standard feedforward pass, a policy network observes the internal state of the CNN and outputs a set of modulatory weights. These weights are applied to the feature maps of the convolutional layers for subsequent passes of the same input image. This iterative refinement process, framed as a form of "thinking," allows the network to dynamically emphasize or suppress features to correct initial misclassifications. The control policy is trained using reinforcement learning, specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to maximize classification accuracy, with a boosting mechanism to focus on initially misclassified examples. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-100, showing a significant improvement over the baseline Maxout network.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and conceptually appealing idea. The separation of a powerful, pre-trained perception model (the Maxout CNN) from an adaptive control policy that modulates its internal workings is a clever approach. It elegantly combines supervised learning (for the base feature extractors) with reinforcement learning (for the control policy). This post-hoc, "meta-learning" approach to improving a fixed model is an interesting research direction that is distinct from standard end-to-end training of recurrent or attention models. The framing of this process in the context of fast, pre-attentive vision followed by slower, deliberative processing is intuitive and well-motivated by cognitive science.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a tangible and significant performance improvement. Achieving a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-100 over a strong baseline like Maxout networks is a notable result and establishes a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data at the time of writing. The qualitative example in Figure 3, showing the correction of a 'cat' misclassified as a 'dog', provides compelling anecdotal evidence for the method's effectiveness.

3.  **Ambitious Problem Formulation:** The authors tackle a challenging problem: how to add adaptive, recurrent computation to large-scale deep networks. Their choice to use an evolution strategy (SNES) to handle the million-parameter policy space, while computationally expensive, is a pragmatic solution to a difficult optimization problem that might be intractable for traditional RL methods without significant engineering.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Methodological Justification and Baselines:** The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough justification of its methodological choices and comparison to simpler baselines.
    *   **RL Algorithm:** The choice of SNES is justified by the large parameter space, but the authors dismiss standard policy gradient methods without empirical comparison. While challenging, methods like REINFORCE or more advanced actor-critic variants could potentially be applied, especially with techniques like parameter sharing in the policy network. A discussion or experiment comparing SNES to a policy gradient baseline would make the choice more convincing.
    *   **State and Action Representation:** The state representation `o_t` is formed by averaging the activations of each output map, discarding all spatial information. This is a very coarse abstraction of the network's internal state. It is unclear if this is sufficient or if a richer representation (e.g., using spatial pooling or a small convolutional network to process the feature maps) would yield better results. Similarly, the action `a_t` applies a single scalar modulator to an entire feature map. This is a strong constraint, as it prevents the policy from spatially focusing attention within a map. The limitations of these design choices are not discussed.
    *   **Alternative Approaches:** A critical missing baseline is simply fine-tuning the original Maxout network on the "difficult" examples that dasNet is trained to fix. It is possible that some of the performance gain comes from simply exposing the model to these hard cases again. Another baseline could be ensembling `T` feedforward passes with small initial perturbations, or simply training a larger/deeper network with a similar computational budget.

2.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The proposed method introduces significant computational overhead. Training the policy with SNES requires `p` (population size) full `T`-step rollouts for `n` images per generation, which the authors note took four days. More importantly, inference time is increased by a factor of `T` (here, `T=5`). This makes the model five times slower at test time. The paper does not discuss this trade-off between accuracy and computational cost, which is a crucial consideration for practical applications. The scalability of the SNES-based training to even deeper networks (e.g., VGG, ResNet) with more feature maps (and thus larger policy parameter spaces) is also questionable.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, the paper lacks some key details for full reproducibility. The exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network is not specified. The observation function `h(·)` is described, but the precise dimensions of the resulting state vector are not given. The policy network `π_θ` is a simple linear mapping, and there is no discussion of whether more complex function approximators were considered. These missing details would make it difficult for other researchers to replicate the work.

4.  **Analysis:** The analysis of the learned policy is somewhat superficial. The information-content analysis of the final gate values is interesting, but it doesn't provide much insight into *what* the policy has learned. The visualization in Figure 3 is helpful, but the authors admit it is hard to qualitatively analyze. A more systematic analysis, perhaps by identifying which classes benefit most or what types of visual features are consistently up/down-weighted for certain ambiguous pairs (e.g., cat vs. dog), would greatly enhance the paper's contribution.

In summary, this paper presents a highly original and thought-provoking method for adding adaptive, attentive processing to deep networks. The core idea is strong, and the reported results are impressive. However, the work is hampered by a lack of comparison to simpler baselines, significant and unaddressed computational costs, and a somewhat coarse implementation of the state/action spaces. Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results make it a valuable contribution to the field that is likely to inspire future work on meta-control and iterative refinement in deep learning.
