This paper introduces dasNet, a fascinating approach to injecting a form of internal deliberation into deep convolutional networks via feedback connections and reinforcement learning. The core idea – allowing a network to re-evaluate its initial classification through iterative refinement of filter activations – is highly novel and addresses a compelling point about the limitations of purely feedforward processing, drawing a nice analogy to human visual perception. The authors rightly point out that current CNNs lack a mechanism for self-correction and propose an elegant solution using RL to learn an attentional policy.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The novelty is significant. While recurrent connections and attention mechanisms are not new, the *learned* selective attention applied *internally* within a CNN through RL is a genuinely fresh perspective. The use of SNES is pragmatic given the scale of the policy parameters and shows good engineering awareness. The empirical results on CIFAR-10/100, demonstrating improved performance on challenging instances and a relative improvement of 6% over a strong baseline, are encouraging. The visualization of filter activation changes in Figure 3 is insightful, providing some qualitative evidence that the learned policy is indeed modulating filters in a meaningful way. The dynamics experiment (Figure 2) is also helpful in establishing the stability of the learned behavior.

However, the work is not without weaknesses. While the results are promising, the improvement of 6% feels somewhat modest given the complexity of the approach. A more extensive evaluation on larger, more realistic datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would be crucial to assess the scalability and generalizability of dasNet. The ablation studies are limited. What is the impact of the number of iterations (T)? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of RL algorithm parameters (λ values)? A more thorough analysis of these factors would strengthen the claims. The observation space, based on average activations and output probabilities, feels potentially limited. Exploring more informative observations, perhaps incorporating intermediate feature representations, could lead to further improvements. The explanation of *why* certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized remains largely speculative, and connecting the learned behavior to interpretable features would be valuable. The discussion of related work, while broad, could benefit from a more focused examination of recent advancements in attention mechanisms specifically designed for CNNs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The concept of internally learned selective attention through feedback and RL is a significant departure from conventional CNN architectures.
*   **Promising results:** The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement, albeit moderate, on CIFAR-10/100.
*   **Sound methodology:** The use of SNES is justified and the experimental setup is reasonable.
*   **Addresses a fundamental limitation:** The paper tackles the crucial issue of self-correction and robustness in deep learning models.
*   **Opens new research directions:** This work could inspire further research into the development of more intelligent and adaptive CNNs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest performance gain:** The 6% improvement feels somewhat small considering the complexity of the approach.
*   **Limited scalability analysis:** The evaluation is limited to relatively small datasets.
*   **Lack of thorough ablation studies:** The sensitivity to key hyperparameters is not adequately investigated.
*   **Limited interpretability:** The reasons behind the learned filter modulations are not fully understood.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, I believe this paper represents a bold and potentially transformative contribution to the field. The core idea is highly original, the methodology is sound, and the results are encouraging. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction for research in deep learning. While further work is needed to address the weaknesses and fully explore the potential of dasNet, I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the points raised in the discussion during a potential rebuttal period. I value the novelty and ambitiousness of this work and believe it has the potential to stimulate further research in this exciting area.
