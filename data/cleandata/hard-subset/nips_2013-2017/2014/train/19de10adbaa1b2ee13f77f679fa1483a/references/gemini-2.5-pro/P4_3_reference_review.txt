### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, rapid feedforward pass, the network can perform additional passes over the same input image to refine its classification. During these subsequent passes, a control policy modulates the activity of the CNN's internal convolutional filters by applying multiplicative weights to their output maps. This policy is learned using reinforcement learning (RL) after the base CNN has been fully trained via supervised learning.

The motivation draws an analogy to human vision, where an initial "pre-attentive" perception can be followed by a slower, more deliberate "attentional" phase to resolve ambiguity. The authors frame this attentional process as an RL problem where the policy network takes the internal state of the CNN as an observation and outputs an action, which is the set of modulatory weights for the filters. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space, the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) for optimization. The method is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, using a Maxout network as the base model. The results show that dasNet can correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 for unaugmented data.

This work relates to previous research on recurrent connections in neural networks and the use of RL for visual attention. However, it distinguishes itself by applying these concepts to modulate the internal workings of a pre-trained, state-of-the-art deep network, rather than controlling external mechanisms like foveal saccades or being limited to simpler datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**

The paper presents a complete piece of work with a clear methodology and supporting experiments. The technical approach is sound. The framing of the filter modulation as an RL problem is appropriate, and the choice of SNES as an optimizer is well-justified given the large, continuous action space and the corresponding high-dimensional policy parameter space, which would be challenging for traditional RL algorithms.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The authors use a strong baseline (Maxout network) and demonstrate a clear performance improvement on standard benchmarks. The analysis extends beyond a simple accuracy score, including a qualitative visualization of the attentional process, an investigation of the system's dynamics over multiple time steps, and an experiment confirming that the learned attentional gates contain significant class-relevant information.

A notable weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the computational cost. The proposed method requires T (in this case, 5) full forward passes through the network at inference time, representing a significant increase in latency compared to the baseline. Furthermore, the RL training phase is computationally expensive, taking several days on a GPU. This trade-off between accuracy and efficiency is a critical aspect for practical applications and should be addressed.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are explained clearly. The introduction and related work sections effectively position the contribution within the broader context of deep learning and computational neuroscience. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the training procedure.

However, some implementation details could be specified more clearly to improve reproducibility. For instance, the composition of the observation vector `o_t` is described conceptually (concatenating average map activations, intermediate layer activations, and class probabilities), but its precise dimensionality, normalization, and construction are not detailed. While the visualization in Figure 3 is illustrative, the authors rightly note that interpreting the changes in high-level filters is difficult, which limits the intuitive understanding of the learned policy.

**Originality:**

The paper's primary contribution is novel. While concepts like feedback, recurrence, and attention in neural networks are not new, the specific mechanism proposed here is original. The idea of training a secondary control policy via RL to dynamically modulate the filters of a pre-trained deep network "in hindsight" is a distinct and creative approach. It separates the initial feature learning (via supervised learning) from the subsequent attentional refinement (via RL), which is an interesting architectural paradigm. This differs from prior work that often integrates recurrence directly into the training process or focuses on external attention mechanisms.

**Significance:**

The work addresses a significant and challenging problem in deep learning: improving model performance on ambiguous or "hard" examples that a single feedforward pass may misclassify. The ability to iteratively "re-think" a prediction is a valuable capability that could lead to more robust and reliable models. The reported state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (at the time) demonstrates the practical potential of the approach. The ideas presented could inspire further research into hybrid systems that combine fast, feedforward processing with slower, deliberative, and adaptive mechanisms.

The primary limitation on the work's significance is its scalability. The experiments are conducted on CIFAR datasets, which feature small images. It is unclear how the SNES-based optimization would scale to models designed for high-resolution images like those in ImageNet, where the number of filters—and thus the dimensionality of the action and policy parameter spaces—is substantially larger. This scalability question is a key factor in determining the broader impact of the method.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** The core idea of using a separately trained RL policy to modulate the internal filters of a pre-trained CNN for iterative refinement is a novel and compelling contribution.
2.  **Performance:** The method achieves a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-10), demonstrating its effectiveness.
3.  **Significance:** The paper tackles the important problem of improving deep network robustness on difficult classification instances, proposing a promising direction for future research.
4.  **Soundness:** The technical approach is sound, and the experiments are well-designed, including qualitative and quantitative analyses of the model's behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The significant increase in inference time (5x in the experiments) is a major practical drawback that is not analyzed or discussed by the authors.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are confined to small-scale datasets, and the scalability of the RL optimization approach to larger models and datasets like ImageNet is not addressed, leaving its broader applicability an open question.
3.  **Reproducibility:** Some key details of the RL observation space are underspecified, which could present challenges for reproducing the results.
