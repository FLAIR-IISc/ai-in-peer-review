

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which enhances standard CNNs by adding feedback connections that modulate filter activations through a reinforcement learning (RL) policy. The authors claim that dasNet can improve classification performance on difficult cases by iteratively focusing attention on discriminative features through multiple passes over the same image. The method uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn an attention policy that weights convolutional filters during inference, with experiments showing a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 compared to a vanilla Maxout network.

## Strengths
- The paper identifies an interesting problem: how to enable CNNs to "think" more about ambiguous cases during inference, which aligns with human visual processing.
- The idea of using reinforcement learning to learn attention mechanisms through feedback connections is conceptually appealing and potentially valuable for improving classification of difficult cases.
- The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 show promising improvements, particularly on ambiguous cases.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Equation Formatting and Notation**: The paper contains multiple formatting errors in mathematical expressions. For example, Equation (1) reads "yℓj = i=c∑ i=0 ϕ(xi ∗ F ℓi,j)" instead of the proper summation notation. Similarly, Equation (2) has "yℓj,x,y = b max i=0" rather than "yℓj,x,y = max_{i=0}^b". These errors make the paper difficult to follow and suggest lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Confusing Boosting Mechanism**: The paper defines λboost as "λboost = { λcorrect if d = ∥vT ∥∞ λmisclassified otherwise," which is mathematically incorrect. The notation implies a set rather than a piecewise function, and "d = ∥vT ∥∞" is confusing since ∥vT ∥∞ is a scalar value representing the maximum probability, not the correct class. The correct interpretation seems to be that λboost = λcorrect when the prediction is correct (i.e., when the predicted class matches the true label), but this is not clearly explained.

3. **Lack of Baseline Details**: The paper claims "a relative improvement of 6% with respect to the vanilla CNN" but fails to specify exactly what the baseline is. Is the baseline the Maxout network with the same architecture and training protocol? The paper mentions "data augmentation following global contrast normalization and ZCA normalization" but doesn't provide the exact architecture or training details of the baseline model.

4. **Insufficient Experimental Analysis**: The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism versus the specific choice of SNES. There's no comparison with other RL methods (e.g., PPO, DDPG) to demonstrate why SNES was the optimal choice. The paper also doesn't address the computational cost of the iterative process during inference.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "λboost" for the weighting factor but later refers to it as "λboostd" in Equation (8), which is confusing and inconsistent.

### Clarity and Presentation Issues
1. **Algorithm 1 Formatting**: The algorithm contains inconsistent symbols like "a0 ⇐ 1" (should be "a0 = 1" or "a0 ← 1") and "F [i] ⇐ f(θi)" (should be "F[i] = f(θ_i)"). These formatting errors undermine the paper's credibility.

2. **Missing Figure 3**: The paper references "Figure 3" to illustrate the attention mechanism but does not include the figure in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the claims about filter emphasis.

3. **Typographical Errors**: "Training took of dasNet took around 4 days" contains a clear grammatical error ("took of" should be "took").

4. **Unverified Claims**: The paper claims to establish "a new state-of-the-art result for this challenging dataset" but fails to compare with other recent state-of-the-art methods on CIFAR-10/100 (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets), making this claim unsubstantiated.

### Theoretical and Practical Concerns
1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis of why the proposed approach should work better than standard CNNs, beyond the intuitive argument about human vision. There's no discussion of the convergence properties of the SNES-based policy learning.

2. **Limited Scope of Experiments**: The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10/100 with only T=5 iterations. The paper claims "the dynamics stay stable when these are evaluated for as many as 10 steps," but doesn't provide evidence of how the performance scales with T beyond the training value.

3. **No Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't address potential limitations, such as the computational overhead of the iterative process during inference or whether the method generalizes to other datasets or tasks.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows some promising results, the numerous technical errors, unclear explanations, and lack of sufficient experimental validation significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's claims about being a "new state-of-the-art" are not properly supported by the evidence provided, and the methodological details are insufficient for reproducibility.

The paper has potential, but as presented, it fails to meet the high standards of technical rigor expected at NIPS. The numerous formatting errors, inconsistent notation, and lack of clear baseline comparisons make it difficult to assess the true contribution. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
