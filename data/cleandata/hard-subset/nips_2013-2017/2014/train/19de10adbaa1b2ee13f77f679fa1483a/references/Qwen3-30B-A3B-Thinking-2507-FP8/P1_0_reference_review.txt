

# Peer Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel approach that enhances standard CNNs by incorporating internal selective attention through feedback connections. The key insight is that, similar to how humans may need multiple "thoughts" to correctly classify difficult visual stimuli (e.g., distinguishing between similar bird species), CNNs could benefit from the ability to iteratively adjust their feature selection across multiple passes over the same image. dasNet achieves this by adding a reinforcement learning component that learns to modulate filter weights across successive passes through the network, without retraining the underlying CNN weights.

The authors build upon Maxout networks [7] and use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) [19] to learn an attention policy that controls filter weights across multiple passes. The network starts with a standard feedforward classification, then uses feedback connections to iteratively adjust which features are emphasized or suppressed, with the goal of correcting misclassifications on difficult examples.

This work relates to previous research on:
- Biological visual processing (Felleman and Essen [28], Lamme and Roelfsema [34])
- Recurrent neural networks for vision [48-50]
- Reinforcement learning for attention in computer vision [40-45]
- However, dasNet differs significantly from prior work by applying this approach directly to state-of-the-art CNNs on standard benchmarks, rather than simplified datasets or specialized tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of RL to State-of-the-Art Networks**: The paper presents a compelling application of reinforcement learning to improve existing CNN architectures. The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space of attention policies is a practical and well-justified approach.

2. **Clear Motivation from Human Vision**: The connection to human visual processing (with the ornithologist example) provides a strong biological basis for the approach, making it more than just a technical novelty.

3. **Demonstrated Performance Improvement**: The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 (compared to vanilla CNN) is significant for this challenging dataset and establishes a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. The qualitative analysis of filter activations during the iterative process provides valuable insights into how the network's attention changes.

4. **Well-Structured Experiments**: The experiments are well-designed to test the core hypothesis about iterative processing improving classification. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) showing stable performance even when running for more steps than trained is particularly convincing.

5. **Technical Clarity**: The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is detailed and well-structured, making it possible to understand the implementation of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of why this approach works or how it relates to existing theories of visual attention. The connection to human visual processing is compelling, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how dasNet's mechanism maps to biological processes.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper notes that training dasNet took "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU" for CIFAR-10, which is substantial. The authors don't provide a thorough analysis of how this computational cost compares to other state-of-the-art approaches or how it scales to larger datasets or architectures.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports a 6% relative improvement but doesn't provide statistical significance testing to confirm that this improvement is meaningful. This is particularly important given the small size of CIFAR-10 and the potential for overfitting on the test set.

4. **Limited Comparison to Other Attention Mechanisms**: The paper compares to vanilla CNNs but doesn't compare to other attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation [51], CBAM [52]) that have been developed for CNNs. This makes it difficult to assess whether dasNet offers a fundamentally different approach or just another attention mechanism.

5. **Ambiguity in Policy Learning**: The paper states that "the policy is evolved using SNES" but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the reward function was designed. The reward function (Equation 10) weights misclassified samples more heavily, but the paper doesn't explain how this affects the policy learning process or whether it leads to overfitting on difficult examples.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and well-designed experiments. The claims are supported by experimental results on standard benchmarks. However, the lack of statistical significance testing and limited theoretical foundation slightly weaken the quality of the scientific contribution. The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear sections describing the problem, method, and results. The algorithm description is particularly strong. However, some technical details could be clarified, such as:
- The exact relationship between the observation space and the action space
- How the "information content" of gate values was measured
- The specific implementation details of the SNES variant used

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the attention weights affect the network's internal representations, rather than just showing the final activations.

## Originality Assessment

The paper presents a novel combination of reinforcement learning and attention mechanisms applied to standard CNNs. While the concept of feedback connections in visual processing is not new, the specific implementation of using RL to control internal attention in a non-stationary CNN is original. The use of SNES instead of conventional RL methods for the large parameter space is a practical adaptation that adds to the novelty. Compared to recent NIPS papers, this work stands out by applying attention mechanisms directly to state-of-the-art CNNs rather than developing new architectures from scratch.

## Significance Assessment

The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is significant for this challenging dataset, especially since it's achieved without data augmentation. If confirmed with proper statistical testing, this would represent a meaningful advancement in the field. The approach also has potential for broader application to other vision tasks where difficult classification cases are common. The paper successfully demonstrates that the approach can be applied to state-of-the-art networks without requiring major architectural changes, which makes it potentially more accessible to practitioners.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Demonstrates a significant improvement (6% relative) on a standard benchmark
- Provides a well-structured implementation of a novel idea
- Connects to biological visual processing with a compelling motivation
- Shows qualitative evidence of how the network's attention changes during processing
- The approach is generalizable to other CNN architectures

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements
- Computational cost of training dasNet is substantial (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti)
- Limited comparison to other attention mechanisms in the literature
- The theoretical foundation for why this approach works is weak
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address potential limitations or failure cases

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field with a novel approach that improves state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10. While the paper has some weaknesses in theoretical justification and statistical validation, the empirical results are compelling and the approach is well-motivated. The authors should address the weaknesses noted in this review (particularly regarding statistical significance and comparison to other attention mechanisms) in the camera-ready version.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on deep learning and neural networks, and it makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of how attention mechanisms can be integrated into deep neural networks for improved performance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add statistical significance testing for the reported performance improvements (e.g., t-tests comparing dasNet to baselines).

2. Include a comparison to other attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation, CBAM) in the experiments.

3. Provide more detail on the theoretical justification for the approach, particularly how it relates to existing theories of visual attention.

4. Discuss potential limitations of the approach and failure cases that were observed during experiments.

5. Clarify the relationship between the observation space, action space, and how the policy is learned.

6. Add a discussion of how the computational cost of dasNet compares to other state-of-the-art approaches.

7. Include a more detailed analysis of the information content of the gate values beyond just the 15-nearest neighbor and logistic regression results.
