**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments standard deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, fast feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional "thinking" steps on the same input image. In these subsequent steps, a learned policy modulates the activations of convolutional feature maps, effectively up-weighting or down-weighting the influence of specific filters to refine the classification. The base network is a pre-trained Maxout CNN. The control policy, which maps the network's internal state to modulatory actions (gate values), is trained using a black-box optimization method, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), framed as a reinforcement learning problem. The objective is to correct initial misclassifications on difficult examples. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 without data augmentation.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and conceptually appealing approach. Moving beyond purely static, feedforward architectures towards dynamic, recurrent processing is an important direction for the field. The motivation, drawn from the two-stage (pre-attentive vs. attentive) model of human vision, is compelling and provides a strong foundation for the work. This method of "post-hoc" refinement of a pre-trained network is a unique contribution that could inspire a new class of models that iteratively process information.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement over a strong baseline (Maxout network), achieving a 6% relative improvement and a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 (for unaugmented data). This is a significant result that validates the practical utility of the proposed approach, moving it beyond a purely conceptual contribution.

3.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The authors provide additional experiments to analyze the system's behavior. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2), showing that the system remains stable even when run for more steps than it was trained for, is a good indicator of robustness. The experiment demonstrating that the final gate values themselves contain significant class-relevant information is a clever way to validate that the learned policy is performing a meaningful task, rather than just randomly perturbing the network.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Methodological Justification:**
    *   **Choice of RL Algorithm:** The use of SNES is justified by the large parameter space of the policy. However, this justification is insufficient. Standard policy gradient methods (e.g., REINFORCE, A2C, PPO) are routinely used to train policies with millions of parameters. A more thorough discussion comparing SNES to gradient-based RL methods is needed. As a black-box, gradient-free method, SNES can be significantly less sample-efficient, and it is unclear if it is the optimal choice here.
    *   **State and Policy Representation:** The state representation `o_t` is formed by concatenating the *average* activation of each output map, along with higher-level features. Averaging throws away all spatial information, which seems like a critical loss of information for a policy that aims to modulate spatially-organized feature maps. Furthermore, the policy `π_θ` is a simple linear transformation followed by a custom softmax. A more expressive policy, such as a multi-layer perceptron, might be better suited for this complex control task. These design choices feel overly simplistic and under-justified.

2.  **Experimental Gaps:**
    *   **CIFAR-100 Results:** While CIFAR-100 is used for the dynamics analysis, the main results table (Table 1) only reports performance on CIFAR-10. Given that CIFAR-100 has more classes and more potential for ambiguity, it serves as a more challenging and perhaps more appropriate testbed for an attentional-correction mechanism. The omission of final classification accuracy on CIFAR-100 is a significant weakness.
    *   **Computational Cost:** The trade-off between performance and computational cost is not discussed. Training dasNet takes an additional four days *after* the base model is trained. More importantly, inference requires `T+1` forward passes through the network. This is a substantial increase in computational cost at test time (a 6x increase for T=5). It is crucial to compare the dasNet's performance against a baseline of simply training a larger or deeper feedforward network that has a similar computational budget. It is possible that a larger static network could achieve similar gains without the complexity of the RL framework.

3.  **Clarity and Analysis:**
    *   **Action Space:** The actions modulate entire feature maps uniformly. This is a coarse form of attention. The authors acknowledge that spatial attention is a direction for future work, but the limitations of the current approach should be more clearly discussed.
    *   **Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 3 is intriguing but the analysis is superficial. The authors state, "It is hard to qualitatively analyze the effect," but a deeper attempt would strengthen the paper. For instance, can the filters that are up-weighted be linked to specific features that help disambiguate "cat" vs. "dog" (e.g., snout shape, ear texture)? Without this, the mechanism remains a black box.
    *   **Equation 7:** The description of the modified softmax function is slightly unconventional ("scaled by the dimensionality... so that elements... average to 1"). A more formal definition would improve clarity for reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This paper presents a highly original and significant idea: enabling a deep network to iteratively "re-think" its predictions through an RL-trained internal attention mechanism. The concept is well-motivated and the experimental results on CIFAR-10 are strong, demonstrating the potential of this research direction.

However, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning the justification for key methodological choices (SNES, state representation), the omission of crucial experimental results (CIFAR-100 accuracy, computational cost comparison), and a somewhat shallow analysis of the learned mechanism.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the core idea are substantial. This work opens a new and exciting avenue for research in deep learning. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance, with the strong recommendation that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version of the paper, particularly by including the CIFAR-100 results and providing a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs and methodological choices.

**Decision:** Weak Accept.
