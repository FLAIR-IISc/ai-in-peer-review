Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with recurrent, top-down “attention” connections that modulate the per-feature-map activations multiplicatively over multiple passes on the same image. A policy π maps observations (concatenation of average feature-map activations from all layers, intermediate classifier activations, and class probabilities) to actions (a vector of positive gates, one per output map, normalized via a scaled softmax so the mean gate is 1). At test time, an image is processed T times: the first pass uses identity gates; subsequent passes use the policy’s actions to reweight feature maps dynamically. The policy is learned after standard supervised training of the CNN, by optimizing the final-step negative log-likelihood (boosted for misclassified training examples) using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), chosen for computational scalability with ~10^6 policy parameters.

The work is positioned within biologically inspired feedback and selective attention in vision (e.g., Felleman & Van Essen; Lamme & Roelfsema), and computational attention/recurrence in vision and RL (e.g., Denil et al.; O’Reilly; Fukushima; Larochelle & Hinton). Relative to prior CNNs that are purely feedforward, dasNet introduces internal, state-dependent, learned feedback that modulates intermediate representations rather than, e.g., moving a fovea.

On CIFAR-10/100, the authors report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline, claiming state-of-the-art performance for (test-time) unaugmented data. Qualitative analyses suggest the policy differentially up/down-weights maps across layers to correct initial mistakes (e.g., cat vs dog), and that final-step gate vectors carry class-relevant information.

Quality
- Positives: The mechanism is well-motivated, technically sound at a high level, and the system appears to deliver consistent gains on CIFAR-10/100. The feedback loop is stable beyond the trained horizon, and the observation/action design is sensible. The scaled softmax for gating is a thoughtful design choice.
- Concerns:
  - Learning method: The policy is a single linear layer with softmax over a very high-dimensional action; it is fully differentiable through the recurrent unrolled computation. The choice to use SNES rather than backpropagation through time (BPTT) or policy gradients is not convincingly justified beyond dimensionality. Gradient-based training seems feasible, likely more sample-efficient, and would allow end-to-end learning and better ablations.
  - Baselines: Missing comparisons to (i) simple fine-tuning of the CNN on hard examples; (ii) a recurrent/gated variant trained with BPTT; (iii) per-layer/static learned gates; (iv) multi-pass inference with fixed gates; (v) ensembling multiple passes without feedback; (vi) using only top-layer gates. It is therefore hard to attribute improvements specifically to learned feedback.
  - Experimental detail: The paper claims “state-of-the-art for unaugmented data,” but the base CNN is trained with data augmentation. Clarify what “unaugmented” means at test time and show exact numbers vs prior SOTA with consistent protocols. Report multiple runs/seeds and confidence intervals.
  - Compute and scalability: The approach multiplies inference cost by T and adds heavy SNES training. The paper does not quantify the test-time overhead or amortized training cost relative to alternative recurrent/attention baselines.
  - Objective/notation clarity: The loss equations (8–10) are hard to parse (e.g., the role of d and λboost). A standard cross-entropy formulation would be clearer.

Clarity
The main idea and algorithmic flow are clear, and the biological/RL context is well-motivated. However:
- Some notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., loss expression and definition of d).
- The policy class is not explicitly described as linear (it is implied by πθ(o) = σ(θ o)); stating this clearly would help interpret capacity.
- More architectural details (numbers of maps per layer, action/observation dimensionalities, parameter counts) should be provided for reproducibility.

Originality
The specific combination—feature-map-wise multiplicative feedback learned via an evolved policy acting on layer summaries—is a novel instantiation of internal attention for CNNs at the time. Prior work has used RL for attention (e.g., saccades/glimpses) and recurrence/feedback in vision, but modulating intermediate CNN maps across multiple passes with a learned policy is an original angle. That said, the learning approach (black-box evolution for a differentiable policy) is unconventional and arguably suboptimal.

Significance
Demonstrated gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline indicate potential. If robust and generalizable, the idea of learned, internal, feature-wise feedback could be impactful and complementary to standard feedforward improvements. However, the current empirical evidence is limited to small images, lacks strong baselines, and does not quantify compute trade-offs, which tempers practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, biologically inspired mechanism that is simple to integrate with existing CNNs.
- Measurable improvements on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong baseline.
- Interesting diagnostic analyses (gate informativeness, stability beyond T).
- Potentially general approach to “think twice” by reweighting internal features.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak justification for SNES over gradient-based training; likely sample-inefficient and cumbersome.
- Missing strong and fair baselines and ablations to isolate where gains come from.
- Limited scope (CIFAR only), modest claimed SOTA without thorough comparison details.
- Reproducibility and clarity issues (loss definition, hyperparameters, compute costs).

Suggestions for improvement
- Train the policy (and optionally the CNN) end-to-end with BPTT; compare to SNES and report sample/compute efficiency and accuracy.
- Add ablations: gates only in top layer vs all layers; T=1 vs >1; static learned gates; different observation subsets; stochastic vs deterministic policy; capacity of policy (add hidden layer).
- Include stronger baselines: standard fine-tuning on hard examples; multi-pass without feedback; simple calibration/reweighting in the classifier; small recurrent CNNs without explicit RL.
- Provide complete experimental details and multiple seeds; report test-time latency and training compute.
- Extend to a larger dataset or higher resolution (e.g., downsampled ImageNet) to assess scalability.
- Clarify the loss and notations; provide code or pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Decision
Recommendation: Reject (weak). The paper presents an appealing idea and promising results, but the evidence is not yet rigorous enough for NeurIPS. With stronger baselines, clearer methodology, end-to-end training comparisons, and broader evaluation, this work could become a solid contribution.
