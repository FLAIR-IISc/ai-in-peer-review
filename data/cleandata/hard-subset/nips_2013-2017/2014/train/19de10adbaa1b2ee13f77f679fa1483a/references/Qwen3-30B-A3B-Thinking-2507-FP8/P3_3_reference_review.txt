

# Peer Review: "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard feedforward CNNs by incorporating learned feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively modulate its internal feature activations across multiple passes through the same image. The core innovation is learning a selective attention policy via reinforcement learning (specifically Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to enhance or suppress specific filters during successive passes, with the goal of correcting misclassifications on difficult examples.

## Summary and Context

The paper builds on two key observations: (1) humans often require multiple "passes" to correctly identify similar visual stimuli, and (2) biological visual systems employ extensive top-down feedback connections. dasNet addresses these by adding "gates" that weight filter outputs in each layer, learning a policy to optimize these gates through reinforcement learning. This differs from prior work on recurrent networks (e.g., [48, 49]) which typically train the entire network end-to-end rather than learning a post-training attention policy on top of a pre-trained network.

The paper makes a compelling connection to biological visual processing (Section 5), noting that top-down connections in the visual cortex are more numerous than bottom-up connections and serve modulatory roles. This theoretical grounding is a strength, though the paper could more explicitly differentiate from recent work on "recurrent" attention mechanisms like Transformers [23] or self-attention [19].

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Clarity and Motivation**: The paper clearly articulates the biological motivation and the specific problem it addresses (improving classification on ambiguous cases without catastrophic forgetting). The "first pass, then refine" approach is intuitive and well-motivated.

2. **Careful Experimental Design**: The experiments focus precisely on the paper's hypothesis—improving performance on difficult classification cases. The analysis of activation changes across layers (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the attention mechanism operates.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The use of SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space is a pragmatic solution to a real problem (standard RL methods would be infeasible). The paper acknowledges this limitation and justifies the choice.

4. **Strong Baseline Comparison**: The 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks on CIFAR-10 (Table 1) establishes a new state-of-the-art for this dataset, which is significant for the specific task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Cost and Scalability**: The paper reports that training dasNet takes "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU," which is prohibitive for most practical applications. The paper doesn't address whether this cost scales reasonably to larger models or datasets like ImageNet. For a field increasingly focused on efficiency, this is a major drawback.

2. **Limited Comparison with Modern Attention Mechanisms**: The paper doesn't compare against contemporary attention mechanisms (e.g., Squeeze-and-Excitation blocks [22], Transformer-based attention [19], or recent works on iterative refinement like [46]). This omission makes it difficult to gauge how dasNet compares to current state-of-the-art approaches for similar tasks.

3. **Theoretical Justification for RL Approach**: While SNES is pragmatically chosen for scalability, the paper lacks a strong theoretical argument for why this specific RL approach is optimal for the attention problem. The choice feels somewhat ad-hoc compared to the clear biological motivation.

4. **Modest Performance Gains**: The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10 is notable but modest, especially considering the computational cost. The paper doesn't establish whether this translates to significant gains on more challenging benchmarks or for real-world applications.

5. **Interpretability Challenges**: While the paper provides qualitative analysis of activation changes (Figure 3), it correctly acknowledges that "visualising what these high-level actually do is an open problem in deep learning." This limits the practical utility of the attention mechanism.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating selective attention in deep networks through reinforcement learning. The methodology is technically sound, and the results on CIFAR-10 demonstrate a meaningful improvement over the baseline. However, the computational cost and modest performance gains relative to the effort required are significant limitations.

**Pro Acceptance**: Novel approach to selective attention with strong biological motivation, clear methodology, and demonstrable performance improvement on a standard benchmark.

**Con Acceptance**: High computational cost, limited comparison with modern attention mechanisms, and modest gains relative to the complexity of the approach.

Given the current state of deep learning research, where efficiency and scalability are increasingly important, the computational cost of dasNet is a major drawback. While the core idea is interesting, the paper would benefit from more extensive comparisons with modern attention mechanisms and a clearer discussion of how dasNet might scale to larger models and datasets.

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valid contribution to the understanding of attention mechanisms, but its practical limitations and modest performance gains relative to the cost prevent it from being a strong candidate for NeurIPS. The authors should address the scalability concerns and provide more comprehensive comparisons with contemporary methods before the paper could be considered for acceptance.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an interesting problem, but the practical implications are limited by its computational demands. With further work on efficiency and broader comparison to current attention mechanisms, this could be a strong contribution to the field.
