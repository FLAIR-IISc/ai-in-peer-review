This paper introduces dasNet, a deep convolutional neural network architecture incorporating feedback connections and trained via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES) to dynamically adjust convolutional filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to mimic the iterative, attention-focused processing observed in the human visual cortex, allowing the network to refine its classifications through successive passes over an image. The authors demonstrate improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 compared to previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets.

This work builds on a substantial body of research in deep learning, particularly CNNs [1, 4, 6, 7]. The introduction effectively situates dasNet within this context, highlighting the limitations of traditional feedforward architectures and drawing parallels to biological visual processing [16, 17, 28, 30]. The use of Maxout networks [7] as the base architecture is a reasonable choice, given their previous success. The application of reinforcement learning to modulate filter activations is a novel extension, addressing the challenge of high-dimensional control spaces using SNES [19]. Related work sections cover relevant areas like recurrent connections [31, 34, 35], RL for attention [40, 41, 42] and iterative processing [48, 49], and correctly points out that this work differs by applying these techniques to a state-of-the-art CNN framework to achieve performance improvements.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The integration of feedback connections and reinforcement learning for dynamic filter modulation represents a unique approach to CNN design.
* **Performance:** The reported improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. Achieving state-of-the-art results, even on unaugmented data, is significant.
* **Biological Inspiration:** The motivation from human visual processing provides a strong rationale for the architecture.
* **Scalability (SNES):** The use of SNES addresses the practical challenge of optimizing a large number of parameters, making the approach feasible for complex networks.
* **Analysis:** The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provides some insight into the network’s decision-making process, and the dynamics study (Figure 2) shows promising stability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The architecture is considerably more complex than standard CNNs. The additional training process using SNES adds a significant computational burden (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti).
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates improved performance, a more comprehensive ablation study would be beneficial. For example, it is unclear how much each component (feedback connections, SNES, Maxout base network) contributes to the overall improvement.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions fixed values for several hyperparameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2, T), but does not provide details on how these were selected. This raises concerns about sensitivity to these parameters.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis of filter activations (Figure 3) is limited. While it shows changes in activation, it lacks a deeper interpretation of what these changes signify in terms of feature representation. What specific features are being emphasized or suppressed?
* **SNES Details:** While SNES is mentioned, a more detailed explanation, or a pointer to a comprehensive resource, might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the technique. The supplementary material reference is not enough.
* **Reproducibility:** While the paper aims for completeness, a detailed listing of hyperparameters, and perhaps code availability, would greatly enhance reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to CNN design.
* The reported performance improvements are significant and demonstrate the potential of the method.
* The use of SNES tackles a critical challenge in applying reinforcement learning to large-scale networks.
* The work is relevant to the NIPS community, addressing a core problem in deep learning with a creative solution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The added complexity and computational cost may limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The lack of a comprehensive ablation study weakens the claims about the contribution of each component.
* The limited qualitative analysis makes it difficult to understand the inner workings of dasNet.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the method is complex and requires further investigation, the novelty of the approach and the demonstrated performance improvements are compelling. The work provides a promising direction for future research in dynamic and attention-focused neural networks. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of each component.
2.  Provide more justification for the chosen hyperparameters.
3.  Expand the qualitative analysis of filter activations, offering a more in-depth interpretation of the observed changes.
4.  Provide more details about SNES implementation or a clear pointer to external resources.
5.  Consider making the code and hyperparameters publicly available to facilitate reproducibility.
