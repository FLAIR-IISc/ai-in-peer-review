This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture incorporating feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies – SNES). The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification, mimicking aspects of human visual processing where multiple feedforward passes are used for ambiguous cases. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 compared to a baseline Maxout network.

The paper is well-structured, presenting a clear motivation, a detailed explanation of the dasNet architecture, and experimental results. It appropriately cites relevant work in CNNs, reinforcement learning, and visual attention. The use of Maxout networks as a base provides a solid foundation. The explanation of SNES, while concise, is adequate given references to prior work. The experiments are focused and demonstrate a clear performance improvement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections within a CNN architecture, learned through reinforcement learning (SNES), is a novel approach. While recurrent connections have been explored in vision (e.g., [48, 49, 50]), learning the feedback weights via RL, and specifically SNES for high dimensionality, is a significant contribution.
*   **Performance:** The reported performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 establish the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (on unaugmented data) is a strong result.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and the training process. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.
*   **Justification:** The motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing (e.g., [16, 17, 31]), is well-articulated and provides a plausible rationale for the approach.
*   **Thorough experimentation**: The authors perform a dynamic study, showing that the network’s performance remains stable after the training steps. They also analyze the gates and show that they contain useful information for classification.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** Training dasNet takes a significant amount of time (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU). This may limit the scalability and accessibility of the approach. The paper doesn’t offer much discussion of strategies for reducing this cost.
*   **Limited Analysis of Feedback:** While the paper shows a visualization of filter changes (Figure 3), a more in-depth analysis of *what* the feedback mechanism is learning would be beneficial.  What specific features are being emphasized/suppressed, and why? This analysis is currently somewhat superficial.
*   **SNES Details:** While SNES is appropriately referenced, the specific hyperparameter choices for SNES (e.g., population size, mutation rates) are not extensively discussed. This limits reproducibility.
*   **Comparison to alternatives**: The authors only compare against a Maxout network. It’s unclear how this approach would perform compared to more recent architectures like ResNets or DenseNets.
*   **Generalizability**: The performance is shown on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. Results on more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would further demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving CNN performance through the incorporation of learned feedback connections.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance improvement on challenging datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   The use of SNES for training demonstrates a practical solution to the challenges of learning in a high-dimensional parameter space.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of training dasNet is high.
*   The analysis of the feedback mechanism is limited.
*   The lack of comparison to more modern CNN architectures.
*   The limited generalizability to larger and more complex datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and potentially significant contribution. The novelty of the approach, combined with the demonstrated performance improvements, outweighs the weaknesses. While the computational cost and limited analysis are concerns, they do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work. The paper is of high quality and suitable for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. A minor revision addressing the analysis of the feedback mechanism and discussion of computational cost might further strengthen the paper.
