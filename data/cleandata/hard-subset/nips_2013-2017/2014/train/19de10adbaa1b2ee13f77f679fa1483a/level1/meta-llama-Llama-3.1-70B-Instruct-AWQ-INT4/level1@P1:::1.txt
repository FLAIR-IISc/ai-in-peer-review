This paper introduces a novel deep neural network architecture called Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet) that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors draw inspiration from the human visual cortex, where feedback connections play a crucial role in object recognition. The proposed architecture allows the network to iteratively focus its attention on the most discriminative features in an image, leading to improved classification performance.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The use of Maxout networks as the underlying model for dasNet is a good choice, given their state-of-the-art performance in object recognition tasks. The authors also provide a detailed description of the reinforcement learning approach used to train the attentional policy, including the use of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES).

The experimental evaluation of dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets shows promising results, with dasNet outperforming the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets. The authors also provide a qualitative analysis of the attentional policy, demonstrating that it is able to correct misclassifications by emphasizing and de-emphasizing certain filters.

The paper's strengths include its novelty, clarity, and significance. The proposed architecture is a significant departure from traditional feedforward convolutional neural networks, and the use of reinforcement learning to train the attentional policy is a unique approach. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are significant, demonstrating the potential of dasNet to improve classification performance.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. One of the main limitations is the computational cost of training dasNet, which requires a significant amount of time and computational resources. The authors mention that training dasNet took around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, which may be a barrier for some researchers. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the attentional policy, including a comparison with other attentional mechanisms.

In terms of originality, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, as it introduces a novel architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections. The use of reinforcement learning to train the attentional policy is also a unique approach. However, the paper builds upon existing work in the field, including the use of Maxout networks and SNES.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the potential of dasNet to improve classification performance on challenging datasets. The results are significant, and the paper has the potential to impact the field of deep learning, particularly in the area of computer vision.
