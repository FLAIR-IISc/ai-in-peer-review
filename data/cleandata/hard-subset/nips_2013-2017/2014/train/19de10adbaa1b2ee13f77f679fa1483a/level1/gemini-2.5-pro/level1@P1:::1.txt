**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feed-forward convolutional neural network (CNN) with a recurrent feedback mechanism to implement a form of selective attention. The core idea is to improve classification accuracy, particularly on difficult or ambiguous examples, by allowing the network to perform multiple processing "passes" on a single image. The base model is a pre-trained Maxout network. In each subsequent pass, a control policy modulates the activations of the convolutional filter maps, effectively enhancing or suppressing certain features. This policy is learned via reinforcement learning (RL), where the state is derived from network activations and the action is the set of modulatory weights for all filters. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over 1 million), the authors employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method, for training. The authors motivate their approach from neuroscience, drawing parallels to the pre-attentive (feedforward) and attentive (recurrent) phases of human vision. They demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet by achieving state-of-the-art results on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, outperforming the baseline Maxout network.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and significant contribution. At a time when deep learning research was heavily focused on deeper and more efficient feed-forward architectures, this work introduces a compelling recurrent, top-down attention mechanism. It moves beyond the static processing paradigm of traditional CNNs and proposes a dynamic, iterative refinement process. This idea of internal, selective attention was forward-thinking and pre-dates many of the attention mechanisms that later became highly influential in the field.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The claims of the paper are well-supported by strong empirical evidence. The authors don't just demonstrate their idea on a toy problem; they apply it to a strong, state-of-the-art baseline (Maxout networks) on challenging benchmark datasets (CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100). The reported 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data, is a convincing demonstration of the method's efficacy.

3.  **Novel Methodological Combination:** The combination of a deep CNN with a reinforcement learning framework for attention is innovative. The choice of SNES to tackle the extremely high-dimensional policy space is a clever and practical solution to a very difficult optimization problem, showcasing the potential of evolutionary strategies for training complex neural components where gradients may be difficult to compute or lead to poor local optima.

4.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is clearly written and the proposed model is well-explained with the help of Algorithm 1. The motivation from the neuroscience literature on the human visual cortex is well-argued and provides a strong conceptual foundation for the architecture.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Justification of RL Algorithm:** The paper states that standard RL methods are impractical due to the large parameter space, leading to the choice of SNES. However, policy gradient methods (e.g., REINFORCE) with neural network function approximators are also capable of handling very large parameter spaces. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion or empirical comparison justifying why SNES is preferable to policy gradients in this context. Is it due to variance issues, exploration challenges, or computational efficiency?

2.  **State Representation:** The composition of the observation vector `o_t` (concatenated average map activations, intermediate activations, and class probabilities) seems somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors acknowledge that this provides only "partial state information," the work could be improved by an ablation study analyzing the contribution of each component of the state vector. This would provide valuable insight into what information the policy is using to make its decisions.

3.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The training process is computationally intensive, requiring 4 days on a GPU *after* the base CNN has already been trained. This two-stage training process (supervised pre-training followed by RL) and the high cost of the RL phase might limit the practicality and scalability of the approach to larger models or datasets like ImageNet. An end-to-end training procedure, while likely more challenging, would be a more elegant and powerful extension.

4.  **Interpretability:** While the visualization in Figure 3 is an interesting attempt to understand the learned policy, the authors rightly note that it is "hard to qualitatively analyze". The analysis showing that final gate values contain significant class information is a good quantitative step, but the mechanism by which the network "corrects" its predictions remains somewhat opaque. This is a general challenge in deep learning, but it is particularly pertinent here given the model's dynamic nature.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Introduces a highly original and significant idea of adding a learned, recurrent attention mechanism to deep CNNs.
*   Demonstrates a clear and impressive improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline on standard benchmarks.
*   Presents an innovative and successful application of evolution strategies to a challenging reinforcement learning problem in a deep learning context.
*   The work opens up a new and promising research direction for building more dynamic and biologically plausible vision models.

**Arguments against Acceptance**

*   The training methodology is computationally expensive and not end-to-end, which may limit its practical impact.
*   Some design choices, particularly the state representation and the choice of RL algorithm, could be better motivated or justified against alternatives.
*   The analysis of the learned attention mechanism is preliminary, leaving the inner workings of the model somewhat unclear.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant piece of work. The introduction of a dynamic, internal attention mechanism trained with reinforcement learning is a strong conceptual contribution that is backed by excellent empirical results. While there are some weaknesses regarding computational cost and justification for specific design choices, the strengths of the paper—particularly its novelty and its demonstrable performance gains—far outweigh these concerns. This paper presents an important step beyond purely feed-forward models and is likely to inspire future research into attention and recurrent processing in deep networks. I strongly recommend acceptance.
