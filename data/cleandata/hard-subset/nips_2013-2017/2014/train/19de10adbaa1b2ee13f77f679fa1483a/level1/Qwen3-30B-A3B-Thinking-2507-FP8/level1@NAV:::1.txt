

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into traditional CNNs to enable internal selective attention through sequential processing. The authors propose that by allowing the network to iteratively adjust filter sensitivities during classification (rather than being stationary), performance on ambiguous cases can be improved. dasNet uses Maxout networks as a base and trains feedback connections via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle the large parameter space. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, where it achieves a 6% relative improvement over vanilla CNNs, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented datasets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing body of work on attention mechanisms in deep learning. It builds upon previous NIPS work on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013) and relates to broader neuroscience-inspired architectures like the Neural Abstraction Pyramid (Behnke, 2012). The authors effectively distinguish their approach from prior work on recurrent networks (e.g., Wyatte et al., 2013) by focusing on post-training internal attention rather than structural changes to the network.

Strengths include strong technical execution, thorough experimental evaluation with insightful visualizations of attention dynamics, and a clear demonstration of the approach's effectiveness on challenging classification cases. The SNES implementation is well-justified for the large parameter space. The paper also thoughtfully addresses potential limitations through ablation studies.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of computational efficiency (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), which may hinder practical adoption, and insufficient exploration of how the approach might generalize to larger datasets like ImageNet. The connection to recent transformer-based attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) is also underdeveloped.

Quality: Strong experimental validation, but limited scalability analysis. Clarity: Well-written with excellent visualizations, but some technical details on SNES could be clarified. Originality: Novel integration of feedback connections for internal attention in CNNs. Significance: Demonstrates tangible improvements on standard benchmarks, though practical deployment considerations are not fully addressed.

Pro: Novel approach to attention in CNNs, strong empirical results, clear presentation. Con: Computational overhead, limited generalization analysis, insufficient comparison to recent attention mechanisms.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the field of attention mechanisms in deep learning. I recommend acceptance.
