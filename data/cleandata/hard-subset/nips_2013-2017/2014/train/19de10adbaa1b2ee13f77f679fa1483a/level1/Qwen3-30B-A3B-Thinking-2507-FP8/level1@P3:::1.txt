

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections to enable internal selective attention during classification. The authors argue that traditional CNNs are stationary and feedforward, whereas biological visual systems use recurrent processing and feedback for improved performance. dasNet allows a trained CNN to iteratively refine its classification through multiple passes over the same image, using feedback connections to dynamically modulate filter sensitivities. The attentional policy is learned using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), addressing the high-dimensional parameter space (over 1 million parameters) that would be intractable for conventional reinforcement learning.

The paper relates dasNet to previous work on hierarchical visual processing (Felleman & Essen, 1991), noting the importance of top-down connections in the primate visual cortex. It also connects to reinforcement learning approaches for visual attention (Schmidhuber, 2004; Bar et al., 2003), but argues that previous work has not been applied to state-of-the-art CNNs for performance improvements. The authors build upon Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013), which were the state-of-the-art for image classification at the time.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear methodology. The dasNet architecture extends Maxout networks with attention gates that modulate filter activations. The use of SNES for policy evolution is justified by the high dimensionality of the parameter space. The experimental evaluation on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 is well-designed, focusing on ambiguous classification cases where the network corrects misclassifications. The results show a 6% relative improvement over the vanilla CNN on CIFAR-10, establishing a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented data. However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis. While the authors mention "feedback is trained through direct policy search," they provide no theoretical guarantees or analysis of why this approach should outperform alternatives. The experimental results are promising but limited to two datasets without comparison to other attention mechanisms (e.g., spatial attention, channel attention) that have been explored in the literature.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The methodology section explains the dasNet architecture and SNES-based policy learning in sufficient detail. The experimental setup is clear, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figure 3 showing attention dynamics). However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational cost. The authors mention training took "around 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU," but don't compare this to the base CNN training time or other attention mechanisms, which is important for assessing practicality.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution in applying reinforcement learning to learn attention policies in deep CNNs. While prior work has explored feedback connections in neural networks (e.g., Fukushima, 1980; Restoring partly occluded patterns, 1989), dasNet is distinct in learning feedback connections through reinforcement learning rather than explicit design. However, the paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its work from other recent attention mechanisms. It doesn't cite or discuss key related work like CBAM (Woo et al., 2018) or SE blocks (Hu et al., 2018), which also address attention in CNNs but through different mechanisms. The paper also doesn't clarify how dasNet differs from the "Deep Attention Selective Network" by Wang et al. (2017), which shares a similar name and concept.

**Significance**: The results are significant in that dasNet establishes a new state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 for unaugmented data. The ability to correct misclassifications through iterative processing demonstrates the value of recurrent processing in deep learning. The approach could potentially be extended to other vision tasks. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear roadmap for scaling to larger datasets like ImageNet or integrating with modern techniques like batch normalization or residual connections. The authors also don't discuss how the approach would interact with contemporary CNN architectures.

**Overall Assessment**: dasNet presents a compelling approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs. While the theoretical foundations could be strengthened, the experimental results are promising and the approach is well-motivated. The paper would be significantly stronger with additional comparisons to related work, a more detailed theoretical analysis, and a thorough discussion of computational considerations.

**Strengths**:
- Novel approach to internal selective attention in CNNs
- Demonstrated improvement over state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100
- Clear experimental design focusing on ambiguous classification cases
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Weaknesses**:
- Lack of theoretical analysis
- Limited comparison with other attention mechanisms
- Insufficient discussion of computational cost and scalability
- No discussion of integration with modern deep learning techniques

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions). The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning by introducing a new mechanism for internal selective attention. While not a dramatic breakthrough, it offers a promising direction for improving CNN performance through recurrent processing. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
