- Introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet): a CNN with learned feedback connections (via SNES) that iteratively modulate filter sensitivities for classification.  
- Demonstrates improved results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, achieving new state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets.  
- Novelty: learning internal attentional mechanisms within a CNN using reinforcement learning is a relatively new and strong contribution.  
- Methodological novelty: use of SNES to train a very high-dimensional policy for CNN control is an original solution to conventional RL limitations.  
- Builds on prior work in CNNs, Maxout networks, and RL, and is inspired by neurophysiological evidence for feedback in vision.  
- Distinguishes itself from other attention/recurrent models by directly learning filter modulation weights via RL.  
- Strength — Performance: clear empirical gains on standard benchmarks.  
- Strength — Motivation: well-argued connection to human iterative/attentional visual processing.  
- Strength — Experiments: thorough experimental design with analysis of dynamics and helpful visualizations of filter/gate changes.  
- Strength — Presentation: generally clear, well-organized writing and reasonable explanation of SNES.  
- Weakness — Computational cost: training is expensive (4 days on GTX 560 Ti), raising scalability concerns.  
- Weakness — Hyperparameter sensitivity: limited analysis of sensitivity/ablation (e.g., T steps, SNES learning rates, regularization).  
- Weakness — Generalization: demonstrated only on CIFAR-10/100; applicability to larger or different datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is unclear.  
- Weakness — Interpretability: visualizations provided but the reasons behind filter modulation and policy decisions remain opaque.  
- Weakness — SNES justification: choice of SNES over alternative RL methods is not fully justified or compared.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, impactful approach with promising empirical results, strong motivation, and solid evaluation.  
- Rejection arguments: high computation, unknown generalization, limited interpretability, and insufficient justification of SNES choice.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions; request more on hyperparameter sensitivity, interpretability of learned policy, and discussion of scalability/generalization.
