Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a convolutional architecture augmented with learned feedback connections that modulate feature-map activations across multiple inference passes. Starting from a trained Maxout CNN with dropout, the authors add multiplicative gates per output map in each layer. At each time step, an “attention” policy maps summary statistics of the current network state (average feature-map activations per Maxout layer, pre-softmax activations, and class probabilities) to the next set of gates, and the same image is reprocessed for T iterations. The policy is trained by black-box optimization (Separable Natural Evolution Strategies, SNES) on a boosted negative log-likelihood objective that emphasizes misclassified examples. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet yields improved accuracy over the feedforward Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art among single models on unaugmented data at the time.

The work connects to neuroscientific evidence for recurrent and top-down modulation in visual cortex, and to prior ML research on selective attention and recurrent processing for vision (e.g., saccadic models, feedback networks, and iterative refinement). It differs from earlier attention and recurrence approaches by (a) modulating channel-wise filter sensitivities globally (per-feature-map gates) instead of spatial attention, (b) training a high-dimensional deterministic policy with SNES rather than gradient-based RL or supervised backprop through time, and (c) applying it on top of a then state-of-the-art Maxout CNN to demonstrate accuracy gains on CIFAR benchmarks.

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: The architecture is well-defined. Multiplicative gating with positivity constraints (softmax scaled to mean one) preserves stability across passes. The observation space is carefully constructed to summarize internal activations. The SNES optimization objective is explicit and includes L2 regularization and a boosting factor to weight misclassifications.
- However, the training objective’s description has a likely notational error: Eq. (10) defines λboost using “d = ||vT||∞” while d is elsewhere the correct class label. This should be clarified (presumably a conditional on whether argmax vT equals the ground-truth).
- The choice of SNES is justified by the policy’s million-scale parameters and high-dimensional action/observation spaces. Still, no comparisons are provided to gradient-based alternatives (e.g., backprop through unrolled gates, supervised learning of gating, or policy gradients), nor to smaller policies that reduce dimensionality (e.g., per-layer gates). The black-box choice may be substantially less sample-efficient than backprop for deterministic policies.
- Experiments: CIFAR-10/100 results indicate consistent improvements over the baseline Maxout network; the dynamics are stable when evaluated for more steps than trained, and the gate vectors carry predictive information. Missing are confidence intervals, per-class confusion improvements, and comparisons to strong baselines such as unrolled recurrent CNNs trained end-to-end. The computational cost is substantial (multi-day training) and test-time overhead is not quantified.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear with a helpful algorithm outline and architectural equations. The observation construction and action normalization are described concretely. Some notation is overloaded (e.g., d in Eq. 10) and could be corrected. Important implementation details (exact dimensions of observation and action vectors, layer sizes per dataset) could be summarized more explicitly. The stated claim “unaugmented datasets” is ambiguous given that training uses standard data augmentation; clarifying whether this refers to test-time augmentation or committee models would help.

Originality:
- The core idea—iteratively modulating internal CNN feature sensitivities via learned feedback—is novel relative to prior RL-based attention models (mostly spatial/saccadic) and to purely feedforward state-of-the-art CNNs of the time. The particular use of global per-feature-map gating trained with SNES on high-dimensional observations is a distinctive design. While multiplicative channel gating conceptually resembles known modulation mechanisms, combining it with multi-pass inference and evolutionary policy search on top of competitive CNNs appears original within the cited literature.

Significance:
- The approach yields measurable accuracy gains on CIFAR-10/100 and demonstrates a general strategy for iterative refinement that could be applied to other CNNs. The contribution is incremental empirically (improvements over a strong baseline) but conceptually meaningful in bringing top-down modulation into modern CNNs. Practical impact is moderated by computational overhead, the large policy size, and the lack of comparisons to more scalable end-to-end recurrent training, which might achieve similar or better gains with less complexity. The non-spatial nature of the attention limits applicability to tasks where spatial focus is important.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural modification that is easy to integrate into existing CNNs.
- Demonstrated, consistent improvements over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100.
- Stable iterative dynamics and auxiliary analysis indicating gate informativeness.
- Well-motivated connection to neuroscientific feedback and prior ML attention work.

Weaknesses:
- Missing ablations: no comparison to end-to-end backprop through unrolled recurrent gates, supervised gating, or smaller action spaces (per-layer gates).
- Ambiguity/error in the boosting/loss definition; limited reporting of statistical significance and runtime overhead.
- Policy is very high-dimensional and trained with black-box optimization; scalability and sample efficiency concerns.
- Attention is non-spatial and based on average activations, potentially discarding useful spatial information.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct and clarify the loss/boosting definition and all notation.
- Provide ablations comparing SNES to gradient-based training (e.g., BPTT on unrolled T steps) and to reduced action spaces (per-layer gating, low-rank or shared gates).
- Report compute cost and test-time overhead; include error bars and per-class confusion changes.
- Evaluate on additional datasets or with stronger baselines (e.g., committees) to establish broader significance.
- Explore spatially aware observations/actions and assess whether spatial attention or hybrid mechanisms further help.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated incorporation of feedback into modern CNNs.
- Solid empirical improvements on standard benchmarks.
- Clear, reproducible architectural idea with potential to inspire follow-up work on iterative inference.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited ablations and missing comparisons to end-to-end recurrent training weaken the case that SNES-driven attention is necessary or superior.
- Notational ambiguity and lack of statistical rigor.
- Computational overhead and scalability concerns due to a very high-dimensional policy.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and reasonably clear contribution with novel feedback mechanisms and positive empirical results, though the experimental evidence could be strengthened with ablations and clearer reporting.
