This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN architecture incorporating feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) to dynamically modulate filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is to mimic the iterative, attention-driven processing observed in human vision, allowing the network to refine its classifications over multiple passes. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 compared to previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets.

The paper’s approach builds upon existing work in CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998; Ciresan et al., 2012; Krizhevsky et al., 2012), Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 1998; Wierstra, 2006). It draws inspiration from neurophysiological studies highlighting the role of feedback connections in visual processing (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000; Gilbert & Sigman, 1999).  The use of SNES for training a high-dimensional policy is novel, given the challenges of applying conventional RL methods to complex CNN control tasks (Schaul et al., 2015).  Related work in attention mechanisms and recurrent processing (Denil et al., 2012; Stollenga et al., 2015) is acknowledged, but the dasNet approach distinguishes itself through the direct learning of filter modulation weights via RL.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning attentional mechanisms *within* a CNN via reinforcement learning, specifically to modulate filter activations, is a relatively novel approach. Combining RL with CNN feedback is a strong contribution.
* **Performance:** The reported performance improvements on CIFAR-10/100, establishing a new state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets, are significant.
* **Justification:** The motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing, is well-articulated. The authors convincingly argue for the potential benefits of iterative processing and dynamic filter selection.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The experiments appear well-designed and include an analysis of the network's dynamics and gate activations, offering insights into how the attentional mechanism functions.  The visualization of filter changes (Figure 3) is helpful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of SNES, while potentially complex for some readers, is reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  The training process (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is quite demanding, which may limit the scalability of the approach to larger datasets or more complex architectures. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw but should be acknowledged.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., number of steps T, learning rates for SNES, regularization parameters). A more comprehensive ablation study would strengthen the findings.
* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** The performance gains are demonstrated on CIFAR-10/100. It’s unclear how well the dasNet approach would generalize to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., ImageNet).
* **Interpretability:** While the authors provide some visualization of filter changes, the underlying reasons for those changes remain somewhat opaque. A deeper investigation into the learned attentional policy could provide more insights into the network’s behavior. Specifically, what kinds of features are being emphasized/de-emphasized and why?
* **SNES Justification:** While SNES is a valid choice, the rationale for *specifically* choosing SNES over other RL methods isn’t fully compelling. The authors mention the large parameter space, but a more detailed comparison with alternative approaches would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to improving CNN performance through learned attentional mechanisms.
* The experimental results are promising and demonstrate a clear performance gain on standard benchmarks.
* The connection to neurophysiological principles is well-motivated and provides a compelling rationale for the work.
* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost of training is high.
* The generalization ability to other datasets is unknown.
* The interpretability of the learned attentional policy is limited.
* The justification for choosing SNES specifically could be stronger.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The idea of learning internal attention mechanisms within a CNN using reinforcement learning is innovative, and the results are encouraging. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding computational cost and generalization, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should consider including a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and exploring the interpretability of the learned policy in more detail. It would also be beneficial to address the limitations related to scalability and potential generalization performance.
