

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which extends standard convolutional neural networks by incorporating feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its filter sensitivities during classification. The key innovation is a reinforcement learning approach (using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to learn a policy that dynamically modulates convolutional filter weights across multiple passes through the same image. The authors argue that this "internal selective attention" mechanism, inspired by biological visual systems with top-down feedback connections, allows the network to correct misclassifications by emphasizing or suppressing specific filters on subsequent passes.

The paper relates its work to previous NIPS contributions in several ways. It builds on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013) and acknowledges related work on attention mechanisms (e.g., Learning to Attend with Deep Architectures for Image Tracking, Denil et al., Neural Computation 2014) and recurrent processing (e.g., Recurrent Processing During Object Recognition, O'Reilly, Frontiers in Psychology 2013). However, it does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on attention mechanisms in deep learning, which often use more direct and computationally efficient approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Approach:** The use of SNES to handle the large parameter space (over 1 million dimensions) is a practical solution to a real problem in learning attention policies. The authors provide a detailed description of the dasNet architecture and training procedure, making it reproducible.

2. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (6% relative improvement), particularly on ambiguous classification cases. The ablation studies showing the network's ability to correct misclassifications (e.g., cat vs. dog examples) are compelling.

3. **Biological Inspiration:** The connection to biological visual processing (with top-down feedback connections) is well-articulated and provides a meaningful motivation for the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why this approach should work. While the authors claim "the network corrects itself by emphasising and de-emphasising certain filters," they don't provide a clear explanation of the mechanism or how this relates to the properties of the image data.

2. **Comparison to Existing Attention Mechanisms:** The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing attention mechanisms in deep learning. For example, it doesn't compare with methods like the Neural Attention Model (Bahdanau et al., NIPS 2014) or the Self-Attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., NIPS 2017), which are more efficient and have been shown to work well in various tasks.

3. **Computational Cost:** The training time (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) is not justified against the modest improvement. For a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10, this computational cost may not be justifiable in practice.

4. **Scalability:** The paper doesn't address whether this approach would scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks, which limits its practical significance.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its implementation and provides strong empirical results. However, it lacks theoretical depth and doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the approach. The authors are careful to note that dasNet is trained on top of an already-trained CNN, which avoids catastrophic forgetting, but they don't provide a thorough analysis of when this approach is beneficial versus when it's unnecessary.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear, and the figures help illustrate the key concepts. However, the connection between the biological inspiration and the technical implementation could be clearer.

**Originality:** While the specific use of SNES to learn attention policies is novel, the core idea of using feedback connections for selective attention is not entirely new. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish how this differs from or improves upon existing attention mechanisms. The authors cite relevant work but don't clearly articulate the novel contribution.

**Significance:** The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is meaningful but not revolutionary. The approach is limited to the specific context of correcting misclassifications on already-trained networks, and it's unclear whether this would generalize to other domains or tasks. The computational cost may limit its practical significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Demonstrates a novel approach to incorporating feedback connections in deep networks
- Provides strong empirical results on standard benchmarks
- Offers a biologically inspired solution to a real problem in deep learning
- The SNES approach is a practical solution to a challenging optimization problem

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The improvement is modest (6% relative) compared to computational cost
- Lacks theoretical justification for the approach
- Doesn't sufficiently differentiate from existing attention mechanisms
- Limited discussion of scalability and practical limitations

**Overall:** This is a technically sound paper with interesting results, but the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The modest performance gains don't fully justify the computational cost, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how this approach differs from or improves upon existing attention mechanisms in deep learning. While the biological inspiration is compelling, the paper would benefit from more rigorous theoretical analysis and a clearer articulation of the novelty compared to prior work.

I recommend rejection, as the paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel or significant to warrant publication at NIPS. The authors would benefit from either providing a stronger theoretical foundation, demonstrating more substantial improvements, or better differentiating their approach from existing attention mechanisms in the literature.
