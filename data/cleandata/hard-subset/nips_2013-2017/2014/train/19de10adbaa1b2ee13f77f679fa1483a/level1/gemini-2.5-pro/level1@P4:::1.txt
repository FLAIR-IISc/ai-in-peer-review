### Summary

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with a recurrent feedback mechanism. The core idea is to move beyond the static, single-pass evaluation of traditional CNNs and allow for an iterative refinement process, inspired by attentional mechanisms in the human visual system. The base model is a state-of-the-art Maxout network, which is first trained conventionally using supervised learning. Subsequently, a feedback policy is learned on top of this frozen network. This policy operates over multiple "passes" on a single input image. In each pass, it generates a set of modulatory weights that scale the output feature maps of each convolutional layer, effectively directing the network's "internal attention" to specific features.

Due to the high dimensionality of the policy's parameter space (over one million), the authors eschew standard reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms like policy gradients in favor of a black-box optimization method, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The policy is trained to minimize the classification loss, with an emphasis on correcting initially misclassified samples through a boosting-like reward structure. The authors evaluate dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating that the iterative attention mechanism improves classification accuracy over the baseline Maxout network and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on unaugmented CIFAR-10.

This work relates to prior research on recurrent and attentional models in computer vision, much of which was inspired by neuroscience. However, it distinguishes itself by applying these concepts to a modern, high-performing deep CNN and framing the learning problem as a large-scale policy search solved with evolution strategies.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness)**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of using a policy to modulate filter activities is clear (Equation 5), and the training procedure (Algorithm 1) is well-defined.
    *   The choice of SNES as the optimization algorithm is well-justified given the scale of the policy's parameter space. Standard RL methods would likely struggle with such a high-dimensional continuous action space and non-stationary dynamics.
    *   The experimental setup is solid. The authors use a strong baseline (Maxout network) and evaluate on standard, competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100).
    *   The paper includes valuable ablation-style analyses. The investigation into the stability of the network dynamics for different numbers of steps (Figure 2) and the analysis confirming the information content of the learned gate values are good additions that strengthen the claims.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The comparison of SNES to other RL methods is purely based on a scalability argument. It would strengthen the paper to include an empirical comparison to a policy gradient method, perhaps on a smaller version of the problem, to concretely demonstrate the superiority of the chosen approach.
    *   The computational cost is a significant concern. The training process appears very expensive (4 days on a GPU, on top of the initial CNN training), and inference requires `T=5` forward passes through the network. A discussion of the trade-off between the performance gain and this added complexity is missing.

**Clarity**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly laid out in the introduction, drawing a compelling analogy to human vision.
    *   Algorithm 1 provides a good overview of the training procedure. Figure 1 helps visualize the iterative process of observation and action.
    *   The related work section effectively situates the paper within both the deep learning and neuroscience literature.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Certain implementation details are sparse, which could hinder reproducibility. For instance, the exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters, kernel sizes) is not specified. The construction of the observation vector `o_t` is described in list form, but its total dimensionality and the exact composition are not explicitly stated. More details on the hyperparameters for SNES (e.g., learning rate, variance initialization) would also be beneficial.

**Originality**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The core contribution is novel. While recurrent connections and attention are not new concepts, the specific mechanism of an external policy learning to modulate internal filter activations of a pre-trained deep CNN is a new and interesting approach.
    *   The application of a large-scale evolution strategy to learn this internal attentional policy for a state-of-the-art classifier is also a novel combination of techniques. It differs significantly from contemporary attention models that often focused on spatial attention and were trained end-to-end with backpropagation.
    *   The concept of an "internal spotlight of attention" on features, rather than spatial regions, presents a fresh perspective.

**Significance**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement over a strong baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art result on unaugmented CIFAR-10 at the time of writing. This provides evidence that moving beyond purely feedforward architectures is a viable path to better performance.
    *   The work opens up a promising research direction. The idea of post-hoc improvement of a trained model through a learned, sequential decision-making process could inspire further research into hybrid learning systems and more dynamic neural network architectures. The qualitative results in Figure 3, showing the correction of a misclassification, provide an intuitive demonstration of the method's potential.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The performance gain (e.g., 9.39% to 8.81% error on CIFAR-10) is solid but not dramatic, especially when weighed against the significant increase in computational complexity for both training and inference. This may limit its practical applicability in resource-constrained settings.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel architecture and training paradigm combining deep CNNs, reinforcement learning, and evolution strategies to implement an internal attention mechanism.
2.  **Performance:** Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (unaugmented CIFAR-10), demonstrating the efficacy of the approach.
3.  **Impact:** The core idea of iterative refinement and modulating internal network features is thought-provoking and could stimulate future research in dynamic and adaptive neural networks.
4.  **Soundness:** The methodology is well-reasoned, and the experimental evaluation is thorough, including analyses that support the paper's central claims.

### Arguments against Acceptance
1.  **Computational Cost:** The method is computationally very intensive, which may limit its practical utility and adoption. The paper does not sufficiently address this trade-off.
2.  **Reproducibility:** Key details about the network architecture and hyperparameters are missing from the main text, making it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results.
3.  **Limited Comparison:** The choice of SNES is justified only by argument; an empirical comparison to alternative policy-learning algorithms is absent.
