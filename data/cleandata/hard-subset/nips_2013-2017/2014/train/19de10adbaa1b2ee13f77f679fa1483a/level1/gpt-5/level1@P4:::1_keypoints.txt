- Paper introduces dasNet: a Maxout CNN augmented with multiplicative per-feature-map gates and an attention policy that iteratively reprocesses images for T steps, trained with SNES on a boosted negative log-likelihood.  
- Reports improved accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 and claims state-of-the-art among single models on unaugmented data at the time.  
- Connects to neuroscience (recurrent/top-down modulation) and prior ML attention/recurrence work; differs by global channel-wise gating, SNES training, and application on a strong Maxout baseline.  
- Architecture and gating design are well-defined and stable (softmax scaled to mean one); observation space and SNES objective are explicitly described.  
- Notational error/ambiguity in the boosting/loss definition (Eq. 10 uses d inconsistently); requires clarification.  
- Choice of SNES is justified by policy dimensionality but lacks comparisons to gradient-based alternatives (BPTT, policy gradients) or reduced-dimension policies; black-box approach may be sample-inefficient.  
- Experiments show consistent gains, stable dynamics beyond training steps, and informative gate vectors.  
- Missing experimental details: no confidence intervals, no per-class/confusion analyses, no comparisons to end-to-end unrolled recurrent CNNs, and test-time/runtime overhead is unreported.  
- Clarity is generally good with helpful algorithmic description, but some notation is overloaded and implementation specifics (observation/action dimensions, layer sizes) are under-specified; “unaugmented” claim is ambiguous.  
- Originality: novel combination of iterative per-feature-map modulation and evolutionary policy search on top of a competitive CNN; conceptually distinct from spatial/saccadic attention models.  
- Significance: conceptually meaningful for introducing top-down modulation into CNNs with measurable accuracy gains, but empirical contribution is incremental and practical impact limited by compute and non-spatial attention.  
- Strengths: easy-to-integrate architectural change, consistent empirical improvements, stable iterative behavior, and solid motivation linking neuroscience and ML.  
- Weaknesses: lacks key ablations (vs. BPTT/supervised gating/per-layer gates), contains notation errors, uses a very high-dimensional black-box policy raising scalability/sample-efficiency concerns, and ignores spatial information.  
- Suggestions: fix notation and loss definition; add ablations comparing SNES to gradient-based training and reduced action spaces; report compute/test-time costs and error bars; evaluate on more datasets and explore spatial or hybrid attention.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated feedback mechanism with solid empirical gains and a reproducible idea likely to inspire follow-ups.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient ablations/comparisons to show necessity or superiority of SNES, notational/rigor issues, and concerns about computational overhead and scalability.  
- Overall: technically sound and clear contribution with positive results, but experimental evidence and reporting need strengthening for stronger acceptance.
