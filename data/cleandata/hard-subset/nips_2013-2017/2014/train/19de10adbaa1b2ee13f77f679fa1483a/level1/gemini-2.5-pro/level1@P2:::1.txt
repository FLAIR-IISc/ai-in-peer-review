### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, rapid feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional processing steps on the same input image. In these subsequent steps, a learned policy dynamically modulates the activations of the convolutional filters, effectively allowing the network to "focus" on or suppress certain features to improve its classification, particularly for difficult or ambiguous cases.

The authors build upon a state-of-the-art Maxout network. After this base network is trained via standard supervised learning, a control policy is trained using Reinforcement Learning (RL) to manage the attentional modulation. The policy's state is derived from the network's internal activations (e.g., average feature map activations, final layer activations), and its action is a vector of weights that are applied to the output maps of each convolutional layer. Due to the very high dimensionality of the policy parameter space (over one million parameters), the authors eschew traditional RL algorithms in favor of Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a scalable black-box optimization method. The experiments, conducted on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, demonstrate that dasNet can correct initial misclassifications and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 dataset.

### **Evaluation**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and significant idea with strong experimental backing. The work is well-motivated by concepts from neuroscience, particularly the idea of a fast feedforward visual pass followed by slower, recurrent, attentional processing. It successfully translates this concept into a concrete and effective computational model.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed methodology—a two-stage training process where a base CNN is trained first, followed by an RL-based policy to control its internal dynamics—is well-conceived and clearly described in Algorithm 1. The choice of SNES is well-justified as a practical solution to the challenge of optimizing a policy with an extremely large, million-dimensional parameter space, where traditional policy gradient methods might struggle. The claims are well-supported by the experimental results. The performance improvement over the strong Maxout baseline is significant, and achieving a state-of-the-art result on CIFAR-10 lends strong credence to the approach. The authors are also careful in their analysis, providing an investigation into the system's dynamics over extended time steps and an information-theoretic analysis of the learned attention gates, which confirms that the policy is learning meaningful, class-relevant information. This is a complete piece of work, not just a preliminary idea.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, and the necessary background on Maxout networks and Reinforcement Learning is provided. The description of the dasNet architecture and training algorithm is easy to follow. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the iterative process. The experimental section is clear, and the results are presented cogently. The example in Figure 3, which visualizes the changes in filter activations for a specific image, is particularly insightful for helping the reader build intuition about what the network is doing. The paper provides enough detail (hyperparameters, architecture description) that an expert reader should be able to attempt a reproduction of the results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While ideas of recurrence, feedback, and attention have been explored in neural networks before (as the authors discuss in the Related Work section), their application in this specific manner is novel. The key contribution is the concept of learning a high-dimensional policy to dynamically and sequentially modulate the internal feature extractors of a modern, deep CNN *post-training* to refine its predictions. This combination of a state-of-the-art CNN architecture with a policy trained via evolutionary strategies to control its internal computations for classification is a significant and novel synthesis of ideas from deep learning, RL, and computational intelligence. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work that often used simpler models or demonstrated concepts on less challenging tasks.

**Significance:** The results and ideas presented in this paper are highly significant.
1.  **Performance:** The paper demonstrates a clear, practical benefit, advancing the state of the art on a competitive benchmark (CIFAR-10 without data augmentation). This shows the approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but a powerful method for improving performance.
2.  **Conceptual Advance:** More importantly, it opens up a new and exciting research direction. It challenges the purely feedforward paradigm dominant in deep learning at the time and shows the potential of incorporating dynamic, instance-specific, recurrent computations. This work can be seen as a precursor to the more sophisticated attention mechanisms that have become central to deep learning.
3.  **Future Impact:** Other researchers are very likely to build on these ideas. The framework of using RL to learn control policies for network internals could be extended in many ways, for example, by learning more complex spatial attention or by adapting the network for tasks beyond classification.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for Acceptance (PRO):**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a highly original and compelling architecture that integrates feedback and learned attention into a state-of-the-art CNN.
*   **Performance:** Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed method.
*   **Strong Motivation:** The approach is well-motivated by established principles from neuroscience, providing a solid conceptual foundation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the proposed method is described clearly.
*   **Significance:** The work opens up promising new avenues for research into dynamic and recurrent computation in deep networks.

**Arguments for Rejection (CON):**
*   **Computational Cost:** The training procedure is very computationally intensive (4 days for the policy training alone), which may limit its widespread adoption and further exploration by researchers with fewer resources.
*   **Black-Box Training:** The use of SNES, while pragmatic, is a black-box optimization method. This provides fewer insights into the learning dynamics and the structure of the loss landscape compared to gradient-based methods.
*   **Limited Analysis of Policy:** While the authors provide some analysis, a deeper investigation into *what* general strategies the policy learns would be beneficial. The current analysis relies on a single, albeit illustrative, example.

### **Conclusion**

Despite the minor weaknesses concerning computational cost and the black-box nature of the training, this is an outstanding paper. The novelty of the idea, the strength of the results, and the potential impact on the field far outweigh the concerns. The paper presents a significant step forward in designing more dynamic and powerful neural network architectures. It is a clear and enthusiastic recommendation for acceptance.
