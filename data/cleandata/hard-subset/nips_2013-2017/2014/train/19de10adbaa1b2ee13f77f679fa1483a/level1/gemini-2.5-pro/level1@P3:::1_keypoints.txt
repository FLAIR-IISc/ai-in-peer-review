- Paper introduces dasNet: feedback-enabled CNN with modulatory gates to perform multiple processing passes per image, inspired by two-stage human vision.
- Method: train a Maxout CNN, add per-map gates, and learn a linear policy via Separable NES using CNN internal states to set gates across T passes.
- Empirical result: achieves new state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10 with ~6% relative improvement over Maxout and corrects hard misclassifications.
- Originality/significance: novel, biologically motivated approach adding recurrent, input-dependent attentional processing to CNNs.
- Strong empirical validation supports the core hypothesis that sequential refinement improves feedforward models.
- Methodological soundness: pragmatic two-phase training, clever use of SNES for very high-dimensional policy, and a boosting-style reward to focus on errors.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and aided by algorithm/figure illustrations.
- Computational cost/scalability: RL training is very expensive (4 GPU days) and inference is T× slower; trade-offs and accuracy vs T need clearer analysis.
- Ablation/design choices missing: need studies on observation vector components, choice of linear policy vs nonlinear alternatives, and disentangling boosting effects from the attention mechanism.
- Reproducibility: main text omits key Maxout architecture details needed to reproduce results.
- Qualitative analysis: filter visualizations are speculative; the gate-class relevance experiment is stronger evidence.
- Arguments for acceptance: highly novel idea, SOTA empirical result, neuroscience motivation, and useful methodological contribution (SNES).
- Arguments for rejection: high computational burden, insufficient ablation studies, and some reproducibility gaps.
- Conclusion: significant and forward-thinking work with strong results; weaknesses are addressable, recommend acceptance.
