- Proposes dasNet: a recurrent Maxout CNN with input-dependent per-feature-map multiplicative gates applied over T iterative passes.
- After each pass an observation vector (layer averages, classifier activations, class probs) is fed to a large policy that scales feature maps for the next pass.
- The policy (~10^6 params) is trained post hoc with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES); reported gains on CIFAR-10/100 and claimed SOTA for unaugmented evaluation.
- Connects design to neuroscience ideas of feedback and selective attention and provides qualitative analyses of gating dynamics.

- Builds on Maxout/dropout and prior feedback/attention and saccade/glimpse models, but acts in feature space via per-channel gating rather than image-space glimpses.
- Uses scalable black-box NES for high-dimensional policy search, distinct from contemporaneous CNN architectural advances.

- Mechanism is well-defined and implementable; training objective (final-step cross-entropy with boosted weight on errors) is reasonable but somewhat ad hoc.
- Stability is briefly addressed (performance peaks near trained T), but broader stability analysis is limited.

- Reports ~6% relative improvement over a Maxout baseline and claims SOTA, but exact baseline/dasNet error rates and definition of “unaugmented” are missing.
- Key empirical comparisons to strong contemporaneous baselines (NIN, stochastic pooling, dropconnect, committees) are lacking; runtime and 5x inference cost not reported.

- Using SNES to optimize a differentiable, million-parameter policy is unusual; no comparisons to gradient-based alternatives (BPTT, policy gradients) are provided.
- Observation compresses spatial info to map averages, restricting policy to channel-wise attention and limiting expressivity; this trade-off is not fully justified.

- Presentation is clear on mechanism and algorithm, but important details are omitted: Table 1 numbers, variances, SNES hyperparameters, and precise observation/action dimensionalities.
- The term “unaugmented datasets” is ambiguous given the base CNN uses augmentation; train/test augmentation and single-model vs ensemble settings need clarification.

- The recurrent, input-conditional reweighting idea is novel in the venue and distinct from glimpse models; large-scale SNES use is unusual though not necessarily optimal.

- If validated, the method is significant: a simple, general recurrent internal attention mechanism that can boost strong feedforward CNNs.
- Practical significance depends on compute overhead, robustness, and comparisons to end-to-end differentiable feedback, which are not fully explored.

- Strengths: conceptually elegant per-feature-map recurrent gating, demonstrated gains on Maxout baseline, scalable policy search with SNES, and initial dynamics analyses.
- Weaknesses: underspecified quantitative comparisons, missing ablations (which layers gated, observation components, policy nonlinearity, T effects), no backprop-based policy comparisons, limited overfitting/validation analysis, and unreported runtime/memory overhead.

- Requests for authors: report exact test errors for baseline and dasNet (with/without test-time augmentation), justify SNES vs BPTT/policy gradient, specify observation/action dimensionality and policy architecture, report sensitivity to T and hyperparameters, clarify SNES validation/overfitting and inference speed overhead.

- Suggested improvements: add comprehensive ablations and gradient-based training experiments, clarify augmentation/ensemble settings, include runtime/memory benchmarks, and explore spatial attention or selective layer gating.

- Arguments for acceptance: simple, biologically motivated mechanism with empirical gains and novel application of feedback in deep CNNs that may have broad impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete empirical evaluation and comparison, methodological choice of SNES not justified, and practical costs/robustness insufficiently analyzed.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — interesting and potentially impactful idea but needs clearer, stronger empirical comparisons and ablations to fully support claims.
