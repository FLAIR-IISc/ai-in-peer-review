- Introduces dasNet: a recurrent feedback mechanism that augments a pre-trained Maxout CNN to perform iterative, selective attention for improved classification on ambiguous images.
- Policy modulates convolutional filter map activations across multiple passes; actions are modulatory weights, state derives from network activations.
- Uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to train a very high-dimensional policy (over 1M parameters) as a black-box optimizer.
- Neuroscience motivation: models pre-attentive (feedforward) and attentive (recurrent) phases of human vision.
- Strong empirical results: sets new state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 with a 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10.
- Original and significant contribution: introduces dynamic, learned internal attention to CNNs ahead of mainstream attention mechanisms.
- Novel methodological combination: effective application of RL/evolution strategies with deep CNNs to learn top-down attention.
- Clear exposition and motivation, supported by Algorithm 1 and neuroscience grounding.
- Insufficient justification for choosing SNES over policy-gradient RL methods; lacks empirical comparison or detailed rationale.
- Observation/state vector design (concatenated activations and probabilities) appears ad-hoc and needs ablation to show which components matter.
- High computational cost and two-stage training (supervised pre-training then RL) limit scalability and practicality for larger datasets/models.
- Interpretability is limited: visualizations are inconclusive and the mechanism of correction by the policy remains opaque.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, strong empirical gains, innovative use of evolution strategies, and promising new direction for dynamic vision models.
- Arguments against acceptance: expensive, not end-to-end; some design choices (state representation, RL algorithm) need stronger justification; analysis of learned attention is preliminary.
- Overall conclusion: high-quality, original work with strong results that outweigh weaknesses; recommended for acceptance.
