Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a modification of a trained feedforward Maxout CNN that introduces internal, learned feedback to modulate the sensitivities of convolutional feature maps across multiple passes over the same image. At test time, an image is processed for T iterations. After each pass, a policy maps summary statistics (per-map average activations across layers, pre-softmax activations, and current class probabilities) to a set of positive multiplicative gates, one per feature map per layer. These gates reweight feature maps on the next pass, enabling the network to emphasize or suppress features to correct initial mistakes. The policy is learned after standard supervised training of the CNN, via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), optimizing a boosted negative log-likelihood on the final pass, with higher weight on misclassified examples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline and claim state-of-the-art on unaugmented data.

Relation to prior work
The work is motivated by evidence for recurrent and top-down modulatory connections in visual cortex and prior computational models using recurrence and attention. It relates to Maxout CNNs with dropout, recurrent/feedback models that improve robustness and figure-ground segregation, and RL-based selective attention (e.g., saccadic/glimpse models and tracking). Relative to previous attention mechanisms that select spatial glimpses, dasNet performs internal, channel-wise attention over feature maps and claims scalability via SNES for million-parameter policies. It connects to recurrency used for iterative refinement in vision tasks, but applies it to improve SOTA CNNs on standard benchmarks.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea: multiplicative, per-map gating controlled by a learned policy that uses internal activations; easily composable with existing CNNs.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline; qualitative example demonstrates correction of an initial mistake.
- Pragmatic training: decouples supervised feature learning from policy learning; uses a simple, stable observation (average map activations) and positive gates to avoid destabilizing activations.
- Scalability: uses SNES to handle a very high-dimensional policy parameter space without BPTT, with stable dynamics even when evaluated beyond the training horizon.
- Biological motivation is well-grounded, with appropriate citations on feedback and top-down modulation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Learning approach: The entire feedback pathway is differentiable through time. A gradient-based alternative (BPTT) to train the gating policy (or the whole recurrent network) is not attempted or compared against. The argument for SNES (policy dimension) is plausible but not fully justified; gradient methods might be more sample-efficient.
- Baselines and ablations: Lacking comparisons to (i) single-pass, image-conditioned gating (i.e., T=1 equivalent or static per-map scalars learned by supervised learning), (ii) gating only the top conv layer, (iii) a learned linear reweighting of class logits, or (iv) a shallow recurrent layer trained with SGD. Ablations would clarify where gains come from and how much recurrence is needed.
- Objective and “RL” framing: The policy is optimized on a supervised loss (boosted cross-entropy on the final pass) with no temporal credit assignment or discounting; this is black-box supervised optimization rather than sequential RL. This should be acknowledged more directly.
- Computational cost: Inference is T times more expensive (T=5), but no timing/throughput numbers or energy/latency discussion are provided. This is important for practical significance.
- Reporting and clarity of results: The text reports a “6% relative improvement” and “state-of-the-art on unaugmented data” but does not present absolute error rates and confidence intervals in the provided excerpt, nor comparisons to contemporary single-model CNNs trained without committees/test-time augmentation. Please report absolute CIFAR-10/100 errors, training vs test-time augmentation settings, and baselines under the same protocol.
- Observation design: Using only per-map averages discards spatial information; while this simplifies the policy, it may limit achievable gains. A brief comparison to richer observations (e.g., low-resolution spatial maps or top-layer maps only) would be informative.
- Action constraints: Gates are positive and normalized to have average 1 (scaled softmax), which enforces competition and precludes strong suppression (near-zero) or negative modulation. An analysis of this design choice (e.g., allowing unnormalized positive gates or learned per-layer temperature) would strengthen the paper.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: Method is technically sound and well-motivated; optimization details (SNES sampling, fitness shaping, regularization) are reasonable. However, the choice of ES over gradient-based training is not empirically justified by comparisons, and experiments are limited to CIFAR without broader stress tests (occlusion, noise).
- Clarity: The main idea and algorithm are clearly described. Some implementation details are deferred to supplement (e.g., SNES update specifics), and key hyperparameters (batch size n, observation/action dimensions) are not fully specified here. Clarify the “unaugmented” claim given that data augmentation was used to train the base CNN.
- Originality: The specific combination—internal channel-wise attention learned via policy search to modulate conv feature maps over multiple passes—is novel and distinct from spatial attention/glimpse models and generic recurrent CNNs.
- Significance: Gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong baseline indicate potential. The idea of internal attention via feedback is broadly relevant. Practical impact is moderated by added inference cost and the absence of comparisons to gradient-based recurrent training.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, biologically inspired mechanism that is simple to add to existing CNNs.
- Empirical improvement over a strong Maxout baseline; claimed state-of-the-art single-model performance.
- Addresses an important theme (attention and feedback) timely for NIPS.
- Demonstrates scalable black-box policy search in a million-parameter setting.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear how much benefit comes from recurrence versus a single learned reweighting.
- No comparison to gradient-based training of the same recurrent architecture.
- Limited analysis of computational trade-offs and sensitivity to T.
- Ambiguity around the “unaugmented” SOTA claim; absolute numbers and standardized protocols should be reported.

Minor comments
- Please report absolute error rates with/without dasNet for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and specify if test-time augmentation/ensembles are used.
- Provide inference-time cost multipliers and wall-clock times for T=1..5.
- Consider visualizing gate trajectories and their class-conditional distributions.
- Fix minor typos and unify notation (e.g., indices and layer superscripts).

Overall, this is a compelling and timely idea with encouraging results. With stronger experimental comparisons and clearer reporting, it would make a solid contribution. I lean toward weak accept.
