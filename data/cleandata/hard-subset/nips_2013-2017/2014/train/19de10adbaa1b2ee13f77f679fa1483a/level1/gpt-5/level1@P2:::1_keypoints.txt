- Proposes dasNet: a CNN with learned internal feedback that multiplicatively modulates feature-map activations across multiple passes.  
- Policy maps summary statistics (mean feature-map activations, pre-activations, softmax) to multiplicative gates applied in subsequent passes.  
- Uses Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a million-dimensional policy in a gradient-free way.  
- Builds on deep CNNs, biological top-down modulation evidence, and RL/ES attention models; differs by modulating feature sensitivities rather than spatial glimpses.  

Strengths:
- Novel integration of global, per-feature-map multiplicative attention implemented as learned feedback.  
- Biologically motivated design aligning with modulatory top-down influences, not adding feedforward capacity.  
- Empirical gains over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100 without test-time augmentation, especially on ambiguous examples.  
- Clear algorithmic formulation (observations, actions, iterative passes) and a principled loss for policy search.  
- Initial analyses show performance peaks near trained step T and gate vectors encode class-relevant info.  

Weaknesses:
- Missing important baselines: repeated feedforward passes without gating, averaging softmax, and supervised/backprop-trained gating networks.  
- No comparison to other recurrent/feedback CNNs or glimpse-based attention models on CIFAR benchmarks.  
- Limited ablations: layer-wise benefits, whether top-layer gating suffices, sensitivity to observation choices, policy capacity, and varying T.  
- Confusing loss notation (ambiguous use of d) and unclear condition for boosting term; needs correction.  
- High computational cost: inference is T× slower and training adds days; no compute vs. accuracy analysis or memory footprint reported.  
- SNES in million-dimensional space raises sample inefficiency and overfitting concerns; limited regularization/overfitting analysis.  
- Reproducibility issues: missing architectural/training specifics (action/observation sizes, per-layer maps, kernel sizes, SNES hyperparameters, stopping criteria).  
- Claim of “state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets” lacks context, absolute numbers, confidence intervals, and apples-to-apples comparisons.  

Quality:
- Method is technically sound and stable, but evaluation lacks baselines and statistical significance testing to isolate sources of improvement.  

Clarity:
- Overall readable with helpful figures and pseudocode, but some notation (loss/boosting) is confusing and implementation specifics are omitted.  

Originality:
- Fresh angle: learned global internal attention modulating feature maps across passes; novel use of SNES at this scale in this context.  

Significance:
- Impactful concept that can correct initial mistakes and complement strong CNNs, but current empirical support is modest and limited to CIFAR.  

Suggestions:
- Add baselines: multi-pass no-gating, supervised/backprop gating, top-layer-only gating, random/shuffled gates, and smaller-capacity policies.  
- Fix and clarify loss/boosting notation; provide full observation/action dimensions and all hyperparameters; report variance across runs.  
- Report compute metrics (FLOPs/time), memory footprint, and accuracy vs. T trade-offs.  
- Expand analysis: per-class confusion improvements, robustness to noise/occlusion, sensitivity to observation statistics.  
- Explore lower-rank or structured policies to reduce parameter count and compare SNES to policy-gradient/backprop approaches where feasible.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually compelling, biologically inspired mechanism with tangible improvements on standard benchmarks without ensembling/augmentation.  
- Opens a practical path to learned internal attention and iterative refinement in deep nets.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient comparative baselines and ablations to attribute gains and justify SNES and full architecture choices.  
- Missing implementation details and unclear loss hurt reproducibility.  
- Computational overhead vs. benefit not quantified; evaluation limited to CIFAR only.  

Overall:
- Original and well-motivated method with promising results, but empirical thoroughness, clarity, and reproducibility need substantial improvement.
