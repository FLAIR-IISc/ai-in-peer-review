This paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension of Maxout CNNs that incorporates learned top-down feedback to modulate feature-map activations during inference. After standard supervised training of a feedforward Maxout model, the authors train a high-dimensional deterministic policy that maps internal summaries of network activity (averaged feature-map activations, classification pre-activations, and softmax outputs) to multiplicative gates over all feature maps. The policy is optimized with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle ~10^6 parameters. On CIFAR-10/100, the method iteratively refines predictions over T passes and reports improved accuracy over a vanilla CNN and state-of-the-art on unaugmented data. The work is positioned against feedforward CNNs (e.g., AlexNet, NIPS ‘12) and Maxout (ICML ‘13), connects to biological evidence for recurrent/top-down modulation (Lamme & Roelfsema; Bar), and relates to RL-driven attention and recurrent processing (e.g., Denil et al.; Larochelle & Hinton).

Quality: The approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The training algorithm and observation/action design are explicit, and the boosting-style loss emphasizes difficult cases. Experiments show consistent gains, stability beyond the trained horizon, and an informative analysis of gates. However, comparisons are limited: there is no ablation of (i) which layers need gating, (ii) gate dimensionality, or (iii) SNES vs simpler optimizers or end-to-end backprop for the policy. Compute/latency overhead (T passes) and sensitivity to T, population size, and λ’s are not quantified. Statistical significance and variance across runs are missing.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithmic description. Some details are underspecified (exact architecture sizes, SNES hyperparameters, validation protocol for selecting T, wall-clock/throughput). The observation vector dimensionality and memory footprint could be clarified.

Originality: The combination of internal feature-wise attention learned via black-box policy search and applied at scale to CNNs is novel relative to prior spatial attention/saccade models and earlier recurrent formulations.

Significance: Demonstrates that feedback can improve recognition on standard benchmarks and provides a practical recipe for high-dimensional control of deep nets. Impact is moderate without stronger baselines (e.g., data augmentation, ensembles) and broader datasets.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and biologically inspired mechanism for internal attention in CNNs
- Clear performance gains on CIFAR-10/100 and insightful dynamics analyses
- Scalable treatment of million-parameter policies via SNES

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablations and baselines; unclear necessity of SNES vs gradients
- Overhead and deployment implications not quantified
- SOTA claim restricted to unaugmented settings; lack of significance tests and run-to-run variance
