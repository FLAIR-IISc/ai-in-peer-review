This paper introduces dasNet, a deep convolutional neural network augmented with feedback connections learned via reinforcement learning (specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies - SNES). The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively refine its classification by modulating filter activations based on past performance, mirroring, at a high level, aspects of human visual processing. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 compared to a baseline Maxout network, particularly on challenging instances.

The paper builds on a rich history of work in both deep learning and biologically inspired computation. The motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing and the limitations of purely feedforward CNNs, is well articulated. The authors correctly position their work within the context of recurrent connections in the brain (Felleman & Essen, Gilbert & Sigman) and previous attempts to incorporate feedback into CNNs (e.g., iterative reconstruction, face localization). The choice of SNES to handle the large parameter space for the attention policy is sensible and justified. The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates awareness of relevant literature, including both classical and more recent approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of reinforcement learning (specifically SNES) to learn feedback connections within a deep convolutional network is a novel approach.  Existing work often focuses on attention mechanisms *within* a layer or integrating external attention signals. Learning to modulate filter sensitivities *across* layers via a learned policy is a unique contribution.
* **Performance:**  Outperforming the state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR datasets is a significant result, demonstrating the practical benefit of the proposed approach.
* **Justification:** The biological motivation, while not strictly required for NIPS, provides a compelling rationale for the work and contextualizes it within broader cognitive science principles.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears technically sound, with clear explanations of the Maxout network, the RL framework, and the SNES algorithm. The experiments are well-defined, and the results are presented clearly.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The visualization of filter activation changes (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how dasNet is adapting its internal representations. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) offers further understanding of the iterative process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability:** While SNES allows training a large policy, its computational cost is substantial (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti).  The paper doesn’t sufficiently address the scalability of this approach to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or more complex architectures.  A discussion of potential optimizations or alternative RL algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Interpretability:**  While the visualization of filter activation changes is helpful, fully understanding *why* the network is emphasizing or de-emphasizing specific filters remains challenging. Deeper interpretability analysis is needed. The statement that changes in the highest layer are “most influential because they are closest to the final output layers” feels somewhat simplistic; the influence of earlier layers shouldn't be discounted.
* **Action Space:**  The policy outputs a vector of weights for all filters in all layers. This seems potentially redundant. Could a more structured action space (e.g., modulating filter groups or applying layer-specific transformations) reduce the dimensionality of the policy and improve training efficiency?
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks extensive ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific components of dasNet. For example, what happens if the initial Maxout network is trained with different levels of data augmentation?  How does the number of iterative passes (T) affect performance?
* **SNES Parameter Sensitivity:** The authors don’t detail how the SNES hyperparameters (population size, learning rate, etc.) were chosen and whether the results are sensitive to these choices.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to incorporating feedback into deep CNNs.
* It achieves state-of-the-art performance on challenging datasets.
* The detailed analysis and visualizations provide valuable insights into the behavior of the network.
* The work has the potential to advance our understanding of attention mechanisms and biologically inspired computation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and scalability of the approach are significant concerns.
* The lack of comprehensive ablation studies limits our understanding of the contribution of specific components.
* The interpretability of the learned policy remains limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field.  The novelty of the approach, combined with the demonstrated performance improvements, outweighs the identified weaknesses.  However, addressing the scalability and interpretability concerns would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the approach, and explore potential optimizations.
2.  Include more comprehensive ablation studies to assess the impact of key design choices (e.g., T, data augmentation, action space structure).
3.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the learned policy, attempting to explain *why* specific filters are being modulated.
4. Briefly mention the sensitivity of the SNES parameters.
