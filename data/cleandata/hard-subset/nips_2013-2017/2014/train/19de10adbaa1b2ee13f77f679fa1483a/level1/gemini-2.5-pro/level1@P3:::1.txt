This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into a standard feedforward Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The core idea is to enable the network to perform multiple processing passes over a single image, dynamically modulating the activities of its convolutional filters at each pass. This is motivated by the two-stage process of human vision: a fast, initial feedforward sweep followed by a slower, attentional phase involving recurrent processing.

The methodology consists of two stages. First, a state-of-the-art Maxout network is trained conventionally using supervised learning. This network is then augmented with modulatory "gates" for each convolutional output map. A policy, represented by a linear mapping, is then trained using reinforcement learning to control these gates. The policy takes the internal state of the CNN (average map activations, intermediate layer outputs, and class probabilities) as input and outputs a vector of gate values for the next pass. Due to the high dimensionality of the policy parameter space (over 1 million), the authors cleverly employ Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization method, for training. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this iterative refinement process corrects initial misclassifications on difficult examples, leading to a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 test set.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and the importance of the problem it addresses. At a time when deep learning research was heavily focused on deeper and more efficient feedforward architectures, this work introduces a compelling, biologically-inspired mechanism for recurrent, attentional processing within a CNN. It moves beyond static function approximation and explores dynamic, input-dependent computation. The idea of an "internal spotlight of attention" that can refine a network's "thoughts" is a significant conceptual contribution that opens up a promising new research avenue.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is not just conceptually interesting; it is backed by strong empirical evidence. Achieving a state-of-the-art result on a competitive benchmark like CIFAR-10 (for unaugmented data) demonstrates the practical value of the proposed approach. The 6% relative improvement over a very strong baseline (Maxout networks) is non-trivial and validates the core hypothesis that sequential processing can improve upon purely feedforward models.

3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The two-phase training scheme is pragmatic and effective. The use of SNES to train the high-dimensional policy is a key enabling factor and a clever circumvention of the difficulties that standard policy gradient methods would face in such a large parameter space. The design of the reward function, which incorporates a boosting-like term to focus on misclassified samples, is also a sensible choice for the problem of correcting errors.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 makes the overall process easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The RL training phase is extremely computationally expensive (4 days on a GPU). Furthermore, inference is T times slower than a standard CNN. This is a major practical limitation. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this trade-off. How does the performance gain vary with the number of passes, T? The dynamics experiment in Figure 2 is a good start, but a plot of final accuracy vs. T (and vs. inference time) would provide a clearer picture of the costs and benefits.

2.  **Ablation Studies and Design Choices:** Several key design choices are not fully justified.
    *   The observation vector `o_t` concatenates information from all layers. Is all of this information necessary? An ablation study analyzing the contribution of each component (e.g., activations from different layers, class probabilities) would provide valuable insight into what information the policy relies on.
    *   The policy `π_θ` is a simple linear transformation. Was a more complex, non-linear policy (e.g., a small MLP) considered? A brief discussion of this choice would be helpful.
    *   The "boosting" mechanism (`λ_misclassified` >> `λ_correct`) seems crucial. It would be valuable to disentangle its effect from the attention mechanism itself. A strong baseline would be to simply fine-tune the original Maxout network with the same weighted loss function, without the dasNet architecture, to see how much of the gain is attributable to the boosting scheme alone.

3.  **Reproducibility:** While the overall method is clear, some details required for full reproduction are missing from the main paper. Specifically, the architectural details of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters per layer, kernel sizes, strides) are not provided. While these may be in supplementary material, including them in the main text would improve its self-containedness.

4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The authors rightly note that it is difficult to analyze the filter visualizations in Figure 3. The interpretation provided is speculative. While this is a notoriously hard problem, the claims could be softened. The experiment demonstrating that the final gate values contain class-relevant information is a much stronger piece of analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and significant idea of adding internal, learned selective attention to a deep CNN.
*   Presents a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark.
*   The approach is well-motivated by neuroscience and tackles the important problem of improving performance on ambiguous inputs.
*   The clever use of SNES for policy search is a valuable methodological contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The high computational cost may limit the practical impact and follow-up work.
*   The paper lacks ablation studies to justify certain design choices and isolate the sources of performance gain.
*   Reproducibility is hampered by a lack of architectural details in the main text.

**Conclusion:**

This is a very interesting and forward-thinking paper. The conceptual contribution of adding a dynamic, internal attentional mechanism to a state-of-the-art CNN is significant and timely. This contribution is supported by strong empirical results that advance the state of the art. While the work could be strengthened by a more thorough analysis of computational trade-offs and design choices, its novelty and importance outweigh these weaknesses. The paper is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research on attention, recurrence, and reinforcement learning within deep vision models. I recommend acceptance.
