

# Review of "Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work
This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward CNNs with feedback connections to enable selective internal attention. Unlike standard CNNs that process images in a single pass with fixed parameters, dasNet allows the network to iteratively refine its classification by dynamically adjusting filter sensitivities through a policy learned via reinforcement learning. The authors draw inspiration from biological visual systems, where top-down feedback connections play a role in attentional processing and resolving ambiguous visual inputs. The paper builds on Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., ICML 2013) and applies Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to learn a policy that controls filter activations across multiple passes through the network. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, where it achieves a 6% relative improvement over the vanilla Maxout network.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a well-structured technical approach that addresses a meaningful limitation of standard CNNs. The dasNet architecture is clearly defined with equations (Equations 5-11) and the training algorithm (Algorithm 1) is thoroughly described. The authors provide appropriate ablation studies (Figures 2-3) demonstrating how the attention mechanism evolves across iterations, including qualitative analysis of filter activations. The computational approach using SNES to handle the large policy space (over 1 million parameters) is technically sound and well-justified by the authors.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the architecture and training process. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the experimental results are presented with sufficient detail to understand the methodology and outcomes. The supplementary material (referenced but not included here) appears to provide additional implementation details that would aid reproducibility.

**Significance:** The work demonstrates that incorporating feedback connections for selective attention can improve state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks without requiring additional training data. The 6% relative improvement on CIFAR-10/100 is significant for these challenging datasets, especially when considering the paper's focus on unaugmented data. The approach provides a new perspective on how to enhance existing CNN architectures rather than proposing a completely new network design.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** While the core idea of using feedback connections for selective attention is novel in the context of deep learning for image classification, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work. The authors cite relevant neuroscience literature (Felleman & Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000) but do not adequately contrast with prior computer vision work on recurrent processing (e.g., Wyatte et al., 2013; Schmidhuber, 2008), which also explored feedback connections for object recognition. The paper's claim that "this research is often applied to simplified datasets" is accurate, but it would strengthen the novelty argument to explicitly discuss how dasNet differs from prior work that did attempt to improve state-of-the-art performance.

**Technical Depth:** The paper lacks theoretical justification for why the specific architecture and SNES approach would outperform alternatives. While the authors justify SNES due to the large parameter space, they don't explore why other RL methods (e.g., policy gradients) wouldn't be feasible, or why the specific observation space (Equation 9) is optimal. The paper also doesn't provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity compared to other attention mechanisms.

**Evaluation Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which are relatively small datasets. The paper doesn't address how dasNet would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet, which would be important for assessing the approach's broader applicability. The 4-day training time on a GTX 560 Ti GPU (without the original CNN training) is substantial, but the paper doesn't discuss the computational cost-benefit tradeoff.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to enhancing CNNs with feedback connections for selective attention. The dasNet architecture is novel in its application to state-of-the-art image classification tasks, and the results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over the baseline. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Demonstrates a 6% relative improvement over the state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10/100
- Proposes a novel architectural extension to CNNs with practical implementation
- Well-structured technical approach with clear mathematical formulations
- Provides detailed analysis of the attention mechanism's dynamics

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited comparison with relevant prior work on feedback connections in CNNs
- Lacks theoretical justification for key design choices
- Evaluation limited to relatively small-scale datasets
- Computational cost (4 days of training) is substantial without clear discussion of tradeoffs

## Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that feedback connections can be effectively incorporated into deep learning architectures to improve classification performance. While the novelty could be strengthened by more thorough comparison with related work, the technical execution is sound and the results are significant for the benchmark datasets. The paper meets NIPS quality standards in terms of technical rigor and clarity, though the scope of evaluation could be expanded. Given the 2013-2017 timeframe of the conference, this work represents an important step toward more biologically plausible and adaptive deep learning architectures.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited comparison with prior work on feedback connections in CNNs.
