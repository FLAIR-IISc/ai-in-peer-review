## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into deep convolutional neural networks to enable dynamic filter sensitivity adjustment during classification. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus its attention on relevant filters, inspired by the recurrent processing observed in the human visual cortex. This attention is learned through reinforcement learning, specifically using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the policy space. The authors demonstrate that dasNet outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The work builds upon the established foundations of CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998), Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018). It addresses a limitation of traditional feedforward CNNs – their static nature – by introducing a dynamic element through feedback connections, echoing ideas explored in biological vision (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000). The use of SNES for policy learning is a pragmatic solution to the challenges posed by the large parameter space, drawing from recent advances in evolutionary strategies (Wierstra, 2014; Schaul et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of feedback connections *and* reinforcement learning for dynamic filter selection within a CNN is a novel approach.
*   **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100, even with unaugmented data, is a significant accomplishment.
*   **Biological Inspiration:** The motivation stemming from the human visual system provides a compelling rationale for the work.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the dasNet architecture and training process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific components of dasNet (e.g., the number of feedback iterations, the choice of SNES parameters).
*   **Computational Cost:** The training process is computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), which may limit the scalability of the approach to larger datasets and more complex architectures.
*   **Interpretability:** While the authors visualize filter activations, a deeper understanding of *what* the network is learning to attend to would be valuable. The qualitative analysis is limited.
*   **SNES Justification:** The justification for choosing SNES over other RL methods could be more thorough. A comparison to a simpler RL algorithm, even on a smaller scale, would strengthen the argument.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The work addresses an important limitation of traditional CNNs and offers a biologically inspired solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed ablation studies and interpretability analysis weakens the claims.
*   The computational cost is a significant practical concern.




**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, performance, and biological inspiration make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting more detailed ablation studies and a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention mechanisms.
