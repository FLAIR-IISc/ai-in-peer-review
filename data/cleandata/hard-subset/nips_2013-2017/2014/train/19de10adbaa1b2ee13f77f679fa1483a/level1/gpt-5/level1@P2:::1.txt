Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with internal, learned feedback connections that multiplicatively modulate feature-map activations across layers over multiple passes on the same input. After a standard feedforward pass, a deterministic policy maps a summary of internal activations (averaged feature-map activations, classification pre-activations, and softmax outputs) to an action vector that scales feature maps in all layers during subsequent passes. The policy is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box, gradient-free optimizer with a diagonal covariance Gaussian search distribution, to cope with a million-dimensional policy parameter space. Using a Maxout CNN with dropout as the base model, dasNet achieves improved accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented test-time setting), particularly on ambiguous examples.

This work situates itself within: (a) deep CNNs for vision (e.g., Maxout, dropout, GPU training), (b) biological evidence for top-down, recurrent modulation in cortex, and (c) RL/ES approaches to control and attention in vision (e.g., saccadic/iterative models, glimpses, recurrent feedback). In contrast to previous RL-based attention that often attends spatially or uses simplified datasets, dasNet modulates internal feature sensitivities and is evaluated on standard benchmarks with a competitive base CNN.

Strengths
- Conceptually novel combination: internal, per-feature-map multiplicative attention controlled by a learned policy; feedback operates across multiple layers and time steps to refine decisions.
- Solid motivation from biological vision and prior iterative/attention models; design aligns with modulatory top-down influences rather than adding new feedforward capacity.
- Demonstrated empirical gains over a strong Maxout baseline on CIFAR-10/100 without test-time augmentation or model ensembling; improvement appears to concentrate on ambiguous cases.
- Clear algorithmic scaffolding: define observations, actions, iterative passes, and a principled if simple loss for policy search; uses SNES to handle high-dimensional policy search where conventional RL is impractical.
- Initial analysis of dynamics (performance peaks near trained number of steps T, remains stable beyond) and evidence that learned gate vectors carry class-relevant information.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope and baselines are limited. Missing comparisons to:
  - Simple multi-pass baselines (e.g., T repeated feedforward passes with no gating; averaging softmax across passes).
  - Supervised/backprop-trained gating networks (e.g., learn a small MLP to predict gates via cross-entropy, possibly with BPTT).
  - Other recurrent/feedback CNNs or glimpse-based attention contemporaneous to this work on CIFAR.
- Limited ablations: no study of which layers benefit most, whether gating only the top layer suffices, sensitivity to observation choices (e.g., using richer statistics than mean activations), policy capacity (linear vs nonlinear), and T.
- Clarity issues in the loss definition (Equations 8–10): using d in −λboost d log(vT) is ambiguous; likely intended −λboost log vT[ytrue]. The condition d = ||vT||∞ for setting λboost is unclear; should be argmax consistency with the true label.
- Scalability/efficiency: inference is T× slower; training takes days in addition to Maxout training. No measurement of compute overhead vs. accuracy gains, nor discussion of memory footprint. SNES in million-dimensional spaces risks sample inefficiency and overfitting; regularization is included but overfitting analysis is limited.
- Reproducibility: some architectural and training details are omitted (exact sizes of action/observation vectors, per-layer map counts, kernel sizes/strides, SNES hyperparameters beyond population size, stopping criteria). SNES update details are deferred to supplementary.
- Claims of “state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets” need tighter context: many concurrent results use standard test-time augmentation or ensembles. It would help to report absolute numbers, confidence intervals, and a careful apples-to-apples comparison set.

Quality
The method is technically sound: the gating mechanism is straightforward and stable (positive gates centered around 1), and the policy-learning setup is coherent. However, the evaluation lacks stronger baselines and ablations needed to isolate where the gains come from (policy vs. iterative re-computation vs. regularization). The RL/ES choice is justified by dimensionality, but comparisons to lower-capacity policies trainable by backprop would strengthen the case. Statistical significance and compute–accuracy trade-offs are not reported.

Clarity
Overall readable, with clear high-level motivation, architectural description, and algorithm pseudocode. That said, some notation is confusing (loss/boosting terms) and several implementation specifics are missing, which hinders reproducibility. Figures are helpful but qualitative analysis of what gates do remains speculative (acknowledged by the authors).

Originality
The integration of a global, learned, internal attention policy that modulates feature maps via feedback over multiple passes is a fresh angle relative to standard feedforward CNNs and many prior attention models (which were often spatial). Using SNES for policy search at this scale is also novel in this context. The work is a meaningful step toward trainable feedback in deep vision models.

Significance
The idea is impactful: introducing learnable top-down control that can correct initial mistakes is broadly relevant. Gains on CIFAR-10/100, while modest, suggest the approach can complement strong CNNs. With stronger empirical support and broader benchmarks, this line could influence subsequent work on recurrent/attention mechanisms. The current paper advances the state of the art in a constrained setting and opens a promising direction.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: (1) multi-pass no-gating; (2) supervised/backprop gating; (3) gating limited to top layers; (4) random or shuffled gates; (5) smaller-capacity policies. Report accuracy and compute cost.
- Clarify and correct loss/boosting notation; detail observation/action dimensions and all hyperparameters; report variance across runs.
- Provide compute metrics (training/inference FLOPs/time) and analyze accuracy vs. T.
- Expand analysis: per-class confusion improvements, robustness to noise/occlusion, sensitivity to observation statistics.
- Consider lower-rank or structured policies to reduce parameter count; compare SNES to policy gradients where feasible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually compelling, biologically inspired feedback mechanism integrated into modern CNNs.
- Demonstrates tangible improvements on standard benchmarks without ensembling/augmentation.
- Opens a practical path to learned internal attention and iterative refinement in deep nets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient comparative baselines and ablations to attribute gains and assess necessity of SNES and full architecture.
- Limited clarity on loss and missing implementation details hurt reproducibility.
- Computational overhead vs. benefit not quantified; scope limited to CIFAR only.

Overall, the paper meets the criteria reasonably well on originality and presents a sound, clearly motivated method, but falls short on empirical thoroughness and clarity in some technical details. With the suggested additions, it would make a strong contribution.
