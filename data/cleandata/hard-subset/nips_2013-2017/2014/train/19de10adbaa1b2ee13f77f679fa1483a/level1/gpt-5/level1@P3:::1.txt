Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension of a Maxout CNN that introduces input-dependent, per-feature-map multiplicative gates. At test time, an image is passed through the network for T iterations. After each pass, an observation vector is computed by concatenating layer-wise average activations, classification-layer activations, and the current class probabilities. A learned policy maps this observation to an action vector that scales each feature map in every convolutional/maxout layer on the next pass. The policy is a large linear(ish) network with ~10^6 parameters, trained after standard supervised CNN training via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box, diagonal-covariance NES variant chosen for scalability. On CIFAR-10/100, dasNet improves over its feedforward Maxout baseline and claims state-of-the-art results for unaugmented evaluation. The paper connects the design to neurobiological evidence for feedback and selective attention, and presents qualitative analyses of the internal gating dynamics.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on Maxout networks with dropout [Goodfellow et al., ICML’13], adding recurrent top-down/bottom-up modulation akin to proposals in both computational neuroscience and machine vision that emphasize feedback [e.g., O’Reilly; Wyatte et al.], and selective attention/saccade-based RL [Larochelle & Hinton; Denil et al.]. In contrast to glimpse/saccade models that act in image space, dasNet acts in feature space via per-channel gating. Using NES to optimize a high-dimensional policy follows prior work on scalable evolutionary strategies (Wierstra et al.; Schaul et al.). Compared to contemporary CNN advances (e.g., Network-in-Network; stochastic pooling), this work’s novelty is in dynamic, input-conditional reweighting of internal representations via feedback and the use of black-box policy search for this control.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mechanism (a_t multiplicative gates; observation-to-action via a policy; unrolled T-step evaluation) is well-defined and straightforward to implement. The training objective for the policy is the final-step cross-entropy with boosted weight on misclassified samples; the choice is reasonable but somewhat ad hoc. Stability is discussed (performance peaks around the trained T and degrades minimally thereafter in a small two-layer setting).
- Empirical support: Results indicate a relative ~6% improvement over the baseline Maxout CNN on CIFAR-10/100 and a claimed SOTA for “unaugmented datasets.” However, key details are missing: the exact baseline error rates, exact dasNet error rates, whether “unaugmented” refers to test-time augmentation only (the base CNN is trained with augmentation), and comparisons to strong contemporaneous baselines (e.g., NIN, stochastic pooling, dropconnect, committees vs. single models). A 5x inference cost (T=5) is implied but runtimes are not reported.
- Methodological choices: Using SNES to optimize a million-parameter deterministic policy is unusual given that the entire system is differentiable if unrolled; no comparison is provided to gradient-based training (e.g., BPTT through T steps) or standard policy gradient methods. The observation discards spatial information (map averages), so the policy can only perform channel-wise attention—this limits expressivity and should be discussed as a design trade-off.

Clarity
The main mechanism is clearly described with equations and a training algorithm. However:
- Table 1 and exact numbers are missing in the provided text; significance and variance are not reported.
- The term “unaugmented datasets” is potentially confusing, as the base CNN uses data augmentation. Clarify train-time vs. test-time augmentation and single-model vs. ensemble comparisons.
- SNES details are deferred to the supplement; at least summarizing the update rule and hyperparameters would aid reproducibility.
- The dimensionalities of observations/actions and the policy architecture are only roughly specified.

Originality
The idea of input-conditional, recurrent feature reweighting within a CNN via learned feedback connections is novel in the NIPS literature of the time and distinct from contemporaneous glimpse models. The use of large-scale SNES for learning such a policy is also unusual, though arguably not the only or most natural choice. The paper positions itself well within both neuroscience-inspired feedback and RL-for-vision communities.

Significance
If the claimed improvements over strong single-model baselines on CIFAR-10/100 hold, the work is significant: it demonstrates that simple, recurrent, internal attention can boost a high-performing feedforward CNN. The approach is general and could be applied to other architectures/tasks. However, the practical significance depends on compute overhead, robustness, and comparisons to alternative training (e.g., end-to-end differentiable feedback), none of which are fully explored.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant mechanism: per-feature-map recurrent gating learned post hoc.
- Demonstrated gains over a strong Maxout baseline; qualitative evidence of correcting confusions (e.g., cat vs. dog).
- Scalability of policy search to high dimensions with SNES.
- Initial analysis of dynamics and information content in gates.

Weaknesses
- Missing or underspecified quantitative comparisons and exact numbers; unclear SOTA claim context.
- No ablation studies: gating only top layers vs. all; role of each observation component; linear vs. nonlinear policy; effect of T on the full model.
- No comparison to backpropagation-based training of the policy through unrolled passes.
- Limited analysis of overfitting/regularization and validation protocol during SNES; small SNES batch sizes risk overfitting.
- Compute cost at test time (T passes) and wall-clock comparisons not discussed.
- Observation discards spatial information; action constrained to be positive via softmax-scale; design choices not justified.

Questions for the authors
- Please report exact test errors for baseline Maxout and dasNet on CIFAR-10/100, with/without test-time augmentation, and compare to contemporary single-model baselines.
- Why was SNES preferred over BPTT or policy gradient? Did you try end-to-end differentiable training?
- What is the exact observation/action dimensionality, and what is the policy architecture?
- How sensitive are results to T, λ parameters, population size, and the boosted loss?
- Do you use a held-out validation set for early stopping of SNES? Any signs of overfitting?
- What is the inference speed overhead relative to the baseline?

Suggestions
- Add comprehensive ablations and training alternatives (unrolled BPTT).
- Clarify augmentation/ensemble settings and include stronger baselines.
- Report runtime and memory overhead.
- Explore adding spatial attention signals and/or gating only higher layers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general, and biologically motivated mechanism that yields improvements on standard benchmarks.
- Novel application of feedback within deep CNNs at scale.
- Potentially broad impact and extensibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is incomplete; SOTA claim is not fully substantiated within the provided text.
- Methodological choice (SNES) not justified against simpler, differentiable alternatives.
- Practical costs and robustness not analyzed.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs clearer, stronger empirical comparisons and ablations to fully support its claims.
