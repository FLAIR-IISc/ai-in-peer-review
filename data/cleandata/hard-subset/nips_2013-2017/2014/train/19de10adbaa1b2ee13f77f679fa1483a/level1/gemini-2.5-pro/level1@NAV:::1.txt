This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments traditional feedforward CNNs with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial feedforward pass, a control policy can modulate the activations of convolutional filters for subsequent, iterative passes on the same image. This allows the network to "re-examine" an image, focusing on different features to resolve ambiguities. This approach is well-grounded in neuroscience literature on recurrent processing in the visual cortex. While previous works have used reinforcement learning (RL) for visual tasks like learning saccades, this paper is novel in applying RL—specifically, Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to directly learn a high-dimensional policy for modulating the internal filters of a state-of-the-art classification model (Maxout networks).

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—learning a policy to dynamically gate internal convolutional filters for iterative refinement—is a highly original and promising idea. It presents a principled way to incorporate feedback and sequential processing into otherwise static CNNs, potentially opening a new research direction.
*   **Quality:** The experimental results are strong, achieving a new state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. This demonstrates that the proposed mechanism is not just conceptually interesting but also practically effective. The choice of SNES to tackle the challenging million-dimensional policy optimization problem is well-justified and technically sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and does a good job of situating the work within both deep learning and computational neuroscience contexts.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the overall method is clear, the composition of the state vector for the RL policy (concatenating averaged map activations, etc.) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed justification or ablation study on the state representation would strengthen the paper. The high computational cost and complexity of the RL training phase might also hinder reproducibility and broader adoption.
*   **Quality of Analysis:** The qualitative analysis of the learned attention mechanism (Figure 3) is insightful but preliminary. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of interpreting high-level filters, the claims about correcting misclassifications could be better supported by more systematic analysis across different classes or error types.
*   **Significance:** The performance gains, while establishing a new state-of-the-art, come at a significant computational cost. A discussion of this trade-off would be valuable. It is unclear if the performance improvement justifies the added complexity for most practical applications.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strength and originality of the core idea and the impressive empirical results.
