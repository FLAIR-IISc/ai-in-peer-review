This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors argue that traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are limited by their stationary and feedforward nature, and that incorporating feedback connections can improve classification performance. The paper presents a comprehensive overview of the dasNet architecture, including its components, training algorithm, and experimental evaluation.

The main idea of dasNet is to harness the power of sequential processing to improve classification performance by allowing the network to iteratively focus its internal attention on certain features extracted from images. The authors achieve this by augmenting a standard Maxout network with feedback connections that modulate the activity of the convolutional filters. The weights of these connections implement a control policy that is learned through reinforcement learning after the CNN has been trained in the usual way via supervised learning.

The paper provides a thorough description of the dasNet architecture, including the Maxout network, the reinforcement learning framework, and the training algorithm. The authors also present experimental results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating that dasNet outperforms the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets.

Strengths:

1. Novel architecture: The dasNet architecture is a significant departure from traditional CNNs, and the authors provide a compelling argument for its potential benefits.
2. Comprehensive evaluation: The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation of dasNet, including comparisons to state-of-the-art models and analysis of the network's dynamics.
3. Clear writing: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the dasNet architecture and its components.

Weaknesses:

1. Computational cost: The authors note that training dasNet is computationally expensive, requiring 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU. This may limit the applicability of the approach to larger datasets or more complex tasks.
2. Limited analysis: While the paper presents some analysis of the network's dynamics, it would be beneficial to include more in-depth analysis of the attention mechanism and its impact on classification performance.
3. Comparison to other attention mechanisms: The paper does not provide a direct comparison to other attention mechanisms, which would be beneficial for understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of dasNet.
