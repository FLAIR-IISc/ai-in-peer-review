- Introduces dasNet, which augments a pretrained Maxout CNN with a learned recurrent feedback policy that iteratively modulates convolutional feature-map activations.
- The policy runs for multiple passes per image, generating modulatory weights to direct “internal” feature attention rather than spatial attention.
- SNES (Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) is used to train the high-dimensional policy (~1M+ parameters) as a black-box optimization instead of standard policy-gradient RL.
- Trained policy minimizes classification loss with a boosting-like emphasis on correcting initially misclassified samples.
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing improved accuracy over the Maxout baseline and claiming new state-of-the-art on unaugmented CIFAR-10.
- Technical formulation and training procedure are clear and well-specified (Equation 5, Algorithm 1).
- Choice of SNES is justified by scalability concerns for very high-dimensional continuous action spaces.
- Experimental setup uses a strong baseline and includes ablation-style analyses (step stability and gate information content).
- Novelty lies in externally learned modulation of internal filter activations and applying large-scale evolution strategies to this problem.
- The idea of an internal spotlight on features (not spatial regions) offers a fresh perspective on attention mechanisms.
- Computational cost is high: expensive additional training (4 days on GPU) and inference requires multiple forward passes (T=5).
- Lack of empirical comparison between SNES and alternative RL/policy-gradient methods weakens the argument for SNES being superior.
- Reproducibility concerns due to missing implementation details (exact Maxout architecture, observation vector dimensionality, SNES hyperparameters).
- Performance gain (e.g., 9.39% → 8.81% error on CIFAR-10) is modest relative to the added computational complexity.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel architecture/training paradigm, state-of-the-art results, potential research impact, and sound methodology with supportive analyses.
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, missing reproducibility details, and insufficient empirical comparison to other policy-learning methods.
