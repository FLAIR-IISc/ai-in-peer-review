

# Review: Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment traditional feedforward CNNs with feedback connections that allow the network to iteratively adjust its internal attention during classification. The key innovation is a reinforcement learning approach (via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies) to learn how to modulate filter sensitivities across multiple passes through an image, rather than relying on a single feedforward pass. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves classification performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving a new state-of-the-art result for unaugmented data.

The paper relates well to previous work on visual cortex architecture (Felleman & Essen, 1991), which describes extensive feedback connections in biological visual systems. It also builds upon recent work in attention mechanisms (e.g., Zeiler & Fergus, 2013) and reinforcement learning applications in computer vision (e.g., Schmidhuber, 2004; Bar, 2004). Unlike prior approaches that used RL for attention on simplified datasets, dasNet applies this concept directly to state-of-the-art CNNs, demonstrating feasibility and performance gains.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear methodology. The dasNet architecture is well-defined, with a clear description of how feedback connections modify filter activation weights during iterative processing. The use of SNES to handle the large policy parameter space is reasonable given the complexity of the problem. The experimental setup is appropriate, with careful attention to the evaluation of ambiguous classification cases. The authors provide sufficient detail about their implementation (T=5 steps, λcorrect=0.005, etc.) for reproducibility. However, the paper lacks a theoretical justification for why this feedback mechanism improves performance beyond empirical results. The claim that "the network corrects itself by emphasising and de-emphasising certain filters" is supported by qualitative examples, but the paper does not rigorously analyze *why* these adjustments lead to improved classification.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The technical details of the dasNet architecture are explained logically, with appropriate equations and figures. The experimental results are presented in a clear manner, with Figure 3 providing a useful visual example of how attention changes across processing steps. However, the description of the reinforcement learning component could be clearer. The paper states that "the observation vector, o0, is constructed by concatenating the following values extracted from M," but does not provide sufficient detail about how these values were selected or why they are meaningful. The paper would benefit from a more thorough explanation of the observation space design.

**Originality:** The core idea of using feedback connections to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification is novel compared to standard feedforward CNNs. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by noting that "this research is often applied to simplified datasets for demonstration purposes due to computation constraints, and are not aimed at improving the state-of-the-art." The dasNet approach directly targets improving state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks. However, the paper does not sufficiently address how this differs from other recent attention mechanisms (e.g., channel attention, spatial attention) that have been proposed in the deep learning literature. While the authors cite relevant work, they could better situate their contribution within this growing area.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates a 6% relative improvement over the vanilla CNN on CIFAR-10, which is significant for this challenging dataset. The authors successfully show that a simple boosting mechanism (weighting misclassified samples more heavily) can effectively focus the attention mechanism on difficult cases. The approach is biologically inspired, which adds conceptual value. However, the computational cost (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU) is substantial, and the paper does not address whether this approach would scale to larger datasets like ImageNet. The authors also do not explore how this might be integrated with other recent advancements in CNN architecture (e.g., ResNets, EfficientNets).

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
1. Novel approach to introducing feedback connections in CNNs, with clear biological motivation.
2. Demonstrates significant performance improvement on standard benchmarks without data augmentation.
3. Provides both quantitative results and qualitative analysis of attention changes.
4. Well-motivated and well-structured paper that addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning.

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
1. Limited theoretical justification for why the feedback mechanism improves performance.
2. The 6% improvement may not be considered substantial in the context of deep learning research (where smaller improvements are common).
3. Does not sufficiently differentiate from other attention mechanisms in the literature.
4. Lacks scalability analysis to larger datasets or more complex architectures.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to introducing selective attention in CNNs through feedback connections. While the performance improvement is significant for the CIFAR datasets, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and more thorough comparison to other attention mechanisms. The computational cost is notable but reasonable for the dataset size. Given the novelty of the approach and its demonstrated performance gains, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues around the RL component and to better contextualize the contribution within the broader attention mechanism literature. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that feedback connections can improve performance on standard benchmarks, and it opens up interesting avenues for future work on recurrent processing in deep networks.
