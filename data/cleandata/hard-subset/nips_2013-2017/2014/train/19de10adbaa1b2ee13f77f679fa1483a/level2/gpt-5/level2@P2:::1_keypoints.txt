- Summary: Proposes dasNet, a recurrent extension of Maxout CNNs that applies learned multiplicative, channel-wise gates over T sequential passes; policy trained post-hoc with SNES to improve final-step classification on CIFAR-10/100.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to feedback, attention, and recurrent processing literature but is distinctive in applying learned internal feedback attention to a modern high-performing CNN and using large-scale SNES.
- Strength: Simple, clear internal attention mechanism (per-feature-map multiplicative gates) that can iteratively refine predictions.
- Strength: Demonstrates iterative refinement with qualitative evidence of meaningful deeper-layer modulations.
- Strength: Scales policy search to ~million-parameter controllers using Separable NES, an interesting application of evolutionary strategies.
- Strength: Reports empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a strong Maxout baseline without ensembling/test-time augmentation.
- Strength: Ablation on number of steps indicates stable dynamics beyond the trained horizon.
- Weakness: “Reinforcement learning” framing is misleading; training is black-box supervised optimization with no environment stochasticity or multi-step credit-assignment justification.
- Weakness: No comparison to gradient-based alternatives — backpropagation through time (BPTT) or end-to-end differentiable training of the gates is not attempted or justified.
- Weakness: Experimental reporting lacks detail — absolute error rates, standard deviations, and full comparisons to contemporary strong baselines are missing.
- Weakness: Claim of “state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets” is ambiguous and needs consistent definition and comparison protocol.
- Weakness: Softmax-based action parameterization enforces positive, sum-constrained gates (average=1), which limits expressiveness and lacks ablation.
- Weakness: Observations use per-map averages, discarding spatial information; no spatial-attention baselines are provided to attribute gains.
- Weakness: Computational overhead is non-trivial (multiple passes; days of SNES training) with no compute vs. accuracy trade-off analysis or comparison to scaling the baseline.
- Weakness: Limited ablations — no one-pass learned reweighting baseline, no BPTT-trained iterative inference, and unclear interpretation of gate-only results.
- Quality: Method is technically implementable and clear, but key design choices (SNES, softmax, observation design) are not empirically justified.
- Clarity: Paper is readable with a helpful algorithm description but omits important details (network/policy sizes, T rationale, hyperparameters, exact metrics) and misuses “unaugmented.”
- Originality: Novel combination of internal channel-wise feedback attention and large-scale SNES policy learning in a modern CNN context.
- Significance: Potentially impactful if gains hold under rigorous comparison, but broader adoption requires demonstrations versus gradient-trained recurrent/gated variants and deeper evaluations.
- For acceptance: Novel, simple mechanism with observed benchmark gains and an interesting cognitive-to-CNN bridge; demonstrates scalable controller learning.
- Against acceptance: Insufficient experimental rigor, unjustified methodological choices, and ambiguous SOTA claims.
- Suggested improvements: Provide full quantitative results (absolute errors, variances), clarify augmentation protocol, and compare to contemporary SOTA under matched conditions.
- Suggested improvements: Add ablations — train policy by BPTT, remove/relax softmax constraint, per-layer vs. all-layer gating, one-pass vs. multi-pass, and spatial observation variants.
- Suggested improvements: Report compute/training/inference cost and compare to similar-budget model scaling; include per-class analyses, confusion matrices, and diagnostic examples.
- Suggested improvements: Test sensitivity to T at test time and provide robustness/generalization analyses to ensure policy does not overfit.
