## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture augmenting standard convolutional neural networks with feedback connections to enable dynamic filter sensitivity adjustment during classification. The core idea, inspired by human visual processing, is to allow the network to iteratively refine its focus on relevant features, particularly in ambiguous cases. The feedback weights are learned via Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the policy space. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets compared to previous state-of-the-art models on unaugmented data.

The paper’s strength lies in its biologically inspired approach to improving CNN performance. The motivation for incorporating feedback mechanisms is well-articulated, drawing parallels to human visual attention. The use of SNES to tackle the large parameter space of the attentional policy is a pragmatic solution. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating a clear performance gain on challenging datasets. The visualization of filter activation changes during the iterative process provides some insight into the network’s internal decision-making. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of interpreting the learned filter changes, the analysis remains superficial. A more in-depth investigation into *what* features are being emphasized or suppressed, and *why*, would significantly strengthen the contribution. The experimental setup, while demonstrating improvement, lacks a comprehensive comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly those employing data augmentation. The choice of Maxout networks as the base architecture feels somewhat dated, and the justification for this choice isn't entirely convincing. The paper also lacks a discussion of computational cost; training with SNES is likely significantly more expensive than standard backpropagation, and this trade-off isn't addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to improving CNNs through biologically inspired feedback mechanisms.
*   Demonstrated performance improvement on standard datasets.
*   Addresses a challenging problem of improving robustness in ambiguous cases.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of the learned attentional policy.
*   Comparison to current state-of-the-art is incomplete.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational cost.
*   Base architecture choice feels dated.

**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field. While the analysis and experimental evaluation could be more thorough, the core idea is novel and the results are promising. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting a more detailed analysis of the learned attention mechanisms, a comparison to more recent baselines, and a discussion of the computational overhead. The authors should also clarify the rationale for using Maxout networks.
