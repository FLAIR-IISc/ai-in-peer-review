Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a CNN augmented with top-down feedback that multiplicatively modulates feature-map activations over multiple passes on the same image. The modulation policy is learned after supervised training via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), enabling million-parameter policies to adjust “internal attention.” On CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), dasNet improves over a Maxout baseline and reports state-of-the-art for single models without data augmentation or ensembles. The work is positioned within biologically inspired recurrent processing and selective attention, and relates to Maxout (Goodfellow) and prior RL/attention efforts (e.g., Denil; Larochelle & Hinton), as well as top-down/recurrent accounts in neuroscience.

Claims:
- Feedback through a learned policy can correct initial misclassifications by reweighting feature maps across iterative passes.
- SNES can efficiently train such high-dimensional policies.
- Achieves SOTA on unaugmented CIFAR-10/100.

Quality: The method is valid and clearly described at a high level; equations and the training loop are explicit. The empirical support includes gains on CIFAR-10/100, a dynamics study showing stability for T steps, and evidence that gate activations carry class information. However, statistical significance is not reported, ablations are limited (e.g., policy complexity, per-layer gating, effect of T, or comparison to simpler differentiable gating trained via backprop/REINFORCE). Compute and parameter overheads vs. accuracy are not quantified.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. Some implementation details important for reproducibility (exact architecture/hyperparameters, training schedule, code/pseudocode for SNES update) are sparse for a conference paper claiming SOTA.

Originality: Combining global feature-map gating with learned feedback via black-box optimization in a modern CNN is novel relative to prior RL-based attention that typically targets saccades or spatial glimpses. The framing as internal selective attention is compelling.

Significance and usefulness: If robust, the approach suggests a practical path to add iterative refinement to strong feedforward models, potentially useful for hard cases. Demonstrations are limited to small benchmarks; broader validation would strengthen impact.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Compare to end-to-end differentiable recurrent CNNs (e.g., simple gated feedback, ConvRNN/GRU) and to policy gradients/REINFORCE for the same policy class.
- Provide ablations: number of passes, policy size, per-layer vs. per-map gating, and effect of boosting weights.
- Report parameter counts, inference-time cost (T passes), and accuracy vs. latency trade-offs.
- Test with/without data augmentation and against contemporary baselines (including ensembles) for fairness.
- Add per-class confusion analysis and more systematic visualizations.

Pros:
- Clear, biologically motivated idea; simple to integrate with CNNs.
- Demonstrated improvements and stable recurrent dynamics.
- Insightful use of SNES for very high-dimensional policies.

Cons:
- Limited ablations and statistical rigor.
- Restricted benchmarks and unclear compute overhead.
- Missing comparisons to strong recurrent/differentiable alternatives.
