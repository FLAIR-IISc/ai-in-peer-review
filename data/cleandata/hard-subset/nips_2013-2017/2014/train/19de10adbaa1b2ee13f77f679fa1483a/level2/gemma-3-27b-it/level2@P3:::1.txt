This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into deep convolutional neural networks to enable dynamic adjustment of filter sensitivities during classification. The core idea is inspired by the observation that human perception often involves iterative processing and attention allocation, and aims to replicate this in CNNs via reinforcement learning, specifically using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The work claims to outperform state-of-the-art models on unaugmented CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The strengths of the paper lie in its innovative approach to incorporating attention mechanisms into CNNs through feedback connections and reinforcement learning. The motivation drawing from human visual perception is compelling, and the application of SNES to a large parameter space, while computationally demanding, demonstrates a practical solution to training such a system. The reported performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are a positive signal. Furthermore, the attempt to visualize the changes in filter activations provides some intuition into the network’s behavior, although more thorough analysis is needed (as acknowledged by the authors). The connection to related work, particularly in visual attention and recurrent processing, is reasonably well-established, providing context for the contribution.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. While the paper asserts state-of-the-art performance, the comparison lacks crucial details. The specific model used for comparison isn’t clearly identified (simply referred to as a "previous state-of-the-art CNN"). A more precise specification of the baseline model, including its architecture and training details, is essential for reproducibility and a fair assessment. It’s unclear if the baseline model also used data augmentation, which significantly affects performance. The performance improvement of 6% is stated as “relative,” but the absolute improvement should also be reported for better interpretability. 

The description of the reinforcement learning process, particularly the observation space construction, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The rationale behind concatenating average activations and class probabilities as the observation is not fully justified. A more rigorous analysis of the information content within the observation space might reveal whether it truly captures the necessary state information for effective attention control. The choice of hyperparameters (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2) is empirically determined without significant justification. A sensitivity analysis or ablation study exploring the impact of these parameters would strengthen the findings.

The visualization of filter changes, while interesting, is limited. The figure shows "change of layer 0", but doesn't clearly explain *what* constitutes a change – is it absolute difference in activation, percentage change, or another metric? The claim that complex suppression and enhancement patterns are found needs more quantitative support. Providing examples of specific filters that undergo significant changes and their impact on classification would be helpful. 

The discussion of related work, while present, could be more comprehensive. Several recent attention mechanisms in CNNs (e.g., SENet, CBAM) are not mentioned, and a comparison to these approaches would position the work more effectively. The paper leans heavily on biological plausibility as motivation, but doesn't sufficiently address how the SNES-learned policy aligns with known neural mechanisms in the brain.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novelty: The integration of feedback connections and reinforcement learning for dynamic filter adaptation is a fresh approach.
* Performance: The reported improvements on CIFAR-10/100 are encouraging.
* Technical Soundness: The application of SNES to this problem is a clever solution to a challenging optimization problem.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Lack of detail in performance comparison: The baseline model is not precisely defined, hindering evaluation.
* Limited justification for design choices: The observation space and hyperparameters lack strong theoretical or empirical grounding.
* Superficial visualization: The filter change analysis lacks quantitative support and clear explanation.

To improve this paper, the following revisions are recommended:
1. **Provide a detailed description of the baseline model** used for comparison, including its architecture, training procedure (data augmentation included), and performance metrics.
2. **Report both relative and absolute performance improvements.**
3. **Justify the construction of the observation space** with a more rigorous analysis of its information content.
4. **Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters** (λcorrect, λmisclassified, λL2).
5. **Enhance the visualization of filter changes** with quantitative metrics and specific examples of influential filters.
6. **Expand the related work section** to include recent attention mechanisms in CNNs and discuss the differences.
7. **Provide a more in-depth discussion of the biological plausibility** of the SNES-learned policy.



The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above is crucial to strengthen its scientific rigor and increase its impact.
