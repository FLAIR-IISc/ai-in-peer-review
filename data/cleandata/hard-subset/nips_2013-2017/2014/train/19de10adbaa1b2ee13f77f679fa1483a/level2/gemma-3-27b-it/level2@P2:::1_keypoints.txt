- Proposes dasNet: a novel feedback-enabled CNN that iteratively adjusts filter sensitivity to focus attention during classification.  
- Learns the attentional policy with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to handle high-dimensional policy space.  
- Demonstrates improved performance and claims state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.  
- Well-motivated biologically, contrasting static feedforward CNNs with dynamic, feedback-driven processing.  
- Visualization of filter activations offers qualitative insight into the attentional mechanism.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited—comparison mainly against a Maxout baseline and not against recent architectures (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet).  
- Lacks comprehensive quantitative analysis linking learned attention weights to input feature saliency.  
- Unclear analysis of the impact of T (number of passes) on accuracy and computational cost.  
- SNES-based training is computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti) with no discussion of efficiency improvements.  
- Weak connection and comparison to related recurrent attention work (e.g., LSTM/GRU-based mechanisms).  
- The "information content" measure using k-NN and logistic regression is poorly justified and its 40–45% accuracy is not well interpreted.  
- Strengths support acceptance: novel idea, effective SNES use, and demonstrated empirical gains.  
- Reasons against acceptance: insufficient robustness in evaluation, limited quantitative attention analysis, high training cost, and weak related-work comparison.  
- Recommendation: weak accept with major revisions—expand benchmarks, provide rigorous quantitative attention analyses, discuss training efficiency, and strengthen comparisons to recurrent attention methods.
