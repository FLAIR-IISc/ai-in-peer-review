- Proposes dasNet: a deep network with feedback connections for internal selective attention, using a policy learned via SNES to adjust filter sensitivities.  
- Core claim: dynamic filter modulation improves classification on ambiguous CIFAR-10/100 cases and achieves new SOTA for unaugmented datasets.  
- Strength: neuroscience-inspired motivation for iterative attention mechanisms.  
- Strength: solid experimental validation on standard benchmarks.  
- Strength: ablation studies show stability across interaction steps (T=1 to T=9).  
- Strength: policy contains meaningful information (gate-value classification yields 40.7% 15-NN accuracy).  
- Strength: use of SNES is pragmatically justified for handling million-dimensional policy space.  
- Weakness: novelty limited—feedback/selective attention and recurrent processing have prior work (e.g., O’Reilly et al., Denil et al.).  
- Weakness: misleading SOTA claim—evaluation against outdated Maxout networks rather than contemporary models (ResNet, EfficientNet).  
- Weakness: modest 6% relative improvement over Maxout, smaller than typical modern CNN gains (15–20% on CIFAR-100).  
- Weakness: unclear distinction from established attention modules (SENet, CBAM) that also modulate feature importance.  
- Weakness: high computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) with no scalability analysis.  
- Weakness: qualitative filter-change analysis lacks causal evidence linking attention adjustments to accuracy gains.  
- Recommendation: benchmark against contemporary architectures (e.g., ResNet-50).  
- Recommendation: clearly differentiate dasNet’s feedback mechanism from existing attention modules.  
- Recommendation: provide rigorous analysis of when and why the feedback mechanism improves performance.  
- Conclusion: technically sound but marginal novelty and limited significance as presented, warranting rejection.
