- Proposal: dasNet augments a trained feedforward Maxout CNN with learned multiplicative per-feature-map feedback gates applied over multiple passes to refine predictions.
- Policy: low-dimensional observations (per-map averages, pre-activations, class probs) map to layer-wise scaling factors via a scaled softmax; policy trained post-hoc with Separable NES due to large parameter count.
- Empirical claim: consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100 (typically T=5 passes), reportedly SOTA for single models on unaugmented data.
- Relation to prior work: connects to biological recurrent/top-down vision and computational attention/recurrence, differing by learned internal feedback and black-box policy search.
- Strength — simplicity: mechanism is generic, easy to integrate, and uses simple multiplicative gating.
- Strength — empirical gains: shows consistent performance improvements and stable dynamics when running more steps than trained.
- Strength — analysis: “information in gates” study indicates gates capture class-relevant structure.
- Weakness — baselines/ablations missing: no BPTT-trained recurrent/gating baseline despite full differentiability.
- Weakness — policy architecture unclear: need comparison to simpler linear or shallow MLP policies and rationale for softmax choice.
- Weakness — observation components unclear: ablate using only class probabilities vs. including per-map averages and pre-activations.
- Weakness — gating granularity unclear: ablate per-layer vs. last-layer-only gating to assess lower-layer contributions.
- Weakness — trivial baselines missing: multi-pass without learned feedback or confidence calibration as simple refinement baselines are not tested.
- Reporting issue — augmentation/conflict: abstract claims “unaugmented” SOTA but base Maxout was trained with augmentation; needs clarification and matched comparisons.
- Reporting issue — statistics: request exact error rates with standard deviations and confusion-matrix improvements for targeted ambiguous pairs.
- Computational cost: test-time cost scales by T forward passes; report wall-clock slowdowns, FLOPs, and compare accuracy–compute trade-off to enlarging the base CNN.
- Training cost: policy training is expensive (population 50, 4 days on GTX 560 Ti); report sample efficiency (generations, evaluations) for practicality.
- Objective design concerns: loss uses only final step and boosts misclassified samples; suggest testing auxiliary/intermediate losses and curriculum schemes.
- Dynamics concern: request statistics on gate distributions, saturation, or oscillations across layers and steps.
- Reproducibility gaps: specify observation/action dimensionalities, SNES hyperparameters, policy details, and stopping criteria; share code if possible.
- Clarity: paper is generally well written with helpful pseudocode, but ambiguities remain about policy architecture, dimensionalities, and augmentation.
- Originality: novel combination of learned internal per-feature-map attention with NES-trained controller applied to modern CNNs.
- Significance: broadly applicable approach that could influence recurrent/feedback CNNs if validated with stronger baselines and larger-scale tasks.
- Pros for acceptance: clean mechanism, biologically motivated, stable multi-step behavior, and promising analyses.
- Cons for acceptance: missing strong baselines/ablations, unclear compute–accuracy comparisons, augmentation/SOTA ambiguity, and limited evaluation to CIFAR.
- Suggestions: add BPTT and other ablations, clarify augmentation and SNES settings, report compute overhead, provide per-class/confusion analyses, and release code.
- Overall assessment: promising and novel with encouraging results but needs stronger baselines, clearer reporting, and more experiments to firmly establish benefits; borderline accept pending revisions.
