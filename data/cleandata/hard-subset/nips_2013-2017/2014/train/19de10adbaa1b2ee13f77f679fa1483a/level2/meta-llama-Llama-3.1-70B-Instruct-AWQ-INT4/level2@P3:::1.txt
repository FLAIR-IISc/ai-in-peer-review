This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel deep neural network architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections. The authors propose a non-stationary CNN that can adapt its behavior post-training, allowing the network to iteratively focus its attention on the most discriminative features in an image. The attentional policy is learned through reinforcement learning using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a variant of Natural Evolution Strategies.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components. The introduction provides a good overview of the limitations of traditional CNNs and the motivation for developing a new architecture that can adapt its behavior post-training.

The technical soundness of the paper is good. The authors provide a detailed description of the dasNet architecture, including the feedback structure, the attentional policy, and the reinforcement learning algorithm used to train the policy. The experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet in improving classification performance, especially on difficult classification instances.

The clarity of the paper is also good. The authors use clear and concise language to explain complex concepts, and the figures and tables are well-organized and easy to understand.

The originality of the paper is high. The authors propose a novel architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections, which is a significant departure from traditional CNNs. The use of reinforcement learning to train the attentional policy is also a new contribution.

The significance of the paper is high. The authors demonstrate that dasNet can improve classification performance on challenging datasets, and the architecture has the potential to be applied to a wide range of computer vision tasks. The paper also contributes to the understanding of how internal selective attention can be used to improve the performance of deep neural networks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel architecture that incorporates internal selective attention through feedback connections, which is a significant departure from traditional CNNs.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet in improving classification performance on challenging datasets.
* The paper contributes to the understanding of how internal selective attention can be used to improve the performance of deep neural networks.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components, making the paper easy to understand.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes that the attentional policy can be learned through reinforcement learning using SNES, which may not be the case in all scenarios.
* The experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets may not be representative of all computer vision tasks, and the performance of dasNet may vary depending on the specific task and dataset.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of dasNet, which may be a concern for large-scale applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and original, and it contributes significantly to the field of computer vision. The arguments for acceptance outweigh the arguments against acceptance, and the paper is recommended for acceptance.
