This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections. The core idea is to allow the network to perform multiple processing passes on a single input image, iteratively modulating the activity of its own convolutional filters to refine its classification. The authors start with a pre-trained, high-performing Maxout network. They then introduce modulatory gates for each feature map, which are controlled by a policy. This policy takes as input a state representation derived from the network's activations in the previous pass and outputs an action vector that scales the filter activities for the next pass.

Crucially, this control policy is trained using reinforcement learning (RL) after the initial supervised training of the base network is complete. Due to the high dimensionality of the action space (one gate per feature map) and the state space, the authors opt for a black-box optimization method, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), to learn the policy parameters. The reward function is designed to focus on correcting misclassifications from the initial feedforward pass. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (without data augmentation), outperforming the baseline Maxout network. The work is well-motivated by the recurrent and attentional processing in the human visual cortex and connects to prior computational work on recurrence and RL for vision, but applies these concepts to a modern, state-of-the-art deep learning model.

**Quality**
The paper is technically interesting and the empirical results are strong. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on these competitive benchmarks is a significant achievement. The claims are well-supported by the experiments presented.

However, there are a few points regarding the technical approach that could be clarified. The choice to use SNES, a black-box optimizer, is justified by the large parameter space of the policy. While this is a reasonable argument, it would be beneficial to discuss why more conventional policy gradient methods were not considered or why they might be unsuitable. For instance, would it be possible to design a lower-dimensional policy network (e.g., another CNN that takes the state representation as input) that could be trained with policy gradients? The current policy appears to be a single linear layer (Eq. 7), which might be a restrictive choice. A more detailed justification of SNES over alternatives would strengthen the paper.

The training process appears to be extremely computationally expensive (4 days for the RL phase). This is a major practical limitation. A brief discussion of this cost and potential avenues for making the training more efficient would be a valuable addition.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and the core idea is communicated effectively. The diagrams and algorithms are helpful for understanding the overall process.

Some areas could be improved for clarity and reproducibility:
- The exact architecture of the baseline Maxout network (number of filters per layer, kernel sizes, etc.) is not specified. Including these details would allow for a more direct comparison and reproduction of the results.
- The construction of the observation vector `o_t` feels somewhat ad-hoc, combining average map activations, intermediate layer activations, and the final class probabilities. A brief rationale for why this specific combination was chosen as the state representation would be helpful.
- The description of the policy `π_θ` in Equation 7 seems to imply a simple linear mapping from observations to actions. Given that the parameter vector `θ` is stated to have close to a million weights, this suggests `dim(O)` and `dim(A)` are both in the thousands. A more explicit description of the dimensions of the state, action, and parameter spaces would be useful.

**Originality**
The paper's main contribution is highly original. While recurrence and feedback have been explored in neural networks before, this work is novel in its successful application to a modern, state-of-the-art deep CNN for object recognition. The framing of internal filter modulation as a sequential decision-making problem to be solved with RL is a creative and powerful idea. The combination of a pre-trained deep network with a subsequently-trained RL-based attention policy is a novel training paradigm. The approach of modulating *internal feature representations* over time is a distinct and interesting form of attention compared to more common approaches that focus on spatial regions of the input image.

**Significance**
The work is significant for two main reasons. First, it provides a compelling demonstration that moving beyond purely feedforward architectures can lead to tangible performance gains on challenging benchmarks. This could inspire further research into recurrent and attentive mechanisms within deep networks. Second, it successfully applies large-scale RL to fine-tune the behavior of a deep network, opening a promising direction for combining deep learning and RL. The reported state-of-the-art results will likely draw considerable attention from the community.

The primary concern regarding significance is the scalability and generalizability of the approach. The experiments are limited to the relatively small images of the CIFAR datasets. It is unclear how the computational cost and the performance gains would scale to larger-scale problems like ImageNet. Nonetheless, the paper presents a powerful proof of concept that is likely to be influential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** The core idea of using RL-trained feedback connections to modulate internal filters in a deep CNN is highly original and conceptually interesting.
2.  **Performance:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without data augmentation, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
3.  **Significance:** This work opens up a promising new research direction at the intersection of deep learning, reinforcement learning, and attention mechanisms.
4.  **Analysis:** The paper includes valuable analysis of the network's dynamics and the information content of the learned gate activations, going beyond simply reporting performance metrics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The training method is extremely computationally intensive, which may limit its practical applicability and the ability of others to build upon this work.
2.  **Methodological Justification:** The paper could provide a stronger justification for some methodological choices, particularly the use of SNES over other RL algorithms and the specific design of the state representation.
3.  **Scalability:** The experiments are confined to CIFAR, and it remains an open question how the approach scales to larger and more complex datasets.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and interesting contribution, backed by impressive empirical results. While there are some concerns about computational cost and clarity on certain methodological details, the strengths in originality and significance are substantial. I lean towards acceptance.
