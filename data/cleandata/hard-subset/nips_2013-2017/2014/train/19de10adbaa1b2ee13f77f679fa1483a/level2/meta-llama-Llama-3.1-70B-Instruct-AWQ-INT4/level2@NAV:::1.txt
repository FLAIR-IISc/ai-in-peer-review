This paper proposes a novel architecture called Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), which introduces feedback connections to traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to model selective attention. The authors demonstrate that dasNet can improve classification performance by iteratively focusing the attention of its filters on the most discriminative features in an image.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. The authors provide a clear explanation of the dasNet architecture and its components, including the feedback connections and the reinforcement learning algorithm used to train the policy.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise explanations. The authors provide sufficient details about the dasNet architecture, the experimental setup, and the results.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel approach to modeling selective attention in deep CNNs, which is a significant improvement over traditional feedforward architectures. The use of feedback connections and reinforcement learning to train the policy is a unique contribution.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate that dasNet can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, outperforming previous models on unaugmented data. The authors also provide insights into the dynamics of the dasNet policy, showing that it can learn to correct misclassifications and improve performance over time.

**Strengths**:

* Novel architecture that models selective attention in deep CNNs
* Improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets
* Insights into the dynamics of the dasNet policy

**Weaknesses**:

* The paper could benefit from more analysis of the dasNet policy and its behavior on different datasets
* The experimental setup could be more comprehensive, including more datasets and comparison to other state-of-the-art models
* The paper could provide more details about the computational resources required to train and evaluate dasNet

**Arguments for acceptance**:

* The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of deep learning
* The results demonstrate improved performance on challenging datasets
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow

**Arguments against acceptance**:

* The paper could benefit from more analysis and evaluation of the dasNet policy
* The experimental setup could be more comprehensive
* The paper could provide more details about the computational resources required to train and evaluate dasNet

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper, as it presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and the results demonstrate improved performance on challenging datasets. However, I suggest that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version of the paper.
