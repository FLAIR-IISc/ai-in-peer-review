## Review of Deep Networks with Internal Selective Attention through Feedback Connections

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture augmenting standard convolutional neural networks with feedback connections to enable dynamic filter sensitivity adjustments during classification. The core idea is inspired by the iterative, attention-driven processing observed in the human visual cortex, addressing the limitation of static, feedforward CNNs. The authors train these feedback connections via Scalable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high dimensionality of the control space. Results on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 demonstrate improved performance compared to prior state-of-the-art models on unaugmented datasets.

The paper clearly articulates the motivation for introducing feedback mechanisms, grounding it in neuroscientific observations and limitations of current CNNs. The use of SNES is well-justified given the scale of the policy parameters, and the experimental setup seems sound. Achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR, even without data augmentation, is a significant accomplishment and strengthens the claim of effectiveness. The visualization of filter activations and their changes during iterative processing, while limited, provides some insight into the network's behavior. The inclusion of a dynamic evaluation showcasing the network’s stability across multiple steps strengthens the study. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the related work section is extensive, it lacks a deeper engagement with recent advancements in attention mechanisms *within* deep learning (e.g., self-attention, attention gates) and how dasNet differs beyond the addition of recurrent connections. The practical implications of the increased computational cost due to the iterative processing are not adequately discussed. While SNES tackles the parameter space, its computational demands are significant (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti), which could limit scalability. Further, the explanation of *what* the network learns to attend to remains somewhat vague; simply showing activation changes doesn't provide a deep understanding of the learned policy. The use of Maxout networks, while justified in the context of the original work, feels somewhat dated and may limit the generalizability of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Introduces feedback connections trained via RL to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities.
*   Performance: Achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented).
*   Motivation: Grounded in neuroscientific principles and addresses a limitation of standard CNNs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited explanation of learned attention patterns.
*   Computational cost not fully addressed.
*   Dated architecture choice (Maxout).
*   Lack of comparative analysis with existing attention mechanisms.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising approach to incorporating attention into deep neural networks. While the current implementation has limitations, the core idea is innovative and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the missing comparison to modern attention mechanisms and a more in-depth discussion of the computational trade-offs. A more insightful analysis of *what* the network is learning to attend to, beyond simply showing activation maps, would also significantly strengthen the paper.
