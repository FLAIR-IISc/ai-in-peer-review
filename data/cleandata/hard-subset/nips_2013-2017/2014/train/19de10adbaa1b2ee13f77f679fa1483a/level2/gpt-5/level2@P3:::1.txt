Summary
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a modification of convolutional neural networks that introduces recurrent, top-down feedback to modulate internal feature activations over multiple passes on the same input. A base Maxout CNN is trained in the standard supervised manner. Afterward, a high-dimensional policy is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to output per-feature-map multiplicative gates at each pass, conditioned on a compact observation consisting of averaged map activations, pre-softmax activations, and current class probabilities. The policy aims to improve predictions for difficult cases by enhancing/suppressing selected features over T sequential steps. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (unaugmented), the method improves over the base Maxout network and is reported to surpass previous single-model results. Additional analyses probe stability when evaluating beyond the trained number of steps and demonstrate that the learned gates contain discriminative information.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to a long line of work on recurrent and top-down processing in biological vision, and to computational models that use recurrence or RL to guide attention or feedback (e.g., saccade selection, feature gating, iterative reconstruction). Relative to glimpse/saccade-based attention and biologically inspired feedback models, the contribution here is an internal, channel-wise attention mechanism over CNN feature maps, trained via black-box policy search at scale and evaluated on competitive vision benchmarks.

Quality
- The approach is technically coherent: gating is inserted at well-defined locations, the observation vector is reasonable, and the action normalization (softmax scaled by |A|) stabilizes magnitudes. Training isolates supervised learning of the base CNN from subsequent policy evolution.
- Experimental evidence shows consistent gains over the base model and stability of dynamics beyond the trained horizon. Qualitative visualizations provide insight into the modulation pattern.
- However, important experimental details are missing or insufficiently quantified: exact absolute error rates and standard deviations, comparisons against strong baselines with data augmentation and test-time ensembling, computational overhead (training and inference), and sensitivity to T, observation components, and policy capacity. Overfitting control for policy learning (validation protocol) is unclear.
- SNES is an appropriate choice for million-parameter policies, but the paper would benefit from more analysis on sample efficiency, convergence, and whether smaller/structured policies suffice.

Clarity
The high-level idea, algorithmic loop, and notational setup are generally clear. The training algorithm and observation/action definitions are spelled out, and the motivation is well framed. Minor issues include small typos and occasional notational inconsistencies (e.g., at vs. at+1 around Eq. 7), and missing architectural specifics (exact dims of O and A, layer/channel counts of the base model).

Originality
The combination of internal, feature-map–wise selective attention with learned top-down feedback, trained via large-scale NES and demonstrated on CIFAR-10/100, appears novel for the period. While gating and feedback are familiar ideas, their particular instantiation and optimization at this scale, with empirical gains on standard benchmarks, constitute a meaningful contribution.

Significance
If the reported improvements are robust, the work provides evidence that lightweight recurrent feedback on feature channels can complement feedforward CNNs and improve classification, potentially inspiring broader use of learned internal attention in CNNs. The approach is generic and may transfer to other architectures and datasets. The inference-time cost grows linearly with T; understanding the accuracy–compute trade-off would help assess practical impact.

Strengths
- Clear and biologically motivated formulation of top-down feedback for CNNs.
- Scalable policy optimization via SNES for million-parameter control.
- Demonstrated improvements on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), with qualitative analyses and a stability study across steps.
- Simple mechanism (channel-wise gating) that integrates cleanly with existing CNNs.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete reporting of experimental details: absolute errors, variance, computational cost, and validation protocol.
- Limited ablations: no breakdown of which observation components matter, policy capacity/structure, or placement of gates.
- Evaluation limited to unaugmented data; lack of comparison to contemporary strong baselines with augmentation or simple test-time ensembling.
- The policy appears linear in the observation; the representational adequacy of such a policy is not discussed.
- Potential overfitting of the policy due to training solely on the supervised training set without a clear early stopping criterion.

Requested clarifications
- Exact test error rates (with confidence intervals) for base Maxout and dasNet on CIFAR-10/100; details of the “previous state-of-the-art” configuration used for comparison.
- Whether data augmentation was also evaluated; if not, why, and how results compare to augmented single models and committees.
- Full specification of O and A dimensions, policy structure (linear vs multi-layer), and gate placement per layer.
- Validation procedure and stopping criteria for SNES; measures taken to prevent overfitting.
- Training and inference costs (relative to the base model) and the accuracy–compute trade-off as a function of T.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Report precise performance numbers with variability, include comparisons to augmented baselines and simple ensembles, and quantify computational overhead.
- Provide ablations on T, policy capacity, observation components, and gate placement to isolate what drives gains.
- Describe the validation/early-stopping protocol for policy learning to address overfitting concerns.

Optional:
- Explore structured or lower-rank policies (e.g., per-layer or grouped gates) to reduce parameter count and improve sample efficiency.
- Compare SNES to gradient-based policy learning where feasible, or hybrid approaches.
- Extend to spatially selective actions or attention masks and assess incremental benefit.
- Add more systematic visualizations linking gate changes to class disambiguation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated integration of feedback-based attention into CNNs.
- Demonstrated improvements on standard benchmarks with a simple, general mechanism.
- Scalable optimization of high-dimensional policies, potentially useful beyond this application.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently detailed empirical evaluation and lack of comparisons to strong augmented baselines make it hard to assess true impact.
- Limited ablation and analysis of what aspects of the design are critical.
- Practicality concerns (compute overhead) are not quantified.

Overall, the idea is promising and relevant, and with stronger empirical rigor and comparisons, the contribution could be significant.
