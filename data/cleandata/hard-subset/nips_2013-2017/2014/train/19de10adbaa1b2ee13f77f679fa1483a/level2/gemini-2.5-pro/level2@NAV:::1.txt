This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that augments a standard feedforward convolutional neural network (CNN) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable the network to perform multiple, sequential passes on a single image, iteratively refining its classification by dynamically modulating the activity of its internal convolutional filters. This internal "selective attention" mechanism is framed as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem, where a policy network learns to enhance or suppress filter activations based on the network's state from the previous pass. This approach is biologically motivated by the recurrent and top-down processing observed in the primate visual cortex. The authors use a Scalable Natural Evolution Strategy (SNES) to train the policy, citing the high dimensionality of the parameter space as a challenge for traditional RL algorithms.

The strengths of the paper are significant:

*   **Originality and Significance:** The concept of an internal, learned attentional mechanism that modulates filter sensitivities over time is highly novel and presents a compelling departure from purely feedforward models. It introduces a form of "computational thinking" for difficult classification problems, which could inspire a new class of dynamic and adaptive network architectures.
*   **Quality:** The claims are well-supported by strong empirical results. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets (for unaugmented data), which validates the effectiveness of their approach. The analysis of the learned gate activations, confirming they contain class-relevant information, adds further credibility.

The paper also has some weaknesses that could be addressed:

*   **Clarity and Justification:** The choice of SNES is justified by the large parameter space, but the paper would be strengthened by a comparison or at least a more detailed discussion regarding why more conventional policy gradient methods were not used. It is unclear if they are computationally infeasible or simply perform poorly.
*   **Evaluation:** The qualitative analysis of filter changes is interesting but remains speculative, as the authors note. A more quantitative analysis could be beneficial. For instance, does the network's confidence consistently improve over the T steps, especially on samples that were initially misclassified?
*   **Scalability:** The computational cost is a major drawback, requiring four days of training on top of the pre-trained model. A discussion of the scalability to larger datasets like ImageNet and the trade-off between performance gain and computational overhead would be valuable.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting an exciting and well-executed idea. The weaknesses are primarily opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws. The proposed dasNet architecture is a significant contribution that advances the field by successfully integrating feedback and reinforcement learning into modern deep networks.
