- Introduces dasNet: CNNs with feedback connections that iteratively modulate filter sensitivities via a learned reinforcement-learning policy.  
- Policy is evolved with Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to very high-dimensional parameter space (>1M parameters).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, reporting a 6% relative improvement over the Maxout baseline on unaugmented data.  
- Provides a compelling biological motivation linking feedback/attention to human visual refinement.  
- Visualizations (e.g., ambiguous cat/dog cases) intuitively show dynamic adjustment of feature importance.  
- Technical rationale for using SNES over standard RL is reasonable given policy scale.  
- Strength: clear demonstration of a functional internal attention mechanism addressing limits of stationary CNNs.  
- Experimental setup isolates attention’s contribution to ambiguous cases and uses boosting to focus on misclassified samples.  
- Novelty is unclear: paper insufficiently differentiates dasNet from prior RL-based attention and recurrent-processing work.  
- Missing citations/analysis of recent related work (NIPS 2013–2014) that may cover similar ideas.  
- Reproducibility issues: key methodological details omitted (exact observation vector dimensionality, precise action space size, and why T=5).  
- Ambiguity in implementation: unclear whether action a^ℓ_j scales activations before or after convolution (Equation 5).  
- Significance limited: modest performance gain and unsubstantiated "state-of-the-art" claims without comparison to contemporaneous models (e.g., GoogLeNet, VGG).  
- Practical concern: inference-time overhead from multiple passes (~5× slower for T=5) is not discussed.  
- Clarity mostly good but some vague phrasing and lack of quantitative justification for observation features’ contributions.  
- Recommended fixes: explicitly contrast dasNet with prior RL/recurrent methods, provide exact observation/action dimensions and ablations, compare to modern baselines, and discuss computational trade-offs.  
- Overall: promising mechanism and results, but insufficient novelty framing, missing methodological detail, and unverified significance prevent it from being a high-impact contribution as-is.
