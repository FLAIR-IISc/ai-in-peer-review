This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that incorporates feedback connections into deep convolutional neural networks to enable dynamic filter sensitivity adjustment during classification. The core idea is to allow the network to iteratively focus its attention on relevant features, mimicking aspects of human visual processing. The attentional policy controlling these connections is learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, drawing parallels between the static nature of traditional CNNs and the dynamic, feedback-driven processing in the human visual system. The problem addressed—improving robustness to difficult classification instances—is relevant and the proposed solution, dasNet, is conceptually interesting. The use of SNES for policy learning is also a notable contribution, given the high dimensionality of the policy space. The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance improvement over baseline models, and the visualization of filter activations provides some insight into the network’s attentional mechanism. The paper adequately references relevant work in CNNs, reinforcement learning, and visual attention.

However, there are several weaknesses. Firstly, the empirical evaluation, while showing improvement, could be more thorough. The comparison is primarily to a single baseline (Maxout network) and doesn't fully explore the performance landscape against more recent, advanced CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet) even without augmentation. A more robust benchmark would increase confidence in the claimed superiority. Secondly, while the visualization of filter activations offers a glimpse into the attentional mechanism, it lacks a comprehensive quantitative analysis. It would be beneficial to measure the correlation between the learned attention weights and the saliency of corresponding features in the input images. Also, how does the choice of T (number of passes) impact performance and computational cost? This is not thoroughly investigated. Thirdly, the reliance on SNES, while justified due to the parameter space, makes the training process computationally expensive (4 days on a GTX 560 Ti). A discussion of potential strategies for improving training efficiency would be valuable. Finally, the connection to related work on recurrent neural networks with attention mechanisms (e.g., those employing LSTMs or GRUs) is relatively weak. The authors position dasNet as novel, but a more detailed comparison with these existing approaches, and highlighting the unique contributions of dasNet’s feedback-based approach, is warranted.

Specifically, the "information content" measurement via k-NN and logistic regression feels tacked on and lacks sufficient justification. Why these methods specifically? What does the resulting 40-45% accuracy actually *mean* in the context of the full network performance?

**Strengths:**

*   Novel architecture incorporating feedback connections for dynamic filter adjustment.
*   Effective use of SNES for policy learning in a high-dimensional space.
*   State-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
*   Clear motivation based on biological plausibility and limitations of feedforward CNNs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited comparison to modern CNN architectures beyond the Maxout network.
*   Lack of quantitative analysis of the attentional mechanism.
*   High computational cost of SNES training.
*   Insufficient discussion of related work on recurrent attention mechanisms.
*   Weak justification for the "information content" measurement.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and conceptually sound approach to improving CNN performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a statistically significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The use of SNES is a clever solution to the challenges of policy learning in a high-dimensional space.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient robustness.
*   The paper lacks a detailed quantitative analysis of the attentional mechanism.
*   The computational cost of training is a significant barrier to wider adoption.
*   The connection to related work could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field. However, several weaknesses need to be addressed before acceptance. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for major revisions. The authors should: 1) Expand the empirical evaluation to include comparisons to more recent CNN architectures; 2) Provide a more rigorous quantitative analysis of the attentional mechanism; 3) Discuss strategies for improving training efficiency; and 4) Strengthen the connection to related work on recurrent attention mechanisms. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the paper's impact and credibility.
