Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment a trained feedforward CNN (specifically a Maxout network) with internal, learned feedback that multiplicatively gates feature-map activations over multiple passes on the same image. The gating policy takes as input low-dimensional summaries of intermediate activations (per-map averages in conv layers, classification pre-activations, and class probabilities) and outputs layer-wise, feature-map-wise scaling factors whose mean is constrained to 1 via a scaled softmax. The policy is trained after supervised training of the base CNN using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), motivated by the high dimensionality of the observation/action spaces and hence of the policy parameter vector (~10^6). On CIFAR-10/100, with T iterative passes (typically T=5), dasNet reports improvements over the base CNN and claims state-of-the-art among single models on unaugmented data.

The work connects to a long line of research on recurrent processing and top-down modulation in biological vision (e.g., Felleman & Van Essen; Lamme & Roelfsema) and to computational attention/recurrence in vision, including RL-based selective attention, recurrence for recognition and reconstruction, and modern CNN variants (Maxout, dropout). Relative to prior CNN work at NIPS/ICML circa 2013–2014 (Maxout, NIN, stochastic pooling), the key novelty is learned internal feedback that alters feature-map sensitivities at test time to iteratively refine predictions, trained by black-box policy search rather than backprop.

Quality
Strengths:
- The mechanism is simple, generic, and easy to integrate with existing CNNs: multiplicative per-map gates controlled by a policy.
- The experimental results on CIFAR-10/100 show consistent gains over the same base CNN; the paper also provides a qualitative example and a stability study of running more steps than trained (dynamics remain stable).
- The “information in gates” analysis (classifying from final gate values) suggests the policy learns nontrivial class-relevant structure.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification:
- Baselines and ablations are limited. Important comparisons that seem feasible include:
  - A backprop-through-time (BPTT) baseline that trains the gating policy with gradients (the whole system is differentiable if the softmax policy is a standard linear/nonlinear layer), versus the SNES approach. The claim that standard RL is impractical due to parameter count does not address BPTT, which is the default for differentiable recurrent models.
  - A simpler linear policy (no softmax, mean constraint by normalization) and/or a shallow MLP policy. The current policy appears to be a single linear layer with softmax; why is this architecture chosen, and is it sufficient?
  - Using only class probabilities as observations vs. including per-map averages and pre-activations; which components matter?
  - Per-layer gating vs. only gating the last conv layer; what is the contribution of lower-layer gates?
  - Multi-pass without learned feedback (e.g., T passes with a=1) or confidence calibration/temperature scaling as a trivial “refinement” baseline.
- Reporting and comparability:
  - The abstract claims SOTA on “unaugmented datasets,” but Section 6 states that the base Maxout net was trained with data augmentation. Please clarify training/test-time augmentation and compare against contemporary single-model baselines with matching augmentation protocols (e.g., Maxout, DropConnect, NIN, stochastic pooling).
  - Provide exact error rates with standard deviations over multiple runs, and confusion-matrix improvements on the “ambiguous” pairs to substantiate the targeted benefit.
- Computational cost:
  - Test-time cost scales by T forward passes plus a lightweight policy evaluation. Please report wall-clock slowdowns and FLOPs, and discuss the accuracy–compute trade-off versus simply enlarging the base CNN for a similar compute budget.
  - Training cost of the policy (population size 50, 4 days on GTX 560 Ti) is noted; more details on sample efficiency (generations, evaluations) would help assess practicality.
- Objective design:
  - The loss uses only the final step and boosts misclassified samples. Have you tried auxiliary losses at intermediate steps (encouraging monotonic improvement), or curriculum on λmisclassified?
  - Do gates saturate or exhibit oscillations? Some distributional statistics of a over layers and steps would be informative.
- Reproducibility:
  - Please specify the exact observation and action dimensionalities (number of maps per layer, inclusion of biases in the policy), SNES hyperparameters (learning rates, noise scale), and stopping criteria.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The pseudocode is helpful. Some ambiguities remain (policy architecture details, dimensionalities, augmentation protocol, precise baselines), and a figure summarizing the observation/action interfaces (sizes per layer) would aid reproducibility. The qualitative figure is useful; adding per-layer gate histograms over the test set would improve interpretability.

Originality
Introducing learned, internal, per-feature-map selective attention via feedback to a modern CNN and training the controller by high-dimensional NES is a novel and interesting combination. While recurrent and attention mechanisms have precedents, applying global feature-map gating to refine predictions in multiple passes and demonstrating improvements on CIFAR-10/100 with a state-of-the-art base model is original for the NeurIPS 2014 timeframe.

Significance
The approach is broadly applicable to CNNs and offers a path to iterative refinement without changing the base feature extractor. If validated with stronger baselines and clearer compute–accuracy trade-offs, this could influence work on recurrent/feedback CNNs and attention. The gains reported are modest but meaningful; demonstrating on larger-scale datasets or tasks (e.g., ImageNet, detection/segmentation) would further increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean mechanism for internal attention with measurable gains.
- Bridges biologically inspired feedback with modern CNNs.
- Demonstrates stability over multiple steps and provides some analyses beyond headline numbers.
- Potentially general method applicable to other architectures and tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (BPTT-trained feedback, ablations) leave it unclear whether SNES and the specific design choices are necessary.
- Limited reporting on compute cost and comparisons under matched budgets.
- Ambiguity about augmentation and SOTA claims; results need clearer context.
- Evaluation confined to CIFAR; impact would be stronger with larger-scale evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations listed above, especially a gradient-trained recurrent/gating baseline.
- Clarify augmentation protocols and compare to contemporary single-model SOTA under matched settings.
- Report compute overhead and analyze accuracy vs. T and vs. base-model size.
- Detail policy/observation dimensionalities and SNES settings; release code if possible.
- Provide broader quantitative analyses (per-class confusions, error typology) to support the selective-attention narrative.

Overall assessment
Promising and novel idea with encouraging results, but the empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines and clearer reporting to firmly establish the benefit and necessity of the proposed components. I lean toward borderline accept if the authors can address the core comparison and clarity issues; otherwise, it is a solid submission that could benefit from additional experimentation.
