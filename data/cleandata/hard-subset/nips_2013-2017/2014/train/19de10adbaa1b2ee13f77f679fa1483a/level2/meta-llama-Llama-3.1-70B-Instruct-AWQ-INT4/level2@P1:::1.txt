**Summary**

This paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a novel architecture that integrates internal selective attention through feedback connections into deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors propose a non-stationary CNN that can adapt its behavior post-training, allowing it to iteratively focus its attention on the most discriminative features in an image. The attentional policy is learned through reinforcement learning using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results on unaugmented data.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel architecture**: The paper proposes a new architecture that integrates internal selective attention into deep CNNs, which is a significant departure from traditional feedforward CNNs.
2. **Effective use of reinforcement learning**: The authors use SNES to learn the attentional policy, which is a scalable and efficient method for optimizing large parameter spaces.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: DasNet achieves state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
4. **Well-written and organized**: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise explanations.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational cost**: The proposed architecture requires significant computational resources, which may limit its applicability to larger datasets or more complex tasks.
2. **Limited analysis**: The paper provides limited analysis of the learned attentional policies and their impact on the classification performance.
3. **No comparison to other attention mechanisms**: The authors do not compare dasNet to other attention mechanisms, which would provide a more comprehensive understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its components. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are well-presented. However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the learned attentional policies and their impact on the classification performance.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise explanations. The authors provide a good balance of technical details and high-level explanations, making the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel architecture that integrates internal selective attention into deep CNNs, which is a significant departure from traditional feedforward CNNs. The use of SNES to learn the attentional policy is also a new contribution.

**Significance**

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of dasNet on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results on unaugmented data. The proposed architecture has the potential to improve the performance of deep CNNs on a wide range of tasks, particularly those that require selective attention.
