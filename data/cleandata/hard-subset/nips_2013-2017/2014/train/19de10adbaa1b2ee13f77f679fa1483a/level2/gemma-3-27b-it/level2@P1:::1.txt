This paper introduces dasNet, a deep convolutional neural network architecture augmented with feedback connections, aiming to mimic the iterative and attentional processing observed in the human visual cortex. The core idea is to allow the network to dynamically adjust its convolutional filter sensitivities post-training using a policy learned via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES). The authors demonstrate improved performance on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets compared to existing state-of-the-art models on unaugmented data.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in CNNs (LeCun, 1989; Ciresan et al., 2012), Maxout networks (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 1998). The motivation, drawing parallels to human visual processing (Felleman & van Essen, 1991; Lamme & Roelfsema, 2000), is well articulated. The connection to related work in recurrent processing (Wierstra, 2006; Knoll & de Freitas, 2014) and attention mechanisms (Schmidhuber et al., 2015; Denil et al., 2017) is adequately established, although the discussion could benefit from further contextualization of how dasNet differentiates itself from these previous approaches (particularly in terms of the specific feedback mechanism and learning strategy).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of learning a policy to modulate filter activations *after* initial supervised training is interesting and represents a departure from traditional, static CNN architectures. The use of SNES to tackle the high-dimensional policy space is a practical and clever solution.
*   **Performance:** The reported performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are encouraging, demonstrating the potential of this approach. Establishing a new state-of-the-art on unaugmented data is a significant achievement.
*   **Analysis and Visualization:** The visualization of filter activations and their changes during iterative processing (Figure 3) provides some insight into the network's behavior, albeit limited. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) suggests stable performance even with longer iterative processing.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the CIFAR datasets are standard benchmarks, the paper lacks an evaluation on more complex and realistic datasets (e.g., ImageNet). This limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Policy Interpretation:** The learned policy is a "black box." More effort should be made to understand *why* certain filters are emphasized or de-emphasized in specific cases. While the visualization helps, it’s difficult to discern meaningful patterns without further analysis. What specific features or image characteristics trigger particular filter adjustments?
*   **Computational Cost:** The training process takes 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti GPU, excluding the time for initial Maxout network training. This is a significant computational burden, which may hinder adoption. A discussion of the computational trade-offs and potential optimizations would be valuable.
*   **SNES Sensitivity:** The authors do not thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the SNES algorithm to hyperparameters (e.g., population size, learning rate). A more comprehensive sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the results.
*   **Comparison to Attention Mechanisms:** The paper doesn't deeply compare to more recent attention mechanisms commonly employed in CNNs (e.g. Squeeze-and-Excitation). A comparison would help contextualize dasNet's contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving CNN performance through feedback connections and reinforcement learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance improvement on standard benchmarks.
*   The use of SNES to address the high-dimensional policy space is a practical and effective solution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The analysis is somewhat limited, lacking evaluation on more complex datasets and a thorough understanding of the learned policy.
*   The high computational cost may limit the practicality of the approach.
*   The connection to more modern attention mechanisms isn’t thoroughly explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and promising approach to enhancing CNNs with feedback and attention mechanisms. While the analysis is somewhat limited and the computational cost is a concern, the novelty and demonstrated performance improvements warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, specifically addressing the need for more in-depth analysis of the learned policy and a discussion of computational trade-offs. Exploring comparison to existing attention mechanisms would also strengthen the contribution.
