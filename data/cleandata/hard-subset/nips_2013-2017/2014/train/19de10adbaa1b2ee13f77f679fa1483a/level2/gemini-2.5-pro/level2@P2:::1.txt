This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with feedback connections to enable a form of internal, selective attention. The core idea is that after an initial, rapid feedforward pass, the network can perform several additional processing steps on the same image. In these subsequent steps, a learned control policy modulates the activity of convolutional filters, effectively emphasizing or suppressing certain features to improve classification, particularly for ambiguous cases.

The proposed method involves a two-stage training process. First, a strong baseline CNN (a Maxout network) is trained via standard supervised learning. Afterwards, this network is augmented with multiplicative gates on its convolutional layer outputs. A policy, which maps the internal state of the network to gate values, is then trained using reinforcement learning (RL) to minimize a classification loss. Due to the very high dimensionality of the policy parameter space (over 1 million), the authors use Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), a black-box optimization algorithm, for the policy search. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that this attentional mechanism improves classification accuracy over the baseline model, achieving state-of-the-art results on the unaugmented datasets at the time.

**Quality**
The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The motivation for moving beyond purely feedforward processing is well-argued, drawing inspiration from the recurrent and attentional processing in the human visual system. The proposed mechanism—using a learned policy to modulate filter gains—is a concrete and plausible way to implement this idea. The choice to use SNES for policy optimization is a pragmatic solution to the challenge of learning in a million-dimensional parameter space, where traditional RL methods might struggle.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The authors demonstrate a clear performance improvement on two standard, challenging benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100). The analysis of the network's dynamics, showing that performance peaks at the trained number of steps (`T`) and remains stable thereafter, is a good validation of the learned policy. The experiment confirming that the final gate activations contain significant class-relevant information further supports the claim that the policy is learning a meaningful strategy.

One weakness is that the analysis of *what* the policy learns is still somewhat high-level. The authors rightly note that visualizing and understanding high-level features is an open problem. However, a more in-depth analysis could strengthen the paper. For instance, are there patterns in which filters are modulated for specific, commonly confused class pairs (e.g., cats vs. dogs)? Averaging the policy's actions across many such ambiguous images might reveal systematic strategies.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and the main ideas are presented clearly. Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 effectively illustrate the overall process. The motivation and connection to related work are well-articulated.

However, the manuscript could be improved by providing more specific implementation details to aid reproducibility.
-   The precise architecture of the base Maxout network (number of filters, kernel sizes, etc.) is not specified.
-   The dimensionality of the observation vector `o_t` and the action vector `a` are not given. These numbers are crucial for understanding the scale of the RL problem and the size of the policy matrix `θ`.
-   The abstract claims dasNet "outperforms the previous state-of-the-art model on unaugmented datasets," but the baseline performance of the vanilla Maxout network used in the experiments is not reported. Providing this number would make the claimed 6% relative improvement more concrete.

**Originality**
The work is highly original. While the ideas of recurrence and attention in vision are not new, this paper is one of the first to successfully integrate them into a state-of-the-art deep learning model for object recognition. The concept of learning a policy for *internal* attention (modulating existing filters) rather than *external* attention (e.g., learning saccades to parts of an image) is particularly novel. The combination of a pre-trained deep network with policy search via an evolution strategy is also an innovative approach. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work, which often focused on simpler models or datasets.

**Significance**
The paper's contribution is significant. It presents a successful departure from the purely feedforward paradigm that dominated deep learning for vision at the time. The results demonstrate that sequential, dynamic processing can demonstrably improve upon strong baselines, opening up a promising avenue for future research. The work likely inspired subsequent research on attention mechanisms, recurrent models for vision, and the integration of reinforcement learning with deep supervised models. The ideas presented are general and could be applied to other architectures and domains beyond image classification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and well-motivated architecture that incorporates feedback and internal attention into deep CNNs.
*   **Strong Results:** Achieves state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10/100 (unaugmented), demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   **Significance:** Opens up a new research direction by showing the benefits of sequential processing and learned dynamic adjustments within deep networks.
*   **Soundness:** The technical approach is sound, and the experimental methodology is solid.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Reproducibility:** Key details of the network architecture and the RL setup are missing, which would make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results exactly.
*   **Analysis Depth:** The analysis of the learned policy's behavior is preliminary. A deeper investigation could provide more insight into why the method works.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a novel idea, solid execution, and significant results. The weaknesses related to clarity and analysis depth are minor and could be addressed in a final version. The work represents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
