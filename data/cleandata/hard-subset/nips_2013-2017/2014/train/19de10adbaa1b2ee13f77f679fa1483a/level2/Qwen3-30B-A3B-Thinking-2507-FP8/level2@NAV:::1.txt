

This paper proposes dasNet, a novel deep network architecture incorporating feedback connections to enable internal selective attention during classification. The core claim is that dynamically adjusting filter sensitivities via a policy learned through Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) improves performance on ambiguous cases in CIFAR-10/100, achieving a new state-of-the-art for unaugmented datasets. 

Strengths include a compelling neuroscience-inspired motivation for iterative attention mechanisms and solid experimental validation on standard benchmarks. The ablation studies (e.g., Figure 2) effectively demonstrate the stability of the approach across varying interaction steps (T=1 to T=9), and the policy's information content is credibly verified via gate-value classification (40.7% 15-NN accuracy). The use of SNES to handle the million-dimensional policy space is pragmatically justified given the limitations of conventional RL for such high-dimensional control.

However, the work suffers from significant limitations in novelty and significance. The core idea of using feedback for selective attention is not new—prior work (e.g., O’Reilly et al., 2013; Denil et al., 2014) has explored recurrent processing and attention mechanisms in similar contexts. Crucially, dasNet is evaluated against Maxout networks (a 2013 architecture), not recent SOTA models (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet), making the claimed "state-of-the-art" misleading. The 6% relative improvement over Maxout is modest compared to the 15-20% gains typical in modern CNN advances on CIFAR-100. Additionally, the paper fails to clarify how dasNet’s feedback mechanism differs from established attention modules (e.g., SENet, CBAM), which also modulate feature importance. 

The computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) is substantial without addressing scalability, and the qualitative analysis of filter changes (Figure 3) lacks causal evidence linking attention adjustments to accuracy gains. While the method is technically sound, the significance is undermined by insufficient comparison to current literature and the absence of a clear, novel contribution beyond incremental improvement over outdated baselines. 

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) benchmark against contemporary architectures (e.g., ResNet-50), (2) clarify the distinction from existing attention mechanisms, and (3) provide a more rigorous analysis of when/why the feedback mechanism succeeds. As presented, the novelty is marginal and the significance limited, warranting rejection.
