

This paper proposes DasNet, a novel architecture that introduces feedback connections to enable internal selective attention in deep CNNs. The core idea is to dynamically modulate convolutional filter sensitivities across multiple passes over an image using a policy evolved via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES), rather than relying solely on feedforward processing. The method builds on Maxout networks, with attention gates (a^ℓ_j) controlling filter weights during iterative passes, and uses SNES to handle the high-dimensional policy space (over 1 million parameters). Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 show a 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks on unaugmented data, with qualitative analysis demonstrating that the network corrects misclassifications (e.g., distinguishing cat/dog) by selectively enhancing/de-emphasizing filters across layers.

**Strengths:**  
The motivation from neuroscience (e.g., top-down feedback in visual cortex) is compelling and well-justified. The technical approach to handle the large policy space via SNES is pragmatic and well-motivated, addressing a genuine bottleneck in applying RL to CNNs. The results on CIFAR-10/100 are significant—establishing a new SOTA for unaugmented data—and the ablation studies (e.g., dynamics for varying T, gate-value classification) provide strong evidence that the attention mechanism is functional and meaningful. The visualization in Figure 3 effectively illustrates how filter activations change across passes to resolve ambiguous cases, which is a valuable contribution to interpretability.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Novelty vs. Related Work:** While the *implementation* of feedback via SNES is novel, the *concept* of iterative refinement with feedback is not entirely new. Prior work (e.g., [48,49] on recurrent image reconstruction, [40,41] on RL for selective attention) demonstrates similar ideas, though often on smaller tasks. The paper underemphasizes how DasNet differs from these—e.g., it does not compare against iterative methods like [48] on CIFAR-10/100. The claim of "state-of-the-art" should clarify it is relative to *unmodified* Maxout networks, not the broader SOTA (e.g., ResNet was emerging in 2014).  
2. **Methodology Rigor:** The loss function (eq 8) uses a heuristic "boosting" term (λ_misclassified > λ_correct) without ablation to justify its design. It would strengthen the paper to test alternative weighting schemes or show sensitivity to λ values. Additionally, the SNES implementation details (e.g., population size p=50, Σ diagonal approximation) lack justification—why not compare against standard NES or simpler RL baselines (e.g., policy gradients on a reduced policy space)?  
3. **Clarity Issues:** Algorithm 1 is confusing (e.g., "a^0 ⇐ 1" as initial action, but equation 5 suggests a^ℓ_j modifies weights). The observation space (h(M_t)) is vaguely defined—how are "average activations" computed? The paper states the policy is deterministic but uses softmax (eq 7), which is inconsistent.  

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- Explicitly contrast DasNet with iterative methods like [48] (e.g., "Our method scales to SOTA CNNs where [48] used simpler models").  
- Add a small ablation: Test DasNet with T=1 (no feedback) vs. T=5 to quantify the value of multiple passes.  
- Clarify the policy design: If the action space is continuous (a^ℓ_j ∈ ℝ), why use softmax? Suggest using a linear layer + tanh for bounded outputs.  
- Discuss computational cost more thoroughly: 4 days on a GTX 560 Ti for training DasNet is substantial—how does this compare to training a larger ensemble?  

**Overall Assessment:**  
DasNet presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to incorporating internal attention via feedback, with meaningful empirical results on CIFAR. The core idea is plausible and aligns with biological insights, and the results are convincing for the task. However, the novelty is somewhat overstated relative to existing literature on iterative processing, and the methodology would benefit from more rigorous baselines and clearer technical exposition. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on *enhancing* existing CNNs rather than designing new architectures, and the interpretability insights (e.g., layer-specific filter changes) are particularly strong.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. The work is high-quality, addresses a meaningful gap in CNN design, and delivers robust results. While the novelty claims could be tempered, the technical contribution and empirical validation are sufficient for NIPS. With minor revisions (addressing the above points), this would be a strong paper.
