- Proposal: dasNet adds feedback connections trained via SNES to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities, claiming "internal selective attention" yields improved classification on ambiguous CIFAR-10/100 cases (84.2% reported).  
- Strength: Neuroscience-inspired motivation (feedback in visual cortex) is well-grounded and Figure 3 convincingly visualizes attention dynamics.  
- Strength: Use of SNES for high-dimensional policy learning is technically appropriate and the experiments focus on ambiguous cases where the method should help.  
- Major weakness: Inadequate baselines and misleading SOTA claim — paper omits comparison to true 2012/2013 SOTA (e.g., Ciresan et al. 85.2% with augmentation), making the claimed improvement marginal.  
- Major weakness: Flawed experimental justification — the boosting loss heavily up-weights misclassified samples (λ ratio ≈200) without explanation or ablation, likely biasing results.  
- Major weakness: Small absolute performance gain (0.7% on CIFAR-10) with no statistical significance reported.  
- Major weakness: Methodological ambiguities — the observation vector h(M_t) and how activations are concatenated across layers are poorly defined, hurting reproducibility.  
- Major weakness: Training dynamics unclear — policy trained for T=5 but performance stable beyond T=2, raising questions about generalization vs. overfitting to T.  
- Major weakness: Overstated originality — misses and misrepresents prior feedback/iterative works (e.g., ICML/NIPS papers) and claims no RL applied to state-of-the-art nets incorrectly.  
- Minor issue: "Information content" experiment (15-NN/logistic on gates) lacks baselines and the 40.7% claimed result is unconvincing.  
- Minor issue: Computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) is reported without context or comparison to base network training time.  
- Minor issue: Inconsistency between text and Table 1 referencing CIFAR-100 vs. CIFAR-10.  
- Overall assessment: Idea is plausible and motivated, but evidence is insufficient due to missing comparisons, unexplained hyperparameters, and methodological gaps; contribution is incremental.  
- Suggestions: Compare against Ciresan and other 2013 SOTA, justify or ablate λ parameters, clarify the observation/policy input, and correct SOTA/related-work claims.  
- Recommendation: Reject — incremental work with unverified claims and insufficient evidence.
