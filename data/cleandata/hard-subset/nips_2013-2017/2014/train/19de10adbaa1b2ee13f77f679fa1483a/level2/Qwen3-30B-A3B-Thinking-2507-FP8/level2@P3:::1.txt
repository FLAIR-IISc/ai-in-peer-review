

The paper introduces Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), which augment standard convolutional networks with feedback connections to enable iterative selective attention during classification. The core idea is to allow a trained CNN to refine its internal feature processing through a reinforcement learning policy that modulates filter sensitivities across multiple passes over the same input. The policy is evolved using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) due to the high-dimensional parameter space (over 1 million parameters), and the approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, where it achieves a 6% relative improvement over the baseline Maxout network on unaugmented data.

The paper demonstrates a compelling biological motivation for feedback connections, drawing parallels to human visual processing where attentional mechanisms refine initial classifications. The experimental results on ambiguous cases (e.g., misclassified cat/dog images) provide intuitive evidence that dasNet dynamically adjusts feature importance, as visualized in Figure 3. The method’s technical justification for using SNES over conventional RL approaches is reasonable given the scale of the policy space, and the incremental improvement over the baseline is statistically significant on the test sets.

The primary strength lies in the clear demonstration of a functional attention mechanism within a deep network, addressing a gap in stationary CNNs. The work is technically sound, with well-structured experiments that isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism to ambiguous cases. The use of boosting (via λboost) to focus on misclassified samples is a practical touch that aligns with the goal of refining difficult predictions without catastrophic forgetting.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper’s novelty and significance claims. The core idea of using recurrent feedback for attention refinement is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. Related work cites RL-based attention methods [40–45] and recurrent image processing [48–50], but the paper fails to clarify how dasNet’s approach differs from these. For example, [48] uses recurrent connections for image reconstruction, while [40] learns saccades with RL—dasNet’s focus on *internal filter modulation* during classification should be explicitly contrasted. Without this, the novelty appears overstated, as the paper does not cite or analyze recent NIPS work (e.g., 2013–2014 papers on recurrent networks) that may have addressed similar concepts.

Methodological details are insufficient for reproducibility. Algorithm 1 describes the training process but omits critical specifications: the dimensionality of the observation vector (e.g., how many features are concatenated from the Maxout layers), the exact size of the action space (beyond "≈1000 dimensions"), and the rationale for fixed T=5. The paper claims "filter sensitivities" are altered, but Equation 5 does not clarify whether a^ℓ_j scales the activation *before* or *after* convolution, which is essential for understanding the mechanism. The absence of these details hinders evaluation of the technical contribution.

The significance is limited by the modest performance gain (6% relative) and lack of broader context. The claim of "state-of-the-art" on CIFAR-10/100 is unsubstantiated without comparison to contemporary models (e.g., GoogLeNet [5], which was published in 2014 and outperformed Maxout networks). The paper’s baseline (Maxout) was already surpassed by other architectures by 2014; the results should be contextualized against these to justify the improvement. Additionally, the inference-time overhead (5× slower due to T=5 passes) is not discussed, raising practical concerns for real-world deployment.

Clarity is generally good, but the writing occasionally sacrifices precision for brevity. For instance, the explanation of the observation vector (Section 4) lists components but does not quantify their contribution to the policy’s decision-making. The phrase "these values should still be meaningful enough" is vague and unverified; empirical evidence (e.g., ablation studies on observation features) would strengthen this claim.

To improve, the authors should: (1) clarify the distinction between dasNet and prior RL-based attention methods in a dedicated subsection, citing relevant NIPS 2013–2014 papers; (2) provide precise details on the observation/action space dimensions and their impact on performance; (3) compare against more recent baselines (e.g., GoogLeNet, VGG) to validate the "state-of-the-art" claim; and (4) discuss the computational trade-offs of iterative processing. These revisions would address the novelty gap and contextualize the significance more effectively.

Overall, the work presents a plausible mechanism for internal attention refinement with promising experimental results. However, the lack of clear differentiation from existing approaches, insufficient methodological detail, and unverified significance claims prevent it from meeting the bar for a high-impact NIPS contribution. With the suggested revisions, the paper could make a stronger case for its novelty and practical relevance.
