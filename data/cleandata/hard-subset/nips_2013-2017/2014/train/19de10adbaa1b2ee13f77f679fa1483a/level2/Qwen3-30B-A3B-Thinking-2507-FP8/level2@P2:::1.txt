

This paper proposes dasNet, a deep network architecture that introduces feedback connections trained via Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to dynamically adjust filter sensitivities during classification. The core claim is that this "internal selective attention" mechanism improves performance on ambiguous CIFAR-10/100 cases by iteratively refining feature importance, achieving a 6% relative improvement over vanilla Maxout networks. 

**Strengths:** The motivation from neuroscience (feedback connections in visual cortex) is well-grounded, and the visualization in Figure 3 effectively demonstrates attention dynamics during classification refinement. The implementation of SNES for high-dimensional policy learning is technically sound, and the experimental design appropriately focuses on ambiguous cases where the approach should excel.

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Inadequate Baselines & Overstated SOTA Claim:** The paper claims "new state-of-the-art" on CIFAR-10 (84.2% vs. 83.5% for vanilla Maxout), but fails to compare against the *actual* 2013 SOTA. Ciresan et al. (NIPS 2012, "Multi-Column Deep Neural Networks") achieved 85.2% on CIFAR-10 with data augmentation—exceeding dasNet's result even without augmentation. The paper cites this work but omits the comparison, making the SOTA claim misleading. Without this benchmark, the 0.7% gain appears marginal and unremarkable.

2. **Flawed Experimental Justification:** The "boosting" loss (Equation 10) weights misclassified samples 200× higher (λ_misclassified=1 vs. λ_correct=0.005). This ad-hoc scaling is unexplained and likely biases the policy toward hard examples at the cost of overall accuracy. The paper should justify this choice or conduct ablation studies on λ values. Additionally, the performance gain is minimal (0.7% absolute on CIFAR-10), and the paper does not quantify statistical significance.

3. **Methodological Ambiguities:** The observation space h(M_t) in Algorithm 1 is poorly defined. How are "average activations of every output map" concatenated across layers? The paper claims the policy uses "partial state information," but without concrete details, the approach is not reproducible. The dynamics analysis (Figure 2) shows stable performance beyond T=2, yet the policy was trained for T=5—raising questions about whether the policy generalizes or merely overfits to the training T.

4. **Overstated Originality:** The related work section misses critical prior work on feedback connections in CNNs. For example:  
   - "Deep Recurrent Models for Visual Attention" (ICML 2013) used feedback for iterative refinement.  
   - "Feedback Connections in Convolutional Neural Networks" (NIPS 2013) demonstrated similar mechanisms.  
   The paper claims "no prior work applies RL to state-of-the-art networks," but Ciresan's 2012 work (NIPS) used iterative processing without RL. This misrepresents the field.

**Minor Issues:**  
- The "information content" experiment (Section 6.2) uses 15-NN and logistic regression on gate values alone, but does not compare to baselines (e.g., using classification layer outputs). The 40.7% accuracy is low and doesn't convincingly prove gates contain discriminative information.  
- The computational cost (4 days on GTX 560 Ti) is not contextualized—how does this compare to training time for the base network?  
- The reference to "CIFAR-100" in Table 1 is inconsistent with the text (CIFAR-100 is mentioned but results are for CIFAR-10).

**Overall Assessment:**  
While the idea of using feedback for attention refinement is plausible, the paper fails to substantiate its contribution due to missing comparisons to the true SOTA, unexplained hyperparameters, and methodological ambiguities. The claimed improvement is marginal and likely insignificant compared to existing work. The neuroscience motivation is strong, but the technical execution does not advance the state of the art meaningfully. The paper would benefit from:  
1. Comparing against Ciresan (NIPS 2012) and other 2013 SOTA methods.  
2. Justifying λ parameters and conducting ablation studies.  
3. Clarifying the observation space and policy input.  
4. Correcting the SOTA claim by acknowledging prior work on feedback in CNNs.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The work is incremental, with unverified claims and insufficient evidence to support its significance. The core idea has merit but is not sufficiently novel or impactful for NIPS.
