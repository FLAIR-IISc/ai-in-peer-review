Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Attention Selective Networks (dasNet), a recurrent extension of convolutional Maxout networks that implements internal, channel-wise selective attention via feedback connections. Given an input image, the network performs T sequential passes; after each pass it forms an observation vector from per-map average activations and classifier outputs, and a learned policy produces multiplicative gates for each feature map in each layer. This reweighting modulates subsequent passes, potentially correcting difficult classifications. The policy is trained after standard supervised training of the base CNN, using Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES) to directly optimize a fitness based on the final-step cross-entropy with boosted weighting on misclassified samples. Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 report improvements over a strong Maxout baseline and claim state-of-the-art results for “unaugmented datasets” (interpreted as non-ensemble, no test-time augmentation).

The work connects to cognitive and neurophysiological literature on feedback, attention, and recurrent processing, and to machine learning work on attention, saccades, and recurrence (e.g., Fukushima’s backward paths, recirculation, Denil et al., Larochelle & Hinton), but is distinctive in applying feedback attention to a modern, high-performing CNN and training a high-dimensional policy via SNES.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple mechanism for internal attention: multiplicative, per-feature-map gates that are learned to improve difficult cases.
- Demonstrates iterative refinement over multiple passes, with qualitative analyses suggesting meaningful modulations in deeper layers.
- Scalable policy search for a million-parameter controller using SNES; interesting use of evolutionary strategies for a high-dimensional control problem.
- Empirical gains on CIFAR-10/100 over a competitive Maxout baseline, without ensembles or test-time averaging.
- Ablations on the number of steps show stable dynamics beyond the training horizon.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- “Reinforcement learning” framing is somewhat misleading. The policy is trained via black-box optimization of a supervised loss on labeled data, with no environment stochasticity or credit assignment beyond the final step. Backpropagation through time (BPTT) would be directly applicable here, and the paper does not justify why gradient-based training of the policy (end-to-end differentiable gates) was not attempted or compared.
- Experimental reporting is insufficiently detailed. The exact numerical improvements, standard deviations across runs, and comparisons to the best contemporary baselines (e.g., Network-in-Network, stochastic pooling, DropConnect, or stronger Maxout variants) are not fully presented. The “6% relative improvement” is hard to interpret without absolute error rates and confidence intervals.
- The claim of “state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets” is ambiguous. The base CNN is trained with data augmentation; presumably “unaugmented” refers to test-time augmentation and ensembles. This should be clarified and compared consistently to prior SOTA under the same training/testing conventions.
- The action parameterization uses a softmax scaled to enforce average gate = 1, which constrains gates to be positive and sum to a constant. This precludes global up/down scaling and forces purely redistributive gating, potentially limiting expressiveness. No ablation is provided to assess the impact of this constraint (e.g., allowing unconstrained positive gates or log-gates).
- Observations are reduced to per-map averages, which discards spatial information; the paper acknowledges this. However, the lack of any spatial attention baseline (e.g., gating per spatial region or per layer) leaves unclear how much of the gain comes from channel-wise vs. spatially-aware mechanisms.
- Runtime and complexity overhead are non-trivial (T passes; SNES training for 4 days). There is no analysis of computational cost vs. accuracy trade-offs, nor comparison to simply deepening/widening the baseline network at similar compute.
- Limited ablations: no comparison to a simple one-pass learned reweighting (e.g., a linear function of logits applied once), to iterative inference trained by BPTT, or to using only the final-layer gates. The reported “gate-only classification” result (≈45% on CIFAR-100 for a small network) is difficult to interpret without context.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and implementable. The optimization objective and training loop are clear. However, key choices (SNES vs. gradient learning, softmax constraint, observation design) are not empirically justified.
- The empirical section demonstrates improvements but lacks breadth and rigor (complete tables, baselines, statistical analysis, error breakdowns).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable, with a helpful algorithm description. Important details are missing: exact network and policy architecture sizes (number of maps per layer, dimensions of observation/action), hyperparameters (T selection rationale), and precise metrics and baseline configurations. The “unaugmented” terminology needs disambiguation.

Originality:
- The combination of internal channel-wise attention learned post hoc via black-box policy search in a modern CNN is novel for the period, even if related ideas (feedback, recurrence, attention) existed. Using SNES at this scale is interesting.

Significance:
- If the reported gains over strong baselines hold under rigorous comparisons, the idea is potentially impactful: iterative, learned internal attention can correct difficult cases without ensembling. However, broader adoption would benefit from demonstrating competitiveness versus gradient-trained recurrent/gated variants and from more comprehensive evaluations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, simple attention mechanism with a clear implementation and observed gains on standard benchmarks.
- Bridges cognitive notions of recurrent attention with contemporary CNNs.
- Provides a scalable recipe (SNES) for high-dimensional controller learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental evidence is not sufficiently thorough; key comparisons and details are missing.
- Methodological choices (SNES vs. BPTT; gating constraints) are not justified by ablations.
- Ambiguity around “state-of-the-art on unaugmented datasets.”

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide full quantitative results: absolute error rates on CIFAR-10/100, with and without test-time augmentation, and comparisons to contemporary SOTA under matched protocols; include variance over multiple random seeds.
- Add ablations: (a) train the same gating policy by BPTT (end-to-end differentiable) and compare; (b) remove the softmax-sum constraint (e.g., log-parameterized positive gates) and assess; (c) per-layer gating only vs. all layers; (d) one-pass gating vs. multiple passes; (e) observation variants including spatial summaries.
- Report compute/training time and inference overhead; compare to simply increasing model capacity within similar budgets.
- Clarify the meaning of “unaugmented datasets” and ensure fair comparisons.
- Provide more diagnostic analyses: per-class gains/losses, confusion matrices, and examples where refinement helps vs. hurts.
- Discuss stability and generalization: how sensitive is performance to T at test time, and does the policy overfit?

Overall, this is an interesting and promising direction, but stronger empirical validation and clearer comparisons are needed to fully substantiate the claims.
