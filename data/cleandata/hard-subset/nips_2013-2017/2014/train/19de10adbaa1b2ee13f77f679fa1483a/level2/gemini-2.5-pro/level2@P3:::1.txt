**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Deep Attention Selective Network (dasNet), a novel architecture that extends traditional feedforward convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with feedback connections. The core idea is to enable a pre-trained CNN to iteratively re-process an input image, dynamically modulating the activity of its internal convolutional filters in each pass. This modulation, framed as an internal selective attention mechanism, is controlled by a policy network. After the base CNN is trained via standard supervised learning, this policy is trained using reinforcement learning—specifically, Separable Natural Evolution Strategies (SNES)—to improve classification accuracy, particularly on challenging or ambiguous examples. The work is motivated by recurrent and top-down processing in the human visual cortex, which is thought to involve a fast initial feedforward pass followed by slower, attentional processing. The proposed method is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, where it achieves a new state-of-the-art result on unaugmented CIFAR-10.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method of augmenting a state-of-the-art Maxout network with a modulatory policy is well-defined. The choice of SNES as the optimization algorithm for the policy is a pragmatic and well-justified solution to the challenge of training a policy with a million-dimensional parameter space, which would be difficult for many standard reinforcement learning algorithms. The experimental results are convincing and support the central claims of the paper. The inclusion of qualitative analysis (Figure 3) and an investigation into the system's dynamics over extended time steps (Figure 2) strengthens the work, demonstrating that the learned policy is stable and contains meaningful information for classification.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the training procedure, and Figure 1 effectively illustrates the iterative processing loop. The connection to biological vision is explained well and provides strong motivation for the architecture. The description of the base Maxout network and the SNES algorithm are clear and provide the necessary context.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While attention and recurrent processing are established concepts, the specific mechanism proposed here—learning a policy via RL to modulate the filter gains *inside* a pre-trained, deep CNN—is a novel contribution. It effectively combines ideas from deep learning and reinforcement learning to address a fundamental limitation of static, feedforward models. The related work section is thorough and does an excellent job of positioning this contribution with respect to prior work on recurrent networks and RL in computer vision, correctly highlighting that previous efforts were often limited to simpler models or tasks.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. By demonstrating a state-of-the-art result on a challenging benchmark, the paper shows that enabling a network to perform additional, guided computation can lead to tangible performance gains. This introduces a promising paradigm where networks can "think" or "deliberate" on difficult inputs, moving beyond a single, fixed feedforward pass. This concept is likely to inspire future research into more sophisticated recurrent and attentional architectures that can allocate computational resources dynamically. The successful application of a black-box optimizer like SNES to such a high-dimensional control problem in a deep network is also a significant result.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but could be further improved with additional analysis and discussion on the following points:

*   **Analysis of Observation Space:** The observation vector for the policy is constructed by concatenating averaged map activations, intermediate layer activations, and class probabilities. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion motivating this specific choice. For instance, why use average activations, which discards spatial information? An ablation study analyzing the importance of each component of this observation vector would provide valuable insight into what information the policy relies on to make its decisions.
*   **Sensitivity to Number of Passes (T):** The number of processing steps, T, is set to 5 based on experimental determination. While the dynamics experiment in Figure 2 shows stability for T > 5, a plot showing the final classification error as a function of T would be very informative. This could reveal the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy improvement, and show at what point the returns diminish.
*   **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The training process is computationally intensive (4 days for the policy training alone). A brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential avenues for future work to improve efficiency would be valuable. Furthermore, while the results on CIFAR are impressive, a discussion on the potential challenges of scaling this approach to larger and more complex datasets like ImageNet would help frame the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and well-motivated architecture that combines deep learning with reinforcement learning for internal attention.
*   Achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the unaugmented CIFAR-10 benchmark, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach.
*   The work opens a significant new research direction for dynamic, adaptive neural networks that can perform iterative refinement.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and the experiments are well-executed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational requirements for training the policy are substantial, which may limit immediate practical adoption.
*   The experimental evaluation is confined to the CIFAR datasets.

Overall, the arguments in favor of acceptance are much stronger. This is a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research that is likely to have a substantial impact on the field.
