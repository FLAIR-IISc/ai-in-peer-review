### Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Summary**

This paper presents a significant extension of the graphical model framework for analyzing missing data, specifically building upon the "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) introduced in Mohan et al. [2013]. The core idea is to represent the data generating process and the missingness mechanism within a single causal graph, allowing for a formal analysis of when a target query (probabilistic or causal) can be recovered from the observed, incomplete data.

The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other, a common scenario in longitudinal studies.
2.  A new theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness indicators and their Markov blankets.
3.  Novel graphical criteria for establishing the *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3). This is a crucial step forward, as it provides a way to prove that recovery is impossible under a given set of assumptions.
4.  A demonstration that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. This highlights a key distinction and provides a powerful new tool (Theorem 4).
5.  Application of these results to the practical problem of attrition, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery in this context and correcting claims from prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and ubiquitous problem in all empirical sciences. Providing a formal, graphical language to reason about missing data is of immense value. This work moves beyond the standard MAR/MCAR/MNAR trichotomy to provide fine-grained, model-specific conditions for recoverability.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The contributions are highly original. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3) is a standout feature. Knowing when a problem is unsolvable is as important as knowing how to solve it. Furthermore, the insight in Section 6—that causal queries can be recovered even when their probabilistic components are not, due to the different separation criteria (d-separation vs. d*-separation)—is a profound and non-obvious result that significantly advances the field.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound, building rigorously on the established foundations of graphical models and do-calculus. The theorems appear correct and are well-motivated by clear examples (e.g., Example 1 and 3) that illustrate the limitations of prior work and the power of the new results. The application to attrition provides a concrete and compelling use case.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and structured logically. It clearly states its contributions relative to prior work, particularly Mohan et al. [2013]. The use of small, illustrative graphs (Figures 1-4) is very effective in communicating complex graphical conditions.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of Definitions:** While generally clear, some key definitions could be more precise to avoid potential ambiguity for readers less familiar with the specific literature.
    *   The definition of "collider path" in Theorem 2 ("a path in which every intermediate node is a collider") is slightly non-standard. Typically, the status of endpoints matters. A more formal definition would be helpful.
    *   The definition of "inducing path" in Theorem 4 is tied to Verma and Pearl [1991], where it has a precise meaning (a path where every non-endpoint is a collider and an ancestor of an endpoint). It would be beneficial to explicitly restate this definition to ensure the conditions of the theorem are fully understood.

2.  **Practical Implications and Assumptions:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the practitioner can specify a correct m-graph. This is a very strong assumption. While the paper cites Mohan and Pearl [2014] on the testability of these graphs, the main text would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the practical challenges of model specification and the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification. For instance, what happens if an edge between a substantive variable and a missingness indicator is omitted or incorrectly added?

3.  **Connection to Mainstream Statistics:** The related work section adequately covers the graphical model literature but could draw stronger connections to the mainstream statistical literature on missing data. For example, the MAR condition is essentially a graphical condition (d-separation of a variable from its missingness indicator, given the fully observed variables). The results in this paper provide a graphical semantics for when methods like Maximum Likelihood or Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) are expected to work, and more importantly, when they will fail (i.e., in MNAR cases where the graphical recovery conditions do not hold). Explicitly framing some results in this context could broaden the paper's impact and reach.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is perfectly acceptable and valuable for a NIPS paper, its significance would be further enhanced by a small simulation study. For example, one could simulate data from the graph in Figure 3, show that naive estimation of P(y|do(z)) is biased, and then demonstrate that the proposed recovery formula produces a consistent estimate. This would provide a concrete validation of the theoretical claims.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes several novel and important contributions to a critical area of research. It significantly extends the formal graphical framework for missing data, providing powerful new tools for both recoverability and non-recoverability. The distinction between probabilistic and causal query recovery is a particularly insightful contribution. Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity of definitions and a lack of discussion on the practical aspects of model specification, the theoretical strengths are substantial. The work is original, technically solid, and likely to be influential for researchers working on causal inference and missing data.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
