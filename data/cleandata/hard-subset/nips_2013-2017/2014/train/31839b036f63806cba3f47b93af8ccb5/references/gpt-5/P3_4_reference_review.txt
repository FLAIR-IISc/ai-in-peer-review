Summary
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) in four directions: (i) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that allows ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) new constructive recovery formulas for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via partitions of R and Markov blankets in semi-Markovian models; (iii) graphical criteria that certify non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) sufficient conditions for recovering identifiable causal effects P(y|do(x)) even when associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x)) are not recoverable, exploiting do-calculus, dormant independences, and d*-separation. The paper culminates with necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery of joint and causal queries in simple attrition (|Vm|=1), and corrects misconceptions in the attrition literature.

Relation to prior work
The paper is firmly grounded in the causal/missing-data literature: it builds on m-graphs (Mohan et al., NIPS 2013) and do-calculus/identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; Pearl 2009). It connects to classical MAR/MCAR assumptions (Rubin 1976) and ML/EM/IPW/MI practices (Dempster et al. 1977; Lauritzen 1995; Little & Rubin 2002; Robins & Rotnitzky 1992). The novelty lies in extending recoverability beyond graph-blind MAR/MCAR to MNAR settings via precise graphical criteria, and in showing causal effects can be recoverable even when probabilistic targets are not.

Strengths
- Technical contributions: 
  - Generalized ordered factorization (Theorem 1) that admits R variables and requires minimal conditioning sets, handling interacting missingness mechanisms.
  - Constructive sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) without admissible sequences (Theorem 2, Corollary 1), using partitions of R and Markov blankets in semi-Markovian ADMGs with latent confounding.
  - Clear necessary non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), which are practical red flags for analysts.
  - Recovery of causal effects via do-calculus despite inseparability of Y and Ry (Theorem 4), articulating the gap between probabilistic and causal recoverability.
  - Tight characterizations for simple attrition: necessary and sufficient conditions for both P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6), with explicit estimands.
- Conceptual impact: Clarifies when causal inference is feasible under MNAR, and when standard probabilistic recoverability barriers do not apply.
- Relevance: Addresses ubiquitous attrition and longitudinal missingness, beyond MAR/MCAR.

Weaknesses
- Algorithmic specificity and completeness: Section 4 claims “general algorithms,” but the submission largely presents conditions and resulting formulas. A concrete, reproducible decision procedure (inputs: m-graph; outputs: recoverable? estimand) is not fully specified except in special cases. Complexity analysis and pseudo-code are missing.
- Scope limitations: m-graphs forbid R→V (R cannot be a parent of substantive/latent variables). While standard for missingness mechanisms, some longitudinal settings might have feedback between study processes and measurements; discussing practical implications of this restriction would help.
- Clarity and notation:
  - The generalized ordered factorization hinges on minimal conditioning sets; an explicit algorithm to compute minimal Xi and to search for admissible orders would improve usability.
  - Theorem 2’s construction (R partitions, Mb(R(i)), collider paths) would benefit from a worked example with an explicit estimand and a step-by-step derivation. Several references to figures are essential but not elaborated in text.
  - Some notational overload (R_w vs Rw, Rx vs R_xm, etc.) could be streamlined; a consolidated notation table would help.
  - Several references are malformed; ensure proper bibliographic formatting.
- Empirical validation: No simulations or empirical case studies are provided. Given the practical importance, even small synthetic experiments showing finite-sample behavior and comparisons with IPW/MI/EM under MNAR would strengthen the contribution.
- Robustness and sensitivity: The approach presumes a correct m-graph; while tests for misspecification are cited (Mohan & Pearl, 2014), there is no discussion of robustness of estimands to plausible graph misspecifications, nor guidance for sensitivity analysis.

Technical assessment (Quality)
The claims are generally well-grounded in established graphical causal semantics (ADMGs, d-separation, do-calculus) and build on prior theorems. The necessity results for non-recoverability and the N&S characterizations in simple attrition are especially compelling. However, some results are only sufficient (Theorem 2), and the lack of a unifying, complete decision procedure for general P(V) recoverability limits immediate applicability. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; including intuition for key steps (e.g., why minimality is essential in Theorem 1) would aid comprehension.

Clarity
The paper is dense but mostly clear for an expert audience. It would benefit from:
- A running example carried through Sections 3–6, with explicit manifest-to-underlying conversions using V*.
- Algorithmic checklists or pseudo-code for: (i) testing admissibility; (ii) constructing partitions R(i) and Mb(R(i)); (iii) verifying Theorem 4’s conditions.
- Improved notation consistency and reference formatting.

Originality
The extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013)—admissible sequences including R, non-recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability under inseparability—appear novel and non-trivial. The simple attrition N&S results provide a clean, practically relevant boundary.

Significance
High potential impact for both theory and practice in causal/missing-data analysis, especially in longitudinal/attrition settings and MNAR regimes. The graphical conditions are actionable and can guide analysts away from futile estimation attempts or toward valid estimands.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide a sound/complete decision procedure (or clearly state limitations) for P(V) recoverability in general semi-Markovian m-graphs. Is there a known boundary between Theorem 2’s sufficiency and overall necessity?
- Give pseudo-code and complexity for constructing admissible sequences and for the Theorem 2 estimand.
- Discuss how to choose separating sets Z in practice, and whether minimal separator search is needed.
- Include a small synthetic study comparing proposed estimands to IPW/EM under MNAR, and sensitivity to mild graph misspecification.
- Clarify the role of positivity (strictly positive over complete cases) and how violations affect theorems.

Pros
- Solid, nontrivial theoretical advances.
- Clear practical criteria (recoverability and non-recoverability).
- Important message: causal estimands may be recoverable even when probabilistic ones are not.
- Useful N&S results for attrition.

Cons
- Limited algorithmic detail and no empirical validation.
- Heavy reliance on figures/appendix; some clarity and notation issues.
- Assumes accurate m-graphs; robustness not discussed.

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on clarifying algorithms, tightening notation, and (ideally) adding a small empirical illustration. The theoretical contributions are significant and relevant to the NeurIPS community interested in causal inference and graphical models under missing data.
