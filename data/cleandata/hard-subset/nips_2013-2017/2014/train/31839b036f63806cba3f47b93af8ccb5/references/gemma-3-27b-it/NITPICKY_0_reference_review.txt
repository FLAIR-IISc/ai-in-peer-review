This paper aims to extend the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to facilitate the recovery of probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data. While the topic is undeniably important and the use of m-graphs provides a formal approach, the paper suffers from a relentless focus on technical details and corner cases, often at the expense of broader clarity and impact. The numerous theorems and corollaries, while potentially correct, are presented in a dense and often impenetrable manner, making it difficult to grasp the core contributions.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem but lacks a high-level overview of *why* these extensions to the existing framework are crucial. The authors establish the foundation with a review of m-graphs and recoverability, which is necessary, but becomes verbose. The subsequent sections relentlessly present theoretical results – sequential factorization, recovery in the absence of an admissible sequence, non-recoverability criteria, and causal query recovery – each building on the previous one with increasing complexity. While thoroughness is appreciated, the sheer volume of theorems, definitions, and examples becomes overwhelming. 

A major weakness lies in the lack of intuitive explanation alongside the formal derivations. For example, Theorem 2 feels unnecessarily complex and the connection to practical scenarios (beyond the attrition example) is not clearly articulated. The example illustrating Theorem 1, while detailed, relies heavily on mathematical manipulation without providing a clear conceptual understanding of *why* the factorization works. The minimality requirement in Definition 2, while important, is not adequately justified in terms of its practical implications. 

The treatment of causal queries is the strongest aspect of the paper. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and Ry are inseparable is a noteworthy result. However, even here, the presentation is dense and the examples (particularly Figure 3) require significant effort to follow. The discussion of attrition, while relevant, feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't fully exploit the potential of the developed framework.

The related work section is comprehensive but reads like a checklist rather than a critical assessment of existing literature. The authors could benefit from a more focused discussion of how their work specifically advances the state-of-the-art.

**Specific concerns:**

* **Notations:** The proliferation of notation (Vo, Vm, R, etc.) is cumbersome and makes the paper harder to read.
* **Minimality:** The importance of minimality in the ordered factorization is mentioned but not fully explained.  Why is it so critical? What happens if it's violated?
* **Examples:** While numerous, examples often focus on specific cases and do not provide generalizable insights.
* **Clarity vs. Rigor:** The paper prioritizes mathematical rigor at the expense of conceptual clarity.
* **Proof Length:** The proofs are relegated to the appendix, which is standard, but the sheer length and complexity suggest a potential for simplification.
* **Formatting:** Minor inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., spacing around equations) detract from the overall polish.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a formal framework for reasoning about recoverability of queries under missingness.
* The result regarding the recoverability of causal queries even when Y and Ry are inseparable is significant.
* The work extends existing results on m-graphs and provides more general conditions for recoverability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper is overly dense and difficult to understand, even for experts in the field.
* The relentless focus on technical details overshadows the broader significance of the work.
* The presentation lacks intuitive explanations and practical examples.
* The sheer volume of theorems and definitions makes the paper feel overwhelming.
* Minor formatting inconsistencies detract from the overall quality.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents technically sound results, its lack of clarity and overwhelming complexity significantly diminish its impact. The paper feels more like a comprehensive catalog of theorems than a cohesive scientific contribution. A major rewrite, with a focus on simplifying the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations, is necessary before this work would be suitable for NIPS. The authors need to prioritize communicating the *essence* of their ideas over demonstrating every possible theoretical corner case. A good paper explains *why* something is important; this paper primarily demonstrates *that* something is possible.
