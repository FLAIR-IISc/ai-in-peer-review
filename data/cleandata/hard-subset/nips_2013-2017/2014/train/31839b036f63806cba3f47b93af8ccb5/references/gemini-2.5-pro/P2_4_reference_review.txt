### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors build upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework introduced in their prior work (Mohan et al., 2013), which provides a formal graphical representation of the causal processes governing both the substantive variables and the missingness mechanisms. The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, which now accommodates dependencies between missingness mechanisms (a common scenario in longitudinal studies).
2.  A new theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, relying on partitioning the missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets.
3.  Novel graphical criteria that serve as necessary conditions for recoverability (i.e., they identify when recovery is impossible). This is a significant contribution, as it establishes formal limits on what can be estimated.
4.  A key finding that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. This is demonstrated using the concepts of d*-separation and dormant independence.
5.  An application of these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in experimental studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims made in previous literature.

### Review and Justification

This paper presents a strong, theoretically-grounded contribution to the challenging problem of statistical and causal inference with missing data, particularly in the "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) setting.

**Quality: (Excellent)**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are presented as formal theorems, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The work is built upon the well-established foundations of graphical models and Pearl's *do*-calculus, which lends it rigor. The definitions are precise, and the arguments presented in the examples logically follow from the theorems.

*   **Strengths:** The work is a complete theoretical piece. It doesn't just provide sufficient conditions for recovery but also introduces necessary conditions (non-recoverability criteria in Theorem 3), which provides a more complete picture. This is a mark of careful and thorough research. The analysis of causal query recoverability (Section 6) is particularly insightful, showing a subtle but crucial distinction between statistical and causal estimands in the context of missing data.
*   **Weaknesses:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a venue like NIPS, the claims would be substantially more impactful with empirical validation. A simulation study could have been included to demonstrate the proposed recovery algorithms in practice: generate data from a known m-graph, introduce missingness according to the model, apply the recovery formulas, and show that the estimates converge to the true population quantities. This would also help illustrate the practical bias that would arise from naively using methods that assume MAR. Furthermore, the entire framework rests on the assumption that the m-graph is correctly specified, which is a very strong assumption in practice. While the authors cite their own work on testing m-graphs, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of these results to model misspecification would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity: (Good)**
The paper is well-organized and follows a logical progression from probabilistic to causal queries. The introduction and background sections clearly set the stage. The use of small, targeted examples for each theorem (e.g., Example 1 for Theorem 1) is helpful for illustrating the core concepts.

*   **Strengths:** The problem statement is clear, and the contributions are explicitly listed. The notation, though dense, is consistent. The figures effectively illustrate the graphical structures being discussed.
*   **Weaknesses:** The paper is quite dense and assumes significant familiarity with causal graphical models, d-separation, and the *do*-calculus. Some definitions, particularly for Theorem 2 (R(part), Mb(R(i))), are introduced very quickly and are difficult to internalize on a first read. A single, more complex running example that is revisited throughout the paper to illustrate the application of successive theorems might have been more pedagogically effective than several disconnected examples. The language is formal and precise but could be challenging for readers not already steeped in this specific literature.

**Originality: (Excellent)**
The paper makes several novel contributions to the field. While it builds on the authors' previous work, the extensions are significant and non-trivial.
*   **Strengths:** The formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* appear to be new and are a valuable contribution, as they prevent researchers from attempting impossible estimation tasks. The central finding—that causal effects can be recovered even when the underlying joint distribution is not—is a profound and original insight. It highlights that the information required for causal inference is different from that required for associative inference and can be more robust to certain missingness patterns. The application to attrition, which refutes a necessary condition proposed in prior work (Garcia, 2013), demonstrates the power and novelty of the proposed framework.

**Significance: (Very Good)**
The problem of missing data is ubiquitous in machine learning and all empirical sciences. Most existing methods rely on the strong and often untestable MAR assumption. This paper provides a principled, graph-based framework for tackling the much harder MNAR case.
*   **Strengths:** The results have the potential to be highly impactful for researchers in fields like econometrics, epidemiology, and social sciences, where missing data due to attrition and self-selection is a major threat to validity. The ability to formally reason about whether a causal effect is recoverable from a given dataset and a set of assumptions is a powerful tool. The clear graphical criteria can guide study design and data analysis. The paper advances the state of the art by providing concrete algorithms and conditions for a problem class that is often considered intractable.
*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance is somewhat limited by the two points mentioned under "Quality": the strong assumption of a correctly specified causal graph and the lack of a demonstrated, practical implementation of the recovery algorithms. While theoretically sound, the immediate path to application for a practitioner is not fully laid out.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Provides novel, rigorous results for the fundamental problem of missing data, especially in the challenging MNAR setting.
2.  **Novel Key Insight:** The separation of recoverability for causal vs. probabilistic queries is a major conceptual advance with important practical implications.
3.  **Completeness:** The paper provides both sufficient conditions for recovery and necessary conditions for non-recoverability, making the framework more complete and useful.
4.  **Clear Advancement of the Field:** It significantly extends prior work and corrects misconceptions in the existing literature on attrition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of any simulation or real-data experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and robustness of the proposed methods.
2.  **Strong Underlying Assumptions:** The framework's utility is contingent on the ability to specify a correct m-graph, a non-trivial and potentially unverifiable task in many real-world scenarios.

**Recommendation:**
The strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance, strongly outweigh its weaknesses. The lack of experiments is a limitation but does not undermine the importance of the theoretical results themselves. This is a high-quality contribution that advances our understanding of inference with missing data. I recommend acceptance.
