This paper presents a significant extension of the 'missingness graph' (m-graph) framework for analyzing and recovering statistical queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. [2013], the authors introduce several novel theoretical results. The core contributions are: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that allows for dependencies among missingness mechanisms, (2) a new method for recovering the joint distribution in cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables, (3) the first formal graphical criteria for determining when a query is *non-recoverable*, and (4) a crucial result showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not. The paper culminates by applying these results to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Advances:** The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) are particularly valuable, as they provide researchers with formal tools to understand when their assumptions are insufficient, a topic largely untreated in prior graphical model literature on missing data. The distinction drawn between the recoverability of probabilistic queries (e.g., P(Y|X)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) in Section 6 is a key conceptual insight. The demonstration that a causal effect can be identified even when the outcome variable Y is inseparably linked to its missingness mechanism Ry is novel and important.

2.  **Increased Generality and Applicability:** The proposed theorems substantially broaden the scope of problems that can be handled by the m-graph framework. Theorem 1's inclusion of R variables in the factorization order addresses more realistic scenarios, such as longitudinal studies where one dropout decision influences another. Theorem 2 provides a powerful alternative for complex, non-factorizable models, moving beyond the limitations of the previous work.

3.  **Clear Practical Relevance:** By providing graphical criteria, the paper offers a principled and accessible way for practitioners to reason about their missing data problems. The application to attrition (Section 7) is a compelling example. The finding that P(y|do(x)) is non-recoverable in Figure 4(a) because of a collider path between Y and Ry, despite X not being an ancestor of Ry, corrects a misconception in the literature (Garcia [2013]) and provides clear guidance for study design and analysis.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and the arguments are presented logically. The use of small, illustrative examples (e.g., Example 3 for causal recovery) is effective in conveying the intuition behind the formal theorems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Exposition of Theorem 2:** While powerful, the presentation of Theorem 2 is dense. The concepts of a "collider path," the partitioning of R variables into `R(part)`, and the substantive Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))` are introduced very quickly. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive, step-by-step explanation of how to construct `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` for a given graph, perhaps with a small worked example directly in Section 4, rather than deferring entirely to the appendix. This would make the paper's most general result on probabilistic recovery more accessible.

2.  **Scope of Causal Recovery Results:** The results on causal recovery are among the most exciting in the paper, but their scope seems limited in the current presentation. Theorem 4, for instance, is stated for the case of a single partially observed variable (`|Vm| = 1`). It is unclear how these ideas generalize to settings with multiple missing variables, where their missingness mechanisms might interact. A discussion of the challenges or future directions for causal recovery with multiple Vm variables would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions stand on their own, the claims would be more impactful with a small simulation study. For instance, demonstrating the result from Example 3—where a naive method like complete case analysis or simple imputation would fail to recover the true P(y|do(z)), while the proposed method succeeds—would provide a powerful, concrete illustration of the framework's practical value.

4.  **Connection to Other Missing Data Frameworks:** The related work section provides a good overview, but it could more deeply connect the graphical criteria to the assumptions underlying other popular methods. For example, how do the graphical conditions for recoverability relate to the assumptions required for Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) or Multiple Imputation (MI) to yield consistent estimates? A brief discussion on how an m-graph could be used to justify (or invalidate) the assumptions of these other methods would better position this work within the broader statistics and machine learning literature.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theorems appear correct, and the proofs are provided in the appendix. The work is a complete and substantial extension of prior research.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some of the more complex definitions and theorems could benefit from more intuitive explanations and in-text examples.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability are novel contributions to the field.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. They provide both a deeper theoretical understanding of inference with missing data and practical, graphical tools for researchers. This work is likely to be built upon by others in the causality and graphical models communities.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents novel and important theoretical results, particularly the first graphical criteria for non-recoverability.
*   Significantly advances the state-of-the-art in using graphical models for missing data problems.
*   The insight on recovering causal effects when probabilistic associations are unrecoverable is a major contribution.
*   Provides clear, applicable results for common problems like attrition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The presentation of some key results (esp. Theorem 2) is dense and could be improved for accessibility.
*   The lack of any empirical or simulation-based evidence makes it harder to gauge the practical performance of the proposed recovery algorithms.
*   The scope of the causal results is somewhat limited to a single missing variable in its formal statement.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents novel, significant, and technically sound results that advance our understanding of a fundamental problem in data analysis. The critiques are primarily suggestions for improving the exposition and exploring future extensions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
