- Paper extends missingness graphs (m-graphs) with four main contributions: refined sequential factorization, new recoverability result for semi‑Markovian models, non‑recoverability criteria, and conditions/procedures for causal query recovery including attrition.  
- Refines ordered factorizations to allow missingness indicators R, handling interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Theorem 2 gives joint-distribution recoverability in semi‑Markovian models via R partitions and Markov blankets when no admissible sequence exists.  
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 provide graphical non‑recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions.  
- Theorems 5–6 give necessary-and-sufficient conditions for recoverability under simple attrition (|Vm|=1), practical and sharp.  
- Positions m‑graphs as a unifying language to diagnose recoverability and construct R‑conditioned estimators.  
- Builds on Mohan et al. (2013) and leverages do‑calculus, identifiability results, dormant independencies, and classical MAR/MCAR paradigms.  
- Novel contributions include general non‑recoverability criteria for semi‑Markovian m‑graphs and sharp attrition results.  
- Strength: clear separation between probabilistic and causal recoverability, showing causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic ones are not.  
- Strength: generalization of ordered factorizations to include R is a powerful, natural extension unlocking important cases.  
- Strength: non‑recoverability criteria are useful diagnostics complementing recovery results.  
- Strength: attrition results are easy to check, correct misconceptions, and are substantively useful.  
- Strength: solid causal inference grounding; do‑calculus-based recovery is appropriate and rigorous.  
- Weakness: heavy notation and several non‑standard constructions lack intuitive/algorithmic guidance.  
- Weakness: modeling assumption that R cannot be parents of V∪U should be emphasized; practical implications of conditioning on R′y need more discussion.  
- Weakness: uncertainty about tightness/completeness of necessary conditions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) and overlap between theorems.  
- Weakness: need clearer hierarchy/organization of results and relation to prior theorems (e.g., Mohan et al., 2013).  
- Weakness: entirely theoretical paper — lacks synthetic/empirical validation comparing to ML methods (MAR, IPW, MI).  
- Weakness: exposition issues — typos, inconsistent notation, poorly formatted references; needs thorough editing.  
- Specific request: provide algorithm and complexity for finding admissible sequences (Theorem 1).  
- Specific request: give worked examples where Theorem 2 succeeds beyond brief mentions and a self‑contained derivation for Theorem 4’s example.  
- Specific request: provide a catalog of patterns for attrition/inducing paths and discuss extensions without positivity.  
- Pros for acceptance: advances MNAR recoverability, clarifies probabilistic vs causal recoverability, and gives sharp attrition results.  
- Cons for acceptance: dense/opaque exposition, no empirical demos, organizational redundancy, and reference issues.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on improved clarity (algorithms/partitions), illustrative examples or a small simulation, and polished writing/references.
