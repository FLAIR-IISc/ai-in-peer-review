This paper presents a formal graphical framework for analyzing the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) representation introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], the work extends previous results and introduces several new theoretical contributions. The m-graph explicitly models the observed variables, the missingness indicator variables (R-variables), and their causal relationships. The central question is whether a target query (e.g., a joint distribution or a causal effect) can be consistently estimated from the observed data, given a set of graphical assumptions about the data generating and missingness processes.

The main contributions are:
1.  An extension of the sequential factorization theorem from Mohan et al. [2013] to handle dependencies among missingness mechanisms, a common scenario in longitudinal studies.
2.  A novel method for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the R-variables and using their Markov blankets (Theorem 2).
3.  The first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3). These criteria identify structural impediments to recovery, such as a variable being adjacent to its missingness mechanism or connected by a specific type of collider path.
4.  A key finding that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries, P(y,x) or P(y|x), are not. This is demonstrated through an example and formalized in Theorem 4, which leverages concepts like d*-separation from the causal inference literature.
5.  An application of these general results to the problem of attrition, where necessary and sufficient conditions for the recovery of causal effects are provided, correcting what the authors claim are misconceptions in prior work.

The work is situated within the literature on graphical models for causality and missing data. It significantly advances the line of research initiated in Mohan et al. [2013] by providing a more complete set of tools for both recovery and non-recovery, and by establishing a crucial link to the identifiability of causal effects.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several highly original and significant contributions. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a major conceptual advance. Prior work has largely focused on sufficient conditions for recovery; providing necessary conditions helps delineate the precise limits of what can be learned from incomplete data under a given set of assumptions. The insight that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not is profound. It demonstrates that the structure of a causal query can allow one to circumvent certain missing data problems, a non-obvious result that deepens the connection between the fields of causal inference and missing data analysis. The results are likely to be impactful for researchers working on these topics.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It presents a series of formal theorems that generalize previous work and introduce new capabilities. The definitions are precise, and the arguments leading to the theorems are logically structured. The proofs provided in the appendix (not fully checked but assumed correct) suggest a rigorous and complete piece of theoretical work. The application to the specific, practical problem of attrition effectively demonstrates the utility of the general framework.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors use illustrative examples effectively to build intuition for the formal theorems (e.g., Example 1 for sequential factorization, Example 3 for causal recovery). The progression from probabilistic to causal queries is logical, and the final section on attrition provides a satisfying application of the developed theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Accessibility and Clarity:** The paper assumes a high level of expertise in both the m-graph framework from Mohan et al. [2013] and advanced topics in graphical causal models (e.g., do-calculus, d*-separation, inducing paths). While appropriate for a specialized audience, its accessibility to the broader NIPS community is limited. A more self-contained background section could have mitigated this. Furthermore, some key definitions (e.g., "collider path," "R(part)," "Mb(R(i))") are introduced immediately following the statement of the theorems that use them, which can disrupt the reading flow. Presenting these definitions beforehand would improve clarity. The definition of a "collider path" as a path where *every* intermediate node is a collider is non-standard and should be clarified and justified.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the work would be substantially more compelling with the inclusion of simulations or experiments. An empirical study could demonstrate that the proposed recovery procedures yield consistent estimates in finite samples where the theory predicts recoverability, and fail where the non-recoverability criteria hold. This would also provide insight into the practical performance of the estimators, such as their variance and robustness to near-violations of the graphical conditions.

3.  **Practical Scope:** The graphical conditions for recoverability, particularly in Theorems 2 and 4, can be quite strict. A discussion of the practical settings in which these conditions might be plausibly met would be beneficial. For instance, what types of real-world problems would satisfy the condition that a variable is not a neighbor of its missingness mechanism and not connected to it by a collider path? Providing such intuition would help bridge the gap between the formal theory and its application.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. This is a technically solid and complete theoretical paper. The primary weakness is the absence of any empirical validation to support the theoretical claims.
*   **Clarity:** Good. The paper is well-structured, but its dense technical nature and reliance on prerequisite knowledge make it challenging for non-experts. Minor organizational changes regarding definitions could improve readability.
*   **Originality:** Very High. The paper introduces novel concepts, most notably the graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability. It represents a significant and original extension of prior work.
*   **Significance:** High. The work provides a powerful and fine-grained formal language for reasoning about missing data problems that go beyond the classical MCAR/MAR/MNAR distinctions. It offers fundamental insights that are likely to influence future research in causal inference, statistics, and machine learning.
