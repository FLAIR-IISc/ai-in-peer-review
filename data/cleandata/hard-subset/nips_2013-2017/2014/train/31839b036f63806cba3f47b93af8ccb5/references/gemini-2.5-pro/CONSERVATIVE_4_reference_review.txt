**Summary**

This paper extends the framework of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), previously introduced by the authors, to establish graphical criteria for the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The authors present several new theoretical results. They generalize a sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms. They provide a theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where this factorization fails, based on the Markov blanket of missingness indicators. The paper also introduces novel graphical criteria for determining when a query is *non-recoverable*, a useful contribution for practitioners. A key finding is that causal effects can sometimes be recovered even when the underlying joint distribution is not, particularly when the outcome variable is not separable from its missingness mechanism. These results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies. This work positions itself within the graphical models and causality literature, offering a non-parametric, model-based alternative to traditional statistical methods for missing data which often rely on assumptions like Missing at Random (MAR).

**Strengths**

1.  **Formal Rigor:** The paper is theoretically dense and provides a formal, graphical language for reasoning about complex missing-not-at-random (MNAR) scenarios. The theorems are presented clearly with proofs supplied in the appendix.
2.  **Novel Theoretical Contributions:** The paper introduces several new and non-trivial graphical conditions. The criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are a valuable addition, as they help define the boundaries of what is possible. The result that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not (Section 6) is an important and insightful conceptual point.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** For a highly theoretical paper, the authors do a good job of using small graphical examples to illustrate their main theorems, which aids in comprehension. The paper is well-structured and the arguments are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the absence of any experimental results. The claims are purely theoretical. While theoretical contributions are valuable, a paper at a conference like NIPS should provide some evidence of practical utility or at least a proof-of-concept on simulated data. There are no simulations to verify that the derived estimators are well-behaved with finite samples, nor are there any applications to real-world datasets to demonstrate the framework's advantage over established methods. This makes the work feel incomplete and its practical impact difficult to assess.

2.  **Strong and Untestable Assumptions:** The entire framework hinges on the assumption that the user can specify the true m-graph correctly. This is a very strong requirement. In most real-world applications, the causal mechanisms governing missingness are precisely what is unknown and difficult to model. A misspecified graph would lead to invalid conclusions, making the proposed methods potentially fragile in practice. The paper does not adequately discuss the sensitivity of its results to such model misspecification.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods:** The related work section lists standard statistical approaches like Maximum Likelihood with EM, Multiple Imputation (MI), and Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW), but it fails to engage in a meaningful comparison. It is unclear under what conditions the proposed graphical approach offers a tangible advantage over a well-specified parametric selection model or a doubly-robust estimator, which are the workhorses of missing data analysis. The paper does not provide a compelling argument for why a researcher should abandon these well-understood, widely implemented methods in favor of this new, more abstract framework.

4.  **Practicality of the Proposed Estimators:** The paper demonstrates that certain queries are "recoverable" and provides formulae for them (e.g., Theorem 2, 5). However, these are expressed in terms of population probabilities. It is not immediately obvious how these quantities would be estimated from finite data, what the statistical properties (e.g., variance) of such estimators would be, or how they would scale computationally. Without this link to practical estimation, the results remain largely theoretical curiosities.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel and technically sound theoretical results in the challenging domain of MNAR data.
*   It provides a formal basis for reasoning about recoverability, which could potentially guide future research.
*   The distinction between the recoverability of causal versus probabilistic queries is an important insight.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The work is entirely theoretical with no experiments, simulations, or real-data applications, making its practical relevance and validity difficult to judge. This is a major shortcoming for a NIPS paper.
*   The reliance on a correctly specified m-graph is a strong, often unrealistic assumption that limits the practical applicability of the methods.
*   The paper fails to adequately compare its proposed framework against the large body of established and widely used statistical methods for handling missing data.
*   The contribution feels more incremental than transformative, building directly on the authors' own prior work without sufficiently demonstrating a significant leap forward for the field at large.

**Conclusion**

While this paper presents an interesting and technically sound extension of the m-graph formalism, its contributions are purely theoretical. The lack of any empirical validation, coupled with the strong, untestable assumptions required by the framework, raises serious questions about its practical significance and robustness. The paper does not make a convincing case for its superiority or even its complementarity to established statistical methods. As such, the work feels incomplete and more suited to a specialized workshop on causality or a theoretical journal than to the main NIPS conference, which generally expects a clearer demonstration of impact and applicability.
