This paper studies identification and recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors: (i) generalize the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R in the factorization (Theorem 1), enabling recovery when R mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a new recovery formula and sufficient conditions for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2 and a corollary), using partitions of R and Markov blankets; (iii) introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) show that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries are not, via do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4 and Example 3); and (v) give necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1) for joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6). Proofs are relegated to the appendix; the main text illustrates with small examples.

Relation to prior work: The paper is framed squarely in the Pearl/Shpitser causal-graphical program, extending the m-graph framework from Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013). The causal identification background draws on do-calculus (Pearl, 2009) and d*-separation and dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl). The related work correctly contrasts with MAR/MCAR-based ML/EM and IPW/MI approaches that are “graph blind.”

Strengths
- Clear formalization of recoverability questions in m-graphs, and useful unification with causal identification concepts.
- The extension to include R variables in admissible sequences (Theorem 1) is natural and addresses cases (e.g., longitudinal settings) where missingness mechanisms interact.
- Non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable for diagnosing when assumptions are insufficient; these conditions are easy to check graphically.
- The causal/probabilistic asymmetry is interesting: demonstrating recoverability of P(y|do(x)) even when P(y|x) or P(x,y) are not is a noteworthy message for practitioners.
- The simple attrition results (Theorems 5–6) provide crisp necessary-and-sufficient graphical conditions and concrete estimands.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Lack of empirical validation. The paper presents only toy examples; there are no simulations or real data case studies to demonstrate that the proposed recovery formulas yield good finite-sample performance, nor comparisons against EM/MI/IPW in MNAR settings. For NeurIPS, even theory papers benefit from at least synthetic validation to stress-test assumptions (e.g., positivity, graph misspecification) and to quantify robustness.
- Algorithmic specificity is limited. The text claims “general algorithms” (Section 4), but beyond closed-form factorizations there is no explicit procedure or pseudo-code for: (a) deciding recoverability for arbitrary queries in general m-graphs, (b) constructing admissible sequences when they exist, (c) computing R-partitions and Mb sets at scale, or (d) integrating do-calculus steps automatically (analogous to the ID algorithm). Complexity considerations and completeness of the proposed procedures are not discussed.
- Assumptions and scope restrictions need sharper articulation. Early on the paper assumes R nodes are not parents of substantive or latent variables (R → V/U prohibited), which is strong and excludes plausible missingness feedback (e.g., dropout affecting subsequent measurements). Later sections consider edges between R’s, but the prohibition R → V/U still limits applicability; the impact of this assumption on each theorem should be made explicit.
- Theorem clarity and novelty. Several results feel close to re-statements or modest generalizations of Mohan et al. (2013). For example, Theorem 1’s admissible sequence condition largely extends the earlier ordered factorization to include R; the real novelty and breadth would be clearer with nontrivial examples where earlier theorems provably fail but the new one succeeds. Similarly, Theorem 2’s conditions (“no neighbor or collider path between X and Rx”) resemble the necessary conditions later in Theorem 3; it would help to map precisely how Theorem 2 generalizes prior necessary-and-sufficient results and where it is strictly stronger or weaker.
- Causal recoverability claims depend on advanced constructs (d*-separation, dormant independence), but only one small example is provided. Theorem 4’s conditions are nontrivial to verify; without an automated test or more examples, practical applicability is unclear.
- Positivity and support issues are treated briefly (strict positivity over complete cases). Many real settings violate this; extensions or sensitivity to near-violations should be discussed.
- Clarity and presentation: notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., multiple overlapping uses of primes and r-subscript sets), figures are essential but not available in the submission, and references have numerous formatting errors and missing details. These hinder reproducibility.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Can you provide an explicit algorithm (or decision procedure) for constructing admissible sequences that include R, or certifying their nonexistence? What is its complexity?
- For Theorem 2, how are R partitions and Mb sets computed in general ADMGs with bi-directed edges, and how sensitive is the recovery formula to misidentifying collider paths?
- Can you include simulations demonstrating recovery vs. EM/MI/IPW in MNAR scenarios, and showing the causal/probabilistic asymmetry (e.g., Example 3) in finite samples?
- How robust are your estimands to mild misspecification of the m-graph (given Mohan & Pearl, 2014)? Any diagnostic or sensitivity analysis recommended?
- Please clarify the scope of the “R is not a parent of V or U” assumption with respect to each theorem, and discuss whether any results extend when R → V is allowed.

Pros
- Solid theoretical contributions within an important, under-explored niche (MNAR with explicit mechanisms).
- Useful negative results (non-recoverability criteria).
- Clear takeaways for attrition with necessary-and-sufficient conditions.

Cons
- No empirical evaluation or practical tooling; limited guidance for practitioners.
- Some results feel incremental over prior NIPS work; novelty could be better evidenced.
- Presentation issues and heavy reliance on appendix proofs and missing figures.

Recommendation: While the theoretical insights are promising, the paper in its current form lacks empirical validation and algorithmic concreteness expected for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental section, provide explicit procedures and additional illustrative examples, and improve clarity. As it stands, I lean toward rejection, with potential for a strong resubmission after addressing the above.
