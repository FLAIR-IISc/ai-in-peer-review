This paper presents an extension of missingness graphs (m-graphs) to address the recovery of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data. While the topic is undeniably important and the extension of existing theory is valuable, the presentation suffers from a high degree of technical density, a lack of clear motivation for certain choices, and numerous minor (but collectively substantial) flaws in notation and presentation.

The core idea – extending sequential factorization and demonstrating the potential for recovering causal effects even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable – is a positive contribution. However, the paper frequently prioritizes mathematical generality over intuitive explanation. Many theorems are stated without sufficient context or justification, leaving the reader struggling to understand their practical implications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper exhibits a high degree of mathematical rigor in extending the framework of m-graphs.
*   **Novelty:** The demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not is a potentially significant result.
*   **Comprehensive Treatment:** The paper attempts a broad coverage of different scenarios, including simple attrition and more complex missing data mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The writing is excessively dense and often lacks intuitive explanation. The sheer number of definitions and theorems, presented in rapid succession, makes the paper difficult to follow. Several sentences are awkwardly worded.
*   **Notation Inconsistency:** Notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For instance, the definition of *R* variables is initially presented clearly, but the shorthand notations (e.g., *Rw*, *Wo*, *Wm*) are introduced without sufficient explanation of their intended scope, leading to potential ambiguity. The use of primed variables (X') to denote X=0 is particularly jarring and could be more clearly defined.
*   **Lack of Motivating Examples:** While examples are provided, they are often buried within the theoretical development and do not effectively illustrate the practical significance of the theorems. The attrition example in Section 7 is the most effective, but more such illustrations would be beneficial.
*   **Minimality Requirement:** The emphasis on minimality in Definition 2 feels somewhat arbitrary. While mathematically correct, the rationale for this requirement is not adequately explained.
*   **Theorem 3’s Conditions:** The conditions in Theorem 3 for non-recoverability feel somewhat weak. A more comprehensive characterization of non-recoverability could be more useful.
*   **Extensive Use of 'Remarkably':** The repeated use of "Remarkably" feels unnecessary and detracts from the scientific tone.
*   **Overly Detailed Related Work:** The related work section is exhaustive but reads as a laundry list rather than a focused discussion of relevant contributions. The discussion of other methods (ML, imputation) feels superficial.
*   **Minor Errors/Omissions:** The paper contains a number of minor errors (e.g., grammatical issues, typos) and omissions (e.g., missing references).

**Specific Concerns:**

*   In Section 2, the explanation of V* and R variables could be more intuitive. The purpose of the proxy variable V* isn't immediately clear.
*   Equation (1) is presented without sufficient discussion of the underlying assumptions.
*   In Example 1, the steps involved in rewriting P(X,Y,Z) are not fully explained. The jump from one equation to the next requires more justification.
*   The definition of d*-separation in Section 6, while correct, is presented abruptly and without sufficient context.
*   Theorem 5 in Section 7 feels disconnected from the preceding discussion and lacks a clear motivating example.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The paper extends a valuable theoretical framework (m-graphs) to address an important problem (missing data).
*   The results on recovering causal effects are potentially significant.
*   The paper is technically rigorous.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is extremely difficult to read and understand.
*   The notation is inconsistent and confusing.
*   The presentation lacks clarity and intuitive explanation.
*   The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its potential, this paper is currently unsuitable for NIPS. The lack of clarity, the inconsistent notation, and the sheer density of technical details make it inaccessible to a broad audience. While the core ideas are valuable, they are obscured by the presentation.  A major revision addressing the clarity and readability issues, along with careful attention to notation and presentation, is required before this paper could be considered for acceptance. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors need to focus on making the work accessible and demonstrate its practical relevance with more compelling examples.
