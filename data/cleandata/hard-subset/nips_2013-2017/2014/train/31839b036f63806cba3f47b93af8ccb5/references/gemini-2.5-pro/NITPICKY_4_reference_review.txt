This paper extends the graphical modeling framework for missing data, known as m-graphs, introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The goal is to establish conditions under which probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from datasets with missing values, particularly in Missing Not At Random (MNAR) scenarios. The main contributions are: (1) a generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) a new theorem for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning missingness variables; (3) novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a topic largely unaddressed in prior work; and (4) a demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying joint distributions are not, with a specific application to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem in all empirical sciences. The use of a formal graphical language to diagnose and handle missing data is a powerful and principled approach.
*   The contribution of providing necessary conditions for recoverability (Theorem 3) is significant. Sufficient conditions tell us what is possible, but well-defined criteria for non-recoverability are arguably more valuable in practice, as they prevent researchers from attempting impossible estimation tasks.
*   The key result in Section 6—that recoverability of a causal query `P(y|do(x))` does not require recoverability of the corresponding probabilistic query `P(y,x)`—is insightful and important. It highlights a crucial distinction and opens up new avenues for causal inference in the presence of complex, non-ignorable missingness.
*   The application to attrition is concrete and demonstrates the utility of the proposed framework. The authors make a strong case for their criteria being more accurate than those in existing literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]), which, if correct, is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's interesting theoretical contributions are unfortunately undermined by a significant lack of care in its writing, organization, and formatting. The number of minor and not-so-minor flaws is substantial and gives the impression of a hastily prepared manuscript.

1.  **Clarity and Precision:** The paper suffers from numerous instances of imprecise or confusing technical definitions, which is a critical flaw for a theoretical paper.
    *   **Definition 5 (Inducing Path):** The definition provided ("every node on the path is a collider and an ancestor of either X or Y") is non-standard. The classical definition of an inducing path (Verma and Pearl, 1991) is different. The authors seem to be conflating it with what they earlier call a "collider path" (a path where every *intermediate* node is a collider). This ambiguity is critical, as Theorem 4 and Theorem 6 rely on the concept of an inducing path. Which definition is being used? This must be clarified.
    *   **Theorem 1:** The notation in condition (2) is opaque. `Xr`, `RXm`, and `RXi` are not explicitly defined, forcing the reader to guess their meaning (e.g., is `RXi` the set of R-variables for the variables in the set `Xi`?).
    *   **Theorem 2:** The key definitions of `collider path`, `R(part)`, and `Mb(R(i))` are provided *after* the theorem statement. This is poor organization and makes the theorem statement difficult to comprehend on first reading.
    *   **Definition 4 (d*-separation):** The definition is presented so tersely as to be uninformative to anyone not already deeply familiar with the work of Shpitser and Pearl [2008]. Key notation like `Gw` is used without being defined.

2.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a general lack of polish that is not up to the standard expected at NIPS.
    *   **Reference List:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is riddled with formatting errors, inconsistent styling, and strange artifacts (e.g., `\Q2002\E`). Several entries are garbled, such as the one for Allison: "Statistical Methods in Medical Research,, Allison.,? \Q2002\E, Allison.,". This is unacceptable and makes it difficult to even identify the cited works.
    *   **Citations:** In-text citation style is inconsistent (e.g., `(Mohan et al. [2013])` vs. `Mohan et al. [2013]`).
    *   **Typos:** There are numerous distracting typos and grammatical errors, such as "An path p" (Definition 5), a stray leading period in the title of Section 6.1, and an incorrectly placed citation in Footnote 1.

3.  **Organization:** The flow of the paper could be improved. As noted, defining concepts after they are used in a theorem is confusing. Similarly, the introduction of dense shorthand notation in Section 2 could be handled more gently, perhaps defining terms closer to where they are first applied.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents novel, non-trivial theoretical results on the important problem of missing data.
*   The insights regarding the separation of probabilistic and causal recoverability are significant and advance the state of the art.
*   The introduction of graphical non-recoverability criteria is a valuable and original contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is sloppy. The high number of errors, from critical definitional ambiguities to severe formatting problems in the bibliography, demonstrates a lack of care that falls far below the expected standard for a top-tier conference.
*   The lack of clarity in key definitions (especially "inducing path") raises concerns about the technical soundness of the theorems that rely on them. Without precise definitions, the results are not verifiable.
*   The poor presentation makes the paper unnecessarily difficult to read and evaluate, hindering its potential impact.

**Recommendation:**
While the core ideas in this paper are promising and potentially significant, I must recommend **rejection** in its current form. The sheer number of flaws in clarity, precision, and presentation is too substantial to overlook. These are not minor issues that can be fixed with a few wording changes; they require a thorough and careful revision of the entire manuscript. The authors should be strongly encouraged to revise the paper to fix the ambiguous definitions, clean up the notation, reorganize the presentation of theorems, and completely overhaul the bibliography. A polished version of this work could be a very strong contribution, but this submission is not yet ready for publication.
