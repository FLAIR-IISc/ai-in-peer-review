### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), introduced by Mohan et al. [2013], for analyzing and recovering statistical quantities from data with missing values. The work moves beyond the common MCAR and MAR assumptions to provide a principled, graphical approach for the more general Missing Not At Random (MNAR) setting. The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, now applicable to models where missingness mechanisms can influence each other (Theorem 1).
2.  A novel method for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness indicators and their Markov blankets (Theorem 2).
3.  New, formally-defined graphical criteria that are necessary for recoverability, allowing researchers to identify situations where a query is provably *non-recoverable* under the given model assumptions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).
4.  A key theoretical insight showing that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, particularly when the outcome variable is inseparable from its missingness mechanism (Section 6, Theorem 4).
5.  An application of these results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims made in prior work (Section 7, Theorems 5 & 6).

This work builds directly upon the graphical model framework for causality (Pearl [2009]) and its recent application to missing data problems (Mohan et al. [2013]), providing a significant expansion of the theory and its practical applicability.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data, which affects virtually all experimental sciences. By providing a formal, graphical language to reason about complex MNAR scenarios, this work offers a powerful alternative to traditional methods that often rely on restrictive or untestable assumptions. The ability to determine recoverability from the graphical structure of the assumed data generating process is a major step forward for principled data analysis.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper presents several novel and important contributions. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorems 3 and 5) are particularly valuable; much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for recovery, but providing clear, graphical tests for *non-recoverability* is a critical tool for practitioners to understand the limits of their data and assumptions. The finding that causal queries can be recoverable when their probabilistic counterparts are not is a deep and non-obvious result that elegantly connects the theories of causal identification (do-calculus) and missing data.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The proposed theorems are derived within the well-established formalisms of graphical models and do-calculus. The definitions are precise, and the arguments logically connect graphical criteria to statistical recoverability. The application to attrition demonstrates a strong command of the problem domain and showcases the power of the proposed framework to clarify and correct existing results in the literature.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the derivations are compelling, the practical impact of the work would be significantly strengthened by including simulations. For instance, the authors could generate data from a known m-graph (e.g., Figure 2(b) or Figure 3), introduce missingness according to the model, apply the recovery formulas from Theorems 2 or 4, and show that the resulting estimates converge to the true population parameters as sample size increases. This would not only provide an empirical sanity check for the theorems but also serve as a concrete guide for practitioners looking to implement these methods.

2.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is very dense and assumes a high level of expertise in graphical models, causality, and the specific m-graph framework. While appropriate for a specialized audience, its accessibility could be improved.
    *   **Intuition:** More intuition behind the conditions of the main theorems would be beneficial. For example, the condition `R(i) ∩ RMb(R(i)) = ∅` in Theorem 2 could be explained more intuitively as a requirement that a group of missingness mechanisms is not "self-causing" through its substantive Markov blanket.
    *   **Practical Roadmap:** The paper presents a collection of powerful but distinct theorems. A practitioner might be unsure which one to apply. A summary table or a flowchart that maps graphical features (e.g., "Are R-variables connected?", "Is there a collider path from X to Rx?") to the appropriate theorem or conclusion (e.g., "Use Theorem 1," "Use Theorem 2," "P(V) is non-recoverable by Theorem 3") would be an invaluable addition for guiding users.

3.  **Minor Definitional Clarification:** The definition of the Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))` in Section 4 is slightly non-standard, defined as substantive variables that are "neighbors or connected to variables in R(i) by a collider path." It would be helpful to briefly clarify how this relates to the standard definition (parents, children, and spouses) within the context of semi-markovian models to avoid potential confusion.

### **Decision Rationale**

This paper presents a strong, original, and significant theoretical contribution to the fields of graphical models, causality, and statistics. It provides a principled and powerful framework for reasoning about missing data under complex MNAR assumptions. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the crucial insight into the recoverability of causal effects in the face of non-recoverable probabilistic distributions are major advances. While the paper would benefit from empirical validation and improved accessibility for a broader audience, these are addressable points that do not detract from the core quality and importance of the theoretical results. The work is a solid fit for NeurIPS and is likely to inspire further research in this critical area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant, cross-disciplinary problem.
*   Presents multiple novel and non-trivial theoretical results.
*   Technically sound and rigorously argued.
*   Clarifies and corrects previous work in the specific domain of attrition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Purely theoretical, lacking any experiments or simulations to validate the proposed estimators.
*   High technical barrier to entry may limit its immediate impact on practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The strengths, particularly the originality and significance of the theoretical contributions, far outweigh the weaknesses. The suggestions for improvement can be incorporated into a final version to further enhance the paper's impact.
