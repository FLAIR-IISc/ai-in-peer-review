- Generalizes Mohan et al.’s m-graphs by incorporating missingness indicators (R) into an ordered/sequential factorization (Theorem 1) that handles interactions among R nodes.  
- Introduces a new recovery strategy for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, partitioning R into components and conditioning on Markov blankets (Theorem 2; Corollary 1 for no-latent-confounding subclass).  
- Provides graphical non-recoverability criteria that give necessary conditions for joint and conditional recoverability, ruling out neighbor or all-collider paths between a variable and its R (Theorem 3; Corollary 2).  
- Shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, using d*-separation/dormant independence (Example 3; Theorem 4).  
- Gives necessary-and-sufficient graphical criteria for recoverability in simple attrition settings (|Vm|=1), correcting prior claims (Theorems 5 and 6).  
- Situates the work within and extends prior literature on MAR/MCAR, likelihood/EM, IPW/doubly-robust methods, and causal identification/do-calculus.  
- Conceptual advance: demonstrates nontrivial cases where causal estimands are identifiable under MNAR despite non-identifiable probabilistic relations.  
- Broadens scope of identifiability tools by extending ordered factorization to interacting R mechanisms and offering an alternative recovery route when admissible sequences fail.  
- Provides practical guardrails via non-recoverability theorems that help practitioners detect impossible recovery problems.  
- Attrition results are clear, practically relevant, and likely to be widely used.  
- Claims of providing “algorithms” are overstated; paper offers constructive expressions but lacks an explicit decision procedure or complexity/completeness statements.  
- Theorem 2’s restrictions (no neighbor/collider paths between Vm and R) are strong; need discussion of prevalence and handling of partial violations.  
- Focuses on recoverability but lacks guidance on finite-sample estimation (estimators, variance, positivity, regularization) making practical application harder.  
- Notation and presentation issues: some terse/inconsistent notation and ambiguous mutilation notation; positivity should be defined precisely and set shorthands standardized.  
- Proofs are relegated to the appendix; brief proof sketches or key lemmas in the main text would improve accessibility.  
- More illustrative examples (including cases where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 succeeds) and small simulations would strengthen understanding and empirical relevance.  
- Several citations are inconsistently formatted and should be cleaned.  
- Technically solid and careful, with proofs supporting claims and clear lineage to identifiability tools.  
- Generally readable for experts but dense; clarity would improve with more intuition, figures, and a decision flow.  
- High originality in extending m-graphs, new recovery approach, non-recoverability criteria, and the causal-versus-probabilistic recoverability result.  
- High theoretical significance for causal inference with MNAR and timely attrition results, with potential wide impact if algorithmic clarity is added.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical advances, fills gaps, corrects attrition misconceptions, and supplies useful non-recoverability conditions.  
- Cons for acceptance: algorithmic claims exceed provided detail, limited finite-sample estimation guidance, and clarity/formatting issues.  
- Questions for authors: please provide a unified decision procedure combining Theorems 1–6 and complexity guarantees.  
- Questions for authors: include an explicit example where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 succeeds with step-by-step recovery.  
- Questions for authors: discuss sensitivity to violations of strict positivity over complete cases and possible remedies.  
- Questions for authors: will you release code that takes an m-graph and query and returns recoverability checks and recovered expressions?  
- Recommendation: Accept conditional on strengthening algorithmic presentation and clarity; reviewer’s confidence is high.
