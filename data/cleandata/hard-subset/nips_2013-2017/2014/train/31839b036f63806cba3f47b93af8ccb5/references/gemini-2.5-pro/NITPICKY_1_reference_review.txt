**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values, specifically focusing on the Missing Not At Random (MNAR) setting. The authors build upon their previous work (Mohan et al., 2013) on 'missingness graphs' (m-graphs) to provide a more comprehensive theoretical framework. The key contributions are: (1) An extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms. (2) A new theorem for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables and their Markov blankets. (3) Novel graphical criteria for determining when a joint or conditional distribution is *non-recoverable*. (4) A significant and counter-intuitive result showing that causal effects, `P(y|do(x))`, can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distribution, `P(y,x)`, is not. (5) An application of these results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, where they provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correct claims made in prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Theoretical Results:** The paper presents a series of strong theoretical contributions. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) is particularly valuable, as it provides researchers with a tool to know when recovery is provably impossible under their stated assumptions. This is a crucial and often overlooked aspect of the missing data problem.

2.  **Key Insight on Causal vs. Probabilistic Queries:** The demonstration in Section 6 that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying joint distribution is not is a powerful and important finding. It highlights a fundamental distinction between observational and interventional queries in the context of missing data and could have significant implications for causal inference in practice. The use of do-calculus and d*-separation to formalize this is elegant.

3.  **Concrete Application and Refinement of Prior Work:** The application to attrition is a clear and compelling use case. By providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery in the "simple attrition" case (Theorems 5 and 6), the authors offer a complete solution for an important sub-problem. Furthermore, their graphical counterexample to a condition proposed by Garcia [2013] demonstrates the increased precision and utility of their framework over existing approaches.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial, the manuscript is marred by a significant number of flaws in clarity, organization, and presentation that detract from its quality and make it difficult to parse.

1.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   Several key definitions are introduced *after* they are used in theorems. For instance, the definitions for `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` are given after their appearance in Theorem 2. Similarly, `Pasub(R(i))` is defined after its use in Corollary 1. This forces the reader to jump back and forth and hinders a linear understanding of the results.
    *   The notation is at times dense and potentially confusing. For example, in Section 2, `V` is used to denote both the set of all vertices in the graph `G(V, E)` and the set of substantive variables. The notation for removing edges (`GX` with over/under bars) is used without being explicitly defined in the text, assuming familiarity that not all NIPS readers may possess.
    *   The link between the theoretical conditions and the resulting estimands could be made more explicit. In Example 1, the paper states "by theorem 1 we have," followed by a final equation. The intermediate steps showing precisely how each factor `P(Y|X,Z,R'x, R'y, R'z)` and `P(Z|R'z)` satisfies the conditions of Theorem 1 are omitted, requiring the reader to perform this verification themselves.

2.  **Technical and Formatting Inconsistencies:**
    *   There are several minor grammatical and typographical errors. For example, in Section 2, "Missingness graphs as discussed below was first defined" should be "were first defined". In Section 3, "theorem-4" should be capitalized as "Theorem 4" for consistency. The subsection heading in Section 6 is labeled ".1" instead of "6.1".
    *   The citation style is inconsistent within the text, with both `(Mohan et al. [2013])` and `Mohan and Pearl [2014]` being used.
    *   The **reference list is fundamentally broken**. It appears to be the result of a failed BibTeX compilation, with extraneous characters (`.,?`, `\Q`, `\E`), repeated author names, and inconsistent formatting throughout. This is a major oversight that suggests a lack of careful preparation of the manuscript and makes it impossible for a reviewer to properly check the cited literature.

3.  **Assumptions:** The paper presents some conditions with very strong underlying assumptions without fully discussing their limitations. For instance, Corollary 1 assumes that "no latent variable is a common parent of X and any member of R". This rules out many plausible real-world scenarios where unmeasured factors could influence both the substantive variables and the probability of missingness. A discussion of the implications of this assumption would strengthen the paper.

**Decision**

This paper contains ideas of high originality and significance that would be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working on causality and graphical models. The result distinguishing the recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries is especially noteworthy.

However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. The poor organization, notational ambiguities, and especially the completely garbled reference section demonstrate a lack of care that falls below the standards expected for a top-tier conference. A substantial number of minor flaws, when aggregated, constitute a major barrier to understanding and trusting the work. While the core ideas are strong, the execution of the paper is weak. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors are strongly encouraged to undertake a major revision focusing on clarity, organization, and presentation before resubmitting to a future venue.
