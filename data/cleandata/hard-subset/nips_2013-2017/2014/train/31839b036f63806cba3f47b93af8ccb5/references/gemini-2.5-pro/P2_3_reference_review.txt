### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), for analyzing and recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work is a direct and substantial extension of the authors' previous research (Mohan et al. [2013]), which introduced the m-graph formalism and the concept of recoverability. The primary goal is to provide graphical criteria to determine if a target quantity (e.g., a joint distribution, a conditional probability, or a causal effect) can be consistently estimated from incomplete data, particularly in "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) scenarios.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Generalization of Sequential Factorization:** The paper extends the factorization theorem from Mohan et al. [2013] to handle more complex scenarios where missingness mechanisms (represented by R-variables) can influence each other, a common situation in longitudinal studies.
2.  **New Recovery Conditions:** It introduces a novel theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails. This is based on partitioning the missingness mechanisms and using their Markov blankets.
3.  **Novel Non-Recoverability Criteria:** A significant contribution is the introduction of formal graphical criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) that *preclude* recoverability. These conditions, based on paths between a variable and its missingness indicator, are crucial for identifying when a query is fundamentally unanswerable given the model assumptions.
4.  **Decoupling Causal and Probabilistic Recoverability:** Perhaps the most insightful result is demonstrating that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distributions (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. This is achieved by leveraging tools from do-calculus.
5.  **Application to Attrition:** The paper applies these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting what it claims is a misconception in prior work (Garcia [2013]).

This work positions itself within the causal inference literature pioneered by Pearl, using graphical models to make assumptions about the data-generating process explicit. It stands in contrast to traditional statistical methods for missing data (e.g., Multiple Imputation, Maximum Likelihood) which often rely on blanket assumptions like "Missing At Random" (MAR) without a formal language to express or verify the more complex dependencies that might exist in MNAR settings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are presented as formal theorems, and the authors state that proofs are provided in the appendix. The framework is internally consistent and builds logically upon established concepts in graphical models and causality (d-separation, do-calculus, inducing paths). The formalization of non-recoverability is a particularly strong and complete piece of work.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its complete lack of empirical validation. While this is a theoretical work, the claims could be substantially strengthened with simulations. For example, demonstrating that the estimation formula from Theorem 2 converges to the true parameter in a simulated setting, while a naive approach like complete-case analysis fails, would provide compelling evidence for the theory's correctness and utility. Similarly, showing a case where the non-recoverability criteria hold and demonstrating that different underlying data generating processes compatible with the m-graph lead to different estimates would powerfully illustrate the concept of non-recoverability. The paper also operates under the strong assumption that the m-graph itself is correctly specified, a non-trivial requirement in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and follows a logical progression from probabilistic to causal queries. The use of examples to illustrate the application of theorems (e.g., Example 1 for Theorem 1) is helpful. However, the manuscript is extremely dense. It introduces a significant amount of specialized notation and terminology (e.g., `R(part)`, `Mb(R(i))`, d*-separation) that will be challenging for readers not already deeply familiar with Pearl's causal inference framework. While the definitions are provided, more intuitive explanations accompanying the formalisms would greatly improve accessibility. The core ideas are powerful, but their presentation could be a barrier to broader impact.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on Mohan et al. [2013], the extensions are non-trivial and significant. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* is, to my knowledge, a novel contribution to the field and addresses a critical gap. The most original and striking insight is the demonstration that causal effect recoverability does not imply probabilistic query recoverability. This insight elegantly connects the distinct concepts of statistical estimability under missingness with causal identifiability, using the tools of do-calculus in a novel context. This is a clear and important departure from previous work which often conflates these issues.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is potentially very high. Missing data is a ubiquitous and fundamental problem in machine learning and all empirical sciences. This paper provides a principled, assumption-explicit framework for reasoning about what can and cannot be learned from incomplete data, especially in complex MNAR settings where standard methods fail. The results for causal queries are particularly impactful. Researchers in fields like epidemiology, economics, and social sciences frequently face attrition and missing data in their attempts to estimate causal effects. This work provides them with a formal language and tools to assess if their research questions are answerable from their data. The ability to recover a causal effect even when the outcome variable's distribution is non-recoverable is a powerful and non-obvious result that could change how analyses are conducted in these areas.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents novel, rigorous, and important theoretical results on a fundamental problem.
*   **High Originality:** The non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal/probabilistic recoverability are significant and original insights.
*   **High Potential Impact:** Provides a principled framework for handling MNAR data, with direct applications to challenging real-world problems like attrition in causal studies.
*   **Advances the State of the Art:** Substantially moves beyond existing work on graphical models for missing data and provides a more nuanced understanding than the traditional MAR/MCAR/MNAR trichotomy.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **No Empirical Validation:** The lack of any simulations or experiments is a notable weakness. The practical performance and behavior of the proposed estimators are not demonstrated.
*   **Limited Accessibility:** The paper is very dense and uses highly specialized notation, which may limit its readership and immediate uptake by practitioners.
*   **Assumes Correct Model:** The entire framework rests on the assumption of a correctly specified m-graph, a strong prerequisite whose feasibility is not discussed.

### Conclusion

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes significant and original contributions to the challenging problem of inference from missing data. Its key results—especially the formal non-recoverability criteria and the demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even when observational distributions cannot—are insightful and important. While the lack of empirical validation is a drawback and the presentation is dense, the quality, originality, and significance of the theoretical work are substantial. It provides a foundational framework that future research can build upon. I recommend acceptance.
