

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to develop a comprehensive graphical approach for handling missing data problems. The authors present general conditions for recoverability of probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and demonstrate a key insight: causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when associated probabilistic relations (such as P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable. The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions:

1. It refines the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other (Section 3)
2. It develops general algorithms for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization fails (Section 4)
3. It introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Section 5)
4. It demonstrates that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated (Section 6)
5. It applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies (Section 7), providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for causal effect recovery

The work builds upon Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl and Mohan (2013), extending their framework to handle more complex missingness mechanisms while providing a rigorous foundation for understanding recoverability. The paper also relates to the broader literature on missing data (Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002) and causal inference (Pearl, 2009; Shpitser and Pearl, 2006).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Depth and Rigor**: The paper makes substantial theoretical contributions with well-defined theorems and proofs. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle interacting missingness mechanisms is a significant improvement over prior work. The conditions for recoverability are carefully articulated and justified.

2. **Novel Insight on Causal Queries**: The key insight that causal queries can be recovered even when associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable (Section 6) is particularly valuable. The example in Figure 3 and Theorem 4 demonstrate this counterintuitive result with mathematical rigor, challenging the prevailing assumption that causal effects require recoverable probabilistic relations.

3. **Comprehensive Coverage**: The paper addresses both probabilistic and causal queries, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability across a wide range of missing data scenarios. The application to attrition problems in Section 7 is especially relevant to real-world longitudinal studies.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The definitions are precise, the notation is consistent, and the examples (e.g., Example 1) effectively illustrate the application of the theorems.

5. **Practical Relevance**: The results have direct implications for fields like epidemiology, social sciences, and clinical trials where missing data and attrition are common challenges. The necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery in attrition problems (Theorem 6) rectify prevailing misconceptions in the literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Validation**: While the paper provides theoretical results, it lacks empirical validation on real datasets. The claims about recoverability are purely theoretical, and including some synthetic or real-data examples would strengthen the paper by demonstrating practical applicability.

2. **Complexity of Framework**: The m-graph framework is quite complex, with many new definitions and conditions. While necessary for the theoretical development, this complexity may limit accessibility for practitioners. A brief discussion of implementation challenges or potential software tools would be helpful.

3. **Comparison with Existing Methods**: The related work section (Section 8) covers relevant literature but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of the proposed approach with existing methods for handling missing data (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting). A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the advantages and limitations of the proposed framework.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is important for practical applications with large datasets.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

*Pro Arguments:*
- The paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data analysis
- The insights about causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic relations are not are novel and important
- The results provide a comprehensive framework for understanding recoverability conditions
- The paper is well-written and the theoretical development is rigorous
- The application to attrition problems has clear practical relevance

*Con Arguments:*
- Lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess practical utility
- The framework is quite complex and may have limited accessibility for practitioners
- The paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison with existing methods for handling missing data
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for the proposed algorithms

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to the field of missing data analysis. The authors have extended the m-graph framework in meaningful ways, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries. The key insight about causal queries being recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not is particularly valuable and represents a substantial advance over prior work.

The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses an important problem in data analysis. While it lacks empirical validation, this is common for theoretical papers in machine learning and causal inference, especially when the focus is on developing a general framework rather than a specific algorithm.

The paper's contributions are sufficiently novel compared to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere. The related work section adequately covers relevant literature, and the paper clearly articulates how it extends previous work. The theoretical results advance the state of the art in graphical models for missing data.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contributions are substantial, the paper is well-written, and the insights have significant implications for both the theory and practice of missing data analysis. This work would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief empirical demonstration on a small synthetic dataset to illustrate the practical application of the theoretical results.
2. Provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed framework with existing methods for handling missing data (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting).
3. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly for large-scale datasets.
4. Add a brief discussion of implementation challenges and potential software tools that could be developed based on this framework.

## Conclusion

This paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the area of missing data analysis using graphical models. The results provide a comprehensive framework for understanding when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from incomplete data. The insights about causal queries being recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not are particularly valuable and represent a substantial contribution to the field. I believe this paper would make an excellent contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
