This paper extends the graphical model framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) from Mohan et al. [2013] to analyze the recoverability of queries from datasets with missing values. The primary contributions are: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) a new theorem for recovering joint distributions when factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness variables; (3) novel graphical criteria that preclude recoverability; and (4) a demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic distributions are not, particularly in cases where a variable is not separable from its missingness mechanism. These results are then applied to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

The work is situated within the causal inference literature on missing data, building directly on the authors' prior work and extending it to more complex scenarios. It contrasts with the broader machine learning literature, which often assumes data are Missing at Random (MAR) and employs methods like Maximum Likelihood Estimation or Multiple Imputation. This paper's focus is on the more challenging Missing Not at Random (MNAR) setting, providing a formal, graphical language for reasoning about recoverability.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of non-ignorable missing data from a principled, model-based perspective.
*   The establishment of graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3) is a valuable contribution, providing researchers with a tool to quickly determine when their assumptions are insufficient for consistent estimation.
*   The result in Section 6—that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when P(y|x) is not—is a significant and counter-intuitive finding. It highlights the distinct nature of causal and statistical queries in the context of missing data.
*   The application to attrition in Section 7 provides concrete, necessary and sufficient conditions for a common problem and corrects a claim in the existing literature (Garcia [2013]), demonstrating the utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite these strengths, the paper is beset by a substantial number of flaws in clarity, presentation, and technical exposition that, in aggregate, undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Self-Containment:** The paper is exceptionally dense and assumes a high degree of familiarity with the authors' previous work (Mohan et al. [2013]), making it very difficult for a general NIPS reader to follow. Key definitions (e.g., m-graph components, recoverability, compatibility) are reviewed too briefly. The distinction between `Rw` and `Wr` is subtle and would benefit from an explicit example. The introduction of new, complex concepts is often abrupt and lacks sufficient intuition. For example, Theorem 2 introduces `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` with highly technical definitions but provides little intuition for what these partitions and blankets represent in practice. Similarly, Section 6 invokes advanced concepts like "d*-separation" and "dormant independence" with only a citation, leaving the reader unable to follow the logic of Theorem 4 without consulting external papers.

2.  **Lack of Polish and Attention to Detail:** The manuscript suffers from a general lack of care, most egregiously in the reference section. The bibliography is riddled with severe formatting errors, including stray LaTeX-like commands (e.g., "? \Q2002\E"), inconsistent author formatting ("Dempster et al\.," vs. "Little and Rubin."), and repeated author names ("Allison.,? ..., Allison.,"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference and calls into question the overall diligence with which the work was prepared.

3.  **Methodological Nuances:**
    *   The justification for the "minimality" condition in Definition 2 relies on a single illustrative example. A more formal discussion of why this specific condition is critical for the admissibility of a sequence would strengthen the argument.
    *   The footnote on page 2 regarding datasets that are not strictly positive over complete cases ("is sometimes feasible") is vague and dismissive of a potentially important edge case. Under what conditions is it feasible?
    *   Theorems 2 and 4 provide existence criteria for recoverability ("if no Mb(R(i)) contains...", "if there exists Z,W such that..."). However, they offer limited constructive guidance on how to find these sets or partitions in a complex graph, which may limit their practical applicability beyond the small examples provided.

4.  **Organization and Readability:** The flow of the paper feels more like a technical report than a conference paper. All proofs are relegated to an appendix, which is standard, but the main text lacks even high-level proof sketches or intuitions that would help the reader build confidence in the results without constant cross-referencing. The introduction of example 1 after Theorem 1 is helpful, but similar intuitive walkthroughs are missing for the more complex Theorems 2 and 4.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in experimental science (MNAR data).
*   Presents novel theoretical results, particularly the separation of recoverability for causal vs. probabilistic queries.
*   Provides useful negative results (non-recoverability criteria).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper is poorly written, dense, and not self-contained, severely limiting its accessibility and impact.
*   The lack of attention to detail, especially the broken reference list, falls far below the quality standard for NIPS.
*   The contribution feels incremental over prior work (Mohan et al. [2013]) and its practical utility is not fully demonstrated beyond small, stylized examples.
*   Key concepts are introduced without adequate explanation, requiring the reader to be a domain expert in this specific subfield.

**Recommendation:**

Reject.

While the research direction is valuable and the core ideas appear to have merit, the execution is deeply flawed. A scientific paper must not only present correct results but must also communicate them clearly and professionally. This manuscript fails on the communication front. The combination of dense, inaccessible writing and a shocking lack of presentational polish makes it unsuitable for publication at NIPS in its current form. The paper requires a thorough revision to improve its clarity, self-containment, and basic formatting before it can be reconsidered.
