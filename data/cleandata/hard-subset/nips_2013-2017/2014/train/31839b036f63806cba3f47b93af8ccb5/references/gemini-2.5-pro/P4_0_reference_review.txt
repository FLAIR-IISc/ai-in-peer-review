This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called missingness graphs (m-graphs), to analyze the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work is a direct and substantial extension of Mohan et al. [2013], which introduced the m-graph formalism and the foundational concept of recoverability. The authors' main contributions are: (1) a generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other; (2) a new theorem for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not applicable, based on partitions of the missingness variables; (3) novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, identifying conditions under which a query is impossible to estimate consistently; and (4) an analysis of causal query recoverability, demonstrating the key insight that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic query P(y, x) is not. Finally, these results are applied to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The problem of missing data is fundamental and pervasive across all empirical fields that use machine learning. The paper moves beyond the common MAR/MCAR assumptions and provides a principled, model-based framework for reasoning about the more complex MNAR (Missing Not At Random) case. The development of formal criteria for when a query can or cannot be recovered is a significant contribution, as it can prevent researchers from applying methods that yield biased or meaningless results. The insight that causal queries can be recoverable when their probabilistic counterparts are not is particularly important, connecting the fields of missing data and causal inference in a non-trivial way.

2.  **Originality:** The paper presents several novel theoretical results. While it builds upon the m-graph framework of Mohan et al. [2013], the extensions are non-trivial and address important gaps. The generalization of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and the alternative recovery method (Theorem 2) expand the applicability of the framework. Most notably, the introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) appears to be a new and valuable contribution to the literature. This provides a formal basis for determining when a problem is unsolvable under the given model assumptions, a crucial but often overlooked aspect. The application to attrition also demonstrates novelty by correcting and clarifying claims in existing work (e.g., Garcia [2013]).

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The contributions are presented as formal theorems and corollaries, building logically upon established concepts from graphical models and causality (e.g., d-separation, do-calculus, Markov blankets). The arguments presented in the main body are clear and well-reasoned, and the examples provided (e.g., Example 1 and 3) effectively illustrate the utility and mechanics of the proposed theorems. The proofs are deferred to an appendix, which is standard practice. The proposed conditions are graphical, making them transparent and interpretable, which is a major strength of this type of analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, there are no experiments on either synthetic or real-world data. Such experiments would be valuable to: (a) demonstrate the practical application of the recovery algorithms derived from the theorems, (b) assess the robustness of the methods to near-violations of the graphical conditions, and (c) provide a comparison against standard methods (e.g., imputation, full information maximum likelihood) to show in which scenarios the proposed approach offers a tangible advantage. Without this, the practical significance is somewhat diminished.

2.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense and assumes a high level of expertise in graphical models, causality (specifically do-calculus), and the preceding work by Mohan et al. [2013]. Concepts such as "collider path," "partitions of R variables (Rpart)," and "Mb(R(i))" are introduced very rapidly in Section 4 with minimal exposition, which could make the paper challenging for readers not already deeply familiar with this specific subfield. While the writing is precise, a bit more intuition behind the definitions and theorems could broaden the paper's accessibility and impact.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not discuss the practical challenges of applying this framework. The entire analysis hinges on the correctness of the specified m-graph, which encodes strong, often untestable assumptions about the data generating and missingness processes. The paper provides no guidance on how a practitioner might construct or validate such a graph from domain knowledge, or how sensitive the conclusions are to misspecification of this graph.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in machine learning.
*   Provides several novel, significant, and technically sound theoretical results.
*   The introduction of formal non-recoverability criteria is a particularly strong and original contribution.
*   The connection between the recoverability of causal vs. probabilistic queries is an important insight.
*   The results advance the state-of-the-art in principled reasoning about non-ignorable missing data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work is purely theoretical, lacking any empirical validation to demonstrate its practical utility or robustness.
*   The presentation is dense and may be inaccessible to a broad NIPS audience outside of the specialized causality community.
*   Practical issues regarding the specification of the underlying m-graph are not addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong theoretical paper that makes several novel and significant contributions to the formal understanding of missing data. The technical quality is high, and the results, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the analysis of causal queries, are important for the field. The primary weakness is the complete absence of empirical results, which limits the assessment of its practical impact. However, for a conference like NIPS that values foundational theoretical advances, the novelty and significance of the contributions are substantial. The paper provides a valuable new set of tools for researchers dealing with the complex problem of missing data.
