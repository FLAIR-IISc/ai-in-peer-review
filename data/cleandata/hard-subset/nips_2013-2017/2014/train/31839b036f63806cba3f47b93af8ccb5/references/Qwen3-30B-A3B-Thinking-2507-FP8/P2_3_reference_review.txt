

# Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

This paper introduces "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal framework for modeling missing data processes and determining when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from incomplete datasets. The authors significantly extend the work of Mohan et al. (2013) by providing general conditions for recoverability of probabilistic queries, demonstrating that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable, and applying these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors provide clear definitions, theorems, and proofs that build upon and extend previous work. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 4, which demonstrates that causal queries may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated—a significant insight that challenges previous assumptions in the field. The paper also correctly identifies and addresses limitations in Mohan et al. (2013), particularly regarding cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another.

The paper's theoretical framework is well-constructed, with careful attention to the distinctions between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries. The examples provided (particularly Example 3) effectively illustrate the key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when the underlying distributions are not. The proofs referenced in the appendix provide sufficient technical detail for experts to verify the claims.

The paper is careful to distinguish between necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability (e.g., Theorems 2, 5, and 6), which demonstrates methodological rigor. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that the framework assumes the m-graph is correctly specified.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with a clear progression from background to technical contributions. The definitions are precise and consistent with the causal inference literature, and the examples effectively illustrate the abstract concepts. The paper's structure follows a logical flow: it introduces the problem, presents theoretical foundations, develops new results, applies them to attrition problems, and situates the work within the literature.

The writing is clear and accessible to researchers familiar with causal inference, though the paper assumes some background knowledge in graphical models and causal inference. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the theoretical results. The figures (particularly Figures 2-4) are well-designed and effectively complement the text. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly well-explained through the examples.

## Originality

This paper makes several important original contributions to the field of missing data. The most significant contribution is demonstrating that causal effects may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4 and Example 3). This challenges the prevailing assumption that causal recoverability requires probabilistic recoverability, which is a substantial theoretical advance.

The paper also provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic (Theorems 2-3) and causal (Theorems 5-6) queries under simple attrition, which rectifies previous misconceptions in the literature (as noted in Section 7.2). The authors correctly identify that Garcia (2013) incorrectly assumed that X being an ancestor of Ry was necessary for non-recoverability, when in fact the collider path between Y and Ry is the critical factor.

The paper's focus on graphical criteria for recoverability represents a significant advance over previous work that relied on assumptions like MAR or MCAR. While Mohan et al. (2013) introduced m-graphs, this paper substantially extends their framework to handle more complex missingness mechanisms and provides a more comprehensive characterization of recoverability.

## Significance

Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, making this work highly significant. The paper provides a formal framework that researchers can use to determine whether their specific missing data problem allows for consistent estimation of target queries. This has practical implications for experimental design, data analysis, and interpretation of results in fields where missing data is common (e.g., clinical trials, social sciences, longitudinal studies).

The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly important for causal inference, as many researchers work with causal questions but may not realize that causal effects might be recoverable even when the underlying distributions are not. This insight could change how researchers approach missing data in causal studies.

The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is especially timely, as attrition is a major challenge in longitudinal studies across many fields. The necessary and sufficient conditions provided in Theorems 5 and 6 represent a substantial advance over previous approaches that relied on weaker assumptions or failed to provide complete characterizations.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong theoretically, it lacks empirical validation. The authors present a theoretical framework but do not demonstrate its application to real datasets, which would strengthen the practical significance of the work. Additionally, the paper doesn't address computational aspects of implementing the recovery algorithms, which would be important for practitioners.

The related work section (Section 8) is somewhat brief and could better engage with recent literature on missing data in machine learning. For instance, the work on collaborative filtering with non-random missing data (Marlin et al., 2007, 2009, 2011) could be more directly contrasted with the current approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in handling missing data. The paper makes significant original contributions by demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not, and by providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in various settings. The theoretical results are rigorously developed, clearly presented, and address a fundamental problem in data analysis.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a formal framework that researchers can use to determine whether their specific missing data problem allows for consistent estimation of target queries. The paper's insights will likely influence how researchers approach missing data in causal inference studies.

I recommend acceptance. This paper represents a significant theoretical advance in the field of missing data, with clear implications for both theoretical and applied research. The contributions are substantial, well-justified, and well-positioned within the literature. While empirical validation would strengthen the work further, the theoretical contributions alone are sufficient for a high-quality NIPS paper.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Significant theoretical advance showing causal queries can be recovered when probabilistic queries cannot
- Comprehensive characterization of recoverability conditions for probabilistic and causal queries
- Clear distinction between necessary and sufficient conditions
- Direct application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
- Well-structured and well-written theoretical contribution

*Weaknesses:*
- Limited empirical validation (though not required for a theoretical paper)
- Brief related work section that could better engage with recent literature
- No discussion of computational implementation challenges

This paper makes a clear and substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of missing data problems, which aligns well with NIPS's focus on theoretical advances in machine learning and statistics.
