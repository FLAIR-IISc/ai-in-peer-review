Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors: (i) generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R and provide a strengthened sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) enabling recovery in models where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) develop a new recovery result (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1) for joint distributions when admissible sequences do not exist, based on partitioning R variables according to adjacency/collider-path structure and conditioning on their Markov blankets; (iii) present graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint (Theorem 3) and conditional (Corollary 2) distributions; (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x), P(x,y)) are not, using do-calculus and dormant independences (Theorem 4 and Example 3); and (v) give necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability under simple attrition (|Vm|=1) for both joint (Theorem 5) and causal (Theorem 6) queries, correcting claims in prior work (Garcia 2013). The work relates to graphical identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006) and extends the m-graph program (Mohan et al. 2013; Mohan & Pearl 2014), while contrasting with traditional MAR/MCAR, IPW, ML/EM, and MI approaches (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002; Lauritzen 1995; Robins et al. 1994).

Strengths
- Technical contributions: Extends the ordered factorization approach by explicitly incorporating R variables and minimal conditioning sets, enabling recovery in settings with interacting missingness mechanisms (e.g., R variables with edges). Theorem 2 provides a principled construction for recovery without admissible sequences via R-partitions and Markov blankets, which seems novel within the m-graph literature. The non-recoverability criteria add useful guardrails that have been underdeveloped in prior work. The attrition results provide crisp necessary and sufficient conditions, improving practical guidance.
- Conceptual advance: The demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable when the outcome is inseparable from its missingness mechanism is important and counters common intuitions tied to probabilistic recoverability. The use of do-calculus and d*-separation/dormant independence to exploit Ry=0 conditioning is well-motivated.
- Clarity of goals and structure: The paper is organized around clear questions (when and how to recover), with formal definitions and constructive estimands. Examples illustrate the mechanisms behind the theorems.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Formal clarity and readability: Some definitions and conditions (e.g., Theorem 1 condition (2) involving Yi=Rz, constraints on Xi and R sets) are terse and non-intuitive. Provide a step-by-step algorithm (with complexity discussion) to search for admissible sequences under Definition 2 and Theorem 1, including how to enforce minimality and how to decide when to include R variables in the ordering. Similarly, for Theorem 2, include pseudocode for constructing R-partitions, computing Mb(R(i)) in ADMGs, and checking the condition R(i) ∩ R_Mb(R(i))=∅.
- Scope and completeness: Theorem 1 is sufficient but not necessary; Theorem 2 covers a class of semi-Markovian models but imposes the precondition that no partially observed variable is adjacent to, or collider-connected with, its own missingness mechanism. Clarify the coverage of remaining cases (e.g., are there models outside Theorems 1/2/5 where joint distributions are recoverable, and can your methods detect them?). For non-recoverability, Theorem 3 provides necessary conditions for recoverability (hence sufficient for non-recoverability), but they are not complete; discuss the gap and whether stronger graphical impossibility results are possible.
- Causal recoverability details: Theorem 4 relies on d*-separation and identifiability of P(W1, Z|do(X)) and P(Y|do(W1),do(X),Z,Ry=0). Provide guidance or examples on verifying these conditions mechanically (e.g., via ID/IDC algorithms), and explicitly state required independences for Rule-2/Rule-3 steps in Example 3. Include the missing citation to Shpitser & Pearl (2008) on dormant independence.
- Empirical illustration: While theory-focused papers are appropriate for NeurIPS, a small synthetic study demonstrating finite-sample behavior of the proposed estimands versus standard MAR-based methods under MNAR would strengthen significance and clarity, especially for attrition scenarios.
- Notational and editorial issues: Several typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., GX and GX both used; “.1 Recovering...” section header; occasional mismatch of variables x/z; punctuation in references). Clean up references (years, venues, missing entries), add the 2008 dormant independence citation, and ensure figures are fully legible with stated assumptions.

Quality
- The claims are generally well-motivated and consistent with established causal graphical reasoning. The provided estimands align with equation (1) and do-calculus logic. Without proof verification, the results appear technically sound; however, clarity on algorithmic procedures, completeness boundaries, and explicit conditions in Theorem 4 would improve rigor for readers.

Clarity
- Overall readable and logically organized, but some conditions are opaque on first reading. More intuition, diagrams, and procedural descriptions would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Extends recoverability via ordered factorizations to include R nodes, introduces new recovery formulas when no admissible sequence exists, and formalizes non-recoverability criteria. The necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition and the explicit contrast between probabilistic and causal recoverability are original and valuable.

Significance
- Missing data is central across ML and causal inference. The graphical criteria and constructive estimands can influence both methodology and practice, particularly beyond MAR. The attrition results address a common applied need.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances the state of theory for MNAR using graphs with new sufficient conditions and impossibility criteria.
- Clarifies the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability, enabling causal estimation under challenging missingness.
- Offers crisp, testable conditions for attrition problems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some conditions and procedures are not easily actionable; lack of algorithmic detail may hinder adoption.
- No empirical demonstrations to complement theory.
- Editorial and reference issues, and some missing citations, reduce polish.

Overall recommendation
- Leaning toward accept, contingent on revisions to improve clarity, add algorithmic guidance, correct citations, and, if possible, include a brief empirical illustration.
