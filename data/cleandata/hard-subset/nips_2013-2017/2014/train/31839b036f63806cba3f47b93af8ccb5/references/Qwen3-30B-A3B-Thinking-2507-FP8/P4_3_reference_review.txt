

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to provide general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build upon the formal representation of missingness processes introduced in prior work, extending the framework to handle complex scenarios where missingness mechanisms interact. The key contributions include general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions), demonstration that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not, new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical results. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs, with complete proofs in the appendix. The paper logically extends Mohan et al. (2013) by addressing cases where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., edges between R variables), which previous work could not handle. The examples (e.g., Example 1, Example 3) effectively illustrate the application of theorems, particularly the distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries. The authors carefully address limitations, noting their conditions are necessary for recoverability in specific problem classes.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written with logical progression from foundational concepts to complex results. Precise definitions (m-graphs, recoverability, general ordered factorization) are consistently applied throughout. The examples enhance understanding of abstract concepts, and the connection between theory and practical application (attrition problems) is well-explained. The writing is technical but accessible to researchers familiar with causal inference and graphical models.

**Originality:** The paper makes several significant contributions. The key novelty is demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not, challenging the prevailing view that causal effects cannot be recovered if underlying probabilistic relations are not recoverable. This represents a substantial theoretical advance beyond Mohan et al. (2013). The paper also introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3), which addresses an important gap in the literature. The application to attrition problems provides concrete evidence of the framework's practical value.

**Significance:** Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, and this paper provides a formal framework for understanding when queries can be recovered from missing data. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries has important implications for researchers, as it shows causal effects may be estimable even when standard probabilistic approaches fail. The application to attrition problems (common in longitudinal studies) demonstrates practical relevance. The paper advances the state of the art in missing data analysis by providing a more comprehensive framework for handling missingness mechanisms, particularly in cases where missingness mechanisms interact.

**Strengths:** Clear extension of prior work on missingness graphs; important distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries; new graphical criteria for non-recoverability; practical application to attrition problems; well-structured theoretical development with supporting examples; complete proofs provided.

**Weaknesses:** Limited discussion of computational complexity of recovery algorithms; minimal connection to practical implementation (e.g., software tools); comparison with existing methods beyond the related work section could be more explicit.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
Pro: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions that extend the state of the art in missing data analysis. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries is particularly novel and valuable. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant problem in statistical analysis.

Con: The paper's primary focus is theoretical, with limited discussion of practical implementation challenges and computational considerations.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of missing data analysis. The theoretical advances in understanding when causal queries can be recovered despite non-recoverable probabilistic relations represent a significant step forward. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and provides a valuable extension to the missingness graphs framework. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly noteworthy, as it addresses a fundamental limitation in previous work. The application to attrition problems demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical results. Given its technical soundness, clarity, and significant theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance.
