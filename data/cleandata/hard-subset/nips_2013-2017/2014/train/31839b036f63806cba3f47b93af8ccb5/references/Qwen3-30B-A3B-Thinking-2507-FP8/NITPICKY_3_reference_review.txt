

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends Mohan et al. [2013] by developing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using "missingness graphs" (m-graphs). The authors present sufficient conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions (via sequential factorization), necessary conditions for non-recoverability, and demonstrate that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not. The paper also applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing recoverability conditions for causal queries under missingness, which is an important gap in the literature. The extension of Mohan et al. [2013] to handle interactions between missingness mechanisms (R variables) is well-motivated. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly insightful and addresses a key limitation in existing work.

## Weaknesses

### Notational Inconsistencies (Critical)
The paper suffers from severe notational inconsistencies that undermine clarity and rigor:

1. Inconsistent use of primes: The paper uses R', R′, R'x, and R'x interchangeably without clear definition. For example, in equation (1) "R′x denotes Rx = 0" but later uses "R′y" without this definition.

2. Ambiguous set notation: The definition of "Rw" (as "Rw is the set containing missingness mechanisms of all partially observed variables in W") contradicts its use in Theorem 1 where "Rw" appears in "Xr∩RXm = ∅" without defining the subscript relationship.

3. Confusing variable definitions: The statement "V is partitioned into Vo and Vm such that Vo ⊆ V" is logically inconsistent since Vo is defined as a subset of V, making "Vo ⊆ V" redundant. Similarly, "substantive variables" are defined as X ∈ Vo∪Vm, but Vo and Vm are already subsets of V.

### Technical Ambiguities
1. Theorem 1 states "Yi⊥⊥(Ryi , Rxi)|Xi \{Ryi , Rxi}" but doesn't clarify whether Rxi refers to the missingness mechanism of Xi or some other variable. This ambiguity makes the condition impossible to verify.

2. The minimality requirement in Definition 2 is crucial but not adequately explained. The example shows why minimality matters, but the paper fails to provide a formal justification for why non-minimal factorizations fail.

3. Theorem 2 states "R(i) ∩RMb(R(i)) = ∅" but RMb(R(i)) is not defined. The paper later defines Mb(R(i)) as "the markov blanket," but this standard term has a precise meaning that differs from what's used here.

### Minor but Significant Errors
1. In equation (1): "v∗i = f(rvi , vi) = {vi if rvi = 0 m if rvi = 1" - the "m" is undefined and should be replaced with a proper missingness indicator (e.g., "m" should be "m_i" or "missing").

2. The paper claims "P (X,Y, Z) = P (Y |X,Z,R′x, R′y, R′z)P (Z|R′z) ∑ Ry P (X|R′x, Ry)P (Ry)" but the summation "∑ Ry" should be "∑_{Ry}" for clarity.

3. The reference to "Appendix 10.6" appears without the appendix, making verification impossible.

### Insufficient Novelty
The paper's most significant claim—that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not—is demonstrated through a single example (Example 3). However, the proof relies on do-calculus without explicitly connecting the conditions to the graphical criteria. The paper does not clearly demonstrate why this is a substantial advance over Mohan et al. [2013] or Garcia [2013].

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some valuable insights, the severe notational inconsistencies and technical ambiguities undermine its credibility and reproducibility. The paper's claims cannot be properly evaluated due to unclear definitions and inconsistent notation. The minor methodological improvements presented are overshadowed by these fundamental issues.

The authors should address these notational and definitional problems before the paper can be considered for publication. As it stands, the paper's technical contributions are difficult to assess due to the lack of clarity in its presentation. The reviewers should not be required to decipher inconsistent notation to evaluate the paper's scientific merit.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite with consistent notation, clear definitions of all symbols, and precise mathematical statements. The current version does not meet the technical standards expected at NIPS.
