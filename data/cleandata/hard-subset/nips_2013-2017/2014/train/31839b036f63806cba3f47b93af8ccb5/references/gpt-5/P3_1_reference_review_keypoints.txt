- Extends m-graphs by generalizing ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R and gives a refined sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1).  
- Provides a new recoverability result for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists using partitions of R and Markov blankets via collider paths (Theorem 2) and a corollary for models without latent parents of R.  
- Introduces necessary conditions for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2).  
- Shows identifiable causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when related probabilistic queries are not, using do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4).  
- Gives necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1 in attrition/monotone missingness cases for both joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6).  
- Theoretical contributions appear sound and nontrivial, correcting and strengthening prior ordered factorization results.  
- The constructive estimand in Theorem 2 is intuitive and operational when its conditions hold.  
- Non-recoverability criteria are valuable for practitioners to know when recovery is impossible.  
- Causal recoverability results are insightful, demonstrating estimable causal effects despite probabilistic inseparability.  
- Attrition results clarify and correct misconceptions in applied literature with clean necessary and sufficient characterizations.  
- Many core results lack proof sketches; short intuitions in the main text would aid verification by readers.  
- Several conditions and notations are intricate or ambiguous (e.g., collider vs. inducing paths; minimality constraints); clearer definitions and consistency are needed.  
- The paper lacks empirical demonstrations validating estimands under finite samples and showing failures when conditions are violated.  
- Notation-heavy and occasionally hard-to-parse clauses (e.g., in Theorem 1); recommend consistent earlier definitions for symbols like Xr, RXm, Rz.  
- Manifest compatibility needs a clearer definition and an illustrative table mapping V, V*, R.  
- Collider path definition varies across sections; ensure a consistent definition (colliders vs. substantive colliders).  
- Figures are heavily relied upon and should be fully annotated (latent bidirected edges, districts) and include examples where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 succeeds.  
- Novelty: general ordered factorization with R, Theorem 2’s partition/Markov blanket approach, non-recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability demonstrations are original.  
- Significance: results are actionable for MNAR problems in ML and causal inference, guiding whether to use IPW/EM/MI or redesign data collection.  
- Questions for authors: algorithmic search for admissible sequences and complexity; scope/completeness of Theorem 2; extension of Theorem 4 to multiple partially observed variables; explicit positivity/support assumptions; a practitioner decision flowchart.  
- Suggestions: add proof sketches in the main text, include synthetic experiments, provide pseudocode for searching admissible sequences and constructing R partitions/Mb(R(i)), discuss bounds/sensitivity/partial identification, and fix notation and references.  
- Pros: solid theoretical advances, new non-recoverability criteria, complete attrition characterizations, high relevance to MNAR settings.  
- Cons: no empirical validation, dense/unclear notation, limited discussion of algorithmic complexity and completeness.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution likely to influence the field; recommend acceptance pending clarity improvements and minimal empirical illustration.
