This paper addresses the important problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data, leveraging the framework of missingness graphs (m-graphs). While the topic is undeniably relevant and builds upon prior work by Mohan et al. and Pearl, I find the presentation and justification for the extended theoretical results to be lacking in rigor and practical demonstration. The paper introduces several theorems and corollaries, significantly extending the existing framework, but these extensions feel largely theoretical without sufficient evidence of their practical utility or comparison to established methods for handling missing data.

The core idea of extending sequential factorization to incorporate interactions between missingness mechanisms is sound, but the complexity introduced by the generalized definitions (Definition 2) and theorems (1 & 2) doesn’t appear to translate into a clear algorithmic advantage. The criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 & Corollary 2), while interesting, feel more like theoretical elaborations of existing concepts than groundbreaking new insights. The section on attrition (Section 7) similarly presents theoretical results but lacks a compelling demonstration of how these results outperform standard attrition handling techniques like multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting.

A significant weakness is the absence of empirical evaluation. While the authors acknowledge related work that focuses on empirical approaches, this paper remains entirely theoretical. No simulations or real-world datasets are used to validate the proposed theorems and demonstrate their practical effectiveness. It's difficult to assess the relevance of these theoretical advancements without knowing how they perform in comparison to existing, well-established methods. Specifically, it's unclear how much computational overhead is introduced by these new methods, and whether the benefits (if any) outweigh these costs. The example in Figure 3, while illustrating a scenario where causal effects are recoverable despite non-separability, lacks the concrete numerical validation one would expect.

The paper’s connection to broader machine learning literature on missing data is somewhat superficial. While references are provided, there’s little discussion of how this graphical model approach compares to, for instance, imputation methods that explicitly model the missingness mechanism or approaches based on doubly-robust estimation.  Furthermore, the presentation suffers from a density of notation and definitions, making it challenging to follow the logical flow of the arguments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in statistical inference.
*   Provides a formal, graphical framework for reasoning about missing data.
*   Extends existing theoretical results on recoverability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks empirical validation—no simulations or real-world data analysis.
*   Theoretical extensions feel abstract and lack clear practical guidance.
*   Comparison to existing methods for handling missing data is limited.
*   Heavy notation and complex definitions make the paper difficult to follow.
*   Limited demonstration of advantages over established techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis using graphical models. However, the complete lack of empirical evaluation and the absence of a clear demonstration of practical benefits raise serious concerns. While the theoretical framework is interesting, it remains unclear whether these advancements will translate into meaningful improvements in real-world applications.  I recommend **rejection** at this time, but would encourage the authors to revise the paper with a strong empirical evaluation component and a more detailed comparison to existing methods. The current manuscript feels incomplete and more like a theoretical exploration than a finished piece of work ready for a conference like NIPS.
