**Summary:**
This paper investigates the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. It builds upon the authors' previous work (Mohan et al., 2013) which introduced "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal tool to represent dependencies between variables and their missingness mechanisms. The main contributions of this paper are: (1) A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering probabilistic queries, extending its applicability to cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. (2) A new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible. (3) The introduction of novel graphical criteria for determining when joint and conditional distributions are *non-recoverable*. (4) An analysis of causal query recoverability, demonstrating the key result that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the corresponding joint distribution P(x, y) is not. The authors apply these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data, which is of high importance to the machine learning and statistics communities.
*   The work is theoretically rigorous, presenting a series of formal definitions and theorems to establish conditions for recoverability. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a particularly valuable contribution, as it provides a formal tool to identify when a query is impossible to estimate consistently, potentially saving researchers from fruitless efforts.
*   The insight that causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic quantities are not (Section 6) is non-trivial and interesting. It highlights a key distinction between statistical and causal inference in the context of missing data.
*   The formalization of the problem using graphical models provides a clear language for articulating assumptions about the missingness process, moving beyond the standard MAR/MCAR/MNAR trichotomy.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary and most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of empirical validation. The work is entirely theoretical, presenting conditions and algorithms for recoverability without demonstrating their performance in practice. This raises several critical questions:
1.  **Comparison to Established Baselines:** The literature on missing data is mature, with well-established and widely used methods such as Multiple Imputation (MI) and Expectation-Maximization (EM) for maximum likelihood estimation. These methods are known to provide consistent estimates under MAR assumptions and are the standard toolkit for most practitioners. This paper does not provide any simulation studies or experiments on real datasets to compare its proposed recovery algorithms against these standard baselines. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed graphical approach offers any practical advantage in terms of accuracy, efficiency, or robustness, especially in finite-sample settings.
2.  **Practicality of Estimators:** The paper demonstrates that if a query is recoverable, an estimand exists in terms of the observed data distribution. However, it does not discuss the statistical properties of the resulting estimators (e.g., variance, finite-sample bias). A theoretical guarantee of asymptotic consistency is a necessary first step, but for a contribution to be significant for the NIPS community, some consideration of practical estimator performance is expected.
3.  **Sensitivity to Model Specification:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the practitioner can correctly specify the true underlying m-graph. This is a very strong assumption. In practice, causal discovery is a hard problem, and specifying the dependencies involving unobserved missingness mechanisms is even harder. The paper does not address how sensitive its recoverability results are to misspecifications in the m-graph. Established methods are often more assumption-lean, or at least the assumptions (like MAR) are more widely understood.

A secondary weakness is that the paper reads as an incremental extension of the authors' prior NIPS publication. While the generalizations are non-trivial, the core framework and concepts are not new. This, combined with the lack of empirical work, makes the contribution feel more suited for a specialized theoretical journal than for a broad-audience conference like NIPS, which values demonstrated impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides a rigorous, formal treatment of a difficult and important problem.
*   The non-recoverability criteria are a novel and useful theoretical tool.
*   The results concerning the dissociation of causal and probabilistic query recoverability are a significant theoretical insight.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The complete lack of experimental results or simulations makes it impossible to judge the practical significance or utility of the proposed methods.
*   The work is not compared against any established, widely used methods for handling missing data (e.g., MI, EM).
*   The approach relies on strong, untestable assumptions about the correctness of the graphical model, and its robustness to misspecification is not analyzed.
*   The contribution is an incremental extension of the authors' own prior work.

**Conclusion:**
While this paper presents a set of technically sound and interesting theoretical results for missing data problems using a formal graphical framework, its lack of empirical validation is a critical flaw. Methodological soundness is paramount, but for a new approach to be considered, it must be rigorously tested against established methods. The authors propose a framework but offer no evidence that it leads to better practical outcomes than standard techniques like Multiple Imputation or EM. Without any simulations or real-data analysis, the claims of the paper remain entirely in the theoretical realm, and their practical significance is unproven. For this reason, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The work would be substantially strengthened by the inclusion of an experimental section that validates the proposed recovery algorithms and compares them against standard baselines in various missing data scenarios.
