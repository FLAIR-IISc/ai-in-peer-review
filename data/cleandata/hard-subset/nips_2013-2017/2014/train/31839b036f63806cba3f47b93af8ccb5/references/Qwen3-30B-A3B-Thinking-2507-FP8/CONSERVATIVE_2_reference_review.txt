

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by proposing a formal graphical framework (m-graphs) for determining recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors introduce theoretical conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions and demonstrate that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations aren't, particularly in cases where missingness mechanisms interact.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically coherent contribution to the literature on missing data by formalizing recoverability conditions through graphical models. The definitions of m-graphs, recoverability, and the sequential factorization theorem are well-structured and build logically on Mohan et al. (2013). The paper's theoretical framework is technically sound, with clear proofs and examples that illustrate the necessity of the proposed conditions. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly interesting, as it challenges the conventional wisdom that causal effects require recoverable probabilistic relations.

## Weaknesses
Despite the theoretical soundness, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its contribution to the field:

1. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no experiments on real or simulated data. Without demonstrating how these methods perform compared to established approaches (e.g., multiple imputation, maximum likelihood under MAR), it's impossible to assess practical utility. The claim that "causal effects may be recoverable even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism" remains unverified without empirical evidence.

2. **Limited practical relevance**: The paper fails to provide clear guidance on how practitioners could implement these methods. The examples (e.g., Figure 3) are abstract and don't translate to real-world applications. For instance, the claim that "P(y|do(z)) is recoverable" in Figure 3 is purely theoretical without demonstrating how this would be computed in practice or how it would compare to existing methods.

3. **Insufficient comparison to established methods**: While the related work section references relevant literature (e.g., Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002), it doesn't adequately contrast the proposed approach with standard missing data techniques. The paper claims to "rectify prevailing opinion" (Garcia, 2013) but doesn't provide evidence that their conditions would outperform existing methods in practice.

4. **Overstated significance**: The paper overstates the importance of its contribution. The conditions for causal recoverability (Theorem 4) appear to be narrow cases that wouldn't apply to most real-world missing data problems. The paper doesn't demonstrate how common these "inseparable path" scenarios are in practice.

5. **No discussion of computational complexity**: The paper presents algorithms for recovery but doesn't address their computational feasibility. For large datasets with complex missingness mechanisms, these algorithms might be intractable, which would severely limit practical utility.

## Comparison to Existing Work
The paper builds on Mohan et al. (2013), which itself extended prior work on missing data. However, it doesn't convincingly demonstrate how its approach improves upon existing methods. The literature on missing data (e.g., Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002; Marlin et al., 2011) has established that under MAR assumptions, standard methods like multiple imputation or maximum likelihood produce consistent estimates. The current paper's contribution seems to be a theoretical extension to specific MNAR cases, but without empirical validation, it's unclear whether these cases are practically significant.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the literature on missing data, but it fails to meet the high standards expected at NeurIPS. The lack of empirical validation is particularly problematic, as it's impossible to assess the practical value of the proposed conditions. The paper reads more like a theoretical exercise than a contribution with practical relevance to machine learning and statistics practitioners.

I recommend rejection for the following key reasons: (1) The paper makes strong claims about recoverability without providing empirical evidence, (2) It fails to demonstrate how these methods would be implemented or how they compare to established approaches, and (3) It doesn't convincingly argue for the significance of its contribution over existing work.

The authors should consider conducting empirical experiments to validate their theoretical claims before resubmitting. Without these experiments, the paper remains an interesting theoretical exercise but lacks the practical relevance required for a NeurIPS publication. The field would benefit from a paper that demonstrates the practical utility of these graphical conditions, but this submission does not provide that evidence.
