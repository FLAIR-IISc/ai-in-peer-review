### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. It builds upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The main contributions are: (1) A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms. (2) A new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets. (3) The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions. (4) A significant finding that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, which occurs when the outcome variable Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism Ry. (5) The application of these results to the specific problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting a claim in prior literature.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Important Theoretical Contribution:** The central result of the paper, demonstrating that causal effect recoverability does not require probabilistic query recoverability (Section 6), is a novel and powerful insight. It decouples two problems often treated as linked and suggests that causal inference from incomplete data may be feasible in a wider range of scenarios than previously thought. This is particularly relevant for experimental sciences where missing outcomes are common.
2.  **Increased Generality:** The paper successfully extends the m-graph framework to more complex and realistic missing data scenarios. Theorem 1, which allows for edges between R-variables, and Theorem 2, which tackles models without an admissible sequence, substantially broaden the applicability of this graphical approach.
3.  **Formal Non-Recoverability Conditions:** The introduction of necessary graphical conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) is a valuable contribution. Identifying situations where recovery is impossible is as important as providing methods for when it is possible, and this aspect is often underdeveloped in the literature.
4.  **Rigorous Application and Correction:** The application to attrition (Section 7) is concrete and useful. The derivation of necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5 and 6) provides clear guidance for practitioners. Furthermore, the identification of a flaw in a previous result (Garcia [2013]) demonstrates a careful and critical engagement with the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous minor but distracting issues in clarity and formatting that collectively detract from its quality.
    *   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is severely flawed, containing parsing errors (e.g., "? \Q2002\E") and inconsistent formatting. This is unacceptable for a conference submission and suggests a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Notational Ambiguity:** In Section 2, the notation for graph surgery, `GX` and `GX`, is presented identically in the text, presumably due to a typesetting error in rendering overbars/underbars. This creates immediate confusion.
    *   **Minor Typos and Grammatical Errors:** Errors such as "An path p..." in Definition 5 are present. The footnote on page 2 is an incomplete sentence. The subheading ".1" in Section 6 appears to be a formatting error.
    *   **Density of Material:** The paper introduces a large amount of complex notation and terminology very quickly. Theorem 2, for instance, relies on several new definitions (collider path, R(part), Mb(R(i))) that make the theorem statement difficult to parse on a first read. More intuition-building examples could significantly improve readability.

2.  **Technical Nuances and Completeness:**
    *   **Definition Precision:** The definition of an "inducing path" (Definition 5) seems simplified. The standard definition by Verma and Pearl (1991) is more nuanced. While perhaps equivalent in this context, the authors should clarify if their definition deviates and why, or use the standard one to avoid confusion.
    *   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for some NIPS papers, the claims would be substantially strengthened by even a small-scale simulation. For example, demonstrating an algorithm based on Theorem 2 recovering a joint distribution, or showing the recovery of a causal effect from simulated data corresponding to Figure 3, would provide a valuable proof of concept and aid in reader comprehension. Without this, the practical applicability of these complex graphical criteria remains abstract.

3.  **Originality:** While the paper presents several novel theorems, it is framed as a direct and incremental extension of the authors' own prior work (Mohan et al., 2013). The core framework (m-graphs, the definition of recoverability) is imported. The novelty is concentrated in the specific extensions and the important results in Section 6, but the work may be perceived as less of a standalone contribution.

---
**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The core result in Section 6, decoupling causal and probabilistic recoverability, is a significant and novel theoretical contribution with important implications for causal inference.
*   The paper provides a more complete theoretical picture of recoverability in the m-graph framework by providing both new sufficient conditions (Theorems 1, 2) and necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3).
*   The work is theoretically rigorous and addresses a difficult and important problem in machine learning and statistics.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The paper is marred by a substantial number of presentation flaws, including a deeply problematic reference section, notational ambiguities, and typos. This lack of polish makes the technical content difficult to trust and suggests a rushed submission.
*   The absence of any experimental or simulation-based validation makes it hard to assess the practical utility or feasibility of the proposed recovery procedures.
*   The work is a highly incremental extension of previous work by the same authors, which might limit its perceived novelty.

**Decision:**
I lean towards **rejection**. While the theoretical contributions, particularly the results on causal query recovery, are strong and of interest to the NIPS community, the paper's overall quality is brought down by a significant number of flaws in its presentation. The state of the bibliography is particularly concerning and is not a minor issue. These problems are not merely cosmetic; they impede clarity and give the impression of a lack of care, which is worrying for a dense theoretical paper whose claims rest on detailed proofs. The lack of any empirical validation further weakens the submission. I believe the core ideas are valuable, and I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to address these presentation issues and consider adding a simulation study before resubmitting to a future conference.
