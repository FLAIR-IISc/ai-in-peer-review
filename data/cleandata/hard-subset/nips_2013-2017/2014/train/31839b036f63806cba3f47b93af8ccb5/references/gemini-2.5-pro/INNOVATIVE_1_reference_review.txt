### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the graphical framework of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) for analyzing and recovering statistical queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. [2013], the authors present several significant new contributions. First, they generalize the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex scenarios where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, they introduce a new algorithm for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible. Third, and perhaps most importantly, they establish formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, providing necessary conditions that alert researchers when a query is impossible to estimate given the model assumptions. Finally, the paper explores the recoverability of causal queries, demonstrating the striking result that a causal effect like P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic quantities like P(y,x) are not. The authors apply these findings to the common problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting previous claims in the literature.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold conceptual contributions and its significant expansion of the theoretical frontier for reasoning about missing data.

1.  **Novelty and Significance of Causal Recoverability:** The most compelling contribution is the demonstration that causal queries can be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not (Section 6). This is a profound and non-obvious insight. It decouples the problem of causal inference from the problem of full distributional recovery, suggesting that even in MNAR scenarios deemed hopeless for estimating associations, the causal questions of interest may still be answerable. This has major implications for how researchers in many fields should approach datasets with missing outcomes, particularly in experimental or longitudinal settings. This is precisely the kind of bold, paradigm-shifting idea that this conference should champion.

2.  **Formalizing Non-Recoverability:** The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a crucial and novel step towards a complete theory. Much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for recovery, leaving practitioners in the dark when those conditions are not met. By providing formal necessary conditions, this work helps delineate the fundamental limits of inference under a given set of assumptions. This is a significant service to the community, preventing fruitless attempts to estimate inestimable quantities.

3.  **Theoretical Generalizations:** The paper substantially generalizes previous results. The extension of sequential factorization to allow for dependencies between missingness indicators (R-variables) makes the framework applicable to a much wider class of real-world problems. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a new recovery method for non-factorizable graphs, tackling difficult structures involving colliders and bi-directed edges.

4.  **Principled Approach:** The paper advocates for a rigorous, assumption-explicit approach to a problem that is too often handled with heuristics (e.g., listwise deletion, simple imputation) or overly restrictive assumptions (e.g., MAR). The m-graph framework forces the researcher to be transparent about their assumptions regarding the missingness process, and the theorems provide formal guarantees based on those assumptions.

### Weaknesses

The paper's weaknesses are minor and largely inherent to its nature as a foundational theoretical work.

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical, presenting theorems and proofs without any simulations or experiments on real data. While the theoretical contributions stand on their own, the practical implications would be strengthened by a simulation study. For example, demonstrating that an estimator derived from Theorem 4 consistently estimates a causal effect while naive methods fail spectacularly on a simulated dataset where P(Y) is non-recoverable would provide powerful empirical support for the paper's central claims.

2.  **Density of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and relies heavily on specialized notation and concepts from the graphical models and causality literature (e.g., d-separation, collider paths, do-calculus). While this is necessary for precision, it may limit the accessibility of the work to the broader machine learning audience at NIPS who may not be deeply familiar with this specific formalism. A bit more intuition around the definitions in Section 4, for instance, could be helpful.

### Recommendation

This paper represents a significant advance in the formal understanding of missing data. Its core contributions, particularly the surprising dissociation of causal and probabilistic recoverability and the formalization of non-recoverability criteria, are novel, important, and thought-provoking. It tackles a fundamental problem in machine learning and all empirical sciences with rigor and depth. While the lack of experiments is a limitation, the strength, novelty, and boldness of the theoretical ideas more than compensate. This work provides a new lens through which to view hard missing data problems and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces the novel and highly significant concept that causal effects can be recovered even when the associated distributions are not.
*   Provides the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a crucial step toward a complete theory.
*   Substantially generalizes previous work, making the graphical framework for missing data applicable to a much broader and more realistic set of problems.
*   Presents a rigorous, first-principles approach to a ubiquitous and challenging problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work is purely theoretical, with no empirical validation through simulations or real-world data analysis to demonstrate the practical performance of the derived estimators.
*   The presentation is dense and assumes significant familiarity with specialized concepts, potentially limiting its impact on a general audience.
