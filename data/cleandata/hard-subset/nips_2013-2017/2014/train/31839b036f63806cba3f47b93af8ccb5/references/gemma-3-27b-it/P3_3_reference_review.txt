## NIPS Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) for handling missing data, particularly focusing on conditions for recovering both probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries. It builds upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) and addresses limitations in previous approaches, especially regarding interactions between missingness mechanisms and the recoverability of causal effects even when standard separability conditions are violated. While technically sound and demonstrating significant theoretical contributions, the paper's presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, primarily focused on enhancing readability and providing more intuitive examples.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the pervasive problem of missing data, framing it within the rigorous framework of m-graphs. It extends prior work by refining the sequential factorization theorem to accommodate interacting missingness mechanisms and introducing new criteria for non-recoverability. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries (specifically, P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism – a significant departure from typical assumptions. The work is well-situated within the broader literature on missing data, acknowledging various methods like deletion, maximum likelihood estimation, and inverse probability weighting, and distinguishing itself through its graphical modeling approach and focus on *identifiability* of causal effects.  It clearly builds on and surpasses the previous work by the authors (Mohan et al. 2013, Mohan & Pearl 2014) with deeper theoretical results.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically solid, with clearly stated theorems and proofs (available in the appendix). The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem are well-motivated and address a genuine gap in the existing literature.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The ability to recover causal effects even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism is a significant theoretical advance. This result has practical implications for fields where causal inference is crucial and missing data is common (e.g., epidemiology, social sciences).
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper addresses both the conditions for *recoverability* and *non-recoverability*, providing a more complete picture of the problem.  The introduction of new graphical criteria for non-recoverability is particularly helpful.
* **Application to Attrition:**  Applying the theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition demonstrates the real-world relevance of the work and offers a more nuanced understanding of when causal effects can be reliably estimated in the presence of missing data.
* **Well-Defined Framework:** The use of m-graphs provides a clear and formal way to represent missingness mechanisms and their relationships to observed variables.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:**  The paper is dense and requires significant effort to follow, even for researchers familiar with graphical models and causal inference.  The mathematical notation is heavy, and the numerous theorems and definitions can be overwhelming.
* **Example Complexity:** While examples are provided (e.g., Figure 2, Figure 3), they are often complex and do not immediately convey the intuition behind the theorems.  Simplified examples, perhaps with fewer variables, would greatly improve understanding.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical results. A brief discussion of practical challenges in applying these methods to real-world datasets (e.g., estimating the necessary probabilities, dealing with large m-graphs) would be beneficial.
* **Related Work Depth:** While the related work section is comprehensive, it could benefit from more focused discussion on the practical limitations of existing methods (like multiple imputation) in scenarios where the assumptions underlying those methods are violated.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Definition 2 (General Ordered Factorization):** The concept of "minimality" in this definition is crucial but not immediately intuitive.  Adding a short, illustrative example demonstrating why a non-minimal factorization can fail would be helpful.
* **Theorem 4:** The conditions for recovering P(y|do(x)) when Y and Ry are inseparable are presented but not thoroughly explained. Elaborating on *why* these conditions allow for recovery, perhaps with a more detailed walkthrough of the example in Figure 3, would greatly enhance understanding.
* **Section 7 (Attrition):** The connection between the theoretical results and the practical problem of attrition is strong, but the discussion could be expanded. Specifically, how can practitioners determine if the conditions for recoverability are met in their specific attrition scenarios?
* **Figure 2 (a):** The explanation of why an ordered factorization needs to introduce *Ry* into the order is a bit terse. A more detailed explanation would be helpful.



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data and causal inference.
* Addresses a critical limitation of existing methods regarding the recoverability of causal effects.
* Rigorous mathematical foundation and well-defined framework using m-graphs.
* Demonstrates practical relevance through application to attrition problems.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  The paper's density and lack of intuitive explanations may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The absence of discussion regarding practical implementation challenges.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The paper presents a significant theoretical advance and addresses an important problem. However, improving clarity and providing more intuitive examples would greatly enhance its impact and accessibility. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

*  Simplify the presentation of the key theorems and definitions.
*  Provide more illustrative examples, preferably with fewer variables, to explain the intuition behind the results.
*  Briefly discuss practical challenges and considerations for implementing these methods in real-world datasets.
*  Expand the discussion of attrition to provide guidance for practitioners.
