Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends the m-graph framework of Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) for reasoning about recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missingness. The main contributions are: (i) a refined sequential factorization theorem that generalizes ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators (R variables), enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms influence each other; (ii) a new, more general recoverability result (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models using partitions of R variables and their Markov blankets via collider paths, applicable even when no admissible factorization exists; (iii) new graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint distributions (Theorem 3) and conditional distributions (Corollary 2); (iv) conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, via d*-separation and do-calculus (Theorem 4); and (v) necessary and sufficient conditions for both probabilistic and causal recoverability under simple attrition (|Vm|=1) (Theorems 5–6), challenging some prior claims (e.g., Garcia 2013). Proofs are in the appendix; the body provides intuition and worked examples.

This work builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS’13) and Pearl’s do-calculus, and connects to ADMG/latent variable Markov properties (Richardson 2003). It complements classical MAR/MCAR, ML/EM, IPW/DR, and MI literatures by providing graphical, assumption-explicit criteria that go beyond MAR, and clarifies when graph-blind methods suffice.

Quality (technical soundness and support)
- The results are theoretically oriented, with clear statements of conditions and constructive estimands in several cases (e.g., Example 1, Equations 2–4, Theorems 5–6). The necessity and sufficiency claims for the |Vm|=1 case are valuable.
- The extension of sequential factorization to include R variables is carefully motivated, including the need for minimality in conditioning sets to ensure admissibility.
- The non-recoverability criteria fill a gap in prior work and are stated as necessary conditions for joint recoverability (Theorem 3) and sufficient conditions for conditional non-recoverability (Corollary 2).
- The causal recoverability result when Y and Ry are inseparable is technically interesting and uses established concepts (d*-separation, dormant independences) with constructive estimands.

Concerns:
- Several conditions are subtle and difficult to parse (e.g., Theorem 1 condition (2); definitions of R partitions and Mb(R(i)) in Theorem 2). More intuition, diagrams, or step-by-step algorithms would help practitioners apply them.
- Theorem 2’s assumptions (no neighbor/collider path between any variable and its own missingness mechanism) are strong; the paper should clarify how often these are met and contrast with Theorem 3’s necessary conditions.
- There is no empirical or simulation study to illustrate usability, robustness to graph misspecification, or finite-sample behavior, despite mention of misspecification tests elsewhere.
- Some modeling assumptions are restrictive (semi-Markovian DAGs, no edges R→V/U), which should be justified and discussed regarding scope.

Clarity (presentation and organization)
- The paper is generally well-organized (intro, probabilistic results, non-recoverability, causal, attrition), and examples are helpful.
- However, notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent/unclear: e.g., the statement “R variables may not be parents of variables in V∪U” is central to the class of m-graphs and should be stated more crisply as a modeling assumption; define all shorthand immediately and consistently; clarify difference between Rw and Wr prominently.
- Some theorems would benefit from a short “how to apply” checklist and a running example beside each statement.
- References are poorly formatted and incomplete; fix to NeurIPS format.
- Minor typos and formatting issues (e.g., missing spaces, equation punctuation) detract from readability.

Originality
- Extending ordered factorization to include R variables and handling interacting missingness mechanisms appears novel relative to Mohan et al. (2013).
- The explicit non-recoverability criteria are new and useful.
- Showing causal recoverability despite inseparable Y–Ry paths, with constructive estimands via do-calculus/d*-separation, is an insightful contribution.
- The necessary and sufficient conditions under simple attrition refine prior claims and correct misconceptions in the literature.

Significance
- The results advance the theory of recoverability under MNAR with explicit graphical assumptions and should influence both methodological development and practical analysis of incomplete data, especially in longitudinal/attrition settings.
- Lack of empirical demonstrations and tooling may limit short-term adoption; nevertheless, the theoretical clarity and new criteria are likely to be cited and built upon.

Pros
- Clear theoretical advances beyond prior NIPS work; resolves important open cases (no admissible sequence; interacting R).
- Non-recoverability criteria provide valuable negative guidance.
- Causal recoverability in cases where probabilistic queries fail is both surprising and practically important.
- Necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition.

Cons
- Dense and at times opaque presentation; hard for non-specialists to apply.
- No empirical validation, examples on real/simulated data, or complexity analysis.
- Some assumptions are strong; scope and limitations could be discussed more explicitly.
- Reference formatting and minor writing issues.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a simple procedural checklist or pseudo-code for Theorem 2: how to construct R partitions and Mb(R(i)) and verify conditions; discuss computational complexity.
- Clarify modeling restrictions on edges involving R (no R→V/U) and discuss whether results extend if such edges exist.
- Add at least one simulation demonstrating recovery vs bias under standard methods (complete-case, IPW, MI) across graphs satisfying/failing your conditions.
- Expand on robustness to mild misspecification of the m-graph; can existing tests (Mohan & Pearl 2014) be combined with your criteria?
- Improve definitions and readability of Theorem 1 condition (2) with an intuitive paraphrase and a small illustrative graph.

Overall assessment
Technically strong, original theoretical paper that clearly extends the state of the art on recoverability with missing data and provides new, practically relevant insights for causal queries and attrition. Clarity and empirical illustration could be improved, but the contributions are significant for NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved exposition and references.
