- Significant extension of m-graphs for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Demonstrates causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not identifiable.  
- Generalizes the sequential factorization theorem to handle interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Connects theoretical results to practical attrition problems in longitudinal studies.  
- Built on strong theoretical foundations (m-graphs, do-calculus) with clear theorems and detailed proofs.  
- Key novelty: showing recoverability of causal effects despite non-recoverable joint/probabilistic distributions.  
- Broadens applicability of prior work by handling more complex missingness indicator interactions.  
- Practical relevance emphasized through attrition example and potential impact on observational/experimental research.  
- Thorough and clear positioning within related work, highlighting limitations of ML and MAR approaches.  
- Paper is dense and technically demanding, requiring substantial background in graphical models and causal inference.  
- Notation and rapid argument flow may hinder readers unfamiliar with the literature.  
- Examples are helpful but could be more accessible; visual m-graph depictions and more intuition are needed.  
- Lacks empirical validation—no simulations or real-data analyses to demonstrate performance or scalability.  
- Computational complexity and scalability are not discussed and would strengthen the paper.  
- Corollary 1’s condition R(i) ∩ RPasub(R(i)) = ∅ seems restrictive and needs discussion or relaxation.  
- Theorem 4’s link to d*-separation and dormant independence is an insightful contribution.  
- Section 7’s claim about “rectifying prevailing opinion” should be tempered and more nuanced.  
- Minor typos and grammatical issues present but not substantive.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, addresses key gap, well-grounded frameworks, high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: high technical complexity limits accessibility, lack of empirical work, and dense presentation.  
- Overall score 8.5/10 with recommendation to accept, suggesting added simulations/real-data examples and clearer exposition for broader accessibility.
