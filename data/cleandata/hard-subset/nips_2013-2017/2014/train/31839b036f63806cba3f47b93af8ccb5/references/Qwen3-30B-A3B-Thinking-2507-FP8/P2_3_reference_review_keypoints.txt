- Introduces "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) and a formal framework for modeling missing data and recoverability of queries.
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) with general conditions for recoverability of probabilistic queries.
- Key theoretical insight (Theorem 4): causal queries can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not.
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for probabilistic recoverability (Theorems 2–3).
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for causal recoverability under simple attrition (Theorems 5–6).
- Corrects prior misconceptions (e.g., Garcia 2013) by identifying collider paths, not ancestry, as critical for non-recoverability.
- Clear, rigorous theoretical development with precise definitions, theorems, and proofs; examples (notably Example 3) illustrate concepts well.
- Paper is exceptionally well-organized and accessible to researchers familiar with graphical causal inference; figures effectively support the text.
- Significant practical relevance: informs when consistent estimation is possible in domains with missing data (e.g., longitudinal studies, attrition).
- Strong originality: challenges the assumption that causal recoverability requires probabilistic recoverability and extends graphical criteria beyond MAR/MCAR.
- Lacks empirical validation on real datasets to demonstrate practical applicability.
- Does not address computational implementation or algorithmic aspects for practitioners.
- Related work section is brief and could better engage with recent machine-learning literature on non-random missingness.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant theoretical contribution; recommended for acceptance despite limited empirical and implementation discussion.
