This paper addresses the challenging problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The authors extend existing results by providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly highlighting scenarios where causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. They present new theorems and corollaries concerning sequential factorization, non-recoverability criteria, and recovery in the context of attrition, accompanied by illustrative examples.

The paper is well-structured and logically organized. It begins with a clear introduction to the problem and the m-graph formalism, followed by a review of recoverability concepts. Subsequent sections systematically develop theoretical results, progressing from refining sequential factorization to tackling cases where it fails, establishing non-recoverability criteria, and finally focusing on causal query recovery and attrition. The inclusion of examples throughout the paper aids in understanding the theoretical concepts and their application. The extensive referencing to related work, including both established and recent contributions, is commendable.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a comprehensive and mathematically sound treatment of recoverability under missing data. The theorems and corollaries are clearly stated and appear logically derived.
* **Novelty:** The extension of recoverability conditions, particularly the demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relations are non-recoverable, represents a significant contribution. The refinement of sequential factorization to accommodate interactions between missingness mechanisms is also novel.
* **Generality:** The work generalizes existing results and addresses more complex scenarios, such as those encountered in longitudinal studies with attrition.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application of the results to attrition problems and the provision of graphical criteria for assessing recoverability are practically valuable.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** While the topic is technically demanding, the authors do a good job of explaining the concepts and providing illustrative examples.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism and the abundance of theorems and definitions can make the paper challenging to follow for readers not already familiar with the field of causal inference and missing data.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While examples are provided, there is no empirical validation of the proposed methods on real-world datasets.
* **Assumptions:** The results heavily rely on the correctness of the specified m-graph. The paper doesn't extensively address methods for validating or refining these graphs in practice, which is a crucial challenge in real-world applications.
* **Corollary 2 Specificity**: Corollary 2 feels somewhat limited in its applicability, only applying to conditional distributions and requiring specific relationships between Y and Ry.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of missing data and causal inference.
* The results have the potential to impact research in various domains where missing data is a pervasive issue, such as epidemiology, social sciences, and econometrics.
* The paper is well-written, rigorously analyzed, and thoroughly referenced.
* The extension of recoverability conditions to causal queries is a novel and valuable contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claim of practical impact.
* The complexity of the material may limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
* The reliance on correctly specified m-graphs is a significant limitation in real-world applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. The theoretical results are rigorous and the extension of recoverability conditions to causal queries is particularly noteworthy. While the lack of empirical validation and the complexity of the material are minor drawbacks, the overall quality and significance of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically suggesting that the authors consider adding a discussion of methods for m-graph validation or sensitivity analysis.
