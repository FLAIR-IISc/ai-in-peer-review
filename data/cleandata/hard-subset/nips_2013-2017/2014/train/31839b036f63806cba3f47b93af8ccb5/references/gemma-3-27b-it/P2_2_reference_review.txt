## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid and valuable extension to the existing work on missing data recovery using missingness graphs (m-graphs). The authors successfully generalize previous results, providing broader applicability and, importantly, demonstrating the potential for recovering causal effects even when standard probabilistic recoverability conditions are not met. While the paper is dense and requires a strong background in graphical models and causal inference, the technical contributions are significant and address a crucial problem in scientific inference. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily focused on improving clarity and accessibility.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) for analyzing missing data via m-graphs. It extends the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex missingness mechanisms (where missingness indicators themselves are influenced by other missingness indicators) and develops new criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries.  Crucially, it highlights a key distinction: causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, a result not previously explored in detail within this framework. The authors position their work relative to standard missing data approaches (ML, imputation, inverse probability weighting) and emphasize the benefits of the graphical modeling approach for explicitly representing assumptions and diagnosing potential biases.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (provided in the appendix). The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem and the development of new non-recoverability criteria are particularly strong.
* **Novelty:** The demonstration of causal query recoverability even with inseparable missingness mechanisms is a significant contribution. This challenges the common assumption that probabilistic recoverability is a prerequisite for causal inference in the presence of missing data.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems is a compelling example of the practical utility of the framework. The discussion regarding simple attrition provides actionable insights.
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper covers a broad range of scenarios and presents a detailed analysis of both sufficient and necessary conditions for recoverability.
* **Clear connection to Do-Calculus:** The use of do-calculus to demonstrate the recoverability of causal effects is well-justified and strengthens the results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is very dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge.  The notation is heavy, and the concepts are presented with limited intuitive explanation.  This makes it difficult for readers not already deeply familiar with m-graphs and causal inference to fully grasp the contributions.
* **Examples Could Be Expanded:** While the examples are helpful, they are relatively limited. More illustrative examples, perhaps with varying levels of complexity, would aid understanding.  Visualizations of the m-graphs could be more prominent.
* **Discussion of Computational Complexity:**  While the paper presents theoretical conditions for recoverability, it doesn't address the practical computational challenges of implementing the proposed algorithms for larger datasets or more complex m-graphs.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is primarily theoretical. While the discussion of attrition is a step towards application, a more extensive empirical evaluation, even simulated data, would strengthen the claims.

**4. Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The technical contributions are significant, and the proofs appear rigorous. The paper presents a complete and well-supported analysis.
* **Clarity:** Fair. The paper is difficult to follow for those not already experts in the field.  Significant improvements in writing style and explanations are needed.
* **Originality:** Good. The extension of recoverability to scenarios where the probabilistic relationships are not recoverable is a novel and important contribution.
* **Significance:** Good. The results have the potential to impact how missing data is handled in a variety of scientific disciplines, particularly those relying on causal inference.  The ability to potentially recover causal effects even in challenging missing data scenarios is a valuable advance.



**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous proofs.
* Novel contribution regarding causal query recoverability.
* Addresses a critical problem in scientific inference.
* Clear connection to established causal inference frameworks.

**Con:**

*  Dense and inaccessible writing style.
*  Limited examples and lack of empirical validation.
*  Doesn’t address computational complexity.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance pending the following revisions:

* **Improve Clarity:**  Expand explanations of key concepts and simplify the notation where possible. Consider adding a more intuitive introduction to m-graphs for readers unfamiliar with the framework.
* **Add More Illustrative Examples:** Provide more examples, ideally with visualizations of the m-graphs, to help readers understand the theoretical results.
* **Briefly Discuss Computational Considerations:**  Acknowledge the potential computational challenges of applying the proposed algorithms and suggest possible avenues for addressing them.
* **Consider a Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Even a small-scale simulation study demonstrating the recoverability of causal effects in a realistic scenario would significantly strengthen the paper.




In conclusion, this is a technically sound and potentially impactful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data analysis. Addressing the clarity and accessibility issues will significantly enhance its impact and broaden its readership.
