

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide formal conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors introduce a refined sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that handles cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other, generalizing prior results. They establish non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3), and most significantly, demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated—a key insight that challenges previous assumptions in the literature. The paper applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery.

The work builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl's causal inference framework, extending the theoretical understanding of missing data mechanisms beyond the MCAR/MAR categories. While related work (e.g., Garcia 2013) has addressed attrition, this paper provides the first formal graphical criteria for causal effect recovery in missing data settings.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper presents rigorous theoretical contributions with complete proofs in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries, demonstrating that the former requires Y and Ry to be d-separated (Theorem 3), while the latter can be recoverable even without this condition (Theorem 4). The examples (e.g., Example 3) effectively illustrate the conceptual breakthrough. The paper acknowledges limitations and provides necessary conditions for recoverability, showing scholarly rigor.

**Originality (High):** The key contribution—showing that causal queries can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not—is novel and significant. Previous literature (e.g., Garcia 2013) incorrectly assumed that causal effects require the same conditions as probabilistic recovery. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) also represent a formalization of previously unaddressed concepts. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior NIPS papers on missing data (e.g., Marlin et al. 2007, 2009, 2011) which assumed MAR and did not address causal inference.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear definitions and logical flow. The notation is consistent, and the examples effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The distinction between m-graphs and standard causal graphs is well-motivated. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how d*-separation (Definition 4) differs from standard d-separation, but the core concepts are presented accessibly.

**Significance (High):** Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific domains, and this paper provides a formal framework for determining when queries can be recovered. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability has profound implications for research design and analysis. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) provides concrete guidance for longitudinal studies, potentially improving the reliability of causal inferences in fields like epidemiology and social sciences.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper provides no empirical validation on real or simulated datasets. The methods could be evaluated on standard missing data benchmarks to demonstrate practical utility beyond theoretical soundness.

**Limited Practical Discussion:** The paper doesn't address computational complexity or implementation challenges of the proposed algorithms. For a NIPS audience, discussing how these methods scale to large datasets would strengthen the paper's practical significance.

**Overlooking Some Related Work:** While Garcia (2013) is cited, the paper could better contrast with more recent work on causal inference with missing data (e.g., by Van der Laan and Robins) to clarify the unique contribution to the causal inference literature.

**Narrow Scope of Attrition Application:** The attrition application (Section 7) focuses on "simple attrition" with a single partially observed variable. More complex attrition patterns (e.g., multiple variables with missingness mechanisms interacting) would strengthen the practical relevance.

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of missing data mechanisms in causal inference. The key insight—that causal queries can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separated—represents a significant conceptual advance with clear implications for empirical research. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the paper's theoretical rigor and clarity make it a strong candidate for NIPS.

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a significant contribution to the field. It's sufficiently novel, well-motivated, and addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference. The main weakness (lack of empirical validation) is common in theoretical NIPS papers, especially when the focus is on foundational conditions rather than algorithmic implementation.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides the first formal graphical criteria for causal query recovery with missing data
- Corrects a misconception in prior literature (Garcia 2013)
- Offers precise necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability
- Well-motivated theoretical framework with clear examples

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Lacks empirical validation on real datasets
- Doesn't address computational complexity or implementation
- Limited scope of attrition application

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. The results have the potential to guide both methodological development and practical data analysis in domains where missing data is pervasive. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
