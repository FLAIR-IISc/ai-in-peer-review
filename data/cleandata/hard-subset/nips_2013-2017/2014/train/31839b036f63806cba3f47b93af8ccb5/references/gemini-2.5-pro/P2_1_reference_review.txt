### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework from Mohan et al. [2013], the authors present a set of novel theoretical contributions for analyzing and handling data that is Missing Not At Random (MNAR). The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, which now accommodates dependencies between different missingness mechanisms (R-variables).
2.  A new theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning the R-variables and using their Markov blankets.
3.  Novel graphical criteria for establishing the *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), a crucial and previously under-explored aspect of the problem.
4.  A significant finding that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. This result decouples the challenges of causal and probabilistic inference in the presence of missing data.
5.  Application of these general results to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims in prior literature.

### Review

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes several significant contributions to the principled handling of missing data, a problem of fundamental importance to machine learning and all empirical sciences. The work extends a powerful graphical framework to cover a wider range of scenarios and provides important new insights, particularly regarding the distinction between probabilistic and causal query recovery.

**Quality: (High)**

The paper is technically sound. The theorems are presented formally, and the arguments build logically upon the established foundations of graphical models and do-calculus. The proofs are relegated to an appendix, but the reasoning presented in the main body is coherent and well-argued. The authors demonstrate a careful approach by not only providing sufficient conditions for recoverability but also by formalizing necessary conditions (non-recoverability criteria), which represents a more complete theoretical treatment. For instance, Theorem 3, which uses the graphical structure (neighboring nodes or specific collider paths) to preclude recoverability, is a valuable tool for practitioners to quickly assess if their assumptions are sufficient. The correction of a claim from Garcia [2013] regarding attrition further demonstrates the authors' rigorous engagement with the subject matter.

The main weakness in terms of quality is the complete absence of experimental validation. While this is a theoretical paper, its claims are about the "consistent estimation" of queries from data. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including even a simple simulation study. For example, one could generate data from a known m-graph that satisfies the conditions of Theorem 2, introduce missingness according to the graph, and demonstrate that the proposed estimator converges to the true quantity as sample size increases, while a naive method like complete-case analysis yields a biased result. This would make the theoretical contributions more tangible and convincing.

**Clarity: (Good, but could be improved)**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of illustrative examples after introducing new theorems (e.g., Example 1 for Theorem 1) is very effective for clarifying complex concepts. The structure logically progresses from extending existing methods to introducing new ones, and then applying them to a specific problem (attrition).

However, the paper is extremely dense and assumes a very high level of expertise in Pearl's causal inference framework. Key concepts like "collider path," "R(part)," and "Mb(R(i))" are defined *after* being used in the statement of Theorem 2, which forces the reader to jump back and forth and hinders comprehension. These definitions should precede the theorem. Similarly, concepts like "d*-separation" and "inducing path" are central to Section 6 but are introduced with minimal explanation, making this section challenging for readers not already deeply familiar with the work of Shpitser, Verma, and Pearl. While NIPS papers can be technical, a little more exposition on these prerequisite concepts would broaden the paper's accessibility and impact.

**Originality: (High)**

The contributions are highly original. While building on the authors' prior work, this paper pushes the boundary in several new directions.
-   The criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are, to my knowledge, novel and represent a significant contribution. Much of the literature focuses on when recovery is possible; knowing when it is provably impossible under a given set of assumptions is equally important.
-   The key insight presented in Section 6—that causal effects can be recovered even when the outcome variable Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable—is a profound and non-obvious result. It elegantly demonstrates that the `do` operator can sometimes break dependencies that are problematic for purely observational queries. This is a novel link between the fields of causal identification and missing data recovery.
-   The generalization of the sequential factorization and the new recovery method in Theorem 2 address more complex and realistic missingness scenarios where the mechanisms themselves interact, moving beyond simpler models.

**Significance: (High)**

The results presented in this paper are of high significance. The problem of missing data is ubiquitous, and the standard approaches often rely on untestable and often unrealistic assumptions like MAR (Missing At Random). This paper provides a formal, graphical language to encode more complex MNAR assumptions and rigorously determine what can and cannot be estimated under those assumptions.
-   Researchers in fields like econometrics, epidemiology, and social sciences, who frequently encounter issues like attrition, will find the results in Section 7 particularly useful.
-   The theoretical machinery developed here could inspire new algorithms for machine learning models that are more robust to specific, well-defined types of missing data.
-   The decoupling of probabilistic and causal recoverability is a fundamental insight that could influence how causal inference is performed on incomplete datasets, potentially enabling causal conclusions in situations previously thought to be hopeless.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** The paper introduces several novel, non-trivial, and sound theoretical results (e.g., non-recoverability criteria, recovery without admissible sequences).
2.  **High Significance:** It tackles a fundamental and practical problem in machine learning and provides principled tools for reasoning about complex missingness (MNAR).
3.  **Important Conceptual Insight:** The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not is a major conceptual advance.

### Arguments for Rejection/Major Revision:
1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. The absence of any simulations to validate the proposed estimators is a notable weakness.
2.  **Density and Accessibility:** The paper's dense presentation and heavy reliance on advanced, briefly-explained concepts may limit its accessibility and impact for the broader NIPS community. Reorganizing definitions to precede their use is a necessary improvement.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper with significant and original contributions to a crucial area. Its strengths, particularly the novelty and importance of its results, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. While the clarity could be improved and the addition of experiments would be beneficial, the theoretical core of the paper is strong and impactful enough for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
