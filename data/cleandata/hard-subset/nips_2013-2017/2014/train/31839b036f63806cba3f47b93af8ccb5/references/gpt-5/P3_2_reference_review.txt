Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic (joint and conditional) and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013). The main contributions are: (i) an extension of ordered/sequential factorization to include missingness indicators R, yielding a broader sufficient condition (Theorem 1) that applies when R mechanisms interact; (ii) a general recovery result (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without admissible sequences, based on partitions of R nodes and their Markov blankets reachable via collider paths; (iii) necessary non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when the associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, leveraging do-calculus/dormant independences (Theorem 4); and (v) a focused treatment of “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), with necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6). The work situates itself between the MAR/MCAR literature (Rubin; Little & Rubin) and identification with latent confounding (Shpitser & Pearl; Richardson 2003), and complements the earlier m-graph recoverability results (Mohan et al. 2013; Mohan & Pearl 2014).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The contributions are primarily theoretical. The extensions to sequential factorization (including R nodes with a minimality condition) are plausible and correctly illustrated by Example 1. The partition-based recovery formula in Theorem 2 is well-motivated using ADMG concepts (collider paths, Markov blankets) and seems to strictly generalize earlier sufficient conditions. The non-recoverability conditions target structural obstacles (neighboring X–Rx or collider paths through substantives), consistent with intuition about inseparability and selection. The causal recoverability result (Theorem 4) builds on dormant independence (d*-separation) and do-calculus; the worked Example 3 demonstrates how causal effects can be recovered while P(y|x) is not—an important and sometimes counterintuitive point.
- Proofs are deferred to the appendix; in the main text some results (notably Theorem 1 condition (2) on factors Yi=Rz and the exact role of minimality) would benefit from intuition. Theorem 4’s conditions (existence of Z, W1 and identifiability of P(W1,Z|do(X)) and P(Y|do(W1), do(X), Z, R′y)) are correct in spirit but presented tersely; a proof sketch or algorithmic recipe for constructing Z, W1 from the graph would strengthen confidence.
- No empirical evaluation is provided. While acceptable for a theory paper, even small-scale simulations illustrating finite-sample behavior and robustness to mild misspecifications would be valuable, especially because recoverability results translate into specific estimators (e.g., IPW-like factors P(R=0|·) and complete-case conditioning).

Clarity:
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy. The definition of general ordered factorization and the minimality requirement are key and well-motivated by Example 1. However, the statements around d*-separation and Theorem 4 contain notational inconsistencies (e.g., “W = W \ X”, later using W1; “X ⊥w Y | Z” while also conditioning on W). Please standardize W vs W1 and the subscript notation for d*-separation, and clearly define induced graphs GX, GX,W1.
- The assumption “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” is central to m-graphs; call this out prominently and discuss its practical implications (e.g., attrition preventing measurement does not causally change the underlying Y value).
- Figures are crucial in this topic; ensure all referenced figures contain the necessary structure to follow examples. Expand Example 3 and the attrition examples with explicit graph descriptions (parents/bi-directed edges) to make the recovery steps reproducible.
- References contain formatting errors and incomplete fields.

Originality:
- Extending ordered factorization to include R nodes and requiring minimal conditioning sets is a natural but non-trivial tightening that enables recovery in settings with interacting missingness mechanisms; to my knowledge, this exact condition is new relative to Mohan et al. (2013).
- The partition/Markov blanket-based theorem (Theorem 2) and the non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) fill a gap: formal necessary conditions in m-graphs were missing. The simple attrition results (Theorems 5 and 6) provide crisp necessary and sufficient conditions and correct misconceptions in prior work (Garcia 2013).
- The causal result exploiting dormant independence when Y and Ry are inseparable is insightful and underscores a genuine distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

Significance:
- Practically, these results broaden the class of MNAR problems where analysts can derive consistent estimators without imposing MAR/MCAR. The explicit formulas suggest implementable procedures (conditioning on R′ and weighting by completeness probabilities).
- The characterization for simple attrition is likely to be used in practice and could influence analyses of longitudinal RCTs with dropout.
- The impact would be increased by providing an algorithmic pipeline: given an m-graph and a query, decide recoverability (probabilistic/causal), construct an estimand if possible, or return a certificate of non-recoverability via Theorem 3/Corollary 2. Complexity analysis of searching for admissible sequences and partitions would also be helpful.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advances over prior m-graph work; covers interacting missingness mechanisms.
- First formal non-recoverability criteria in this framework.
- Novel insight that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry.
- Useful, sharp conditions for attrition problems.

Cons for acceptance:
- Presentation issues (notation inconsistencies, missing intuitive proofs) impede accessibility.
- No experiments or empirical illustrations; no implemented algorithm or complexity analysis.
- Some results are restricted (e.g., Theorems 4–6 focus on |Vm|=1), leaving open multi-variable causal recoverability beyond Example 3.
- Assumptions and limitations (positivity; R not parenting V/U) could be discussed more explicitly, including sensitivity to misspecification and links to available tests (Mohan & Pearl 2014).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a high-level decision procedure and pseudocode implementing Theorems 1–3 and 5–6.
- Add at least synthetic experiments showing estimator bias/variance compared to MAR/CC/MI baselines under known m-graphs.
- Clarify Theorem 1 condition (2) with a graphical intuition and a small counterexample when minimality is violated.
- Elaborate Theorem 4: give constructive guidance to find Z and W1; relate to selection diagrams/S-admissibility and transportability to connect with broader causal identification literature.
- Fix notation and references; ensure figures fully specify graphs.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are solid and non-trivial, and the topic is timely and relevant to the NIPS community. With improved clarity and minimal illustrative experiments, the paper would have strong impact.
