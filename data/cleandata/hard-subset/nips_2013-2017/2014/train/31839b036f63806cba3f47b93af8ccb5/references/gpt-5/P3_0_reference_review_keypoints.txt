- Paper extends Missingness Graphs by incorporating missingness indicators R into ordered/sequential factorizations, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact (Theorem 1).
- Introduces a general recoverability theorem using partitions of R and Markov blankets in semi-Markovian models to recover joint distributions when admissible sequences do not exist (Theorem 2).
- Provides graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).
- Shows some causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when corresponding probabilistic quantities are not, including cases with inseparability from Ry (Theorem 4).
- Gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in simple attrition (|Vm|=1) for both joint distributions and causal effects (Theorems 5–6).
- Work is well grounded in previous missing data and causal inference literature and builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013), do-calculus, and dormant independences.
- Conceptual advance: allowing R in admissible factorizations unlocks new practical classes such as longitudinal settings with interacting missingness.
- Theorem 2’s R-partition + Markov blanket formula offers a systematic, intuitive path to recover P(V) and aligns with selection/weighting ideas.
- Non-recoverability criteria give practitioners useful diagnostics to detect impossibility of recovery.
- Theorem 4 and examples illustrate that interventional identifiability can succeed even when probabilistic separability fails.
- Attrition results are crisp, useful, and correct misconceptions in prior literature.
- Manuscript is dense and sometimes imprecise; key definitions and notation (e.g., Xr, RXm, R′x, d*-separation, “⊥w”) need clearer, consistent formalization.
- Theorem 1 is sufficient but not shown necessary; completeness of the recovery tactics is not discussed.
- Theorem 2’s conditions may be conservative; tightness and relation to Mohan et al. (2013) need clarification and a taxonomy showing inclusion relations.
- Apparent tension between Theorem 6’s iff condition and Example 3 requires clarification whether Example 3 lies outside simple attrition or other constraints.
- Paper lacks explicit algorithms: procedures for searching admissible sequences, constructing R-partitions and Markov blankets, and complexity analysis are missing.
- No empirical illustrations or simulations to demonstrate estimators or compare to EM/IPW/MI under MNAR.
- Assumption that R cannot be parents of V ∪ U should be highlighted and its implications briefly reminded to avoid misuse.
- Requests for authors: algorithmic recipe for Theorem 2, formal inclusion/comparison with Mohan et al. (2013), a fully worked example for Theorem 4, and clarification of Theorem 6 vs Example 3 plus definition of simple attrition.
- Technically sound overall, with main proofs in appendix; main text should include more intuition/key proof steps.
- Organization is good but notation and definitions must be tightened and made self-contained.
- Contributions are original and meaningful, extending graphical criteria for MNAR and causal recovery.
- Work has high potential impact on methodology and practice for causal inference with MNAR and attrition.
- Pros: solid theoretical contributions, addresses interacting missingness and attrition, provides both positive and negative results.
- Cons: dense notation-heavy presentation, lack of algorithms and empirical validation, and a need to resolve apparent contradictions.
- Recommendation: leaning toward accept after revision, contingent on improved clarity, reconciliation of subtle points, and ideally a small empirical demonstration; reviewer confidence high.
