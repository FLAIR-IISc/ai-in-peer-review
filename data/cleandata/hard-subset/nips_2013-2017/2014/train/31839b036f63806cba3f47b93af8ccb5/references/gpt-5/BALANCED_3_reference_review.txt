Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops graphical criteria and constructive procedures to decide when probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and certain causal effects are recoverable from data with missing values, using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) as the modeling language (Mohan et al., 2013). The contributions extend earlier results in several ways:

- Sequential factorization with R-nodes: A refinement of the “ordered factorization” approach to allow missingness indicators R to appear in the factorization. Theorem 1 (with a minimality condition) provides a sufficient condition for recoverability when missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., longitudinal settings with edges among R’s).

- Joint recovery beyond admissible sequences: Theorem 2 gives a sufficient condition for joint recoverability when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R variables (Rpart), Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), and collider-path constraints. Corollary 1 treats a subclass with no latent confounding between R and substantive variables and no arrows from partially observed Y to Ry.

- Non-recoverability conditions: Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 provide necessary conditions that preclude recovery of P(V) and P(Y|X), respectively, based on adjacency or collider-path connections between a variable and its missingness indicator.

- Causal queries when Y and Ry are inseparable: Using d*-separations and do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009; Shpitser & Pearl, 2008), the paper shows P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated P(y|x) or P(V) are not. Theorem 4 provides conditions (for |Vm|=1) under which such recovery is possible.

- Attrition: For the “simple attrition” case with a single partially observed outcome, Theorem 5 gives necessary and sufficient conditions for joint recoverability, and Theorem 6 does so for causal effects, including an explicit estimand when separability holds.

These ideas build directly on and extend Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NeurIPS 2013), add negative criteria that were missing there, and connect to identifiability of causal effects (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006). Related literatures in ML and statistics (MAR/MCAR, EM/ML, IPW, MI) are surveyed, and the graphical approach is positioned as a principled alternative when MNAR mechanisms are present.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Allowing R-nodes in sequential factorizations is a neat and useful extension that expands the class of recoverable problems beyond prior ordered factorizations.

- Negative results: Explicit non-recoverability criteria are valuable to practitioners and complement prior work that focused on sufficient conditions only.

- Causal insights: The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when P(V) is not is important and under-appreciated; Theorem 6 for simple attrition provides crisp necessary and sufficient conditions and a practical estimand.

- Scope: The paper addresses interacting missingness mechanisms (edges among R’s), which are common in longitudinal and attrition contexts yet under-explored in prior ML/NeurIPS work.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is notation-heavy and contains several typographical/notation inconsistencies that impede readability. Examples include: GX and GX both defined; inconsistent use of Rz when Z may be fully observed; occasional missing conditions in equations; footnote markers without content; and formatting issues in the references (missing years, inconsistent styles). Figures are referenced but not provided here; their clarity will be crucial.

- Algorithmic completeness and usability: While theorems give constructive estimands in many cases, the paper does not present a unified, implementable algorithm with complexity guarantees for (i) searching admissible sequences with minimal conditioning sets, (ii) building R-partitions and corresponding Mb(R(i)), or (iii) automatically checking Theorem 4’s d*-separation/dormant independence conditions. Practical guidance or pseudo-code would substantially increase impact.

- Empirical validation: No simulations or case studies demonstrate that the proposed estimands yield consistent estimators in finite samples or illustrate robustness to assumption violations. Given the practical focus (attrition, longitudinal studies), even small synthetic experiments would help.

- Relationship among results: The interplay of Theorems 2, 3, and 5 could be tightened. For example, Theorem 3 provides necessary conditions that resemble those assumed in Theorem 2; Theorem 5 (|Vm|=1) claims necessity and sufficiency with a slightly different “all-intermediate-colliders” condition. A unifying statement or explicit contrasts, along with illustrative counterexamples, would clarify the landscape.

- Related work coverage: The paper cites Shpitser & Pearl (2008) in text but the reference appears to be missing. Some recent developments on graphical missing data and selection bias are not discussed (though this may depend on the submission’s timeline).

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically promising; results are plausible and in line with modern causal identification theory. However, the paper relies on appendices for proofs and lacks algorithmic detail and empirical substantiation.

- Clarity: Currently below NeurIPS standards. Several definitions and symbols need polishing, and the narrative could better motivate the conditions and provide intuitive explanations.

- Originality: The extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013) appear novel, especially the negative criteria and the recoverability of causal effects under inseparability.

- Significance: Potentially high for the community interested in causal inference with missing data; the attrition results are practical. Adoption would be enhanced with clearer algorithms and examples.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces meaningful new theory (admissible sequences with R, negative criteria, attrition N&S conditions).
- Bridges probabilistic recoverability and causal identifiability in a useful way.
- Addresses realistic scenarios with interacting missingness mechanisms.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation and notation issues reduce accessibility.
- Lack of an implementable algorithm and empirical demonstrations.
- Some conditions are only sufficient; completeness/storyline could be more unified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear, step-by-step algorithm (with complexity) to decide recoverability and construct estimands under Theorems 1–2 and 4–6; include pseudocode and an open-source implementation.
- Add synthetic experiments comparing the proposed estimators to MAR-based baselines and demonstrating failure modes when conditions are violated.
- Unify and reconcile Theorems 2, 3, and 5; add intuition and minimal counterexamples.
- Clean up notation, fix typos, and standardize references; include the missing Shpitser & Pearl (2008) citation and ensure all figures are legible.
- Clarify positivity assumptions and the “strictly positive over complete cases” requirement; describe how violations are handled.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Rationale: The theoretical contributions are interesting and relevant, but the current clarity, lack of algorithmic detail/implementation, and absence of empirical validation make it premature for publication at NeurIPS. With substantial revisions along the lines above, this work could be competitive.

Reviewer confidence: High in causal-graphical aspects; medium on specific technical lemmas deferred to the appendix.
