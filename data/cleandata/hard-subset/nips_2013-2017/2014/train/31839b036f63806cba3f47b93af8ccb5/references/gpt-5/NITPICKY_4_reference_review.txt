Summary
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using missingness graphs (m-graphs) that explicitly encode the missing-data mechanisms. It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) in four ways: (i) refining the “sequential factorization” approach by allowing ordered factorizations that include R-variables (missingness indicators), enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) providing a general joint-recovery formula for broader semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), with a corollary for models without latent common causes between V and R; (iii) introducing graphical non-recoverability criteria (necessary conditions) for joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) showing that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when Y is inseparable from Ry, via do-calculus/d*-separation, with applications to “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), where the paper states necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery of P(V) and P(y|do(x)).

Relation to prior work: Builds on Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) and related causal identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; 2008; Pearl 2009) and ADMG graph theory (Richardson 2003). It positions itself against MAR/MCAR-based approaches and selection/IPW/imputation literatures.

Strengths
- Topic is important: principled recoverability under MNAR with explicit graphical assumptions is of high interest to ML/causal inference.
- Conceptual advance: allowing R in the ordered factorization is a natural and potentially useful extension beyond Mohan et al. (2013) for interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Theorem-level contributions: general joint recovery (Theorem 2) and non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) could be practically valuable for model diagnosis.
- Insightful point: causal effects may be recoverable even when joint/conditional distributions are not, illustrated with do-calculus and d*-separation/dormant independence.
- Attrition case (|Vm|=1): precise necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6) are potentially crisp and useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and rigor:
- Use of d-separation in graphs with bidirected edges: For semi-Markovian models/ADMGs, the appropriate separation notion is m-separation, not standard d-separation (Richardson 2003). Several statements (Definition 2, Theorem 1, Corollary 2) rely on d-separation; it is unclear whether the intended independence is m-separation, and whether the stated factorizations are valid in general ADMGs.
- Definition 2 (general ordered factorization): indexing inconsistent (Y1..Yk vs Yi+1..Yn), “minimal set” not defined (inclusion-minimal?), and relies on independence in G that may not imply a consistent recursive factorization in an ADMG. A formal statement of when such factorization exists is missing.
- Theorem 1 conditions (especially case Yi=Rz): notation is opaque and partly undefined (e.g., Xr, RXm), and the logical intent is hard to verify. A precise, self-contained condition is needed.
- Theorem 2: relies on collider paths and Markov blankets in ADMGs, but definitions are only partially given; the “no neighbor/no collider path” assumption on Vm–Rx pairs is strong and needs justification. The provided recovery formula resembles selection adjustment, but without proofs or conditions (positivity, testability) it is hard to assess soundness.
- Theorem 3 (necessary conditions for recoverability) and Corollary 2: necessity claims require careful counterexamples or proofs; without them (appendix/figures missing), these claims are hard to trust.
- Section 6 example: P(y|do(z))=P(y|do(z),R′y) requires a selection-on-Ry invariance that should be justified via do-calculus and separation; the text sketches steps but the referenced figure is missing, so correctness cannot be checked.
- Theorem 5 vs Theorem 6: conditions differ (collider-only paths vs inducing paths). The relationship and intuition for this gap should be clarified and proven (again, missing appendices/figures).

Clarity and organization:
- Figures and appendices are missing (“Figure 1–4”, “Appendix 10.x”), preventing verification of examples and proofs.
- Notation is overloaded/inconsistent: V is used both as the node set in G(V,E) and as the set of substantive variables; equation “V = V ∪ U ∪ V∗ ∪ R” is ill-formed. Use distinct symbols (e.g., N for all nodes).
- m-graph primitives: “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” is stated but later the analysis seems to allow edges among R and from V to R; clarify allowed edge types and how they differ from Mohan et al. (2013).
- Independence notation varies between d-separation/m-separation and “⊥w”; definitions of d*-separation and inducing path are given but not integrated with earlier uses.
- Definition of manifest vs underlying distributions is imprecise. The compatibility condition Pm(R′x,Ry,X∗,Y∗,Vo)=Pu(R′x,Ry,X,Vo) is unclear and seemingly missing terms; formalize the observation mechanism thoroughly (Equation 1) and present the full observed-data likelihood.
- Several equations mix starred and unstarred variables without clearly invoking the masking relation (Equation 1).

Originality and related work:
- The paper needs to more clearly delineate what is new vs. what is already in Mohan et al. (2013) and subsequent works. For example, criteria for non-recoverability, the exact scope of Theorem 2, and the attrition characterizations should be contrasted to prior results. Additional related work on MNAR identification could be cited (e.g., Evans & Richardson 2014 ADMG factorization; work on shadow variables/identification under MNAR).

Presentation/formatting:
- References are severely malformed, with placeholders (“? \Q2013\E”), punctuation errors, incomplete venues, and inconsistent author formatting. This is well below NeurIPS standards.
- Typos/notation issues abound: prime notation (R′x), missing spaces, capitalization inconsistencies, equation punctuation, and LaTeX artifacts.
- Footnotes are misplaced; positivity assumptions stated in passing without proper context.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide all figures and full proofs (or at least rigorous proof sketches) in the supplementary; ensure claims labeled “necessary and sufficient” have complete proofs.
- Replace d-separation with m-separation where appropriate; align with ADMG theory and cite the proper factorization results.
- Clean up notation thoroughly; use distinct symbols for node sets; define all shorthand (RX, Xr, etc.).
- Formalize the observation mechanism and compatibility between P(Vo,Vm,R) and P(Vo,V∗,R) carefully.
- Add algorithmic descriptions (pseudocode) for recovery procedures and discuss computational complexity.
- Include small synthetic examples illustrating each theorem (with graphs) and numerical sanity checks.
- Overhaul the bibliography to a standard style; add missing related work where relevant.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled graphical approach.
- Several potentially useful theorems and insights (especially causal recoverability despite inseparability).
- Extends beyond MAR/MCAR and beyond earlier m-graph results.

Cons
- Missing figures/appendices/proofs impede verification.
- Several technical/notation issues (d- vs m-separation, factorization validity).
- Numerous clarity and formatting problems; references in unacceptable state.
- Limited empirical or demonstrative validation.

Recommendation: Reject (strong). The ideas are promising, but the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NeurIPS. With rigorous proofs, corrected graphical/separation foundations, complete figures, and a thorough rewrite, a future version could be impactful.

Confidence: High (I work in causal graphs/missing data).
