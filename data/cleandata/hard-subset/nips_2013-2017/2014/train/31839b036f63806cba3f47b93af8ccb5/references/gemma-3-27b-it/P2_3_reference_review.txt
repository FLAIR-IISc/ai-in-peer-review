## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) for handling missing data. It provides refined conditions for recovering probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries, even when standard recoverability assumptions (like separability of variables and their missingness mechanisms) are violated. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable.  The paper presents new sequential factorization theorems, algorithms, and non-recoverability criteria, and applies these results to the problem of attrition.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Handling missing data is a pervasive challenge in scientific inference, and this work addresses a core limitation of existing methods by focusing on scenarios where standard assumptions fail.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is highly theoretical and builds on a well-defined formal framework (m-graphs).  The theorems are clearly stated and appear to be logically sound.  The proofs are included in the appendix, which is crucial for verification.
* **Novelty:** The demonstration that causal queries can be recovered under conditions where probabilistic queries cannot is a significant and novel contribution.  This circumvents a key limitation of traditional approaches.
* **Generalization of Existing Work:**  The authors successfully extend and generalize the framework of Mohan et al. (2013), broadening its applicability to more complex missing data scenarios (e.g., longitudinal studies, interacting missingness mechanisms).
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common issue in observational studies, demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods.
* **Clear Presentation (for a theoretical paper):** While dense, the paper is relatively well-organized and the key definitions and theorems are clearly delineated.  The examples help illustrate the concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is *highly* theoretical and requires a strong background in graphical models and causal inference to fully understand. The notation is dense and can be challenging for readers not already familiar with the field.  While the examples help, a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper is entirely theoretical. While the inclusion of examples is helpful, it lacks any empirical validation or demonstration of the performance of the proposed algorithms on real or simulated datasets.  This makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the work. It’s unclear how well these theoretical results translate into practice.
* **Complexity:** The theorems and corollaries, while rigorous, are complex and require careful analysis to grasp their implications. This complexity might hinder widespread adoption.
* **Assumptions:** While the work relaxes some common assumptions, it still relies on specific m-graph structures and identifiability of causal effects.  The robustness of the methods to model misspecification is not discussed.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** While the related work section is comprehensive, the discussion of how these methods compare to, and potentially improve upon, existing imputation or weighting techniques (like multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting) could be stronger.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances the understanding of recoverability in the presence of missing data, particularly regarding causal inference. The results are novel, rigorous, and have the potential to impact research in various fields. However, the lack of empirical validation and the high level of theoretical complexity limit its immediate practical applicability and accessibility.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis.
* **Clarity:** Good, but could be improved. The writing is generally clear for experts, but the dense notation and theoretical complexity pose a barrier to broader understanding.
* **Originality:** Excellent. The contribution of recovering causal queries when probabilistic queries are non-recoverable is highly original.
* **Significance:** High.  The work addresses a fundamental problem and has the potential to influence how missing data is handled in causal inference and related fields.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contribution is significant and advances the state-of-the-art in missing data handling.
* The results have the potential to unlock new insights in fields where missing data is a major challenge.
* The paper is well-written (for its target audience) and rigorously proven.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation limits the assessment of practical utility.
* High theoretical complexity might hinder adoption.
* Limited discussion of connection to existing practical techniques.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance pending the following minor revisions:

1. **Expand the discussion of practical implications:**  The authors should briefly discuss how these theoretical results might be implemented in practice and address the challenges of applying these methods to real-world datasets.
2. **Consider a small illustrative simulation:** Even a simple simulation study demonstrating the recoverability of causal effects in a scenario where traditional methods fail would significantly strengthen the paper.
3. **Improve accessibility:** Consider adding a "high-level overview" section summarizing the key contributions in plain language, aimed at a broader audience.  Clarifying the notation where possible would also be helpful.
4. **Strengthen connection to practical methods:** Discuss how these techniques relate to and potentially improve upon existing imputation or weighting techniques.
