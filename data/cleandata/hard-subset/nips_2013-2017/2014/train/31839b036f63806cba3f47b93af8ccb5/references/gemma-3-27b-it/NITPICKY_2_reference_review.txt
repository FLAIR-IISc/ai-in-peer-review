This paper presents an extension of missingness graphs (m-graphs) to recover probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data. While the topic is undeniably important and the authors build upon their previous work, the paper suffers from a multitude of small flaws – in presentation, rigor, and justification – that ultimately detract from its impact and lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction, while setting the stage, lacks conciseness. The motivation is clear, but the phrasing is often verbose. The claim of extending results from Mohan et al. [2013] is true, but the novelty isn't sufficiently highlighted. The organization, as stated, is adhered to, but the sections often feel disjointed, lacking a cohesive narrative flow.

Section 2, defining m-graphs and recoverability, is largely a restatement of previous work. However, the notation is *extremely* dense and requires constant referencing back to definitions. The introduction of V*, R, and their relationship to Vi is presented without sufficient intuition, making it difficult to grasp the overall framework quickly. The explanation of compatibility feels unnecessarily convoluted.

Section 3 introduces the “General Ordered Factorization” and Theorem 1. The extension of ordered factorization is a good idea, but the justification for the minimality requirement is weak – it’s asserted rather than rigorously demonstrated. The example, while illustrative, is overly detailed and distracts from the core concept. The claim that the modification *permits* handling cases where R variables are in separating sets feels underwhelming; it's a natural consequence of the generalization, not a breakthrough.

Section 4 focuses on cases where sequential factorization fails. Theorem 2, while aiming for generality, introduces a significant amount of new notation (Rpart, Mb(R(i))) without clear motivation. The conditions seem ad-hoc, and the connection to the broader problem of recoverability is not explicitly explained. The example using Figure 2(b) helps, but the explanation remains dense.

Section 5 attempts to provide non-recoverability criteria, a valuable addition. However, Theorem 3 feels insufficiently strong. The conditions presented are *necessary* but may not be *sufficient* to preclude recoverability, and this nuance isn't addressed. Corollary 2 is similarly limited in its scope.

Section 6 delves into causal queries and presents the most genuinely interesting result – the potential recoverability of P(y|do(x)) even when Y and Ry are inseparable. However, the presentation is again overly complex. The jump from Rule-2 and Rule-3 of do-calculus to the recovery of P(y|do(x)) in Example 3 feels abrupt and requires a more thorough explanation. Theorem 4 suffers from the same density of notation as earlier sections.

Section 7 applies the results to attrition, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches (Garcia [2013]) and demonstrating the utility of the proposed framework. The discussion is helpful, but the presentation remains cluttered and the connection to practical applications isn't strong enough.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but lacks critical evaluation of the cited papers. The conclusion summarizes the contributions but feels rushed.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor issues: inconsistent use of notation (e.g., using both X and X* interchangeably), a lack of visual clarity in the figures (Figure 2(a) is particularly difficult to decipher), and several grammatical errors. The proofs, relegated to the appendix, are not easily accessible and require significant effort to follow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning.
*   Extends existing work on missingness graphs.
*   Highlights the potential for recovering causal effects under challenging conditions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Overly complex and dense presentation.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for key concepts and theorems.
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, grammar, and visual clarity.
*   Limited impact and practical relevance despite the importance of the problem.
*   The claims of novelty are overstated given the incremental nature of the contributions.



Overall, the paper presents some interesting ideas, but it is currently presented in a manner that is inaccessible and unconvincing. A significant rewrite focusing on clarity, rigor, and conciseness is required before it would be suitable for NIPS. I therefore recommend rejection.
