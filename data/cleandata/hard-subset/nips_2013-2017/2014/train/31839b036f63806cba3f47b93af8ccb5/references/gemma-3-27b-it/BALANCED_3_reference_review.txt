This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, extending prior work on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). The authors refine sequential factorization theorems and develop new criteria for both recoverable and non-recoverable scenarios, with a particular focus on causal effects—demonstrating recovery even when the target variable is inseparable from its missingness mechanism. The paper concludes with applications to attrition problems.

The strength of this paper lies in its formalization of the missing data problem using m-graphs and the rigorous theoretical extensions it provides. The authors build upon Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl & Mohan [2013], systematically addressing limitations of prior work, specifically expanding applicability to scenarios with interacting missingness mechanisms (Section 3) and providing algorithms for cases where standard factorization fails (Section 4).  The core contribution – demonstrating recoverability of causal queries even with inseparability – is significant and counterintuitive, highlighting a key advantage of causal reasoning in the presence of missing data.  The application to attrition (Section 7) grounds the theoretical results in a practically relevant domain.  The extensive proofs in the appendix demonstrate a commitment to technical rigor. The clear organization, with each section building logically upon the previous, is commendable.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While theoretically sound, the practical implications and computational complexity of the proposed algorithms are not discussed. For example, Section 4 mentions algorithms for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization fails, but provides no details on their implementation or scalability. The examples, while helpful for illustration, are relatively simple and do not adequately demonstrate the utility of the theorems in more complex real-world scenarios.  The discussion of related work (Section 8) is comprehensive but somewhat disjointed; it lists a lot of related work but does not always clearly articulate *how* the current work differs or advances the state-of-the-art beyond extending previous work by Mohan et al. The notation, especially involving subsets of variables (e.g., *Wr*, *Wo*, *Wm*), can be dense and requires careful attention, potentially hindering readability.  The paper also relies heavily on theoretical results without providing empirical validation.  While proving recoverability is essential, demonstrating it on simulated or real datasets would bolster the claims.  Furthermore, the definition of ‘simple attrition’ in Section 7 feels somewhat narrow. More nuanced attrition models could be considered. While Theorems 5 and 6 present necessary and sufficient conditions, it isn’t clear how easily these conditions could be checked in practice given the complexity of m-graphs.  The discussion feels very focused on theoretical conditions, lacking a discussion of potential computational challenges in determining recoverability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper presents a solid theoretical framework for analyzing recoverability under missing data, extending previous work.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistically inseparable from their missingness mechanism is a significant result.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and logically structured.
*   **Relevance:** The topic of missing data is critically important across many scientific disciplines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Practicality:** The computational complexity and implementation details of the proposed methods are not addressed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The absence of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   **Dense Notation:** The notation can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion**: No discussion of scalability of these algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.  The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the lack of practical considerations and empirical validation significantly limit its impact. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1.  **Address Computational Complexity:**  Discuss the computational cost of the proposed algorithms and explore potential optimizations.
2.  **Include Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct experiments (simulation or real-world data) to demonstrate the effectiveness of the methods and validate the theoretical findings.
3.  **Simplify Notation:**  Review the notation for clarity and consider using more descriptive variable names.
4.  **Expand Related Work Discussion:** More clearly articulate how this work builds upon and differs from existing research.
5.  **Discuss Practical Applications:** Include a section discussing how these results could be applied in specific domains beyond attrition.
6. **Discuss Scalability:** Discuss the scalability concerns with determining recoverability.
7.  **Consider More General Attrition Models:** Explore the applicability of the results to more complex attrition scenarios.
