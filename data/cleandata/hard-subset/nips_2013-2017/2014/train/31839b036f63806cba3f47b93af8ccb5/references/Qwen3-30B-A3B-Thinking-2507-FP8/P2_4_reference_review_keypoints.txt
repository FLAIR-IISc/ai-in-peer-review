- Introduces a formal graphical framework (m-graphs) extending Mohan et al. (2013) for analyzing missing data.  
- Develops general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries beyond prior factorization results.  
- Provides new graphical criteria for non-recoverability.  
- Demonstrates causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not.  
- Gives necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition problems and corrects prior misconceptions (e.g., Garcia, 2013).  
- High technical quality with formal definitions, theorems, and full proofs.  
- Carefully distinguishes probabilistic versus causal recoverability, a key conceptual advance.  
- Examples (notably Example 3) effectively illustrate practical implications.  
- Well-organized and clearly written, though some concepts (d*-separation, ordered factorization) are complex.  
- Would benefit from a clearer comparison to MAR/MCAR and an intuitive explanation of why causal recovery can succeed when probabilistic recovery fails.  
- Original contribution: overturns the separability requirement for causal recovery and introduces novel non-recoverability criteria.  
- Significant practical impact for fields dealing with missing data (epidemiology, social sciences, ML).  
- Advances beyond traditional MAR/MCAR frameworks with a more nuanced graphical characterization of recoverability.  
- Strengths: theoretical rigor, novel insights, practical attrition applications, and correction of literature.  
- Weaknesses: conceptual complexity for nonexperts, limited discussion of computational implementation, and few concrete real-world dataset examples.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — substantial theoretical contribution likely to influence future work in causal inference and missing data.
