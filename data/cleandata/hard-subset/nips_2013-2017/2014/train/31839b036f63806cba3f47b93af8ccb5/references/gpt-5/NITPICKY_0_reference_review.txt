Summary
This paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) in several ways: (i) generalizes ordered/sequential factorization to include missingness indicators R (Theorem 1), enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms influence each other; (ii) provides a recovery formula for the joint distribution when no admissible sequence exists via a partition of R and Markov blanket conditions (Theorem 2) with a simplified corollary (Corollary 1); (iii) introduces graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) shows certain causal effects are recoverable even when outcomes are inseparable from their missingness indicators (Theorem 4), leveraging do-calculus and dormant independences; (v) for “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6), and argues some prevailing claims in the attrition literature are overly restrictive. Proofs are deferred to an appendix.

Relation to prior work: Builds squarely on m-graphs and recoverability in Mohan et al. (2013), uses d*-separation/dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008), identifiability via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009), and mixed graph Markov properties (Richardson, 2003). The work aims to go beyond MAR/MCAR by giving MNAR-aware recoverability/irrecoverability criteria.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and widely misunderstood problem (recoverability vs. identifiability under missingness).
- Extends ordered factorization to cases with interacting missingness mechanisms and provides a constructive recovery approach (Theorem 1, Example 1).
- Offers novel-looking negative criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), useful for practice.
- Clarifies that certain causal effects remain recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry (Example 3; Theorem 4), which is conceptually valuable.
- Provides necessary and sufficient graphical conditions under simple attrition (Theorems 5–6), with explicit estimands.

Weaknesses (technical and presentation)
- Severe and pervasive notation and clarity problems:
  - Overloaded/incorrect node set: “Let G(V,E) be the causal DAG where V = V ∪ U ∪ V∗ ∪ R” is inconsistent (V equals V ∪ ...). Use a distinct node set (e.g., N = V ∪ U ∪ V∗ ∪ R).
  - GX and GX are both defined as graph edge removals but indistinguishable; subscripts/superscripts appear inconsistently (e.g., GX,W1, RXi, Xr, RXm).
  - Equation (1) formatting is broken and uses lower/upper-case inconsistently (v∗i vs V∗i).
  - Compatibility definition is opaque: Pm(R′x,Ry,X∗,Y∗,Vo) = Pu(R′x,Ry,X,Vo) lacks symmetry (Y missing on RHS); quantifiers on X,Y and the meaning of Ry=1 need to be spelled out.
  - Theorem 1’s conditions are hard to parse: “Yi⊥⊥(Ryi,Rxi)|Xi\{Ryi,Rxi}” and “Z /∈ Xi and Xr∩RXm=∅ and Rz⊥⊥RXi |Xi if Yi=Rz” rely on undefined or inconsistently defined symbols (Rxi, RXm, Xr, Z). Precise definitions of Rxi and RXi are needed.
  - Theorem 4 uses W and W1 inconsistently (“for W = W\X”) and references GX,W1 without having defined this operator earlier.
  - Theorem 5 uses VO, but earlier the notation is Vo (case sensitivity matters); ensure consistency.
- Missing details relative to “algorithmic” claims: Section 4 says “general algorithms,” but the main text provides only formulas/conditions, no algorithmic procedure, complexity, or pseudo-code; no constructive guidance when multiple admissible sequences exist or fail.
- Many claims depend on figures (Figures 1–4), but the textual explanations alone are insufficient to verify admissibility or (non-)recoverability; more self-contained statements would help.
- No empirical demonstrations or synthetic case studies to illustrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to positivity violations, or sensitivity to model misspecification. For a NIPS audience, even minimal experiments or simulation-based illustrations would add value.
- Proofs are entirely in the appendix, with limited intuition in the main text; several “if and only if” results (Theorems 5–6) warrant at least proof sketches or counterexamples for necessity.
- Assumptions and scope need clearer articulation: e.g., “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” is standard in m-graphs, but later results consider edges among R; discuss implications and limitations (e.g., measurement processes).
- Related work is under-developed in terms of positioning: more contrast with prior recovery/non-recovery results (beyond Mohan et al. 2013) and with other ADMG-based missing data frameworks is needed; “rectifies prevailing opinion” based on a working paper (Garcia, 2013) is weak—engage more broadly with the literature.
- References are in very poor shape: inconsistent formatting, missing venues/pages, placeholder tokens (e.g., “?\Q2013\E”), inconsistent capitalization; not NIPS style-compliant.

Specific minor issues and suggestions
- Define “semi-Markovian model” formally when first used; explain relation to latent projections/ADMGs.
- Clarify positivity assumptions (only “strictly positive over complete cases” is mentioned); discuss when extensions beyond positivity are possible and needed.
- In Example 1, explicitly state which variables are partially observed (X,Y,Z?) and why independences with R hold.
- Distinguish “collider path” vs. “inducing path” and explain why Theorem 6 uses the latter while Theorems 2/5 use the former.
- Provide a running example with and without latent confounding between R and V to illustrate Theorem 2 and Corollary 1.
- Statement of Corollary 2: clarify whether “all intermediate nodes in p are in X” means exactly equals X or subset of X; discuss edge cases.

Pros
- Tackles an important problem with clear practical impact.
- Provides new negative criteria and constructive positive conditions.
- Interesting insight: causal effects may be recoverable even when P(Y|X) is not.

Cons
- Significant notation/clarity problems throughout; difficult to verify results.
- “Algorithms” not actually presented; no experiments or simulations.
- Heavy dependence on figures; insufficient self-contained exposition.
- Poorly formatted and incomplete references; numerous typos and inconsistencies.
- Some overlap with prior work is not clearly delineated.

Recommendation: Reject. While the theoretical direction is promising and several contributions appear potentially novel and useful, the paper in its current form is too difficult to parse and verify, lacks algorithmic clarity and empirical illustration, and falls short on presentation quality (notation, proofs in main text, and references). A thorough rewrite with precise, consistent notation, strengthened comparisons, inclusion of concrete algorithms/pseudocode, proof sketches for key results, and illustrative experiments would significantly improve the submission.

Confidence: High.
