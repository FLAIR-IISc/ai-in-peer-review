Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) as a formal device to encode both causal relations among substantive variables and the missingness mechanisms. Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the paper extends recoverability results beyond MCAR/MAR to MNAR settings.

Main contributions:
- Sequential factorization with R-nodes: A generalized notion of ordered factorization that allows missingness indicators (R variables) to appear in the factorization (Definition 2, Theorem 1). This extends Mohan et al. (2013) to models where missingness mechanisms interact (edges among R’s), enabling recovery in cases where factorization over substantive variables alone is insufficient.
- Recovery without admissible sequences: A new recovery result (Theorem 2) for semi-Markovian models that do not admit an admissible sequence, by partitioning R variables into groups not joined by adjacency or collider paths, and using Markov blankets of R-partitions to express P(V) in terms of complete-case and mechanism factors. A corollary (Corollary 1) treats the special case with no latent common causes between R and substantive variables.
- Non-recoverability criteria: Necessary graphical conditions under which P(V) and certain conditionals are not recoverable (Theorem 3; Corollary 2), highlighting the role of adjacency and collider paths between a variable and its missingness indicator.
- Causal recoverability despite inseparability: New conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y is inseparable from Ry (Section 6, Example 3), leveraging dormant independences and d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl 2008) and do-calculus. Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions in the single-missing-variable case.
- Attrition: For simple attrition (|Vm|=1), necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6), correcting claims in Garcia (2013).

These results relate to and extend: Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) on m-graphs and recoverability, Shpitser & Pearl (2006, 2008) on identifiability/do-calculus/d*-separation, Richardson (2003) on ADMGs, and classical missing data literature (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002; EM/ML/IPW). The paper’s emphasis on explicit graphical criteria for both positive and negative results is in line with recent NeurIPS and AISTATS work on causal graphs and missing data.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The contributions are stated as theorems with proofs deferred to the appendix. The results align with known causal graphical principles (e.g., role of collider paths, Markov blankets, and dormant independences), and the examples illustrate when sequential factorization succeeds or fails.
- The generalized ordered factorization (Theorem 1) is carefully constrained by minimality requirements, which are necessary to avoid spurious factorizations; the example demonstrates this point.
- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) offer clear necessary conditions, filling a gap in prior literature that focused mainly on sufficient conditions.
- The attrition theorems (5 and 6) provide clean if-and-only-if characterizations in |Vm|=1 models.
- Some assumptions are important to highlight: positivity over complete cases; R variables cannot be parents of substantive or latent variables (restricting feedback from missingness to data-generating mechanisms); identifiability of causal queries in the substantive model is presupposed in Section 6.
- Algorithmic aspects are under-specified: the “general algorithms” for recovery (Section 4) are not presented concretely; complexity and practical procedures for finding admissible sequences or R-partitions are not detailed.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is generally readable but dense. Notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., W vs. W1 in Theorem 4; “W = W \ X” is unclear), and some definitions appear after they are used (e.g., inducing path vs. collider path; Mb and partitions could be introduced earlier).
- Figures are referenced but not provided here; the exposition depends on them heavily. A running example across sections would aid comprehension.
- The reference list contains formatting issues and apparent omissions (e.g., Daniel et al. 2012 is cited but not listed; many entries have placeholders or inconsistencies). Careful editing is required.

Originality:
- Including R nodes in ordered factorizations and providing conditions for recovery in the absence of admissible sequences extend Mohan et al. (2013).
- The explicit necessary non-recoverability criteria appear novel and valuable.
- The demonstration that some causal effects are recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry is a noteworthy insight grounded in dormant independence.
- The necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition clarify and correct prior claims, indicating genuine novelty.

Significance:
- Missing data is ubiquitous in ML and causal inference. General, checkable graphical criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability are important for both theory and practice.
- The results could influence how practitioners specify and test missingness assumptions and design analysis pipelines, particularly in longitudinal and attrition-prone studies.
- Providing algorithmic procedures and software would substantially increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Advances recoverability theory in MNAR settings with interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Introduces clear non-recoverability criteria.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recoverability, including cases where causal effects are recoverable while P(V) is not.
- Offers necessary and sufficient graphical characterizations for simple attrition.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Exposition requires polishing; some notational issues and unclear conditions (e.g., Theorem 4) may impede adoption.
- Algorithmic details and practical procedures are limited; no empirical illustrations or experiments are provided.
- Scope restriction that R cannot be parents of substantive/latent variables may limit applicability; a discussion of this modeling choice would help.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit algorithms (or pseudocode) to (i) search for admissible sequences; (ii) construct R-partitions and compute Mb(R(i)); (iii) implement recovery formulas with positivity checks.
- Clarify Theorem 4 (W vs W1, define all sets precisely) and add intuition for d*-separation in the missingness context.
- Add synthetic case studies to demonstrate finite-sample performance and robustness to misspecification.
- Tighten notation and proof sketches (e.g., emphasize minimality in Definition 2; restate all required assumptions near each theorem).
- Fix and complete the references; ensure all cited works are present and consistently formatted.
- Discuss the modeling choice that R nodes do not cause V or U, and whether the results extend if such edges were allowed.

Overall, the paper offers substantive theoretical contributions on an important topic, with room to improve clarity and practical guidance. The work is squarely within NeurIPS scope (graphical models, causality, missing data).
