Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan, Pearl and Tian (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) extend “ordered factorization” to include missingness indicators R and give a sufficient condition (Theorem 1) for recoverability via general ordered factorizations; (ii) propose a new decomposition (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in certain semi-Markovian models without admissible sequences, using partitions of R and Markov blankets; (iii) provide necessary (non-recoverability) conditions for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations like P(y|x) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from Ry (Example 3), and present a d*-separation-based criterion (Theorem 4) for |Vm|=1; and (v) analyze “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6). Proofs are in the appendix; examples are used to illustrate.

This line of work sits within causal graphical modeling and missing data mechanisms (Rubin MAR/MCAR; IPW and MI methods; Lauritzen EM for graphical models). It extends the m-graph framework of Mohan et al. (2013) and is related to identification via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl), dormant independence/d*-separation, and work on selection mechanisms.

Strengths
- Tackles an important, challenging setting: MNAR mechanisms encoded graphically, beyond MAR/MCAR.
- Clear conceptual message: causal effects can be recoverable even when joint/conditional distributions are not.
- Theorems for simple attrition give clean necessary and sufficient graphical conditions; the worked do-calculus example is instructive.
- Attempts to provide both positive (recoverability) and negative (non-recoverability) criteria.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013): Theorem 1 is a modest extension (allowing R in ordered factorization). Theorem 2’s scope and novelty versus earlier results are not convincingly delineated; conditions appear strong and partially overlap with later non-recoverability conditions.
- Limited generality of causal results: Theorem 4 and the attrition results largely focus on |Vm|=1; claims about interacting missingness mechanisms are not matched by general algorithms for causal recoverability when |Vm|>1.
- Lack of algorithmic/complexity guarantees: The paper states “general algorithms” but provides only conditions. There is no explicit decision procedure, proof of completeness within a class, or complexity analysis for checking the conditions (e.g., finding admissible sequences with minimality).
- No empirical evaluation: No simulations or applied case studies to illustrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to model misspecification, or practical estimation strategies once identifiability formulas are given.
- Clarity and rigor issues: Notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., “GX and GX represent…”; inconsistent Rw vs Wr; formatting in equations and references). Some assumptions (positivity) are briefly footnoted; guidance for violations is minimal. Theorems framed with informal terms (“general algorithm”) without precise constructs.
- Non-recoverability results (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are only necessary, limiting their practical utility; tightness is not discussed.

Quality
Technically, the statements are plausible and consistent with the m-graph/ID literature, and the appendix is said to contain proofs. However, the lack of an explicit, verifiable algorithm and absence of empirical demonstrations weakens the case. Several conditions are strong or specialized (|Vm|=1), and the contribution appears more like a set of lemmas/examples extending prior work than a complete, general solution. Care with notation/proofs needs improvement.

Clarity
The manuscript would benefit from significant editing. Definitions (e.g., minimality, partitions R(i), Mb(R(i))) should be made more precise and accompanied by procedures for construction. Figures seem essential for comprehension but the explanatory text alone is not sufficient. Reference formatting is inconsistent. Provide a running example end-to-end, with step-by-step application of theorems.

Originality
Moderate. The extension of ordered factorization to include R, the specific non-recoverability necessary conditions, and the attrition characterizations for |Vm|=1 are useful but incremental. The insight that causal effects can be recoverable when P(V) is not is known in principle via do-calculus/dormant independences; the paper packages this within m-graphs but does not present a broadly applicable new identification algorithm.

Significance
Potentially meaningful for researchers working on MNAR with causal graphs, especially the simple attrition case. However, without practical algorithms and empirical validation, the immediate impact on practice and adoption by the NeurIPS community is limited. Broader classes with multiple missingness mechanisms remain largely open.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide an explicit algorithm (with pseudo-code) to decide recoverability via Theorem 1 (including how to find admissible sequences with minimality) and via Theorem 2 (how to construct R partitions and Mb sets), with complexity analysis.
- Clarify the novelty relative to Mohan et al. (2013). Which examples are now solvable that were not before? Provide comparative examples where the new theorems strictly extend prior results.
- For Theorem 3, discuss tightness and provide counterexamples showing that violating the conditions indeed precludes recovery, and conversely cases that satisfy them but are still non-recoverable (to delineate necessity vs sufficiency).
- Generalize Theorem 4 beyond |Vm|=1 if possible, or clearly position it as a special case; relate more formally to dormant independence criteria.
- Add simulation studies demonstrating recovery vs non-recovery, estimator construction from the provided formulas, and comparison to EM/ML, IPW, and MI under MAR/MNAR misspecification.
- Substantially revise notation and references for accuracy and readability; discuss positivity and testability (Mohan & Pearl 2014) more concretely.

Pros
- Addresses important MNAR/causal identification problems.
- Provides some new sufficient/necessary conditions and illuminating examples.
- Clean necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; limited generality beyond |Vm|=1.
- No concrete algorithms or empirical validation.
- Clarity/notation issues; references poorly formatted.
- Non-recoverability criteria only necessary.

Recommendation: Reject
Confidence: High
