### Summary

This paper investigates the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values, a ubiquitous challenge in empirical research. The authors build upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework from their prior work (Mohan et al., 2013) to represent assumptions about the data-generating and missingness processes. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it generalizes a sufficient condition for recoverability, the "sequential factorization" theorem, to handle more complex scenarios where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, it provides a new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, based on graphical properties like collider paths and Markov blankets. Third, and perhaps most novelly, it introduces graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, giving researchers tools to identify when their assumptions are insufficient to obtain a consistent estimate. Finally, the paper extends this analysis to causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))), demonstrating the remarkable result that a causal effect can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint or conditional distribution is not. These theoretical results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that refine and correct previous claims in the literature.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of handling missing data is fundamental and critically important for the machine learning community and beyond. This paper moves beyond standard assumptions like MCAR or MAR, providing a formal, principled framework for reasoning about recoverability under more general "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) conditions. The development of a graphical calculus for this problem is a significant contribution that can help practitioners make more informed decisions about their data analysis.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper presents several novel theoretical results:
    *   The criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) are particularly valuable. Much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for recovery; providing necessary conditions (or sufficient conditions for non-recoverability) is a powerful new tool that allows researchers to falsify the feasibility of their analysis goals given their assumptions.
    *   The insight that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not (Section 6) is profound. Example 3 provides a crisp illustration of this, highlighting a key distinction between statistical and causal inference in the context of missing data. This result could have significant implications for how causal inference is performed on incomplete datasets.
    *   The application to attrition (Section 7) is not merely an example but a contribution in its own right, as it provides sharp, graph-based characterizations that correct and clarify existing literature on the topic.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. It builds upon the established foundations of graphical models and do-calculus. The theorems are stated precisely, and the arguments presented in the main text are well-supported by illustrative examples. The proofs provided in the appendix (assumed correct for this review) suggest a complete and thorough theoretical analysis.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the claims would be substantially bolstered by simulation studies. For instance, the authors could generate data from a known m-graph where a query is recoverable by their proposed method but not by standard methods (e.g., listwise deletion, mean imputation, or even an EM algorithm under a misspecified MAR assumption). Demonstrating that their proposed estimator converges to the true value while others remain biased would provide compelling empirical evidence for the practical utility of their framework. Such a study would also help illustrate the magnitude of the bias that can arise from mishandling MNAR data.

2.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense and assumes significant familiarity with advanced concepts in graphical causal inference (e.g., m-graphs, do-calculus, d*-separation, inducing paths). While precise, this makes the paper challenging for a broader NeurIPS audience.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could improve readability by providing more intuitive explanations for some of the core concepts. For instance, the definitions for *collider path*, *R(part)*, and *Mb(R(i))* are given *after* Theorem 2 is stated, which disrupts the flow. Defining these terms first would make the theorem statement easier to parse.
    *   **Suggestion:** A small summary table or flowchart could be very helpful for practitioners, mapping specific graphical features (e.g., "collider path from Y to Ry") to their implication (e.g., "P(Y) not recoverable") and the relevant theorem.

3.  **Algorithmic Considerations:** The paper provides expressions for recoverable queries but does not discuss the algorithmic complexity of finding them. For a given m-graph and query, is it straightforward to check the conditions of the various theorems? What is the complexity of finding the required factorization or the separating sets? A brief discussion on the algorithmic aspects of applying these results would strengthen the paper's contribution as a practical tool.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes significant and novel theoretical contributions to the important and difficult problem of inference with missing data. The introduction of non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability are standout results. The technical quality is high, and the work is a valuable extension of the graphical models framework to this domain.

The main weaknesses are the lack of empirical validation and the high barrier to entry for non-experts. However, the theoretical strength and originality of the contributions are sufficient to warrant publication at NeurIPS. The suggestions for improving clarity and adding simulations should be considered by the authors to maximize the paper's impact.

**Decision: Accept**
