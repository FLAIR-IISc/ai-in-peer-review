

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present theoretical results showing that certain causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not recoverable. They claim that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism, which they argue "rectifies prevailing opinion" in the literature.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with formal definitions and theorems supported by examples. The authors build logically on previous work by Mohan and Pearl, which is appropriate for theoretical contributions. The paper properly references related work in missing data analysis, causal inference, and graphical models.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a theoretically interesting claim that causal effects may be recoverable under conditions where probabilistic queries are not, which could be significant for causal inference with missing data.
2. The paper provides a thorough theoretical treatment of the problem, with proofs in the appendix.
3. The paper clearly distinguishes between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, which is an important conceptual contribution.
4. The application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies is timely and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** As a theoretical contribution, the paper should at least provide illustrative examples or simulations to demonstrate the practical implications of the theoretical claims. The paper relies entirely on formal proofs without any empirical demonstration of the proposed conditions in real or synthetic data.

2. **Questionable novelty:** The paper's main claim (causal effects may be recoverable when probabilistic relations are not) appears to be a logical consequence of do-calculus and the properties of causal queries, rather than a truly novel insight. The authors cite Shpitser and Pearl [2008] regarding d*-separability, which suggests the approach may not be as novel as claimed.

3. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims to "rectify prevailing opinion" (citing Garcia [2013]), but provides no evidence that previous work was incorrect or that their method would substantially outperform existing approaches. The paper doesn't compare with standard missing data methods like multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting.

4. **Lack of practical guidance:** The paper provides theoretical conditions for recoverability but doesn't discuss how to implement these conditions in practice, how sensitive they are to model misspecification, or how to test whether the conditions hold in real datasets.

5. **Insufficient validation against established baselines:** The paper makes strong claims about recoverability conditions but doesn't provide evidence that these conditions are more useful or practical than existing approaches to missing data. For instance, they don't compare their conditions with the standard MAR/MCAR assumptions that underlie most missing data methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the literature on missing data, but its claims are not sufficiently validated. The main insight appears to be a logical extension of existing causal inference theory rather than a genuinely novel methodological advance. The paper lacks empirical validation, practical guidance, and meaningful comparison with established approaches to missing data.

While the topic of missing data is important, the paper does not demonstrate how its theoretical results translate to practical advantages over existing methods. The authors claim to have "recovered causal effects" under conditions where probabilistic relations are not recoverable, but they don't provide evidence that this approach would actually improve inference in real-world applications.

The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. Simulations demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical conditions
2. Comparison with standard missing data methods
3. Discussion of how to test the proposed conditions in practice
4. More careful articulation of the novelty relative to existing work

Given the NIPS focus on high-quality, rigorously validated contributions, this paper falls short of the expected standard. While the theoretical work is sound, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient demonstration of practical significance make it difficult to endorse. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject** 

The paper's main claim is not sufficiently validated with empirical evidence or practical demonstration, which is particularly concerning given the paper's strong theoretical claims. The theoretical contribution appears to be a logical extension of existing work rather than a truly novel methodological advance. Without evidence that the proposed conditions lead to practical improvements over existing approaches, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for quality and significance.
