Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops graphical criteria for deciding when probabilistic (joint/conditional) and causal queries are recoverable from data with missingness, using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) by: (i) generalizing the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R in the factorization (Theorem 1), enabling recovery when R variables interact; (ii) providing a new route to recover joint distributions absent an admissible sequence via collider-path-based partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2, with a corollary under no V–R confounding by latents); (iii) introducing necessary non-recoverability conditions for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) demonstrating that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when P(V) (or P(y|x)) is not, including cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4), leveraging do-calculus/d*-separation; and (v) giving necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects in the “simple attrition” setting with a single partially observed variable (Theorems 5–6). The work is positioned against classical MCAR/MAR assumptions (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002), ML/EM approaches (Dempster et al., 1977; Lauritzen, 1995; Koller & Friedman, 2009), IPW/MI (Robins et al., 1994; van der Laan & Robins, 2003), and recent m-graph contributions (Mohan et al., 2013; Mohan & Pearl, 2014), and builds on identifiability theory (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009) and ADMG/separation concepts (Richardson, 2003; Shpitser & Pearl, 2008).

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: The technical development appears coherent and largely careful. Extending ordered factorizations to include R variables is natural and useful; the minimality condition is well-motivated. The collider-path partitioning criterion (Theorem 2) is interesting, though the stated applicability (“any semi-Markovian model”) is constrained by its own preconditions (no neighbor/collider-path connection of each X ∈ Vm to Rx), which should be emphasized as a limitation rather than a general solution. The non-recoverability results are valuable but only provide necessary conditions, not full characterizations. The claims about causal recoverability (Theorem 4) are compelling and align with dormant independence ideas, but the scope (|Vm|=1 and specific separations) is narrow. Proofs are relegated to the appendix; more intuition in the main text would help. No empirical evaluation or algorithmic implementation is provided to demonstrate finite-sample behavior or practical usability.

- Clarity: The paper is dense and notation-heavy. Key definitions (compatibility of manifest/underlying distributions, shorthand like R′x) are present but could be streamlined. Several notational inconsistencies/typos are distracting (e.g., mixing R′z/R′ y spacing, “GX and GX,” “X ′ be a shorthand for X=0” without clarifying that binary coding is assumed). The reference list is poorly formatted with placeholders and inconsistencies. Figures are central; ensure they are legible and self-contained. The “general algorithms” in Section 4 are not presented as explicit procedures; adding pseudocode and a worked example would improve clarity.

- Originality: The paper advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013) by broadening sequential factorization to interacting R, proposing collider-path-based recoverability conditions, and providing necessary non-recoverability criteria. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry is an important message, though related ideas exist in dormant independence/selection-bias identification. The simple attrition necessary-and-sufficient criteria seem novel and practically relevant.

- Significance: If correct, the results help practitioners judge when identifiability from MNAR data is possible, especially for causal effects, and correct misconceptions (e.g., attrition recoverability does not require X to be an ancestor of Ry). However, significance would be strengthened by an implementable decision/estimation pipeline and empirical demonstrations on simulated/real attrition scenarios. As-is, the impact is mainly theoretical and incremental relative to Mohan et al. (2013).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual advance: causal effects can be recoverable when associated probabilistic queries are not; concrete graphical criteria are provided.
- Extension of ordered factorization to interacting R variables, with illustrative examples.
- New necessary non-recoverability conditions that can prevent misguided analyses.
- Practical specialization: necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited algorithmic contribution: no complete decision/estimand algorithm or implementation; “general algorithms” are not concretely specified.
- Scope restrictions: several theorems require strong preconditions (e.g., no neighbor/collider path between Vm and Rx; |Vm|=1), limiting general applicability.
- No empirical evaluation; lack of finite-sample demonstrations or sensitivity to positivity violations.
- Clarity/notation and reference formatting issues; some statements overstate generality.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an explicit, sound decision-and-estimand algorithm that, given an m-graph and target query, either returns a recoverability verdict with an estimand or certifies failure, integrating Theorem 1, Theorem 2 partition checks, and Theorem 3/Corollary 2 tests; discuss complexity.
- Include simulated studies showing consistent estimation with proposed estimands, robustness to sample size/positivity, and comparison to MAR/MCAR-blind methods and IPW/MI under MNAR.
- Clarify the scope of Theorem 2: restate the preconditions up front, add intuition and counterexamples, and discuss how it relates to existing ADMG factorizations and nested Markov properties.
- For Theorem 4, broaden beyond |Vm|=1 if possible, or clearly position as a base case; provide a step-by-step do-calculus derivation on a nontrivial example.
- Improve exposition: unify notation (avoid overloading primes; define binary coding vs general outcomes), include pseudocode for finding admissible sequences, and ensure figures annotate separating sets.
- Clean and complete the bibliography (consistent citation formats; fix placeholders) and discuss related graphical identification literature on MNAR beyond the cited works if relevant.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper contains interesting and potentially useful theoretical insights and corrections to prevailing intuitions, especially in the causal/attrition setting. However, to meet NeurIPS standards, it needs a clearer algorithmic contribution, broader applicability or clearer scoping of results, empirical validation, and improved presentation. I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.
