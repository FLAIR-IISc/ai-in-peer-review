## Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Summary:**

This paper extends previous work on missing data using "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to provide sufficient conditions for recovering both probabilistic and causal queries. It refines the sequential factorization theorem, offers algorithms for cases where that theorem fails, and introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries (specifically, P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable *even when* the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, and crucially, even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism. The authors apply these results to the problem of attrition, providing a more nuanced understanding of recoverability in this common scenario.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds directly upon Mohan et al. [2013] and Mohan & Pearl [2014], extending their formal framework for analyzing missing data. It acknowledges and distinguishes itself from classical approaches like listwise deletion, maximum likelihood estimation, inverse probability weighting, and imputation. The authors position their work as a graphical model-based approach that provides explicit conditions for identifiability and recoverability, rather than relying on assumptions like MAR or MCAR. They also relate their work to recent advances in missing data handling within specific fields like recommender systems, and acknowledge prior work by Garcia [2013] on attrition, highlighting where their approach offers improvements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Formal Framework:** The use of m-graphs provides a clear and rigorous way to represent the missingness process and its relationship to the underlying data generating process. This allows for precise statements about recoverability.
*   **Causal Focus:** The ability to recover causal queries even when probabilistic queries are unrecoverable is a significant contribution. This is particularly valuable in applications where causal inference is a primary goal.
*   **Generalization of Sequential Factorization:** Extending the sequential factorization theorem to handle interactions between missingness mechanisms is a valuable technical advance, broadening the applicability of this approach.
*   **Non-Recoverability Criteria:** Providing graphical criteria for *non*-recoverability is as important as identifying conditions for recovery, helping practitioners avoid misinterpreting results.
*   **Attrition Application:** The application to attrition problems provides a concrete example of the paper’s utility and highlights its relevance to a common practical challenge.
*   **Thorough Theoretical Development:** The paper presents a series of theorems and corollaries with clear statements and proofs (in the appendix), demonstrating a strong theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical notation and theoretical development can be dense and challenging to follow for readers not already familiar with graphical models and causal inference. While the authors strive for rigor, the presentation could be made more accessible.
*   **Scalability:** While the theoretical framework is well-developed, the paper lacks discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms proposed for cases where sequential factorization fails. Implementing these algorithms in practice could be computationally intensive.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper relies heavily on theoretical results. While examples are provided, a more extensive empirical evaluation – perhaps with simulations or real-world datasets – would strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods.
*   **Focus on Simple Cases:** While the paper addresses more complex scenarios than previous work, many of the examples and theorems assume relatively simple m-graph structures.  The applicability to highly complex, real-world missing data patterns could be limited.
*   **Minor Notational Inconsistencies:** A few instances of slightly inconsistent notation were observed (e.g. using 'R' for both missingness mechanisms and the set of such mechanisms). While not major, these could lead to confusion.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** Very Good. The paper presents a technically sound and rigorous framework for analyzing missing data. The theorems are well-defined, and the proofs appear to be correct.  However, the lack of empirical validation tempers the assessment.
*   **Clarity:** Good. While the notation is dense, the authors do a reasonable job of explaining the core concepts. A more accessible presentation, with more intuitive explanations and perhaps visual aids, would improve clarity.
*   **Originality:** Good. The extension of m-graphs, the generalization of the sequential factorization theorem, and the demonstration of causal query recoverability in the face of non-separability are all original contributions.
*   **Significance:** Good. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference and provides a more nuanced understanding of missing data. The ability to recover causal queries, even when probabilistic queries are not, is particularly significant.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation for analyzing missing data using m-graphs.
*   Offers a novel and potentially impactful approach to recovering causal effects.
*   Extends previous work and addresses limitations in existing methods.
*   Presents clear criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability.

**Con:**

*   Lacks empirical validation.
*   Computational complexity of the proposed algorithms is unclear.
*   The theoretical framework may be inaccessible to some readers.
*   Limited discussion of scalability to complex real-world scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. While the lack of empirical evaluation is a concern, the theoretical contributions of this paper are significant and the framework it presents is promising. I recommend the authors address the following in a revision:

1.  **Add a section on computational complexity:** Discuss the computational cost of the algorithms for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization fails.
2.  **Consider including a small-scale simulation study:** Demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods on a simulated dataset with a realistic missing data pattern.
3.  **Improve Clarity:** Revise the presentation to make it more accessible, perhaps with more intuitive explanations and visual aids. Address the minor notational inconsistencies.



This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of missing data analysis and has the potential to advance our understanding of causal inference in the presence of incomplete data.
