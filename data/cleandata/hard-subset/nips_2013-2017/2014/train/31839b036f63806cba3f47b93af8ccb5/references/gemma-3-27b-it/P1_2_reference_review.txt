## Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the theory of missing data recovery using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It builds upon prior work by Mohan et al. and Pearl, providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly focusing on scenarios where standard factorization methods fail and crucially, when causal queries can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable. The paper is mathematically rigorous, and the application to attrition problems is a strong motivating example. However, the density of notation and theoretical focus might hinder accessibility for a broader audience.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper focuses on extending the framework of m-graphs for analyzing missing data. It introduces a "General Ordered Factorization" theorem that expands upon previous work, enabling the recovery of probabilistic queries in more complex scenarios (e.g., longitudinal studies with interactions between missingness mechanisms). Crucially, the authors demonstrate that causal queries, specifically P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic relationships P(y,x) or P(y|x) are *not* recoverable. This is a significant contribution, as it highlights a key advantage of causal reasoning in handling missing data. The authors also present criteria for non-recoverability and demonstrate how these results apply to the common problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (provided in the appendix).
* **Novelty:**  The key finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not is a significant advance in the field. This circumvents a long-held limitation.
* **Generality:** The "General Ordered Factorization" theorem expands the applicability of m-graphs to a wider range of problems, including those with interacting missingness mechanisms.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application of these results to attrition, a common challenge in many fields, demonstrates the practical value of the research.
* **Well-Connected to Prior Work:** The paper clearly builds upon and cites relevant work from Pearl, Mohan et al., and others in the field of causal inference and missing data.
* **Clear Non-Recoverability Criteria:** Providing conditions under which recovery *fails* is just as important as providing conditions for success, and this paper does so effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is densely written and uses a significant amount of specialized notation. This could make it difficult for readers unfamiliar with m-graphs and causal inference to fully grasp the contributions.  More intuitive explanations and visual aids could improve clarity.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper provides theoretical guarantees, it lacks empirical validation. Demonstrating the recoverability of causal effects on simulated or real datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Example Complexity:** While the examples illustrate the theorems, they are quite complex and require careful study.  A simpler, more intuitive example might better convey the core ideas.
* **Focus on Simple Attrition:** The application to attrition focuses on a "simple" case. Discussing the limitations of the approach in more complex attrition scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Potential for Over-reliance on Assumptions:** The recoverability results rely on strong assumptions about the m-graph structure. A discussion of how sensitive the results are to misspecification of the m-graph would be valuable.



**Detailed Evaluation Based on Criteria:**

* **Quality (4/5):** The paper is technically sound and the theorems are well-supported by logical reasoning.  The completeness of the work is good, although the lack of empirical validation is a minor drawback. The authors are generally careful in stating their assumptions.
* **Clarity (3/5):** The paper suffers from a high degree of technical density and specialized notation. While mathematically precise, the explanations could be more accessible to a broader audience. Improved visualizations and simpler examples would be helpful.
* **Originality (5/5):** The central finding regarding the recoverability of causal queries when probabilistic queries are not is highly original and represents a significant advancement in the field. The generalization of the factorization theorem is also a valuable contribution.
* **Significance (4/5):** The results have the potential to impact research in a variety of fields where missing data is a common problem, particularly those involving causal inference. The ability to recover causal effects even in challenging scenarios could lead to more reliable conclusions.

**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of missing data recovery.
* The results have the potential to address a practical problem with broad applicability.
* The paper is well-written (albeit dense) and builds upon a solid foundation of prior work.
* The authors clearly identify the assumptions underlying their results.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s density and technical nature may limit its accessibility.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims.
* The focus on simple attrition may not fully capture the complexity of real-world scenarios.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Simplify Examples:** Provide a more intuitive and less complex example to illustrate the key concepts.
2.  **Enhance Accessibility:**  Add more explanatory text to clarify the notation and mathematical concepts. Consider including a glossary of terms.
3.  **Discuss Sensitivity Analysis:** A brief discussion of how sensitive the results are to potential misspecification of the m-graph would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Consider Empirical Validation:** While not essential, including a small-scale empirical demonstration would greatly enhance the impact of the work.



This paper makes a genuine contribution to the field, and addressing these minor revisions would significantly enhance its value and impact.
