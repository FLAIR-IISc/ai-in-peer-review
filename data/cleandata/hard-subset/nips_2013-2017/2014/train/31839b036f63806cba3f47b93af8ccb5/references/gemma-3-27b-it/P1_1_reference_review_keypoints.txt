- Extends m-graphs to give sufficient conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Refines the sequential factorization theorem and provides algorithms for cases where it fails.  
- Introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability.  
- Shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not, and even if Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism.  
- Applies results to attrition problems, illustrating practical relevance.  
- Builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan & Pearl (2014) and distinguishes from classical MAR/MCAR-based methods.  
- Frames the approach as a graphical-model-based identifiability analysis rather than assumption-driven estimation.  
- Acknowledges related work in recommender systems and prior attrition analyses (Garcia 2013).  
- Strength: provides a clear, formal m-graph framework for representing missingness and recoverability.  
- Strength: emphasizes causal inference, enabling recovery of causal effects under conditions where probabilistic recovery fails.  
- Strength: generalizes sequential factorization to handle interactions between missingness mechanisms.  
- Strength: supplies graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability.  
- Strength: solid theoretical development with theorems and proofs (appendix).  
- Weakness: dense mathematical notation and theoretical presentation may be hard for non-experts.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational complexity and practical scalability of proposed algorithms.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation—few simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate utility.  
- Weakness: many examples/theorems assume relatively simple m-graph structures, limiting clear applicability to complex real-world patterns.  
- Weakness: minor notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers.  
- Qualitative assessment: very good quality, good clarity (but dense), original contributions, and significant implications for missing data and causal inference.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical foundation, novel causal recovery results, extension of prior work, and clear recoverability criteria.  
- Cons against acceptance: missing empirical validation, unclear computational cost, accessibility concerns, and limited scalability discussion.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision requests: add a computational complexity section, include a small-scale simulation study, and improve presentation clarity and notation.
