Summary
This paper extends the m-graph framework of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) for reasoning about recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data. The main contributions are: (i) a generalized ordered factorization (Theorem 1) that explicitly includes missingness indicators R in the admissible ordering, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new recovery formula for joint distributions (Theorem 2) based on partitioning R into groups disconnected by adjacency or collider paths, and conditioning on Markov blankets Mb(R(i)); (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) that provide necessary conditions under which P(V) or P(Y|X) cannot be recovered; (iv) conditions under which causal effects can be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not, leveraging do-calculus and dormant independences (Theorem 4), including tight results for simple attrition with a single partially observed variable (Theorems 5–6). The work situates itself relative to Rubin’s MAR/MCAR taxonomy (where model-blind procedures suffice), the maximum likelihood and IPW literature, and prior causal-graphical treatments of attrition (e.g., Garcia 2013), and builds directly on m-graphs, d-separation in ADMGs (Richardson 2003), and identification via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; 2008).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Extending ordered factorization to include R variables is a clean and useful idea that strictly enlarges the class of recoverable problems beyond Mohan et al. (2013), particularly when R nodes have edges among themselves.
- New recovery formula: Theorem 2 provides a constructive factorization of P(V) via complete cases and conditional probabilities of R(i)=0 given their Markov blankets, with clear graphical preconditions. This aligns intuitively with selection-adjustment/IPW but in a more general, graphically checkable form.
- Negative results: The non-recoverability conditions are valuable for practitioners; necessary conditions for P(V) and P(Y|X) reduce time spent chasing impossible estimands.
- Causal effects beyond probabilistic separability: The example and Theorem 4 convincingly demonstrate that P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable, by conditioning on R′y and applying do-calculus. The simple-attrition characterizations (Theorems 5–6) are crisp and practically relevant.
- Fit to NeurIPS: The topic is squarely within graphical models/causal inference with clear methodological implications.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Several statements rely on proofs deferred to the appendix; the main text would benefit from brief proof sketches to convey why the conditions in Theorems 2–4 are necessary/sufficient. In particular, the role of collider paths in both recovery and non-recovery deserves more intuition.
- The definitions and conditions in Theorem 4 (d*-separation, W vs W1) need tightening; the notation “for W = W\X” and use of W1 are unclear, and the identifiability preconditions could be linked more explicitly to the ID algorithm.
- The assumption “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” is standard for m-graphs but should be discussed vis-à-vis longitudinal designs where dropout may affect subsequent measurement processes; clarify how such effects are represented (e.g., via proxies V* and R-to-V* edges only).

Clarity
- Notation issues: Early on, “Let G(V,E) be the causal DAG where V = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R” appears inconsistent; V is reused for both “all nodes” and “substantive nodes.” Throughout, there are minor mismatches (e.g., “Z⊥⊥Rz” vs using Ry elsewhere; “GX and GX” presumably GX and G^X).
- The minimality requirement in Definition 2 is important; however, the text could more systematically explain how to algorithmically find admissible sequences (e.g., by iteratively peeling minimal separating sets).
- Figures are central but not included; several claims refer to Figures 2–4; please include the diagrams and ensure that all conditional independences used in examples are visually checkable.

Originality
- The extension to include R in ordered factorizations and the explicit non-recoverability criteria appear new relative to Mohan et al. (2013). The causal recovery despite inseparability leverages dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl 2008); making this concrete for missing data is useful though not entirely novel.

Significance
- The results are theoretically meaningful and practically relevant for analysts working with MNAR data. However, the paper would be stronger with a more explicit “algorithmic” presentation (procedures/pseudocode) and demonstrations on synthetic/real m-graphs to illustrate gains over prior criteria.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise algorithm to (a) search for admissible sequences (Theorem 1), (b) construct R-partitions and Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2), and (c) test non-recoverability (Theorem 3/Corollary 2). Analyze computational complexity.
- Add proof sketches or intuitive explanations: e.g., why collider paths between Y and Ry obstruct P(V) yet can be circumvented for P(y|do(x)) by conditioning on R′y.
- Clarify Theorem 4’s notation and give at least one fully worked derivation besides Example 3, explicitly invoking do-calculus rules and d*-separation checks.
- Discuss limitations of the assumption “R not parent of V ∪ U” and how designs with skip patterns or measurement interference are modeled.
- Improve writing and references: fix numerous typos, unify notation, and correct the bibliography formatting (several entries have placeholders like “? \Q2013\E”). Ensure all cited results are properly referenced (e.g., Shpitser & Pearl 2008 on dormant independence).
- Include a small empirical demonstration (simulated m-graphs) to show the proposed criteria identify recoverability where prior methods fail.

Pros for acceptance
- Extends the m-graph toolkit with practical, checkable sufficient and necessary conditions.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recoverability, highlighting scenarios where causal effects are recoverable despite MNAR obstacles.
- Clean characterizations for simple attrition with clear implications for practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity and notation issues impede accessibility; figures and formal algorithms are missing from the main text.
- Limited empirical or implementation evidence; readers lack guidance on automating the proposed checks.
- Some reliance on prior do-calculus results without fully self-contained derivations.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The ideas are promising and likely impactful, but the current presentation has notable clarity/notation issues, lacks algorithmic detail and validation, and leaves key proofs/intuition to the appendix. With improved exposition, precise notation, concrete algorithms/pseudocode, corrected references, and illustrative experiments or case studies, this could become a strong contribution.
