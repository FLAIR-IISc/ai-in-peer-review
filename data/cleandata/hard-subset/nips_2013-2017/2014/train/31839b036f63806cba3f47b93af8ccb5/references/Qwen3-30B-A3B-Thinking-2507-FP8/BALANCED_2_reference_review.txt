

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to develop general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present theoretical results showing that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (like P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not, which represents a significant theoretical advance. The paper builds on prior work in causal inference (Pearl, 2009; Shpitser and Pearl, 2006) and missing data literature (Rubin, 1976; Mohan and Pearl, 2014), extending the recoverability framework to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by establishing new graphical conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not (Example 3) is particularly novel and important for causal inference practitioners.

2. **Rigorous Framework:** The authors provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability, which is valuable for researchers trying to diagnose whether their missing data problem can yield consistent estimates. The formalization of non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) fills an important gap in the literature.

3. **Application to Attrition:** The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is well-motivated and corrects a prevailing misconception in the literature (Garcia, 2013), demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

4. **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper systematically addresses multiple aspects of missing data problems, from probabilistic queries to causal queries, and from general missingness mechanisms to specific attrition problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation. While the theoretical results are sound, the practical significance would be greatly enhanced by demonstrating the application of these methods to real datasets or simulations showing how the proposed conditions would be used in practice.

2. **Limited Context for Non-Causal Inference Readers:** The paper assumes familiarity with causal inference concepts (d-separation, do-calculus, inducing paths) without sufficient background for readers outside this specific subfield. More context would make the paper more accessible to the broader NIPS audience.

3. **Insufficient Visualization:** The paper would benefit from more visual examples illustrating the graphical conditions (e.g., showing m-graphs that satisfy vs. violate the conditions in Theorem 3). The current figures (Figures 1-4) are helpful but could be expanded.

4. **Computational Considerations:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the recovery algorithms or practical implementation challenges, which would be important for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the literature on missing data, extending prior work with novel results about causal query recoverability. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and address an important problem in statistical inference. However, the lack of empirical validation and limited accessibility for readers unfamiliar with causal inference are significant limitations for a NIPS submission, which typically values both theoretical and empirical contributions.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. **Include empirical validation:** Add experiments demonstrating the application of the theoretical results to synthetic or real datasets. This would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and relevance to the machine learning community.

2. **Provide more context for causal inference concepts:** Add a brief tutorial section explaining key causal inference concepts for readers unfamiliar with the field, or at least more detailed explanations of concepts like d*-separation in the main text.

3. **Expand visual examples:** Include more figures illustrating the graphical conditions and their application to specific missing data problems.

4. **Discuss computational aspects:** Address the computational complexity and practical implementation challenges of the proposed recovery algorithms.

The paper's core theoretical contributions are valuable and could make a significant impact with the suggested improvements. However, as currently presented, it lacks the empirical validation and broader accessibility that would make it suitable for NIPS. The theoretical advances are important, but the field increasingly values papers that bridge theory with practical applications, which this paper does not sufficiently demonstrate.
