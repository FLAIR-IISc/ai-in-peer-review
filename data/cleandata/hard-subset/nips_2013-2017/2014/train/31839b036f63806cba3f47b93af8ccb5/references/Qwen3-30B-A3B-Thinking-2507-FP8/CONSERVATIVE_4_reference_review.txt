

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) for handling missing data. The authors present theoretical conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from missing data, with particular focus on cases where variables and their missingness mechanisms are inseparable. The paper builds on prior graphical models for missing data, extending the theoretical framework with new theorems and graphical criteria.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically rigorous with well-structured theoretical development. The extensions to sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and the new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) logically build on the foundational work of Mohan et al. [2013]. The examples (e.g., Example 1 and Example 3) effectively illustrate how the theoretical conditions apply to specific graphical structures. The paper's organization is clear, with logical progression from probabilistic to causal queries, and the appendix provides complete proofs as promised.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical validation. As a NIPS reviewer, I must emphasize that theoretical contributions without experimental demonstration of practical utility are insufficient for acceptance. The paper makes strong claims about recoverability of causal effects when probabilistic relations are not recoverable (Example 3), but provides no evidence that this approach would work better than established methods in real data scenarios.

The paper fails to compare its theoretical results with existing approaches. For instance, the authors claim their work "rectifies prevailing opinion" (Section 7.2), but provide no empirical evidence to support this claim against Garcia [2013] or other existing methods. Without benchmarking against established techniques like multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting, or likelihood-based methods, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed graphical criteria offer practical advantages.

The paper also lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability. For a machine learning conference like NIPS, these considerations are crucial for assessing practical applicability. The proposed algorithms (Section 4) aren't evaluated for computational feasibility, which is a significant omission.

**Originality:**
While the paper extends Mohan et al. [2013] with new theorems, the core approach of using graphical models for missing data is not novel. The paper's contribution appears to be theoretical refinement rather than a fundamentally new method. The authors properly cite prior work, but the incremental nature of the extension is not adequately contextualized against the broader landscape of missing data methods.

**Significance:**
The problem of missing data is important, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how its theoretical results would translate to improved practice. The claim that "causal effects may be recoverable even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism" (Abstract) is intriguing, but without empirical evidence showing this works better than existing approaches in real-world scenarios, its significance is questionable. The paper doesn't address whether the proposed conditions are more or less restrictive than existing MAR/MCAR assumptions, which is a critical practical consideration.

**Critical Assessment:**
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's theoretical contributions interesting but insufficiently validated. The field of missing data has a rich literature with well-established methods (e.g., multiple imputation, likelihood-based approaches), and this paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why the proposed graphical framework would be preferable in practice.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Empirical validation on real or simulated datasets
2. Comparison with established missing data methods
3. Discussion of computational complexity and scalability
4. Clearer explanation of practical advantages over existing approaches

Without these elements, the paper remains a theoretical extension of prior work without demonstrating its practical value. While the theoretical contributions are sound, the lack of empirical validation and comparison with established methods makes it difficult to assess whether this work represents a meaningful advance for the field.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. The paper makes a theoretically interesting extension to existing work on missing data, but as a NIPS paper, it fails to meet the conference's standard for empirical validation and practical significance. Theoretical contributions are valuable, but they must be grounded in evidence of practical utility to be considered for publication in a machine learning conference like NIPS. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper with empirical validation and comparative analysis to be considered for acceptance.
