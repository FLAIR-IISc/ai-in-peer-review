This paper tackles the crucial problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data, leveraging the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). The core contribution lies in extending existing recoverability conditions, particularly demonstrating that causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. This is a significant and potentially impactful result, especially given the limitations of traditional MAR/MCAR assumptions. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation, building upon their prior work and that of Pearl and colleagues, and offer theorems and corollaries for assessing recoverability under various scenarios, including attrition.

The paper is well-structured and logically progresses from defining m-graphs and recoverability to presenting increasingly general conditions. The inclusion of examples, like those in Sections 3 and 6, aids understanding of the theoretical results. The explicit discussion of non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) is a valuable addition, moving beyond simply identifying conditions for success. The application to attrition problems in Section 7 grounds the theoretical work in a practical domain.

However, while the theoretical depth is commendable, the empirical validation is conspicuously absent. The paper remains entirely theoretical, relying on proofs and examples to demonstrate the validity of the presented theorems. The lack of experimental evaluation – even on simulated data – weakens the impact. It's difficult to gauge the practical applicability and performance of these methods without assessing how they fare in real-world or controlled settings. The discussion in Section 7 regarding attrition feels incomplete without showcasing how these theoretical conditions translate to concrete improvements in causal effect estimation compared to standard methods.

Furthermore, the notation, while consistent with prior work, is dense and can be challenging to follow. Some definitions (e.g., Rpart, Mb(R(i))) are introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, requiring careful review of the surrounding text. While the authors aim for generality, this sometimes comes at the cost of clarity. A more streamlined presentation of the mathematical framework could enhance accessibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core result – recoverable causal effects even with inseparable missingness mechanisms – is genuinely novel and challenges conventional wisdom.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a high level of mathematical sophistication and provides a rigorous foundation for the proposed methods.
*   **Potential Impact:** If validated, this work could significantly advance the field of causal inference with missing data, particularly in domains where strong assumptions about missingness are difficult to justify.
*   **Formalization of Non-recoverability:** Providing criteria for when recovery *fails* is a valuable contribution, offering practical guidance to researchers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of any experimental results is a significant weakness.
*   **Notational Complexity:** The dense notation may hinder accessibility for researchers unfamiliar with the specific terminology.
*   **Limited Practical Demonstration:** The application to attrition, while illustrative, lacks a concrete demonstration of improved performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation and some notational challenges, I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The core idea – that causal effects can be recovered under weaker conditions than previously thought – is genuinely novel and has the potential to reshape how we approach missing data problems. I am willing to overlook the absence of experiments given the novelty and theoretical depth, and the authors' established track record in this area. However, I strongly encourage the authors to include empirical validation in a future version to solidify their claims and demonstrate the practical utility of their framework.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the lack of empirical validation in future work. The potential impact of this theoretical contribution outweighs the current limitations.
