

# NIPS Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) by introducing a formal graphical framework ("Missingness Graphs" or m-graphs) for analyzing missing data problems. The authors develop theoretical conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and crucially demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can often be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable. The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries beyond the sequential factorization theorem, (2) new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, (3) the key insight that causal effects may be recoverable without separability of Y from Ry, and (4) application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions. This work builds upon but significantly extends the graphical models literature for missing data (Mohan et al., 2013; Pearl & Mohan, 2013) and connects to causal inference frameworks (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006).

## Quality
The paper demonstrates high technical quality with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs (with full proofs in the appendix), establishing clear conditions for recoverability. The paper carefully distinguishes between probabilistic and causal recoverability, which is a significant conceptual advancement. The examples (particularly Example 3) effectively demonstrate the practical implications of the theoretical results. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and provide non-recoverability criteria to complement the positive results.

The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, with clear theorems, examples, and a discussion of related work. The authors are careful to distinguish their contributions from prior work (e.g., correcting Garcia, 2013) and to identify the boundaries of their results. The use of graphical criteria for recovery is a substantial methodological contribution that advances beyond traditional missing data frameworks (MCAR, MAR, MNAR).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from foundational concepts to more complex results. The notation is consistent and well-defined, though the complexity of the graphical concepts (particularly the general ordered factorization and d*-separation) may require multiple readings for full comprehension. The examples (e.g., Example 1 in Section 3) effectively illustrate the application of the theoretical results to concrete scenarios.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the proposed conditions with the standard MAR/MCAR framework, which would help readers unfamiliar with graphical models for missing data. A brief intuitive explanation of why causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones cannot would also enhance clarity.

## Originality
The paper makes several important original contributions. The most significant is the demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even when Y and Ry are not d-separable (Theorem 4 and Example 3), which contradicts the conventional wisdom that separability is required. This represents a fundamental conceptual advance in understanding the relationship between missing data mechanisms and causal inference.

The paper also introduces novel graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2), which fills a gap in the literature. The application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5 and 6) corrects previous misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013). While building on Mohan et al. (2013), the paper substantially extends their work by addressing a broader class of problems and making the key distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

## Significance
This paper addresses a pervasive problem in empirical research—missing data—with significant practical implications. The results provide researchers with a formal framework to determine when causal effects can be reliably estimated from incomplete datasets, which is crucial for fields like epidemiology, social sciences, and machine learning. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not has profound implications for real-world applications where missing data is common.

The paper advances the state of the art by moving beyond the traditional MAR/MCAR framework to provide a more nuanced graphical characterization of recoverability. This represents a significant theoretical advance that will inform future work in causal inference with missing data. The results are immediately applicable to a wide range of practical problems, including attrition in longitudinal studies (Section 7), making the work highly relevant to both practitioners and researchers.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical rigor with clear definitions and theorems
- Key insight that causal effects can be recovered without Y and Ry being separable
- Novel non-recoverability criteria that fill an important gap
- Practical application to attrition problems with necessary/sufficient conditions
- Careful distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability
- Corrects misconceptions in previous literature (Garcia, 2013)

**Weaknesses:**
- Some of the graphical concepts (e.g., d*-separation) may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with causal inference
- Limited discussion of computational aspects (how these conditions would be implemented in practice)
- Could provide more concrete examples of the application to real-world datasets

## Recommendation
This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the literature on missing data and causal inference. The results are rigorous, novel, and address a problem of significant practical importance. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not is particularly compelling and represents a major conceptual advance. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work and provides a comprehensive framework for understanding recoverability in missing data problems.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS as a strong theoretical contribution that will influence future work in causal inference and missing data analysis. The paper meets all four criteria at a high level: it is of high quality, well-written, original, and significant to the field. The results provide researchers with new tools to determine when causal effects can be reliably estimated from incomplete datasets, which has broad practical implications across scientific disciplines.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and should be of interest to the NIPS community, which has a strong focus on causal inference and graphical models.
