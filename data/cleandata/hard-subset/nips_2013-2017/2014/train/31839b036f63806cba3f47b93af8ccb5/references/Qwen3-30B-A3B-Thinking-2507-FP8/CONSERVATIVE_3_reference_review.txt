

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper proposes an extension to Mohan et al. [2013] by introducing Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to formalize missing data mechanisms and establish conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries. The authors present theoretical results showing that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (like P(y,x)) are not recoverable, particularly when variables and their missingness mechanisms are not d-separable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear formalization of missing data mechanisms using graphical models, building logically on Mohan et al. [2013].
- The theoretical results (Theorems 1-6) are well-structured and demonstrate careful mathematical reasoning.
- The paper correctly identifies an important gap in the literature regarding the recoverability of causal effects when probabilistic queries are not recoverable.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is the complete absence of empirical validation. While the theoretical contributions are sound, the paper fails to provide any experimental results demonstrating how these methods would perform in practice. Missing data is fundamentally an applied problem, and NIPS strongly values both theoretical and empirical contributions. Without empirical validation against established baselines (e.g., multiple imputation, maximum likelihood under MAR), the practical significance of these theoretical results remains unclear.

The novelty is overstated. The core idea that causal effects may be recoverable when probabilistic relations are not is subtle, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The related work section properly cites relevant literature, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods in practice.

The paper also lacks any discussion of implementation challenges. The theoretical conditions for recoverability don't translate easily into practical algorithms, and the paper provides no guidance on how practitioners might actually implement these methods. Without addressing computational complexity or practical constraints, the results remain abstract.

Most significantly, the paper doesn't compare its approach to established missing data methods on real or simulated datasets. For instance, how would their method perform compared to multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting on standard missing data benchmarks? Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical conditions provide any practical advantage.

**Originality:** The paper extends Mohan et al. [2013], but the main contribution (causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic queries are not) is a subtle theoretical refinement rather than a significant methodological advance. The approach is largely a formalization of existing concepts rather than a novel technique.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is difficult to assess without empirical validation. While the theoretical question is interesting, the paper doesn't demonstrate how these conditions would lead to better inferences in real-world missing data problems. The claim that "causal effects may be recoverable even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism" is intriguing, but without evidence of practical utility, it remains a theoretical curiosity.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear mathematical definitions. However, the connection between the theoretical results and practical application is weak, which undermines the clarity of the paper's contribution.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical work is technically sound, the paper fails to meet NIPS's standard for empirical validation. The absence of any experiments or comparisons with existing methods is a critical omission for a paper addressing a fundamentally applied problem. Theoretical contributions to missing data are valuable, but they must be accompanied by evidence of practical utility to be appropriate for NIPS.

The authors should consider conducting empirical validation on standard missing data datasets (e.g., from the UCI repository) to compare their approach with established methods like multiple imputation, maximum likelihood, and inverse probability weighting. Only then could they demonstrate whether their theoretical conditions translate to meaningful improvements in real-world missing data problems. Without this, the paper is a valuable theoretical contribution but not a complete scientific work suitable for NIPS.
