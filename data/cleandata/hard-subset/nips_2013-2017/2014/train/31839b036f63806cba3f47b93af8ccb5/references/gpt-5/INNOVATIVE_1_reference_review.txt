Summary and relation to prior work
The paper advances the graphical approach to reasoning with missing data via “missingness graphs” (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (AISTATS’13) and Mohan & Pearl (AISTATS’14), it contributes:
- An extension of ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R, yielding a generalized sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that enables recovery when missingness mechanisms interact (edges among R variables).
- A new recovery route for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2), via partitioning the R variables by collider-path connectivity and using Markov blankets Mb(R(i)). A corollary treats the no-latent-between-R-and-V case.
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), filling an acknowledged gap.
- Conditions under which causal effects are recoverable even when probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x), P(x,y)) are not, including when Y and its missingness indicator Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4), using d*-separation/dormant independence.
- Necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) under simple attrition (|Vm|=1), clarifying limitations of prior claims (e.g., Garcia’13).
All proofs are deferred to the appendix. The work is well-situated within Pearl’s causal identification framework and ADMGs, and it explicitly connects to MCAR/MAR settings and the broader missing data literature.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: The paper goes beyond prior sufficient-recovery results by (i) admitting interacting missingness mechanisms in sequential factorization; (ii) offering general recovery formulas when no admissible sequence exists; and (iii) introducing non-recoverability criteria. The result that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable is particularly striking and useful.
- Significance: Missing-not-at-random (MNAR) datasets are ubiquitous. Clear graphical conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability, plus constructive estimands, are valuable to ML and causal inference communities. The attrition results provide crisp necessary/sufficient criteria in a common experimental scenario.
- Theoretical completeness in scope: The paper covers joint, conditional, and causal queries, articulates when sequential factorization applies, provides an alternative pathway otherwise, and delineates impossibility, which together form a more rounded theory.
- Clarity of the high-level narrative: The overall progression (recoverability via generalized factorization, then beyond via R-partitions, then non-recoverability, then causal queries, then attrition) is well-motivated.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited algorithmic concreteness: Several results claim “general algorithms,” but there is no pseudocode or step-by-step procedure. For Theorem 2, please specify an implementable procedure to:
  1) Compute partitions R(i) (by detecting adjacency or collider-path connectivity among R), and
  2) Construct Mb(R(i)) and the estimand. Complexity discussion would help practitioners.
- Scope limitations for causal recovery: Theorem 4 and the attrition theorems largely treat |Vm|=1. While the single-variable case is important, please clarify how (or whether) Theorem 4 generalizes to multiple partially observed variables and how to operationalize the search for Z,W that satisfy the d*-separation/dormant-independence conditions.
- Practical validation: No empirical demonstrations (synthetic or real) illustrate finite-sample behavior or robustness to graph misspecification. Even a small simulation comparing your estimands vs. ML/MAR-based baselines (or IPW/DR methods) would increase impact and credibility.
- Assumptions and testability: Several results assume strict positivity over complete cases and semi-Markovian models. Please discuss:
  - How the “extension to non-positive complete cases” (footnote) affects the main theorems.
  - Sensitivity to latent confounding beyond what ADMGs capture and to violations of “R variables may not be parents of V∪U.”
  - Practical diagnostics and misspecification tests (link to Mohan & Pearl 2014) in the settings covered by your new theorems.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - You write “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively” but use the same symbol; please disambiguate.
  - Definition of “minimal” in Def. 2 should be made precise (setwise minimality).
  - Theorem statements would benefit from upfront, uniform assumptions (e.g., semi-Markovian ADMGs, positivity) and explicit quantification over variables.
  - Some reference entries are malformed or duplicated; please fix formatting.
- Positioning relative to identification algorithms: Since Section 6 leverages do-calculus/dormant independence, please explain how to integrate your criteria with the ID algorithm programmatically in the presence of m-graphs, and when your conditions are strictly stronger/weaker than applying ID to an augmented graph with missingness indicators.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, original theory that clearly extends prior work and fills important gaps (especially non-recoverability criteria and causal recoverability despite inseparability).
- High potential impact for causal ML practitioners dealing with MNAR and attrition.
- Clear estimands in several nontrivial cases; deep conceptual insight linking missingness structure and causal identification.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of algorithmic detail and empirical validation may hinder adoption.
- Some key causal results target the single-missing-variable case; broader generality is not demonstrated.
- Minor clarity and citation issues.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide a decision procedure (or pseudo-code) to check Theorem 2’s conditions and construct the estimand? How scalable is it?
2) How would you search for Z,W satisfying Theorem 4 in practice? Is there a finite, algorithmic criterion?
3) Do your non-recoverability criteria in Theorem 3 imply completeness for the simple attrition case, or are there corner cases not covered by Theorem 5/6?
4) How robust are your estimands to slight misspecifications in the m-graph?

Overall assessment
This paper makes bold, substantive theoretical contributions to the graphical treatment of missing data, advancing the state of the art beyond Mohan et al. (2013). Despite the lack of empirical validation and some presentation issues, the novelty and potential impact are high. I recommend acceptance, conditional on improving algorithmic clarity and fixing notation/references.
