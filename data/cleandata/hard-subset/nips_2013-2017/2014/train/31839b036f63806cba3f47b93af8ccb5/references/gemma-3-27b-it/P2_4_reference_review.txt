## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid theoretical extension to the framework of missingness graphs (m-graphs) for recovering probabilistic and, crucially, *causal* queries from data with missing values. While the technical details are dense and require a strong background in graphical models and causal inference, the paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating the possibility of recovering causal effects even when standard assumptions for identifiability (like MAR) are violated, and provides useful graphical criteria for assessing recoverability. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to improve clarity for a broader audience.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The authors build upon previous work on m-graphs to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data. They refine the sequential factorization theorem, introduce new criteria for non-recoverability, and importantly, demonstrate that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. The paper addresses the challenging problem of attrition, showing how their framework can be applied to recover causal effects in this common scenario.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:**  The main result – recovering causal effects even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable – is a valuable advancement. This extends the scope of recoverable queries beyond what’s typically achievable with standard MAR assumptions.
* **Rigorous Framework:** The paper is built on a well-defined and formal framework (m-graphs) and utilizes rigorous mathematical proofs (provided in the appendix).
* **Clear Connection to Causal Inference:**  The explicit focus on causal queries and the use of do-calculus demonstrate a strong understanding of the field and its relevance to missing data problems.
* **Practical Implications:** The graphical criteria for recoverability provide a useful tool for practitioners to assess the feasibility of drawing causal conclusions from incomplete datasets. The application to attrition problems highlights the practical relevance.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of existing approaches to handling missing data, including deletion methods, ML estimation, and other graphical model-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **High Technical Barrier:** The paper is *very* dense and mathematically involved.  Readers unfamiliar with m-graphs, do-calculus, and advanced graphical model concepts will likely struggle to fully grasp the contributions.  More intuitive explanations and motivating examples would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical.  While the proofs are convincing, demonstrating the performance of these techniques on real or simulated datasets would strengthen the findings and provide a better understanding of their practical applicability.
* **Notationally Challenging:**  The extensive use of notation, while precise, can be overwhelming.  A more gradual introduction of notation and a clear summary of symbols would improve readability.
* **Some Redundancy:**  Certain concepts (e.g., recoverability definition) are repeated multiple times throughout the paper. Streamlining these sections would improve flow.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs appear rigorous (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review). The completeness of the work is a slight concern given the lack of empirical validation, but the theoretical results are substantial. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of assumptions and limitations.
* **Clarity:** This is the paper's weakest point. The writing is dense and the notation is heavy. Significant effort could be made to improve clarity without sacrificing technical precision.  Adding more illustrative examples alongside the formal proofs would be helpful.
* **Originality:** The extension of recoverability results to the causal setting, especially the demonstration of recovering causal effects when Y and Ry are inseparable, is novel and a significant contribution. The refinements to the sequential factorization theorem also represent an original advancement.  The related work section suggests the authors are well aware of prior contributions.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of causal inference with missing data.  The ability to recover causal effects under weaker assumptions than previously thought is highly valuable.  The graphical criteria offer a practical tool for assessing the feasibility of causal analysis in real-world datasets.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Strong theoretical contribution with a novel result regarding causal effect recovery.
* Rigorous mathematical framework.
* Addresses a critical problem in data analysis (missing data).
* Provides practical tools for assessing recoverability.

**Against:**

* High technical barrier to entry.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Dense and challenging writing style.




**Overall Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity issues. Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Expand Intuitive Explanations:** Add more informal explanations of the core concepts and theorems, along with motivating examples.
2. **Consider Empirical Evaluation:**  Including simulations or applying the techniques to a real-world dataset (even as supplementary material) would greatly strengthen the paper.
3. **Streamline Notation & Writing:**  Reduce redundancy and provide a clear summary of notation early in the paper.  Simplify the writing style where possible without sacrificing technical accuracy.



This paper represents a valuable addition to the literature on missing data and causal inference, and with some improvements to clarity, it will be a valuable resource for researchers and practitioners in the field.
