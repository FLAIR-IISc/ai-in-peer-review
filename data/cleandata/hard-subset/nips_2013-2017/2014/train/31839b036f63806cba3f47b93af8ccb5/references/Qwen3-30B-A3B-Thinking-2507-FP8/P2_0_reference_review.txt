

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper presents an extension of the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to address the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors develop formal conditions under which joint distributions, conditional distributions, and causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered, even when traditional probabilistic recovery fails. The key insight is that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (like P(y,x)) are not, particularly when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-motivated theorems and proofs. The authors build systematically on Mohan et al. (2013), extending their sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (R variables influencing each other). The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic queries versus causal queries is particularly compelling and well-supported by Example 3, which demonstrates that P(y|do(z)) can be recovered even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (violating the separability condition required for probabilistic recovery).

The paper carefully distinguishes between necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms. The extension to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly noteworthy as it resolves a misconception in prior work (Garcia, 2013) regarding necessary conditions for causal effect recovery.

## Clarity

The paper is well-structured and logically organized, with clear definitions and examples that illustrate the theoretical concepts. The notation is consistent and well-explained (e.g., Definition 2 on general ordered factorization). The examples (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) are excellent at demonstrating the practical implications of the theorems.

However, some concepts could be explained more intuitively. For instance, the motivation for the minimality condition in Definition 2 would benefit from a more intuitive explanation beyond the technical counterexample. The transition from probabilistic to causal recoverability in Section 6 would also be strengthened with a more detailed discussion of why causal queries can circumvent the separability requirement.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending Mohan et al. (2013) to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact, which was previously unaddressed. The most novel contribution is the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not. This is a non-intuitive insight that challenges conventional wisdom about missing data.

The paper distinguishes itself from related work (Section 8) by addressing interactions among multiple missingness mechanisms, which previous work (Garcia, 2013; Thoemmes and Rose, 2013) had not properly considered. The necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under simple attrition (Theorem 6) also corrects a misconception in prior literature.

## Significance

This work has substantial significance for the field of causal inference and missing data analysis. Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, and the paper provides a formal framework for practitioners to determine when they can safely recover causal effects despite missingness. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability has important practical implications: researchers can now recognize when causal effects might be recoverable even when they cannot recover the underlying joint distribution.

The application to attrition (Section 7) is particularly valuable as attrition is a major challenge in longitudinal studies. The paper's resolution of the misconception about necessary conditions for causal effect recovery (Theorem 6 vs. Garcia, 2013) will directly impact how practitioners approach attrition problems.

## Strengths

1. The formal framework for missingness processes is well-developed and extends previous work in a meaningful way.
2. The key insight about causal recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability is novel and important.
3. The paper provides both sufficient and necessary conditions for recoverability, which is crucial for practitioners.
4. The extension to handle interactions among missingness mechanisms is a significant technical advancement.
5. The application to attrition problems resolves a misconception in the literature.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks empirical validation; it would be strengthened by showing how these methods perform on real datasets.
2. The practical implementation guidance for applying these criteria could be more detailed.
3. The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical framework without addressing computational complexity or scalability.
4. The connection between the theoretical results and existing missing data methods (like multiple imputation) could be more explicitly discussed.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis and causal inference. The extension of the m-graph framework to handle interacting missingness mechanisms and the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not represent important advances. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of substantial practical significance.

While the lack of empirical validation and implementation guidance are notable limitations, these do not undermine the theoretical contributions, which are substantial and well-supported. The paper meets the standards of NeurIPS as a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the field.

**Recommendation: Accept** (Strong Accept)

The paper's theoretical contributions are significant and well-motivated, with clear implications for both theory and practice. It represents a meaningful advancement over previous work and provides a valuable framework for researchers dealing with missing data in causal inference settings.
