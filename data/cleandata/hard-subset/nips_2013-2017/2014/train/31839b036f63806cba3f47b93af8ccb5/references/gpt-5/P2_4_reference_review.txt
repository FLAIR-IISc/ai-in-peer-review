Summary
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), it (i) generalizes the sequential factorization approach by allowing missingness indicators R to appear in ordered factorizations (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms influence each other; (ii) provides a new sufficient condition for recovering joint distributions that does not rely on an admissible sequence, by partitioning R and using Markov blankets along collider paths (Theorem 2, Corollary 1); (iii) gives necessary graphical conditions for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) shows that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x), P(x,y)) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from RY (Example 3, Theorem 4); and (v) applies the results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery of joint distributions and causal effects in the simple attrition case |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5 and 6), correcting prior claims in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013). Proofs are in the appendix.

Relation to prior work: The work is a nontrivial extension of Mohan et al. (2013) on m-graphs and recoverability, and leverages do-calculus, dormant independence/d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl), and ADMG concepts (Richardson). Unlike common MAR/MCAR-based approaches, it targets MNAR regimes with explicit mechanism modeling. The non-recoverability criteria and simple-attrition completeness results appear novel in this graphical framework.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise, grounded in causal graphical semantics, and consistent with known results. The reliance on minimal separating sets in the generalized factorization is well-motivated and illustrated (Example 1). The non-recoverability conditions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) align with the intuition that inseparability via neighbor or collider paths blocks recovery. The causal recovery despite inseparability (Sec. 6) uses do-calculus correctly by conditioning on RY and exploiting dormant independences. The simple-attrition theorems (5–6) provide if-and-only-if criteria, improving completeness in an important special case.
- Completeness: The general case remains a mix of sufficient and necessary conditions (i.e., not a complete decision procedure for arbitrary m-graphs). Section 4 mentions “general algorithms,” but the main text gives high-level conditions rather than a fully specified, implementable procedure with complexity/termination guarantees. Proofs are deferred.
- Empirics: None, which is acceptable for a theory paper, but even small synthetic demonstrations (e.g., finite-sample bias reductions vs IPW/MI under correct/incorrect m-graphs) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but mostly well-structured. Definitions (manifest vs underlying distributions, general ordered factorization) are clear, and the motivation for minimality is explicit. The roles of collider paths, partitions R(i), and Mb(R(i)) are explained with examples.
- Weaknesses: Some terms (semi-Markovian, dormant independence) are assumed; brief reminders and intuition would help. The main text lacks a succinct algorithmic description for Sec. 4’s “general algorithms.” Figures are crucial for understanding; ensure captions and references tightly match the theorems’ conditions. The references and formatting have visible glitches and typos.

Originality
- The inclusion of R in admissible factorizations, the general sufficient condition based on R-partitions/Markov blankets, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and the result that causal effects can be recoverable despite probabilistic non-recoverability (including inseparability of Y–RY) are novel and important advances over Mohan et al. (2013).
- The necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition and the correction to Garcia (2013) add value.

Significance
- Missing data is pervasive. Providing graphical sufficiency/necessity for recoverability and sharpening when causal effects are recoverable under MNAR is impactful for causal inference, statistics, and ML. The results will likely be built upon, especially the simple-attrition completeness and the “recover causal effects despite inseparability” insight.
- Practical impact hinges on the availability and correctness of m-graphs; nonetheless, these criteria offer clear guidance for method choice and feasibility checks.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advances beyond earlier m-graph work.
- New non-recoverability criteria to complement sufficiency results.
- Elegant use of do-calculus/d*-separation to recover causal effects in hard MNAR cases.
- Complete characterizations in simple attrition; correction of prior claims.
- Good illustrative examples highlighting necessity of minimality and the role of R.

Weaknesses
- Lack of a fully specified, general-purpose algorithm (with complexity/termination) in Sec. 4 in the main text; reliance on appendices.
- No empirical validation or simulation studies.
- Scope limitations: completeness primarily for |Vm|=1; for general |Vm| the results are partial (necessary/sufficient but not both).
- Sensitivity to m-graph misspecification is not addressed; no guidance on learning/validating m-graphs from data.
- Expositional issues: some notation is heavy; several formatting/reference errors.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a concise algorithmic procedure (pseudo-code) for Sec. 4 with conditions for applicability and complexity.
- Discuss robustness to m-graph misspecification and suggest diagnostics/sensitivity analysis (perhaps leveraging Mohan & Pearl 2014).
- Clarify the relationship between Theorems 2 and 3: tightness gaps and examples at the boundary.
- Add one or two synthetic experiments to illustrate finite-sample behavior and compare against IPW/MI under MAR/MNAR.
- Improve exposition: briefly define semi-Markovian models and dormant independence; fix reference formatting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contributions on a core problem; complements and extends prior NeurIPS work.
- Introduces both sufficiency and necessity results, with complete characterizations in an important special case.
- Conceptually important insight: causal recoverability can exceed probabilistic recoverability under MNAR.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited completeness in the general case and lack of implementable, general algorithm in the main text.
- No empirical illustrations.
- Exposition and presentation need polishing.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline strong). Theoretical impact outweighs the presentation and empirical limitations; suggested improvements are feasible in revision.

Confidence: High.
