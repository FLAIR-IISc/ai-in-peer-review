This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), for analyzing the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the authors' prior work (Mohan et al., 2013), this paper makes several significant new contributions. First, it generalizes the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other. Second, it provides a novel theorem for recovery in non-factorizable models based on collider paths and Markov blankets of the missingness indicators. Third, and perhaps most importantly, it introduces the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, giving researchers tools to identify when a query is impossible to estimate consistently. Finally, it demonstrates the surprising and powerful result that causal effects, `P(y|do(x))`, can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distributions, like `P(y|x)`, are not. These theoretical results are then applied to the common problem of attrition in experimental studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data from a principled, model-based perspective. While much of the machine learning literature defaults to methods assuming data is Missing at Random (MAR), this work provides a rigorous framework for the more challenging and realistic Missing Not at Random (MNAR) setting. The ability to graphically determine whether a query is recoverable *before* attempting estimation is a significant contribution to both theory and practice.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper contains several novel and insightful results.
    *   The criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) are a major conceptual advance. Providing necessary conditions for failure is as important as providing sufficient conditions for success, yet this is often overlooked in the literature.
    *   The result on the recoverability of causal queries despite the non-recoverability of their probabilistic counterparts (Section 6) is a highlight. It demonstrates a deep connection between causal identification theory (`do-calculus`, d*-separation) and missing data problems, showing that intervention can sometimes break the dependencies that make observational estimation impossible. This is a non-obvious and powerful finding.
    *   The generalization of the recovery theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) to cover more complex dependency structures among missingness mechanisms extends the applicability of the m-graph framework substantially.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The definitions are precise, the theorems are clearly stated, and the arguments leverage sophisticated concepts from the graphical models and causality literature (e.g., d-separation, collider paths, inducing paths, do-calculus). The illustrative examples (e.g., Example 1, 3) are well-chosen and effectively clarify the necessity and application of the proposed theorems. The correction to the literature on attrition (Section 7.2) further demonstrates the authors' deep command of the subject.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** While the paper is well-organized, the density of the notation and terminology (e.g., `V*`, `R`, `R'x`, `Rw`, `Rpart`, `Mb(R(i))`) may pose a significant barrier to readers not already deeply familiar with Pearl's graphical modeling framework. A small table summarizing the key notation at the beginning of Section 2 would greatly enhance readability and make the paper accessible to a broader NIPS audience.

2.  **Assumption of a Known Model:** The entire framework relies on the strong assumption that the true m-graph is known. While the authors rightly cite their work on testing m-graphs (Mohan and Pearl [2014]), the current paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the sensitivity of these results to model misspecification. For a practitioner, the key challenge is often constructing the graph in the first place. Some guidance or discussion on this process, or the robustness of the conclusions if the graph is slightly wrong, would add significant practical value.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While its theoretical contributions are strong, the claims would be more compelling with a small simulation study. For instance, data could be generated from a known m-graph (like in Figure 3), with missingness introduced according to the model. Applying the proposed recovery algorithm for `P(y|do(z))` and showing that the estimate converges to the true causal effect (while an estimate for `P(y, z)` fails to converge) would provide concrete, empirical validation of the paper's central and most surprising claim.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality, technically deep, and important paper that pushes the frontier of principled reasoning about missing data. Its key contributions—especially the non-recoverability criteria and the distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability—are novel, significant, and likely to influence future research in both causality and machine learning. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and the lack of empirical validation, which do not undermine the core theoretical results. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS, offering a rigorous theoretical framework for a problem of broad practical relevance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
