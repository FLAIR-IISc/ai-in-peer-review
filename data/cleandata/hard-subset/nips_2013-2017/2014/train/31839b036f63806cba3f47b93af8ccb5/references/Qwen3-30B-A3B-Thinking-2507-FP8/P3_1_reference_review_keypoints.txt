- Introduces Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) and extends Mohan et al. [2013] with formal graphical criteria for recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries.
- Theorems 1–2 generalize sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (e.g., longitudinal studies).
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 provide the first formal non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions.
- Theorem 4 shows causal effects can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not (e.g., inseparability between Y and Ry).
- Demonstrates a clear distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability; Example 3 shows do-calculus can bypass probabilistic non-recoverability.
- Section 7 (Theorems 5–6) gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition, correcting prior misconceptions (e.g., Garcia [2013]).
- Thorough related-work coverage, including connections to coarsened-at-random and prior missing-data and causal-inference literature.
- Weakness: purely theoretical with no empirical validation or simulation demonstrating the criteria in practice.
- Weakness: dense and potentially confusing notation (V, V*, R, R*, Rw vs Wr) that could hurt accessibility.
- Weakness: limited discussion of how the graphical criteria inform practical ML methods for missing data (e.g., imputation, IPW, collaborative filtering).
- Weakness: ambiguous definition of “recoverability” — unclear if it refers to statistical consistency, computational feasibility, or both.
- Overall assessment: significant, technically sound theoretical contribution with high impact for causal inference and missing-data research; recommendation to accept.
