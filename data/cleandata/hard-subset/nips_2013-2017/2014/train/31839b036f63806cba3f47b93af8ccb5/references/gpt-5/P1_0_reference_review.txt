Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops graphical criteria and constructive procedures for recovering probabilistic (joint/conditional) and causal queries from data with missing values using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors:
- Generalize the “sequential/ordered factorization” approach to include missingness indicators R in the factorization ordering (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., edges among R nodes).
- Provide new recovery formulas for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via a partitioning of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) that account for collider paths and latent confounding (Theorem 2 and a corollary).
- Introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X) (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), offering necessary conditions that alert when assumptions are insufficient.
- Show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when probabilistic targets (e.g., P(y,z,w)) are not and when Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism (Ry), via do-calculus and d*-separation/dormant independence (Theorem 4 and examples).
- Apply these results to attrition settings, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) when |Vm|=1.

The work sits squarely within the causal/missing data literature (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin; Lauritzen; Robins & Rotnitzky; van der Laan & Robins) and extends recent graphical approaches (Mohan et al., 2013; Mohan & Pearl, 2014; Shpitser & Pearl, 2006/2008; Richardson, 2003). Notably, it provides the first explicit non-recoverability criteria for certain queries and clarifies when causal effects can be salvaged in MNAR regimes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The claims appear largely well-grounded. The extension of ordered factorization to include R and the admissibility conditions are plausible and illustrated. The partition/Markov blanket construction in Theorem 2 is a clear generalization beyond earlier sufficient conditions, and the non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) align with intuition about separability from missingness mechanisms. The use of do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4) to recover P(y|do(x)) under inseparability is a strong conceptual contribution.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the appendix; main text provides intuition and examples but could better guide a reader through verifying theorems algorithmically. The “general algorithms” mentioned in Section 4 are not fully spelled out as procedures with complexity guarantees or a step-by-step method (e.g., how to construct R partitions and Mb sets automatically).
- Empirics: No empirical demonstrations, simulations, or case studies are provided. While this is a theory paper, a small suite of synthetic examples demonstrating finite-sample estimation and robustness (e.g., sensitivity to positivity violations, model misspecification) would strengthen practical relevance.

Clarity
- Organization is reasonable and the narrative is coherent (from definitions to recovery to non-recovery to causal queries and attrition). However, notation and presentation need careful revision:
  - In Section 2, “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively.” uses the same symbol twice; please distinguish (e.g., G\X-out vs G\X-in).
  - The shorthand R′x for Rx=0 is introduced, but then “Ry denotes Ry=1” (without prime); this asymmetry is confusing. Use a consistent convention for 0/1 across all R variables.
  - Definition 2 “minimal set” is crucial; emphasize it more and contrast with chain-rule factorization (as you do in Example 1), perhaps with a short lemma on uniqueness/non-uniqueness and its implications.
  - The condition (2) in Theorem 1 is difficult to parse (“Z /∈ Xi and Xr∩RXm = ∅ and Rz⊥⊥RXi |Xi if Yi = Rz…”). Please rewrite with clearer quantification and notation.
  - The assumption “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” should be stated unambiguously (do you mean “are not allowed to be parents”?), and its implications for model class discussed.
- More diagrams (beyond references to Figures 2–4) and a worked example walking through Theorems 2–3 on the same graph would help.

Originality
- Extending ordered factorizations to include R and providing non-recoverability criteria are novel and important. The generalization (Theorem 2) beyond parent-subset conditions in Mohan et al. (2013) is substantive. The causal recovery under inseparability via d*-separation is an insightful addition that nuances the common belief that inseparability blocks everything. The necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition are crisp and correct a misconception in prior work (Garcia, 2013).

Significance
- Missing data under MNAR and interacting mechanisms is pervasive. Clear graphical conditions and constructive estimands will be valuable to both causal inference and ML communities (e.g., longitudinal studies, recommender systems with non-random missingness). Providing non-recoverability criteria is especially useful in practice. The lack of automated procedures and empirical validation slightly limits immediate impact, but the theoretical advances are meaningful.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide an explicit algorithm: given an m-graph, how to (a) search for an admissible sequence; (b) if none, construct R partitions and Mb sets and test Theorem 2 conditions; (c) either produce a symbolic estimand in terms of observable quantities or certify non-recoverability. Complexity and completeness guarantees would be ideal.
- For Theorem 4, clarify the roles of Z, W, W1 and provide a checklist for verifying d*-separation conditions. Consider including a second example with latent confounding and multiple W variables.
- Discuss positivity requirements more thoroughly and when “extensions to non-strict positivity” are possible; give a concrete example.
- Comment on generalization beyond binary R, and to coarsening beyond missingness (CAR vs MAR/MNAR).
- Improve reference formatting and fix corrupted entries.

Pros
- Strong theoretical advances beyond prior NIPS work (Mohan et al., 2013).
- First explicit non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions in m-graphs.
- Novel and practically important insight: causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparable Y–Ry.
- Clear implications for attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Cons
- Notation/presentation issues hinder accessibility.
- No algorithmic pipeline or complexity analysis; proofs are all in appendix.
- No empirical illustrations or synthetic experiments to demonstrate applicability and estimator behavior.
- Some conditions (Theorem 4) are hard to verify in practice without tooling.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper advances the theory of recoverability under MNAR with interacting mechanisms and contributes valuable non-recoverability criteria and causal recovery results. With improved clarity and a more operational treatment (algorithmic steps, tooling, and illustrative experiments), it would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. Confidence: medium-high.
