

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper introduces a novel framework for handling missing data using "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), extending the work of Mohan et al. (2013) to provide comprehensive conditions for recovering both probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data. The authors present several significant theoretical contributions that substantially advance the field of missing data analysis.

The paper's most compelling novelty lies in demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations (such as P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not recoverable. This challenges conventional wisdom that causal effects require recoverable probabilistic relations and represents a bold theoretical insight. The authors establish this through Theorem 4 and Example 3, showing that causal queries may circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic recoverability.

The paper systematically develops a theoretical framework with several key contributions: (1) a refined sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) applicable to cases where missingness mechanisms interact; (2) a general recovery algorithm for cases where sequential factorization fails (Theorem 2); (3) new non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); and (4) specific conditions for recovering causal effects under attrition (Theorem 6). These results provide a unified graphical approach to missing data problems that extends beyond the MCAR and MAR assumptions prevalent in current literature.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and clearly distinguish it from previous work. The authors correctly note that while previous work (e.g., Garcia [2013]) has analyzed attrition problems, they failed to consider interactions among missingness mechanisms. The paper's correction of Garcia's results (in Section 7.2) demonstrates the practical significance of their theoretical advances.

The paper's organization is logical, with clear examples illustrating key concepts (e.g., Example 1 and Example 3). The mathematical presentation is rigorous and builds coherently on the authors' previous work, though some of the notation may require careful study. The paper's focus on theoretical conditions for recoverability rather than empirical validation is appropriate for its contribution, and the authors properly acknowledge that their framework provides necessary conditions for recoverability rather than a complete algorithm for implementation.

Strengths:
- The key insight about causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic queries are not is highly novel and significant
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework extending previous work on missing data
- The graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) fill an important gap in the literature
- The application to attrition problems demonstrates practical relevance
- The paper clearly articulates how its approach differs from prior work

Weaknesses:
- The paper lacks empirical validation or case studies to demonstrate the practical utility of the framework
- The implementation details for the recovery algorithms are not discussed
- Some readers may find the heavy reliance on graphical criteria challenging without more intuitive explanations

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and theoretical boldness, I find this paper exceptionally strong. The central contribution—that causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic relations cannot—represents a paradigm shift in missing data analysis. This insight alone would be sufficient for a high-impact contribution, and the paper develops this idea with mathematical rigor and comprehensive theoretical support.

While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness for a NIPS paper, I believe the theoretical significance of the contribution outweighs this limitation. The paper makes a substantial theoretical advance that will likely influence future research in causal inference and missing data analysis, and it clearly articulates how it extends prior work.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on missing data (e.g., Marlin et al. [2011]), this paper provides a fundamentally different theoretical foundation rather than incremental improvements to existing methods. The paper's focus on graphical conditions for recoverability represents a significant departure from the more algorithmic approaches prevalent in the literature.

In conclusion, this paper makes a bold, novel contribution to a fundamental problem in statistical inference. The theoretical insights presented are significant, well-motivated, and extend the field in meaningful ways. While empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical novelty and depth of the contribution justify acceptance at NIPS. The paper clearly demonstrates how missing data mechanisms can be handled through graphical models in ways that previous approaches could not, with implications for fields from epidemiology to social science.

I recommend acceptance with confidence. This paper represents a substantial advance in the theoretical understanding of missing data problems and will likely influence future research in causal inference and statistical modeling.
