## Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the existing framework for handling missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It builds upon prior work by Mohan et al. and Pearl, providing more general conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries. The theoretical contributions appear solid and the application to attrition scenarios is relevant. However, the paper is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and more illustrative examples, particularly when introducing new theorems and definitions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of drawing valid inferences from datasets with missing data. It leverages the concept of m-graphs, introduced by Mohan et al. (2013), to explicitly model the missingness process. The authors extend the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex missingness mechanisms, including cases where missingness mechanisms interact.  A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries, P(y|do(x)), can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. This is a significant advancement, as it addresses scenarios where standard MAR/MCAR assumptions fail.  The authors connect their work to related areas like coarsened data, inverse probability weighting, and multiple imputation, but primarily frame it within the causal inference literature pioneered by Pearl.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a strong theoretical foundation, with clearly stated theorems and proofs (in the appendix). The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem and the introduction of new non-recoverability criteria are mathematically sound.
* **Causal Inference Focus:** The ability to recover causal effects even when probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable is a novel and highly valuable contribution. This directly addresses a limitation of many existing missing data methods.
* **Attrition Application:** Applying the results to the common problem of attrition in longitudinal studies demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough overview of relevant literature in missing data, causal inference, and graphical models.
* **Formalization of Non-Recoverability:**  The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a helpful addition, allowing practitioners to assess whether their assumptions are sufficient for valid inference.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:**  The paper is quite dense and requires a strong background in graphical models and causal inference to fully understand. The notation is heavy, and many concepts are introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation. More illustrative examples (beyond the ones provided) would significantly improve comprehension.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks any empirical validation. Demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on real or simulated datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:**  The paper mentions algorithms but doesn’t address their computational complexity or scalability. For large datasets, the proposed methods may be computationally expensive.
* **Theorem Overload:** The numerous theorems and corollaries, while theoretically sound, can be overwhelming.  Prioritization and consolidation might improve readability.
* **Connection to Existing Imputation/Weighting Methods:** While the paper mentions related methods, it doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed approach complements or differs from them in practice. A more explicit comparison would be beneficial.




**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical results appear robust. The proofs, while not read in detail, seem rigorous.  However, the lack of empirical validation lowers the score.
* **Clarity (5/10):** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity, especially for readers less familiar with the intricacies of m-graphs and causal inference. The notation is dense and explanations are often terse.
* **Originality (8/10):** The extension of sequential factorization and the demonstration of causal query recoverability are novel contributions. The work builds upon existing foundations but pushes the boundaries of the field.
* **Significance (9/10):**  The ability to recover causal effects under challenging missing data scenarios is highly significant.  This work has the potential to impact research in various fields, including epidemiology, economics, and social sciences.


**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a critical problem in scientific inference: handling missing data.
* Provides novel theoretical results for recovering causal effects.
* Builds upon a well-established framework (m-graphs).
* Has the potential to impact research in multiple disciplines.

**Con:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
*  Dense and difficult to read.
*  Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*  Doesn't clearly articulate practical implications and comparisons to existing methods.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for publication, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding clarity and empirical validation. Specifically, they should:

* **Provide more illustrative examples:**  Expand on the examples provided to better explain the concepts and theorems.
* **Include empirical validation:** Conduct experiments on real or simulated data to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Discuss computational complexity:** Provide an assessment of the computational cost of the algorithms.
* **Clarify the connection to existing methods:**  Explicitly compare the proposed approach to common imputation and weighting techniques.
* **Streamline the presentation:** Consider consolidating some of the theorems and corollaries to improve readability.



This paper represents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. Addressing the suggested revisions would significantly enhance its impact and accessibility.
