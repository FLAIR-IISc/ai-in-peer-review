### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the graphical framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), introduced by Mohan et al. [2013], to analyze the recoverability of statistical queries from datasets with missing values. The core idea is to represent the dependencies between substantive variables and the mechanisms causing data to be missing within a single causal graph. The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They generalize the "sequential factorization" theorem from their previous work to a broader class of problems, including those where missingness mechanisms can influence one another (Theorem 1).
2.  They provide a new graphical condition and recovery formula for the joint distribution in cases where sequential factorization is not possible (Theorem 2).
3.  They introduce novel and highly useful graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), establishing clear conditions under which consistent estimation is impossible given the model assumptions.
4.  They demonstrate that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, connecting the problem to the do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4).
5.  They apply these results to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting what they claim are misconceptions in existing literature (Theorems 5 & 6).

This work builds directly on the foundational ideas of m-graphs (Mohan et al., 2013) and integrates them with the broader literature on causal inference (Pearl, 2009; Shpitser & Pearl, 2008) to provide a more complete and powerful theory for reasoning about missing data, particularly in the challenging Missing Not At Random (MNAR) setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles the fundamental problem of missing data with a principled, assumption-driven approach. The most significant contributions are the establishment of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Section 5). In a field often focused on sufficient conditions for success, providing necessary conditions for failure is equally, if not more, important for practitioners. These criteria allow researchers to assess a priori whether their assumptions permit a valid analysis.

2.  **Important Conceptual Insight on Causal Queries:** The demonstration in Section 6 that causal effects can be recoverable even when the corresponding conditional probabilities are not is a powerful and non-obvious result. It elegantly illustrates that the information required for causal inference is different from that required for purely probabilistic inference and provides a clear graphical characterization of when this is possible. This is a deep connection between the fields of missing data and causal identification.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Generality:** The paper appears technically sound, with clear theorem statements and proofs provided in the appendix. The work systematically generalizes the results of Mohan et al. [2013], expanding the applicability of the m-graph framework to models with more complex dependencies, such as interacting missingness mechanisms (a common scenario in longitudinal studies).

4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors use illustrative examples and figures effectively to explain complex theoretical concepts (e.g., Example 1, Figure 2, Figure 3), which greatly aids reader comprehension. The application to attrition in Section 7 grounds the abstract theory in a concrete and widely recognized problem.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a contribution of this nature, its impact would be substantially increased by including simulation studies. For example, generating data from a known m-graph where a query is non-recoverable by Theorem 3, and showing that standard methods (e.g., listwise deletion, mean imputation) and even more sophisticated ones (e.g., a misspecified ML model) produce biased estimates would provide compelling evidence for the practical relevance of the theory. Conversely, demonstrating that the proposed recovery formulas yield consistent estimates in recoverable cases would be a powerful validation.

2.  **Practicality of Specifying the M-Graph:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that a correct m-graph is specified by the user. This is a very strong assumption, and in practice, constructing such a graph is a major challenge. While the authors rightly cite their related work on testing m-graphs [Mohan and Pearl, 2014], a brief discussion in the main paper regarding the sensitivity of the proposed methods to model misspecification would strengthen the work. For instance, are the recoverability results brittle, or do they degrade gracefully if a minor edge in the m-graph is wrong?

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   In Section 4, the definitions for the partitions `R(part)` and the Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))` are given *after* the statement of Theorem 2. The paper would be easier to follow if these crucial concepts were defined *before* the theorem that relies on them.
    *   The paper claims to rectify prevailing opinions in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]). While the counterexample in Figure 4(a) is clear, elaborating slightly more on why the previous condition (X being an ancestor of Ry) was considered necessary would help the reader better appreciate the correction being made.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides novel, sound, and significant theoretical results for a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics.
*   The non-recoverability criteria are a major contribution that can prevent researchers from drawing invalid conclusions from incomplete data.
*   The connection between causal recoverability and probabilistic non-recoverability is an important conceptual advance.
*   The paper is well-written and generalizes prior work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks any experimental validation, which limits the assessment of its practical utility and the performance of the proposed recovery algorithms.
*   The strong reliance on a correctly specified model could be seen as a practical limitation, and this aspect is not discussed in depth.

### Final Recommendation

I recommend **Accept**.

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes several novel and significant contributions to the formal understanding of missing data. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability are important advances that are likely to be influential. While the lack of experiments is a clear weakness, the strength and importance of the theoretical results are sufficient for a NIPS paper. The suggestions for improvement, particularly regarding the addition of a discussion on model sensitivity, could be incorporated into the final version to further strengthen the paper.
