### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, building upon the authors' previous work on "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) (Mohan et al., 2013), to determine the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The core problem—handling missing data—is fundamental and pervasive in machine learning and statistics, with a rich history dating back to Rubin's classification of missingness mechanisms (MCAR, MAR, MNAR). While most work in ML assumes the simpler MAR case, this paper provides a principled way to reason about the more complex MNAR (Missing Not At Random) setting.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Generalization of Sequential Factorization (Thm. 1):** The paper extends the factorization method from Mohan et al. (2013) to a broader class of problems, notably those where missingness mechanisms (R-variables) can influence each other, which is common in longitudinal studies.
2.  **Recovery Beyond Factorization (Thm. 2):** It provides a novel sufficient condition for recovering the full joint distribution for semi-markovian models where no admissible factorization sequence exists. This is achieved by partitioning the R-variables and analyzing their Markov blankets.
3.  **Formal Non-Recoverability Criteria (Thm. 3, Cor. 2):** A key contribution is the introduction of the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*. These criteria (based on adjacency or specific collider paths between a variable and its missingness indicator) provide necessary conditions, allowing researchers to quickly identify when a query is impossible to recover under the given model assumptions.
4.  **Recoverability of Causal Queries (Sec. 6):** The paper makes the crucial and insightful point that a causal query, P(y|do(x)), may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic query, P(y,x), is not. This is demonstrated via do-calculus and linked to the concept of d*-separation.
5.  **Application to Attrition (Sec. 7):** The theoretical results are applied to the practical problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects in this setting, and in doing so, correcting claims from prior literature (Garcia, 2013).

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance. Moving beyond MAR assumptions to provide a formal language for reasoning about MNAR scenarios is a significant step forward. The results have the potential to impact how researchers in many fields handle and reason about missing data.
2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a major conceptual advance. It complements existing work on sufficient conditions by providing clear "impossibility" results. The finding that causal queries can bypass the recoverability requirements of their probabilistic counterparts is a deep and non-obvious insight that connects the fields of missing data and causal inference in a novel way.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is theoretically dense and presents a set of rigorous, general results. The theorems build logically upon each other and extend prior work in a clear, well-defined manner. The formalization through m-graphs provides a powerful and expressive language for a complex problem. The application to attrition demonstrates the practical utility of the framework.
4.  **Clarity of Examples:** The use of small, illustrative graphical models (e.g., Fig 2, Fig 3) is very effective in communicating the intuition behind the formal theorems. Example 3, in particular, clearly demonstrates the surprising result about the recoverability of causal effects.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is extremely dense, which may limit its accessibility to a broader NIPS audience. The definitions for Theorem 2 (collider path, R(part), Mb(R(i))) are provided *after* the theorem is stated. This forces the reader to jump back and forth and makes a complex theorem even harder to parse on a first read. Moving these definitions before the theorem would significantly improve readability.
2.  **Lack of Experimental Validation:** While this is a heavily theoretical paper, its claims have direct empirical consequences. The paper would be strengthened considerably by including a simulation study. For instance, the authors could simulate data from a known m-graph (e.g., Fig. 3), introduce missingness according to the model, and then demonstrate that the proposed estimator for P(y|do(z)) indeed recovers the true value, while an attempt to estimate P(w,z,y) fails, as predicted by the theory. This would provide a concrete validation of the theoretical claims.
3.  **Practical Guidance on Estimation:** The paper focuses on establishing *identifiability* (which it calls recoverability), showing that a query can be expressed in terms of the observed data distribution. However, it offers little guidance on the practical aspects of estimation. While it's clear that one can plug in estimates for terms like P(Y*|X*,Z*,R'=0), a brief discussion of the statistical properties of the resulting estimators (e.g., challenges in estimation, potential for high variance) would make the work more complete.
4.  **Assumptions:** The framework relies on the correctness of the user-specified m-graph. While the authors cite their related work on testing these assumptions (Mohan and Pearl, 2014), the current paper would benefit from a short discussion in the main text about the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification, as this is a major practical concern.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**For acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and highly significant problem in machine learning.
*   Provides novel and important theoretical contributions, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the separation of causal vs. probabilistic recoverability.
*   The results are general and provide a principled framework for reasoning about MNAR data, advancing the state of the art.
*   Connects two important areas of research: graphical models for missing data and causal inference.

**Against acceptance:**
*   The paper is very dense and may be difficult for non-specialists to appreciate.
*   The lack of any experimental validation, even a simple simulation, makes the practical implications of the theoretical results less tangible.
*   The presentation could be improved for better readability (e.g., placement of definitions).

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality, technically strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the important problem of inference from missing data. Its key results, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the insights into causal queries, are novel and important. While the dense presentation and lack of experiments are weaknesses, they do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is likely to be influential for researchers working on missing data and causality. I recommend acceptance. Minor revisions to improve clarity, especially the organization around Theorem 2, would be highly beneficial.
