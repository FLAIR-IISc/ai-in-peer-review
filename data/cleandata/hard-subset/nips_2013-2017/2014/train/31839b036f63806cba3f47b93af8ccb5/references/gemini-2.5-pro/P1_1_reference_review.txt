### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the graphical framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], to develop new criteria for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The m-graph provides a formal language to represent assumptions about the data-generating process and the missingness mechanisms. The core contribution is a set of novel graphical conditions and algorithms for determining "recoverability"—the ability to obtain a consistent estimate for a query from the observed, incomplete data.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Generalized Sequential Factorization (Theorem 1):** The paper extends a previous theorem to allow for dependencies among the missingness mechanisms themselves (R-variables), a scenario common in longitudinal studies but unaddressed by the previous formulation.
2.  **Recovery without Admissible Sequence (Theorem 2):** It provides a new, powerful condition for recovering the full joint distribution P(V) in cases where sequential factorization fails. This method relies on partitioning the missingness mechanisms and using their Markov blankets.
3.  **Non-recoverability Criteria (Theorem 3 & Corollary 2):** The paper introduces the first formal graphical criteria for proving *non-recoverability*. These conditions, based on paths between a variable and its missingness indicator, are crucial for practitioners to know when their assumptions are insufficient.
4.  **Causal Query Recovery (Theorem 4):** A key and surprising result is that a causal effect, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic query, P(y,x), is not. This is demonstrated for cases where a variable Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry.
5.  **Application to Attrition (Theorems 5 & 6):** The theoretical results are applied to the common problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting a claim in the existing literature (Garcia [2013]).

This work positions itself as a significant theoretical advancement within the causal inference and graphical models literature on missing data, moving beyond the standard MAR/MCAR assumptions to tackle the more challenging MNAR (Missing Not At Random) case.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes several important and novel contributions. The most significant is the demonstration that causal queries can be recovered under conditions where probabilistic queries cannot (Section 6). This is a non-obvious and powerful result that could have a substantial impact on how researchers in experimental sciences handle missing data. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Section 5) is also a highly valuable and original contribution, providing a practical tool for model assessment.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The theorems are clearly stated, and the progression from extending existing results to introducing new ones is logical. The connections made to established concepts like d*-separation and inducing paths root the new theory in solid foundations. The application to attrition, including the correction of a prior result, showcases a rigorous engagement with the problem domain.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of small, illustrative graphical examples throughout the text (e.g., Figure 2, Figure 3) is extremely effective at building intuition for the abstract theorems. The problem setup and definitions in Section 2 are clear, building directly upon prior, cited work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a NIPS contribution of this nature, its impact would be significantly broadened by including simulation studies. Simulations could demonstrate the finite-sample performance of the proposed recovery algorithms and their robustness to near-violations of the graphical assumptions. For instance, how much bias is introduced if a weak but non-zero edge in the true m-graph is omitted?
*   **Practical Assumption of a Known Graph:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the user can specify the correct m-graph. This is a very strong assumption in practice. While the authors cite their related work on testing m-graphs (Mohan and Pearl [2014]), a brief discussion in the main paper about the challenges of model specification and the sensitivity of the results to misspecification would make the work more complete and grounded for a practitioner.
*   **Density of New Concepts:** The concepts introduced in Section 4 (collider path, R(part), Mb(R(i))) are defined very concisely. The definition of the Markov Blanket `Mb(R(i))` as including substantive variables connected by a collider path is non-standard and could benefit from a more detailed explanation or a step-by-step example of its construction for the graph in Figure 2(b). A reader unfamiliar with these specific graphical concepts might find this section difficult to parse.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance, making several novel theoretical contributions to an important and difficult problem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance:** The paper tackles the challenging MNAR setting, providing principled, graphical tools. The result that causal effects can be identified even when probabilistic associations are not is a major conceptual advance.
2.  **Novelty:** The paper introduces several new graphical criteria, most notably for *non*-recoverability, which has been lacking in the literature.
3.  **Rigorous and Complete:** The work is presented as a complete theoretical piece, extending prior work, introducing new results, and applying them to a practical problem (attrition), even correcting a prior published result.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope (Theoretical):** The absence of any experiments—simulated or real—may limit its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. The performance of the derived estimators in practice remains an open question.
2.  **Strong Underlying Assumptions:** The reliance on a perfectly specified m-graph is a major practical hurdle that is not sufficiently discussed.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   In Section 4, a small subsection or a more detailed walkthrough in the appendix showing how to derive the partitions R(part) and the Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) for Figure 2(b) would greatly improve clarity.
*   While adding a full experimental section may be out of scope for a revision, adding a paragraph in the conclusion or discussion section on the practical challenges of m-graph specification and potential avenues for future work on robust recovery methods would strengthen the paper.
*   The claim in Section 7 that the results "rectify prevailing opinion" is strong. While justified by the counterexample to Garcia [2013], it's worth double-checking that Garcia's result was not stated under more constrained assumptions that are violated in Figure 4(a). (Assuming the authors' reading is correct, this is a strength).

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical paper that pushes the boundary of what is possible in missing data analysis using graphical models. Its strengths in originality and significance far outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
