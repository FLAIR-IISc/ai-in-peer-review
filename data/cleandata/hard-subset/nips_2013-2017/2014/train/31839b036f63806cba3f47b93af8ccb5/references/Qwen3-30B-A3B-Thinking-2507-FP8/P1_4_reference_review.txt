

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) by introducing Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to formally represent missing data mechanisms and provide graphical conditions for recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The authors establish sufficient conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions, demonstrate that causal effects P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable, and apply their results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies. The paper builds upon Pearl's causal inference framework and Mohan's prior work on missingness graphs, making a significant theoretical contribution to the handling of missing data.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix). The authors carefully build upon and extend Mohan et al. (2013), addressing limitations in the prior work by introducing the concept of "general ordered factorization" (Theorem 1) and providing new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3). The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly well-developed, with Example 3 providing a compelling demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not. The paper is complete in its theoretical presentation and adequately addresses the limitations of previous work.

**Originality**: The paper makes several novel contributions. The key insight that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not (Theorem 4) represents a significant theoretical advance over previous work. The new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) fill an important gap in the literature. The application to attrition problems (Theorems 5-6) corrects misconceptions in existing literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013), demonstrating how the authors' framework can rectify previous errors. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in Section 8, particularly in how it handles interactions among missingness mechanisms.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise definitions and consistent notation. The examples (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts. The logical flow from probabilistic to causal query recovery is well-structured. The graphical examples (Figures 1-4) help visualize the concepts. The appendix provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

**Significance**: Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, making this work highly significant. The authors provide practical graphical criteria that researchers can use to determine recoverability of queries in their specific missing data problems. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability has important implications for causal inference studies, particularly in fields like epidemiology and social sciences where missing data is common. The paper's application to attrition problems directly addresses a major practical challenge in longitudinal studies.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity**: While generally clear, Theorem 4 and its associated definition of d*-separation may be challenging for readers without deep familiarity with do-calculus. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not.

**Quality**: The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical results without empirical validation. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of experimental results or real-world case studies limits the practical demonstration of the framework's utility.

**Significance**: The paper's primary contribution is theoretical, which is appropriate for NIPS, but the authors could better articulate how their results would change existing practice. While they reference applications to attrition, they don't provide concrete examples of how researchers would use their graphical criteria in real studies.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of missing data mechanisms, particularly regarding the distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. While the absence of empirical validation is a minor limitation for a NIPS submission, the theoretical novelty and significance of the results outweigh this concern.

The paper clearly meets NIPS's criteria for quality, originality, and significance. It makes a substantial theoretical contribution that will inform future work in missing data and causal inference. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly novel and valuable.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**:
- Novel theoretical insight about causal query recoverability
- Rigorous extension of Mohan et al. (2013) framework
- Clear graphical criteria with practical applications
- Corrects misconceptions in existing literature (Garcia, 2013)
- Well-structured and clearly written theoretical contribution

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
- Limited empirical validation (though not expected for theoretical contribution)
- Some technical concepts may be challenging for readers without causal inference background

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the field of missing data analysis. It would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers working at the intersection of causal inference and missing data. The paper demonstrates clear understanding of the field's prior work while making significant theoretical advances.

**Recommendation**: Accept
