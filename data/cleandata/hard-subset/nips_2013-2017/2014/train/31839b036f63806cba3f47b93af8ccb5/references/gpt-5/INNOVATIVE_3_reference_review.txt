Summary
This paper advances graphical methods for reasoning about missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors: (i) generalize the ordered factorization approach by allowing R-nodes (missingness indicators) in the factorization order, yielding an extended sequential factorization theorem applicable when missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide constructive recoverability procedures for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via partitioning the R-variables and using Markov blanket–based factors; (iii) introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) show that causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when key probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) are not, including when Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism, using do-calculus and d*-separation; and (v) give necessary and sufficient conditions for both joint and causal recoverability in the “simple attrition” case (|Vm|=1). Proofs are deferred to the appendix, and examples illustrate how to construct estimands from manifest data.

Relation to prior work
The paper extends Mohan, Pearl, Tian (NIPS 2013) by (a) expanding sequential factorization to include R and by (b) covering settings without admissible sequences. It interfaces with identification theory (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; d*-separation, inducing paths) and clarifies the relation to MAR/MCAR regimes, CAR (Heitjan & Rubin), and IPW/MI practices. The ADMG perspective (Richardson 2003) underlies several constructions (Markov blankets, collider/inducing paths). The attrition analysis updates and corrects claims from Garcia (2013). To my knowledge, the new non-recoverability criteria and the result that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparable Y–Ry are not previously formalized in this graphical form.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual significance: The separation between probabilistic recoverability and causal recoverability is crisply articulated and operationalized with constructive estimands. This is a bold and important message for practice.
- Extended sequential factorization including R is an elegant generalization and directly addresses interacting missingness mechanisms, a case underexplored in prior works that largely assume MAR or independent mechanisms.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 offer practical recipes for P(V) when admissible sequences fail, using R-partitions and Markov blankets; the estimands are explicit.
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable to guard against overclaiming in MNAR settings.
- Simple attrition: clean necessary and sufficient conditions for both P(V) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorems 5–6), with a constructive estimand that exposes when naive complete-case/MAR approaches fail.
- The examples show how to combine do-calculus with observed proxies V* and selection by setting R′ to construct recoverable estimands.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity and presentation: The notation is dense, and several key ideas (e.g., minimality in Definition 2, construction of partitions R(i), and Mb(R(i))) would benefit from algorithmic pseudocode and a worked example with explicit algebra on a small graph. As presented, recoverability “algorithms” are not fully algorithmic.
- Scope of Theorem 2: The precondition (“no partially observed variable is a neighbor of or collider-connected to its own R”) is strong. Please clarify whether this is only a sufficient condition (it reads as such) and discuss tightness or counterexamples near the boundary.
- Causal recoverability beyond |Vm|=1: Theorem 4 handles a single partially observed variable with inducing paths. It would help to delineate what is known/unknown for multiple partially observed outcomes/treatments, and whether your conditions extend via decomposition or ID with proxy variables.
- Automating do-calculus usage: The paper uses Rules 2–3 and d*-separation ad hoc. Can these be embedded in a decision procedure that takes an m-graph and returns a causal estimand, akin to ID? Even a complexity or decidability discussion would strengthen the contribution.
- Assumptions and testability: You assume positivity over complete cases and that R is not a parent of V∪U. Please discuss how violations (e.g., structural missingness, measurement-side effects of the observation process) affect your results, and how the tests in Mohan & Pearl (2014) interplay with your new criteria.
- Empirical illustration: Even minimal simulations demonstrating finite-sample behavior of the proposed estimands (bias/variance vs MI/IPW under MNAR) would make the results more accessible to practitioners.
- Related work: Consider discussing connections to selection models/pattern-mixture models and to recent recommender-system MNAR identification (e.g., implicit feedback) beyond the cited Marlin lineage.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and well-grounded in causal identification theory. The proofs are mentioned but not visible here; the algebra in examples is consistent with do-calculus and proxy-variable semantics. Some results are sufficient but not characterized as tight; acknowledging limitations is appropriate. Turning the conditions into an implementable procedure would improve completeness.

Clarity
Good high-level organization, but readability suffers from heavy notation and reliance on figures for intuition. More explicit algorithms, examples with numeric toy data, and a running example would help. A proofreading pass is needed (typos, reference formatting).

Originality
High. The extension to interacting missingness mechanisms via R-inclusive factorization, the graphical non-recoverability criteria, and the constructive demonstration that causal effects can be recovered when probabilistic queries cannot are, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial.

Significance
High. Missing data under MNAR is pervasive, and giving users constructive, testable graphical conditions (including negative results) is impactful. The attrition results are practically important and correct misconceptions in the literature. I expect these ideas to be used and extended (e.g., incorporated into toolkits for recoverability/identification).

Pros
- Conceptually bold separation of probabilistic vs causal recoverability with constructive estimands.
- New general conditions and algorithms for P(V) in interacting-missingness settings.
- Useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Clear, necessary-and-sufficient results in simple attrition.

Cons
- Limited algorithmic detail and no empirical validation.
- Some conditions are sufficient but seemingly conservative; tightness not fully explored.
- Clarity/notation could be improved; references have formatting issues.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper introduces substantial, novel theory with clear practical implications, even if some components (automation/experiments) are incomplete. The bold advancement outweighs the minor shortcomings.

Confidence: High
