**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called m-graphs, for reasoning about the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building directly upon the authors' previous work (Mohan et al., 2013), this paper makes several new theoretical contributions. First, it generalizes a sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions to a broader class of problems, particularly those where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, it provides a novel recovery algorithm for cases where the sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness variables. Third, and perhaps most importantly, it introduces the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, identifying conditions under which a joint or conditional distribution cannot be consistently estimated. Fourth, it demonstrates a key separation between probabilistic and causal recoverability, showing that a causal effect like P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint distribution P(y, x) is not. Finally, the authors apply these results to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, deriving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting what they claim are misconceptions in the existing literature.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are presented as formal theorems, and the authors state that proofs are provided in the appendix. The work is a solid, incremental-yet-significant contribution that builds logically on established frameworks in graphical models and causality (e.g., do-calculus, d-separation). The arguments are precise and well-defined. The authors are careful to connect their new concepts (like d*-separation in Theorem 4) to existing literature, demonstrating a strong command of the field.

A potential weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While this is a theoretical paper, even a small-scale simulation study would have greatly strengthened the claims. For instance, demonstrating the recovery algorithm for the causal effect in Example 3 on synthetic data would provide a powerful illustration of the paper's most novel result: that the algorithm successfully recovers the true causal effect from a dataset where standard conditional probability estimation would fail. The paper also doesn't discuss the sensitivity of its results to potential misspecification of the m-graph, which is a critical practical concern.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and structured, but it is extremely dense. The introduction clearly lays out the paper's contributions and organization. The use of small, illustrative examples (e.g., Example 1 and 3) is very effective in making complex theoretical points understandable.

However, the clarity could be improved in a few areas. For instance, in Section 4, the core concepts of "collider path," "R(part)," and "Mb(R(i))" are defined *after* being used in the statement of Theorem 2. Reversing this order would improve readability. The notation can be heavy at times, which is perhaps unavoidable for a topic of this nature, but the paper does a reasonable job of managing it. The definitions are sufficient for an expert reader to understand the results.

**Originality:**
The paper makes several original contributions. While it extends the authors' own prior work, the new results are non-trivial.
1.  **Non-recoverability Criteria (Theorem 3 & Corollary 2):** This is a highly original and valuable contribution. Much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for identification or recovery. Providing necessary conditions (i.e., criteria for failure) is equally important for practitioners, as it tells them when their assumptions are fundamentally insufficient.
2.  **Separation of Causal and Probabilistic Recoverability (Section 6):** The result that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when P(y|x) is not is a deep and insightful finding. It highlights that causal queries can sometimes be less demanding in terms of data requirements than purely associational ones, a counter-intuitive and significant point.
3.  **Application to Attrition (Section 7):** The derivation of necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery under simple attrition (Theorems 5 and 6) and the correction of a claim in prior work (Garcia [2013]) represent a concrete advancement in a specific, important application area.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant for the NIPS community and beyond. Missing data is a ubiquitous problem in machine learning and all empirical sciences. This paper provides a principled, model-based approach to understanding and dealing with it, moving beyond common but often restrictive assumptions like MAR (Missing At Random). The key contributions have the potential for high impact:
*   The non-recoverability criteria can prevent researchers from attempting to estimate queries that are impossible to recover under their stated assumptions.
*   The results on causal recovery open up new possibilities for causal inference in settings previously thought to be intractable due to missing data in the outcome or confounding variables.
*   The formal language of m-graphs provides a clear tool for researchers to encode and communicate their assumptions about the missingness process, which is a major step forward from the often vague descriptions of MCAR, MAR, and MNAR.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** The paper introduces several novel and important theoretical results, including graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the key finding that causal effects can be recovered even when joint distributions are not.
2.  **High Significance:** It addresses the fundamental and widespread problem of missing data with formal rigor, providing tools that are valuable for both theorists and methodologically-minded practitioners in machine learning and statistics.
3.  **Advances the State of the Art:** The paper clearly advances the formal understanding of missing data, builds upon recent developments in graphical models, and corrects misconceptions in the applied literature on attrition.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** Despite its density, the paper uses well-chosen examples to make its core technical ideas accessible.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. The absence of any experiments on synthetic or real-world data makes it harder to gauge the practical applicability and robustness of the proposed methods, especially in finite-sample regimes.
2.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The framework relies on the user specifying a correct m-graph, which may be a strong assumption in practice. The paper does not address the challenges of model specification or the consequences of misspecification.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong, well-executed theoretical paper that makes several significant and original contributions to our understanding of inference from missing data. The weaknesses—primarily the lack of experiments and discussion of model sensitivity—are common for theoretical papers of this type and do not undermine the value of the core results. The strengths, particularly the novel non-recoverability criteria and the insights into causal inference, are compelling. The paper is a solid fit for NIPS and is likely to be influential in the subfields of causality and graphical models. I recommend acceptance.
