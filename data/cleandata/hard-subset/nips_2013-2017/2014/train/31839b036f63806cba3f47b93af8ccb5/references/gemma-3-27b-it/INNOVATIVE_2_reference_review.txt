This paper tackles the crucial problem of causal inference under missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). The core contribution lies in extending recoverability results—showing under what conditions probabilistic *and* causal queries can be consistently estimated despite missingness. Notably, the authors demonstrate that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x)) are non-recoverable, a significant and potentially game-changing result. The paper meticulously develops theoretical conditions (Sequential Factorization, Theorems 1-3, Corollaries 1-2) and provides illustrative examples, including applications to attrition. 

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The problem is fundamentally important, and the presented theoretical framework offers a rigorous approach. The key result—causal recoverability even with non-recoverable probabilistic relationships—is genuinely novel and addresses a limitation in existing literature. The extension of sequential factorization to handle interactions between missingness mechanisms is a valuable technical contribution. The authors convincingly demonstrate the application of their results to the practically relevant problem of attrition, highlighting the potential impact of the work. The inclusion of detailed proofs and careful consideration of necessary *and* sufficient conditions further strengthens the contribution. The paper’s clarity is generally good, with definitions provided and examples used to illustrate complex concepts.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the empirical validation is limited. The examples are helpful for understanding the theory, but a more extensive experimental evaluation—perhaps on simulated data with varying m-graph structures—would significantly bolster the claims. While the authors mention applying the results to attrition, a more detailed discussion of practical challenges in applying these methods to real-world attrition scenarios (e.g., model specification, sensitivity analysis) would be beneficial. The presentation, at times, is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. For instance, the jump from definitions to theorems can be abrupt. The discussion of related work, while covering key areas, could be more focused on specifically how this work distinguishes itself from existing causal inference methods addressing missing data (beyond simply stating they address MNAR). Also, Theorem 5 regarding simple attrition seems a bit too strong a claim, and I would like to see some evidence to support this claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The core result regarding causal recoverability in the face of non-recoverable probabilistic relationships is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental gap in the literature.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a well-developed theoretical framework with clear definitions and carefully proven theorems.
* **Potential Impact:** The findings have significant implications for causal inference in observational studies with missing data, particularly in scenarios like attrition.
* **Bold Approach:** The paper takes on a difficult problem and proposes a sophisticated solution, going beyond incremental improvements.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of extensive experimental validation weakens the confidence in the practical applicability of the theory.
* **Dense Presentation:** The theoretical density can make the paper challenging to follow for readers not already familiar with the m-graph framework.
* **Practical Considerations:** A more detailed discussion of the challenges of applying the methods in real-world settings is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the core result and the theoretical rigor outweigh the limitations in empirical validation. The potential impact on causal inference under missing data is substantial. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the points raised regarding empirical validation and clarity in future work. The paper pushes the boundaries of what is considered possible in handling missing data, which is highly valued at NIPS. This is not a perfectly polished paper, but it’s a *bold* and *important* one.
