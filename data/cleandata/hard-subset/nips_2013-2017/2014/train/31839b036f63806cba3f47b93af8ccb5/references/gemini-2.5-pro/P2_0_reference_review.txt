This paper presents a graphical modeling framework, called 'm-graphs', for analyzing and recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work extends the authors' previous results (Mohan et al., 2013) by providing a more powerful set of theoretical tools. The primary contributions are: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that can handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) a new theorem for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible; (3) novel graphical criteria for determining when a query is *non-recoverable*; and (4) a key finding that causal effects can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic distributions are not. The theory is applied to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, where the authors claim to correct misconceptions in existing literature.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The work is theoretically deep, building upon the well-established foundations of graphical models, d-separation, and Pearl's do-calculus. The theorems are stated formally and appear to be sound (with proofs provided in the appendix). The arguments are logical and well-structured.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Rigorous Framework:** The m-graph framework provides a principled and expressive language for encoding assumptions about missingness processes, moving beyond the often-unrealistic MAR/MCAR assumptions.
    *   **Complete Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides a balanced set of results. It not only gives sufficient conditions for recoverability (Theorems 1 and 2) but also necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3). The latter is particularly valuable, as it allows researchers to identify situations where consistent estimation is impossible under the given model assumptions, preventing futile efforts.
    *   **Sound Application of Causal Theory:** The connection between recoverability and causal identification (using d*-separation and do-calculus in Section 6) is sophisticated and leads to the paper's most insightful result: causal queries are fundamentally different from observational ones in the context of missing data. This is a complete and well-supported argument.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a contribution of this nature, the claims would be strengthened by simulation studies. For instance, demonstrating that the estimators derived from Theorem 1 or 2 converge to the true values on synthetically generated data would provide a valuable sanity check and give practitioners more confidence in the methods. It would also be interesting to see how these estimators perform with finite sample sizes.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, especially for such a technical topic. The introduction clearly outlines the paper's contributions and structure. The use of illustrative examples with figures (e.g., Example 1, Figure 2) is crucial and effective in making the abstract theorems understandable.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Logical Flow:** The paper progresses logically from extending existing methods (Section 3), to introducing new ones (Section 4), to establishing impossibility results (Section 5), and finally to tackling the more complex case of causal queries (Section 6).
    *   **Effective Examples:** The examples are well-chosen to motivate the need for each new theorem and to demonstrate its application. The attrition example in Section 7 grounds the theory in a real-world problem.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   **Dense Notation:** The paper is notation-heavy, which is perhaps unavoidable. Terms like `R(part)`, `Mb(R(i))`, and `Pasub(R(i))` are introduced quickly and require careful attention from the reader. A small table of notation in the appendix could be beneficial.
    *   **Assumed Knowledge:** The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with graphical models and causal inference (e.g., d-separation, collider paths, do-calculus, inducing paths). While appropriate for the NIPS audience, some concepts, like the specific definition of the Markov Blanket in Theorem 2, could benefit from a brief intuitive explanation in the main text.

**Originality:** The work is highly original and represents a significant advance in the formal treatment of missing data.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Substantial Extension of Prior Work:** This is not an incremental paper. It significantly broadens the scope of the authors' 2013 work, enabling the analysis of more complex missingness scenarios (e.g., interacting `R` variables).
    *   **Novel Non-Recoverability Criteria:** To my knowledge, the formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are novel. This is a foundational contribution to the theory of inference with missing data.
    *   **New Insight on Causal vs. Probabilistic Queries:** The central result of Section 6—that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable when P(y,x) is not—is a powerful and non-obvious insight that elegantly bridges the theories of missing data and causal identification.
    *   **Correction of Existing Literature:** The paper demonstrates the utility of its formal framework by identifying and correcting a previously published condition for non-recoverability in attrition studies (Garcia, 2013). This shows clear progress over the state of the art.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to be influential for both theorists and practitioners dealing with missing data.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Addressing a Fundamental Problem:** Missing data is a ubiquitous problem across all empirical sciences. This paper provides a powerful, assumption-explicit toolkit for tackling it, particularly for challenging Missing Not At Random (MNAR) cases where most standard methods fail.
    *   **Practical Implications:** The graphical criteria are powerful because they are purely structural. A researcher can draw their assumed m-graph and use the theorems in this paper to determine if their target query is recoverable before attempting any statistical estimation.
    *   **Impact on Causal Inference:** The paper makes a strong case that reasoning about causal effects from incomplete data requires a formal synthesis of missing data theory and causal calculus. The results could have a significant impact on how causal claims are assessed in fields like epidemiology, economics, and social sciences, where both missing data and causal questions are paramount.
    *   **Foundation for Future Work:** This work lays a theoretical foundation for developing practical recovery algorithms, sensitivity analyses for model assumptions, and new model specification tests.

### Summary and Recommendation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes fundamental contributions to the understanding of missing data through the lens of graphical models. Its strengths lie in its technical rigor, the originality of its results (especially the non-recoverability criteria and the separation of causal from probabilistic recoverability), and the high significance of the problem it addresses. The primary weakness is the absence of any empirical validation, which would have strengthened its practical appeal. However, for a foundational theoretical work, this is a minor limitation. The paper is well-written, clearly advances the state of the art, and provides deep insights that are likely to spur further research.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
