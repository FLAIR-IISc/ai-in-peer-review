Summary
The paper builds on the “missingness graph” (m-graph) framework of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) to study when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing values. The contributions are: (i) a refinement of the sequential/ordered factorization approach that allows R (missingness mechanism) nodes to appear in the factorization, enabling recovery in cases where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new recovery formula for the joint distribution in semi-Markovian models without an admissible ordered factorization, based on partitions of R and Markov blankets connected by collider paths; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its mechanism R_y are inseparable (connected by inducing/collider paths), with an instantiation via d*-separation/dormant independences; and (v) necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in “simple attrition” settings (|V_m|=1), for both joint distributions and causal effects.

The work is positioned within Pearl’s causal graphical framework and do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006, 2008), and extends Mohan et al. (2013). It connects to classical MAR/MCAR assumptions (Rubin) by showing when model-blind estimators suffice, and discusses attrition use cases.

Strengths
- Addresses a central and difficult problem—recoverability under MNAR—where graphical methods can bring clarity.
- Extends the ordered factorization approach to include R nodes; Example 1 illustrates why minimality matters and shows a case that the 2013 theorem could not handle.
- Theorem 2 and its corollary provide constructive recovery expressions in semi-Markovian models under explicit graphical conditions; this is potentially useful beyond the admissible-sequence regime.
- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are helpful in practice, preventing futile attempts at estimation.
- The attrition results (Theorems 5 and 6) give necessary and sufficient graphical conditions in an important special case; the characterization appears crisp.
- The discussion that causal effects may be recoverable when P(V) is not (and when Y and R_y are inseparable) is conceptually important and aligns with modern identification theory.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and notation: There are several inconsistencies and ambiguities that hinder verification. Examples: V is overloaded (G(V,E) with V = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R); R_xi vs R_Xi vs Rw vs R_W; “general ordered factorization” conditions in Theorem 1 use Rxi but later RXi is used; Section 2 mixes manifest/underlying distributions with terse compatibility definitions. Many figures are referenced but not visible in the main text; several proofs are deferred. These issues make it hard to check correctness.
- Assumptions are under-specified. The theorems implicitly rely on standard Markov/faithfulness and positivity assumptions, but only positivity over complete cases is stated. Some recovery formulas condition on RMb(·)=0; this is a stronger positivity requirement that should be made explicit. The role of latent confounding between R and substantive variables is only partially addressed via Corollary 1; the general case could use clearer statements.
- Novelty vs prior work: Several results seem incremental to Mohan et al. (2013)—notably the inclusion of R nodes in ordered factorizations and the extension to interacting R’s—useful, but the boundary of novelty is not crisply delineated. The causal recoverability with inseparability leans heavily on d*-separation/dormant independences; the new contribution (Theorem 4) appears limited to |V_m|=1 and requires nontrivial conditions that are not provided as an algorithmic test.
- “Algorithms” are promised but not actually presented. Beyond theorems, there is no concrete decision procedure, pseudo-code, or complexity analysis for checking recoverability and constructing estimands (contrast with the ID algorithm for causal effects). Practitioners would benefit from a mechanized criterion that, given an m-graph, returns recoverable/not and, if recoverable, an estimand.
- Lack of empirical or computational validation. The paper provides no simulations or real-data examples to: (a) verify that the proposed estimands yield unbiased estimates under missingness; (b) quantify variance/finite-sample behavior; (c) compare against ML/EM, IPW, MI, or graph-blind methods under MAR/MNAR misspecification. For NeurIPS, even theoretical papers typically include synthetic demonstrations.
- Scope limitations: Key positive results either require admissible sequences including R nodes or impose fairly strong graphical restrictions (e.g., |V_m|=1 for necessary/sufficient results on causal effects). The paper would benefit from discussing completeness/tightness: How much larger is the recoverable class compared to Mohan et al. (2013)? Are the non-recoverability conditions tight?
- Related work and citations: References contain formatting issues and some omissions, and there is limited discussion of overlaps/differences with other identification results for nonignorable missingness (e.g., selection models/pattern mixture models; broader semiparametric identification literature). Positioning relative to more recent or concurrent works is minimal.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a precise, unified notation for all R-related sets (RX, Rpart, Mb(·)) and restate Theorem 1 with consistent symbols? What are the exact probabilistic assumptions (Markov, faithfulness, positivity) required for each theorem?
- Is there an algorithmic test (akin to ID) for Theorem 2’s conditions and construction of the estimand? If so, please include pseudo-code and complexity; if not, can you outline how to automate the partitioning and blanket checks?
- How tight are the non-recoverability conditions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2)? Can you provide counterexamples that show necessity but not sufficiency?
- For Theorem 4, can you clarify how to verify the d*-separation conditions in practice and whether the result generalizes beyond |V_m|=1?
- Please include at least synthetic experiments comparing your estimands to EM/MI/IPW under MNAR graphs, demonstrating unbiasedness and robustness.

Minor comments
- Clean up references and equation numbering. Ensure all figures appear and are legible. Define all notation on first use (e.g., semi-Markovian model, inducing path, GX,W).
- Clarify the exact positivity requirement for expressions conditioning on R=0 and RMb(·)=0.

Pros
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem with clear practical relevance.
- Provides new graphical conditions and some constructive estimands beyond earlier work.
- Useful negative results (non-recoverability criteria).
- Clear treatment of simple attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Cons
- Clarity/notation issues impede verification.
- Lacks algorithmic decision procedures and empirical validation.
- Novelty appears incremental relative to Mohan et al. (2013); scope of advances is not quantified.
- Some results are restricted (e.g., |V_m|=1), limiting generality.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The topic is important and the direction is promising, but the current submission lacks the rigor in presentation, algorithmic concreteness, and empirical validation expected at NeurIPS. With clarified notation/assumptions, an explicit decision algorithm, tighter positioning relative to prior work, and at least synthetic validation, this could become a strong contribution.
