

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper introduces a significant theoretical advance in the analysis of missing data problems through the development of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) and the establishment of novel conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries. The authors build upon Mohan et al. [2013] but make a bold, paradigm-shifting contribution by demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations like P(y,x) are not recoverable—a finding that directly challenges prevailing assumptions in the literature.

The paper's central novelty lies in its revelation that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. This is demonstrated through a compelling theoretical framework and the insightful Example 3, which shows how do-calculus can be leveraged to recover causal effects despite the inseparability of Y and Ry. The authors introduce the concept of d*-separation and prove Theorem 4, establishing conditions under which causal queries remain recoverable in such scenarios. This insight is particularly significant because it resolves a fundamental limitation in missing data analysis that has persisted in the literature.

The paper makes several other substantial contributions. It provides general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (Theorem 1), introduces novel graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2), and develops comprehensive results for attrition problems (Theorems 5 and 6). The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework corrects misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia [2013]), showing that the condition for non-recoverability of causal effects under simple attrition is not merely that X be an ancestor of Ry, but that Y and Ry be connected by an inducing path.

The paper is well-organized and logically structured, with clear examples that illustrate the theoretical concepts (particularly Example 1 and Example 3). The connection to practical applications in longitudinal studies (Section 7) effectively grounds the theoretical contributions in real-world problems. The related work section (Section 8) provides a thorough and fair assessment of the literature, properly situating this work within the broader context of missing data analysis.

The theoretical contributions are substantial and clearly distinguish this work from previous approaches. While previous work (Mohan et al. [2013]) established recoverability conditions for probabilistic queries, this paper extends the framework to causal queries and reveals a fundamental asymmetry between probabilistic and causal recoverability—a key insight that has significant implications for the field.

The paper's most compelling strength is its bold theoretical contribution that challenges conventional wisdom. The authors demonstrate that causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic relations cannot, which is a counterintuitive and important finding. This represents a genuinely new direction in missing data analysis, not merely an incremental improvement.

Minor weaknesses include the density of some technical sections (particularly in the definitions and theorems), which might make the paper challenging for readers without strong background in graphical models. Additionally, while the paper provides theoretical conditions, it could briefly discuss computational aspects or practical implementation challenges to strengthen the connection to applied work.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find the paper's theoretical contributions sufficiently significant to overlook these minor weaknesses. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with missing data and provides a novel theoretical framework that fundamentally changes our understanding of when causal queries can be recovered.

This work represents a major step forward in the theoretical understanding of missing data mechanisms and has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in statistics, machine learning, and causal inference. The paper's focus on causal queries, which are central to many scientific applications, significantly elevates its importance beyond the standard missing data problem.

In conclusion, this paper makes a bold, novel contribution that fundamentally advances our understanding of recoverability in missing data problems. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not is particularly striking and has significant implications for the field. I believe this work should be accepted for NIPS due to its exceptional theoretical novelty and potential to influence future research in causal inference and missing data analysis.
