Summary
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) by (i) generalizing the sequential/ordered factorization approach to include missingness-indicator nodes R, enabling recovery of probabilistic queries when missingness mechanisms interact (Theorem 1); (ii) providing a new recoverability result (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and their Markov blankets, applicable even when no admissible ordered factorization exists; (iii) introducing graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Cor. 2); (iv) showing that some causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when corresponding probabilistic quantities are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from Ry (Theorem 4); and (v) giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1) for both joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6). Proofs are relegated to an appendix; examples illustrate the constructions.

Relation to prior work
The work is firmly grounded in the missing data and causal inference literature: Rubin’s MCAR/MAR framework, likelihood/EM approaches, IPW/MI, and causal identifiability via do-calculus (Pearl; Shpitser & Pearl). It builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) by relaxing assumptions (allowing edges among R), broadening factorization strategies, and addressing non-recoverability. The causal recoverability results leverage dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008) and inducing paths (Verma & Pearl, 1991).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Incorporating R into admissible ordered factorizations is a neat and practical extension that unlocks classes (e.g., longitudinal settings with interacting missingness mechanisms) previously out of reach.
- General recoverability theorem (Theorem 2): Using R-partitions and Markov blankets provides a systematic path to recover P(V) beyond sequential factorizations. The formula P(V)=P(V,R=0)∏i P(R(i)=0 | Mb(R(i)), R_Mb=0) is intuitive and aligns with selection/weighting ideas while being graphically justified.
- Non-recoverability criteria: Clear necessary conditions for P(V) and P(Y|X) help practitioners diagnose impossibility and avoid fishing for estimators.
- Causal recoverability despite inseparability: Theorem 4 and Example 3 demonstrate an important phenomenon—interventional queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic separability fails—by judicious use of do-calculus and conditioning on Ry=0.
- Attrition focus: Necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6) are crisp, useful, and correct a misconception in the literature.
- Potential impact: The graphical conditions could influence both methodology (designing estimators) and practice (study design, diagnostic checks).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and notation: The manuscript is dense and sometimes imprecise. Examples:
  - Definition 2: The role of minimality in Xi is crucial; define Xr and RXm formally (used in Theorem 1) to avoid ambiguity.
  - Notation R′x for Rx=0 is used early; please define consistently at first use and stick to one convention.
  - d*-separation definition and notation “X ⊥w Y | Z” is nonstandard and confusing; clearly connect to Shpitser & Pearl (2008).
  - Several arguments hinge on figures (Figs. 2–4) and appendices not included here, making verification hard.
- Theorem scope/completeness:
  - Theorem 1 is sufficient but not necessary; discuss completeness of the recovery tactics (e.g., does including more R nodes in O and searching over admissible sequences approach completeness?).
  - Theorem 2’s conditions (no neighbor/collider path between Vm and its R) may be conservative; a discussion of tightness and comparisons to Mohan et al. (2013) would help. Are there cases covered by Theorem 2 not covered by previous Theorem 5 of Mohan et al. (2013)? A taxonomy with inclusion relations would be valuable.
- Potential tension between results: Theorem 6 states an iff condition for recoverability of P(y|do(x)) under simple attrition: non-recoverable if Y–Ry have an inducing path. Example 3 claims recoverability even when Y and Ry are not separable (and possibly connected by an inducing path). Please clarify whether Example 3 falls outside “simple attrition” (e.g., due to different structural constraints or non-randomized setting), and reconcile the conditions to avoid apparent contradiction.
- Practicality and algorithms:
  - While theorems state existence, the paper would benefit from explicit procedures: how to search for admissible sequences (Theorem 1), how to construct R-partitions and Mb(R(i)) from the latent projection, and how to check conditions efficiently. A complexity discussion would be welcome.
  - No empirical illustration. Even for a theory paper, small simulated studies showing the estimators in action (vs EM/IPW/MI under MNAR) would clarify utility and robustness.
- Assumptions: The prohibition “R may not be parents of V ∪ U” is standard in m-graphs, but its implications (e.g., representing measurement error vs missingness) deserve a brief reminder to avoid misuse.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a clear algorithmic recipe for Theorem 2, including how to test the collider-path conditions and construct Mb(R(i)) on the latent projection ADMG?
- Is Theorem 2 strictly more general than the earlier necessary-and-sufficient result for the subclass in Mohan et al. (2013)? A formal inclusion statement would help.
- For Theorem 4, can you give a simple, fully worked example with the necessary Z,W explicitly identified, and show how the identifiability checks are performed?
- Please clarify the relationship between Theorem 6 and Example 3, and precisely define the structural constraints of “simple attrition.”

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with results consistent with graphical causal/missing data theory. However, key proofs are in the appendix; include intuition and key steps for the main theorems in the main text.

Clarity
- Organization is good but notation and definitions need tightening. Provide self-contained statements and unambiguous symbols. Ensure all figures/equations referred to are present.

Originality
- The extensions (R-aware factorizations, general recoverability without admissible sequences, non-recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability beyond separability) appear novel and meaningful.

Significance
- High potential impact on causal inference with MNAR data and attrition. The graphical criteria are actionable and could guide both estimation and design.

Pros
- Solid theoretical contributions with clear graphical intuition.
- Addresses important practical scenarios (interacting missingness mechanisms, attrition).
- Provides both positive (recoverability) and negative (non-recoverability) results.

Cons
- Dense, notation-heavy presentation; some ambiguity.
- No empirical validation or concrete algorithms/pseudocode.
- Apparent tension between Example 3 and Theorem 6 needs clarification.

Recommendation
- Leaning toward accept after revision. The ideas are strong and relevant to NeurIPS, but the paper should improve clarity, reconcile subtle points, and ideally add a small empirical demonstration. Confidence: high.
