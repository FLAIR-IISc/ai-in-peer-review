- Extends Mohan et al.'s missingness-graph framework to give formal conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Introduces a general framework to determine when joint/conditional distributions and causal effects P(y|do(x)) are estimable from incomplete datasets.  
- Theorem 1 generalizes sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (e.g., longitudinal studies).  
- Theorem 4 shows causal effects can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness indicator Ry are not d-separable.  
- Theorems 3 and Corollary 2 provide novel graphical criteria identifying non-recoverability.  
- Applies theoretical results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies and corrects misconceptions (Theorems 5 and 6; e.g., Garcia 2013).  
- Paper is well-organized with clear definitions and illustrative examples.  
- Lacks empirical validation and comparisons to existing missing-data methods.  
- Notation has minor inconsistencies (e.g., distinction between R variables and Ry) and some complex theorems need more intuitive explanation.  
- Does not address scalability to high-dimensional data or large graphs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, fills gaps on non-recoverability, and has important implications for causal inference in practice.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical significance limited by no empirical results, unclear scalability, and some theorems under-motivated.  
- Overall recommendation: accept as a strong theoretical contribution appropriate for NIPS despite the absence of empirical validation.
