Summary
This paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (2013), the authors: (i) generalize the “ordered factorization” approach to include R-nodes (missingness mechanisms), yielding a broader Sequential Factorization theorem that handles interacting missingness processes; (ii) provide new algorithms/expressions to recover joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via partitions of R and Markov blanket reasoning in semi-Markovian models; (iii) introduce the first explicit graphical non-recoverability criteria (necessary conditions) for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) show that certain causal queries P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y), P(y|x)) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from Ry via d*/dormant independences; and (v) derive necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition (|Vm|=1), covering both joint and causal queries, and clarifying when randomization plus attrition is sufficient (or not) for causal effect estimation. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; examples and corollaries illustrate the main ideas.

Relation to prior work
The work is firmly grounded in Pearl’s causal framework, do-calculus, and ADMGs (Richardson). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) by (1) allowing R-to-R interactions within factorizations, (2) covering cases not handled by earlier sufficient conditions, and (3) providing explicit necessary conditions for non-recoverability. It connects to MAR/MCAR practice, IPW/ML/EM approaches, and recent graphical analyses of attrition/selection bias, while addressing MNAR regimes where standard model-blind techniques fail.

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: The demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of the outcome from its missingness mechanism is conceptually important and practically relevant. The non-recoverability criteria and the necessary/sufficient attrition results fill clear gaps.
- Breadth: The generalization to semi-Markovian models and interacting R mechanisms substantially widens applicability beyond prior ordered factorization results.
- Clarity of insights: The emphasis on admissible sequences (with minimality), R-partitions, Markov blankets, and d*/dormant independences provides clean, checkable graphical conditions.
- Impact potential: Offers principled guidance for when recovery is possible/impossible under MNAR, a long-standing challenge. The attrition results correct common misconceptions in the literature.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical/algorithmic validation: No simulations or empirical case studies demonstrating finite-sample performance or robustness to model misspecification. The paper provides estimands but not practical estimation procedures (e.g., plug-in, semiparametric estimators), variance properties, or sensitivity analysis.
- Clarity/organization: Presentation is dense; several central terms (e.g., minimality in ordered factorization, collider-path vs inducing path) would benefit from a concise “toolbox” section. Some key conditions (e.g., R not parenting V∪U) and positivity assumptions deserve discussion about realism and scope.
- Automation: The decision procedures are not presented algorithmically (no pseudo-code), making it harder for practitioners to implement the recovery/non-recovery checks.
- Figures/notation: Many claims hinge on referenced figures; without them, it is difficult to verify examples. Some reference formatting is broken.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. Theorems and corollaries are precise and connected to known graphical concepts; the extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013) are substantive. However, the work is largely theoretical; adding algorithmic decision procedures and at least minimal empirical illustrations would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
Generally readable for experts in causal graphs, but heavy going. Streamlining notation, consolidating definitions (e.g., collider path vs inducing path, d*/dormant independence), and adding a compact checklist/flowchart for recovery would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Extending sequential factorization to interacting R, providing necessary non-recoverability criteria, and establishing recovery of causal effects under inseparability are all novel and important.

Significance
High. The results will influence how MNAR problems are approached in causal inference and machine learning, with immediate implications for longitudinal studies and attrition. The graphical non-recoverability tests are especially valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise algorithm/flowchart for deciding recoverability (probabilistic and causal), including complexity discussion.
- Include at least one synthetic study illustrating recovery estimands operationalized with simple estimators, and one attrition example (even small-scale) to demonstrate utility.
- Discuss robustness to m-graph misspecification and briefly connect to available tests (Mohan & Pearl, 2014).
- Clarify assumptions: the restriction “R may not be parents of V∪U,” strict positivity over complete cases, and when these may be relaxed.
- Polish references and ensure figures convey all needed conditional independences.

Pros
- Conceptually bold: shows causal recoverability where probabilistic recovery fails.
- Generalization to interacting missingness mechanisms.
- First explicit graphical non-recoverability criteria.
- Clear necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition.

Cons
- No empirical validation or implemented decision procedure.
- Dense exposition; relies heavily on appendix and figures.
- Practical guidance on estimator construction and variance is missing.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite limited empirical validation, the paper offers significant, novel theoretical advances with clear impact on how MNAR problems are analyzed. The bold insight about causal recoverability under inseparability and the new non-recoverability criteria justify inclusion at NIPS.

Confidence
High (familiar with causal graphical models, missing data, and identifiability).
