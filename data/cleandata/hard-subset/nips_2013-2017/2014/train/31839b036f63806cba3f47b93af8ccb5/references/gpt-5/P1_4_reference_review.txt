Summary
This paper extends the “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) framework of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) for reasoning about recoverability (identifiability) of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The main contributions are:
- A generalization of the “ordered factorization” approach to include missingness indicators (R variables), yielding a new sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that handles interactions among missingness mechanisms (e.g., longitudinal settings where R nodes have edges between them).
- A new recovery strategy for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2), based on partitioning R into components not connected by neighbor or collider paths, and conditioning on Markov blankets Mb(R(i)). A simpler corollary (Corollary 1) handles the no-latent-confounding-between-V-and-R subclass.
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) that give necessary conditions for joint and conditional distributions to be recoverable, in particular ruling out neighbor or “all-collider” paths between a partially observed variable and its missingness indicator.
- A key conceptual result: certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic relations P(y, x) or P(y|x) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism RY (Example 3; Theorem 4 via d*-separation/dormant independence).
- Application to attrition: necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) in the “simple attrition” case (|Vm|=1), correcting claims in prior work.

Relation to prior work
This paper is a natural and substantial extension of Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) who introduced m-graphs and initial recoverability results. It positions itself relative to classical MAR/MCAR assumptions (Rubin, Little & Rubin), likelihood/EM approaches (Lauritzen, Dempster et al., Koller & Friedman), IPW and doubly-robust lines (Robins & Rotnitzky; Van der Laan & Robins), and modern ML with nonrandom missingness (Marlin et al.). On the causal side, it builds on do-calculus/identification (Pearl; Shpitser & Pearl) and dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl 2008). The attrition comparison with Garcia (2013) is appropriate and informative.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: showing causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic relations are not is important and nontrivial; the worked example using do-calculus is clear and valuable.
- Broadened scope: Theorem 1 usefully extends the ordered factorization method to cases where R variables interact; Theorem 2 provides a different recovery route when admissible sequences do not exist.
- Non-recoverability criteria: Practical guardrails are essential; Theorem 3/Corollary 2 give useful necessary conditions for practitioners.
- Attrition results: Clean necessary-and-sufficient graphical criteria for |Vm|=1 are likely to be widely used.
- The paper is well rooted in the literature and clarifies relationships to MAR/MCAR, MNAR, d*-separation, and inducing paths.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Algorithmic specificity: The paper claims “algorithms,” but what is provided are constructive identifiability expressions. A more explicit decision procedure/workflow (input: m-graph and query; output: recovered expression or non-recoverability certificate) would improve usability, including complexity and completeness statements (e.g., which classes are covered by Theorem 1 vs. Theorem 2).
- Scope/limitations: Theorem 2 imposes restrictions (no neighbor or collider path between any Vm variable and its R) that are fairly strong; please discuss how often this holds in practice and whether partial violations can be handled.
- Practical estimation: The paper focuses on recoverability. Guidance on estimation from finite samples (e.g., IPW/plug-in estimators for terms like P(Y*|..., R′), variance implications, positivity violations, regularization for high-dimensional conditioning sets) would make the contribution more actionable.
- Clarity/notation: Some notation is terse or inconsistent. For example, “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively” is ambiguous; please use standard mutilation notation (e.g., G\Xout, G\Xin). Define positivity precisely (footnote alluded to) and be consistent about set shorthands (Rw vs. RXm). Include more intuition for Theorems 2–4 (diagrams of collider/inducing paths and Markov blankets would help).
- Proof accessibility: Proofs are in the appendix; brief proof sketches or key lemmas in the main text would help readers assess correctness without flipping back and forth.
- Examples/experiments: Beyond Example 1 and the simple do-calculus example, more diverse m-graphs illustrating each theorem (including one where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 succeeds) would help. Even small simulations showing finite-sample behavior for recovered estimands (bias/variance under MNAR) would strengthen the paper.
- References/formatting: Several citations are inconsistently formatted; please clean.

Quality
Technically solid and largely careful, with clear lineage to established identifiability tools (d-separation, do-calculus, d*-separation). Claims are supported by theorems with proofs in the appendix. The main risk is practical: absence of an explicit algorithm and finite-sample estimation guidance.

Clarity
Generally readable for experts but dense. Improving notation, adding intuitive explanations and more figures, and structuring a decision flow would enhance clarity.

Originality
High. The extension to interacting R mechanisms, the recovery approach without admissible sequences, necessary non-recoverability criteria, and especially the result that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered despite inseparability of Y and RY together constitute meaningful advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013).

Significance
High theoretical significance for causal inference with MNAR data. The attrition results are timely and likely to influence both theory and practice. With added algorithmic clarity, this could become a standard reference.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical advances with clear practical implications.
- Fills gaps left by prior m-graph work; corrects misconceptions in the attrition literature.
- Establishes useful necessary non-recoverability conditions.

Cons for acceptance
- “Algorithm” claims outpace provided procedural detail.
- Limited empirical or practical estimation guidance.
- Some clarity/notation and reference issues.

Questions for authors
1) Can you present a unified decision procedure (flowchart) combining Theorems 1–6 to decide recoverability and construct estimands? Any complexity guarantees?
2) For Theorem 2, can you provide an example where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 applies, with a step-by-step recovery?
3) How sensitive are the results to violations of strict positivity over complete cases? Any remedies?
4) Do you plan to release code that, given an m-graph and query, checks conditions and returns a recovered expression?

Recommendation
Accept if the authors can strengthen the algorithmic presentation and clarity. The contributions are original and significant for NeurIPS audiences interested in causality and missing data. My confidence is high.
