

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present general conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions, show that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not, and apply their results to attrition problems. The paper builds on previous work in graphical models for missing data, particularly Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl's causal inference framework.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing recoverability conditions for causal queries beyond probabilistic ones. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic queries (which requires separability of variables from their missingness mechanisms) and causal queries (which can sometimes bypass this requirement) is theoretically interesting and practically significant. The application to attrition problems in section 7 is well-motivated, and the results provide a formal framework for addressing a common problem in longitudinal studies. The paper is well-organized, with clear theorems and examples that illustrate the theoretical results.

## Weaknesses
Despite the paper's theoretical contribution, I identify several significant issues that undermine the paper's quality and credibility:

1. **Inconsistent and Confusing Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, which creates confusion. For example, in section 2, missingness mechanisms are denoted both as "Ry" and "Ry" (e.g., "Ry" in equation 1 versus "Ry" later). In Theorem 1, the notation "Yi⊥⊥(Ryi , Rxi)|Xi \{Ryi , Rxi}" is mathematically nonsensical since it removes the variables from the conditioning set. The paper should standardize its notation (e.g., consistently using R_x for missingness mechanisms).

2. **Logical Contradictions**: The paper claims in section 6.1 that causal queries can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are connected by a collider path (Example 3), yet Theorem 6 in section 7.2 states that causal effects are recoverable "if and only if Y and Ry are neither neighbors nor connected by an inducing path." Since a collider path is an inducing path (Definition 5), this is a direct contradiction. The paper must resolve this inconsistency.

3. **Unjustified Claims**: In Example 1, the paper claims "P (X,Y, Z) = P (Y |X,Z,R′x, R ′ y, R ′ z)P (Z|R ′ z) ∑ Ry P (X|R′x, Ry)P (Ry)" but provides no justification for why the R'x, R'y, R'z terms appear in the numerator but not the denominator. The transition from P(Y|X,Z) to P(Y|X,Z,R'x,R'y,R'z) is not supported by the preceding conditions.

4. **Inconsistent Theorem Statements**: Theorem 2 states "P (V ) = P (V,R = 0) ∏ i P (R (i) = 0|Mb(R(i)), RMb(R(i)) = 0)" but P(V,R=0) is a joint distribution while the right-hand side is a product of conditional probabilities, creating a dimensional mismatch. The paper should clarify this.

5. **Formatting and Typos**: There are multiple formatting inconsistencies, such as "strictly positive1" in Definition 1 (section 2.1), inconsistent use of asterisks (V* vs V^*), and the reference section contains formatting errors like "Allison.,? \Q2002\E, Allison., " which appears to be a LaTeX error.

6. **Related Work**: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from Mohan et al. (2013), which it claims to extend. The related work section should more clearly articulate what specific results are novel.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some theoretically interesting insights, the numerous technical inconsistencies, logical contradictions, and notational errors undermine its credibility. The paper makes bold claims about recoverability conditions but fails to properly justify or verify them in all cases. The contradiction between section 6.1 and Theorem 6 is particularly problematic, as it suggests a fundamental flaw in the paper's core argument.

The paper's potential significance is overshadowed by these methodological flaws. For a paper to be accepted at NeurIPS, it must not only present novel ideas but also demonstrate technical rigor and consistency. This paper falls short on the latter criterion.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper's general direction is promising, the technical shortcomings are too severe to overlook. The author should revise the paper to resolve the logical contradictions, standardize notation, and provide rigorous justifications for all claims before resubmission. The current version contains too many inconsistencies to be considered a complete and sound contribution to the field.
