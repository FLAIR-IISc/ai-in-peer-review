- Introduces "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to represent missing data processes and determine recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries.
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) with a refined sequential factorization theorem and general recovery algorithms for distributions.
- Provides new graphical criteria for non-recoverability and shows causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not.
- Applies framework to attrition problems in longitudinal studies and gives necessary and sufficient conditions in specific cases (e.g., Theorem 5).
- Technically sound with precise definitions, theorems, and rigorous proofs in the appendix.
- Clearly distinguishes recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, a major theoretical contribution.
- Examples (notably Example 3) effectively illustrate that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparable Y and Ry.
- Theoretical framework is well-constructed and extends prior m-graph work to more complex missingness interactions.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent and well-explained notation.
- Graphical examples (Figures 1–4) are helpful but could use clearer labeling of missingness mechanisms.
- Some technical concepts (e.g., d*-separation) may be hard to follow for readers without causal inference background.
- Original contributions include novel graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) and extending sequential factorization to interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Challenges prevailing assumptions by showing causal recoverability can differ from probabilistic recoverability.
- High significance due to the ubiquity of missing data and potential to prevent erroneous conclusions across disciplines.
- Corrects misconceptions in the literature (e.g., about X being an ancestor of Ry not being necessary for non-recoverability).
- Purely theoretical work that lays a foundation for future statistical methods and potential software implementations.
- Strength: clear conceptual separation between probabilistic and causal recoverability.
- Strength: demonstrates that causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic relations are not.
- Strength: offers precise graphical criteria for non-recoverability and applies to attrition problems.
- Strength: extends prior m-graph framework to handle more complex missing-data mechanisms with supporting examples.
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation or applications to real datasets.
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity of the recovery algorithms.
- Weakness: insufficient connection or comparison to existing statistical methods (e.g., multiple imputation).
- Weakness: needs clearer guidance on translating graphical criteria into practical implementations.
- Weakness: some concepts may be inaccessible to non-specialist readers.
- Recommendation: accept — significant theoretical contribution with important implications despite lack of experiments.
- Suggestion: add discussion of potential software implementations (R/Python) to help practitioners apply the criteria.
- Suggestion: include comparison with existing missing-data methods like multiple imputation to clarify practical relevance.
- Suggestion: briefly explain d*-separation for readers unfamiliar with causal-inference terminology.
- Suggestion: discuss computational complexity of the proposed recovery algorithms for practitioner use.
