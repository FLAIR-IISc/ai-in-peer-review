Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends the “missingness graphs” (m-graphs) framework of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) to deliver new sufficient and necessary conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and, importantly, to show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when analogous probabilistic quantities are not. The main contributions are:
- A generalized ordered factorization (Theorem 1) that allows inclusion of missingness indicators R in separating sets, thereby handling interacting missingness mechanisms (edges among R’s) that were not covered by the earlier ordered-factorization result.
- A general sufficient condition for recovering joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2), using partitions of R (based on collider-path connectivity) and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) of those partitions; plus a practical corollary (Corollary 1) under absence of latent confounding between R and substantive variables.
- Necessary graphical conditions for non-recoverability of the joint distribution (Theorem 3) and of conditionals (Corollary 2). To my knowledge, formal non-recoverability criteria in this setting have been missing from the literature.
- Results on causal queries with missing data: examples and Theorem 4 show that causal effects can be recoverable even when Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry (via do-calculus/dormant independences), contrasting sharply with probabilistic non-recoverability.
- A focused treatment of attrition (|Vm|=1), giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) (Theorem 5) and for recovering causal effects (Theorem 6), refining and correcting claims in Garcia (2013).

These results are situated within the broader missing data literature (Rubin’s MCAR/MAR, ML/EM, IPW/MI, CAR) and connect to causal identifiability (Shpitser & Pearl), acyclic directed mixed graphs (Richardson), and dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl). The work continues the line of causal-graphical approaches to MNAR problems initiated by Mohan, Pearl and co-authors.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The claims are theorem-driven with proofs stated to be in the appendix. The conditions in Theorem 1 are carefully modified to require minimal separating sets, addressing a potential pitfall. Theorem 2 provides a constructive formula for P(V) via P(V,R=0) and factors P(R(i)=0|·), under collider-path-based conditions; this looks internally consistent and generalizes prior sufficient conditions. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3/Corollary 2) are crisp and align with the separability intuition. The causal examples (Example 3) correctly leverage do-calculus to bypass probabilistic non-recoverability via conditioning on R′y. The attrition results (Theorems 5–6) give necessary and sufficient conditions, which is a strong contribution for the single-missing-variable case.
- Completeness: For multiple partially observed variables, the paper does not claim a complete characterization or decision procedure; instead it provides broad sufficient and necessary (but not jointly sufficient) conditions. That is a reasonable scope, but readers should be clear that general decidability is not resolved here.
- Empirical validation: The paper is purely theoretical with illustrative examples. That is acceptable for NeurIPS theory papers, but a brief computational demonstration (e.g., automatic derivation of estimands from graphs) would strengthen impact.
- Assumption transparency: The positivity requirement is stated; the role of semi-Markovian models is used (latent confounding via bidirected edges), though the term could be defined in the main text for completeness.

Clarity
- Organization is logical and tracks the stated contributions. However, the notation is dense and at times hard to parse. Suggestions:
  - Define “semi-markovian model” in the main text and restate the meaning of bidirected edges.
  - Clarify the notational clash “GX and GX” (incoming vs outgoing edge removal) and ensure consistency.
  - Provide intuition after each theorem (beyond examples), perhaps with a small figure highlighting why minimality in Theorem 1 is necessary.
  - Move key algorithmic steps from the appendix (if any) into the main text, or explicitly state complexity/constructive procedures for finding R partitions and Mb(R(i)).
- Minor editorial issues: several references have formatting issues; ensure consistent citation years and venues.

Originality
- Strong. Extending ordered factorization to include R in separators is a nontrivial and impactful refinement. Theorem 2’s partition-and-Markov-blanket approach for recoverability in the absence of admissible sequences appears new. Formal non-recoverability criteria for MNAR graphical models are novel. The demonstration that certain causal effects are recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable (and P(V) is non-recoverable) adds an important conceptual advance over prior probabilistic-centric treatments. The necessary and sufficient characterizations in the attrition case consolidate and correct existing claims.

Significance
- High theoretical significance. Missing-not-at-random inference is notoriously difficult; providing graphical conditions that both enable recovery and warn of impossibility should influence how practitioners model missingness and design studies. The attrition results are directly relevant to longitudinal trials. The causal recoverability insights can change how analysts think about targeting causal queries under missingness.
- Practical impact will depend on tooling. An implementable procedure for (i) detecting the paper’s conditions and (ii) emitting estimands in terms of observables would broaden adoption. Even a small prototype or worked-out recipes would help.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Advances the state of the art in graphical missing data with new sufficient and necessary results.
- Clarifies when causal queries can be recovered despite probabilistic non-recoverability.
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for a practically important special case (simple attrition).
- Careful use of do-calculus and collider-path reasoning; examples are instructive.

Cons (against acceptance)
- No end-to-end algorithm or complexity analysis for deciding recoverability in the general case; conditions are partial (necessary/sufficient but not complete) for multiple partially observed variables.
- Dense notation and some clarity issues; several definitions/assumptions could be made more explicit.
- No empirical demonstration or automated tool to illustrate practical applicability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes meaningful, well-justified theoretical contributions that extend the NIPS 2013 m-graphs line, adds novel non-recoverability criteria, and delivers tight results for attrition and insightful results for causal queries under MNAR. With modest improvements in clarity and a small illustrative tool/example, it would be a clear accept; even as-is, it merits inclusion for its theoretical impact.
