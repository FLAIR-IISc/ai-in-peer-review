## NIPS Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the framework of missingness graphs, providing more general conditions for recovering both probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries in the presence of missing data. The work builds logically upon prior research (Mohan et al., Pearl) and addresses a significant challenge in scientific inference. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation can be dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples beyond those already provided, especially regarding the practical application of the theorems. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper expands upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs). M-graphs provide a formal way to represent the missing data mechanism alongside the underlying causal relationships. The authors refine the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex scenarios where missingness mechanisms interact. They introduce new criteria for non-recoverability and, most significantly, demonstrate that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered *even when* the necessary conditions for recovering the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x)) are not met. This is a key contribution, as it identifies situations where causal inference remains possible despite challenges with standard statistical estimation. The work connects to broader fields like MAR/MCAR analysis, inverse probability weighting, and imputation, but distinguishes itself through its graphical modeling approach and rigorous identification of recoverability conditions.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, with clearly stated theorems and proofs (provided in the appendix). The extension of sequential factorization is well-motivated and addresses a limitation of previous work.
* **Novelty of Causal Recoverability:** The finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not is a significant and potentially impactful result. This challenges traditional assumptions about the limitations of missing data.
* **Formal Framework:** The use of m-graphs provides a clear and structured way to represent the missing data process and reason about recoverability. This facilitates communication and systematic analysis.
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper covers both the conditions for recoverability *and* the conditions for non-recoverability, providing a more complete picture of the problem.
* **Connection to Attrition:**  Applying the framework to the practical problem of attrition demonstrates the relevance and potential impact of the work.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Density & Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically rigorous, which can make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the existing literature on m-graphs to fully grasp the contributions.  More intuitive explanations and examples would be beneficial.
* **Limited Practical Demonstration:** While the attrition example is helpful, more extensive practical demonstrations of the algorithms and criteria introduced in the paper would strengthen the work.  A synthetic dataset illustrating the recovery of P(y|do(x)) in a scenario where P(y,x) is non-recoverable would be particularly valuable.
* **Example Complexity:** The examples provided (e.g., Figure 2, Figure 3) are somewhat complex and require careful study to understand how the theorems are applied. Simpler, step-by-step illustrations could improve clarity.
* **Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions algorithms but does not address their computational complexity.  Given the potential for complex m-graphs, a discussion of scalability would be useful.
* **Corollary Overload**: The numerous corollaries, while logically derived, can feel somewhat repetitive and detract from the main narrative. Some could be consolidated or moved to the appendix.



**4. Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The work appears complete within the scope it sets for itself. The authors are careful to present conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability.
* **Clarity:** The clarity is the main area for improvement. While technically precise, the writing could be more accessible. More illustrative examples, simpler diagrams, and clearer explanations of the key concepts would enhance readability.
* **Originality:** The extension of the recoverability framework, particularly the demonstration of causal query recovery, is novel and makes a significant contribution to the field. The paper builds on existing work but offers substantial new insights.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant for a wide range of fields dealing with missing data, including epidemiology, econometrics, and machine learning. The ability to recover causal effects despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships is a particularly valuable finding.

**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis.
* Rigorous mathematical framework and well-supported claims.
* Potential for real-world impact in various scientific disciplines.
* Addresses a crucial challenge in causal inference.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Density and potential inaccessibility for readers unfamiliar with the existing literature.
* Limited practical demonstration and discussion of computational complexity.
* Some complexity in examples might hinder understanding.




**6. Recommendation & Suggestions for Revision:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

* **Improve Clarity:**  Add more intuitive explanations and simplify the presentation of key concepts.
* **Add a Practical Example:** Include a detailed example illustrating the recovery of P(y|do(x)) in a scenario where P(y,x) is non-recoverable.  Synthetic data would suffice.
* **Discuss Computational Complexity:** Briefly address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms.
* **Consolidate Corollaries:** Review the corollaries and consider consolidating some of them or moving them to the appendix.
* **Consider adding a "Roadmap"**: A brief paragraph at the start of each section outlining its key contribution would help readers navigate the paper's complex structure.



This paper represents a significant advancement in the understanding of missing data and its impact on causal inference. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact and accessibility.
