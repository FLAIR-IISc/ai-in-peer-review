

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of missing data by introducing a formal framework using "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) that extends Mohan et al. [2013] with novel insights about recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The central, bold insight is that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships P(y,x) or P(y|x) are not recoverable—specifically, when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable.

The paper's most compelling contribution is Theorem 4, which demonstrates that causal effects may be recoverable despite the existence of an inseparable path between Y and Ry. This challenges the conventional wisdom that if probabilistic relationships cannot be recovered, causal effects cannot either. The authors illustrate this with a compelling example (Example 3) where P(y|do(z)) is recoverable even though P(w,z,y) is not recoverable, using a clever application of do-calculus and d*-separation.

The paper systematically builds a comprehensive theoretical framework with several key advances:
1. Generalized sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that handles cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other
2. New graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2)
3. Necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition (Theorem 6)
4. A rigorous application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, correcting misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia [2013])

The work is highly original in its conceptual approach. While Mohan et al. [2013] established the foundational framework for recoverability, this paper makes a substantial leap by showing the disconnect between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries—a distinction with profound implications for causal inference with missing data. This is not merely an incremental extension but a fundamental conceptual advance that opens new research directions.

The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions, rigorous theorems, and helpful examples. The related work section effectively situates the contribution within the literature, highlighting how it addresses gaps in prior work (particularly the unexplored interactions among missingness mechanisms). The proofs in the appendix lend credibility to the theoretical claims.

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is ubiquitous in real-world applications across healthcare, social sciences, and machine learning. The paper provides a principled framework for determining when causal effects can be recovered from missing data, which has direct practical implications for researchers designing studies and analyzing datasets with missing values. The authors' insight that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not could fundamentally change how researchers approach missing data problems in causal inference.

While the paper is highly technical, this is appropriate for the theoretical contribution it makes. The only minor limitation is that the empirical validation is limited to theoretical examples rather than real-world applications, but given the paper's focus on theoretical foundations, this is understandable and not a major weakness. The authors clearly acknowledge this as a direction for future work.

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance that pushes the boundaries of how we think about missing data and causal inference. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relationships are not is both novel and impactful—it's the kind of conceptual breakthrough that can reshape an entire field. This is precisely the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS values above methodological perfection.

The paper makes a clear, substantial contribution to the literature on missing data and causal inference. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by establishing new theoretical connections between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The framework they present will likely become a foundation for future work on missing data in causal inference.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper offers a significant theoretical advance that is both novel and impactful, directly addressing a fundamental problem in statistical inference with wide practical relevance. The insight about causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic queries are not is particularly compelling and represents a major conceptual contribution to the field.
