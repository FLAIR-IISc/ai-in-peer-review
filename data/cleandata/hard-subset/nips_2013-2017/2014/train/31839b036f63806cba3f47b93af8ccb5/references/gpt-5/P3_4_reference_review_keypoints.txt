- Paper extends m-graphs in four directions: generalized ordered factorization including R, constructive recovery without admissible sequences, non-recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability despite probabilistic inseparability.
- Provides necessary and sufficient results for joint and causal recovery in simple attrition and corrects attrition misconceptions.
- Builds on Mohan et al. (2013), do-calculus, and classical missing-data methods, extending recoverability to MNAR settings.
- Main technical strength: Theorem 1 — generalized ordered factorization admitting R variables and minimal conditioning.
- Main technical strength: Theorem 2 & Corollary 1 — constructive sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) via R partitions and Markov blankets in semi-Markovian models.
- Main technical strength: Theorem 3 & Corollary 2 — clear graphical criteria certifying non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.
- Main technical strength: Theorem 4 — shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable using do-calculus even when probabilistic quantities are not.
- Main technical strength: Theorems 5–6 — tight necessary and sufficient characterizations and explicit estimands for simple attrition.
- Conceptual impact: clarifies when causal inference is possible under MNAR and when probabilistic barriers do not apply.
- Relevance: addresses common longitudinal attrition and missingness beyond MAR/MCAR regimes.
- Weakness: lacks a fully specified, general decision procedure (inputs/outputs, pseudo-code, complexity) for recoverability in Section 4.
- Weakness: m-graphs disallow R→V edges, limiting applicability where feedback from processes to measurements exists.
- Weakness: clarity/notation issues — need algorithm to compute minimal conditioning sets and streamline overloaded notation.
- Weakness: Theorem 2’s construction is hard to follow without a worked example and step-by-step derivation.
- Weakness: several malformed references and heavy reliance on figures/appendix for essential exposition.
- Weakness: no empirical validation — missing simulations or case studies comparing to IPW/MI/EM under MNAR.
- Weakness: no discussion of robustness or sensitivity to m-graph misspecification and practical guidance for sensitivity analysis.
- Technical assessment: claims are well-grounded in graphical causal semantics, but some results are only sufficient and a unifying completeness result is missing.
- Clarity suggestions: include a running example, algorithmic checklists/pseudo-code for key procedures, and consistent notation table.
- Originality: novel and non-trivial extensions beyond prior m-graph work, especially admissible sequences including R and causal recoverability results.
- Significance: high potential impact for theory and practice in causal/missing-data analysis, particularly for longitudinal/attrition problems.
- Suggestions: provide a sound/complete decision procedure or clarify limits, give pseudo-code and complexity, advise on choosing separating sets, and add synthetic experiments and sensitivity guidance.
- Pros: solid theoretical advances, practical recoverability/non-recoverability criteria, and important insight that causal estimands can be recoverable when probabilities are not.
- Cons: limited algorithmic detail, no empirical validation, reliance on accurate m-graphs, and some clarity issues.
- Recommendation: lean accept, conditional on clarifying algorithms, tightening notation, and preferably adding a small empirical illustration.
