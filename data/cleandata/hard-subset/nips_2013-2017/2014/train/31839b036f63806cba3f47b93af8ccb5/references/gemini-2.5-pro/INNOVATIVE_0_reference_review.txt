**Summary**

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), for analyzing the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building directly upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013), the authors significantly extend the theory of recoverability. The primary contributions include: (1) A generalized "sequential factorization" theorem that can handle dependencies between different missingness mechanisms, a common scenario in longitudinal studies. (2) A novel theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables. (3) A set of graphical criteria for establishing the *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions, providing crucial negative results. (4) The paper's most striking contribution: demonstrating that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, effectively decoupling causal and statistical recoverability. Finally, these theoretical results are applied to the challenging problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding new necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that correct prior claims in the literature.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is an exceptionally strong theoretical paper that addresses the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data with a bold and novel approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper's core strength lies in its conceptual contributions. The idea that one can recover a causal effect even when the joint distribution of the involved variables is unrecoverable is a profound and non-intuitive insight. This challenges the standard pipeline where one first aims to recover the full data distribution before making causal claims. This is precisely the kind of bold, paradigm-shifting work that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Introducing Non-Recoverability Criteria:** A significant portion of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for identification or estimation. This paper takes the important step of providing graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). This is of immense practical importance, as it gives researchers formal tools to recognize when their assumptions are insufficient to answer a query, preventing misguided attempts at estimation. This is a novel and highly valuable contribution.

3.  **Powerful Generalizations:** The paper does not merely make incremental improvements. It substantially generalizes previous results, for example by extending sequential factorization to graphs with interacting R-variables (Theorem 1) and providing recovery methods for graphs that lack an admissible sequence altogether (Theorem 2). This demonstrates a deep command of the subject and opens up a much wider class of real-world missing data problems to formal analysis.

4.  **Clarity through Examples:** Despite the technical density of the material, the authors make excellent use of small, illustrative graphical examples (e.g., Figures 2, 3, 4) to motivate their theorems and build intuition. The step-by-step derivation in Example 3, showing the recovery of P(y|do(z)), is particularly effective at conveying the paper's central message about causal queries.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are significant, there are no experiments on either synthetic or real-world data to demonstrate the practical application of the proposed recovery algorithms. Showing how these estimation procedures perform with finite samples, or applying them to a real dataset to recover a causal effect under attrition, would have made the paper's impact more immediate.

2.  **High Barrier to Entry:** The paper is dense and assumes significant familiarity with the language of causal inference (do-calculus, d-separation, inducing paths, m-graphs). While appropriate for the subject matter, this may limit its accessibility to the broader machine learning audience who may be less steeped in this specific formalism.

**Recommendation**

Despite the lack of empirical results, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. My recommendation is driven by the paper's exceptional novelty and the significance of its conceptual contributions. The primary goal of a top-tier conference like NIPS should be to highlight and promote bold new ideas, even if they are not yet fully mature in their empirical validation. This paper offers a fundamentally new perspective on the interplay between missing data, statistical estimation, and causal inference. The discovery that causal queries can be "easier" to recover than their statistical counterparts is a major finding. Furthermore, the development of formal non-recoverability criteria is a critical and previously missing piece in the practitioner's toolkit.

While empirical validation is certainly a desirable feature, for a paper making such a foundational theoretical contribution, its absence is a minor flaw that can be addressed in future work. The strengths—particularly the originality and potential for long-term impact on how we handle missing data—far outweigh this weakness. This work provides a rigorous and powerful framework that future researchers are highly likely to build upon.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a profound and novel insight: causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not.
*   Introduces the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability, a crucial tool for practitioners.
*   Significantly generalizes existing theory, expanding the scope of problems that can be formally analyzed.
*   Addresses a fundamental, cross-cutting problem in science and machine learning (missing data, especially MNAR).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The work is purely theoretical, with no experiments or simulations to validate the proposed estimators in practice.
