- Overall: Strong theoretical extension of m-graphs for recovering probabilistic and causal queries; recommend accept with minor revisions focusing on practical implications.
- Main idea: Generalizes sequential factorization and recoverability conditions, showing causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic quantities are not.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Mohan et al. (2013) by handling interacting missingness mechanisms and formalizing non-recoverability.
- Strength — Theoretical rigor: Clear definitions, theorems, and proofs supporting the new results.
- Strength — Novelty: Demonstrates the novel and important finding that causal queries may be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships.
- Strength — Non-recoverability formalization: Provides explicit criteria for when recoverability fails.
- Strength — Application to attrition: Connects theory to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.
- Strength — Notation: Generally clear and consistent notation aiding comprehension.
- Weakness — Limited empirical validation: Lacks simulation or real-data experiments comparing practical performance to standard methods.
- Weakness — Complexity: Dense theoretical exposition may be hard for readers unfamiliar with m-graphs and causal inference.
- Weakness — Practical considerations: Insufficient discussion of learning m-graphs, estimating missingness mechanisms, and verifying assumptions.
- Weakness — Attrition example scope: Attrition discussion is brief and could be expanded to cover varied scenarios.
- Quality (NIPS criteria): Technically sound with rigorous proofs but would benefit from empirical support.
- Clarity (NIPS criteria): Generally clear but theoretical density reduces accessibility; needs more intuitive examples.
- Originality (NIPS criteria): Highly original extension to m-graphs and recoverability theory.
- Significance (NIPS criteria): Potentially significant for causal inference with missing data, enabling inference under weaker conditions.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical contribution, rigorous framework, enables causal inference in challenging missing-data scenarios, and relevant application to attrition.
- Arguments against acceptance: No empirical validation, high theoretical complexity, and limited treatment of practical graph-learning challenges.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; add simulations, expand attrition examples, discuss m-graph learning/assumption verification, and include a future work section.
