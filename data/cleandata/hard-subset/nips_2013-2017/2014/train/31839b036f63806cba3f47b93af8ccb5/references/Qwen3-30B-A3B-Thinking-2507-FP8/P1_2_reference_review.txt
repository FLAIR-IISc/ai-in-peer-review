

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary

This paper introduces a formal graphical framework called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to represent missing data processes and determine conditions under which probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from datasets with missing values. The authors extend Mohan et al. (2013) by providing general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions), demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not, and applying their results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies. The paper makes several key contributions: a refined sequential factorization theorem, general algorithms for recovering distributions, new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and a demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not separable.

This work builds on the growing literature at NIPS on graphical models for causal inference (e.g., Pearl, 2009; Shpitser and Pearl, 2006), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by formally addressing the complex interplay between missing data mechanisms and causal queries. The paper relates well to recent work on graphical models for missing data (Mohan et al., 2013; Mohan and Pearl, 2014) while extending it substantially.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-defined concepts, precise theorems, and rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix). The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper makes a compelling case that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not, which challenges conventional wisdom about missing data. 

The theoretical framework is well-constructed and the examples (particularly Example 3 demonstrating recoverability of P(y|do(z)) despite Y and Ry not being separable) effectively illustrate the key insights. The paper also provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in specific cases (e.g., Theorem 5 for simple attrition), which is valuable for practitioners.

One notable strength is the careful distinction between the conditions required for probabilistic versus causal recoverability. This is a significant contribution to the missing data literature, which has traditionally treated these as equivalent problems.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from foundational concepts to more complex results. The notation is consistent and well-explained (though some concepts like d*-separation may require background in causal inference). The graphical examples (Figures 1-4) are helpful, though they could benefit from clearer labeling of the missingness mechanisms.

The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem and explaining the significance of the contributions. The examples (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) are well-chosen to illustrate the key concepts. However, some readers unfamiliar with causal inference might struggle with the more technical aspects of the paper, particularly the relationship between the graphical criteria and the recovery algorithms.

## Originality

This paper makes several important original contributions. The most significant is the demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not—a key insight that challenges prevailing assumptions in the missing data literature. The graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) are novel and fill an important gap in the literature.

The extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (Theorem 1) is also a substantial contribution. While Mohan et al. (2013) introduced m-graphs, this paper significantly extends their framework to handle a wider range of missing data mechanisms.

The paper distinguishes itself clearly from previous work by focusing on graphical criteria rather than statistical methods for missing data, and by making the crucial distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

## Significance

The problem of missing data is pervasive across scientific disciplines, making this work highly significant. The paper provides a formal framework that researchers can use to determine whether their queries can be recovered from datasets with missing values, potentially preventing erroneous conclusions.

The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly relevant to longitudinal studies, which are common in fields like epidemiology and social sciences. The paper corrects a prevalent misconception in the literature (regarding Garcia, 2013) by showing that X being an ancestor of Ry is not a necessary condition for non-recoverability.

While the paper is purely theoretical, it provides a foundation for developing new statistical methods for missing data. The graphical criteria could be implemented in software tools to help researchers assess whether their queries can be recovered from missing data.

## Strengths

1. Clear distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries
2. Novel insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not
3. Precise graphical criteria for non-recoverability
4. Application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
5. Careful extension of Mohan et al. (2013) to handle more complex missing data mechanisms
6. Well-structured theoretical development with supporting examples

## Weaknesses

1. Lacks empirical validation - the paper is purely theoretical with no experiments or applications to real datasets
2. Limited discussion of computational complexity of the recovery algorithms
3. Insufficient connection to existing statistical methods for missing data (e.g., multiple imputation)
4. The paper could better clarify the relationship between the graphical criteria and practical implementation
5. Some concepts (e.g., d*-separation) may be inaccessible to readers without background in causal inference

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis, with clear implications for both theory and practice. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly novel and important. While the paper is purely theoretical, it provides a rigorous foundation that could guide future methodological developments.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in data analysis with important practical implications across scientific disciplines. The technical content is sound, the contributions are well-motivated, and the paper is well-written. The only notable weakness is the lack of empirical validation, but this is understandable given the theoretical nature of the contribution.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the graphical criteria could be implemented in software (e.g., as a package in R or Python) to help practitioners apply the results.
2. Include a comparison with existing methods for handling missing data (e.g., multiple imputation) to clarify the relationship between this theoretical framework and practical approaches.
3. Add a sentence or two explaining d*-separation for readers unfamiliar with causal inference terminology.
4. Consider a brief discussion of computational complexity for the recovery algorithms, as this would be important for practitioners.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when queries can be recovered from missing data. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly noteworthy and will likely influence future work in the field.
