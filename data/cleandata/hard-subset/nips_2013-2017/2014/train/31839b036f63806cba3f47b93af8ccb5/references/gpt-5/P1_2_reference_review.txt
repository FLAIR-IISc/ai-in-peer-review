Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends the graphical approach to missing data introduced by Mohan, Pearl and Tian (NIPS 2013) by providing new sufficient and necessary conditions for the recoverability of probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and causal queries from incomplete data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). The main contributions are: (i) a generalization of the “ordered factorization” approach to include missingness indicators R in the ordering (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms influence each other; (ii) new sufficient conditions to recover joint distributions when no admissible factorization over substantive variables exists (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1), using partitions of R nodes and Markov blankets connected via collider paths in semi-Markovian models (ADMGs); (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) that give necessary conditions for joint and conditional recoverability—importantly providing “no-go” tests that were lacking; (iv) recovery of causal effects when probabilistic recovery fails, including cases where Y and its missingness Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4 via dormant independence/d*-separation), and (v) necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recoverability in the simple attrition case (|Vm|=1) (Theorems 5 and 6), with an explicit estimand. These results connect to and refine prior work on identifiability via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006/2008), MAR/MCAR (Rubin), and ADMG separation (Richardson 2003). The paper also corrects claims in Garcia (2013) regarding attrition.

Quality:
The technical development is largely sound and carefully tied to graphical criteria. The extension of ordered factorizations to include R variables with minimal conditioning sets is well-motivated and the example illustrates why minimality is required. Theorem 2 provides a nontrivial sufficient condition for joint recoverability in semi-Markovian models using collider-path structure and R partitions; Corollary 1 covers a useful subclass without latent parents of V/R. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable and intuitive. The causal section correctly separates identifiability from recoverability and demonstrates that P(y|do(x)) may be estimable even when P(V) or P(y|x) is not. The attrition results (Theorems 5–6) give clean necessary-and-sufficient tests and estimands. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; offering brief proof sketches in the main text could further build confidence.

A few technical points merit clarification:
- The conditions in Theorem 2 are somewhat opaque; an algorithmic description for constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)) and checking the condition R(i) ∩ R_Mb(R(i))=∅ would aid reproducibility.
- The positivity requirement is stated (strictly positive over complete cases); the practical implications for factors like P(R(i)=0 | Mb(R(i)), R_Mb=0) when some strata are rare should be discussed.
- The relationship/differences between “all-collider path” (Theorems 3, 5) and “inducing path” (Theorem 6) could be emphasized, as the latter is stronger; examples illustrating the gap would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized with motivating examples, though notation is dense. Suggestions:
- Fix notational typos: e.g., “GX and GX represent graphs…” likely meant “G^X and G_X” (removing outgoing vs. incoming edges); define these precisely.
- Consolidate shorthand (X′ for X=0, sets Wo, Wm, Wr) and minimize overloading of V/V∗/V.
- Provide a short algorithmic pipeline: (1) check identifiability; (2) search for admissible sequence with R; (3) if none, apply Theorem 2/Corollary 1; (4) test non-recoverability via Theorem 3; (5) if causal, attempt Theorem 4.
- Some figures (referenced as Fig. 2–4) are essential to understanding; ensure they are clear and include the relevant separating sets in captions.

Originality:
The work goes beyond Mohan et al. (2013) by: allowing R–R dependencies in factorization; providing general sufficient conditions in semi-Markovian models beyond cases where Pa(R) ⊆ V; giving explicit non-recoverability criteria; and treating causal recoverability when Y and Ry are inseparable, including necessary-and-sufficient conditions under attrition. To my knowledge, these non-recoverability criteria and the attrition characterizations are new and useful.

Significance:
Recoverability with MNAR mechanisms is a central challenge in causal and probabilistic inference. The graphical conditions and estimands provided here can guide analysts in determining when consistent estimation is possible and how to proceed, and they correct misconceptions in the literature. The results should stimulate further algorithmic development and practical application. Impact would be amplified by more guidance on automated application (search procedures) and empirical demonstrations.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled extensions of graphical recoverability with rigorous conditions.
- Valuable non-recoverability criteria.
- Novel results showing causal effects can be recovered despite inseparable Y–Ry.
- Clean necessary-and-sufficient results for simple attrition with explicit estimands.
- Good integration with do-calculus and ADMG theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Algorithmic aspects are under-specified: provide pseudo-code for finding admissible sequences and for constructing R partitions/Mb(R(i)).
- No empirical validation; even small simulations demonstrating performance of the proposed estimands and illustrating failure cases would strengthen the submission.
- Some conditions (Theorem 2) are complex; add intuition, worked examples, and counterexamples.
- Notational/typographical issues and some reference formatting need attention.

Questions for authors:
1) Can you provide complexity bounds or heuristics for searching admissible sequences with R included?
2) How robust are the estimands to mild violations of the assumed m-graph (model misspecification)?
3) Can Theorem 6 be extended beyond |Vm|=1, or are there fundamental obstacles?
4) Could you include a tool or checklist for practitioners to apply the criteria to real graphs?

Minor issues:
- Clean up reference formatting (many entries have placeholders like “? \Q2013\E”).
- Clarify definition of d*-separation and how practitioners can verify its conditions.
- Ensure all notation (e.g., GX,W1) is defined.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Pros:
- Solid theoretical advances; fills gaps (non-recoverability, causal recoverability beyond separability).
- Practical relevance (attrition) with necessary-and-sufficient conditions and estimands.
- Extends a recognized NIPS line of work.

Cons:
- Limited algorithmic detail and no empirical validation.
- Dense notation and some clarity/typo issues.

With minor revisions to improve clarity and add algorithmic guidance (and ideally a small empirical illustration), this will be a valuable contribution.
