Summary
The paper advances the graphical approach to missing data by extending “missingness graphs” (m-graphs) to derive broad, checkable conditions for when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from incomplete data. Key contributions:

- Generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1): Extends the ordered factorization criterion of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) to include missingness indicators R in the admissible ordering, with a minimality condition. This allows recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (edges among R’s), which defeated earlier results.

- Recoverability without an admissible sequence (Theorem 2 + Corollary 1): Provides a new factorization based on partitions of R (by absence of adjacency or collider paths) and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)). Yields closed-form expressions for P(V) in semi-Markovian models where prior theorems fail.

- Non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 + Corollary 2): Introduces necessary graphical conditions that preclude recovery of joint and conditional distributions. These negative results are valuable to decide when assumptions are insufficient.

- Causal queries recoverable when probabilistic ones are not (Theorem 4 and Example 3): Shows P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y is inseparable from its missingness, leveraging do-calculus, dormant independence, and d*-separation. This clarifies a key conceptual gap between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

- Attrition (Theorems 5–6): For |Vm|=1, gives necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects under “simple attrition,” correcting common misconceptions (e.g., X need not be an ancestor of Ry for non-recoverability).

Relation to prior work
Builds directly on Mohan, Pearl, Tian (NIPS 2013) by relaxing assumptions and broadening applicability. Uses standard causal identification tools (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; Pearl 2009) and connects with MAR/MCAR and CAR literatures (Rubin; Heitjan & Rubin). The explicit negative criteria and the demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable when joint/conditional distributions are not represent a notable conceptual advance. The attrition results relate to and refine Garcia (2013) and align with concerns raised by Thoemmes & Rose.

Strengths
- Novelty: The generalized factorization including R, the R-partition/Mb construction, and the causal-versus-probabilistic recoverability separation are original and important.
- Breadth: Results cover semi-Markovian models with interacting missingness mechanisms and attrition scenarios, with both positive and negative criteria.
- Conceptual clarity: The explicit use of collider-path structure and dormant independence offers crisp, testable graph conditions.
- Potential impact: Provides practitioners with constructive recoverability formulas and “no-go” tests, likely to influence both methodology and practice in ML/causal inference with MNAR data.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/notation: The paper is notation-dense. Some definitions need polishing. For example:
  - “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively.” This appears to be a typo; please disambiguate (e.g., G\out(X) vs G\in(X)).
  - In Sec. 2, “∀X, X ⊆ Vm” likely means “for all X ⊆ Vm.”
  - d*-separation notation in Def. 4 (X ⊥w Y | Z) and the role of W should be clarified carefully.
- Algorithmic concreteness: While theorems provide constructive estimands, the “general algorithms” are not presented as a unified decision procedure. It would help to:
  - Provide pseudocode that, given an m-graph, decides recoverability of P(V) and P(y|do(x)) by trying Theorem 1, then Theorem 2, then Theorem 3/Corollary 2, and for causal queries, Theorem 4/ID-based steps.
  - Discuss computational complexity of checking the conditions (e.g., building R-partitions, detecting collider paths, computing Mb(R(i))).
- Scope/assumptions: The m-graph assumption that R nodes are not parents of V ∪ U is standard in this line but should be discussed. Are there realistic scenarios where missingness can causally influence substantive variables (e.g., dropout affecting subsequent measurement behavior), and how would results adapt?
- Empirical illustration: Even small synthetic experiments illustrating the estimands’ performance and robustness to near-violations of positivity would increase accessibility.
- References/minor: Shpitser & Pearl (2008) is cited but not in the references; please add. Several references have formatting issues. Ensure figures referenced (e.g., Fig. 2–4) are self-contained and legible.

Quality
Theoretical claims are well motivated and consistent with the cited causal inference toolkit. Proofs are in the appendix; the main text provides intuition and worked examples. Some details (algorithmic instantiation, complexity) are thin but not critical for a theory paper.

Clarity
Good overall structure; however, readability suffers from dense notation and occasional typos. More signposting, a glossary of symbols, and a short running example throughout would help.

Originality
High. The extension to interacting missingness mechanisms, the general non-recoverability criteria, and especially the demonstration that causal effects can be recovered when associated probabilistic relations cannot, represent substantial conceptual advances beyond prior work.

Significance
High. The results offer practical, testable conditions and constructive estimands for MNAR settings, a central challenge in ML and causal inference. The attrition results are timely and correct misconceptions in applied fields.

Questions for the authors
- Can your conditions be checked and estimands derived automatically? How does your approach relate to the ID/IDC algorithms when augmented with R nodes and proxy variables?
- How sensitive are your estimands to positivity violations, and can your framework incorporate standard positivity diagnostics?
- Can Theorem 4 be generalized beyond |Vm|=1, and if so, what additional obstacles arise?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contributions with clear practical implications.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recoverability in a nontrivial way.
- Negative results useful to practitioners.
- Corrects prevailing misconceptions on attrition.

Cons for acceptance
- Dense presentation; some notational issues.
- Lacks a unified algorithm and empirical illustration.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces bold, original ideas that substantially extend the state of the art on recoverability under missing data, with high potential impact despite minor clarity and presentation issues.
