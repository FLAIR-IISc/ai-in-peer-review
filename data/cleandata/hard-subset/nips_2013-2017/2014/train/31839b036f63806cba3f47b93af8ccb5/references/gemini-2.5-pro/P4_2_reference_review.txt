This paper presents a formal graphical framework for determining the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the "m-graph" representation introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], the work addresses the challenging problem of Missing Not At Random (MNAR) data, where the missingness mechanism depends on the missing values themselves. The paper's primary contributions are: (1) an extension of the sequential factorization theorem to accommodate dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) a novel theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness variables; (3) new graphical criteria that serve as necessary conditions for recoverability, allowing researchers to identify non-recoverable queries; and (4) a key result showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint or conditional probabilities are not. Finally, these theoretical results are applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The contributions are presented as a series of formal theorems, with proofs provided in the appendix. The work builds logically on established frameworks, including the m-graphs of Mohan et al. [2013] and the do-calculus and d*-separation concepts from the causal inference literature (Pearl [2009], Shpitser and Pearl [2008]). The claims appear well-supported by the theoretical analysis provided. The paper is a complete piece of theoretical work, advancing the formal understanding of inference with missing data.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions. The extension of the factorization theorem to handle inter-dependencies among R-variables (Theorem 1) broadens the applicability of the original framework. The non-recoverability criteria presented in Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 are particularly original and important; while much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for identifiability, providing formal necessary conditions is a significant and practical contribution that helps define the limits of what can be learned from data. The demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not (Section 6) is a non-trivial insight that distinguishes this work from much of the classical missing data literature, which primarily focuses on recovering joint or conditional distributions.

**Significance:** The results presented are potentially of high significance for both researchers and practitioners dealing with missing data, a ubiquitous problem in machine learning and experimental sciences. The graphical criteria provide an intuitive yet formal language for encoding assumptions about the missingness process and for diagnosing recoverability before attempting estimation. The ability to recover causal effects in MNAR settings where standard distributional recovery fails (as shown in Example 3) could enable valid causal conclusions in studies (e.g., clinical trials with attrition) that might otherwise be considered intractable. The application to attrition provides concrete, new necessary and sufficient conditions that challenge and refine existing results in that specific domain (e.g., Garcia [2013]). This work advances the state of the art in the formal analysis of missing data beyond the common MAR assumption.

### Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper's primary weakness is its lack of empirical validation. The contributions are entirely theoretical. While the derivations are presented formally, the paper would be substantially strengthened by including simulation studies. For instance, a simulation based on the model in Figure 3 could demonstrate that the proposed estimator for P(y|do(z)) converges to the true value as sample size increases, while a naive approach (e.g., listwise deletion or direct estimation of P(y|z,w)) would be biased. Such an experiment would not only validate the theoretical claims but also illustrate their practical implications and provide a template for implementation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and structured, but some sections are dense. The new concepts introduced in Section 4 for Theorem 2 (collider path, R(part), Mb(R(i))) are defined very concisely. A more detailed, step-by-step example illustrating the construction of the R-variable partitions and their Markov blankets for the graph in Figure 2(b) would greatly improve the reader's understanding of this central theorem. While the examples provided are helpful, they primarily state the results of applying the theorems rather than walking the reader through the application process.

The scope of the positivity assumption (P(Vo, Vm, R=0) > 0) is mentioned but not discussed in detail. A brief discussion on the practical implications of this assumption and the robustness of the methods to near-violations would be beneficial, as the probability of observing a fully complete case can be very low in datasets with many partially observed variables.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance:** Addresses the fundamental and difficult problem of inference with non-ignorable missing data (MNAR).
2.  **Strong Originality:** Introduces novel and important non-recoverability criteria and demonstrates the surprising result that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying joint distribution is not.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The work is grounded in formal graphical models and causality theory, presenting a rigorous theoretical advancement.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** Provides tools that can help researchers formally reason about their missing data assumptions and determine the feasibility of their target queries.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical, with no simulation studies or real-data experiments to validate the proposed recovery formulae and demonstrate their practical utility.
2.  **Clarity Could Be Improved:** Some of the key theoretical concepts, particularly in Section 4, are presented in a very dense manner that could be difficult for readers to parse without more illustrative examples.

Overall, this paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the fields of graphical models, causal inference, and missing data analysis. The originality and significance of the results, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the findings on causal query recovery, are compelling. While the lack of experiments is a notable weakness, the theoretical depth and importance of the contributions make a strong case for acceptance.
