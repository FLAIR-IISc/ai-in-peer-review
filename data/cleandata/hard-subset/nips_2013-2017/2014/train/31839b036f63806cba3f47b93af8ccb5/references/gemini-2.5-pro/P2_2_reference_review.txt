### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called Missingness Graphs (m-graphs), to analyze the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the authors' previous work (Mohan et al., 2013), this paper makes several key contributions. First, it generalizes the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, for cases where factorization fails, it provides a more general recovery algorithm based on partitioning the missingness variables and their Markov blankets. Third, and notably, it introduces novel graphical criteria for determining when a query is *non-recoverable*, a crucial and often overlooked aspect. Fourth, it demonstrates the powerful and non-intuitive result that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. Finally, it applies these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting what it claims are misconceptions in existing literature.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality: (Strong)**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are presented as formal theorems, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The arguments are logically structured, building from simpler sufficient conditions (sequential factorization) to more general ones, and then supplementing these with necessary conditions (non-recoverability criteria). The provided examples (e.g., Example 1, 3) are effective at illustrating the necessity of the new theoretical machinery and clarifying complex concepts. The work appears to be a complete and mature piece of theoretical research.

A minor weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While this is a theoretical paper, even synthetic experiments demonstrating the proposed recovery algorithms and showing their convergence to the true query value would have strengthened the claims. Furthermore, the paper assumes the m-graph is correctly specified, which is a strong assumption in practice. While the authors cite other work on testing m-graphs, a brief discussion of the sensitivity of their recovery algorithms to model misspecification would have been valuable.

**Clarity: (Good)**
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly lays out the paper's contributions. Each section tackles a specific aspect of the problem, and the progression is easy to follow. The notation, while dense, is consistent and defined upfront.

However, the paper is highly technical and assumes significant familiarity with the language of causal graphical models (d-separation, do-calculus, inducing paths, d*-separation). For a reader not deeply embedded in this literature, parts of the paper, particularly Sections 4 and 6, could be challenging to parse. For instance, the definitions of `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` in Theorem 2 are presented very concisely and could benefit from a more intuitive lead-in. While the examples help, the core definitions remain dense.

**Originality: (Very Strong)**
The paper makes several original and significant contributions. While it extends previous work, the new results are far from trivial.
1.  **Non-recoverability Criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2):** This is a standout contribution. Most literature focuses on sufficient conditions for identification or recovery. Providing simple, graphical necessary conditions for non-recoverability is extremely valuable for practitioners, as it allows them to quickly diagnose when a query is fundamentally unanswerable with the given assumptions.
2.  **Causal vs. Probabilistic Recovery (Section 6):** The demonstration that `P(y|do(x))` can be recovered even when the joint `P(y,x)` is not is a profound and important insight. It elegantly connects the theory of missing data with the deeper structure of causal identification (via d*-separation). This result challenges the common intuition that one must first recover the full joint distribution to answer any query about it.
3.  **Generalization of Recovery Algorithms (Theorems 1 & 2):** These are solid, incremental contributions that significantly broaden the class of problems the m-graph framework can handle, particularly those with interacting missingness mechanisms common in longitudinal studies.

The work is well-situated in the literature, clearly delineating how it moves beyond classical MAR/MCAR assumptions and builds upon the graphical modeling approach to causality and missing data.

**Significance: (Strong)**
The problem of missing data is fundamental and ubiquitous across all empirical sciences, including machine learning. This paper provides a principled, formal framework for reasoning about this problem under complex, "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) scenarios. The results have high potential impact:
*   They provide researchers with tools to formally articulate their assumptions about missingness and algorithmically determine if their research question is answerable.
*   The non-recoverability criteria can prevent researchers from pursuing fruitless analyses or using methods (like naive imputation or listwise deletion) that would produce biased results.
*   The findings on causal query recovery could unlock new possibilities for causal inference in fields like medicine and social sciences, where attrition and other complex missingness patterns are common. By showing that direct causal estimation is sometimes possible without recovering the full joint, this work offers a potential bypass to otherwise intractable problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, high-impact problem with a novel and powerful theoretical framework.
*   The contributions, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the separation of causal from probabilistic recoverability, are original and significant.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly articulates its results.
*   It advances the state-of-the-art in the formal analysis of missing data beyond the standard MAR/MCAR assumptions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is purely theoretical, with no experiments to validate the proposed algorithms or explore their practical performance and robustness.
*   The high technical barrier to entry might limit its immediate accessibility to the broader NIPS audience not specialized in causal inference.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a significant theoretical contribution to an important problem. The originality of the non-recoverability criteria and the insights into causal queries are particularly impressive. While the lack of experiments is a limitation, the theoretical depth and potential impact make this a very strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The work is likely to be influential and to spur further research at the intersection of causality, graphical models, and machine learning under data imperfections. I strongly recommend acceptance.
