This paper addresses the important problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, utilizing and extending the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors build upon previous work by refining sequential factorization theorems and developing new criteria for recoverability, particularly focusing on scenarios where standard approaches fail. A key contribution lies in demonstrating that causal queries, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not, and even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism – a significant result. The application to attrition problems is also a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of recoverability analysis to cases where causal queries are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relations is a genuinely novel and important contribution. This challenges the conventional wisdom regarding the limitations imposed by missing data.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents several theorems and corollaries with clear proofs (in the appendix), demonstrating a strong theoretical foundation. The introduction of "general ordered factorization" and theorems 2 & 5 demonstrate a careful refinement of existing concepts.
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The authors thoroughly explore various scenarios, including those where standard factorization methods fail and introduce graphical criteria for non-recoverability, providing a more complete picture of the problem.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems, a common challenge in observational studies, showcases the practical utility of the proposed framework.  The discussion of simple attrition and its implications is well-articulated.
* **Clear Connections to Prior Work:** The paper effectively contextualizes its contributions within the existing literature on missing data, causal inference, and graphical models, citing relevant papers and building upon established concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Readability:** While mathematically sound, the paper can be dense and difficult to follow for readers not intimately familiar with the m-graph framework and the do-calculus. The numerous definitions and theorems, while rigorous, are presented in a somewhat compact manner. Improved exposition and more illustrative examples could greatly enhance readability.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on theoretical results. While the example illustrating the recoverability of causal effects is helpful, a more extensive empirical evaluation, even with simulated data, would strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed methods.  This is especially crucial for assessing the impact of violations of the assumptions underlying the theorems.
* **Complexity of Theorems:** Some of the theorems (e.g., Theorem 2 and its associated definitions) are quite complex, making them challenging to apply in practice.  A more streamlined presentation or a decision procedure based on the theorems could improve usability.
* **Relationship to Existing Literature on Causal Inference:**  While the paper references relevant work in causal inference, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the proposed approach relates to existing techniques for handling missing data in causal models, such as multiple imputation with propensity score weighting.
* **Appendix Reliance:** The extensive reliance on the appendix for proofs, while standard, makes it difficult to quickly assess the validity of the arguments without significant effort. A more concise summary of key proof ideas within the main text could be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the area of missing data and causal inference.
* The core result regarding the recoverability of causal queries under conditions where probabilistic queries are not recoverable is novel and important.
* The paper is well-grounded in existing theory and provides a rigorous framework for analyzing recoverability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s density and lack of empirical validation might limit its immediate impact.
* The complexity of some of the theorems may hinder their practical application.
* The potential benefits of the theory require demonstration via simulation or real-world application.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. The core result regarding the recoverability of causal queries is particularly compelling. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by improved clarity, a more extensive empirical evaluation, and a more accessible presentation of the complex theorems.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with encouragement to revise.** I believe the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS, but the current version requires substantial improvements in clarity and empirical validation to meet the conference’s standards. I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, I recommend:

1.  **Adding illustrative examples throughout the main text** to clarify the concepts and theorems.
2.  **Conducting a simulation study** to demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed methods and validate the theoretical results.
3.  **Simplifying the presentation of complex theorems** and/or developing a decision procedure for their application.
4.  **Providing a more detailed discussion of the relationship to existing approaches** for handling missing data in causal inference.
5. **Including a concise summary of key proof ideas within the main text.**
