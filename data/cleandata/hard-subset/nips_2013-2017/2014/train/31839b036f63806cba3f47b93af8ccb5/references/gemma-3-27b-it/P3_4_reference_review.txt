## NIPS Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and valuable extension to the framework of missingness graphs (m-graphs) for handling missing data, particularly focusing on recoverability of causal queries. The work is technically sound, builds logically on previous research (Mohan et al., Pearl), and addresses a critical problem in observational and experimental studies. While the presentation is dense and requires a solid background in graphical models and causal inference, the contributions are substantial and warrant acceptance.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper extends the work on m-graphs by establishing broader conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries when data is missing. It refines the sequential factorization theorem, presenting a more general version applicable to scenarios with interacting missingness mechanisms. Crucially, it demonstrates that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered *even when* the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x), P(y|x)) are not identifiable, a significant and often overlooked point.  The authors connect these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition, illustrating how their framework can be applied to identify recoverable causal effects in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a strong theoretical foundation using the framework of m-graphs and do-calculus. The theorems are clearly stated and accompanied by detailed proofs in the appendix.
* **Novelty & Significance:**  The key contribution – the recoverability of causal effects even with non-recoverable probabilistic relationships – is novel and potentially impactful.  It addresses a critical limitation of traditional missing data analysis which often prioritizes estimating joint distributions.  This allows for causal inference in situations previously considered intractable.
* **Generalization of Existing Work:**  The generalized sequential factorization theorem expands the applicability of previous work by handling more complex missingness scenarios, particularly those with interactions between missingness indicators.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition highlights the practical utility of the framework, demonstrating its potential for addressing real-world research challenges.
* **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of relevant literature, positioning their work within the broader context of missing data analysis, causal inference, and graphical models.  They correctly identify the limitations of existing methods (e.g., ML, MAR assumptions) and highlight how their approach offers advantages.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Density:** The paper is quite dense and requires significant prior knowledge of graphical models, causal inference, and m-graphs.  The notation can be challenging, and the flow of arguments sometimes feels rapid.  Readers unfamiliar with the background may struggle to follow the derivations.
* **Example Presentation:** While the examples are helpful in illustrating the concepts, they could be made even more accessible.  A visual depiction of the m-graphs alongside the derivations would aid understanding. Some of the examples (e.g., Example 3) jump directly into applying the theorems without sufficient intuitive explanation.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper lacks empirical validation.  Simulations or analysis of real-world datasets demonstrating the performance of the proposed methods would strengthen the work significantly.  A discussion on computational complexity and scalability would also be beneficial.
* **Corollary 1's Condition:** The stated condition in Corollary 1,  `R(i) ∩RPasub(R(i)) = ∅`, feels somewhat restrictive.  It would be useful to discuss the implications of this condition and whether it can be relaxed under certain assumptions.



**Detailed Comments and Specific Points:**

* **Definition 2 (General Ordered Factorization):**  The importance of the “minimality” requirement is well-explained with Example 1.
* **Theorem 4:**  The connection to d*-separation and dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008) is insightful.
* **Section 7 (Attrition):** The discussion on attrition is well-structured and clearly demonstrates the application of the theoretical results. However, the claim that the findings "rectify prevailing opinion" (Garcia, 2013) should be tempered; a more nuanced discussion of the differences would be appropriate.
* **Related Work:**  The related work section is thorough and provides a good overview of the field.
* **Typos/Minor Issues:**  A few minor grammatical issues exist (e.g., "P (V ) is recoverable because every term in the right hand side is consistently estimable…").




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant theoretical advance in handling missing data, particularly for causal inference.**
* **Addresses a critical gap in the literature by demonstrating recoverability of causal effects even when probabilistic relationships are not identifiable.**
* **Well-grounded in established frameworks (m-graphs, do-calculus).**
* **Strong potential impact on fields relying on observational and experimental data.**

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **High level of technical complexity may limit accessibility.**
* **Lack of empirical validation.**
* **Density of presentation could benefit from simplification and more illustrative examples.**



**Overall Score:** 8.5 / 10

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the paper’s theoretical contributions and potential impact are substantial. The complexity can be mitigated by careful reading, and the lack of empirical validation doesn’t invalidate the theoretical soundness of the work.  I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider including some simulations or real-world analyses in future work to demonstrate the practical benefits of their framework.  They should also strive to make the presentation more accessible to a wider audience, perhaps through clearer explanations and more illustrative examples.
