- Paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) with a refined sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3), showing causal P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separated.  
- Provides necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for causal effect recovery in missing-data settings and applies results to attrition in longitudinal studies.  
- Quality: rigorous theoretical contributions with complete proofs and clear distinction between probabilistic vs. causal recoverability.  
- Originality: novel and significant insight that causal recoverability can differ from probabilistic recoverability, correcting prior misconceptions.  
- Clarity: well-organized, consistent notation, effective examples, though d*-separation could be explained in more detail.  
- Significance: important for practice—formal framework for when queries can be recovered under missingness, with implications for many scientific domains.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation on real or simulated datasets to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity, implementation, and scalability for large datasets.  
- Weakness: could better contrast with additional related work in causal inference (e.g., Van der Laan, Robins).  
- Weakness: attrition application is narrow, focusing on simple cases with a single partially observed variable.  
- Recommendation: recommend acceptance—strong theoretical contribution despite empirical gaps.  
- Pro-accept: first formal graphical criteria for causal recovery with missing data; corrects prior literature; gives precise necessary and sufficient conditions; well-motivated theory with clear examples.  
- Con-accept: lacks empirical validation; omits computational/implementation discussion; limited scope in attrition examples.
