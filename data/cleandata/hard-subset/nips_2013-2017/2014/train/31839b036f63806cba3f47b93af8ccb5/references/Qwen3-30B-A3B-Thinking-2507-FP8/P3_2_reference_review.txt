

# Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

## Summary
This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by developing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs). The authors establish sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint/conditional distributions (Theorem 1, Theorem 2), introduce non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), and make a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4). The paper applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies (Section 7), correcting misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013).

## Quality
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the missing data literature. The theoretical framework is well-developed with rigorous proofs in the appendix. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly valuable, as it challenges the common assumption that causal effects require the same separability conditions as probabilistic queries. The paper correctly identifies that causal queries may circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic recoverability.

However, the paper lacks empirical validation. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of simulations or real-data examples to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed conditions limits the paper's impact. The authors claim their conditions "rectify prevailing opinion" in the literature (Section 7.2), but without empirical demonstrations, this claim remains unverified.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and logically structured, with clear definitions and theorems. The example in Section 3 effectively illustrates the need for minimality in factorization. However, the notation is complex and potentially confusing. Terms like "Rpart" and "Mb(R(i))" are introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, and the distinction between "Rw" and "Wr" may trip up readers. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the graphical concepts and perhaps a more detailed discussion of how to apply the theorems in practice.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the key insight that causal effects may be recoverable without the separability condition required for probabilistic queries. This challenges conventional wisdom in the missing data literature. While the framework builds on Mohan et al. (2013), the extension to causal queries represents a novel theoretical advance. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) also fill an important gap in the literature, as the paper correctly notes that "such criteria have not been treated formally in the literature thus far."

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial for causal inference and missing data research. The results have immediate implications for longitudinal studies with attrition, where the authors demonstrate that prior work (Garcia 2013) contained incorrect assumptions. The paper provides researchers with concrete graphical criteria to assess recoverability of causal effects in the presence of missing data, which could prevent misguided analyses in practice.

However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the absence of computational algorithms or implementation details. The theoretical conditions are valuable, but without guidance on how to practically apply them (e.g., how to identify the separating sets Z in Theorem 4), their utility for practitioners is diminished.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no simulations or real-data examples to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed conditions.
2. **Complex notation**: The paper's notation (e.g., Rpart, Mb(R(i)), Rw vs. Wr) is dense and could be clarified with more intuitive explanations.
3. **No comparison to existing methods**: The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed graphical criteria compare to or integrate with standard missing data methods like multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting.
4. **Computational considerations**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of applying the proposed conditions, which would be important for practical implementation.

## Strengths
1. Clear distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability, with the key insight that causal effects may be recoverable without separability.
2. Formalization of non-recoverability criteria, filling an important gap in the literature.
3. Correcting misconceptions in prior work (Garcia 2013) regarding attrition problems.
4. Well-structured theoretical development with clear examples.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the missing data literature, particularly in establishing the conditions under which causal effects can be recovered from missing data. While the absence of empirical validation and computational considerations limits its immediate practical impact, the theoretical insights are significant and should be of interest to the NIPS community working on causal inference and graphical models.

The paper meets NIPS standards for theoretical contribution but would be stronger with empirical validation. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the clarity issues and consider adding a brief discussion of computational implementation in future work.

**Overall assessment**: Strong theoretical contribution with significant implications for causal inference. The paper advances the state of the art in graphical models for missing data and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
