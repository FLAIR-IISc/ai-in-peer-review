- Generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) allowing ordered factorizations that include interacting R nodes, requiring minimal separating sets for admissibility.  
- New joint-distribution recovery results when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) using partitions of R and Markov blankets, covering semi-Markovian graphs with latent confounding and R–R interactions.  
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) providing necessary conditions that preclude recovery of joint and conditional distributions.  
- Causal-effect recovery (Theorem 4, Example 3): P(y|do(x)) can be identified via do-calculus and dormant independences even when related probabilistic queries are not recoverable.  
- Attrition application (Theorems 5–6): necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) and causal effects when |Vm|=1, correcting prior claims.  
- Substantive extension of Mohan et al. (2013) by handling R–R interactions, adding non-recoverability criteria, and single-missing-variable necessary-and-sufficient results.  
- Technical soundness: claims are plausible and grounded in graphical causal inference; minimal separating sets and partition/Markov-blanket constructions are appropriate.  
- Rigor/completeness concerns: proofs relegated to appendix; some conditions (e.g., Theorem 4 and Rule 3 usage) need more explicit checklists for applicability.  
- Algorithmic gap: Theorem 2 is constructive but no explicit algorithm, step-by-step procedure, or complexity analysis for finding R partitions, Mb(R(i)), and checking conditions.  
- No empirical evaluation: absence of synthetic or real-data demonstrations limits practical validation and understanding of finite-sample/positivity issues.  
- Clarity: generally well organized with clear assumptions and coherent flow from probabilistic to causal queries.  
- Notation and presentation issues: index inconsistencies, ambiguous graph-mutilation notation, unusual shorthand (X′), collider vs inducing-path distinctions, figure and reference formatting problems.  
- Originality: novel extension to R interactions, new non-recoverability criteria, and showing causal recovery despite Y–Ry inseparability are important contributions.  
- Significance: advances missing-data theory in graphical models with constructive estimands useful for methodologists and applied researchers.  
- Suggested broader impact improvements: provide an algorithmic decision/estimand engine, more diverse examples, and discussion of robustness to misspecification and positivity violations.  
- Strengths: principled theoretical extension, new necessary conditions, elegant use of do-calculus/dormant independences, and crisp attrition results.  
- Weaknesses/questions: lacks empirical illustrations, no full decision procedure or computational analysis, unclear constructive method for selecting Z/W1 in Theorem 4, restrictive assumption that R cannot parent V∪U, limited guidance on positivity.  
- Concrete suggestions: add an algorithmic pipeline (input: m-graph, output: recoverability decision + estimand/certificate), include synthetic/real examples, expand Theorem 4 with constructive search methods, and fix notation/references.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical advances, constructive and practically relevant results, alignment with conference interests in causal inference.  
- Arguments against acceptance: perceived incremental advance over Mohan et al. (2013), lack of empirical validation and algorithmic decision procedure, and presentation/notation issues hindering accessibility.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improving clarity and adding an explicit decision/estimand algorithm.
