## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and rigorous extension to the framework of missingness graphs (m-graphs) for reasoning about recoverability of queries under missing data. The authors significantly broaden the applicability of existing results, particularly concerning causal queries, and offer practical guidelines for analyzing recoverability in complex scenarios like attrition. While the theoretical depth is strong, the presentation can be dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples and discussion of practical limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan & Pearl (2014) on m-graphs. It addresses a critical problem in experimental science: biased inferences due to missing data. The core contribution lies in extending the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex missingness mechanisms (where missingness mechanisms influence each other) and, crucially, demonstrating that causal queries, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. This is a significant advance, as it opens the door to identifying causal effects in situations previously considered intractable.  The paper correctly positions itself within the broader literature on missing data handling techniques (ML, IPM, etc.) and highlights the unique advantages of a graphical modelling approach focused on recoverability.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical development is thorough and convincing. The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the introduction of Theorem 2 for handling more complex scenarios are substantial contributions.
* **Causal Inference Focus:** The demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even when variables are inseparable from their missingness mechanism (Section 6) is a key strength and differentiates this work from many existing approaches. This addresses a significant practical limitation.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The application to attrition problems (Section 7) illustrates the real-world utility of the framework.  The discussion of simple attrition and the conditions for recoverability are valuable for practitioners.
* **Clear Definition of Recoverability:** The paper consistently grounds its work in the well-defined notion of recoverability, providing a solid theoretical foundation.
* **Non-Recoverability Criteria:** The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 & Corollary 2) are valuable additions, providing practical guidance for assessing the feasibility of recovery.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Density and Readability:** The presentation is highly theoretical and can be challenging to follow, even for researchers familiar with causal inference and graphical models.  More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples (beyond those already provided) would significantly improve accessibility.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the paper focuses on *whether* recovery is possible, it doesn't address the computational challenges associated with *actually* implementing the recovery algorithms.  Discussing the complexity of finding admissible sequences or applying Theorem 2 would be beneficial.
* **Practical Limitations:**  The assumptions underlying the m-graph representation (correct specification of the graph structure, compatibility with the observed data) are strong. A more nuanced discussion of the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification would be valuable. How can one assess the plausibility of the m-graph?
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While rigorous, it lacks any empirical validation to demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods on real-world datasets. 
* **Connection to existing work on sensitivity analysis:** The paper focuses on recoverability under *assumed* m-graphs. While that is a valid contribution, a brief discussion on how this framework relates to sensitivity analysis (estimating causal effects under uncertainty about the missing data mechanism) would be beneficial.



**4. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Definition 2 (General Ordered Factorization):**  The importance of the minimality requirement could be emphasized further.  Perhaps a more intuitive explanation of why non-minimal factorizations fail.
* **Section 6 (Recovering Causal Queries):** The jump from the example to Theorem 4 feels abrupt.  More explanation of the connection between d*-separation and the recoverability of causal effects would be helpful.
* **Section 7 (Attrition):** The distinction between Theorems 5 and 6 could be clearer. What does Theorem 5 add to the discussion of simple attrition?
* **Related Work:** While comprehensive, the related work section could benefit from highlighting connections to the growing literature on double machine learning and targeted maximum likelihood estimation, which address related challenges in causal inference with missing data.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments FOR Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical advances in the field of missing data and causal inference.
* Rigorous mathematical framework with clear definitions and theorems.
* Addresses a crucial and challenging problem with practical implications.
* Provides novel insights into the recoverability of causal effects under missing data.

**Arguments AGAINST Acceptance:**

* High theoretical density and potential accessibility issues.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity and practical limitations.



**6. Final Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  The paper presents a substantial contribution to the field, but addressing the weaknesses related to clarity, computational considerations, and practical limitations will significantly enhance its impact. I encourage the authors to add more illustrative examples, discuss the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification, and consider including a brief section on computational complexity. Adding a discussion of related work in sensitivity analysis would also strengthen the paper.
