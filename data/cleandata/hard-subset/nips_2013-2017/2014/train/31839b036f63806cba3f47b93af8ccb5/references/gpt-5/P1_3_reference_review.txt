Summary
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to determine when probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) are recoverable from data subject to missingness, including MNAR settings. Key contributions are:
- Extending ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R (“general ordered factorization”) and providing a sufficient condition (Theorem 1) for recoverability via admissible sequences that may include R, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact.
- A general recovery result (Theorem 2) for P(V) in semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R by collider connectivity and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)). A corollary (Corollary 1) handles models without latent confounding between R and substantive variables.
- New graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) that provide necessary conditions for joint and conditional recovery; notably, adjacency or certain collider-path connections between a variable and its missingness mechanism preclude recovery of P(V) or P(Y|X).
- A central insight for causal queries: P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x), P(x,y)) are not—leveraging do-calculus and dormant independences (Example 3, Theorem 4).
- Application to “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1): necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6), correcting claims in prior work (Garcia, 2013).

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) by generalizing sequential factorization and addressing interacting missingness mechanisms. It is grounded in Pearl’s causal inference framework and identification via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; d*-separation/dormant independence), and relates to ADMG Markov properties (Richardson, 2003). Compared to mainstream MAR/MCAR methods (Rubin; EM/ML; IPW; MI), this paper treats MNAR via explicit modeling of the missingness process and provides constructive estimands when possible. The attrition analysis extends and corrects graph-based treatments of dropout (Garcia, 2013) and cautions consistent with Thoemmes & Rose (2013).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formal statements are well-motivated and consistent with causal graph theory. The extension to include R in ordered factorizations is natural and the minimality requirement is clearly justified (Example 1). Theorem 2 and the non-recoverability conditions are plausible and useful. The causal results (Theorem 4) rely on do-calculus/dormant independence appropriately and are illustrated by a concrete example.
- Completeness: Proofs are relegated to the appendix; in the main text, constructive estimands are provided for key cases. However, the paper lacks a systematic algorithmic procedure (with complexity) to search for admissible sequences or to check the conditions of Theorem 2 and Theorem 4. No empirical demonstrations or simulations are provided to illustrate robustness or practical gains.
- Assumptions: The m-graph restriction that R cannot be a parent of V or U is standard but limits some longitudinal settings where missingness decisions could affect future measurements. Positivity over complete cases is assumed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with helpful examples. However:
  - Notation is sometimes opaque: e.g., GX and GX are described as removing outgoing vs incoming edges, but the symbols are identical; later GX,W1 appears without a crisp earlier definition. Please disambiguate intervention/edge-removal notation.
  - The distinction between “collider path” (all intermediates collider) and the variant used in Theorem 3 (“every intermediate node is both a collider and a substantive variable”) should be made explicit early and used consistently.
  - Definitions of R partitions and Mb(R(i)) would benefit from a brief constructive description or pseudocode in the main text.
  - The references contain numerous formatting errors and missing details.
- A short “recipe” for checking recoverability under Theorems 1–3 and 5–6 would greatly aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Extending ordered factorizations to include R and providing general recoverability in the absence of admissible sequences are novel and important over Mohan et al. (2013).
- The non-recoverability graphical criteria are new and practically valuable.
- The result that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry (via do-calculus/dormant independence) is insightful and likely to influence practice.
- The necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition and the correction to prior claims are noteworthy.

Significance
- Missing data under MNAR is pervasive across ML and applied sciences. The paper advances the state of the art by offering constructive estimands, broader classes of recoverable problems (including interacting R), and crisp impossibility criteria. The causal recoverability insights are particularly impactful for practitioners conducting randomized/observational studies with attrition or selection. These results should be useful to both theorists and applied researchers and could inform tooling.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advances beyond prior m-graph results; constructive estimands.
- Useful and novel non-recoverability criteria.
- Important insight on causal recoverability when probabilistic recovery fails.
- Necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition; correction of literature.
- Relevance to a wide range of ML/causal inference applications.

Cons for acceptance
- No algorithms/pseudocode or complexity analysis for finding admissible sequences or checking Theorem 2/4 conditions.
- No empirical illustrations or simulations demonstrating practical benefits or robustness.
- Some notation/clarity issues and numerous reference formatting problems.
- The R→V prohibition limits some realistic longitudinal scenarios; scope and implications should be discussed.

Detailed suggestions
- Provide a high-level algorithm to: (i) search for admissible sequences with minimal conditioning sets; (ii) compute R partitions and Mb(R(i)); and (iii) verify Theorem 4’s conditions (including how to check d*-separation and identifiability inputs).
- Clarify the differences between collider path vs inducing path, and why inducing paths appear in Theorem 6 but collider paths in Theorem 5/3; include a small figure contrasting them.
- Discuss sensitivity to m-graph misspecification and how available tests (Mohan & Pearl, 2014) can be integrated.
- Add a short empirical section with synthetic m-graphs comparing recovery vs standard MAR-based methods (EM/IPW/MI) under MNAR to illustrate bias reduction.
- Fix notation typos (GX; conditioning sets; primes), define all symbols upon first use, and clean up the references.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Strong theoretical contributions with clear significance; minor clarity/algorithmic presentation issues and lack of empirical demonstration prevent a stronger accept, but the paper advances the field meaningfully. Confidence: high.
