## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries in the presence of missing data. It refines sequential factorization theorems, proposes algorithms for cases where those theorems fail, and introduces graphical criteria for non-recoverability. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, and even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism. The authors illustrate these results with examples, including a discussion of attrition.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Handling missing data is a pervasive challenge in observational and experimental science. Developing rigorous theoretical foundations for recoverability is crucial.
* **Novelty:** Extending recoverability analysis to causal queries, and showing recoverability *despite* non-separability, represents a significant advance. This is a key distinction from traditional MAR/MCAR frameworks which often rely on stronger assumptions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents formal definitions, theorems, and corollaries, providing a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed methods. The extension of sequential factorization is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The paper generally follows a logical structure and clearly defines the key concepts (m-graphs, recoverability, etc.). Examples are helpful in illustrating complex concepts.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide an extensive and well-organized review of existing methods for handling missing data, including both traditional statistical approaches and more recent work on graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical development is dense and can be difficult to follow without a strong background in graphical models and causal inference.  The notation, while standard, is extensive and can become overwhelming.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper relies primarily on theoretical results and illustrative examples.  While these examples are helpful, the lack of empirical validation on real-world datasets raises concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed methods.  A simulation study demonstrating the performance of the algorithms would be highly valuable.
* **Practicality of Algorithms:** The paper outlines algorithms but doesn’t provide detailed implementation guidance. The complexity of applying these methods in practice is not fully addressed. How easily could one *actually* determine admissible sequences or identify the necessary partitions for Theorem 2 in a complex m-graph?
* **Potential for Computational Intractability:**  The problem of finding admissible sequences or checking the conditions for non-recoverability could be computationally expensive for large, complex m-graphs. This limitation is not adequately discussed.
* **Focus on Simple Attrition:** While the attrition example is useful, the treatment feels somewhat limited.  A more detailed discussion of different attrition mechanisms and how the proposed methods could be applied in more complex scenarios would be beneficial.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and presents novel theoretical results.  The proofs appear rigorous (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review).  The lack of empirical validation is a significant concern.
* **Clarity (6/10):** While the paper is logically structured, the density of the theoretical development and the extensive notation make it challenging to follow.  More detailed explanations and simplified examples could improve clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The extension of recoverability analysis to causal queries, particularly the demonstration of recoverability under non-separability, is highly original. The refined sequential factorization theorem also contributes to novelty.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results have the potential to be highly significant, particularly for researchers working with missing data in observational studies where causal inference is a primary goal.  However, the practical impact will depend on the feasibility of applying the methods in real-world scenarios.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Presents a significant theoretical advancement in the field of missing data analysis.
* Addresses a fundamentally important problem with broad applicability.
* Offers a rigorous framework for analyzing recoverability of causal queries.
* Extends existing theory in meaningful ways (sequential factorization, non-recoverability criteria).

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks empirical validation, hindering assessment of practical utility.
* The complexity of the methods may limit their adoption by practitioners.
* Concerns regarding computational tractability for large-scale problems.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This is a strong theoretical contribution with the potential to significantly impact the field of missing data analysis. However, the lack of empirical validation is a major concern. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors including a simulation study demonstrating the performance of their algorithms on realistic datasets, and providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and practical challenges associated with applying the proposed methods. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  Furthermore, expanding the discussion of attrition scenarios beyond "simple attrition" would enhance the paper's relevance.
