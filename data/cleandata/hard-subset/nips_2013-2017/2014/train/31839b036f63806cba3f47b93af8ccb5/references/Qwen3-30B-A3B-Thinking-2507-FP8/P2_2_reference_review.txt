

# Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

## Summary

This paper introduces "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal representation to model missing data processes, extending the work of Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors establish conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable. The paper makes significant theoretical contributions by providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability, distinguishing between probabilistic and causal query recoverability, and applying these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies. The work builds upon and extends Pearl's causal inference framework, addressing a critical gap in missing data literature by considering interactions among missingness mechanisms.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs (with complete proofs in the appendix), establishing clear conditions for recoverability. The sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the more general recovery conditions (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and extend prior work to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic queries (Theorem 3) and causal queries (Theorem 4) is particularly well-executed, with Example 3 providing a compelling illustration of how causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic relations are not.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that their approach applies to semi-Markovian models and doesn't address computational aspects. The theoretical framework is sound, with careful attention to necessary conditions for recoverability (e.g., the non-recoverability criteria in Theorem 3). The paper also effectively distinguishes between different missingness mechanisms (MCAR, MAR, MNAR) and their implications for recoverability.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to applications. The authors use consistent notation throughout, and key concepts like "d*-separation" are defined precisely before use. The examples (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts and the necessity of the proposed conditions. The paper makes excellent use of figures to visualize m-graphs and missingness processes.

However, the notation can be dense for readers unfamiliar with graphical models, and some technical terms (like "Rpart" and "Mb(R(i))") require careful parsing. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the theoretical results translate to practical implementation, though this may be beyond the scope of a purely theoretical contribution.

## Originality

This paper makes significant original contributions to the field. The key novelty lies in demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not—a distinction that has not been formally established in the literature. The authors introduce new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) and show how these differ from probabilistic query recoverability conditions. The extension of Mohan et al.'s work to handle interactions among missingness mechanisms (e.g., R variables influencing each other) is also novel and addresses a gap in prior work.

The paper distinguishes itself from related work by:
1. Providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability (not just sufficient)
2. Explicitly distinguishing between probabilistic and causal query recoverability
3. Addressing interactions among missingness mechanisms (not just isolated missingness processes)
4. Correcting prevailing misconceptions about attrition (as shown in Theorem 6)

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific domains, and the paper provides a theoretically grounded framework for determining when queries can be reliably recovered from missing data. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability has important practical implications: researchers can now identify cases where causal effects might be recoverable even when standard probabilistic approaches fail.

The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly significant, as attrition is a major challenge in longitudinal studies. The paper corrects misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia [2013]) and provides necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery under simple attrition. This has direct implications for experimental design and analysis in fields like clinical trials, social sciences, and epidemiology.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes significant advances in the field of missing data analysis. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with novel insights. While it is purely theoretical (without empirical validation), this is appropriate for the scope of the contribution, which is to establish fundamental recoverability conditions.

The paper's most significant contribution is establishing that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not—a distinction with profound implications for missing data analysis. This insight, combined with the rigorous theoretical framework and practical applications to attrition, makes the paper highly valuable to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is well-positioned to make a lasting contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in missing data analysis with clear theoretical results that have practical implications for researchers across disciplines. The work builds upon but significantly extends prior research, and the contributions are clearly articulated and rigorously established. The paper would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
