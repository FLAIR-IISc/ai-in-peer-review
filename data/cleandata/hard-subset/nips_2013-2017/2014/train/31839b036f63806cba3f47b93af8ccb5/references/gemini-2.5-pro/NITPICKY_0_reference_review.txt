### Summary

This paper presents a formal graphical framework, called m-graphs, for analyzing the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work extends previous results by Mohan et al. [2013] in several ways. First, it generalizes the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms. Second, it provides a novel method for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables. Third, it introduces new graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a topic largely unaddressed in prior literature. Finally, and perhaps most significantly, it demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic distributions are not, leveraging concepts from the do-calculus. The authors apply these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, claiming to correct existing results in that domain.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is theoretically dense and aims to provide rigorous, graphical conditions for a challenging problem. The theoretical contributions, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal from probabilistic recoverability, appear sound and are the main strengths of the work. However, the quality of the submission is undermined by numerous small but significant issues. The paper relies heavily on a strong positivity assumption (P(Vo, Vm, R=0) > 0), and the footnote suggesting its relaxation is "sometimes feasible" is too vague to be reassuring. Furthermore, key results like Theorem 4 are restricted to the case of a single partially observed variable (`|Vm|=1`), which is a major limitation that is not adequately discussed. The entire framework also presupposes a correctly specified m-graph, and the paper lacks any discussion on the sensitivity of its results to model misspecification, which is a critical concern for practical application.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is a major weakness. While the core ideas are understandable, the execution is poor.
*   The notation is dense. Key concepts for Theorem 2 (`R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))`) are defined *after* the theorem is stated, forcing the reader to read ahead and then backtrack.
*   Equation numbering is inconsistent. The paper contains many important unnumbered equations (e.g., the main results in Example 1, Theorem 2, Corollary 1), making them difficult to reference and follow.
*   The reference list is improperly formatted to an extent that suggests a lack of care. Multiple entries contain what appear to be unprocessed BibTeX commands (e.g., `? \Q2002\E`), and the formatting style is inconsistent across entries. This is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
*   The text itself contains awkward phrasing (e.g., "An immediate but important implication of trivial recoverability is that if data are MAR or MCAR and the query is identifiable, then it is also recoverable by model-blind algorithms" – the connection between "trivial recoverability" and "model-blind" is not immediately obvious and needs better explanation).

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. It is a direct and non-trivial extension of Mohan et al. [2013]. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is novel and valuable, as it provides a way to prove that a query is impossible to estimate under the given assumptions. The most original and insightful contribution is the demonstration that causal queries can be recovered even when their constituent probabilistic terms are not (Section 6.1). This elegantly connects the missing data literature with foundational results in causal inference (e.g., d*-separation), highlighting that causal quantities can sometimes be more robust than associational ones.

**Significance:** The problem of handling data that is Missing Not At Random (MNAR) is of high importance, as standard methods often fail in this regime. This paper provides a principled, model-based approach to reasoning about what can and cannot be learned from such data. For the causal inference community, the results are significant and thought-provoking. However, the practical significance for the broader machine learning audience may be limited. The proposed criteria are complex and require significant domain expertise to construct the initial m-graph. Without more accessible algorithms or software, and a study of robustness, these methods are unlikely to see wide adoption. The paper's claim to correct prevailing results on attrition (Garcia [2013]) is significant if true, but the argument needs to be presented with maximum clarity to be impactful.

### Arguments for/against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles a fundamentally important and difficult problem (MNAR data).
2.  It introduces novel theoretical contributions, especially the formal non-recoverability criteria and the separation of causal from probabilistic recoverability.
3.  The results provide a deeper understanding of the interplay between graphical models, causality, and missing data.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  The overall presentation quality is poor. The severely flawed reference section, inconsistent equation numbering, and suboptimal organization detract significantly from the paper's credibility.
2.  Several key technical points are either underdeveloped or limited. The relaxation of the positivity assumption is not elaborated upon, and crucial theorems (e.g., Theorem 4) are presented for overly restrictive settings (`|Vm|=1`) without discussing generalization.
3.  The paper fails to discuss the practical issue of robustness to model misspecification, a critical omission for a method that relies entirely on a pre-specified graphical model.
4.  The accumulation of numerous minor flaws suggests the paper was hastily prepared and is not yet ready for publication at a venue of NIPS's caliber.

### Conclusion

While this paper contains novel and potentially important theoretical ideas, its current form is not acceptable. The core contributions are overshadowed by a substantial number of flaws in presentation, clarity, and technical scoping. The state of the reference list alone is a sufficient reason for concern. The ideas are promising, but the paper requires a thorough revision to address the organizational issues, clarify its technical limitations and assumptions, and improve its overall quality before it can be considered for publication. I recommend **rejection**.
