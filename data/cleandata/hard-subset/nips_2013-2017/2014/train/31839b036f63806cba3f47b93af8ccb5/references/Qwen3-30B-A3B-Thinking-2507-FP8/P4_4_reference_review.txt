

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to develop conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors build upon prior work by establishing general conditions for recoverability of probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable. The paper also introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability and applies the results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs provided in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic queries versus causal queries, which is a significant contribution to the field. The paper establishes clear conditions for recoverability (Theorems 1, 2, 5, 6) and non-recoverability (Theorems 3, Corollary 2), and provides detailed examples (Examples 1, 2, 3) to illustrate the theoretical concepts. The distinction between probabilistic recoverability (which requires Y and Ry to be d-separated) and causal recoverability (which can circumvent this requirement) is particularly well-developed. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for specific graphical conditions to apply the recovery procedures.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written for the intended audience of graphical model researchers. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, with careful distinction between key concepts (e.g., recoverability vs. identifiability). The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature on missing data. The examples provided (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) are excellent at clarifying complex theoretical points. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the computational complexity of the proposed recovery algorithms, but the theoretical exposition is generally clear and accessible to experts.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions. The key novelty is demonstrating that causal queries can be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable (Example 3 and Theorem 4). The new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are also novel contributions that fill a gap in the literature. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle missingness mechanisms that influence each other represents a significant theoretical advancement beyond Mohan et al. (2013). The necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under simple attrition (Theorems 5, 6) rectify previous misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013).

**Significance:** Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, and the paper provides a formal framework for determining when missing data can be handled without bias. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries has important theoretical implications for causal inference. The application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies is particularly significant, as attrition is a major obstacle to sound inferences in this domain. The paper advances the state of the art in graphical models for missing data by providing precise graphical conditions for recovery, which will be valuable for researchers working with incomplete datasets.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical contributions extending prior work on m-graphs
- Precise mathematical formulations with well-defined theorems
- Careful distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability
- Practical application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
- Comprehensive literature review that situates the work in context
- Theoretical results provide clear guidance for practitioners

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results (no simulations or real data experiments)
- Does not address computational complexity of the proposed recovery algorithms
- Could better differentiate its contributions from Garcia (2013) on attrition
- The theoretical results are complex and may require significant expertise to apply
- Does not provide guidance on how to handle cases where the conditions for recovery are not met

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- Pro: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data, extending the m-graph framework in novel ways that distinguish between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The results have clear significance for both theory and practice.
- Pro: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and clear exposition of complex concepts.
- Pro: The necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery under attrition rectify previous misconceptions in the literature.
- Con: The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
- Con: The complexity of the conditions may limit accessibility to practitioners without strong graphical model expertise.

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for missing data. It addresses a significant problem with novel theoretical insights that advance the state of the art. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant conceptual advance.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a clear theoretical contribution to an important problem in machine learning and statistics. It is technically sound, well-written, and makes novel contributions that extend the existing literature. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
