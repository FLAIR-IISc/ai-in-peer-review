Summary
This paper advances the graphical approach to missing data via Missingness Graphs (m-graphs), extending Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) along several substantive dimensions. The authors (i) generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R and give a strengthened sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that applies even when missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a new recoverability result (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without an admissible sequence, using partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)); (iii) introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, including when Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism (Theorem 4); and (v) deliver necessary and sufficient conditions for both joint and causal recoverability in the “simple attrition” setting (Theorems 5–6), correcting claims in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013). Proofs are deferred to the appendix; the main text provides examples illustrating how to construct estimands from observed proxies and R.

Relation to prior work: Compared to Mohan et al. (2013), this paper goes beyond MAR/MCAR and MNAR recoverability via factorization by (a) allowing R–R interactions in the factorization scheme, (b) providing general-purpose criteria when admissible sequences fail, and (c) supplying necessary (non-recoverability) conditions—largely missing from the literature. It connects to the causal identification literature (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; 2008) via do-calculus and dormant independences to obtain causal estimands in the presence of missing data, and contrasts with mainstream ML/EM/IPW/MI approaches that assume MAR or model coarsening without explicit causal structure.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and breadth: The general ordered factorization including R, together with the partition/Markov-blanket construction (Theorem 2), significantly broadens the class of models where joint recoverability is attainable. The non-recoverability criteria are especially useful guardrails.
- Important insight on causal queries: Demonstrating recoverability of P(y|do(x)) when P(y|x) is not recoverable—and when Y is inseparable from Ry—is a striking and impactful message that bridges causal identifiability and missing-data mechanisms.
- Clear, actionable graphical criteria: The theorems translate directly into checkable d-separation/dormant-independence conditions and yield explicit estimands in terms of observed proxies and R.
- Attrition case analysis: Necessary and sufficient conditions for both P(V) and P(y|do(x)) when |Vm|=1 provide a crisp resolution to a common practical scenario and correct prior claims.
- Potential impact: The results could reshape how missing-not-at-random problems are approached in causal inference and inspire practical tooling for constructing estimands given an m-graph.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: Several definitions are dense; notation (e.g., primes for R=0, subscripts, set shorthands) could be streamlined. The role of minimality in Definition 2 deserves more intuition and guidance for practitioners.
- Algorithmic concreteness: While theorems yield estimands, there is no explicit algorithmic procedure/pseudocode to (a) detect admissible sequences, (b) compute R partitions and Mb(R(i)), or (c) automatically construct the estimand when multiple options exist. Complexity considerations are absent.
- Empirical validation: No simulations or case studies demonstrate how the proposed constructions behave with finite samples, positivity violations, or model misspecification. Even small synthetic experiments would strengthen the paper.
- Assumptions and scope: The m-graph assumption that R does not parent V∪U may limit some longitudinal/behavioral settings where missingness can change subsequent substantive variables. Discussion of this modeling choice and its alternatives would help.
- References and polish: The bibliography contains formatting glitches and missing publication details; some claims in related work could be better contextualized with precise citations.

Quality
Technically sound, with careful use of graphical criteria and do-calculus. Theorems are plausible and consistent with known identification theory. However, the lack of in-text proof sketches for key results (Theorems 2–4) and no computational analysis leave implementation questions open.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but notation is heavy. Adding a running example through Sections 3–6, brief proof ideas, and a summary “recipe” for practitioners would improve readability.

Originality
High. Extending factorization to include R interactions, providing necessary non-recoverability criteria, and enabling causal recoverability when probabilistic recoverability fails are novel and important contributions beyond Mohan et al. (2013).

Significance
High. These results equip researchers with principled tools to decide when and how to recover queries under MNAR, including widely occurring attrition. The practical relevance is strong if coupled with tooling.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide an explicit algorithm (or pseudo-code) to search for admissible sequences and to construct estimands under Theorem 2?
- What is the computational complexity of checking conditions in Theorems 2–4 on large graphs?
- How robust are the estimands to slight misspecification of the m-graph? Any testability/diagnostics beyond Mohan & Pearl (2014)?
- Can the assumption “R not parents of V” be relaxed, and how would results change?
- Any plans to release a small library that takes an m-graph and outputs the estimand (or declares non-recoverability)?

Pros
- Bold, conceptually novel extensions with clear theoretical value.
- Bridges causal identification and missing-data recovery in a compelling way.
- Provides both positive and negative graphical criteria and corrects misconceptions in a key application (attrition).

Cons
- Heavy notation and limited algorithmic guidance.
- No empirical/simulation validation.
- Some modeling assumptions and references need clearer exposition.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces significant new concepts and directions in the graphical treatment of missing data and causal recovery. Despite presentation and validation gaps, the novelty and potential impact merit inclusion at NIPS. I encourage the authors to add a short “practical recipe,” minimal algorithmic pseudocode, and a small synthetic study if space permits, and to polish references/notation.
