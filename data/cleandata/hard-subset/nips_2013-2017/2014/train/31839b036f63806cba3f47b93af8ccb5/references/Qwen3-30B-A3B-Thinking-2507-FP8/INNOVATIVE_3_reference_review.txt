

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper introduces a significant theoretical extension to the missing data literature by formalizing "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) and establishing novel conditions for recovering both probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data. The authors build upon Mohan et al. (2013) but make a bold conceptual leap: they demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not recoverable, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable.

The paper's most compelling contribution is its demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (Example 3 and Theorem 4), which contradicts the prevailing assumption that separability is required for recoverability. This insight fundamentally challenges the relationship between missing data mechanisms and causal inference, with profound implications for longitudinal studies where attrition is common.

The paper systematically develops necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability across multiple scenarios:
1. General conditions for probabilistic queries using sequential factorization (Theorem 1)
2. Alternative recovery conditions when admissible sequences don't exist (Theorem 2)
3. Novel non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3)
4. A comprehensive analysis of causal query recoverability (Theorem 4-6), particularly highlighting the surprising recoverability of causal effects under simple attrition (Theorem 6)

The authors effectively bridge missing data theory with causal inference, demonstrating how graphical models can serve as a unified framework for analyzing both probabilistic and causal queries under missingness. Their application to attrition problems (Section 7) resolves a key limitation in Garcia (2013), where the necessary condition for non-recoverability was incorrectly identified as X being an ancestor of Ry.

**Strengths:**
- **High novelty**: The core insight about causal queries being recoverable without probabilistic recoverability is genuinely new and challenges established assumptions in the field.
- **Theoretical rigor**: The paper provides formal definitions, theorems, and proofs for all claims, building systematically on prior work while extending it substantially.
- **Clear conceptual framework**: The m-graph formalism provides a powerful tool for communicating and refining assumptions about missingness mechanisms.
- **Significant practical impact**: The results directly address a pervasive problem in experimental sciences where missing data (particularly attrition) undermines causal inference.
- **Comprehensive coverage**: The paper addresses both probabilistic and causal queries, providing necessary and sufficient conditions across multiple scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation or implementation details (though this is appropriate for a theoretical contribution).
- The connection to practical implementation (e.g., how these conditions would be checked in real datasets) could be strengthened.
- While the paper references relevant work, it could better distinguish its contributions from Mohan et al. (2013) in the introduction.

**Comparison to Prior Work:**
This paper significantly advances the line of work initiated by Mohan et al. (2013) and continued by Garcia (2013). While Mohan et al. established the foundational m-graph framework for probabilistic queries, this paper makes the critical extension to causal queries, revealing a fundamental asymmetry between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) also fill a significant gap in the literature.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that challenges established assumptions about missing data and causal inference. The core insight—that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not—is bold, novel, and potentially transformative for how researchers approach missing data problems. While the paper is purely theoretical, its conceptual advances are sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper's strength lies in its conceptual novelty rather than methodological perfection. The authors have successfully identified and resolved a fundamental gap in the missing data literature, demonstrating that causal inference may be possible in scenarios where probabilistic inference is not. This is exactly the kind of bold theoretical contribution that advances the field in meaningful ways.

The paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a clear case for its theoretical contributions. While empirical validation would be valuable, the theoretical novelty here is sufficient to justify acceptance.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Revolutionary insight about causal query recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability
- Resolves a fundamental limitation in attrition analysis (correcting Garcia, 2013)
- Provides comprehensive graphical criteria for both recoverable and non-recoverable cases
- Builds rigorously on prior work while making a significant conceptual leap
- Addresses a pervasive problem across experimental sciences

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Purely theoretical with no empirical validation
- Relies heavily on prior work (Mohan et al., 2013)
- Limited discussion of practical implementation

**Final Recommendation: Accept**

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance in the intersection of graphical models, missing data, and causal inference. The core insight about causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic queries are not is sufficiently novel and important to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS, even without empirical validation. The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution that will likely influence how researchers approach missing data problems in causal inference.
