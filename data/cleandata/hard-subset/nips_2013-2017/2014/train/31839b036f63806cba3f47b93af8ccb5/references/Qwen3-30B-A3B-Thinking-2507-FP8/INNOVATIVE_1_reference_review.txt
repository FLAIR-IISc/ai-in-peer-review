

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper introduces a significant theoretical advancement in the field of missing data analysis by extending the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to address both probabilistic and causal query recovery. The authors build upon Mohan et al. (2013) with novel contributions that fundamentally challenge existing understanding of recoverability conditions.

The paper's most compelling contribution is demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the associated probabilistic relations (such as P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable. This is a bold and counterintuitive insight that fundamentally shifts the theoretical landscape of missing data analysis. The authors establish this through Theorem 4 and Example 3, showing that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are connected by an inducing path - a condition that would make probabilistic recovery impossible according to Theorem 3.

The paper makes several important theoretical advances:
1. It refines the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (Theorem 1)
2. It introduces a general recovery algorithm applicable when sequential factorization fails (Theorem 2)
3. It establishes the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2)
4. It provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition (Theorem 6), correcting prevailing misconceptions in the literature

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and clearly articulated. The authors provide rigorous definitions, theorems, and proofs, with careful attention to the distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies (Section 7) demonstrates practical relevance while maintaining theoretical rigor.

The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty. While many missing data methods focus on probabilistic recovery, this work reveals a fundamental distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability that has been overlooked in the literature. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic structure cannot be recovered is paradigm-shifting and has significant implications for fields like causal inference, epidemiology, and social sciences where missing data is pervasive.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with examples that effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts. The connection to prior work by Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl is well-established, and the authors properly contextualize their contributions within the existing literature.

The main limitation is the lack of empirical validation on real-world datasets. While theoretical contributions are valuable, the absence of practical demonstrations might limit the paper's immediate impact for practitioners. However, given the paper's theoretical nature and the difficulty of obtaining datasets with known missingness mechanisms, this limitation is understandable and doesn't significantly undermine the theoretical contribution.

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance that should be of interest to the NIPS community. The authors successfully demonstrate that causal queries can be recovered under conditions where probabilistic recovery is impossible, which is a novel and important insight. This work fundamentally changes how we think about missing data problems in causal inference.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical development is sound and well-supported by proofs
- Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written
- Originality: The core insight about causal vs. probabilistic recoverability is novel and transformative
- Significance: This work addresses a fundamental problem with broad implications across scientific disciplines

I recommend acceptance. The paper's bold theoretical contribution on the recoverability of causal queries despite probabilistic non-recoverability represents a significant advance over previous work. The authors have successfully demonstrated a new conceptual framework that will likely influence future research in missing data and causal inference. While empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical novelty is sufficiently substantial to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
