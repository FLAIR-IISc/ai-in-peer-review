- Extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework to recover probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.
- Shows causal queries can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, especially when Y and Ry are not d-separable.
- Strong technical quality with well-motivated theorems and proofs building on Mohan et al. (2013).
- Extends sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (R variables influencing each other).
- Carefully distinguishes necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability and provides practical algorithms.
- Example 3 compellingly demonstrates recoverability of P(y|do(z)) despite violations of probabilistic separability.
- Well-structured and clear notation; definitions and examples (Example 1 and 3) effectively illustrate concepts.
- Some concepts lack intuitive explanation (e.g., minimality in Definition 2 and the probabilistic→causal transition).
- Novel contribution: handles interacting missingness mechanisms and shows causal recoverability can contradict conventional wisdom.
- Corrects misconceptions in prior work on attrition (Theorem 6 vs. Garcia, 2013).
- Significant practical implications for causal inference and missing data, especially longitudinal attrition problems.
- Strength: well-developed formal framework that meaningfully extends prior work.
- Strength: novel and important insight about causal vs. probabilistic recoverability.
- Strength: provides both sufficient and necessary conditions useful for practitioners.
- Strength: technical advancement in modeling interactions among missingness mechanisms.
- Strength: resolves a literature misconception regarding attrition.
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation on real datasets.
- Weakness: limited practical implementation guidance for applying the criteria.
- Weakness: does not address computational complexity or scalability.
- Weakness: limited discussion connecting results to existing practical methods (e.g., multiple imputation).
- Overall recommendation: Accept (Strong Accept) — significant, well-supported theoretical contribution despite limited empirical/implementation work.
