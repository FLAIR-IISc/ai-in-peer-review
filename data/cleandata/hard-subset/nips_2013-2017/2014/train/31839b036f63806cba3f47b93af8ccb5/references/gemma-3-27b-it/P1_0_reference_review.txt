## Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) for handling missing data, particularly focusing on conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries. The work builds upon previous NIPS publications by Mohan et al. and expands the scope of recoverability analysis, notably demonstrating that causal effects can be recovered even when traditional probabilistic recoverability fails. While the theoretical developments are strong and the exposition is generally clear, the paper leans heavily towards theoretical contributions with limited empirical demonstration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on a clearer discussion of practical implications and potential challenges in real-world applications.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper extends the concept of recoverability—the ability to obtain consistent estimates of queries in the presence of missing data—using m-graphs. The key contribution lies in refining the sequential factorization theorem and presenting new conditions for recovering both probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries. Previous work (Mohan et al., 2013) established a foundation for recoverability based on ordered factorizations. This paper generalizes that approach to handle more complex missingness mechanisms (where mechanisms interact) and, more importantly, demonstrates that causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are non-recoverable. This is significant as it addresses a limitation of standard MAR/MCAR-based approaches. The authors also address a gap in existing literature by formally identifying conditions for *non*-recoverability and exploring applications to attrition problems.  Related work is discussed reasonably well, referencing key publications in missing data analysis, causal inference, and graphical models.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (provided in the appendix). The extensions to sequential factorization and the conditions for causal recoverability are mathematically sound and well-justified.
* **Novelty:**  The core finding—that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic relationships are not—is a novel and important contribution. This expands the utility of m-graphs beyond simply diagnosing recoverability to actively enabling causal inference in challenging missing data scenarios.
* **Formalization of Non-Recoverability:** Providing explicit criteria for when recoverability fails is crucial for practical application, alerting researchers to situations where assumptions are insufficient.
* **Connection to Attrition:** The application of the framework to attrition problems is a relevant and practical example, highlighting the potential of the approach in real-world longitudinal studies.
* **Clear Notation:** The notation is generally well-defined and consistent, making the theoretical arguments easier to follow.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is overwhelmingly theoretical. While examples are provided to illustrate the theorems, there's a lack of empirical demonstration using simulated or real-world datasets.  Demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed methods through simulations, and comparing them against standard techniques (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting), would significantly strengthen the work.
* **Complexity:** The theoretical framework, while rigorous, can be quite dense and challenging for readers unfamiliar with m-graphs and causal inference.  More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples would enhance accessibility.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not sufficiently address the practical challenges of *learning* the m-graph in the first place.  Estimating missingness mechanisms and verifying assumptions are non-trivial tasks. A discussion of these challenges and potential solutions would be valuable.
* **Scope of Attrition Example:** The attrition example is presented relatively briefly.  Expanding this section to discuss different attrition scenarios and the applicability of the theorems in each case would enhance the practical relevance.



**4. Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. It presents a complete theoretical contribution, although further empirical evaluation would increase confidence.  The authors are careful to define their assumptions and limitations.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear, but the density of the theoretical arguments can make it difficult to follow at times.  Improving the explanations and providing more intuitive examples would improve clarity.
* **Originality:**  The work is highly original, extending existing m-graph frameworks with novel conditions for causal query recoverability.
* **Significance:**  The results are potentially significant, particularly for researchers interested in causal inference with missing data. The ability to recover causal effects under weaker conditions than previously thought is a valuable contribution.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Novel and important theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis.
*  Rigorous mathematical framework with well-defined theorems and proofs.
*  Addresses a critical limitation of existing methods by enabling causal inference in challenging scenarios.
*  Clear connection to a practical problem (attrition).

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Lack of empirical validation to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
*  The complexity of the theoretical framework may limit accessibility.
*  Limited discussion of practical challenges in estimating the m-graph and verifying assumptions.


**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, I suggest the following:

* **Add a simulation study:**  Include a simulation study to demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods in recovering causal effects under various missing data mechanisms and m-graph structures.
* **Expand the attrition example:** Provide a more detailed discussion of how the theorems apply to different attrition scenarios.
* **Address practical challenges:** Briefly discuss the challenges of m-graph learning and assumption verification, and potential approaches to address them.
* **Consider adding a "future work" section:** outlining open questions and potential extensions.



This work represents a significant step forward in the theory of missing data and causal inference. Addressing the minor revisions outlined above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the NIPS community.
