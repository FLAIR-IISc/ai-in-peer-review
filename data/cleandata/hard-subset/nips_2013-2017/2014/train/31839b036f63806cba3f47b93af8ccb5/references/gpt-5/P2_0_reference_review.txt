Summary and relation to prior work
The paper advances the graphical approach to missing data initiated by Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013). It uses Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to formalize missingness mechanisms and asks when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from the manifest distribution. The contributions are:

- Sequential factorization with R variables: A generalization of ordered factorizations (Theorem 1) that allows missingness mechanisms to interact (edges among R). This yields new admissible sequences that include R in the ordering, enabling recovery of joint/conditional distributions in settings where earlier results fail. Example 1 illustrates the necessity of “minimality”.

- Recovery without admissible sequences: Theorem 2 provides a recovery formula for P(V) via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) based on collider paths, covering semi-Markovian models where no admissible sequence exists. Corollary 1 specializes this to models without latent confounding between R and substantive variables.

- Non-recoverability criteria: Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 give graphical necessary conditions precluding recovery of P(V) and P(Y|X), respectively—filling a gap in prior work that focused on sufficient conditions.

- Causal queries: The paper shows that identifiable causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities are not (e.g., Y inseparable from Ry). Using do-calculus and dormant independences (d∗-separation), Theorem 4 provides conditions (for |Vm|=1 with inducing path) under which P(y|do(x)) is recoverable by conditioning on Ry=0 in certain factors.

- Attrition: For “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), Theorem 5 gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of P(V), and Theorem 6 for P(y|do(x)), with a constructive estimand. This corrects an over-optimistic claim in Garcia (2013).

These results extend Mohan et al. (2013) and connect to Pearl (2009), Shpitser and Pearl (2006, 2008), providing both positive (constructive) and negative (impossibility) graphical criteria.

Quality
- Soundness: Theorems are stated with clear graphical preconditions, and the derivations (e.g., Example 3 via do-calculus) are technically plausible and consistent with cited foundations (do-calculus, dormant independence). Providing full proofs in the appendix is appropriate for NeurIPS. The minimality condition in Definition 2 is well motivated by Example 1.

- Completeness: The paper is primarily theoretical; while this is acceptable, it would benefit from algorithmic details in Section 4 (“general algorithms”): concrete procedures for constructing R partitions Rpart and Mb(R(i)), and for checking conditions automatically, with complexity analysis.

- Assumptions: The positivity over complete cases is stated; it would help to discuss sensitivity to violations and monotone missingness. Some conditions (e.g., Theorem 2 precluding neighbor/collider-path relations between Vm variables and their own missingness mechanisms) are strong, but the paper later provides necessary non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) that align with these.

- Empirical validation: There are no simulations or case studies demonstrating the estimands’ empirical behavior (finite-sample bias/variance) or the impact of misspecifying the m-graph. While not required for a theory paper, a small synthetic study illustrating gains (e.g., recovery of P(y|do(x)) when P(y|x) is non-recoverable) would strengthen the work.

Clarity
- Organization is logical and the narrative builds from probabilistic to causal recoverability and then to attrition. That said, the paper is notation-dense. Suggestions:
  - Provide a table summarizing notation (Vm, Vo, V*, R, Rw vs. Wr, GX, GX).
  - Give a step-by-step “checklist” for Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 (how to verify conditions and construct estimands) with a running example.
  - Clarify Definition 2 (minimality) with an explicit formal minimality statement and an example contrasting admissible vs non-admissible factorization.
  - In Theorem 4, unpack Y ⊥w Ry|Z and the role of W1 with more intuition and a simple worked example.
  - Fix several reference formatting issues (incomplete years, inconsistent styles).

Originality
- The extension of ordered factorizations to include R variables, the general recovery formula based on R partitions and collider-path-based Markov blankets, and formal non-recoverability criteria appear new relative to Mohan et al. (2013) and the cited literature. The demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable when associated probabilistic relations are not is conceptually important and, to my knowledge, not previously formalized for m-graphs with explicit missingness mechanisms interacting.

Significance
- Missing data is pervasive across ML and causal inference; practical guidance on when recovery is possible is highly valuable. The non-recoverability criteria provide guardrails, and the attrition results give crisp necessary-and-sufficient conditions practitioners can apply. The insight that P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry is particularly impactful for experimental design and analysis under nonrandom missingness.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a simple algorithm (pseudocode) to (i) find admissible sequences per Theorem 1, and (ii) compute Rpart and Mb(R(i)) per Theorem 2? What is the computational complexity?
- Are the conditions in Theorem 4 extendable beyond |Vm|=1? If not, can you characterize the obstacles?
- How robust are the estimands to mild misspecification of the m-graph (e.g., missing an edge among R variables)?
- Could you add a small synthetic study illustrating recovery vs non-recovery and finite-sample performance?

Pros
- Clear, nontrivial theoretical advances over prior work; both positive and negative results.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recoverability; corrects claims in prior attrition literature.
- Practical estimands provided when recovery holds.

Cons
- Heavy notation; some definitions and conditions are opaque without more intuition and algorithms.
- No empirical illustrations; limited guidance on automated condition checking and practical use.
- Reference formatting issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions and their potential impact outweigh clarity and empirical shortcomings. A revision addressing clarity and providing algorithmic guidance would make this a strong accept. Confidence: high.
