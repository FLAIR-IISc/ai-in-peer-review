This paper presents an extension to the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The authors aim to refine existing recoverability conditions and provide new criteria, particularly focusing on scenarios where simple factorization approaches fail and where causal effects may be identifiable even when probabilistic relationships are not. While the topic of missing data is undeniably important, and the use of m-graphs offers a formal approach, I find the paper to be largely incremental and lacking in substantial empirical validation.

The core contribution revolves around extending the sequential factorization theorem and developing new conditions for recoverability, especially regarding causal queries. The theoretical developments, specifically Theorem 1-6, appear logically sound within the m-graph framework, building upon prior work by Mohan et al. and Pearl. However, the numerous theorems and corollaries are presented with limited illustrative examples beyond those used to motivate the concepts. This makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of these refinements. The claim that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism are inseparable (Section 6) is interesting, but the example provided (Figure 3) feels contrived and doesn’t demonstrate broad applicability.

A major weakness is the almost complete absence of experimental evaluation. The paper is heavily theoretical, relying on proofs and logical extensions of existing frameworks. While theoretical rigor is appreciated, the lack of any empirical validation raises serious concerns. How often do these refined conditions *actually* lead to recoverable queries in real-world datasets? Are the new criteria practically useful in identifying situations where recovery is possible? Without experiments, it is difficult to judge the significance of the theoretical contributions. The discussion of attrition (Section 7) feels similarly detached from practice. Asserting recoverability under specific m-graph structures is insufficient without demonstrating this on real or simulated attrition data.

The related work section is comprehensive but feels somewhat perfunctory. It lists numerous relevant techniques (ML, inverse probability weighting, imputation) but fails to critically position the m-graph approach within the broader landscape of missing data handling. How does this approach compare, in terms of performance, computational cost, and assumptions, to established methods? 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Formalizes the analysis of missing data using a well-defined graphical framework.
*   Presents logical extensions to existing recoverability theorems.
*   Identifies conditions where causal queries can be recovered even under challenging missing data patterns.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation. The theoretical results are not demonstrated on real or simulated data.
*   Limited practical impact. The presented theorems and criteria may be difficult to apply in practice without extensive case studies.
*   Incremental contribution. While the refinements are logical, they don't represent a paradigm shift in the field of missing data handling.
*   Overly theoretical presentation. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples.
*   The connection between theoretical results and practical application remains tenuous.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. The work is technically sound and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature. However, the lack of empirical validation and the incremental nature of the contributions outweigh the theoretical advancements. The paper reads more like a theoretical exploration than a significant contribution to the field, failing to convince me of its practical value or impact. Substantial revisions, including the addition of comprehensive experimental results, would be required for reconsideration. The authors need to demonstrate the real-world applicability of their framework and justify the need for this more complex graphical approach compared to established methods.
