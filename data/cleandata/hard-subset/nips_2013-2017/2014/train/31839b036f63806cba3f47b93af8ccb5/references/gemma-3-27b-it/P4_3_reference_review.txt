This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, leveraging and extending the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The authors build upon their previous results (Mohan et al., 2013) by providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly focusing on scenarios where causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. The paper tackles the challenging problem of attrition and aims to provide a more comprehensive graphical criteria for determining identifiability.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction to the problem and a concise overview of m-graphs and recoverability. Subsequent sections systematically extend existing theorems regarding sequential factorization, handling cases with interacting missingness mechanisms and introducing novel criteria for non-recoverability. The discussion of causal query recoverability is particularly interesting, demonstrating scenarios where causal effects can be identified despite non-separability between variables and their missingness mechanisms. The application to attrition problems further grounds the theoretical results. The extensive related work section provides a good overview of existing methods for handling missing data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims, building upon established concepts in causal inference and graphical models. The use of m-graphs provides a formal framework for reasoning about missing data mechanisms.
*   **Generality:** The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem and the introduction of new recoverability conditions broaden the applicability of the framework to a wider range of problems, including those with complex missingness patterns.
*   **Novelty:** The finding that causal effects can be recoverable even when probabilistic relationships are not is a significant contribution. This result expands the scope of identifiable effects in the presence of missing data.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common problem in observational and experimental studies, demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed framework.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The thorough review of related literature contextualizes the work and highlights its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Density:** The paper employs a significant amount of notation, which can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field. While definitions are provided, the density of symbols may hinder comprehension.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While examples are provided, the lack of empirical validation limits the assessment of the practical performance of the proposed methods. Simulations or real-world datasets could further demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Complexity of Theorems:** Some of the theorems are quite complex and may require careful study to fully understand. Simplifying the presentation or providing more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature on Multiple Imputation:** While related work is discussed, the connection between the m-graph framework and established methods like multiple imputation is not fully explored. Highlighting these connections could offer further insights.
*   **Example Clarity:** While examples are helpful, some could benefit from more detailed explanations or visual representations to aid understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis, extending existing frameworks and providing novel results.
*   The framework is well-motivated, rigorously developed, and applicable to a wide range of problems.
*   The finding regarding the recoverability of causal effects in non-separable scenarios is particularly noteworthy.
*   The paper is clearly written (despite some notational density) and provides a comprehensive review of related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation limits the assessment of practical utility.
*   The complexity of the theorems may hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The paper doesn't fully explore the connection to standard missing data techniques (e.g., multiple imputation).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data analysis. The extension of the m-graph framework and the novel results on causal query recoverability are particularly significant. While the lack of empirical validation and the complexity of some theorems are minor drawbacks, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. The paper is of high quality and addresses a challenging problem in a rigorous and insightful manner, aligning well with the standards of NIPS.
