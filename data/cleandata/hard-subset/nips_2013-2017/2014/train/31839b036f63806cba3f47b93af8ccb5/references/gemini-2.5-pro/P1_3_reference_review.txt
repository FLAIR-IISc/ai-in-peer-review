### Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper presents a formal framework for analyzing and recovering statistical and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) introduced in Mohan et al. (2013), the authors derive a set of novel theoretical results that significantly expand the scope and utility of this graphical approach. The paper's main contributions are:

1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other, which is common in longitudinal studies.
2.  A new algorithm for recovering joint distributions for a class of problems where sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness mechanisms and their Markov blankets.
3.  The first formal graphical criteria for determining when a joint or conditional distribution is *non-recoverable*. This is a crucial contribution, as it provides researchers with tools to identify when their assumptions are insufficient.
4.  A key and surprising finding that causal effects (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x) or P(y,x)) are not. This decouples the problem of causal recoverability from probabilistic recoverability.
5.  An application of these results to the problem of attrition in experiments, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting a claim in prior literature.

The work is situated within the broader context of causal inference and missing data literature, providing a principled, graph-based approach to problems traditionally classified as Missing Not At Random (MNAR), which standard methods like listwise deletion or simple imputation cannot handle correctly.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are presented as formal theorems, and the proofs are provided in the appendix. The work appears to be a complete and self-contained theoretical contribution.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical results are rigorous and well-defined. The introduction of non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) is particularly valuable, as it provides "negative" results that are often as important as "positive" ones in practice.
    *   The distinction made between the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries (Section 6) is a deep and important insight. The demonstration that `do-calculus` can be used to circumvent certain missingness-induced dependencies is elegant and powerful.
    *   The authors are careful to build upon and extend previous work systematically, showing a clear line of progression from their 2013 paper.
    *   The application to attrition (Section 7) grounds the abstract theory in a concrete and common experimental problem, demonstrating its practical relevance and correcting a result in a cited working paper.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The paper is purely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a NIPS paper, the inclusion of a small simulation study would have strengthened the claims. For example, demonstrating that an estimator derived from Theorem 2 converges to the true value with increasing sample size, while a naive approach does not, would have provided compelling empirical support.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written for an audience familiar with graphical models and causality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The structure is logical, moving from generalized sufficient conditions (Sec 3, 4) to necessary conditions (Sec 5), and then to the distinct problem of causal queries (Sec 6).
    *   The use of small, illustrative examples (e.g., Example 1 and 3) is very effective for explaining the mechanics and intuition behind the main theorems.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The paper is dense and assumes significant background knowledge (e.g., `do-calculus`, `d*-separation`, inducing paths). While this is largely appropriate for the NIPS audience, some of the new concepts in Section 4, such as the partitioning `R(part)` and the Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))`, are introduced very quickly and could benefit from more detailed explanation or a step-by-step example.
    *   The notation, while precise, can be challenging at times (e.g., `RPasub(R(i))`). A table of notations in the appendix might be helpful for readers.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it is an extension of Mohan et al. (2013), the new contributions are substantial and novel.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide formal graphical criteria for the *non-recoverability* of queries from missing data.
    *   The key result that causal queries can be identified even when the underlying joint distribution is not recoverable is a fundamental contribution that has not been clearly articulated or proven in this manner before. This significantly advances our understanding of the interplay between causality and missing data.
    *   The generalization of the factorization theorem and the new recovery algorithm in Section 4 address important gaps left by previous work.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a lasting impact on how researchers in machine learning and other empirical sciences reason about and handle missing data.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper provides a powerful conceptual tool (m-graphs) and a set of practical algorithms for a ubiquitous problem. Researchers can use these graphs to encode their assumptions about the missingness process and then use the theorems in this paper to check if their query of interest is recoverable.
    *   The non-recoverability criteria serve as an important safeguard, preventing researchers from drawing invalid conclusions from incomplete data when their assumptions are insufficient for identification.
    *   The results on causal queries open up new possibilities for causal inference in observational studies and experiments plagued by issues like attrition, demonstrating that all is not lost even when standard conditional probabilities cannot be estimated. This could directly influence the design and analysis of clinical trials and longitudinal studies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents several novel, significant, and rigorously proven theoretical results for the fundamental problem of missing data.
2.  The decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability is a major conceptual breakthrough with important practical implications.
3.  Introduces the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability, a critical tool for practitioners.
4.  The framework is general and principled, advancing the state of the art in reasoning about Missing Not At Random (MNAR) problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The presentation is quite dense and could be made more accessible with further exposition on the more complex definitions.
2.  The lack of any empirical validation, even on synthetic data, leaves a small gap between the theoretical claims and their practical performance in finite-sample settings.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes several significant and original contributions to our understanding of missing data within the principled framework of graphical models. The strengths, particularly the novelty and importance of the results on non-recoverability and causal queries, far outweigh the minor weaknesses in presentation and the absence of experiments. The paper advances the state-of-the-art and provides tools and insights that are of high value to the NIPS community and beyond. I strongly recommend acceptance.
