This paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in the study of missing data, utilizing the formal framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The central goal is to establish graphical criteria for the "recoverability" of probabilistic and causal queries from data where the missingness mechanism may be of the Missing Not At Random (MNAR) type. The paper builds directly upon Mohan et al. [2013], extending its results in several important directions.

The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem, allowing for dependencies between missingness mechanisms, which is crucial for settings like longitudinal studies.
2.  A new and more general condition for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets.
3.  The introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*. This is a crucial and novel contribution, providing researchers with tools to determine when their modeling assumptions are insufficient to answer a query.
4.  A powerful and surprising result showing that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. This is demonstrated in cases where an outcome variable is inseparable from its missingness mechanism, a situation that would typically be considered hopeless for probabilistic recovery.
5.  The application of these general results to the specific problem of attrition, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting what the authors claim are misconceptions in the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and the significance of its conceptual contributions. The work boldly tackles the most challenging class of missing data problems (MNAR) using a principled, model-based approach. The results presented here are not merely incremental; they fundamentally alter our understanding of what can be learned from incomplete data.

The most exciting and impactful result is the decoupling of recoverability for causal and probabilistic queries (Section 6). The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when P(y) is not, by leveraging the logic of do-calculus and d*-separation, is a profound insight. This opens up new possibilities for causal inference in practical settings like clinical trials where attrition (an MNAR process) is common and may be related to the outcome variable itself. This is exactly the kind of ambitious, paradigm-challenging work that the field needs.

Furthermore, the development of non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) is a major practical and theoretical advance. Much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for identification or recovery. By providing necessary conditions, the authors give researchers a formal tool to falsify the possibility of recovery under a given set of assumptions, preventing futile estimation attempts.

The theoretical development is rigorous, extending the m-graph framework in non-trivial ways. The generalization of factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms and the new recovery algorithm based on collider paths show a deep command of the underlying graphical model machinery.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's main weakness is its purely theoretical nature. It provides a powerful set of identification results but includes no simulations or empirical studies. While the theoretical contribution is self-contained and stands on its own, the practical implications would be much clearer with some form of validation. For instance, simulations could demonstrate how estimators based on the proposed recovery formulas behave with finite samples and could explore the sensitivity of the results to misspecifications in the m-graph. This absence of empirical grounding is a notable omission.

Secondly, the paper is exceptionally dense. It presumes a very high level of familiarity with the terminology and techniques of graphical causal models (e.g., m-graphs, do-calculus, d*-separation, inducing paths). While this is acceptable for a specialized audience, it makes the paper challenging to penetrate for the broader NIPS community. The definitions for new concepts like `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` are given concisely but would benefit from more intuition and step-by-step illustrations. While examples are provided, they are brief and also require significant expertise to follow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces several novel concepts, most notably the idea that causal effects can be recovered when probabilistic associations cannot. This is a foundational contribution.
*   **Bold Approach:** It pushes the boundaries of missing data analysis, providing a formal language and new tools for the difficult MNAR setting, which is often dismissed as intractable.
*   **New Tools for Practitioners:** The non-recoverability criteria are a significant practical contribution, offering clear guidance on the limits of inference.
*   **Sparks New Research:** This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on developing robust estimators based on these identification formulas and on extending these graphical criteria to even more complex settings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **No Empirical Validation:** The lack of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical utility or robustness of the proposed recovery strategies.
*   **Limited Accessibility:** The paper's density and heavy reliance on specialized knowledge may limit its immediate impact on the broader machine learning audience at NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the lack of experiments and the density of the writing are valid concerns, they are outweighed by the sheer novelty and importance of the theoretical contributions. This paper is not an incremental improvement; it is a bold step forward that challenges existing assumptions about what is possible in the face of missing data. It provides a beautiful synthesis of graphical models, causal inference, and missing data theory. The result on the recoverability of causal effects in seemingly impossible scenarios is particularly striking and promises to be highly influential. This is precisely the type of thought-provoking, foundational work that NIPS should champion, even if its methodological perfection is not yet complete with empirical validation. The weaknesses can be addressed through minor revisions to improve clarity and by positioning the empirical work as a clear next step for future research.
