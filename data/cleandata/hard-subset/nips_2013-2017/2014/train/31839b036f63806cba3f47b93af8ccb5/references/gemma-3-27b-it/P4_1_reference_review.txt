This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. It extends existing results by presenting general conditions for recovering queries, particularly highlighting that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. The work addresses a crucial problem in observational studies and experimental science where missing data is prevalent. The authors aim to provide both sufficient conditions for recovery and criteria for identifying non-recoverability, offering practical guidance for researchers. 

The paper is well-structured, progressing logically from background on m-graphs and recoverability to extensions of the sequential factorization theorem, algorithms for handling cases where factorization fails, non-recoverability criteria, recovery of causal queries, application to attrition problems, related work, and conclusion. The notation is generally consistent, although potentially dense for readers unfamiliar with the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical foundation, building on previous work by Pearl and Mohan, and rigorously defining concepts like general ordered factorization and d*-separation. Theorems and corollaries are clearly stated, and proofs are relegated to the appendix, maintaining readability.
*   **Novelty:** The key contribution lies in demonstrating that causal queries, specifically P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism (Ry). This is a significant result, expanding the scope of recoverable queries under missing data scenarios.  The extension to more complex missingness patterns (interactions between R variables) is also valuable.
*   **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper explores both sufficient conditions for recoverability *and* necessary conditions for non-recoverability, providing a more complete picture. The application to attrition, a common real-world problem, adds practical relevance.
*   **Clear Examples:** The use of examples (Figures 1, 2, 3, and 4) effectively illustrates the theoretical concepts and clarifies the application of the theorems.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The related work section is comprehensive, providing context and acknowledging relevant research in missing data handling, including discussions of ML, inverse probability weighting, and other graphical model approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Accessibility:** The paper assumes a strong background in causal inference and graphical models. The notation can be challenging for those not already familiar with the field. More intuitive explanations of concepts like d*-separation and inducing paths might improve accessibility.
*   **Practicality of Algorithms:** While the paper presents theoretical conditions for recoverability, the practical implementation of the algorithms for cases where sequential factorization fails (Section 4) is not detailed. Providing more concrete guidance or pseudo-code could enhance the paper’s utility.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on theoretical results.  Including a small-scale simulation study or illustrative example with real data to demonstrate the recoverability (or non-recoverability) in practice would strengthen the findings. This is especially important considering the complexity of the conditions.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** While the paper extensively discusses *recoverability* given an m-graph, it dedicates less attention to the crucial problem of *eliciting* a correct m-graph in the first place.  A brief discussion of sensitivity analysis to m-graph misspecification could be beneficial.
* **Repetitive phrasing:** Certain concepts, like the definition of recoverability, are repeated verbatim several times, leading to redundancy.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in the field of missing data handling, particularly concerning causal inference.
*   The novelty of demonstrating recoverability of causal effects even when probabilistic relations are unrecoverable is substantial.
*   The paper is rigorously presented and builds upon a well-established framework.
*   The comprehensive coverage of recoverability and non-recoverability criteria is valuable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s accessibility is limited by its technical depth and heavy reliance on specialized notation.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the theoretical findings.
*   The limited discussion of the practical challenges of m-graph elicitation and sensitivity analysis is a drawback.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. The novelty of the results and the rigor of the analysis outweigh the weaknesses related to accessibility and empirical validation. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors consider adding a section discussing potential challenges and practical considerations related to m-graph specification, as well as providing more guidance on the practical implementation of algorithms outlined in the paper. A simple illustrative example would also be beneficial.
