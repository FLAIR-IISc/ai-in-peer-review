This paper presents a formal graphical framework for reasoning about the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work is a direct and significant extension of the authors' prior work on "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) [Mohan et al., 2013], which provide a way to explicitly model the causal mechanisms responsible for missing data alongside the substantive variables of interest.

The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, extending its applicability to models where missingness mechanisms can influence each other (a common scenario in longitudinal studies).
2.  A new theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets.
3.  The introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3). These are necessary conditions, providing a method to prove that a query is impossible to recover under a given set of assumptions. This is a novel contribution to the field, which has largely focused on sufficient conditions for recoverability.
4.  A key finding that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distributions (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. This is demonstrated by leveraging tools from do-calculus.
5.  An application of these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims made in related literature.

The paper is well-situated within the literature on graphical models and causality, building primarily on the work of Pearl and colleagues, and contrasts its formal, model-based approach with more traditional statistical methods like multiple imputation and inverse probability weighting, which often rely on blanket assumptions like Missing At Random (MAR).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theorems are stated with formal precision, and the proofs are provided in the appendix. The claims are well-supported by graphical examples that clearly illustrate the concepts being introduced (e.g., the necessity of the "minimality" condition in Definition 2, or the recovery of a causal effect in Example 3). The work appears complete, presenting a coherent set of extensions to the m-graph framework. The authors are careful to delineate the conditions under which each theorem applies.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. It begins by reviewing the necessary background on m-graphs before systematically presenting its new contributions. The progression from simple recovery conditions (sequential factorization) to more complex ones, and then to non-recoverability and causal queries, is logical. The examples are crucial for understanding the application of the theorems and are effectively employed. The notation, while dense, is consistent with the literature in this area.

One minor point for improvement would be in the exposition of Theorem 2. The concepts of `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` are introduced concisely; a slightly more detailed, step-by-step worked example for a non-trivial graph could further aid reader comprehension of this new recovery algorithm.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. While it builds on the authors' previous work, the extensions are non-trivial and significant.
- The most original contributions are the formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3). Providing tools to prove impossibility is a novel and valuable addition to the literature on missing data.
- The result that causal queries can be recovered even when observational queries cannot (Section 6) is a powerful and non-obvious insight. It elegantly connects the graphical machinery for missing data with the established machinery of do-calculus and d*-separation in a new way.
- The application to attrition, which provides a necessary and sufficient condition and refutes a claim from prior work, also constitutes a novel contribution to that specific domain.

**Significance:** The results presented are important for both researchers and practitioners dealing with missing data, a ubiquitous problem.
- The formal framework allows for a principled, assumption-based analysis of missing data problems that goes beyond the standard MAR/MCAR/MNAR trichotomy.
- The non-recoverability criteria are particularly significant, as they provide a formal tool to determine when the stated modeling assumptions are insufficient to answer a query, thereby preventing researchers from drawing unfounded conclusions from biased estimates.
- The findings on causal queries are highly significant. They show that the challenges of missing data do not necessarily preclude causal inference, and provide a clear path for recovering causal effects in settings previously thought to be intractable. This could have a substantial impact on fields like epidemiology, social sciences, and economics, where both missing data and causal questions are prevalent.

The primary limitation is the lack of empirical validation. The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is appropriate for the nature of the contribution, its impact could be broadened by including a simulation study. For instance, demonstrating that an estimator based on Theorem 2 or Theorem 4 converges to the true parameter value with increasing sample size, while a naive approach (like complete case analysis) is biased, would provide compelling empirical support for the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents novel and technically sound graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability of queries from missing data.
*   The result separating the recoverability of causal effects from that of probabilistic distributions is a significant and insightful contribution.
*   Addresses a fundamental and widespread problem in machine learning and applied sciences with formal rigor.
*   The application to attrition demonstrates the practical utility of the framework and corrects misconceptions in the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper is purely theoretical. The absence of simulations or real-data experiments means the practical performance of the proposed recovery procedures is not assessed.
*   The work is an extension of a previous NIPS paper, which may be seen by some as incremental, although the extensions are substantial and address key limitations of the original work.

Overall, the paper's strengths, particularly its originality and the significance of its theoretical contributions, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It advances the state of the art in the formal analysis of missing data and provides the community with valuable new tools.
