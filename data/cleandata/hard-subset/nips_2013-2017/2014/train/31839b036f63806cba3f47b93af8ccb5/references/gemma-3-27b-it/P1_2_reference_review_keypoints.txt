- Extends m-graph theory with a "General Ordered Factorization" enabling recovery of probabilistic queries in more complex missing-data scenarios.  
- Shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic queries are not, highlighting a key advantage of causal reasoning.  
- Provides criteria for non-recoverability and applies results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.  
- Paper is mathematically rigorous with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (appendix).  
- Novel contribution: demonstrating recoverability of causal queries despite non-recoverable probabilistic relations.  
- Generality: expands applicability of m-graphs to interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Practical relevance: addresses common attrition issues and has broad potential impact.  
- Well-grounded in prior work, building on Pearl and Mohan et al.  
- Strength: gives clear non-recoverability conditions as well as recoverability results.  
- Weakness: dense notation and heavy theoretical focus reduce accessibility to non-experts.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation on simulated or real datasets.  
- Weakness: examples are complex and could benefit from a simpler illustrative case.  
- Weakness: attrition application focuses on a simple case; limits and extensions to more complex attrition are under-discussed.  
- Weakness: results depend on strong m-graph assumptions; sensitivity to misspecification is not analyzed.  
- Quality score: 4/5 — technically sound but missing empirical support.  
- Clarity score: 3/5 — precise but technically dense; needs better exposition and visuals.  
- Originality score: 5/5 — highly original theoretical advance.  
- Significance score: 4/5 — results could meaningfully impact fields dealing with missing data.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions focused on improving clarity, providing simpler examples, discussing sensitivity to graph misspecification, and (optionally) adding empirical validation.
