### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs), introduced in prior work by the authors, to provide a more comprehensive set of graphical tools for analyzing recoverability of queries from datasets with missing values. The core idea is to represent the causal relationships between variables and their missingness mechanisms in a single graph, and then use graphical criteria to determine if a target query (e.g., a joint distribution or a causal effect) can be consistently estimated.

The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms, which is common in settings like longitudinal studies.
2.  A new theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization fails, relying on concepts of collider paths and Markov blankets of the missingness indicators.
3.  The first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions, providing necessary conditions that can definitively rule out estimation.
4.  A key insight that causal queries (e.g., `P(y|do(x))`) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., `P(y,x)`) are not, by leveraging tools from do-calculus and d*-separation.
5.  An application of these results to the problem of attrition, where necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects are derived, correcting results from previous literature.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes several strong theoretical advances. The move from sufficient conditions (Theorems 1 & 2) to necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is particularly impactful. Providing formal criteria to determine when a problem is *unsolvable* given the assumptions is as important as providing solutions, and this has been lacking in the graphical modeling literature for missing data.

2.  **Novel and Deep Connection to Causal Inference:** The highlight of the paper is Section 6, which demonstrates that recoverability of causal queries is a distinct and sometimes more attainable goal than recoverability of probabilistic queries. The example in Figure 3, where `P(y|do(z))` is recoverable but `P(y,z,w)` is not, is an elegant and powerful demonstration of this principle. By connecting the m-graph framework with the sophisticated machinery of do-calculus and d*-separation, the authors provide a new and fruitful bridge between the missing data and causal identification literatures.

3.  **Increased Generality and Practicality:** The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to allow for edges between R-variables makes the framework applicable to a wider and more realistic class of problems. Similarly, the application of the framework to attrition (Section 7) grounds the abstract theory in a concrete, common, and challenging problem, showing the practical utility of the proposed graphical criteria. The correction of a result from prior work (Garcia [2013]) demonstrates a clear advance in the state of the art.

4.  **Systematic Approach:** The paper builds systematically on the authors' previous work, creating a coherent and increasingly powerful formal system for reasoning about missing data. The progression from simple factorization to more complex cases, and from probabilistic to causal queries, is logical and well-motivated.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is technically dense, and while precise, some of the new concepts could be made more intuitive. For instance, the conditions in Theorem 2, involving partitions `R(part)` and Markov blankets `Mb(R(i))`, are complex. A small, dedicated illustrative figure for these specific concepts would significantly improve clarity and help the reader build intuition, beyond the examples given in Figure 2.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Demonstration:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical results are strong and stand on their own, the paper would have greater impact if it included a section demonstrating the practical application of the recovery algorithms. For a query deemed recoverable, how is the estimand actually computed from a finite dataset? A simulation study showing that the proposed estimators are indeed consistent, while estimators for non-recoverable queries are biased, would provide compelling validation of the theory.

3.  **Scope of Assumptions:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the practitioner can specify a correct m-graph. While the authors appropriately cite their work on testing m-graphs, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the recoverability results to misspecification of the graph would be a valuable addition. For example, how robust are the conclusions if a weak dependency (a faint edge) is omitted from the graph?

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **On Theorem 1:** The introduction of minimality in Definition 2 is a subtle but crucial point. The subsequent example does a good job of explaining why this is necessary. This is a nice refinement of the previous work.
*   **On Theorem 4:** This theorem beautifully formalizes the insight from Section 6.1. It leverages advanced concepts like inducing paths and d*-separation. It would be helpful to explicitly state that the conditions in this theorem essentially ensure that the `R_y` variable can be "adjusted for" in the causal world, even if it cannot be in the observational one.
*   **On the Attrition Results:** The necessary and sufficient conditions in Theorems 5 and 6 are very clean and powerful results for a common special case. This section is a strong selling point for the utility of the framework.

### Assessment

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theorems appear sound, and the proofs are provided in the appendix. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but is very dense. As noted, more illustrations for the most complex concepts would improve accessibility. The organization is logical.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the authors' previous work, the new non-recoverability criteria, the theorems for more complex dependency structures, and especially the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability are novel and significant contributions.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for both researchers in causality and machine learning, and for practitioners dealing with missing data. The paper provides a principled, graphical language to reason about a pervasive and difficult problem. The finding that some causal effects are recoverable from MNAR (Missing Not At Random) data, where standard methods fail, could have a major impact on how such problems are approached in experimental sciences.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the understanding of missing data within the graphical models framework. The connection drawn between causal identification and recoverability from missing data is particularly novel and insightful. Despite its density and lack of experiments, the theoretical strength and importance of the results make it an excellent fit for NIPS. The weaknesses are addressable and could be incorporated into a final version to further improve the paper's impact.
