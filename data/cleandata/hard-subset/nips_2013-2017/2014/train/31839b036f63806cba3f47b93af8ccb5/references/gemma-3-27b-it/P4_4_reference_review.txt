This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, utilizing Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). Building upon prior work by Mohan et al. [2013], the authors extend the conditions for recoverability, focusing on scenarios where standard factorization methods fail and highlighting the surprising ability to recover causal effects even when the underlying probabilistic relations are non-recoverable. The paper covers sequential factorization, introduces new non-recoverability criteria, and specifically addresses the problem of attrition.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound with a strong theoretical foundation. Theorems and corollaries are presented with clear statements and supporting examples. The extension of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and the non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) seem logically derived and build effectively on previous work. The application to attrition is relevant and well-motivated. However, the density of notation and the complexity of some theorems might make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the domain.  The presentation of results related to attrition (Section 7) feels somewhat repetitive of earlier theorems, merely restating them in the context of attrition; a more concise presentation leveraging the generality of earlier results would be preferable. While the authors mention the importance of strictly positive complete cases, a deeper discussion on the practical implications of this assumption would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, following a logical progression from definitions and foundational concepts to more advanced results. However, the writing style is dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge.  Definitions are often introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, potentially hindering understanding for those less familiar with missing data theory. While examples are provided, they often serve to illustrate specific applications of theorems rather than clarify the underlying concepts. The notation is consistent but heavy, and could be simplified in several instances. More visual aids accompanying the theorems and definitions would enhance clarity. The extensive use of numbered equations without much contextual text surrounding them hinders readability.

**Originality:** The work presents a significant advancement in the field of missing data analysis, particularly in the context of graphical models. While building upon existing work, the authors extend the boundaries of recoverability analysis, addressing more complex scenarios and demonstrating the potential for causal inference even in the presence of non-recoverable probabilistic relations. The exploration of conditions for non-recoverability is a novel contribution, providing useful guidance for practitioners. The application to attrition, while not entirely new, is strengthened by the theoretical framework presented. The key novelty lies in extending existing theory to scenarios with interacting missingness mechanisms and highlighting the distinct recoverability properties of causal queries.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper have the potential to significantly impact the field of causal inference and statistical modeling with missing data. The ability to recover causal effects even when probabilistic relations are non-recoverable is a powerful finding with practical implications for various domains. The non-recoverability criteria provide valuable guidance for assessing the limitations of analysis and avoiding misleading conclusions. The work contributes to a more nuanced understanding of missing data mechanisms and their impact on inferential validity. The theoretical framework offers a rigorous foundation for developing robust and reliable methods for handling missing data in complex datasets. This work is likely to be used by researchers and practitioners interested in causal inference and missing data analysis.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a rigorous and well-developed theoretical framework.
*   **Novel Extensions:** The authors extend existing theory to address more complex missing data scenarios.
*   **Significant Results:** The finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not is significant.
*   **Practical Implications:** The work has practical relevance for causal inference and statistical modeling.
*   **Clear Organization:** The paper is logically structured and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dense Writing Style:** The paper's writing is dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge, reducing accessibility.
*   **Heavy Notation:** The extensive use of notation can hinder understanding.
*   **Limited Intuition:**  Definitions and theorems lack intuitive explanation for non-expert readers.
*   **Repetitive sections:** The application of general theorems to attrition (section 7) is somewhat repetitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong technical paper with significant contributions to the field of missing data analysis. The theoretical rigor and novel extensions warrant acceptance. However, improving the clarity of the writing and reducing the density of notation would greatly enhance its impact and accessibility. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns.
