Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values using missingness graphs (m-graphs), extending Mohan et al. (2013). It refines the sequential factorization approach to allow ordered factorizations that include missingness indicators R, yielding sufficient conditions (Theorem 1) for recovering joint/conditional distributions in settings where missingness mechanisms may influence each other. When no admissible sequence exists, the paper proposes a new recovery result (Theorem 2) based on partitioning R by (non-)adjacency and absence of collider paths, coupled with Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), and provides a simpler corollary for models without latent confounding between substantive and R variables. The paper also introduces graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).

For causal queries, it emphasizes that recoverability may hold even when P(V) is not recoverable, e.g., P(y|do(z)) with Y inseparable from Ry (Example 3), leveraging d*-separation/dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008). The paper applies these ideas to “simple attrition,” giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6) when |Vm|=1.

The work is anchored in the Pearl/Shpitser identification literature, selection/coarsening frameworks (Rubin; Heitjan & Rubin; Robins & Rotnitzky), and builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013). To my knowledge, explicit graphical non-recoverability criteria and the particular extensions of ordered factorization to include R are not in prior NeurIPS proceedings, though related ideas exist in causal identification with selection bias and in dormant independence.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, longstanding problem: principled handling of MNAR using explicit causal assumptions.
- Extends established methodology (m-graphs) with clear, graph-theoretic criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability. The admissible general ordered factorization (Theorem 1) is a natural and useful refinement.
- Theorem 2 provides a constructive formula for P(V) in a class of semi-Markovian models beyond earlier sequential factorizations, with an interpretable partition/Mb structure.
- Clarifies that causal effects can be recoverable when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, with explicit conditions (Theorem 4) and a practically relevant treatment of attrition (Theorems 5–6).
- Good conceptual contribution: separating “identifiability” in the substantive model from “recoverability” under missingness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited validation and practicality: The paper is almost entirely theoretical. There are no experiments (even synthetic) to demonstrate finite-sample behavior, numerical stability, or robustness to model misspecification, nor comparisons to standard MAR-based ML/EM, IPW, or MI methods in MNAR settings. Without empirical evidence, it is hard to assess the practical impact for the NeurIPS audience.
- Algorithmic clarity and scope: Section 4 speaks of “general algorithms,” but the paper largely states criteria and estimands without a complete decision procedure, complexity analysis, or pseudocode to check the graphical conditions and construct estimands systematically (e.g., an explicit sound-and-complete algorithm for recoverability given an m-graph). As written, users may struggle to apply the results beyond the given examples.
- Assumption strength and realism: Several central results require strong separability constraints, e.g., Theorem 2 assumes no Vm node is a neighbor of—or collider-connected to—its own missingness mechanism, which may fail in common MNAR scenarios. Attrition results are limited to |Vm|=1; many practical studies have multiple missing variables and interacting mechanisms.
- Potential correctness/clarity gap in Example 3: The step P(y|do(z)) = P(y|do(z), R′y) is nontrivial. In general, conditioning on Ry=0 can distort the distribution if Ry depends on Y, so this equality needs explicit conditions (e.g., dormant independence statements) and a precise citation. The text attributes overall recoverability to d*-separation, but the equality itself should be justified or avoided in favor of a derivation that never conditions on Ry without conditions.
- Presentation and notation: The manuscript is notation-heavy, and key objects (e.g., minimality in Definition 2, Mb and partitions) would benefit from a concise algorithmic checklist and more guided examples. Several references are malformed, and the paper relies heavily on appendices for proofs with limited intuition in the main text.
- Testability and model criticism: While prior work (Mohan & Pearl, 2014) is cited, this paper does not show how to connect its new criteria to testable implications or diagnostics from data, which would be valuable for practitioners constructing/validating m-graphs.

Quality
The theoretical development is generally plausible and builds on established causal graph machinery, but key steps (notably in Example 3) require clearer and more explicit conditions. Proofs are deferred; without stronger exposition and at least some empirical corroboration, the claims feel under-validated for a broad ML audience.

Clarity
Readable for experts in causal graphs, but dense and not self-contained for a NeurIPS-wide readership. Improving definitions, adding a decision procedure, and fixing references would help substantially.

Originality
Moderate. The work extends Mohan et al. (2013) with meaningful, but incremental, theoretical results (generalized factorization, non-recoverability criteria, and attrition characterizations). The novelty lies in sharper graphical conditions rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.

Significance
Potentially useful to researchers using causal graphs for MNAR, but the impact is limited by scope (e.g., |Vm|=1 in key causal results) and shortage of practical validation and tooling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an explicit algorithm to decide recoverability and construct estimands, with complexity and completeness guarantees where possible.
- Add synthetic experiments validating recoverability formulas under MNAR, illustrating bias of MAR methods, and robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify and justify the conditioning step in Example 3 or re-derive without that equality; state precise dormant independence conditions needed.
- Expand beyond |Vm|=1 for causal effects or discuss obstacles clearly.
- Improve exposition: worked examples with full derivations, clearer notation, figures, and corrected references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances a timely topic with concrete new graphical criteria.
- Solid theoretical lineage; extends widely cited prior work.
- Clear insight that causal recoverability can exceed probabilistic recoverability.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical validation or implementation; limited practical guidance.
- Some results hinge on strong structural assumptions; causal results restricted to |Vm|=1.
- Clarity and possible correctness issues (Example 3 equality); heavy reliance on appendices.
- Missing a general decision/estimation algorithm and complexity analysis.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are interesting but need clearer derivations, a systematic algorithmic treatment, and empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards and to convince a skeptical reader of practical utility.
