- Extends m-graphs to give broader conditions and algorithms for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Refines sequential factorization theorems and provides algorithms when those theorems fail.  
- Introduces graphical criteria for non-recoverability and shows causal effects can be recoverable even if probabilistic relations are not or Y is not d-separable.  
- Important problem with broad applicability to observational and experimental science.  
- Novel contribution: recoverability of causal queries despite non-separability, distinguishing it from MAR/MCAR frameworks.  
- Strong theoretical rigor with formal definitions, theorems, corollaries, and extended sequential factorization.  
- Generally clear presentation and helpful examples for complex concepts.  
- Comprehensive and well-organized related work survey.  
- Theoretical development is dense and notation-heavy, making it hard to follow without strong background.  
- Lacks empirical validation; needs simulation or real-data studies to demonstrate practical performance.  
- Algorithms lack detailed implementation guidance and practical application steps (e.g., finding admissible sequences).  
- Potential computational intractability for large or complex m-graphs is not adequately discussed.  
- Attrition treatment is useful but limited; needs broader discussion of varied attrition mechanisms.  
- Review scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, rigorous framework, and meaningful extensions to existing theory.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical evidence, complexity may hinder adoption, and scalability concerns.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on adding simulation/empirical validation, addressing computational complexity and practical implementation, and expanding attrition discussion.
