

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) by providing general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan and Pearl (2014) to develop a comprehensive framework for determining when queries can be recovered from datasets with missing values. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) refined conditions for recovering probabilistic queries, (2) new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and (3) the significant insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not (specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable).

The paper demonstrates theoretical soundness through rigorous definitions, theorems, and proofs provided in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))), showing that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separable—a finding that corrects previous misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013). The paper provides clear examples illustrating the application of theorems to specific m-graphs, which aids in understanding the abstract concepts.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from foundational concepts to more complex results. The notation is consistent and well-defined, though some technical terms (e.g., d*-separation, inducing paths) may require familiarity with causal inference literature. The authors effectively relate their work to previous NIPS contributions, particularly Mohan et al. (2013), and clearly articulate how their results extend and improve upon prior work.

The originality of the paper is strong. While Mohan et al. (2013) introduced m-graphs and basic recoverability conditions, this work provides significantly more general conditions for recoverability and introduces novel results about causal queries. The non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions are particularly noteworthy, as they formally address a gap in the literature. The paper distinguishes its contributions clearly from related work, including Garcia (2013) and Thoemmes and Rose (2013), which make similar claims but fail to account for interactions between missingness mechanisms.

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is a pervasive challenge across experimental sciences, and the paper provides a unified graphical framework for determining when queries can be recovered. The results have practical implications for researchers dealing with missing data, particularly in longitudinal studies where attrition is common. The paper's correction of previous misconceptions (e.g., Garcia 2013) about necessary conditions for recoverability represents a meaningful advance in the field.

Strengths:
- Comprehensive theoretical framework extending Mohan et al. (2013)
- Clear distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries
- Rigorous proofs and well-constructed examples
- Identification of novel non-recoverability criteria
- Practical application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies
- Clear articulation of contributions relative to prior work

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical validation of theoretical results on real datasets
- No discussion of computational complexity of the proposed recovery algorithms
- Relatively brief treatment of practical implementation considerations
- Could provide more concrete examples of how to apply the criteria in real-world scenarios
- The connection to recent machine learning work on missing data (e.g., Marlin et al. 2011, 2007, 2009) could be strengthened

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with clear theoretical contributions that advance the state of the art. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries is particularly novel and important, as it resolves a key limitation in previous approaches. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing formal graphical conditions for recoverability that can be applied broadly.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a rigorous extension of Mohan et al. (2013) with significant theoretical advances
- Introduces novel results about causal query recoverability that correct previous misconceptions
- Offers formal non-recoverability criteria that fill a gap in the literature
- Presents a comprehensive framework with clear practical applications
- Well-organized, clearly written, and technically sound

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation of theoretical results
- Some technical concepts may be challenging for readers without strong causal inference background
- Could provide more detailed discussion of computational aspects

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of causal inference and missing data analysis. The theoretical results are sound, novel, and significant, with clear implications for practical applications. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions relative to prior work. While some aspects of implementation could be strengthened, the theoretical advances represent a meaningful step forward in the field. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a significant contribution to the state of the art in graphical models for missing data problems.
