Summary
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (2013), it proposes: (i) a generalized sequential factorization that allows ordering over R-nodes (missingness mechanisms), enabling recovery when R variables interact; (ii) a more general theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets defined through collider paths, with a corollary under additional no-latent assumptions; (iii) new graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint (Theorem 3) and conditional (Corollary 2) distributions; (iv) results showing that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic queries are not, including a do-calculus-based example and a theorem (Theorem 4) phrased in terms of d*-separation; (v) an application to “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint (Theorem 5) and causal (Theorem 6) queries.

Relation to prior work is mainly to Mohan, Pearl, and co-authors, do-calculus identifiability (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006), and standard missing-data frameworks (Rubin, Little & Rubin). The paper’s aim is squarely in the line of graphical approaches to MNAR recovery.

Strengths
- Important problem: principled recovery under MNAR is highly relevant to ML and causal inference.
- Conceptual extension: allowing R variables in the sequential factorization is a natural and potentially useful generalization, illustrated in Example 1.
- Non-recoverability conditions: Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 could help practitioners quickly diagnose impossibility.
- Emphasis on cases where causal effects are recoverable while probabilistic queries are not highlights a subtle and practically important distinction.

Weaknesses (technical correctness and clarity)
1) Internal inconsistency regarding “simple attrition”:
- Example 3 and Theorem 4 argue that P(y|do(z)) may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (and |Vm|=1 appears to hold in the example).
- Theorem 6 then states an iff condition that P(y|do(x)) is recoverable under simple attrition if and only if Y and Ry are neither neighbors nor connected by an inducing path. This contradicts Theorem 4 and the earlier narrative claiming recoverability “despite the existence of an inseparable path between Y and Ry.”
- This contradiction must be resolved (either by correcting statements, tightening assumptions, or clarifying that Theorem 4 does not fall under “simple attrition”).

2) Notational errors and ambiguities that impede verification:
- Early on: “Let G(V,E) be the causal DAG where V = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R.” V cannot equal V ∪ …; this likely intends the node set N = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R.
- “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively.” Both notations are identical; likely distinct symbols were intended (e.g., G\X-out vs G\X-in).
- Definition of compatibility between manifest and underlying distributions is unclear and uses Rx=0 and Ry=1 together in ways that are hard to parse; please formalize using consistent set notation and quantifiers.
- Prime notation: X′ is defined as X=0; later R′x is used; ensure consistent use and specify domain for binary coding of R.
- Theorem 1 conditions use undefined or unclear symbols: Rxi, RXm, Xr, Rz⊥⊥RXi|Xi, “Z /∈ Xi”, etc. Please define RXi as the set of missingness indicators for Xi, etc., and make the statements precise.
- In Example 1, Z is treated as if it has Rz (Z* appears later), yet factorization uses P(Z|Ry)=P(Z), which conflates whether Z is partially observed; clarity is needed on which variables are in Vm.
- Theorem 4 mixes W, W1, and GX,W1 without prior definition of W1 or the graph operation GX,W1; d*-separation is quoted but the definition provided is nonstandard and imprecise.
- Multiple places have missing spaces, inconsistent capitalization, and typos that make reading difficult.

3) Overstated claims of “general algorithms”:
- Section 4 announces “general algorithms” but provides only theorem statements and a product formula; there is no actual algorithmic procedure, pseudo-code, or complexity discussion. Please either provide a constructive algorithm (e.g., to find R-partitions, MBs, and assemble the estimand) or refrain from calling these algorithms.

4) Proofs and reproducibility:
- All proofs are deferred to the appendix. For a theory paper, at least outlines of the key proofs (or core lemmas) should be in the main text. Without them, it is hard to assess correctness, especially given the notational issues and the internal contradiction noted above.

5) Relation to prior work and positioning:
- The paper leans heavily on Mohan et al. (2013). It would help to delineate clearly which cases/new conditions are genuinely beyond that work, and to provide concrete examples where the new results strictly apply and the old do not (beyond figure references).
- The use and definition of ADMG Markov blankets (Richardson, 2003) in Theorem 2 should be more carefully introduced, with a self-contained definition and an example.

6) Experimental/empirical illustration:
- No empirical evaluation or synthetic demonstrations are provided to illustrate the gain in recoverability or robustness of the proposed procedures. Even a small set of synthetic m-graphs with simulated data validating the formulas would strengthen the paper.

7) References and formatting:
- The reference list is severely malformed (e.g., “?, \Q2013\E,” repeated author tokens, inconsistent journal info). This does not meet NeurIPS standards.
- Footnote markers (e.g., “1”) and LaTeX artifacts (e.g., R′y) persist; many punctuation/spacing errors.
- Figures are central to the arguments but not described in sufficient detail in text; ensure all graphs are fully annotated and that claims (e.g., d-separation relations used in examples) are verifiable from the figure captions.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely topic.
- Potentially useful extensions: inclusion of R in ordered factorization; non-recoverability criteria.
- Highlights subtle difference between probabilistic recoverability and causal recoverability.

Cons
- Internal contradiction between Theorem 4 and Theorem 6; unclear scope of Example 3.
- Numerous notational/typographical issues that impede understanding and raise correctness concerns.
- Lack of concrete algorithms, proof sketches in main text, and empirical illustrations.
- References and formatting below publication standards.

Recommendation: Reject (clear, though potentially promising with substantial revision)
Significance could be high if the theory is corrected and presented cleanly, but the current manuscript’s inconsistencies and presentation issues are too severe for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to: (i) fix the contradiction around simple attrition; (ii) rigorously clean up notation and definitions; (iii) provide constructive algorithms and proof sketches; (iv) add illustrative simulations; and (v) correct references and formatting. My confidence in this recommendation is high.
