This paper presents an extension of work on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to address recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. While the topic of missing data is undeniably important, and the use of graphical models offers a principled approach, this paper largely feels like an incremental extension of prior work with limited demonstrated practical impact. My primary concern revolves around the lack of substantial empirical validation; the examples provided are illustrative but fall far short of demonstrating the utility and robustness of the proposed methods on real-world datasets.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem and connects to prior work (Mohan et al., 2013; Pearl & Mohan, 2014). The theoretical development—refining the sequential factorization theorem and introducing new non-recoverability criteria—is mathematically sound, as far as I can assess without a full, independent verification of all proofs (which are relegated to the appendix). However, the theoretical focus heavily outweighs empirical evaluation. The paper introduces theorems and definitions, building upon a fairly specialized framework, but does not convincingly show *why* these extensions are crucial or what problems they solve that existing methods cannot. The assertion that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y is not separable from its missingness mechanism is potentially significant, but the example provided (Section 6) feels contrived, and its generalizability is unclear. 

The treatment of attrition (Section 7) is similarly limited. While applying the theorems to this domain is reasonable, the discussion feels superficial. The comparison with Garcia (2013) highlights a nuance but doesn’t demonstrate a clear advantage of the presented m-graph approach over more established attrition-handling methods like multiple imputation. The authors claim to "rectify prevailing opinion," but this claim isn’t substantiated by a comparative analysis. The extension to datasets with interacting missingness mechanisms (R variables influencing each other) is a positive contribution, but the conditions for applicability (e.g., minimality in definition 2) may be restrictive in practice.

The related work section is thorough, covering relevant approaches to missing data. However, it primarily serves to position the authors’ work *relative* to existing methods rather than critically comparing its performance. 

**Strengths:**

*   Formal and rigorous treatment of missing data using m-graphs.
*   Extension of existing theoretical results for recovering causal queries.
*   Identification of new non-recoverability criteria.
*   Thorough coverage of related work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation. The examples are illustrative but not representative of real-world complexities.
*   Lack of comparison with established methods (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting).
*   The practical significance of the theoretical extensions is not clearly demonstrated.
*   Conditions for applicability (e.g., minimality) may be restrictive.
*   The writing is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing missing data.
*   The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem are a valid contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of substantial empirical validation significantly weakens the paper.
*   The paper’s impact is limited by its highly specialized nature and lack of comparative performance.
*   The presented methods do not appear to offer a clear advantage over existing techniques in practical scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical contributions are valid, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the limited demonstration of practical significance make it unsuitable for NIPS at this time. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a significant advancement with demonstrable impact. The authors should substantially expand the empirical evaluation, including comparisons with standard methods on realistic datasets, before resubmitting. A more focused presentation with more intuitive explanations would also improve its accessibility.
